PREFACE
This document presents the Implementation Completion Reports (ICRs) for three projects in
the Republic of Mauritius: (1) the Industrial and Vocational Training Project (IVTP), for which IBRD
Loan 3401-MAS in the amount of $5.40 million was approved by the Board on September 5, 1991;
(2) the Education Sector Development Project (ESDP), for which IBRD Loan 3578-MAS in the
amount of $20.0 million was approved by the Board on March 23, 1993; and (3) the Higher and
Technical Education Project (HTEP), for which IBRD Loan 3859-MAS in the amount of $16.0
million was approved by the Board on March 23, 1995. The Financing Plans for the three projects
specified counterpart financing in the amounts of US$2.3 million (IVTP), US$58.7 million (ESDP)
and US$10.5 million (HTEP). None of the three projects involved external co-financing in addition to
the IBRD loans. The projects were declared effective on March 5, 1992 (IVTP), June 22, 1993
(ESDP) and September 6, 1995 (H-TEP).
The loan amounts for all three projects were reduced during implementation by mutual
agreement of Government and the Bank: US 1 million of the IVTP Loan was cancelled at the time of
the mid-term review (March 1995), and US$6 million of the ESDP Loan and US$12 million of the
HTEP Loan were cancelled in December 1997. Final disbursements took place on March 2, 1999
(IVTP), March 4, 1999 (ESDP) and March 10, 1999 (HTEP). Balances (in US$ equivalent) of
(1) US$ 1,119,288.89 (2) US$ 1,712,744.92 and (3) US$ 908.571.05 were cancelled on (1) April 20,
1999 (2) April 16, 1999 and (3) April 21, 1999. These balances were equivalent to 21 percent, 9
percent and 6 percent of the original loan amounts.
All three projects were closed on February 28, 1999, the first two after having been extended
by two months beyond the original closing date of December 31, 1998, and the third almost two years
earlier than the SAR closing date of December 31, 2000. The three projects were closed by mutual
agreement of the Bank and the Govermment of Mauritius because of persistent delays in
implementation and disbursement.  The ICR exercise provides an opportunity for both the
Government and the Bank to assess the reasons for this experience and the lessons to be learned.
Many of the factors affecting implementation were common across the three projects, and the decision
was taken to combirie the three ICRs in a single mission. Other factors, however, were unique to one
or another of the three projects, and in order to achieve accountability for the successes and failures of
project implementation, each of the projects receives fill ICR coverage in one chapter of the report.
The introductory chapter contains the three evaluation summaries.
The mission to prepare the ICR was in the country for three weeks in January/February 1999.
The mission was led by Peter R. Moock (Principal Economist, EASHD), who was assisted by Arvil
Van Adams (Sector Manager, AFTH4). Jon Lauglo (Technical and Vocational Education Specialist,
AFTHD), Marylou Bradley (Operations Analyst, AFTH4) and Joseph Bredie (Senior Education
Specialist, AFTH4) took particular responsibility, respectively, for the separate evaluations of IVTP
(Chapter 2), EDSP (Chapter 3) and HTEP (Chapter 4). The statistical tables for each project are
included in the first annex (A) at the end of each of these three chapters. The evaluations are based on
documents in the project files and on information from interviews with government officials, project
staff and project stakeholders. The Borrower's contributions to the ICRs were given to the Bank in
advance of the mission and are included at the end of each chapter, in Annex B. The Aide-Memoires
for ICR missions are appended as Annex C.



1. INTRODUCTION AND EVALUATION SUMMARIES
A. INTRODUCTION
1.1   Mauritius consists of a group of islands in the Indian Ocean with a total land area of
2,045 square kilometers (799 square miles). The main island, Mauritius, has a land area of
1,865 square kms and a population of 1.1 million. Rodrigues, 600 kms to the east, has a land
area of 108 square kms and a population of about 38,000. The country also has two
dependencies, St. Brandon and Agalega. Mauritius is one of the most densely populated
countries in the world, with a density of about 540 people per square kn. Currently, the
country's population is growing at a rate of about 1.1 percent a year.
Political and Economic Context
1.2 Mauritius achieved independence from Britain in 1968. The Government is a
parliamentary democracy. In the 30 years since independence, what was a low-income and
heavily agricultural economy has evolved to one that is middle-income and much more
diversified pursuing a vision of rapid technology-based growth. Gross domestic product
(GDP) per capita has grown from US$700 in 1970 to about US$3,500 today. The real rate of
growth over this long period (about 5.6 percent) is among the world's highest. Over the past
two decades, the proportion of the population living below the poverty line has fallen from
above 20 percent to below 5 percent, while differences in household incomes have narrowed.
Between 1981 and 1992, the Gini coefficient went from 0.46 to 0.37, a marked change in the
direction of greater income equality.
1.3 Sugar occupies over 85 percent of Mauritius' agricultural land, and in 1996 it
contributed about 8 percent of GDP and 29 percent of export earnings - down from about one-
fifth and one-half 25 years earlier. The Government has introduced incentives for economic
diversification.  More of agriculture is now in flowers, vegetables and fruits, and
manufacturing and tourism account for growing shares of GDP. About 24 percent of GDP in
1996 was in the manufacturing sector. An export processing zone (EPZ) created in 1976 now
contributes about half of value-added in the manufacturing sector and half of total export
earnings. Mauritius has become one of the world's largest exporters of textiles, a sector
which makes up about 85 percent of EPZ output. Tourism, the third major source of income,
has expanded from less than 1 percent of GDP and less than 3 percent of foreign earnings in
the 1970s to about 5 percent and 25 percent, respectively, today. The number of tourists,
about 540,000 in 1997, has doubled approximately during the 1990s. About 27 percent of
visitors come from France, 15 percent from Reunion and 10 percent from South Africa, and
most of the rest are from European countries other than France.
1.4 Since the early 1990s, the country's economic performance has come under some strain.
Mauritius' heavy dependence on preferential trade arrangements with the European Union
(EU), its largest market, underscores the fragility of its earlier success. Real growth rates in
the EPZ sector have declined throughout the 1990s. Mauritius' competitiveness has fallen as

1.8 The University of Mauritius (UoM) is the only full university currently in the country,
although the Mauritius Institute of Education (MIE), the Mauritius College of the Air (MCA)
and the Mahatma Gandhi Institute (MGI) also qualify as tertiary education institutions (TEIs).
The annual output of university graduates has increased in recent years to nearly 500, but this
number remains small in relation to Mauritius' population and vision, and in comparison to
countries at a similar stage of development. Initial plans are in the offing to establish a new
and prestigious TEI, the Mauritius University of Technology. The Tertiary Education
Commission (TEC) was established in 1988 to provide coordination and oversight for all TEIs
in the country, to administer a one-line budget allocation from the Ministry of Finance (MOF)
in support of the tertiary sub-sector, and to encourage regional initiatives, e.g., with other
Indian Ocean Rim Association for Regional Cooperation (IORARC) member states. The
Technical School Management Trust Fund (MTF) performs a similar function in regard to the
polytechnic institutes, which now number three. Middle-level technician education has
expended in recent years, but the MTF needs to do more to promote the role of technicians in
the economy and stimulate demand.
1.9 At the tertiary level, the orientation and preparation towards science and engineering
studies are weak. Critics of UoM say that it operates more like a glorified secondary school
than a degree-granting institution. The focus has been on teaching in the narrow sense, and
because it is a non-residential campus apparently, most activities at the University come to an
end at 4 p.m. each afternoon. Quality improvements at the UoM have been sluggish until
recently. According to a survey by TEC employers are concerned by the narrowness of UoM
graduates and the absence of work-skills and initiative. Graduates' contributions to strategic
economic and social activities are considered small and uneven. Until recently, university
staff conducted little or no research (a problem that the Higher and Technical Education
Project tried to address, but with only some success). Particularly research in support of the
country's socio-economic vision has been lacking.
1.10 Those students who do not qualify for tertiary education, or drop-out of secondary and
want to learn a trade, can access the free training offered by the Industrial and Vocational
Training Board (IVTB) and many private training centers. IVTB (the development of which
body has been supported under the first of the three projects reviewed in this ICR) is the
largest provider of training but its effectiveness is undermined by a lack of consensus whether
it should be principally a provider or principally a facilitator/regulator of training. It also lacks
the institutional capacity to directs its investments to training offering the greatest public
benefits. IVTB's effectiveness is also hampered in that many trainees are inadequately

percent. Public education is free and tertiary education free of tuition charges. Rapid increases
in enrollment and operating costs, particularly at tertiary level, are beginning to tax the
sustainability of the current policies. While TEC is promoting new fee-based programs in
health, environmental and information technologies in line with the sector's vision, a cost-
analysis of expanding free secondary and higher education has yet to be done, suggesting gaps
in policy analysis and lack of clear directions.
Sector Policy
1.12 The opportunities missed to achieve the sector's vision are attributable in a large part to
weaknesses in policy on the part of the Government. In Mauritius, there has been no lack of
policy documents and proposals on education and training (E&T). An inventory of official
documents on E&T in the country (since the "Nichols Report on Secondary Education in
Mauritius" in 1947) was carried out by the United Nations Development Program (UNDP) in
1997. This exercise resulted in a preliminary list of 73 such documents, many of them
recommending broad reforms in the system of E&T. These reports often touch on the same
set of issues and make similar policy recommendations, but the cumulative impact of these
documents has been less than one might hope and expect, because of a failure to develop a
political consensus for implementing the specific actions recommended. Sometimes, policy
documents have been associated with a particular Government. When Parliamentary
elections result in a new Government, what was official policy is quietly shelved and replaced
by a new policy, which may or may not be very different from the old, but which must be
implemented often by a new group of officials, resulting inevitably in confusion and delay.
1.13 There are a few documents that should be taken into account in order to understand
developments since independence and the current status of E&T in the country. Some of
these, it will be seen, have been quite closely linked with the preparation and/or
implementation of the three Bank-supported projects reviewed in this ICR.
1.14 One early document is "Government Aid to Private Secondary Schools," the report of a
1974 commission chaired by the Honorable Mr. Justice Ramphul which set the policy,
implemented in 1976 and continuing mostly intact to the present day, for (1) the elimination
of all fees in pre-tertiary education, private as well as public, and (2) the establishment of the
Private Secondary School Authority (PSSA), which administers a complicated system of
government grants to registered private schools in Mauritius. Two proposals over the
following 15 years (the first that of Sir Kher Jagatsingh in 1979, "The Future in Our Hands -
Mauritian Education for Today and Tomorrow," and the second that of Mr. Ramesh
Ramdoyal in 1990, "Proposal for Structural Reform") pointed the way towards a restructuring
of the 6-plus-5-plus-2 education system and the elimination of the CPE for selective purposes
at the end of Standard 6.
4



1.15 These led to the 1992 "Master Plan for the Year 2000," a comprehensive policy
document calling for nine years of basic compulsory education and for a wide range of other
reforms to promote quality and equity in pre-tertiary education, many of which were
implemented only in part. The Master Plan was the key document around which the Bank-
supported Education Sector Development Project was developed. Elections in 1997 resulted
in a new Government and, eventually, in the appearance of a new policy for E&T articulated
in the "White Paper - Pre-primary, Primary and Secondary Education," which appeared in
September 1997, and firther elaborated in the "Action Plan for a New Education System in
Mauritius," submitted to the Cabinet in March 1998 and given preliminary approval only
recently.
1.16 Two 1988 Parliamentary Acts constitute important E&T milestones and are linked to
the Bank-supported projects reviewed in this ICR. Act 8 of 1988 established the Industrial
and Vocational Training Board (IVTB) and its governing body, the Industrial and Vocational
Training Council (IVTC). The Industrial and Vocational Training Act Imposition of Levy
Regulations (GN 11/89, section 16) followed in early 1989 and specified the levy on wages to
be used to finance training activities, whether facilitated in the private sector by IVTB or
operated directly by IVTB in its own training centers. The principal objective of the Bank-
financed Industrial and Vocational Training Project was to support the development of the
IVTB.
1.17 The second relevant legislative action was Act 9 of 1988, which established the Tertiary
Education Commission (TEC). One of several key objectives of the Education Sector
Development Project was to assist TEC "to prepare institutional development plans for higher
education institutions," and the main objective of the Higher and Technical Education Project
was "to support the Government's education sector program for higher and polytechnic
education ... required to support a more competitive economy."
Bank Group's Role in the Sector
1.18 The World Bank has financed five E&T training projects in Mauritius. The First
Education Project (Credit 501/Loan 1033-MAS) for US$7.0 million was signed in 1974 and
completed in 1980. It sought to expand and improve the quality of secondary education and
increase access to secondary schools for urban and rural poor children. This project was
completed with a delay of about 18 months, and its objectives were only partially achieved.
While new curricula and textbooks were produced, and teachers were trained in science and
practical subjects, programs in rural education were not implemented because of a lack of
demand. Enrollments in project secondary schools did not reach projections, and problems
were encountered in equipment installation.
1.19 The Second Education Project (Loan 1543-MAS) for US $15.2 million was signed in
1978 and completed in 1995. Its objectives were to build capacity in the Ministry of
Education, improve the quality of primary teacher education, and build and equip junior
secondary schools. This project also included a component for a new Central Training Office
(CTO), the precursor of IVTB. The Project was amended in 1983 to support primary school
rehabilitation, curriculum development and construction of education facilities in the island of
Rodrigues. The CTO was established but never became operational because the Government
refocused its priorities and amended the Project to concentrate on primary education. The
other project objectives were accomplished.
5



1.20  The three projects that were closed on October 31, 1998 and that are the subject of this
ICR are the Industrial and Vocational Training Project (Loan 3401-MAS), the Education
Sector Development Project (Loan 3578-MAS) and the Higher and Technical Education
Project (Loan 3859-MAS). The Evaluation Summaries for the three projects follow below
after first looking briefly to the future and reflecting on the cross-cutting themes emerging
from these projects. The full Project Implementation Assessments and related annexes follow
in Chapters 2, 3 and 4.
Looking to the Future
1.21  Mauritius continues to move toward more sophisticated, higher value-added
agriculture, industry and services, which require a better educated workforce.  High
employment and pressure on wages emphasize the need for increasing productivity through
education. If developments within the country have been insufficient to create a sense of
urgency for accelerating progress toward a more effective education system, then external
developments promise to add new pressures. Events in East Asia are rapidly changing the
economic landscape and intensifying competition in a global economy. Mauritius is looking
at a narrow window of opportunity through the end of this decade and into the new
millennium to sustain its place as a leader in the region among the newly industrializing
economies. Providing quality education and access to training for its citizens lies at the core
of a response to this challenge.
1.22 This ICR, covering three World Bank-financed projects supporting basic and
secondary education, higher and technical education, and industrial and vocational training,
has provided an opportunity to look broadly at the sector and factors underlying the education
system's ability to meet the challenges imposed by economic development and the demand
for a workforce that can adapt to new technologies and higher value-added production. While
each project provides its own set of implementation lessons, a number of cross-cutting themes
emerge. These themes are highlighted here and include: (i) the need for a national consensus
on education policies and strategies that transcends governments and unites all stakeholders;
(ii) the importance of agreement on sector policies in advance of sector investrnents; and (iii)
the need for clearly defined implementation procedures supporting policies and strategies.
1.23  Building consensus among stakeholders, especially around complex and highly
sensitive sector policies, is paramount to creating a sense of ownership and facilitating
effective implementation of development programs. Mauritius has not lacked for education
reform proposals. What it has failed to achieve, as shown in the three cases at hand, is a
national consensus around the reforms that could survive changes in government and political
leadership. Education along with training are too important to be whipsawed by political
forces of change. In one form or another, each of the World Bank-financed projects has
suffered from changes of government and political leadership that altered the course of sector
reforms. The much needed national consensus remains elusive even in the case of the most
recent sector reforms announced in the "White Paper - Pre-primary, Primary and Secondary
Education". As a result, the achievement of a nine-year compulsory education cycle through
the establishment of middle schools - opposed by some stakeholders, is hampered and likely
delayed. Consensus about the regionalization of secondary education, the establishment of
the Mauritius University of Technology and the affordability of free higher education has also
not yet been established at this time, making it difficult to move forward. If the narrow
6



window of development opportunity is to be realized, Mauritius must build an enduring
national consensus around education reforms.
1.24 Among the remaining themes, the realization of reforms requires both sound policies
and efficient implementation mechanisms and transparent operational guidelines.
Agreements on policy reforms need to precede sector investments. The expansion of training
capacity by IVTB when debates were still underway over its role as a provider of training is
an example of this. As a matter of fact, there is still no agreed policy for vocational training.
Work on this is to restart now that the responsibility for training has been moved to another
ministry from education. As illustrated in the review below, inadequate efforts were taken in
planning the three projects to align project scope and interventions realistically with local
implementation capacity. The lack of efficient implementation mechanisms, including
transparent operational guidelines, well-defined roles, responsibilities, and areas of
accountability impeded the timely execution of the three projects and may have also acted as a
disincentive to promoting ownership of the outcomes. The absence of well-articulated
performance indicators also made it difficult to measure success and provide accountability.
1.25  If Mauritius is to remain a leader among newly industrializing countries and pursue its
vision of rapid, technology-based growth, it must seize the opportunity for education and
training reforms. The World Bank remains ready to assist the Government in realizing this
objective. Although opportunities exist for lending services, it is more likely that non-lending
services will be a priority in the future for building consensus around sector reforms.
Ongoing work on public expenditure analysis of the welfare state will examine spending
levels for education and their sustainability. Follow-up assistance might focus on analysis
and costing of reforms in the new "Action Plan" for education and strategies for sector
financing, especially in higher education. The Bank could also play a constructive role in the
debate of IVTB's involvement in training finance and delivery by bringing international
experience and economic analysis to bear on the issues.
B. INDUSTRIAL AND VOCATIONAL TRAINING PROJECT - EVALUATION SUMMARY
Background
1.26 The Industrial Vocational Training Board (IVTB) was set up in 1988 as an agency to
finance, operate and regulate/facilitate training and to advise on training policy. It was to
have income from a payroll-levy and from government and was to give grants to industry to
subsidize training expenses.
Statement of Objectives
1.27  The Project had as an overarching goal the building of institutional capacity within the
IVTB to make it the leading center of training in Mauritius.
1.28  The Project contained four supporting objectives:
Provide selected technical assistance and training for the staff of the IVTB to create and
build up the Board's institutional capacity. The technical assistance and training would
build capacity in counterpart local staff and provide short-term expertise in specialized
topics.
7



* Provide opportunities for trainers throughout Mauritius, including in the private sector,
to have access to modern training methods and materials through the establishment of a
training Resource Center. The Project would supply equipment and training materials,
and technical assistance to design the Center and prepare equipment lists and training for
key staff.
* Provide training in specific sectors and industrial skills. The Project would support
training that was cross-sectoral in nature and relevant to several industries support on a
residual basis when other sources of financing were unavailable.
* Provide technical assistance and training to establish a job classification system for the
provision of labor market information. The Project would finance development of a
system of job classifications to aid labor market analysis and monitoring of training needs.
1.29 The goal of making IVTB the leading national training center was in keeping with
Mauritius' strategy for industrial and vocational skills designed to sustain the country's
industrial dynamism into the next century. The goal also agreed with the Bank's subsequently
prepared Country Assistance Strategy.
Achievement of Project Objectives.
1.30 IVTB runs both full-time courses for school leavers and part-time in-service courses
for industry. These provisions have expanded during the project life. It has a system of tracer
studies for monitoring its own training. It has mounted an apprenticeship scheme with the
goal of replacing much of the pre-service full-time course provision.
1.31 The Project, however, has played a minor part in the development of these activities
and it has failed to reach private trainers with services other than certification. Achievement
of objectives with project finance has been modest and uneven.
* Institutional Development. The contribution of the Project to institutional development is
marginally unsatisfactory. On the positive side, the project exceeded targets set at mid-
term for use of technical assistance, but it has financed only a small amount of staff
development.
* Development of a Resource Center. The Bank did finance the targeted acquisition of
books, periodicals, and training data bases. One person has been trained by the Project but
was promoted to another part of IVTB. Equipment was procured, but was of poor quality.
The IVTB met its commitment to renovate the Resource Center. Circulation of materials
and media has been modest. The Center is not accessible to private training providers as
planned.
*  Support for cross-sector training.  The achievement in this component has been
satisfactory, even though it has suffered from the divergence of views within the Council
and Government on whether IVTB should continue its expansionary role in the delivery of
training services.  A target established at the mid-term  review for financing the
development of 11 training courses has been met. Further, when IVTB's and the
Government's own contributions are included (see Table 2-7), the expenditures on such
courses exceed the estimates made at Appraisal.
8



* Development of Job Classifications. This component was effectively shelved at the mid-
term review. The financing for the component was reallocated to setting up a management
information system for IVTB and computerizing its office. Achievement of this modified
objective is largely satisfactory.
Implementation Record and Major Factors Affecting the Project
1.32  Throughout the Project, disbursements have been unsatisfactory-- in spite of the
cancellation at mid term, March 1995, of US$1.0 million, and subsequent adoption of new
and purportedly realistic implementation and procurement plans.  Implementation has
persistently lagged behind plans and revised plans. A series of circumstances contributed to
unsatisfactory implementation: (i) deficiencies in Project design and planning; (ii) access to
alternative and cheaper finance: (iii) uncertainty about IVTB's future functions; and (iv)
weakened Bank commitment to the Project components.
1.33  In its design, the Project failed to identify clear targets for activities to be financed.
This deficiency was remedied at mid-term, but the targets set were a mixture of program and
Project targets, and once jointly developed, were largely ignored by the Bank in subsequent
supervision. The design sought to integrate Project management into the ongoing activities of
IVTB, but later in 1994, both sides agreed a Project Coordinator was needed to facilitate
implementation. The design also exaggerated the importance of the development of a jobs
classification system as a contributor to training decisions, and the component was dropped at
mid-term.
1.34 IVTB has been the beneficiary of a strong economy and able to finance many of its
development activities without resorting to the Loan. Uncertainty of Government policy has
also played a role in slow implementation. Since early 1996, IVTB has had three different
Ministers in as many ministries. The frequent changes have produced uncertainty about
IVTB's future.
1.35 The Bank's own changing view of IVTB's role as a provider of training affected
implementation. It supported studies and a workshop on the question of IVTB's future role,
and took a position favoring IVTB as a promoter of training, rather than a provider. When the
Bank withheld financing for expansion of two training course on procedural grounds in
October 1997, IVTB elected to finance development of these courses from its own resources.
Project sustainability
1.36  Overall sustainability is likely. The institutional capacity developed by the Project is
likely to be used in the future even if staff are later reorganized to a separate training agency.
The cross-sectoral courses are fully developed and in use. The Resource Center is more
problematic. It will require upgrading of equipment to be effective in the modem
communications age. The Director is the only fully trained librarian on staff.
1.37  The future of IVTB and the sustainability of the Project overall are threatened, at least
temporarily, by the reduction of Government financing. In 1998, the Government budget for
IVTB was reduced to a token 10 Rupees, as a signal that it should reduce its accumulated
reserves from the training levy. It has done so, but is now approaching a critical stage
9



requiring restoration of Government financing. The Ministry of Finance has responded
affirmatively.
Bank performance
1.38  Bank performance in Project identification was satisfactory. The Project was
consistent with country policy and with Bank policy. Questions might have been raised about
the job classifications component in view of its narrow contribution to labor market
infornation, but the other components were relevant to overall goal of IVTB become a
leading center for training in Mauritius.
1.39 Performance during preparation and appraisal, however, was marginally unsatisfactory
with the failure to include clear appraisal criteria for financing cross-sectoral training in the
SAR (p. 16). In supervision, the Project was retrofitted for performance indicators at the mid-
term review, but the indicators focused on inputs and outcomes with little attention to impact.
More important, the indicators were largely ignored by subsequent supervision missions.
1.40 Despite continuous delays in disbursements and disagreements on policy issues,
supervision reports rated the Project as satisfactory until March 1998, seven months prior to
its closing. The signals were there much earlier in the slow disbursement, loan cancellation at
mid-term, policy disagreements, and failure of IVTB to adhere to Bank procurement
procedures. The lack of realism in ratings warrant an unsatisfactory overall rating on
supervision.
Borrower Performance
1.41  Borrower performance was satisfactory in Project preparation, and marginally
unsatisfactory in implementation and operation of the Project. As noted, there were
discontinuities in political leadership within the same government. This created uncertainty
about the agency's future once the issue of reform had been put on the policy agenda without
being resolved since 1996. Resolution of these differences is essential to further development
of the sector.
1.42  The Borrower also shares responsibility with the Bank for exaggerated optimism about
implementation. At the mid-term review IVTB pursued initially a smaller cancellation of the
Loan (US$0.4 million) than subsequently agreed. It also raised upward its targets for staff
development.
Assessment of Outcome
1.43  Despite some useful accomplishments, the Project is rated overall as unsatisfactory.
Future Operations
1.44  Future operations should be developed only after a consensus has emerged around the
future of IVTB. The World Bank could play a constructive role in helping build this
consensus through economic and sector analysis of training, public and private, and access to
international experience with issues of training finance and delivefy.
10



Key Lessons Learned
1.45 . The following are some of the lessons learned:
(a) Agents and procedures for project coordination can accelerate procurement in slow
disbursing projects.
(b) Building consensus around difficult sector policies requires transparency of approach and
the building of confidence among all stakeholders.
(c) Clearly defined procedures are essential for appraising sub-projects during
implementation.
(d) Time-bound, monitorable performance indicators are important to guiding
implementation and measuring achievement of objectives.
C. EDUCATION SECTOR DEVELOPMENT PROJECT - EVALUATION SUMMARY
Introduction
1.46  The Education Sector Development Project (ESDP) was prepared to assist the
Government with the implementation of its education development program for the period
1993-1998, as defined in its Education Sector Master Plan (ESMP). To support the policy
objectives of the ESMP, the Project financed a portion of the Government's five year (1993-
1998) Core Investment Program (CIP), with a loan of US$ 20.0 million. The Project became
effective on June 22, 1993 and closed on October 31, 1998, two months earlier than originally
scheduled. In light of weak Project implementation and the persistent low level of
disbursements, US$ 6.0 million was cancelled on December 31, 1997, at Government's
request. The total amount disbursed for the CIP was US$ 77.41 million of which US$ 12.28
million was from the loan. The remaining balance of the loan (in US dollars equivalent),
totaling US$ 1.712,744.92 million has been cancelled.
Statement of Project Objectives
1.47  The Project's objectives were to:  (a) increase access to and promote equity of
education; (b) improve the quality of education at all levels; and (c) strengthen education
sector management. To assist in meeting these objectives, the Loan supported policy
measures to: extend basic education from six to nine years; upgrade under-qualified teachers
at the primary and secondary level; reform student assessment, examination and testing
procedures; decentralize educational administration and supervision to the regional levels;
rationalize the development of the tertiary education; and develop the capacity of the Ministry
of Education and Scientific Research (MOESR), formerly the Ministry of Education, Science
and Technology. These objectives are relevant for the country's overall development strategy
and are consistent with the human resources development strategy articulated in the Bank's
country assistance.
11



Achievement of Project Objectives
1.48  The achievement of Project objectives is uneven. Project objectives to increase access
to education were largely met. Project objectives to improve the quality of education and to
strengthen the institutional capacity of the sector were only partially met
Increase Access to and Promote Equity of Education
1.49  The achievement in this component has been marginally satisfactory. During the
implementation phase, the scope of Project support in regard to increasing access to primary
and secondary education was modified to accommodate the needs in these sectors. In lieu of
rehabilitating and constructing primary schools, the Project constructed and equipped 45 pre-
primary schools of which 43 are now operational. The Project also constructed and equipped
seven (and not five as planned), state secondary schools (SSS), providing for 7,000 student
places, which are now all in use. In all, 29 primary schools were constructed, extended and/or
rehabilitated, and 12 SSS have been extended/renovated using government resources. In the
absence of a viable and affordable financing mechanism, PSS school improvements were
financed by the Government through concessional loans made available through the
Development Bank of Mauritius. Sixty schools have borrowed a total of Rs 77,743,400 under
this loan scheme.
Improving the Quality of Education
1.50  The contribution of the Project to improving the quality of education was not fully
satisfactory. MES staff benefited from training to strengthen their professional roles. The
school leaving examination at the end of Standard 6 (the Certificate of Primary Education or
CPE) was redesigned and relevant personnel have been trained in its use. The scheme and
materials for introducing Continuous Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE) have been developed
and piloted. CCE was introduced at Standards 4, 5, and 6 in primary, rather than beginning
with Form I in secondary as originally envisaged.
1.51  School certification at the "O" level was enhanced for 15 subjects. Question banks
and a data processing system were established and are in use. Examination and test-related
equipment and computers have been procured. A national diagnostic assessment for Standard
3 was prepared, but it has not yet been implemented. A national orientation assessment for
Form III was not prepared due to policy changes in 1995. The structure of certificate and
diploma courses has been revised and simplified. A Distance Education Unit has been set up
at MIE. Distance education for A-level in five subject areas has been launched. School
administration training has also been given. The skills of Grade "C" teachers were upgraded.
Strengthening Sector Management and Institutional Development
1.52 Achievement of this goal is marginally unsatisfactory. Improvements of facilities
supported by the Project at the University of Mauritius (UoM) have been achieved. Despite
delays, equipment has been installed and is in use. Interventions to strengthen the capacity of
the Technical Education Commission (TEC) were financed from government resources.
Plans for establishing a National Schools Inspectorate (NIS) have been prepared, but the NIS
is not yet operational. Similarly, the regional education centers (RECs) have not been
upgraded as envisaged under the Project. The overall plan to make the EMIS operational was
12



completed in 1997 but has not been fully implemented. Several staff benefited from the IIEP
Program. Planning and coordination in MOESR at the central and local levels was weak.
Although project design highlighted the importance of capacity building within the education
sector, achievements were not satisfactory. There was virtually no research studies carried
out to improve sector program development, including improvements in the management of
private sector education.
Implementation Record and Major Factors Affecting the Project
1.53  Project implementation has been generally unsatisfactory.  Although the Project
increased access to education, it has only partially implemented key activities to support
quality improvements in education. The Project fell short in implementing interventions to
strengthen the overall capacity of the education sector. Several factors that affected the
Project were within Government's control. These factors include: (a) weak project design;
(b) frequent changes in personnel; (c) insufficient and inadequately trained staff, including the
lack of efficient operational mechanisms, resulting in poor project management and
coordination; (d) shifts in government and new policy changes; and (f) lack of support for
private secondary schools.
Project Sustainability
1.54  Project sustainability is uncertain. The new policy reforms defined in the Action Plan
build on the broad development objectives of the Master Plan. However, they differ in terms
of details, timing, and particular policy measures and programs to be implemented. While
most ongoing project interventions will be completed using local resources, other
interventions planned under the project may require some modification and/or strengthening
to reflect new policy changes and to better respond to the needs of the education sector. This
may lead to greater demands for resources in the education sector.
1.55 The implementation of the AP is currently underway. MOESR, however, has not yet
fully elaborated concrete measures to support the new policy reforms.   The costs of
implementing the AP reforms, including an operational plan, have also not been prepared. It
is therefore uncertain how and in what context interventions supported under the project will
be sustained. Despite the low level of loan disbursements, it must be recognized, however
that Govemment's overall contribution to the implemented of the CIP has been substantial.
Bank Performance
1.56  Bank performance was satisfactory during project identification and preparation, but
was less satisfactory during appraisal. Given the weak capacity in the sector, the appraisal
mission should have examined more closely the institutional framework, administrative
structure, and operating mechanisms that would be required to enable the effective
coordination between program activities and their timely implementation by various entities.
1.57 Bank performance during supervision was unsatisfactory. Reports on most Bank
supervision missions did not sufficiently and, in some cases, accurately flag important
elements surrounding problems identified. Supervision missions generally concentrated on
physical inputs and did not adequately and systematically monitor progress achieved with
regard to quality improvements in education and to strengthening institutional capacity in the
13



education sector. There were important recurring problems that were not addressed in a
timely and, in some cases, transparent manner and certainly not well in advance of project
closing when appropriate action can be taken.
Borrower Performance
1.58  Borrower performance was uneven throughout the project cycle.  Borrower's
performance during the preparation phase was satisfactory in identifying key policy reforms
required to improve learning achievements. Its performance, however, was less satisfactory
in  articulating the steps necessary for successful implementation.   Administrative
arrangements and appropriate operational mechanisms required to effectively jump start
project implementation were not established prior to implementation. Shifts in government
and frequent changes in personnel, combined with poor operational guidelines affected the
efficiency with which project activities could be implemented. The Borrower was slow to
address implementation problems.
Assessment of Outcomes
1.59  Project outcome was marginally unsatisfactory. Although the Project achieved most
of its objectives to increase access to education, it has only partially implemented key
activities to support quality improvements in education; work completed in this area, while
important, have just begun to address the issues and bring about changes by which education
in Mauritius can be strengthened and be made more responsive to meeting the overall social
and economic goals of the country. The Project fell short in implementing interventions to
strengthen the overall capacity of the education sector which is paramount to the challenges of
implementing new policy reforms and for sustaining key operations in the sector.
Future Operations
1.60  Changes in government did not necessarily alter key development objectives in the
education sector. With the introduction of the AP, efforts are being taken to facilitate a
broader dialogue with stakeholders around the new policy reforms and how they will be
implemented. In parallel, efforts are being made to complete ongoing Project interventions
using local resources. In some cases, ESMP measures supported under the Project are being
modified and/or strengthened to ensure greater equity in education.
Key Lessons Learned
1.61  Key lessons from the Education Sector Development Project are:
(a) Building consensus around difficult and sensitive sector reform policies requires
continuous dialogue with and the building of support from all stakeholders.
(b) Projects will fail if little attention is paid to implementation.
(c) Frequent changes in personnel, including roles and responsibilities, impede higher levels
of achievements of Project development objectives.
14



(d) Improving learning achievements requires a holistic and integrated approach, which goes
beyond the measurement of quantitative inputs.
D. HIGHER AND TECHNCAL EDUCATION PROJECT - EVALUATION SUMMARY
Introduction
1.62 The Higher and Technical Education Project was supported by a Loan in the amount
of US$ 16.0 million. The Government was to finance the remaining US$10.5 million of the
total estimated cost of US$26.5 million. US$12.0 million of the Loan was cancelled in
December 1997. The Loan closed on Februaiy 28, 1999 at the request of the Borrower, more
than two years early. The total disbursements was US$ 3.1 million. The remaining balance
(in US dollars equivalent) of US$ 908,571.05 million has been cancelled.
1.63 Project Objectives:
. Strengthening the University of Mauritius (US$19.4 million, baseline costs), by:
(1) upgrading staff and facilities to improve education quality; (2) increasing the number
of quality graduates; (3) improving links with employers to increase curriculum relevance
and graduate marketability; (4) developing viable postgraduate education and research
programs in strategic areas; and (5) enhancing the efficiency of UoM operations.
* Rationalizing polytechnic education (US$5.0 million baseline costs), by: (1) improving
the quality of faculty, curricula and facilities; (2) supporting the development of business
programs and strengthening links with UoM and the private sector; (3) strengthening the
policy and analytic capacity of the Management Trust Fund (MTF).
Achievement of Project Objectives
1.64 Whereas the Project achieved nearly all activities in support of the objective to
rationalize polytechnic education, it fell far short in its objective to strengthen UoM. Overall,
therefore, the Project is rated as unsatisfactory.
Strengthening the University of Mauritius
1.65  Most of the targets for this component were not achieved, and the overall outcome
must be judged unsatisfactory. (1) Upgrading staff andfacilities. Sixty-seven academic staff
commenced training, mostly at UK Universities, against a target of 158 staff. Expanding the
library and engineering faculty facilities to house more books and accommodate a greater
number of students was not accomplished. These works are only in the design phase.
However, several facilities have been upgraded and equipment procured. (2) Increasing
quality graduates. The planned enrollment increase has been exceeded. Enrollments are now
3,160 against the 1998-99 target of 2,700. This will increase the number of graduates, but it is
too early to assess their quality. (3) Improving links with employers. The faculty-industry
councils have been reactivated and provide regular inputs into curricula development.
Demand for engineering and science graduates remains strong. For graduates of other
faculties, it is uneven. (4) Developing post graduate education and research. Degree-level
students have increased from 50 percent of UoM enrollments at appraisal to 88 percent today.
Only four research projects were submitted to the research fund by graduate students or
15



faculty, and since the Government would not agree to funding consumables from the Loan,
even these have been blocked up to now. Research activity remains small and without
strategic direction. (5) Enhancing efficiency of operations. A course credit system has been
introduced ahead of schedule, and greater use is being made of distance education delivery on
campus.
Rationalizing Polytechnic Education
1.66  The objectives of this component have been achieved.  It is rated satisfactory.
(1) Improving quality offaculty, curricula andfacilities. Nearly all staff of the Droopnath
Ramphul Polytechnic have been trained. Curricula were designed with the help of specialists
and led to quality 4ttainment as shown on the externally scored exams. The existing facilities
of the Droopnath Ramphul State Secondary School were rehabilitated on time, and the
extension to accommodate the rest of the polytechnic was build on an adjacent site with only
a short delay. (2) Supporting business programs and strengthening links with UoM and the
private sector. Programs in accounting, marketing, banking and information systems at
Droopnath Ramphul enroll now about 300 students who enter the polytechnic after their A-
levels. Of the first batch of 70 graduates, some 65 percent were employed after less than six
months according to the results of a mail tracer survey conducted by the polytechnic. Links
with UoM are effective and have facilitated the transfer of the first four diploma courses from
the University to the polytechnics. The industry-faculty advisory committees meet regularly.
(3) Strengthening the policy and analytic capacity of TffF. The MTF meets regularly and
manages the system well, but its capacity for policy and labor market analysis was not
developed under the Project.
Implementation Record and Major Factors Affecting the Project
1.67  Implementation of some project activities was unsatisfactory for several reasons. At
UoM, the quality assurances, accreditation, course credit system and incentives for research
were new and unfamiliar to staff who did not have the technical expertise at the start of the
Project to introduce these efficiently into their programs. UoM was also not familiar with
Bank procurement procedures and considered them complex, inflexible and the principal
cause for delays. Also, some targets (e.g., number of academic staff to be trained) were
unrealistic, which meant that staff never felt that they could be achieved during the life of the
Project. Moreover, enrollments at UoM increased more rapidly than planned, and the
implementation of project activities became a heavy burden, on top of the increased teaching
loads. At MTF, the project activities had been prepared with the help of technical assistance
and scaled in line with technical and implementation capacity. Implementation of this
component was efficient.
Project Sustainability
1.68 The achievements of the UoM component could be maintained, but recent increases in
enrollments will increase university operating costs, and current reluctance to plan and
implement measures for cost recovery put the achievements at risk. Also, policies concerning
private provision could expose UoM to greater competition in the future. UoM is currently
preparing its strategic plan. Sustainability will depend on how it will address these policy
issues, and on the development of consensus among stakeholders in support of the plan. The
polytechnic component can be sustained if market demand remains strong. Although MTF
16



does not have in-house labor market analytic capacity, it uses tracer studies and stays in touch
with its constituency through the industry-faculty advisory committees.
Bank Performance
1.69 Bank performance at identification was partially satisfactory. The Project was
consistent with the Government's policies of producing quality higher- and middle-level
human resources and strategic new knowledge. Bank performance during preparation and
appraisal was unsatisfactory. Interventions were new and complex and not sufficiently
explained. The Bank did not include technical assistance to help UoM to develop technical
and implementation expertise, nor did it assure that the Project was fully owned by UoM and
supported by TEC and the Government. Targets agreed at appraisal were unrealistic,
particularly for staff development and research. Although implementation and disbursement
were slow from the start, Bank supervision missions were reluctant to adjust targets. Also,
the Bank became preoccupied with procurement issues and gave inadequate attention to
policy and technical issues. Bank performance vis-a-vis the polytechnic component was
satisfactory at all points in the project cycle.
Borrower Performance
1.70 Borrower performance was satisfactory during preparation but marginally
unsatisfactory during and following appraisal. At negotiations, Government lowered the
targets for integration of the four tertiary institutions and for cost-recovery, and the plans it
agreed to submit in these areas were never developed. TEC was not given a clear supporting
role in the Project. During preparation and implementation, there have been three different
Education Ministers. UoM may have been given mixed signals, although there was not any
clear and explicit change in the Government's policy for higher education during this period.
UoM was not successful in getting staff to commit fully to project implementation,
particularly when the time and efforts to implement project activities competed with their
increasing teaching loads.
Assessment of Outcome
1.71 The impact of the Project on UoM is marginal, and although the impact on the
polytechnics is substantial, overall, the project is rated unsatisfactory. At UoM, only 25 staff
obtained graduate degrees. The objective to raise the percentage of Ph.D. holders from 25
percent in 1994 to 55 percent by 2006, which would create a critical mass, will take much
longer. Basic quality assurance mechanisms were introduced, but procedures to establish
accreditation, which would make it possible for UoM students to continue their studies at
other institutions, were not developed. An important outcome of the Project was the shift in
enrollments from diploma to degree programs. This will increase the output of graduates for
the economy, but the quality of these is yet to be assessed. At the polytechnics, on the other
hand, the production of middle-level technicians in business areas and information systems is
likely to have a beneficial impact on the economy. The diplomas are recognized overseas,
suggesting that the technicians produced meet international quality standards. MTF did not
develop its market analytic capacity but is managing the polytechnic system efficiently and in
response to market demands.

Future Operations
1.72 . UoM is preparing a medium-term strategic plan, as are the other T-EIs and TEC. The
plan is likely to propose a continuation of the quality improvements, enrollment increases, and
facilities expansion supported under the Project. The plan will be reviewed by Government,
which should lead to greater consensus on UoM's role in producing trained workers and new
knowledge relevant to Mauritius' economy in the future. It is hoped that TEC's overarching
strategy will set out a clear policy framework for the tertiary education sub-sector and will
address issues of integration, equity of access, cost recovery, diversification, alternative
modes of provision (including private education). TEC's strategy should also look at
conditions of service for teaching staff. Lecturers at the University are given little incentive
currently to introduce innovations in teaching or to engage in research. The Bank could
examine the affordability of the University's strategic plan, in the context of the overall
analysis of the welfare state. It could also work with TEC on its overall strategy. MTF plans
to complete remaining Project activities, add a program in business information in response to
market demand and, in general, adjust program offerings in relation to market demand.
Key Lessons Learned
1.73  The following are some of the lessons learned from implementing the Project:
(a) To keep implementation on schedule, it is essential that ownership of the Project be
established at national and institutional levels.
(b) The Government and the Bank need to insist on sound sectoral and sub-sectoral policies
before investing in complex improvement and expansion projects such as this one.
(c) The Bank must pay close attention to the political economy of the country and take
account of the lessons from history in determining what kind of policy changes are
feasible and what role stakeholders and beneficiaries should play in this process.
(d) Project objectives need to be scaled to take account of the local implementation capacity,
and project complexity should only be increased once the technical and implementation
capacity has increased.
(e) Procurement procedures need to be harmonized with those of the Central Tender Board
(CTB). This is a national, not a sectoral, issue.
(f) The statutes of the governing councils and boards for tertiary education need to be
updated to reflect the need for greater academic and financial autonomy of the
institutions in order for them to implement effective projects such as this one.

2. INDUSTRIAL AND VOCATIONAL TRAINING PROJECT
A. INTRODUCTION
2.1   The Industrial and Vocational Training Project (IVTP) was the third Project financed
in the education sector by the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development
(IBRD), and the first to focus on vocational training. The First Education Project was
approved in 1974 with a blend of IDA (International Development Association) and IBRD
financing of US$7.0 million, and the second in 1978 with IBRD financing of US$15.2
million. The two projects helped expand and improve the quality of secondary school
facilities, increase access of urban and rural poor to secondary schooling, improve equity and
quality in primary education, and strengthen the capacity of the Ministry of Education for
planning and administration.
2.2 During implementation of the IVTP, two additional loans for education were approved
by the Bank. The Education Sector Development Project was approved in 1993 for US$20
million to assist the Government to implement its education development program for the
period 1993-1998, as described in its Education Sector Master Plan. In 1995, the Higher and
Technical Education Project was approved for US$16 million with the goal of providing high
quality university graduates, technicians and non-degree specialists in support of the
Government strategy of expanding technology-based industrial and service employment.
2.3 The IVTP became effective March 5, 1992, with financing of US$5.4 million. The
Government/IVTB was to finance the remaining US$2.3 million of the total estimated cost of
US$7.7 million. At Government's request, it was closed October 31, 1998, two months early.
In view of the slow progress achieved with Project implementation and disbursements,
US$1.0 million was canceled following the mid-term review in March 1995. Total Loan
disbursements, exclusive of civil works, is estimated at US$3.28 million. The estimated Loan
balance (in US dollar equivalent) of US$ 1,119,288.89 has been cancelled.
B. STATEMENT/EVALUATION OF OBJECTIVES
2.4 The Project had as an overarching goal the building of institutional capacity within the
Industrial Vocational Training Board (IVTB) to make it the leading center of training in
Mauritius. The IVTB was established under the Industrial and Vocational Training Act of
1988, Act No. 8 dated April 15, 1988. The IVTB was set up to advise the Prime Minister on
matters related to training; monitor the needs for training; administer, control and operate
training schemes; and provide for, promote, assist in and regulate the training or
apprenticeship of persons who are, or will be, employed in the commercial, technical or
vocational fields.
19



2.5  The Project contained four supporting objectives:
(i) Provide selected technical assistance and training for the staff of the IVTB to create and
build up the Board's institutional capacity (US$ 710,000, base cost, of which IBRD would
finance US$ 666,000). The technical assistance and training would build capacity in
counterpart local staff, provide short-term expertise in specialized topics, including
curriculum design, training levy management, and monitoring and evaluation techniques; and
provide specialists for carrying out initial training needs analyses, curriculum design and
course preparation, including equipment lists for cross-sector training programs.
(ii) Provide opportunities for trainers throughout Mauritius, including in the private sector, to
have access to modem training methods and materials through the establishment of a training
Resource Center (US$1.46 million, of which IBRD would finance US$750,000). The Project
would supply furniture and fittings, books, periodicals, journals, tapes and training films. In
addition, it would finance subscriptions to training data bases; equipment to create,
manufacture and produce training aids, audio-visual aids and other training materials; and
technical assistance to design the Center and prepare equipment lists and training for key
staff.
(iii) Provide training in specific sectors and industrial skills (US$4.03 million, of which
EBRD would finance US$2.85 million). The Project would support training that was cross-
sectoral in nature and relevant to several industries, e.g. supervisors, quality assurance,
maintenance. It would finance equipment, materials, instructional services, and operational
support on a residual basis when other sources of financing were unavailable. Agreement on
which training would be financed followed established IVTB procedures outlined in the Staff
Appraisal Report.
(iv) Provide technical assistance and training to establish a job classification system for the
provision of labor market information (US$350,000, all IBRD-financed). The Project would
finance development of a system of job classifications to aid labor market analysis and
monitoring of training needs.
2.6 The goal of making IVTB the leading national training center was clear enough and
realistic at Project inception. The country had developed a broad strategy for industrial and
vocational training that was shared with stakeholders at a Conference in March 1989. The
Project was consistent with the strategy and efforts to develop a training partnership for the
Government with the manufacturing and service sectors. The Project followed a decade of
successful growth built on labor-intensive development and low-cost labor. It recognized that
future development would need to come from improving productivity in existing industries
through the introduction of advanced technology and skilled labor and fostering the growth of
new and more capital-intensive industries.
2.7  The Project was also consistent with the Bank's overall assistance strategy in Mauritius,
which supports human development, including the development of a skilled labor force
capable of meeting the needs of a growing and diversifying economy and for sustaining
growth in an increasingly competitive international environment. However, the Project fell
victim to an emerging debate within Government and the private sector over the roles of
IVTB as a provider of training versus a promoter of training through policy development,
20



regulation, and provision of market information. Other factors joined to slow implementation.
The Project is rated overall as unsatisfactory.
C. ACHIEVEMENT OF PROJECT OBJECTIVES
2.8 Achievement of project objectives has been modest and uneven. IVTB is the leading
center for training and the scope of its activities expanded during the life of the project, but
the Project has played a marginal role in this outcome. A robust economy and training levy
revenues have enabled the IVTB to finance most of its expansion and development without
recourse to the Loan. Sector dialogue on making IVTB a promoter of training and out-
placing its provider role has been unsatisfactory.
Institutional Development
2.9 The contribution of the Project to institutional development is rated unsatisfactory. The
Bank expected to finance 40 staff months of technical assistance and overseas training for up
to five senior staff for 60 months. It also planned five short-term study tours for other senior
staff. At the mid-term review in March 1995, Government proposed an expansion of these
targets even while Project disbursements at this point were only 13 percent of the Loan,
whereas projections at appraisal had been 61 percent. The Bank challenged the increase, but
subsequently accepted the proposal and revised the budget accordingly.
2.10 Institutional development did take place, with 150 person-months of technical
assistance financed under the Project. This covered development of a trades testing system,
curriculum materials and training of trainers for IVTB's courses. It did not include the
outreach function for private providers and training in industry. IVTB has financed ad hoc
staff development from the scholarships offered by bilateral agencies and from its own
resources. Very little staff development occurred with project financing. Such few study
tours which were made, were reportedly confined to IVTB's director and Council members
and were not financed out of Project funds.
2.11 In early 1996 the Project became entangled in an internal dispute within the IVTB
Council and between IVTB and other parts of Government, over the future role of IVTB as a
provider of training. The Council was newly appointed after the change of Government in
1995. The Bank was asked and agreed to finance a management audit in 1996 to make
recommendations on the roles of IVTB as a promoter and provider of training, and later in
December of that year to organize a workshop on international experience with training
reforms.
2.12 The new Council turned down in this period what it described as an overly ambitious
staff development plan and required IVTB to produce a corporate plan as a guide to staff
development.  Several versions of the corporate plan were subsequently produced, but
rejected by the Council. No agreement on a staff development plan or its implementation has
yet emerged for Bank financing.
2.13 Without significant Bank involvement, institutional development proceeded. A survey
carried out in October 1998 by the Mauritius Employers Federation showed that most
employers are satisfied with the training policies of the Government. The main elements in
that policy have been the IVTB and the levy-grant system. The proportion of the training levy
21



used to reimburse up to 75 percent of approved enterprise training expenses has risen from
about 20 percent in the early 1990s to 37 percent (estimated by IVTB) in 1998. The volume
of such refunds has risen from Rupees 12.9 million to Rupees 35.5 million.
2.14 IVTB runs examinations, certifies private providers, and advertises the offerings of
these providers in its own annual catalogue of courses offered. It monitors its own training
with the use of tracer studies tracking trainees and their employers to provide feedback to
curriculum designers. IVTB is monitoring unit costs of training. It does not, however,
monitor performance of private providers or enterprises using training levy proceeds.
Approximately 20 percent of its instructors are now employed under fixed-term contracts to
enhance the organization's flexibility.
2.15 IVTB enrollment in full time courses increased from 471 trainees in 26 courses in 1992
to 1,569 trainees in 1998. The number of full-time courses expanded from 24 in 1992 to 38 in
1998. More than 120 part-time courses, 80 percent of which are targeted as in-service courses
for industry, have been developed. The enrollment in these short courses, of varying duration,
exceeds 2,400, up from 601 in 1992. IVTB has mounted in the last three years an
apprenticeship scheme, so far with 350 apprentices--with the long term aim of expanding
these to replace much of the pre-service full-time course provisions. During the Project
period the IVTB has also phased out pre-vocational courses.
Development of a Resource Center
2.16 Achievement of this goal is unsatisfactory. The Bank did finance the acquisition of
books, periodicals, and training data bases. The targets set at the mid-term review were
achieved and staff were trained. Technical assistance to prepare equipment lists was financed.
The IVTB met its commitment to renovate the Resource Center adding additional space above
the first floor of the building in Ebene. The Center does provide out-reach services to IVTB
centers, though its services are used mainly by IVTB staff and trainees located nearby the
Center. Services are not yet accessible to private training providers.
2.17 Circulation of training materials and media is modest and decreased when IVTB moved
its headquarters to the new Phoenix location. Space for the Resource Center in Phoenix was
dropped from the plan by the Council as overly ambitious. Slightly over 100 books and 100
periodicals were lent per month in 1997, with the number dropping to 43 and 17 respectively
in 1998 after the headquarters move. Project investment in staff development was lost in one
case when a person trained by the Project was promoted to a job for which the acquired skills
were not directly relevant. Another trained person was moved to a Reading Room established
at IVTB headquarters. Equipment lists prepared with Bank-funded technical assistance
produced poor quality equipment for making and reproducing training films.
Support for cross-sector training
2.18 The achievement in this component has been satisfactory, even though it has suffered
from the divergence of views within the Council and Government on whether the training
agency should continue its expansionary role in the delivery of training services. The number
of courses to be developed was left flexible at appraisal and at the mid-term review a target of
11 courses was adopted. This target has been reached if one counts as separate courses those
at two different levels in the same field (see Table 5A for details). In fact, if one goes by total

project expenditures and includes civil works - which were entirely financed by IVTB and the

2.19 In October 1997, and again in November 1997, the Bank declined to issue non-

Development of Job Classifications
2.20 This component was effectively shelved at the mid-term review following the

for the component was reallocated at mid-term to setting up a management information
system for IVTB and computerizing its office. Achievement of this modified objective was
largely satisfactory. Labor market information on trainee placements and employer
assessments of training quality and emerging skill needs is currently provided by the use of
tracer studies.
D. LIPLEMENTATION RECORD AND MAJOR FACTORS AFFECTING THE PROJECT
2.21 Throughout the Project, disbursements have been unsatisfactory-- in spite of the
cancellation at mid term of US$1.0 million, and subsequent adoption of new and purportedly
"realistic" implementation and procurement plans. Implementation has persistently lagged
behind plans and revised plans. A series of circumstances contributed to unsatisfactory
implementation: (i) deficiencies in Project design and planning, (ii) access to alternative and
cheaper finance, (iii) uncertainty about IVTB's future functions; and (iv) weakened Bank
commitment to Project components.
Deficiencies in Project Design and Planning
2.22 In the Institutional Development and Cross-Sectoral Training   components,
implementation was slowed by lack of clear targets for activities to be financed by the Project.
At the time of appraisal, performance indicators were not yet required by Bank policy.
Revisions in Project design after mid-term, in March 1995, failed to remedy this problem. The
retrofitted indicators mixed Project performance and the general performance of IVTB as a
program and set unrealistic targets for staff development and technical assistance.
Performance indicators focused on output and not impact.
2.23 At appraisal, the Bank argued that it was desirable not to have separate Project
management on the grounds that it would help integrate the Project into the ongoing activities
of the IVTB. Later IVTB and the Bank agreed that this slowed implementation. A
23



coordinator for Project implementation was appointed in 1994. Subsequent Project
supervision reported improved implementation.
2.24 Project design exaggerated the importance of the development of a jobs classification
system as a contribution to training decisions. At mid-term both parties agreed that IVTB
already had a serviceable way of classifying occupations for training purposes, without
contributions from this Project component.
Access to Alternative and Cheaper Financing
2.25 IVTB could grow without using the IBRD loan, because with sustained national GDP
growth rates exceeding 5 percent, it turned out to have ample financing. The need for Bank
financing was reduced. Until recent years, the IVTB was building up reserves from unspent
revenues from the levy-grant system. It could invest from its own resources and from grant
money from donors like French Cooperation and the European Union. These sources were
cheaper than the IBRD loan, and in the case of the IVTB's own resources, also more
accessible and without restrictions. This picture changed, however, as the Ministry of Finance
in 1998 held back government funding and forced IVTB to use its levy reserves, which are
now rapidly diminishing.
Uncertainties About IVTB's Future Functions
2.26 After a change of government in early 1996, the new Minister of Human Resources
Development and Employment responsible for IVTB put organizational reform of the
functions performed by IVTB (delivery of training, financing, regulating) on the policy
agenda. He appointed a new Council and a new Chairman, who appointed a new Director of
IVTB. In 1997, within the same government, IVTB was reassigned to the Ministry of
Education. Then in November 1998, the parent ministry was changed yet again to the
Ministry of Environment, Human Resource Development, and Employment. Since early
1996, IVTB has had three different Ministers. The frequent changes slowed implementation
of the Project and produced uncertainty about its future.
Weakened Bank Commitment to Project Components
2.27 The Bank began to signal in its communications with IVTB in 1995 its strong interest in
the reorganization of IVTB and restricting its role to training policy development, finance,
regulation, and provision of market information for guiding training decisions. The Bank
proposed that training delivery be devolved to a separate organization at arms length from the
regulation and financing of training to promote competition. Support for this could be found
among some members of the Council and within Government. The Bank was requested in
1996 to fund a management audit, which later supported this recommendation. This was
followed by a December 1996 workshop that examined training reforms internationally with
similar conclusions.
2.28 Meanwhile, IVTB was attempting to privatize some of its training, but concluded that
there was little private interest in absorbing training functions. Some members of the IVTB
Council, even some from the private sector, agreed that there was a role for IVTB in the
delivery of selected skills training, and they continued to give support for this. The Bank,
however, continued to push the reorganization agenda in communications, and when it
24



refused in October 1997, on procedural grounds, to issue a non-objection for Project support
of two training programs which the Council and its chairman had supported, IVTB withdrew
further requests for Bank financing and disbursement under this component ceased.
E. PROJECT SUSTAINABILIY
2.29 Overall sustainability is likely. The institutional capacity developed by the Project is
likely to be utilized in the future, even if staff are later reorganized to a separate training
agency. The cross-sectoral training programs developed are fully utilized and are likely to be
sustained. The Resource Center is more problematic. Its current location is not optimum for
playing an outreach role, and it will require upgrading of equipment to be effective in the
modem  communications age.  The Director is the only trained librarian on staff.
Development of the management information system and computerization of headquarters,
which replaced the job classifications component, are sustainable.
2.30 However, the future of IVTB and the sustainability of the Project overall are threatened,
at least temporarily, by the reduction of Govemment financing. In the SAR, Government was
expected to provide 85 percent of capital spending by IVTB, the balance to come from
training levy resources. Government and IVTB, using the training levy, were expected to
split recurrent spending needs evenly. In 1998, the Government budget for IVTB was
reduced to a token 10 Rupees, as a signal that it should reduce its accumulated reserves from
the training levy. IVTB has done so, but is now approaching a critical stage requiring
restoration of Govemment financing. The Bank's own study of the training levy anticipated
this, and the Council chairman was earlier aware of this likely development. The Ministry of
Finance has responded affirmatively to this need, but will have to ensure further budget
allocations for sustainability.
F. BANK PERFORMANCE
2.31 Bank performance in Project identification was satisfactory. The Project was fully
consistent with the Government's development strategy and the Bank's Country Assistance
Strategy, and its support for a national training authority was consistent with the then
emerging Bank policy paper on technical and vocational educatioa and training. Questions
might have been raised about the job classifications component in view of its narrow
contribution to labor market information, but other components for institutional development,
support of a Resource Center, and a last resort financing for cross-sector training were
relevant to the Project objective of helping IVTB to become a leading center for training in
Mauritius.
2.32 Performance during preparation and appraisal was marginally unsatisfactory. The
Bank's last resort role for financing training implied IVTB would fill the gap left by the
private sector in training provision. The appraisal criteria for financing this training were
insufficiently described in the SAR (p. 16). Four criteria were listed: (i) IVTB would do
training needs analysis to assess demand for a course; (ii) a cost proposal would be prepared;
(iii) the course would be vetted by the appropriate training advisory committee, there were 24
of these committees subsequently organized; and (iv) the Bank would review and agree to the
resulting training proposal.
25



2.33 The lack of clarity underlying the training needs analysis subsequently contributed to
the dispute between the Bank and IVTB on financing the two courses on maintenance and
advanced design. Had the Bank team at pre-appraisal required the drafting of an annex
outlining each step and the criteria to be used in the subsequent appraisal of training
proposals, it would have reduced the grounds for dispute. This might have been done in the
SAR, but is more likely to have been done in a Project Implementation Manual which is
approved by the Bank as a condition for appraisal. The Bank's late introduction of cost-
benefit analysis as a funding criterion was resented for this reason in that it did not appear in
the SAR or legal agreements by reference.
2.34 The Bank team, as already mentioned, did not identify time-bound, monitorable
indicators of performance for the Project, nor were they required to under then Operations
Guidelines. During supervision of the Project at mid-term, performance indicators were
identified, but focused on inputs and outcomes with little attention to impact indicators. More
important, the indicators were largely ignored by subsequent supervision missions. Updates
were not found attached to supervision reports after 1996, nor in the Project files. No
references are made to these indicators in supervision reports. They appear to have been done
to satisfy a requirement and then forgotten.
2.35 Supervision missions and Bank management maintained an optimistic view of Project
performance. Despite continuous delays in disbursements and disagreements on policy
issues, supervision reports rated the Project as satisfactory until March 1998, seven months
prior to its closing. The signals were there much earlier in the slow disbursement, loan
cancellation at mid-term, policy disagreements, and failure of IVTB to adhere to Bank
procurement procedures to warrant an unsatisfactory overall rating.
G. BORROWER PERFORMANCE
2.36 Borrower performance was satisfactory in Project preparation, and marginally
unsatisfactory in implementation and operation of the Project. Discontinuities in political
leadership and shifting of IVTB to three different ministries during the Project life played an
important role in the uncertainties of the agency's future and the absence of consensus
surrounding its roles. The determination of whether the provision and promotion of training
by IVTB should be split remains unsettled today with divisions present within the Council
and between IVTB and the Ministry of Finance. Resolution of these differences is essential to
further development of the sector.
2.37  The Borrower's procurement of technical assistance and failure to follow Bank
procedures produced problems such that Bank supervision missions noted in December 1994
and again in November 1996 the need for IVTB to follow agreed procedures. It was the
failure to follow these procedures that led to the Bank's inability in October 1997 to provide a
non-objection for technical assistance to develop training for maintenance and advance design
courses. The Borrower also shares responsibility with the Bank for exaggerated optimism
about implementation. At the mid-term review IVTB pursued initially a smaller cancellation
of the Loan (US$0.4 million) than subsequently agreed. It also raised upward its targets for
staff development.

H. ASSESSMENT OF OUTCOME
2.38 . Despite some useful accomplishments, the Project is rated overall as unsatisfactory.
The absence of agreed performance indicators of impact make it difficult to assess the Project
impact. Institutional capacity was strengthened, at least at the outset of the Project, but the
Project's contribution was largely through technical assistance. Little staff development was
financed. A Resource Center was established and equipped, although its services have not yet
reached beyond IVTB to a wider training community. Cross-sectoral training was developed
and the number trained increased over the life of the Project, but disagreements over IVTB's
future role as a provider of training inhibited a wider impact for the Project. The Bank's
impact on the sector policy dialogue is uncertain and it has been unable to help build
consensus in the sector.
I. FUTURE OPERATION
2.39  The sector continues to be held back by the lack of a comprehensive vision for its
future and a consensus among stakeholders as to the roles and responsibilities of the public
and private sectors in vocational training. IBRD at the Government's request has closed the
current operation early and cancelled the remaining funds. Future operations should be
developed only after a consensus has emerged around the future of IVTB. Toward this
objective, IBRD can play a constructive role in helping build this consensus through
economic and sector analysis of training, public and private, and access to international
experience with issues of training finance and delivery.
J. KEY LESSONS LEARNED
2.40 The following are some of the lessons learned from this operation:
(a) In slow disbursing projects, attention should be given to whether coordination agents or
procedures can accelerate the flow of information and actions needed to undertake
procurement in timely fashion.
(b) Where consensus breaks down among stakeholders on important policy issues in the
course of a Project, it is important to be transparent with all stakeholders in analyzing issues
underlying disagreements.
(c) When the appraisal of sub-projects for training or other activities is to take place during
implementation of a Project, it is essential that appraisal procedures be clearly spelled out in a
Project implementation manual and agreed to at negotiations.
(d) All projects need time-bound, monitorable performance indicators to avoid overly
optimistic assessments of performance and disbursement potential, and these indicators
should form an integral part of the dialogue during supervision.
(e) Management should review supervision reports carefully and raise questions about
satisfactory ratings of development objectives and implementation where there are persistent
signals of problems throughout the implementation of a Project.

ANNEX B: IVTP AIDE MEMOIRE
REPUBLIC OF MAURITIUS
INDUSTRIAL AND VOCATIONAL TRAINING PROJECT
(IBRD LOAN 3401-MAS)
Aide-Memoire (Revised February 12,1999)
Implementation Completion Report Mission
January 25 - February 12, 1999
World Bank Mission Members
Peter Moock (Team Leader), Arvil Van Adams (Sector Manager),
Marilou Bradley (Operations Analyst), Joseph Bredie (Senior Education Specialist), Jon
Lauglo (Vocational and Technical Education Specialist)
1.    A World Bank mission visited Mauritius from January 25 to February 12, 1999, to
prepare ICRs (Implementation Completion Reports) for three Bank-supported education and
training projects, all of which were closed on October 31, 1998. The three projects were
closed by mutual agreement of the Bank and the Government of Mauritius because of
persistent delays in implementation and disbursement.  The ICR exercise provides an
opportunity for both the Government and the Bank to assess the reasons for this experience
and the lessons to be learned.
2.    This Aide-Memoire reports only on those findings of the three-part mission that relate
to the implementation and closing status of the Industrial and Vocational Training Project
(MBRD Loan 3401-MAS). Separate Aide-Memoires have been prepared on the findings
relating to the Education Sector Development Project (IBRD Loan 3578-MAS) the Higher
and Technical Education Project (IBRD Loan 3859-MAS).
3.    The mission met with key staff in the ministries overseeing institutions responsible for
the implementation of the three projects. The mission also visited a number of institutions
that received inputs under this Project. A full list of those met in connection with the ICR for
Industrial and Vocational Training Project (IVTP) is included in an annex to this Aide-
Memoire. The team members thank all those who gave generously of their time and provided
access to relevant information for this exercise.
Achievements and sustainable outcomes
4.   The overarching goal of the project was to assist the Industrial Vocational Training
Board (IVTB) in becoming the leading center for training in Mauritius. Established under the
Industrial and Vocational Training Act of 1988, IVTB has largely achieved this objective, but
the Project has played a marginal role in this outcome. Overall, the mission considers the
achievements sustainable. A robust economy and training levy revenues have enabled the
IVTB to finance most of its expansion and development without recourse to the Loan. The
Project had four objectives in support of the IVTB:
(i)  to provide selected technical assistance and training for the staff of the IVTB to create
and build up the Board's institutional capacity;
43



(ii)  to give opportunities for trainers throughout Mauritius, including in the private sector,
to have access to modem training methods and materials through the establishment of a
training Resource Center;
(iii) to offer training in specific sectors and industrial skills;
(iv)  to provide technical assistance and training to establish a job classification system for
the provision of labor market information.
5.    Despite some useful accomplishments, the mission has rated the Project overall as
unsatisfactory. Institutional capacity was strengthened, at least at the outset, but the Project's
contribution to this was largely through technical assistance with only modest amounts of
staff development financed. A Resource Center was established and equipped, although its
services have not reached beyond IVTB to a wider training community. Cross-sectoral
training was developed and the number trained increased over the life of the Project, but
disagreements over IVTB's future role as a provider of training inhibited a wider impact for
the Project. The Bank's impact on the policy dialogue in the sector is uncertain and it has
been unable to build consensus among stakeholders.
6.    Throughout the life of the Project, disbursements have persistently lagged behind
initial plans at appraisal and behind later revised plans at the mid-term review in March 1995.
At mid-term review, US$1.0 million of the US$5.4 million Loan was cancelled at
Government's request. The Project at completion expects to disburse about US$3.3 million,
with the balance of the Loan to be cancelled. The mission observes a series of factors
contributing to unsatisfactory implementation: (i) deficiencies in Project design and planning;
(ii) access to alternative and cheaper finance: (iii) uncertainty about IVTB's future functions;
and (iv) weakened Bank commitment to the Project components.
7.    In its design, the Project failed to identify clear targets for activities to be financed.
This deficiency was remedied at mid-term, but the targets set were a mixture of program and
Project targets, and once jointly developed, were largely ignored by the Bank in subsequent
supervision. The design sought to integrate Project management into the ongoing activities of
IVTB, but later in 1994, both sides agreed that a Project Coordinator was needed to facilitate
implementation. The design also exaggerated the importance of the development of a jobs
classification system as a contributor to training decisions, and the component was dropped at
mid-term.
8.    The mission observes that the IVTB was the beneficiary of a strong economy and able
to finance many of its development activities without resorting to the Loan. It notes,
however, that this picture has changed, if perhaps temporarily, as the Ministry of Finance in
1998 has held back government funding and forced IVTB to use its levy reserves, which are
now rapidly diminishing. Since early 1996, IVTB has had three different Ministers in as
many ministries.   The mission concludes that the frequent changes have slowed
implementation in this period and produced uncertainty about IVTB's future.
9.    Beginning as early as 1995, the mission found a disagreement over sector policy and
the role of IVTB emerging within Government and the private sector. The question was
whether IVTB should continue its expansionary role as a provider of training, or devolve this
responsibility to the private sector and focus instead on its role as a promoter of training
through regulation, financing, and provision of market information. The Project fell victim to
44



this disagreement. There were different views on this issue within the new IVTB Council
appointed in 1996, and between IVTB and other Government bodies, including the Ministry
of Finance. The Bank supported studies and a workshop on the issue. Instead of remaining
neutral, however, the Bank was seen as taking a position favoring IVTB as a promoter of
training. This advocacy was a source of strain on the Bank's relation with IVTB during the
later implementation period.
10.   This strain was exacerbated in 1997 when the Bank withheld financing for two new
programs of cross-sectoral training, on the grounds that IVTB had not followed the Bank's
procurement procedures. The mission notes that the failure of the Bank, during appraisal and
in project documents, to spell out clearly the criteria for approval of cross-sectoral training
may have contributed to this outcome. It also observes, however, a series of earlier
communications (in 1994 and again in 1996) in which the Bank had pointed out minor
procurement irregularities. With adequate financing of its own, IVTB stopped submitting
requests for Bank financing of cross-sectoral training after October 1997, and this helps to
explain the significant under-disbursement of the Loan.
Lessons learned
11. The following are some of the lessons learned:
(a)  Agents and procedures for project coordination can accelerate procurement in slow
disbursing projects.
(b)  Building consensus around difficult sector policies requires transparency of approach
and the building of confidence among all stakeholders.
(c)  Clearly  defined procedures are essential for appraising sub-projects during
implementation.
(d)  Time-bound, monitorable  performance  indicators  are  important to  guiding
implementation and measuring achievement of objectives.
12.   Mauritius has determined that its future development will come from improving
productivity in existing industries through the introduction of advanced technology and
skilled labor and fostering the growth of new and more capital-intensive industries. The
mission supports this strategy but observes that realization of this goal continues to be held
back by the lack of a comprehensive vision for IVTB's future and a consensus among
stakeholders as to the roles and responsibilities of the public and private sectors in vocational
training. Completion of IVTB's corporate plan, now in process, and building a broad
consensus around the plan are high priorities.
13.   The mission feels that future EBRD operations should be developed only after a
consensus has emerged around the future of IVTB. Toward this objective, IBRD could play a
constructive role in helping to build this consensus through economic and sector analysis of
training, public and private, and providing access to international experience with issues of
training finance and delivery.

Next steps
14.   On the basis of the information gathered during the mission, as well as a study of
World Bank documents and supervision reports, the Bank will now prepare an ICR. This will
be presented to Government in draft by the middle of March 1999, to obtain factual
corrections before the report is finalized. The Government's own contribution to the ICR,
which was submitted to the Bank prior to the mission and discussed with relevant parties
duriing the mission, will be appended as an annex to the report. The final ICR will be
submitted officially to Government by the Bank in April.

3. EDUCATION SECTOR DEVELOPMENT PROJECT
A. INTRODUCTION
3.1   Although Mauritius enjoys a high literacy rate and relatively skilled labor, the quality
of its labor force falls short of that required to sustain growth in a competitive international
environment. Recognizing the shortcomings in education that impede the country's economic
development, the Government of Mauritius in the late 1980s embarked on the preparation of
its Education Sector Master Plan (ESMP), articulating the policy measures and investment
programs required for establishing a viable system of education and training that would
produce the human resource base required to sustain and accelerate the economic and social
development envisaged for the country.
3.2 The key policy objectives of the ESMP (formally entitled the "Master Plan for the Year
2000" and adopted by Parliament in 1992) are: (a) to increase access to and promoting equity
of educational opportunity at all levels, (b) to improve the quality and efficiency of education
at all levels; and (c) to strengthen sector management and institutional development. The
Plan, completed in 1991, was supported by an education sector investment program (ESIP) to
be implemented during the period 1991-2000. From the ESIP, a core investment program
(CIP) was prepared in order to finance key activities necessary for the successful
implementation of the ESMP and the achievement of its policy objectives.
3.3 The Education Sector Development Project (ESDP), the fourth project in the education
sector to be financed by the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD),
was prepared to assist the Government in implementing, during the period 1993-1998, its
education development program as articulated in the ESMP. To support the policy objectives
of the ESMP, the Project financed a portion of the Government's five-year (1993-98) core
investment program for the education sector. The total cost of the CIP was estimated at US$
78.7 million, of which the Project provided a loan totaling US$ 20.0 million. The balance of
US$ 58.7 million was to be provided by the Government. The Project became effective on
June 22, 1993 and closed on February 28, 1999, two after earlier than the original closing
date. In light of the slow progress achieved with project implementation and disbursements,
US$ 6.0 million was canceled on December 30, 1997, at Government's request. The total
amount disbursed for the CIP was US$ 77.41 million of which US$ 12.28 million was
disbursed from the loan. The remaining loan balance of US$ 1,712,744.92 has been
cancelled.
B. STATEMENTEVALUATION OF OBJECTIVES
3.4 The overall objective of the ESDP was to assist the Government of Mauritius with the
implementation of the policy reforms and associated investment programs of the ESMP and,
through this process, to strengthen the capacity of the Ministry of Education and Scientific
Research (MOESR), formerly the Ministry of Education, Science and Technology, to develop
53



and implement sustainable sector investment programs. Specifically, the Project's objectives
were to:
(i)  Increase access to and promote  equity of education, by:  (a) providing for the
rehabilitation of 25 dilapidated primary schools and construction of 13 new primary schools
in under-served regions; (b) renovating 21 state secondary schools and constructing five new
secondary schools, providing for 8,000 additional student places; (c) providing financial
support to upgrade up to 55 private secondary schools and expand enrollment capacity by
7,000 student places; and (d) expanding enrollment capacity at the University of Mauritius to
about 5,000 student places.
(ii) Improve the quality of education, by: (a) funding the design and implementation of a
renewed national system to assess student achievement; (b) supporting pre-service and in-
service teacher training; and (c) providing library materials and teaching aids.
(iii) Strengthen education sector management, by: (a) assisting the Tertiary Education
Commission (TEC) to prepare institutional development plans for tertiary education
institutions; (b) establishing an educational management information system (EMIS);
(c) supporting research to explore alternative approaches for more effective, efficient and
flexible delivery of education; (d) developing a viable institutional framework for a national
schools Inspectorate; and (e) establishing a network of regional education centers to provide
educational support to schools at the regional level.
3.5 The objectives of the project were fully consistent with the Government's education
strategy as laid out in the ESMP. They were consistent with the Bank's country assistance
strategy for Mauritius, which supports human resources development, including the creation
of a skilled labor force capable of meeting the needs of a growing and diversifying economy
and for sustaining growth in an increasingly competitive international environment.
3.6  There have been two changes in government since the inception of the ESMP in 1992.
Until 1995, the ESMP continued to be the primary conduit for implementing policy reform
measures in the education sector. Following the elections and until 1997, there was, on
balance, a halt to implementation of key policy measures as defined in the ESMP. In 1997, a
new reform of the education sector was initiated by the current Government as articulated in
the "White Paper for Pre-Primary, Primary and Secondary Education" (WP) appearing in
September 1997. The WP affirms earlier proposals for nine years of compulsory education,
for the reform and ultimately the elimination of the CPE, and for the integration of education
and training. An Action Plan (AP) to elaborate the policy measures in the WP was prepared
and approved by Cabinet in March, 1998.
C. ACHIEVEMENT OF PROJECT OBJECTIVES
3.7 The achievement of project objectives is uneven. Project activities aimed at expanding
access have mostly been completed, although with substantial delays. Project activities aimed
at quality improvements and strengthening of sector planning and management, however,
have been completed only partially. In some cases, key activities in these two categories have
not even been started. The achievement of project objectives was affected by several factors,
among which included shifts in government and the subsequent introduction of new policy
changes which led, in some cases, to modifications of ongoing interventions supported under
the Project.

Increase Access to Education and Promote Equity
3.8 The achievement in this component has been marginally satisfactory. The Project
achieved most of its objectives for increasing access, although with substantial delays.
During the implementation phase, the scope of project support in regard to increasing access
to primary and secondary education was modified to accommodate the needs in these sectors.
In lieu of rehabilitating and constructing primary schools as originally envisaged at appraisal,
the Project supported the construction and equipping of 45 pre-primary schools in areas where
pre-primary education was not available in 1996, of which forty-three schools are now
operational. The construction of the two remaining pre-primary schools in Rodriques has not
yet started, although they are planned and scheduled to be completed by the end of FY99.
Eight schools were constructed and 21 schools extended and/or rehabilitated using
government resources.
3.9 Twelve state secondary schools (SSSs) have been extended and/or renovated using
government resources. Under the Project, seven (and not five as planned) SSSs were
constructed and equipped, providing for 7,000 student places, all now occupied. One new SSS
was build with local funds during the same time. The construction of these schools
experienced substantial delays, however. The processes relating to site selection and site
acquisition, which started only during implementation, took more than two years to complete.
In a few sites, no agreement could be reached with the owners, and the land was acquired
through compulsory acquisition.  Delays were experienced equally during the pre-
construction and construction phase.
3.10 On balance, quality standards in the construction and the cost-effectiveness of procuring
works and equipment for these secondary schools were not always assured. A few schools
are suffering from water leakage; some walls have started to crack and ceilings given way. In
one school, staff toilets were not adequately provided; in this school for girls, the toilets for
girls and male faculty members were combined in one room. Stairways in the same school
are misplaced for convenience and safety. In another school, classroom space does not
accommodate furniture and equipmenit needs. A few schools have not been provided with
laboratory equipment. The result is that a few schools cannot conduct classes requiring
laboratories. Students have to use laboratories in nearby schools. In a few cases, the costs of
works were far higher than estimated. This deficiencies can partly be explained by the weak
management and coordination of the Ministry of Public Investment (MP) who was also
responsible for other projects. The MPI has not been able to install the fixtures and electrical
supply for equipment already procured for five SSSs. This equipment can thus not be
installed or used in these schools.
3.11 At the request of the Government, the Loan Agreement was amended in December
1995 to include a program of assistance to the PSSs under the Project. However, in the
absence of a viable and affordable financing mechanism for assisting private schools, project
funds were not utilized to support school improvements. Instead, the rehabilitation of PSSs
was financed from concessional loans made available through the Development Bank of
Mauritius. Sixty schools have borrowed a total of Rs 77,743,400 under this scheme.
3.12 Several factors led to low levels of borrowing. Loan interest rates were generally not
favorable to managers/renters. Given the uncertainties as to how PSSA will be managed in
the future under the AP, mangers/renters were also reluctant to borrow funds for investments
55



over which they might have no control in the future. In addition, Government opted to use its
own resources rather than borrow funds under the Project.
Improving the Quality of Education
3.13 The contribution of the Project to improving the quality of education was not fully
satisfactory. MES capacity to design and implement the proposed reform of the student
assessment and examination system was strengthened; twenty persons benefited from
training. MES research programs were expanded. The school leaving examination at the end
of Standard 6 (the Certificate of Primary Education or CPE) was redesigned to strengthen its
assessment and testing capabilities, taking into account essential learning competencies
(ELCs) for all children and desirable learning competencies (DLCs) for those who can go
beyond the essential. Inspectors, teachers and CPE markers have been trained in the use of
the CPE. The scheme and materials for introducing Continuous Comprehensive Evaluation
(CCE) have been developed, piloted, and for which teachers, inspectors, head teachers and
deputy teachers were trained in its use. CCE was introduced at Standards 4, 5, and 6 in
primary, rather than beginning with Form I in secondary as originally envisaged. School
certification at the "O" level was enhanced for 15 subjects. Question banks, including a data
processing system, were established and are in use. Examination- and test-related equipment
and computers have been procured. A national diagnostic assessment for Standard 3 was
prepared, but has not yet been implemented. A national orientation assessment for Form III
was not prepared due to policy changes in 1995.
3.14 Certificate and diploma courses were revised and simplified. MIE moved to a semester
system and course-credit system. The Professional General Certificate of Education (PGCE)
and Bachelor of Education (B.Ed.) courses were organized along the same lines. A Distance
Education Unit was set up at MIE. Distance education for A-level students in five subject
areas (chemistry, physics, English, mathematics and economics) was launched. This is
supplemented in each case by a residential program for practical work. Bachelor of Education
courses have been started, offered jointly by UoM and MIE. B.A. courses in economics,
business studies and physical education are being offered through distance education. A
course on "Training in Careers Education and Guidance" for secondary school teachers and a
two year in-service course leading to the Advanced Certificate in Education are also offered*
through distance education. School administration training is also being given: 260 head
teachers and 450 deputy head teachers have been trained. The skills of Grade "C" teachers
were upgraded by distance education and through a B.Ed. course for HSC holders.
Strengthening Sector Management and Institutional Development
3.15 Achievement of this goal is marginally unsatisfactory. Improvements of facilities
supported by the Project at the University of Mauritius (UoM) were achieved. The
improvements comprised procured equipment for all faculties, especially for the Engineering
Faculty, which was in the process of upgrading most of its programs from diploma to degree
level. Although procurement was slow, equipment has been installed and is being use.
Interventions to strengthen the capacity of the Technical Education Commission (TEC) were
financed from government resources. Currently, TEC has five professional staff and is
facilitating and/or managing a large number of regional and national tertiary programs, as
well as coordinating institutions in the tertiary sub-sector.

not yet operational for several reasons. There was a general lack of consensus among, and

educational planning were prepared but were never approved and, therefore, not implemented.

E. PROJECT SUSTAINABILITY
3.28 Project sustainability is uncertain, although some of the resources proposed under the
AP could help to sustain it. The AP was approved by the Cabinet in March 1998 and was
made available to the public in December 1998. Although the new policy reforms defined in
the Action Plan follow in some ways the broad development objectives of the Master Plan,
they differ in terms of details, timing, and particular policy measures and programs to be
implemented. Most ongoing project interventions will be completed using local resources.
Other interventions planned under the project may require some modification and/or
strengthening to reflect new policy changes and to better respond to the needs of the education
sector. This may lead to greater demands for resources in the education sector.
3.29 Despite low levels of Loan disbursements, the Government has made substantial
financial contributions toward the implementation of the CIP. With the introduction of the
AP, however, it is not clear how and in what context project interventions will be sustained.
Concrete measures to support AP reform proposals have not yet been fully elaborated. A cost
and financing plan, including operational guidelines which define how implementation will be
phased, have also not yet been prepared.
F. BANK PERFORMANCE
3.30 Bank performance was satisfactory during project identification and preparation. The
team conducted a thorough analysis of the issues in the sector and the options for addressing
these issues. Bank performance during appraisal, however, was not fully satisfactory. Given

the weak capacity in the sector, the appraisal mission should have examined more closely the

3.31 Bank performance during supervision was unsatisfactory. Reports on most Bank

project performance has been systematically rated as "satisfactory," despite the numerous
delays and ongoing problems reported in the same reports.
3.32 The Bank was weak in reaching timely agreement with the Borrower on corrective
measures to be taken as problems arose. There were important recurring problems that were
not addressed in a timely and, in some cases, transparent manner. By the time that the Project
was rated as "unsatisfactory," in the period soon before project closing, it was already too late
for corrective actions to be taken.
G. BORROWER PERFORMANCE
3.33 Borrower performance was uneven throughout the project cycle.  During the
preparation phase, the performance of the Borrower was satisfactory in identifying key policy
reforms required to improve learning achievements. The Borrower's performance, however,
was less satisfactory in articulating the steps necessary for successful implementation.
Administrative arrangements and appropriate operational mechanisms were not established
prior to embarking on project implementation.
3.34 The Borrower's performance during the implementation phase was unsatisfactory.
Limited efforts were to improve the administrative arrangements and operational mechanisms
required for effective project management and implementation. This led to poor coordination
and monitoring and often disconnected approaches to implementation. Reporting on project
performance were not systematically carried out. Insufficient and inadequately trained staff,
including frequent changes in staff, led to discontinuities in the implementation of key tasks.
Although computers were purchased to assist the Finance and Procurement units, they were
not used to monitor project procurement and disbursements. On balance, the Borrower was
slow to address implementation problems. In some instances, new policies are being
implemented without a fully elaborated operational and implementation plan.
H. ASSESSMENT OF OUTCOME
3.35 Project outcome was marginally unsatisfactory. Although the project did increase
access to pre-primary, primary and secondary education, such expansion is neither enough nor
meaningful if not accompanied by qualitative improvements in schooling and education that
promote higher achievements. This is evident from the fact that quality improvements have
again been proposed in the AP. The project has only partially implemented key activities to
60



support quality improvements in education; work completed, while important, have just begun
to address the issues and bring about changes by which education in Mauritius can be
strengthened and be made more responsive to meeting the overall social and economic goals
of the country. The project fell short in implementing interventions to strengthen the overall
capacity of the education which are, among others, paramount to the challenges of
implementing new policy reforms and for sustaining key operations in the sector.
I. FUTURE OPERATION
3.36 Changes in government did not necessarily alter key development objectives in the
education sector. Following the broad development objectives of the Master Plan, new policy
reforms under the AP aim to further strengthen the overall performance of the education
system, with a view to increasing learning achievements and ensuring greater equity in
education. Although currently the AP serves as the primary conduit for strengthening the
overall performance of the education system, there is continued support to implement and/or
complete several ESMP-related interventions planned under the project using local resources,
albeit in modified ways. In parallel, efforts are being taken by Government to facilitate a
broader dialogue with key stakeholders around the new policy reforms and how these reforms
are to be implemented. The full elaboration of concrete measures to support the new policy
reforms, a cost and financing plan, and operational and implementation guidelines will be
required to facilitate full implementation of the AP.
J. KEY LESSONS LEARNED
3.37 Key lessons from the Education Sector Development Project are:
(a) Building consensus around difficult and sensitive sector reform  policies requires
continuous dialogue with and the building of support from all stakeholders.
(b) Projects will fail if little attention is paid to implementation. Project implementation
issues should be addressed during the project identification stage and not during
implementation. The establishment of an effective administrative structure, operational
and implementation guidelines, clearly defined roles, responsibilities, are vital to
ensuring smooth and efficient project implementation.
(c) Frequent changes in personnel, including roles and responsibilities, impede higher levels
of achievements of project development objectives.
(d) Improving learning achievements requires a more holistic and integrated approach that
go beyond the measurement of quantitative inputs. The Borrower and Bank supervision
missions should give systematic attention to and then follow up on the qualitative
elements of project implementation.

1.   A World Bank mission visited Mauritius from January 25 to February 12, 1999, to
prepare ICRs (Implementation Completion Reports) for three Bank-supported education and
training projects, all of which were closed on October 31, 1998. The three projects were
closed by mutual agreement of the Bank and the Government of Mauritius because of
persistent delays in implementation and disbursement.  The ICR exercise provides an
opportunity for both the Government and the Bank to assess the reasons for this experience
and the lessons to be learned.
2.   This Aide-Memoire reports only on those findings of the three-part mission that relate
to the implementation and closing status of the Education Sector Development Project (IBRD
Loan 3578-MAS). Separate Aide-Memoires have been prepared on the findings relating to
the Industrial and Vocational Training Project (IBRD Loan 3401-MAS) and the Higher and
Technical Education Project (IBRD Loan 3859-MAS).
3.   The mission met with key staff in the ministries overseeing institutions responsible for
the implementation of the three projects. The mission also visited a number of institutions
that received inputs under this Project and discussed general issues having to do with higher
education development in Mauritius with the Tertiary Education Commission. Field visits
were carried out in selected pre-primary, primary and secondary schools and regional
directorates in Zones 1, 3 and 4. A full list of those met in connection with the ICR for the
Education Sector Development Project (ESDP) is included in an annex to this Aide-Memoire.
The team members thank all those who gave generously of their time and provided access to
relevant information for this exercise.
Achievements and sustainable outcomes
4.   We have briefly assessed the achievements and sustainable outcomes of the project in
terms of its three objectives:  (i) increasing access to education and promoting equity;
(ii) improving quality, and (iii) institutional development and strengthening sector
management.
5.  Increasing access to education and promoting equity. The Project constructed and
equipped 45 pre-primary schools, of which 43 are now operational. In addition, seven state
secondary schools (SSSs) were constructed and are operational. However, a few of these
SSSs are not yet fully equipped. In all, 29 primary schools were constructed, extended and/or
87



rehabilitated and 12 SSSs extended/renovated using government resources.  School
construction suffered substantial delays. The Ministry of Public Infrastructure (MPI) was less
than effective in planning and coordinating the civil works component of the Project. This
was partly because MPI was also working on other projects.
6.  Improvements in private secondary schools (PSSs) were not financed by the Project but,
rather, with Government resources. In the absence of a viable and affordable financing
mechanism, PSS school improvements were financed from concessional loans made available
through the Development Bank of Mauritius.  Sixty schools borrowed a total of Rs
77,743,400 under this scheme.
7.  Improving the Quality of Education. The contribution of the Project to improving the
quality of education was not fully satisfactory. MvIES staff benefited from training to
strengthen their professional roles. The school leaving examination at the end of Standard 6
(the Certificate of Primary Education or CPE) was redesigned, and inspectors, teachers and
CPE markers were trained in its use. The scheme and materials for introducing Continuous
Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE) were developed and piloted, and relevant staff were trained
in their use. CCE was introduced at Standards 4, 5 and 6 in primary, rather than beginning
with Form I in secondary as originally envisaged.
8.   School certification at the "O" level was enhanced for 15 subjects. Question banks
were established and a data processing system was installed and is in use. Examination and
test-related equipment and computers were procured. The national diagnostic assessment for
Standard 3 was prepared, but it has not yet been implemented.  A national orientation
assessment for Form III was not prepared due to policy changes in 1995. The structure of
certificate and diploma courses has been revised and simplified. A Distance Education Unit
was set up at MIE. Distance education for "A" levels in five subject areas was launched.
School administrator training was given. The skills of Grade "C" teachers were upgraded.
9.  Institutional Development and Strengthening Sector Management. Achievement of
this goal has been unsatisfactory. The facilities of the University of Mauritius (UoM) were
improved. Although with delays, equipment for this component was procured and is in use.
Interventions to strengthen the capacity of the Tertiary Education Commission (TEC) were
financed from government resources. Plans for establishing a National Schools Inspectorate
(NIS) were prepared, but the NIS is not yet operational. Similarly, the regional education
centers (RECs) were not upgraded as envisaged under the Project. An implementation plan
for making these centers operational was not prepared. However, consistent with the ongoing
decentralization, regional directorates have been established in all seven regions and seven
regional directors have been appointed. Each region is currently preparing their overall
development plan. The overall plan to make the EMIS operational was completed in 1997 but
has not been fully implemented. All heads of government primary and secondary schools
benefited from in-country training provided by the International Institute of Educational
Planning (IIEP).
10. Planning and coordination in the Ministry of Education and Scientific Research
(MESR) at the central and local levels was weak. Although project design highlighted the
importance of capacity building within the education sector, achievements were not
satisfactory. Project management has been generally unsatisfactory throughout the six years
of implementation. Several factors impeded the efficiency with which project activities were
executed: (a) weak project design; (b) frequent changes in personnel; (c) insufficient and
88



inadequately trained staff, including the lack of efficient operational mechanisms, resulting in
poor project management and coordination; (d) shifts in government, leading to new policy
changes and, in some cases, requiring modifications in the approach to and timing of
implementing particular ongoing and/or planned project interventions; and (e) lack of support
for private secondary schools.
Lessons learned
11. The project had only limited impact in terms of improving the performance of the
education sector, in general, and improving learning achievements, in particular. Project
implementation suffered from a mixture of factors, ranging from poor project design to weak
institutional capacity to disconnected approaches to project planning, management and
implementation. Although the implementation of the AP has begun, there are several policy
measures for which operational guidelines have not been fully elaborated.
12. The implementation experience of ESDP has taught MESR, including its implementing
agencies, and the World Bank several key lessons:
(a)   Building consensus around difficult and sensitive sector reform policies requires
continuous dialogue with and the building of support from all stakeholders.
(b)   Projects will fail if little attention is paid to implementation.
(c)   Frequent changes in personnel, including roles and responsibilities, impede higher
levels of achievements of project development objectives.
(d)   Improving learning achievements requires a holistic and integrated approach , which
goes beyond the measurement of quantitative inputs.
13. The AP was approved by the Cabinet in March 1998. It was made available to the
public in December 1998. New policy reforms defined in the AP build on the broad
development objectives of the Master Plan, but they differ in terms of details, timing and
particular policy measures and programs to be implemented. The Government intends to use
local resources to continue ongoing and planned interventions supported under the Project.
Efforts are currently being taken by the Government to facilitate a broader dialogue with key
stakeholders around the new policy reforms and how these reforms are to be implemented.
The Government of Mauritius recognizes that it is important to build broad support for the
reform process and articulate measures for its implementation.
14. The World Bank supports the Government of Mauritius in its efforts to improve the
quality of education and raise learning achievements. As the Government mobilizes public
support for the Action Plan, the Bank could be asked to help facilitate the dialogue with key
stakeholders. It could also be asked to provide international expertise to help with the
elaboration and costing of AP measures. Finally, if needed, financing could be requested for
activities required for program implementation.

ANNEX C: ESDP GOVERNMENT'S CONTRIBUTION TO LIPLEMENTATION COMPLETION
REPORT
EDUCATION SECTOR DEVELOPMENT PROJECT: LOAN 3578-MAS
I. Introduction
The Education Sector Master Plan, developed in partnership with and advice from the
IBRD, UNDP, ILO and UNESCO and completed in June 1991, articulates a package of long-
term policies designed to strengthen and modernize the Mauritian education system. At the
Donors' meeting held in Paris, the World Bank was among one of the foreign funding
agencies to pledge support to the implementation of the Master Plan, and eventually through a
loan agreement financed the plan to the tune of US$ 20 m.
II Original Project Goal
The objective of the project was to assist the Government of Mauritius to implement
policy reforms and associated investment programmes of the Education Sector Master Plan.
II. Specific Project Objectives
The key policy objectives are:-
(a) To increase access to and promote equity of educational opportunity at all levels;
(b) To improve the quality and efficiency of education; and
(c) To strengthen sector management and institutional development.
IV. Accomplishment of the Goal - General and Global
The objectives of the Master Plan with regard to access were that:-
(a)  Every child should attend a Pre-Primary School and the increase should be from 80% to
100%;
(b) Every child should be provided with an agreed standard of basic education through the
introduction of a minimum of 9 years of schooling, so that 100% of the children should
attend Primary School in lieu of the 97%;
(c)  The in-take at Secondary level should register a rise from 50% to l55%, and
(d) At the Tertiary Sector i.e. University of Mauritius, the enrolment should go from 2000
students to 3200 students by the year 2000.
93



V. Quality
(a) Upgrading of Primary School Teachers,
(b) Provision of necessary tools in schools,
(c) Examination and assessment system as a positive means of improving standards in
schools.
VI. Management
Management involves: -
(a)  Decentralisation of the education system;
(b)  Participation of communities in the running of schools;
(c)  The creation of a National Inspectorate; and
(d)  The development of Tertiary Education under the aegis of the Tertiary Education
Commission.
VII. Global Assessment
It is difficult at this point in time to access and evaluate the global impact of the
project, especially in view of delays encountered in its implementation, in part due to
circumstances beyond control and in part by changes in demand for education or policies for
education. Certain results, though, relating to access could be visible and positive. More
children have now the opportunities to attend school, especially at the Pre-Primary level, so
that the foundation is laid for a better future in their academic performance.
On the other hand, quality and equity are still  matters of concern.  The
underprivileged need not only the school buildings but they must be provided with dedicated
teachers to help them through. Improvements at all levels can be brought into the schools but
if the teacher does not deliver the goods, quality will be a far cry. Again, while it is easy to
improve the physical facilities rapidly, quality is seem as a long-term process. The mentality
in the classroom should change. The social and environmental background is also an
important factor to consider. So long as there is no change in these directions, quality and
equity will be meaningless words.
The change in the examination system has not materialised yet. There is a cry for a
change, but several considerations make it difficult to bring modification to the system replete
with sensitive issues.
The National Inspectorate is another issue which is difficult to address. Such a
concept did not take into account the realities of as well as the obstacles in the system, not to
speak of the administrative problems. The gap is too large between the Primary and
Secondary sectors, in terms of qualifications. It has been a compartmentalised system for
94



years. Even from an administrative point of view, it is difficult to merge the inspectorate of
all levels, since the conditions do not lend themselves to it.
At the time of conceiving the plan, it did not take much to formulate theories but
translating them into action can prove and has proved heavy going.
VIII. Accomplishment of Specific Project Objectives
Access to education has not been impossible due to the construction or
extension/renovation of buildings. Government has, through its annual budget, made
financial resources available for the on-going programme of rehabilitating/renovating and
constructing Primary Schools. The project, which was partly committed to that activity, has
instead and in response to changing needs financed the construction of 45 Pre-Primary Units:
43 in Mauritius and 2 in Rodrigues. It must be pointed out that, since the past 8 years, a Pre-
Primary Unit has been set up, wherever possible, in existing Primary Schools and it is the
Government policy to include a Pre-Primary Unit in the construction of any new Primary
School. The number of Government Pre-Primary Schools has gone up from 138 in 1996 to
175 in 1998 and the enrolment rate from 80% to 98% for the same period.
In the Primary sector, there has been the extension/renovation of 21 Primary Schools
and the construction of 8 schools, some to replace old buildings and others new. The
enrolment has moved from 129,000 in 1993 to 130,000 in 1998. Construction of new schools
and renovation of old buildings are on-going activities of the Ministry: on average, one or
two new buildings and four to five renovation works per year.
The Secondary sector has not remained inactive. 12 existing Government Secondary
Schools have been extended/renovated since 1993. 7 instead of 5 SSS have been constructed
under the project. The enrolment is 94,300 in 1998 compared to 87,600 in 1993, an increase
of 8% as projected in the Appraisal Report. Renovation works on other remaining State
Secondary Schools will be carried out since provisions will be made in the budget during the
ensuing years.
The Tertiary level equally shows a marked improvement. The enrolment is 2,161 in
1993, compared to 3,135 in 1998. However, as early as 1993, the Bank together with the
Government of Mauritius decided to embark on a separate project for the tertiary sector so
that the component dealing with the University of Mauritius was removed, except the
acquisition of equipment which was effected.
Private Secondary Schools which should have been rehabilitated under this project
have instead benefited a loan from the Development Bank of Mauritius. To-date 60 Private
Secondary Schools have taken advantage of this facility and an approximnate amount of Rs
77.5 m. has been disbursed by the DBM.
Hence, construction/renovation/rehabilitation at all levels has enabled the expansion of
intakes, the general elimination of sub-standard rented facilities, redressed severe over-
crowding in some schools and serviced a number of regions, rural or the one- time
underprivileged, thereby reducing the ordeal of travelling long distances to schools.
95



Much ground has been covered with a view to improving the quality and efficiency of
education. Significant progress has been made in the bid for CPE reforms. Levels of learning
in tenns of Essential and Desirable Learning Competencies for Standard IV, V & VI have
been laid down. Developed document "Learning Competencies for all" has been supplied to
all schools and there has been the intensive training of Teachers, Deputy Headteachers,
Inspectors & CPE Markers. 20 workshops were organised in Mauritius & Rodrigues.
The Scheme of Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation was developed, tried and
piloted and materials prepared. Training of teachers, DHTs & HTs & Inspectors took place in
6 workshops.
The scheme, after piloting, has not been expanded for circumstances beyond control,
but will be implemented in all schools in the context of the Action Plan. Separate assessment
sheets are provided for the assessment of slow-learners.
A number of reports has been produced covering annual statistics, items analysis
(CPE), for examiners, among others.
To strengthen the professional role of the MES, all officers except one have been
trained either on long-term or short-term attachment programme.
Equipment already purchased and consisting of software and hardware enable the
MES to enhance their computer aided activities.
The Mauritius Institute of Education has equally contributed to a larger measure in
upgrading quality. A number of courses has been set up to provide avenues in the teaching
field. The Teacher's Diploma started in 1996 has produced 112 qualified teachers to-date, the
Diploma in Educational Supervision & Inspection 28, Advanced Certificate in Education,
since 1993 (General Purpose & Asian Languages) 3075, Bachelor in Education 118.
About 1200 Primary Teachers have been trained since 1993 and this is an on-going
activity (pre- & in-service), whereas the Secondary School teachers categorised 'C' have been
eliminated. Training was given to the 3413 'C' teachers. On the other hand, 1650 State
Secondary Teachers have successfully, followed the Teacher's Diploma Course.
The third objective has been lagging in completion. This is due to reasons already
evoked earlier.
The Tertiary Education Commission has expanded and strengthened its capacity to
rationalise the tertiary sector in co-ordinating and supporting inter-alia planning, budget, staff
development. There is now a one line budget item in the name of TEC for the tertiary sector.
TEC is gradually developing a strategy to strengthen its role. A consolidated development
plan for the four tertiary institutions has already been prepared.
The Education Management Information System has been designed but is not
operational. This has been due to constant change of the officer in charge of that component,
so that each time the new officer has had to learn the job from scratch. No fully integrated
system has yet been put in place to provide linkages within the operating units of the Ministry,
nor the Ministry of Economic Planning and Ministry of Finance. The Government, though,
now plans to install a much more comprehensive system with its own resources.
96



All 217 Headteachers of Government Primary Schools as well as the 31 Rectors of the
Secondary sector have been trained in educational management to facilitate the
decentralisation process now under way. This training was provided by the RIEP (Paris)
through a contract under this project. Management training is also available to MoE staff. No
research work and studies, though, have been carried out by the Ministry staff. This is
normally under the ambit of the Mauritius Institute of Education, Mauritius College of the Air
and Mauritius Examination Syndicate.
The National Inspectorate has not taken off as it was suggested in the Master Plan. As
said earlier, numerous reasons made it difficult to be implemented. The Action Plan has
adopted a more rational approach in that while Primary Inspectorate structure is operational, a
Secondary Inspectorate proper is being created comprising some 25 officers spread region
wise. Recruitment procedures have been initiated. Currently, all Inspectors and Technicians
are based in regions and are active in the schools, playing the role they have been trained for.
Decentralisation of the education system did not take place at all. Eight Regional
Centres were to be upgraded as Regional Education Centres to perfonn a service function to
provide educational support services for the benefits of teachers and pupils. These centres
were not upgraded in terms of building and equipment since funds were not made available.
The centres were not seen as a priority.
With the Action Plan coming into operation, this aspect has been taken into account.
In fact, already seven Regional Directors have been appointed and with their respective cohort
officers have started implementing the decentralisation policy as addressed in the Action Plan.
The existing RECs' have not been extended nor equipped but the functions and concept as
evoked in the SAR are being executed within the regional directorate philosophy. The
community, as conceived in the Master Plan, was given too much importance. There was
exaggerated interference in the administration of schools. Fortunately their importance is now
being toned down and the community is seen more as a consultative body.
IX. Role of Government
Government pledged to support the Master Plan by providing counterpart funds.
Financing of allowances and operating costs would be the responsibility of Government who
would also finance about 66% of the foreign exchange costs involved in the construction,
purchase of equipment, teaching aids, training and technical assistance. In fact, counterpart
funds were regularly made available in the annual budget and no undue delay was ever met in
the payment of bills.
A Ministerial Steering Committee chaired by the Minister of Education was to provide
policy and operational guidance for the implementation of the Master Plan. It would meet
regularly to review the status of project implementation and resolve issues. The Steering
Committee met regularly, to start with once every fortnight and then once a month and
progress was being closely monitored.
The Project Implementation Unit was to assume responsibility for all procurement
matters and for supervising civil works under the project. The PIU would also handle the
preparation of disbursement applications and keep the loan accounts.
97



The PIU was set up and staffed by a Project Manager, assisted by a Deputy Project
Manager. The procurement office had a staff of 4, headed by a Principal Store Officer, while
the Finance Section had a Senior Finance Officer and a Finance Offilcer. These were
seconded by the Ministry of Finance to the PIU, specifically to attend to the implementation
of the project. At one time, with a change in the Ministry, there was a change in the
administrative procedures to get the Unit absorbed in the general structure of the Ministry.
This created confusion and chaos and the project suffered. Fortunately the situation has now
been set right again with a change at the head of the Ministry.
On the other hand, the Ministry of Education also put in place the Master Plan Co-
ordinating Unit (MPCU) to monitor progress of work of the implementation agencies and to
act as facilitators. Officers designated to be co-ordinators in the Unit, though given some
basic on the job training and supported by a Consultant attached to the Ministry, could not
adapt themselves to this new culture and type of work, Any degree of prodding could not get
them to react and to do even simple things.
After one year of implementation work, the then Head of the MPCU was replaced and
the Head of the PLU was given the added responsibility of the M:PCU. However, this
important responsibility should have been accompanied with a degree of status and authority
to enable proper management.  Unfortunately it was not so; hence the inability to be
thoroughly effective and efficient. Movement of key staff in vital sectors equally proved a
handicap to smooth and effective implementation.
All civil works were undertaken by the Ministry of Public Infrastructure, the then
Ministry of Works. Considering the volume of responsibility devolved on that Ministry, and
having to attend to the demands of the whole of the Government service, that Ministry could
not execute construction of the seven SSS within the time limit.
X. The Role of the Bank
Right from the conception of the project, the Bank has played a significant role in the
preparation of the Education Sector Master Plan (ESMP) by providing professional support
and advice, in co-operation with other UN Agencies. The Bank identified consultants,
prepared reports and statistical data prior to the Appraisal Report being finalised. Once the
Loan Agreement was signed, a supervision mission was fielded every three months to
monitor progress. This was felt to be too many and too frequent and the PIU was not given
time to breathe in between missions. Not much significant progress could be visible on each
mission, especially at the early stage of take-off, and the PIU had to bear the brunt.
The Bank could not understand the fact that delay in acquisition of land or in finalising
drawing plans could not be imputed to the Ministry of Education. Acquisition of land is a
time-consuming procedure which could take months and even years to finalise, especially if it
is on a compulsory basis. The Bank could have ensured, before the signing of the Loan
Agreement, that land was already available and vested with the Ministry, and not taken for
granted any information, erroneous as this could be, given to them by the negotiating team.
The Bank also constantly reminded the PIU that Goverrnent was paying commitnent
charges on all undisbursed amount, and this argument was echoed by other Ministries. The
98



Bank was impressing upon the PIU the need to analyse tenders very quickly, even in a matter
of a couple of weeks. The Bank failed to understand the highly sensitive issue of tenders in
Mauritius, as all bidders feel they are potential winners. The PIU could not avail itself of the
services of end-users at the opportune time for technical evaluation exercises. Another factor
which caused delays.
Another equally significant explanation for slow disbursement was the volume of
work devolved on the PIU. In fact the PIU was set up to work on the African Development
Bank (ABD) project, with the personnel as shown in a previous paragraph. But later the Unit
found itself responsible for other additional projects viz: the ESDP, HTEP and the one funded
by the Agence Francaise de Developpement (AFD), without a corresponding increase in staff.
The four or five officers in the Unit had to prepare tender documents, launch tenders, analyse
bids, make recommendations, prepare contract and lists of items awarded, receive
consignments, carry out surveys and distribute to schools - quite tall orders - not to speak of
administrative duties and others.
Otherwise, there has been excellent relationship with the Bank. The supervision
mission staff acted as facilitators all the time, were always ready to help. The Bank was
always quick in replying to any correspondence and was flexible on a number of issues, yet
dogmatic when needed.
XI. Conclusion
To conclude, it can be said that it has been a valuable and enriching experience to have
worked on this project and much benefit has been obtained due to the co-operation of the
Bank staff.

4. HIGHER AND TECHNICAL EDUCATION PROJECT
A. INTRoDucTIoN
4.1   The Higher and Technical Education Project was the fifth Project financed in the
sector by the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD). It was the first
to focus exclusively on post-secondary technical, and higher education. The fourth Project,
the Education Sector Development Project, included assistance for higher education to
strengthen the Tertiary Education Commission (TEC) and upgrade equipment at the
University of Mauritius (UoM). These objectives were achieved, although for TEC mostly
with Government resources. The Government subsequently decided that in view of the
demand for quality human resources for support of its strategy of expanding technology-based
industry and services, a separate Project would need to be identified to produce these middle
and higher-level human resources. Identification of the Project commenced in 1993.
4.2 The Project became effective June 6, 1995 with financing of US$16.0 million. The
Government was to finance the remaining US$10.5 million of the total estimated cost of US$
26.5 million. It got off to a slow start and only a small amount of Loan funds was used during
most of the first two years. At the request of the Government, US$12.0 million of the Loan
was cancelled in December 1997. Subsequently, the Government and the Bank agreed that it
would be more efficient to close the Loan early and complete the Project at a more realistic
pace using Government's own resources. The Project was closed February 28, 1999 after
three years and two months of the original five years of implementation. The total amount
disbursed from the Loan was US$ 3.1 million. The remaining balance of US$ 908,571.05 has
been cancelled.
B. STATEMENT/ EVALUATION OF OBJEcrIVEs
4.3 The main objective of Project was to support the Government's tertiary education
program contained in the 1991 Education Sector Master Plan (ESMP), which aimed to
produce the human resources required to support a more competitive economy. The Project
would help produce higher- and middle-level manpower in the sciences, engineering,
technology and management. This manpower would have the capacity to raise the quality,
cost-effectiveness and diversity of Mauritius' industrial and services sectors. In addition, the
expansion of postgraduate studies and research at UoM, supported by the Project, would
produce the new knowledge necessary to develop Mauritius into the financial and
technological hub it planned to become in the Indian Ocean. The Project would assist the
Government's program by: (a) strengthening the UoM; and (b) rationalizing polytechnic
education.
101



Strengthening the University of Mauritius
4.4 UoM was to be strengthened by: (a) upgrading staff and facilities to raise the quality of
education; (b) increasing the number of quality graduates in all fields, (c) improving links
with employers, (d) developing viable postgraduate education and research programs, and
(e) enhancing the efficiency of university operations.
4.5 Upgrading the quality of education through upgrading staff and facilities. The
Project would finance: (a) Improvements of staff quality. Academic staff were to be sent
abroad for graduate training. To provide flexibility, some new staff would be recruited on a
contractual basis. Staff would be encouraged to get involved with the private sector in
research, consulting and advisory services.  (b) Links with reputable international
universities. Foreign academics would be recruited on short-term contracts to teach at UoM,
train local staff and assist in the accreditation of programs. (c) Gradual introduction of a
course credit system. This would facilitate students taking courses in different institutions to
broaden their degrees. (d) Development of the library and other facilities. The library was
too small, and several facilities, such as the faculty of engineering, required upgrading or
expansion.
4.6 Increasing the number of quality graduates in all fields and especially science,
engineering and management. The Project would support expansion of enrollments from
2,000 to 3,200 full-time equivalents (FTEs) by the year 2000, and 5,000 FTEs by 2006,
thereby producing more and better qualified professionals. The number of graduates by 2006
would be over 1,000 degree-holders annually, about 70 MBAs and about 80 postgraduates
annually.
4.7 Improving links with employers. The Project would help to make the statutory
Advisory Committees fully functional across all faculties by 1997 and organize regular
seminars with practitioners.
4.8 Developing viable postgraduate education and research programs. The Project
would support the establishment of postgraduate programs. Teaching and research would be
closely linked and scholars from different disciplines would collaborate. Research by
postgraduate students and academic staff would be supported by improving equipment and
through a research fund. On-going research would be firmly linked to the strategy of the
Mauritius Research Council.
4.9 Enhancing the efficiency of UoM operations. The Project would support an audit of
the use of facilities and faculty of all four tertiary institutions. It would also assist with the
extension of the Library hours.
Rationalizing Polytechnic Education
4.10 Polytechnic education was to be rationalized by: (a) improving the quality of faculty,
curricula and facilities, (b) expanding output, and (c) improving management and labor
market linkages.
4.11 Improving the quality of faculty, curricula and facilities. The Project would assist
teachers with taking internships in industry, undertaking consultancies or joint industry-
polytechnic research, as well as training new staff It would help improve admission
102



standards, the balance between applied and basic science, and theory and practice. Facilities
and equipment at Flacq would be improved.
4.12 Expanding polytechnic education. The project would finance the transformation of
the Droopnath Ramphul State Secondary School at Pamplemousses into a polytechnic that
would train middle-level managers in finance, accounting, management and information
science. It would also provide equipment for the new polytechnic at Rose Hill.
4.13 Inproving management and labor market linkages. The Project would help
strengthen the capacity of the MTF by establishing a secretariat and a management
information system, organizing regular meetings with employers, and conducting tracer
studies and labor market surveys to inform students about employment.
4.14 The objectives were fully consistent with the Government's economic development
strategy as laid out in the Technology Strategy for Competitiveness, developed with extensive
private/public sector participation in 1994.  The objectives were clear and considered
important at the time of identification and remain so today. The interventions to improve
quality at the UoM were justified in terms of making the University, the only one in the
country, the center for producing university-trained professionals and for research in response
to an increasingly more complex economy. Also, upgrading the quality, and expanding the
output of middle level technicians and supervisors was in line with changes at the enterprise
level where increasing automation and concerns about quality were creating a need for quality
technicians. At identification, the potential for these two key institutions to take on a broader
role in the economy was there; the resources of the Project would make it possible for them to
meet the Government's objectives.
C. ACHoEVEMENT OF PRoJECr OBJECTIVES
4.15 Achievement of the objectives has been uneven. The Project helped to produce more
higher and middle-level manpower. But the capacity of the higher-level ones to raise the
quality and diversity of Mauritius's industry and service sectors is uncertain given that quality
improvements at UoM have been marginal. Very little new knowledge in finance and
technology through research has been contributed. On the other hand, the projected quantity
and quality of middle-level technicians are being produced at the polytechnics. The Project
should be rated as unsatisfactory for the UoM component and satisfactory for the polytechnic
component, and unsatisfactory as a whole. The overall Bank's and Borrower performance
should be rated as unsatisfactory.
Strengthening the University of Mauritius
4.16 Enhancing quality. The achievement of this objective, which included: improving
staff quality; establishing links with international universities followed by accreditation;
introducing a course credit system and development of the library and other facilities, was
unsatisfactory. For staff upgrading, it was envisaged that 120 staff would undergo training to
increase the percentage of Ph.D.-holders from 25 percent in 1994 to 55 percent by 2006.
After a slow start and just over three years of implementation, eight staff members have
obtained Ph.D.'s and 17 Master's, mostly, from UoM or from U.K. universities. Presently,
nearly 30 percent of staff possesses Ph.D.'s. Delays developed because personal or family
constraints prevented designated staff from participating. A decision by UoM management to
103



have staff register primarily at UoM for upgrading (and the fact that most of these then went
to U.K. universities with which UoM already had historical twinning arrangements, and few
to universities in other parts of the world) meant that (i) the faculty missed an opportunity to
learn from a broad spectrum of international experience in higher education, (ii) the new links
with non-U.K. universities that the Project was supposed to foster never materialized; and
(iii) the UoM had little opportunity to leam about accreditation in different parts of the world,
something else that the Project was designed to foster and which, in the end, was not
introduced at UoM. On the other hand, the UoM did make a beginning with establishing
quality assurance measures across the University, and a course credit system was introduced
in every faculty in 1998, ahead of schedule.
4.17 The development of the Library and the Faculty of Engineering which was scheduled to
start in mid 1996, has not been achieved. For the Library, almost 11,700 titles are being
procured, albeit with significant delays. About 4,400 books have been delivered and are
available for student use although the library is too small to accommodate many more books.
Expansion of the Library is only in the early design phase. Similarly, the engineering faculty
has received more equipment, but it can no longer accommodate further increases in student
enrollments because the expansion of the facilities is much delayed. The delay at both sites
was caused by (1) the need to undertake a space audit and (2) poor communications and
collaborations between the Bank, UoM and the Ministry of Public Infrastructure (MPI). At
one point, MPI proceeded to evaluate bids from design architects and award a contract
without the Bank's No Objection and without involving UoM.
4.18 Increasing the number of quality graduates. This objective was not only achieved
but exceeded in a quantitative sense. The overall target was for enrollments to reach 2, 700
by 1998. Actual enrollments were 3, 460 at the time of this ICR. Enrollments in science,
engineering and social studies and humanities increased slightly faster than in other faculties.
The structure of enrollments by level also improved. The number of postgraduate students
about doubled. Those in Master's prograrns, including MBA students, almost doubled. All of
these increases exceeded 1998 targets. The transfer of diploma courses from the University to
the polytechnics in order to free resources for degree-level programs was also achieved. A
first group of four diploma courses was transferred. Diploma students at UoM declined from
550 in 1994 to about 470 (100 full-time and the rest part-time) in 1998, again exceeding the
target that had been set.
4.19 Improving labor market relevance. Achievement of this objective was marginal.
Advisory Committees have been re-activated and are now more systematically involved in
curriculum discussions. Industrial stages for students have, however, not yet been organized
except in the faculty of engineering. UoM organizes a few executive training courses, and a
small number of staff engage in consulting assignments with industry. The UoM has
established a University Consultancy Center to promote links with industry.
4.20 Developing viable postgraduate and research programs.  This objective was
partially achieved. The number of staff with postgraduate degrees increased somewhat, and
the number of postgraduate students doubled. But efforts to expand research and link it
firmly with the strategy of the Mauritius Research Council have failed. Only four research
projects were submitted for financing from the research fund established under the Project,
and none of these went forward because the Government would not approve the use of Loan
funds for consumable materials. The UoM has not been able to produce a research plan that
104



would provide a clear sense of direction, or flexible guidelines and procedures, or incentives.
All of these would need to be done to establish a dynamic research culture.
4.21 Increasing Efficiency at the University of Mauritius. Achievement of this objective
is satisfactory. The space audit was undertaken, financed by TEC, but it did not include an
audit of staff use, nor did it cover other TEIs in Mauritius. Some sharing of facilities is done
with the MIE. The introduction of the course credit system has improved efficiency, and the
campus-wide distance teaching system has enabled students to follow classes at different
locations. Library hours have been extended in 1996 to 7 p.m. on weekdays
Rationalizing Polytechnic Education
4.22 Improving quality and relevance.  This objective has been achieved.  At the
Droopnath Ramphul Polytechnic, 18 teaching staff (nearly the entire staff) have been trained
in Singapore or in Australia. A large number at the Rose Hill and Flacq Polytechnics have
been trained in France. Curricula have been upgraded. Examination papers for Droopnath
Ramphul, marked in Australia, showed good levels of attainment. The diplomas of the
Droopnath Ramphul Polytechnic are officially recognized in Singapore and Australia.
4.23 Expanding polytechnic education. This has been achieved. In the first phase, the
Droopnath Ramphul State Secondary School was rehabilitated and its classrooms converted to
laboratories. In the second phase, an extension was build on an adjacent piece of land, albeit
with a delay of nine months. The Droopnath Ramphul Polytechnic has been fully equipped
and provided with furniture, books and materials of good quality. Equipment at the Flacq
Polytechnic was substantially upgraded, and one civil technology laboratory at the Rose Hill
Polytechnic was also equipped from project funds. This was done with little delay and good
results.
4.24 Improving Management and Labor Market Linkages. Achievement of these
objectives is partially satisfactory. Links with employers are good. The industry-faculty
advisory committees meet regularly and have organized, among other things, industrial
attachments for all students. The attachments and the annual industry-day have facilitated
recruitment. Polytechnic students are readily absorbed into the job market. Sixty-five percent
of the Droopnath Ramphul graduates were employed six months after graduation. Links with
UoM are good and facilitated the transfer of diploma courses from UoM to the polytechnics.
One intervention was not achieved, and that was enhancing MTF's capacity for policy
analysis and labor market monitoring. The analyst who was to be recruited to provide these
services to the MTF was not recruited because it was difficult to find a suitable candidate and
because the Government did not approve this recruitment, suggesting that for the time being
existing ministry staff could fulfill this role.
D. IPLEMENTATION AND MAJOR FACTORS AFFECTING THE PROJECT
4.25 Implementation started to fall behind targets right from the start at the UoM. First Bank
supervision missions treated this as a temporary set-back, but delays began accumulating in
most areas of implementation. Annual implementation plans became meaningless and in most
cases fell short of the performance monitoring indicators. The use of Loan funds reflected the
slow implementation, disbursement was much below projections in the first semester and
stayed that way almost throughout the short life of the Project. At the polytechnic on the
105



other hand, progress with implementation was steady, and the component was implemented,
closely following the performance monitoring indicators. A number of factors contributed to
unsatisfactory implementation: (i) unrealistic staff development targets; (ii) low levels of
commitment at UoM; (iii) lack of familiarity with Bank procedures; and (iv) poor
communications between Government Agencies and rigid regulations.
Unrealistic Staff Development Targets at UoM
4.26 The Bank imposed unrealistic staff development targets in an attempt to raise staff
quality quickly and build twinning relationship with international universities. UoM staff had
difficulties understanding the projections in the SAR, and concerns about the increase in
teaching loads and staff-student ratios, but accepted the targets at appraisal. Bank staff did not
listen or were not hearing university administrators when they talked about personal, family,
cultural and technical factors limiting the number of teaching staff who could be away.
Moreover, the UoM has close historical and cultural links with U.K. universities and resisted
the Bank's preference that training options in the U.S. or in Asia be considered. It took a
separate consultant's study before the Bank agreed, at the 1997 mid-term review, to lower
training targets, but by that time commitment to staff development had waned.
Low Levels of Commitment at UoM
4.27 During preparation, UoM staff complained about the ambitious targets of the Project
and the difficulty of implementing staff development, procurement, physical expansion,
accreditation and research all at the same time. At the early stages of implementation when
targets became impossible to achieve, the time and effort to implement the Project became
burdensome, and staff's own work increased, commitment which was lukewarm during
preparation dropped substantially. The component manager had a difficult time, getting staff
to provide information or implement the activities they were responsible for, or explain the
reasons for the lack of progress. Complex and time-consuming procurement procedures,
particularly for books, aggravated the situation, and commitment to the Project sank low.
Lack of Familiarity with Bank Procedures
4.28 Bank procedures for bid evaluation for equipment and book procurement caused
difficulties at the UoM, MTF and the Central Tender Board (CTB). Lack of familiarity with
Bank procedures by these agencies caused delays, while the Bank and CTB tried to come to
an understanding about the interpretation of articles in the bidding documents.  The
procurement of books caused problems with the demand by the Bank and the CTB concerning
specifications as well as bid evaluation, resulting in almost one year delay in deliveries.
Several issues about procedures and bid evaluation remain to be agreed upon between the
Bank and the CTB.
Poor Communications between Government Agencies and Rigid Regulations
4.29 Construction at Droopnath Ramphul suffered delays because the MPI gave the site first
to the Ministry of Housing and then it was difficult to get it back to Education. The MPI also
failed to keep either the UoM or the MTF informed about the design and specifications of the
buildings they were preparing for them. Also, rigid recruitment regulations, such as the Public
106



Services Committee having to approve the post of the policy analyst and the Pay Research
Bureau having to decide on the remuneration level, resulted in the analyst never being
recruited in the end. The MTF still lacks the capacity to analyze policy and assess labor
market demand for the polytechnic system.
E. PROJEcT SUSTAINABIL
4.30 The Project was closed early, about three-fourths into the original implementation
timetable. The Government is committed to completing the Project in due course using its
own funds. The sustainability of what was achieved under the Loan is assessed as probable.
Borrower Commitment
4.31 The Government has indicated that it plans to complete the Project and has made the
resources available to UoM and to the polytechnics, at least through the current budget cycle.
Longer-term commitment is unclear in view of elections in two years and possible changes in
the institutional and policy environment, although the polytechnics would seem to less subject
to changes in these areas. The polytechnics have a more effective stakeholder constituency,
suggesting that the achievement of the objectives are likely to be sustained.
Institutional and Policy Environment
4.32 The institutional environment in the tertiary sub-sector is complex. The Ministry of
Education and Scientific Research (MESR) is responsible for higher education policies and
budgets. TEC is in charge of coordination and sector resource allocation and use, and UoM
has a certain degree of autonomy through its Council and Senate. The MESR is currently
preoccupied with the Action Plan for pre-primary, primary and secondary but, through TEC,
is formulating initiatives for more private provision of higher education and more alternative
delivery modes such as distance learning, twinning with overseas institutions and a greater
focus on higher-level studies in areas where Mauritius has a strategic interest. While, in the
short term, UoM is likely to continue its current strategy of responding to social demand and
increasing enrollments accordingly, and while continued quality improvements will improve
UoM's attractiveness for local students, substantial changes in the institutional environment
could affect UoM as it currently operates. UoM is presently drawing up its strategic plan, and
it may have to begin considering stronger integration and collaboration with local and
overseas institutions or more non-traditional options such as distance, off-site or executive
programs.
4.33 The sustainability of UoM will also depend on the policies for cost-recovery and private
(for-profit) provision. The Government is divided about this issue, but there is recognition that
continued quality improvements and expansion at recent rates are not sustainable. UoM has
little financial independence. Its revenue-generating capacity from teaching, consulting and
research is small at present. Policies in this area are lacking. One of the issues UoM struggles
with is incentives for staff. Although the Council has autonomy with respect to conditions of
service, it has not exercised this right and the incentive structure follows the practice in the
public service.
4.34 Competition from private universities may affect UoM's ability to attract and retain
quality faculty, unless it can improve its financial footing and offer more attractive
107



compensation packages. The Bank will collaborate with Government to complete an
economic analysis of the current welfare state provisions, to enable Government to examine
alternatives and make the necessary policy choices. This could include an analysis of the
affordability of UoM's strategic plan. In contrast, the polytechnics have paid attention to
quality and accreditation of their diplomas, and demand for the programs they offer is likely
to remain high. The institutional environment will, however, change for them as well,
particularly with respect to distance technologies and overseas providers, but the policy
environment is likely to be favorable for so long as labor market demands are sustained.
Economic and Financial Viability
4.35 The sustainability of the current economic growth trend and the further expansion of
industry and services will affect job creation and, in tum, the demand for middle- and higher-
level manpower from UoM and the polytechnics. The demand for UoM graduates has been
consistently high for engineering and science graduates but has fluctuated and tended to
decline for management, law and social sciences graduates. It is unlikely that UoM can
continue to respond to the demand for engineering admissions, given the delay with the
expansion of the facilities for that faculty experienced under the Project. The overall viability
of UoM will depend on its ability to keep capital and recurrent costs in line with benefits as
perceived by students and the Government.
4.36 The polytechnics have experienced a strong demand for their graduates, especially those
from the engineering and business streams. Recent fluctuations in the demand for accounting
graduates have been resolved, with the banking sector increasing its demand, and the current
weak demand for IT graduates is likely to change as reliance on computers in the services
sector increases. The polytechnics, like UoM, are publicly financed and have little revenue-
earning experience and capacity. They should be better positioned, however, than UoM to
maintain public financing or react to private providers by increasing earnings from testing,
quality audits and similar services.
F. BANK PERFORMANCE
4.37 Bank performance was deficient during preparation and part of implementation, at least
for the UoM component. It was close to satisfactory for the polytechnic component during
these same phases. During preparation of the UoM component, the Bank proposed state-of-
the art practices, including staff upgrading through international training, the use of high-
quality interim faculty to raise the standards of teaching and research, the introduction of
quality assurance mechanisms and accreditation, and a shift in focus away from diploma-level
teaching to degree courses and research. But, many of these were new to UoM, and UoM staff
felt that they needed more time to acquire the capacity to implement them, and felt these
interventions were imposed by the Bank.. Moreover, the targets and timetable imposed by the
Bank turned out to be unrealistic and not feasible. Bank staff also paid little attention to the
culture of the UoM and its historical ties with U.K. Universities and insisted that staff would
go to universities in the US or Asia.
4.38 The Bank pushed less for needed reforms such as greater equity (increased access for
less well-off students), rationalization/integration of programs and cost-recovery, since there
was resistance to these among some stakeholders. Also, the Bank undertook an economic
analysis of project costs and benefits, but this was not done together with UoM or TEC, for
108



whom it would have been a useful experience. At appraisal, the Bank failed to assess UoM's
implementation capacity, the level of commitment within Government, as well as ownership
by UoM staff. A last minute lapse in government commitment led to a reduction in the size of
the Loan after appraisal, and reduced assurances for integration and cost-recovery at
negotiations.
4.39 The Bank provided semi-annual supervision missions, but much of the time and effort
during these missions and in supervision from headquarters were dedicated to procurement
issues. Technical and financial issues got little attention during supervision. Also, requests
from other tertiary institutions for technical advice met with little response from the Bank. It
took till the mid-term review for UoM to become familiar and comfortable with the project
interventions, but by then the targets had already been scaled back substantially.
4.40 The Bank's performance during preparation and appraisal of the polytechnic component
was helped by the technical assistance provided earlier by the Singapore Polytechnic. Most of
the planning, curriculum choices and requirements for staff training had already been
prepared, and the Bank was able to build on this work during preparation and to appraise the
component relatively efficiently.  The polytechnic component received somewhat less
attention from the Bank than the UoM component because of Bank staff preoccupation with
the latter.
G. BORROWER PERFORMANCE
4.41 The performance of the Borrower during preparation and appraisal was marginally
deficient, particularly in regard to the UoM component. The Government failed to submit to
the Bank plans for integration and cost-recovery. The sector ministry relied mostly on TEC to
work with the Bank during preparation, but TEC at the time did not have the necessary staff,
the technical expertise or the time and resources to assure proper design and scaling of the
Project. UoM lacked technical expertise and familiarity with a few of the proposed
interventions. It failed to come to grips with the size and complexity of the staff and facility
development activities or with plans for the research fund. Also, UoM after agreeing to
recruit high quality but expensive interim staff backed away from this agreement on the basis
that this would create imbalances in incentives. UoM management participated in the
preparation and supervisions, but increasingly with less conviction and ownership.
4.42 The Borrower's performance during preparation and supervision of the polytechnic
component was satisfactory. The MTF functioned efficiently and technical assistance and
bilateral support were forthcoming, so the polytechnic did not require much involvement from
Government. Financial, institutional and technical requirements were also less substantial
since the Loan amount was only about a fifth of the total Loan.
H. ASSESSMENT OF OUTCOME
4.43 Overall, the Project had a substantial impact on the polytechncis but only a limited one
at the UoM. At the University, almost none of the physical expansion objective, less than one
third of the quality objective, and only part of the efficiency objective have been achieved.
This assessment is made three-fourths into the original implementation period, and with
Government having given its commitment to completion of the Project, albeit at a slower
109



pace. But the long-term sustainability of UoM in its present role is questionable especially in
light of the reluctance to address the issue of cost recovery in higher education.
4.44 At the polytechnics, on the other hand, the quality and expansion objectives has been
achieved. The Government has provided the resources to complete this component and the
investment can probably be maintained.
Re-assessment of economic rate of return
4.45 The economic internal rate of return for the Project was estimated at appraisal to be 21
percent for the University component and 15.5 percent for the Polytechnic component. A 20-
year stream of benefits for the Project was estimated by projecting additional enrollment by
type of degree produced by the Project and summing for each year the additional earnings
expected for each graduate. Enrollments were expected to grow from 1,700 to 5,000 by 2004
for the University and from 229 to 1,552 by 2002 for the Polytechnics. Costs included capital
and incremental recurrent cost each year, plus the opportunity cost for each additional
graduate.
4.46 These calculations produced a 20-year stream of project benefits and costs for the
University and Polytechnic components. The annual differences in the benefits and costs for
each component generated a 20-year profile of net benefits. Due to costs being incurred up
front with the Project investment, the net benefits were initially negative and later positive as
the new graduates entered the labor market and began receiving additional earnings. Internal
rates of return were calculated for the stream of net benefits. Monte Carlo simulations were
run to test the robustness of these estimates. The results confirmed the viability of the two
Project components under all cost-benefit scenarios.
4.47 Re-assessing the benefits and costs of the Project leads to the conclusion that earlier
estimates of the internal rate of return for the University and Polytechnic components were
exceeded. This conclusion is based on recognition that enrollment gains have exceeded
projections and thus will lead to a larger stream of benefits. At the same time, project costs
have been much lower than projected with the result that the overall net benefits associated
with the Project are higher than projected at appraisal and will lead to internal rates of return
higher than those estimated. Stated simply, crowding students into the University, less so in
the case of the Polytechnics, without expansion of facilities and capital costs implies an
increase in the projected rate of return for the Project.
4.48 The logic of this conclusion, however, has its limits, as increased student crowding will
eventually lower the quality of education and reduce the expected incremental earnings of
graduates. The reduced incremental earnings associated with lower quality will lower the
stream of benefits and lower, subsequently, the project net benefits. The result will be lower
internal rates of return to the Project. Student crowding is not a sustainable alternative to the
comprehensive strategy planned in the Project of completing the expansion of facilities and
introducing various quality assurance measures. No earnings data are available to illustrate a
decline in incremental earnings with the current enrollment gains. It is sufficient to say at this
point that additional enrollments without completing the expansion of facilities and qualify
measures planned at the time of appraisal threatens the economic viability of the Project.

I. FUTURE OPERATION
4.49 UoM is preparing a medium-term strategic plan, as are other TEIs and TEC. This plan,
scheduled for release in April 1999, is likely to propose a continuation of current efforts to
improve quality, increase enrollments, and expand facilities. It would be important that the
strategic plan be given a wide audience and address issues such as cost-recovery, integration,
institutional management and equity, which were given short shrift in the design or in the
implementation of the Project. The Bank could assist with the economic analysis of the
University's strategic plan and its affordability. The polytechnic sector is planning to first
complete the Project and then, based on its analysis of the responsiveness of the polytechnics
to demands in the economy as learned from tracer studies, adjust its program offerings and its
scale of operations to meet the real demand. MTF's stated intention at this time is to make
only such fine-tuning adjustments, and this is cause for optimism.
J. KEY LESSONS LEARNED
4.50 The main lessons learned from the implementation experience with this Project are the
following:
(a) To keep implementation on schedule, it is essential that ownership of the Project be
established at national and institutional levels and with the major stakeholders.
(b) The Government and the Bank need to insist on sound sectoral and sub-sectoral policies
before investing in complex improvement and expansion projects such as this one.
(c) The Bank must pay close attention to the political economy of the country and take
account of the lessons from history in determining what kind of policy changes are
feasible, what role stakeholders and beneficiaries should play in this process and how
long it is likely to take for sensitive policies in education to become accepted and owned.
(d) Project objectives need to be scaled to take account of the local implementation capacity,
and project complexity should only be increased once the technical and implementation
capacity has increased. The Bank's recently introduced adaptable lending instruments
have such phasing built in.
(e) Procurement procedures need to be harmonized with those of the Central Tender Board
(CTB). This is a national, not a sectoral, issue.
(f) The statutes of the governing councils and boards for tertiary education need to be
updated to reflect the need for greater academic and financial autonomy of the
institutions, in order for them to implement effective projects such as this one.

ANNEX B: HTEP AIDE MEMOIRE
(IBRD LOAN 3859-MAS)
Aide-Memoire (Revised February 12,1999)
Implementation Completion Report Mission
January 25 - February 12, 1999
World Bank Mission Members
Peter Moock (Team Leader), Arvil Van Adams (Sector Manager),
Marilou Bradley (Operations Analyst), Joseph Bredie (Senior Education Specialist), Jon
Lauglo (Vocational and Technical Education Specialist)
1.   A World Bank mission visited Mauritius from January 25 to February 12, 1999, to
prepare ICRs (Implementation Completion Reports) for three Bank-supported education and
training projects, all of which were closed on October 31, 1998. The three projects were
closed by mutual agreement of the Bank and the Government of Mauritius because of
persistent delays in implementation and disbursement. The ICR exercise provides an
opportunity for both the Government and the Bank to assess the reasons for this experience
and the lessons to be learned.
2.   This Aide-Memoire reports only on those findings of the three-part mission that relate
to the implementation and closing status of the Higher and Technical Education Project
(IBRD Loan 3859-MAS). Separate Aide-Memoires have been prepared on the findings
relating to the Industrial and Vocational Training Project (IBRD Loan 3401-MAS) and the
Education Sector Development Project (IBRD Loan 3578-MAS).
3.   The mission met with key staff in the ministries overseeing institutions responsible for
the implementation of the three projects. The mission also visited a number of institutions
that received inputs under this Project and discussed general issues having to do with higher
education development in Mauritius with the Tertiary Education Commission. A full list of
those met in connection with the ICR for the Higher and Technical Education Project (HTEP)
is included in an annex to this Aide-Memoire. The team members thank all those who gave
generously of their time and provided access to relevant information for this exercise.
Achievements and sustainable outcomes
4.   We have briefly assessed the achievements and sustainable outcomes of the project in
terms of its two objectives.
5.   Strengthening the University of Mauritius. To produce quality high-level human
resources and strategic knowledge, the UoM facilities were to be expanded and improved,
staff and programs upgraded, enrollments increased, especially in engineering, science and
management, institutional efficiency enhanced and research substantially expanded.
Achievements after three years of implementation fall short of targets in terms of expanded
facilities, staff upgrading and research production but have been exceeded in terms of
enrollments and, to a lesser extent, some of the quality and efficiency improvements.
6.   Expansion of the library and faculty of engineering facilities to house more books and
accommodate a greater number of students was not accomplished. Work is still only in the
127



design phase. The campus space audit that needed to be done took time, and collaboration
among the Bank, the Ministry of Public Infrastructure (MPI) and UoM was inefficient and
caused serious delays. The Ministry of Finance, however, assures the Bank and the
University that this will be completed in the next few years with government funding.
Upgrading of facilities was achieved with some delay, which those responsible for
implementation attribute, at least in part, to the Bank's complex procurement procedures. The
upgrading of 120 staff to increase the number of Ph.D.-holders from 25 to 55 percent of total
teaching staff by 2000 was not achieved; only 24 staff obtained graduate degrees. The
original target was unrealistic in view of family, cultural and technical (pedagogical)
constraints. The planned enrollment increase has been not only achieved but substantially
exceeded. Students number 3,460 at present as compared with the 1998-99 target of 2,700.
Moreover, the structure of enrollments improved substantially, with a much greater proportion
of students in degree programs (88 percent) as compared with the situation at appraisal (less
than 50 percent).
7.   Teaching quality and efficiency were to be improved, and this has been largely
achieved. Basic quality assurance mechanisms are now in place in every teaching faculty.
However, accreditation remains on the back burner. Efficiency has improved with the
introduction, ahead of schedule, of a course credit system, and the establishment of a campus-
wide distance education system. On the other hand, producing new knowledge in technology
and finance through the research fund established under the Project was not achieved. There
was lack of agreement as to what could be financed from the Loan, a shortage of qualified and
experienced staff, inadequate incentives and no strategic direction or research plan.
8.   A number of factors contributed to unsatisfactory implementation of the UoM
component: (i) The Bank had failed to scale the Project in line with UoM's technical and
implementation capacity, and many of the targets agreed at appraisal were unrealistic;
(ii) UoM was unfamiliar with many of the project innovations and, therefore, did not own
these fully but, rather, saw them as imposed by the Bank; (iii) UoM was also not familiar with
Bank procurement procedures and felt that these were demanding, overly complex and the
cause for much delay; and (iv) the overload of changes all at once (rapid and substantial
enrollment increases, staff going outside for training, the introduction of quality assurance and
attempts to expand research) imposed an intolerable implementation burden on UoM and
contributed to the lack of ownership and the delays in implementation.
9.   Rationalizing Polytechnic Education. To produce middle-level manpower, the
Project provided assistance and resources to upgrade programs and equipment in the
polytechnic at Flacq, provided equipment for the polytechnic at Rose Hill and established a
new polytechnic with four business programs on the site of the former Droopnath Ramphul
Secondary School. Capacity for policy and labor market analysis was to be built in the
Technical Schools Management Trust Fund (MITF), which manages the polytechnics, to
ensure that the content and output of the polytechnic programs in the country would reflect
market demand. The above objectives were achieved satisfactorily, with the exception of the
recruitment of a policy analyst in MTF.
10. Establishment of the Droopnath Ramphul business polytechnic was fully achieved. The
construction of the new wing met with a nine-month delay, but procurement of equipment,
furniture, materials and books was done efficiently for the most part as was also true for the
Flacq and Rose Hill polytechnics. More importantly, the teaching programs at Flacq were
128



upgraded; those for Rose Hill were developed and are now functioning well; and Droopnath
Ramphul is now providing a two-year higher-level diploma program in response to the
demand from HSC holders and the economy; it now enrolls some 300 students.
1 1. All 18 teachers at Ramphul have been trained either in Singapore or in Australia. This
is reflected in the good examinations results for students at Ramphul as reported by the
Australian external examiners. The Ramphul diploma is recognized in both Australia and
Singapore. Droopnath Ramphul has ample scope for expansion and will introduce a business
information course later this year in response to market needs. Although MTF did not acquire
the full-time policy and labor market analyst, it has instituted tracer studies and has, thus far,
responded prudently to market needs. The present results auger well for the long-term
sustainability of the polytechnic project component.
12.  Implementation of this component was satisfactory. Certain factors contributed to this
result: (i) Early identification of market needs and what was feasible in terms of institutional
capacity led to a high degree of ownership in MTF and among staff in the polytechnics; and
(ii) The MTF board met regularly, was kept informed about implementation progress and
used its influence to get things done. Some delays occurred, especially early in the Project,
because MTF was unfamiliar with the Bank's procurement procedures and also because
communications were inadequate between MPI and MTF, as well as the Bank.
13.  The Bank and the Borrower performed satisfactorily throughout with respect to the
polytechnics component. While the Bank's procurement procedures were demanding and the
Borrower's recruitment procedures rigid, these factors caused only minor delays in
construction and procurement. A policy analyst was not hired because of a decision taken in
the parent ministry.
Lessons learned
14.   Overall, during the period of implementation, the Project had a substantial impact on
the polytechnics but only a limited one at UoM. At the University, the interventions for
introducing quality assurance, establishing accreditation, training large numbers of academic
staff (selecting from universities internationally and not just in the U.K.) and expanding
facilities substantially were either not in line with historical and cultural traditions at the
University and, therefore, not fully owned, and/or overly complex and ambitious in relation to
implementation capacity at the time. Also, existing conditions of service provided inadequate
incentives in relation to the increases in teaching loads and expanded research production
envisaged under the Project. At the polytechnics on the other hand, because the Project was
prepared in line with capacity and needs, implementation was successful.
15. Lessons learned from the implementation experience are:
(a)  Ownership and commitment, if not strong at the time of identification, must be
systematically developed during preparation and verified at appraisal.
(b) Project objectives and targets should be scaled to match implementation capacity.
(c)  Bank procurement procedures need to be harmonized with those of the Government's
own Central Tender Board.
129



(d) The relationship between parent ministries, boards or governing councils, on the one
hand, and institutions themselves, on the other, needs to be clarified and perhaps
modified in order that responsibilities, as well as checks and balances, are understood.
16. The Government has committed itself to completion of the project using its own
resources. UoM is currently preparing a strategic plan, which is likely to suggest further
quality improvements and facilities expansion for enrollment increases. It is essential that it
also address issues of long-term affordability and sustainability. The tertiary sector is
diversifying. Private provision, distance education programs and changes in the role of other
TEIs are being developed, and these will have an impact on UoM. The Bank could assist with
an examination of the affordability of further expansion of the tertiary education sub-sector,
including UoM. It could also work with TEC on the overarching strategy needed to address
issues of governance, financing and cost recovery, as well as conditions of service, quality
assurance and accreditation. MTF has a solid plan for completing this component of the
Project, adding programs in response to market demand. The polytechnics could face
competition from private providers but should be able to sustain the investments made under
the Project.
Next steps
17. On the basis of the information gathered during the mission, as well as a study of World
Bank documents and supervision reports, the Bank will now prepare an ICR. This will be
presented to Government in draft by the middle of March 1999, to obtain factual corrections
before the report is finalized. The Government's own contribution to the ICR, which was
submitted to the Bank prior to the mission and discussed with relevant parties during the
mission, will be appended as an annex to the report. The final ICR will be submitted
officially to Government by the Bank in April.
18. Again, we thank the Minister of Education and Scientific Research, the Vice Chancellor
of the University, the Director of the Management Trust Fund, the PIU, and all others who
have aided us in our mission.
Mauritius, February 12, 1999

ANNEX C: HTEP GovERNMENT'S CONTRIBUTION To IMPLEMENTATION
COM[PLETION REPORT
HIGHER AND TECHNICAL EDUCATION PROJECT - Loan 3859-MAS
INTRODUCTION
During the implementation of the Education Sector Development Plan and as early as
1993, it was agreed that the expansion and upgrading of the University which was
envisaged at Appraisal would be undertaken more efficiently under a separate project.
Hence, the emergence of the Higher and Technical Education Project Loan 3859 MAS to
the tune of US$ 16 m. Subsequently in 1998, there was a cancellation of US$ 12 m. from
the loan.
ORIG1NAL PROJECT GOAL
The main objective of the HTEP is to support the Government's Education Sector
Programme for higher and polytechnic education, which aims to produce the human
resources required to support a more competitive economy. The project will help
produce higher and middle level manpower in the sciences, engineering, technology and
management, who in turn will have the capacity to raise the quality, cost effectiveness
and diversity of Mauritius industrial and service sectors. In addition, the expansion of
post graduate studies and research at the University, supported by the project, will
produce new knowledge to develop Mauritius into the financial and technological hub it
plans to become in the Indian Ocean.
SPECIFIC PROJECT OBJECTIVE                        ;
The project will assist the Government programme by:
(a) Strengthening the University of Mauritius, and
(b) Rationalising polytechnic education.
A. UNIVERSITY COMPONENT
Accomplishment of the Goal - General and Global
Strengthening the UOM:
(i)  Upgrading the quality of education through upgrading staff and facilities, thus
making UOM more attractive for Mauritian students;
(ii) Increasing the number of quality graduates in all fields with particular emphasis on
science, engineering and management;
133



(iii) Improving links with employers to make the curriculum more relevant to national
needs and increase the marketability of graduates;
(iv) Developing a viable post graduate education and research programme; and
(v) Enhancing the efficiency of the UOM.
Global Assessment
The University of Mauritius feels that the project did not take off as it should because
from the outset, the staff, right from the grassroot level, were not motivated nor
committed to it. They considered that the project did not emanate from them, it was
imposed on them, hence some lukewarm approach. Again, although Bank officials
mentioned that there would be flexibility in implementing the Staff Appraisal Report, this
was not done. The HTEP contained several deficiencies, even at its conception e.g.
unrealistic figures for Staff Development, no funding beyond 2000 A.D. It is not always
possible to draw up and rigidly adhere to a plan covering a five year period. The mid-
term review achieved little, mainly because it was quickly followed by the cancellation of
the loan. The procurement procedures were also very complex and time consuming, and
were therefore demotivating. Despite these negative points, the main value of the HTEP
was that it committed Government, with the World Bank, to sustain certain activities over
a considerable period of time so as to improve all aspects of function and activity at the
UOM. The project contains objectives which are true and relevant to the UOM and its
significance come to light at a time when the decision was taken to cancel the loan. In
fact the project outlines the direction which the University should take. It is felt that if
the project were to have been implemented over a large period of time, in over a 10 year
period, the objectives would have been achieved.
Accomplishment of Specific Project Objectives
Enhancing quality
University accreditation is initially proceeding by accreditation of courses by overseas
professional institutions.  No accreditation visit to a particular faculty, or to the
University as a whole, has so far taken place.
For a considerable time, there was a problem of interpretation on the meaning of
accreditation. For the UOM, the initial approach is mainly to get accreditation from
professional bodies e.g. U.K. Engineering Councils. A major drawback lately was the
passing away of the Pro Vice-Chancellor responsible for that part of the project.
However, as part of the exercise, a quality assurance system is being put in place. Its
introduction at all stages and for all courses is very important in relation to content,
context, etc.
The extension of the Library and the Faculty of Engineering has unfortunately not taken
off. Delay is due originally to the time taken by TEC to commission a Space Audit
Report for the University. Once the report was available after more than some 16
134



months. Procedures started off very slowly and with considerable confusion, but
Architectural contract was in due course awarded to Ramphul Associates. Owing to
certain problems mentioned by the World Bank, progress came to a complete halt in
February 1998. The request for doubling the original size of construction works
compounded the problem because of tender procedures involved as well as the need to
decide whether to construct in one or two phases.
Deliveries of equipment needed have generally fully been achieved, although problems
arose in a couple of cases. The UOM felt that they should have been more involved in
the award of contracts and taken a more significant lead in the procurement exercises, so
that University procedures would have been adopted. However, the real situation is that
University was not very co-operative, and junior technical staff were sent to the PIU to
evaluate highly sophisticated equipment.
Of some 8,000 book titles requested for purchase since 1996, only some 2,000 volumes
have been delivered, although another consignment of some 2,000 volumes is arriving
shortly.
The reason for the slow progress is due to the fact that there was much early confusion
about, and revision of, booklist formats. Also 6ertain important information about the
books viz: correct titles, BB SN, was not available from the University, hence it took time
to muster all the correct data.
Staff Upgrading
This is an on-going activity, but is well behind SAR target. To-date, the project has
supported training for 47 academics for a total of 58 study visits overseas.
The targets for this component, by year, given in the SAR are unrealistically high.
Indeed the source of the data is disputed by the UOM. Although the targets have been
revised downwards by a consultant of IDP Australia, yet it is still a cause of concern.
Even with the provision of local funds, this aspect is causing severe problems, especially
for academics registered overseas, whose studies will continue after 2000 A.D. Failure to
meet targets is also due to difficulties in release from teaching duties, less than optimum
progress in research, family commitments, age and other factors.
Recruitment of University Staff/Interim Faculty
This is on-going. From1995 to 1998, the UOM has recruited a total of 41 new
academics; this activity is on target. However, the University has faced the problem of
unavailability of qualified staff in certain scarcity areas.
Regarding the Interim Faculty, this has fared more or less well so far, if slowly. On the
other hand, the salary packages given the SAR are not acceptable in the Mauritian
context. As a result only local professional packages plus some inducement can be
offered, and the total package is probably not sufficient to attract academics from the
developed world.
135



Only 2 faculty development seminars have been held. So progress must be considered
slow. 2 others were in the pipeline, but had to be revised and expanded as a result of
Bank's comments.
Efficiency of Resource Use
All UOM courses are now modular.
Increase Overall Enrolments and Access to Science, Engineering, Management
Programmes
The UOM total student enrolment has witnessed a tremendous leap forward. The number
of qualified applicants has increased significantly over the past two years, and is greater
than the number of new students places available each year. The enrolment figure at
1998 is 3,680 compared to 3,220 in the SAR; hence this is above the SAR target. Except
for the Faculty of Law and Management, the other Faculties have recorded student
enrolments which are over SAR targets.
Postgraduate Research and Research Fund
There are presently 110 students (including staff ) enrolled on M.Phil/Ph.D. research
programmes. However no progress has been observed as far as using the Research Fund
is concerned. Plans for faculty and students research were drafted but could not be
implemented due to lack of agreement between the UOM and the Central Tender Board
about procedures and funding of consumable materials.
B. POLYTECHNIC COMPONENT
This component will help to:-
(a)  Improve quality of polytechnic education by upgrading admission and curriculum
standards, teaching materials and equipment provision, and staff training programme,
(b) Expand polytechnic education by establishing a new engineering programme at
Rose Hill and a key non-engineering programme such as accounts and business at
Droopnath Ramphul; and
(c)  Improve management and labour market linkages by strengthening the
Management Trust Fund.
Global Assessment
The responsibilities of the Management Trust Fund have been extended to manage more
polytechnics with a view to providing technicians and higher technicians for both the
service and manufacturing sectors of the Mauritian economy. New polytechnics have
been launched and all of them are meeting the objectives for which they have been set up.
Linkages with enterprises have greatly improved.  However, the participation of
industries in the management of courses needs further improvement with a view to
136



absorbing the trained manpower available on the market. Furthermore, the input of
industries is needed to diversify the courses offered in the polytechnics.
The polytechnics are gradually taking over from the UOM some of its diploma courses.
Although the primary aim is to train students for the world of work, some students would
be more interested to complete a degree course at the UOM. At present there is no
pathway leading to this achievement. Linkages with the UOM will have to be improved.
Accomplishment of Specific Project Objectives
Admission of students at the polytechnic has not been effected on an entrance
examination basis but by selection in the light of HSC/A level results and in order of
merit. New curricula have been developed to provide a balance between basic and
applied science.
Internship of students in industries has taken place on a regular basis. They have been
posted in industries for a period of six to eight weeks per academic year. Unfortunately,
similar achievement has not been possible in the case of teachers. A reluctance on the
part of industries has been noted in this direction. However, signs of a more positive
attitude are evident at the horizon, since the partnership between public and private sector
is improving.
Two workshops of short duration, i.e. three days, have been organised in Rose Hill and in
Pamplemousses. The field covered is in Engineering: in Rose Hill the workshop dealt
with "Cost of Reduction of Energy" whereas in Pamplemousses the subject was
"Application of Software Information in Information Technology".
No Policy Analyst has been recruited despite the holding of two exercises. In fact no
suitable candidate could be identified for the post. However, management and teaching
staff are being trained by foreign consultants from Australia and France to carry out
tracer studies.
Faculty/Industry Advisory Committees in each field, 4 in Pamplemousses and 3 in Rose
Hill have been set up. These committees are made up of members of the public and
private sector on a 50-50% basis and they meet regularly once a month.
Expansion of Polytechnic
The Droopnath Ramphul Polytechnic in Pamplemousses, the non-engineering
polytechnic, is operational since May 1995, offering courses in IT and Business
Administration with options in Marketing, Accountancy, and Banking and Financial
Services. On the other hand the polytechnic set up under joint venture Mauritius/Agence
Frangaise de Developpement has a department of Civil Engineering in lieu of the
Computer Hardware Engineering field as proposed in the Appraisal. This change in
policy is because the IVTB has been running such a course since 1996, hence the need to
avoid a duplication. This Civil Engineering course, started in 1998, is taking over the
diploma course of the UoM.
137



Enrolment of students now stands at 760, short of the projected target of 1,550. The
reason for this low enrolment may be attributed to the delay in the completion of
construction works at the Polytechnic. In fact, the works will be completed in December,
1998. It will then be possible to accommodate more studerits since the demand is ever
present.
The low intake has had a bearing on the production of graduates either with 3 year
training after 0 level or 2 year training after A level. The figures are 52 and 81
respectively compared to the targeted figures of 320 and 120. It is to be noted that the
under achievement in respect of those on a 3 year course is also due to the fact that the
course has been discontinued since 1996.
Improving Management and Labour Market Linkages
Since 1994, the Technical School Management Trust Fund Act has been amended to give
greater autonomy and responsibilities to the Trust Fund and the Board. The MTF Board
comprising members of both public and private sectors on a 50-50% basis meets
regularly once a month.
A Management Information System has not yet been set up. This is due on the one hand
to the absence of a Policy Analyst and on the other to the exorbitant fees that was claimed
by a private consultant. However, work along the line is being done at institutional level.
Every 2 months, meetings are held with the private sector to discuss the market trends
and surveys so that appropriate action could eventually be taken for the curriculum to be
updated by the relevant committee.
C. THE ROLE OF GOVERNMENT
Government pledged to support the Higher Technical Education Project (HTEP) by
providing counterpart funds. In fact counterpart funds were regularly made available in
the annual budget and no undue delay was ever met in the payment of bills.
A Ministerial Steering Committee chaired by the Minister of Education was to provide
policy and operational guidance for the implementation of the project. It would meet
regularly to review the status of project implementation and resolve issues. The Steering
Committee met regularly, to start with once every fortnight and then once a month, and
progress was being closely monitored.
The Project Implementation Unit was to assume responsibility for all procurement
matters and for supervising civil works under the project. The PIU would also handle the
preparation of disbursement applications and keep the loan accounts. The University of
Mauritius as well as the Management Trust Fund would appoint a Component Co-
ordinator and Manager as well as an Accountant to assist in the project in their respective
capacities and liaise with the PIU for relevant actions. These requirements were fulfilled
by both UOM and the MTF.

D. THE ROLE OF THE BANK
Right from the beginning, the Bank has played a significant role in the preparation of the
Higher and Technical Education Project (HTEP) by providing professional support and
advice. The Bank identified consultants, prepared reports and statistical data prior to the
Appraisal report being finalised. Once the Loan Agreement was signed and became
operational, a supervision mission was regularly fielded to monitor progress.
The UOM felt that the World Bank had been exerting constant pressure to increase the
rate of disbursements. The UOM, on the other hand, had always tried to use any source
of funding judiciously and responsibly.
Coupled with the disbursement pressure, the Bank had always been unwilling to
reallocate funds between categories, e.g.  from Academic Staff Development to
equipment. Most of the major reallocations proposed by the UOM at mid-term review
were retained by the Bank; however, others were not, e.g. allocation for equipment needs
and maintenance of expensive equipment.
The World Bank strongly attempted to convince the UOM to turn to countries other than
U.K., especially for Academic Staff Development activities. The UOM's comments on
their long history and extensive links with many U.K. institutions, and their inability to
force academics to train elsewhere, were apparently not well received.
On wider aspects, the Bank did not seemed to appreciate that the UOM had to act within
local realities and constraints. A typical example is the huge salary proposed by the Bank
for Interim Faculty in order to attract overseas professors. When apprised of such
difficulties, the Bank did not fully appreciate their gravity.
The Management Trust Fund, though, felt that the involvement of World Bank was quite
positive. The MTF appreciated the contribution of the World Bank team in charge of the
project. The team was largely influential in getting the MTF to set up Faculty/Industry
Advisory Committees and initiate linkages with industries to train a work force needed in
enterprises.
Furthermore the World Bank intervened rapidly to approve overseas training programmes
for management and teaching staff and to make funds available for the procurement of
teaching and learning equipment and materials.
The supervision mission made important contributions to enable the proper monitoring
and development of the polytechnic component.
E. CONCLUSION
To conclude, it can be said that it has been a valuable and enriching experience to have
worked on this project. The collaboration and advice of the World Bank should enable us
to complete the project successfully, although we shall miss the presence of the
supervision mission team, especially in difficult stages of operation.
