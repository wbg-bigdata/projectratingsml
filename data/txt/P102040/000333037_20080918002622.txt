Benefits:               The measures supported by the proposed GRGC-2 are expected to have

Risks:                  Several risks could jeopardize the expected outcomes and benefits o f

                        which the budget i s heavily dependent. This risk i s mitigated by the
                        ongoing intense Bank staffs dialogue with the government, the IMF and
                        the multi-donor budget support group on macroeconomic policies and
                        the reformprogram. Second, implementing the PRS could be made more

                        targets or if substantial new and unavoidable spending requirements

                                        1. INTRODUCTION

 1.1    This program document proposes a Governance Reform and Growth Credit (GRGC-2)
for SDR 6.4 million ( U S 1 0 million equivalent). The proposed operation would be the second in
a programmatic series in support o f Sierra Leone's transition from post-conflict recovery to
sustainable development. The operation supports reform measures to: (i)             preserve the fiscal space
needed for poverty reduction; (ii)    promote efficiency, transparency and accountability in the use
o f public resources through enhanced public financial management and governance; and (iii)
improve the investment climate and promote exports. The programmatic series i s grounded upon
the government's Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS) and i s consistent with the Bank's 2006-09
Country Assistance Strategy (CAS).' The programmatic series also builds upon a wide range o f
analytical work on public financial management as well as sectoral policies and outcomes and
complements similar support provided by the AfDB, DflD and the EC.

 1.2    Sierra Leone's civil war ended in January 2002, just over six years ago. The United
Nations Peacekeeping Mission completed its phased withdrawal o f troops inDecember 2005, as
agreed by the United Nations Security Council. The United Nations (UN),however, continues to
support the country through its new Integrated Office. Parliamentary and Presidential elections
were heldinAugust 2007, with a Presidential run-off election inSeptember 2007. The elections,
judged to be free and fair, resulted ina transfer o fpower to the former oppositionparty. The new
leadership has placed strong emphasis on a campaign against corruption and more investment in
physical infrastructure to buttressongoing programs inhealth and education.

1.3     Sierra Leone has made substantial progress in consolidating social and economic security
for its people. The country has now embarked on a transition from post-conflict recovery to
broad-based growth and poverty reduction. Implementation o f the first full PRS began with the
2005 budget. Local councils (LC) beganimplementing their first budgets in 2005 following the
passage o f the Local Government Act and the election o f local governments in 2004 (the first
such elections in over 30 years). These developments have been complemented by a broad array
o freforms aimed at the promotion o ftransparent and inclusive governance.

1.4     Support from the donor community for Sierra Leone's programs has been quite strong.
Between 2001 and 2006, Sierra Leone received more foreign aid per-capita than almost any other
country. Much o f this aid came in the form o f direct budget support. InNovember 2006, four
donors (the African Development Bank (AfDB), the U.K. Department for International

1.5      Sierra Leone successhlly implemented a three-year arrangement under the IMF-

1.6      The challenge now i s to deepen the reform program during a period o f political
consolidation following the 2007 elections, and to do so in the context o f a difficult global
economic environment o frapidly rising fuel and food prices.


                                        2. COUNTRY CONTEXT

                      A. RECENTECONOMIC               DEVELOPMENTS         INSIERRA LEONE

1. Growth

2.1      Sierra Leone's strong recovery, which began in 2000, continued for an eighth unbroken
year into 2007 when real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) grew by an estimated 6.8 percent
despite highandrisingoil prices (see Table 2.1 below). Mucho fthis growth was concentrated in
the informal agricultural, fishing, mining, and services sectors that make up the bulk o f the
economy. Formal economic activity i s confined primarily to large scale mining, construction,
retail services, tourism, and government employment.

2. ExternalBalance

2.2     Sierra Leone has broadened its export base from an almost exclusive concentration on
diamonds in recent years to include rutile, bauxite, and a growing volume o f cash cropsS2
Estimated export growth was positive for all products in 2007 but slowed relative to both GDP
and performance inprevious years. The GDP share o f exports o f goods and non-factor services
fell to 21 percent in 2007 from 25 percent in 2006. Imports contracted by 4.3 percentage points
o f GDP, as a consequence o f falling exports and rising oil prices. The external current account
deficit (including official transfers) increased to -3.8 percent o f GDP from -3.5 percent o f GDP in
2006. Officialreserves reachedUS$208 million or 4.5 months o f import coverage.

2.3     The current account balance was financed mainly by concessional external assistance and
debt relief. InDecember 2006, Sierra Leone reached the Completion Point under the Enhanced
Heavily IndebtedPoor Countries (HIPC) initiative and, in so doing, also gained additional relief

2.6     Sierra Leone's broadly positive performance inmacroeconomic management masks some
emerging issues: revenues declined as a share o f GDP in 2007 and fell short o f ambitious
program targets, while weak expenditure commitment controls allowed a substantial
accumulation o f domestic arrears in2007; also, donor support was delayed in2007 on account of
fiduciary concerns. Inaddition, monetary policy may need to become more proactive inthe face
o f ongoing international food and fuel inflation and sporadic supply shocks. The government i s
acting to address these issues. Under the ongoing PRGF, an agreement was reached with the
IMF on a program for the remainder of 2008 aimed at consolidating macroeconomic
stabilization.   Revenue collection efforts have been stepped up by the National Revenue
Authority through improved customs valuation and the collection o f tax arrears. Discretionary
duty waivers and other tax exemptions have been reduced sharply. Also, additional legislative
changes have brought off-budget revenues from some ministries into the consolidated revenue
fund.The enactment of the Finance Billandsubsequent adoptiono frevenue generating measures
contained therein will support the government's revenue mobilization targets.

2.7     Expenditure controls are being strengthened and the Government has agreed that the
balance o f domestic arrears accumulated in 2007 would be cleared in 2008, within the 2008
resource envelope, after they are audited. Poverty reducing expenditures have been expanded to
include the provision o f emergency power following the weak performance o f the National
Power Authority between 2006 and 2007. The government i s also working to address donor
concerns about fiduciary arrangements, particularly the backlog o f published public accounts. As
o f July 2008, the Accountant General's Department had eliminated the backlog in public
accounts and is now current through 2007. The Office o f the Auditor-General has completed the
audits o f the public accounts for the years 2004 to 2006. The audited accounts for 2002 to 2006
as well as the un-audited accounts for 2007 have been laidbefore Parliament. All these accounts
have been published. A recently completed Public Expenditure and Financial Accountability
(PEFA) Performance Report and a Procurement Audit report, both commissioned on behalf o f
the multi-donor budget support group, established that the quality o f Sierra Leone's public
financial management is on par with the average performance o f other IDA countries and
identifiedaspects o fpublic financial management (PFM) inneed o f additional strengthening.

4. Inflation

2.8     The increases in food prices, and the pass-through effects o f the increases in oil prices,
have already resulted in an acceleration o f inflation in2007 through the second quarter o f 2008.
The 12 month inflation rate for June 2008 increased to 16.8 percent from 13.8 percent in
December 2007 and 8.3 percent in December 2006. According to data from Statistics Sierra
Leone (the government's statistical authority), Freetown consumer prices for imported rice show
an accelerating rate o f inflation: the cost o f imported rice in December 2007 was 21 percent
higher than in the preceding December while the June 2008 price was 49 percent higher than the
preceding June. Similarly, the price o f wheat flour in December 2008 price was 11 percent
higher than a year earlier and the June 2008 price was 28 percent higher thanthe preceding June.
The official price o fpetrol increased by 27 percent between June-2007 and June-2008 following


                                                  4

large increases inpreceding years. The Government mitigated some o f these price increases by
reducing import tariffs on rice, flour, and petroleum products. These actions, however, are
unlikely to be sustainable in the face o f increased international prices, low fiscal revenues and
increasing social tensions.

5. Monetary and Exchange Rate Policy

2.9      Reductions in the fiscal deficit as a share o f GDP have allowed for a declining trend in
the growth rate o f reserve money from 30 percent in 2002 (and higher in earlier years) to 13
percent in 2004 and 11 percent in 2006. This pattern was broken only in 2005 and 2007. In
2005, aid inflows could not be fully absorbed, resulting in a build-up o f foreign assets so that
reserve money growth inthat year was 24 percent. In2007, reserve money exceeded the program
target (26 percent against 15.6 percent) due to unexpectedly highbank reserves caused mainly by
the establishment o f two new foreign banks. Since March 2008, the IMF has reclassified the
exchange rate system as a "conventional peg" system to reflect the relative stability o f the
nominal exchange rate managed through weekly auctions by the Bank o f Sierra Leone (BSL).
The nominal, end-of-period rate on treasury bills increased to 21.3 percent in 2007 from 14.2
percent in2006. The nominal exchange rate depreciated in 2007 enough to offset inflation, thus
allowing the real effective exchange rate to remain broadly stable.


                         B. MACROECONOMIC FOR2008-2010OUTLOOK

2.10     The global economic prospects for traded goods suggest that Sierra Leone will continue
to be subjected to high fuel and food prices for the next few years. The terms o f trade are
expected to move against Sierra Leone in 2008, declining by 13.5 percent, as imported fbel and
food prices continue to accelerate faster than export commodity prices, before showing slight
gains in 2009 and 2010 (see Table 2.2). Crude oil prices are forecast to rise by 52 percent in
2008. Eventhough the price i s then expected to fall by 2 percent in2009 and another 7 percent
in 2010, the price in 2010 will still be almost double the 2005 price. The outlook for rice is
equally stark: the international price is expected to rise by 99 percent in 2008 and will be 1.7
times the 2005 price by 2010. Similar outcomes are forecast for sugar and wheat.

detailed in the 2005-07 PRS and the IMF-supported PRGF program include the maintenance o f

                                          D. LESSONS  LEARNED

4.10     The design o fthe proposed operation has benefited from a number o f lessons drawn from
guidance from Operations Policy and Country Services (OPCS), and the implementation o f the
development policy operations ERRC I-IV and GRGG-1. Key lessons are highlightedbelow:

    Experience has shown that the potential benefits o f development policy operations during
    fragile transitions often justify the risks o f engagement12(OPCS, 2005). In some cases, the
    international community reaches a consensus that the benefits o f stabilizing a fragile
    transition are worth the risks and looks to the Bank to play a key role inhelping to coordinate
    and channel financial support inthese countries. Operations in this context usually support
    an extended state-building and recovery process following a programmatic approach. When
    used flexibly, the programmatic approach can accommodate uncertainties as regards
    implementation capacity andprovide a framework for policy development.

    Political transitions can affect PRS implementation both before and after elections,
    particularly as a consequence o f the natural turn-over o f ministerial staff, This led to a
    reduction in the number o f triggers proposed for the final operation in the programmatic
    series.

    Only actions critical for achieving results should be selected as prior actions or conditions for
    disbursement.     The number o f donor benchmarks, including those from the Bank, was
    reduced to 27 in2008 from 42 in2006. Further efforts to consolidate the PAF on the basis o f
    criticality will be pursued.

    Lending for development policy should not rely on conditionality alone to catalyze and
    implement reforms. To be fully effective, the policy dialogue must be supported by other

5.18     Government Budgeting and Accountability Act (GBAA). Several damaging amendments

communities.        The new law was enacted by Parliament on February 4, 2005. Supporting
Financial Management Regulations were adopted inJune 2007.

5.47     Current situation and challenges. Imports accounted for 28 percent o f GDP in2007 and
exceed exports by 7 percentage points. This large trade gap i s the consequence o f an old anti-
export policy bias prior to the war, war-time export losses and highpost-conflict reconstruction
requirements. Sierra Leone i s a member o f two regional economic groupings: the Economic
Community o f West African States (ECOWAS) and the Mano River Union (MRU). The vast
majority o ftrade is conducted with Europe and the UnitedStates, inbothdirections.

5.48     The small market within Sierra Leone cannot, by itself, generate enough demand for
sustained high rates o f growth: sales in overseas markets should therefore be considered an
essential element o f the national growth strategy. Sierra Leone usedto have a moderately diverse
export base consisting o f unprocessed diamonds, iron ore, bauxite, rutile, fish, palm oil, coffee,
cocoa and tourism. Exports o f cash crops fell, well before the war, as a result o f inappropriate
policies. Accessible iron ore was gradually exhausted while the rutile and bauxite mines were
shut down due to the conflict. Thus, recent export activity has been limited to diamonds with
cocoa holding a distant second place. More work remains to be done to restore cash crop exports
to historical levels and then to expand and diversifj them. Fish exports are significant but little
o f the revenue i s captured by Sierra Leone. It should also be noted that Sierra Leone has leased
some offshore blocks for oil exploration.

private sector was only 5 percent o f GDP in 2005 compared to an average o f around 17 percent
inSub-Saharan Africa and 65 percent inOECD countries. There is no credit reporting database.
Interest rates are market determined. The end-year Treasury-bill rate was 16 percent compared to
a year-end inflation rate o f 13.8 percent. The spread between bank loan and deposit rates
remains high and has been increasing due in part to the risks posed by weak property rights and
judicial system. The capital market consists mainly o f short-term government debt instruments.
Only a few company equityshares have beenissued and the only trading that has taken place has

        d. Reform of key public enterprises

5.65    Current situation and challenges. The poor management o f several key public
enterprises has been a significant drag on economic growth and job creation. Among these
enterprises are the utilities controlling the supply o f power, water and non-cellular
telecommunication. Other choke points include the public entities controlling road maintenance
and the port operations. The challenge for the government will be to ensure that these constraints
are loosened up as quickly as possible. While better management under government ownership
can be an option, experience from around the world shows that some form o f commercialization
or privatization (whether through outright sale, concessions, contracts or other means) is usually
more successful.

5.66    Power, One o f the most fundamental constraints onjob creation and poverty reduction in
Sierra Leone i s access to electric power. The production o f power by NPA has essentially
collapsed: production in 2006 was estimated to be only 3.7 megawatts compared to the already
very low 14 megawatts in 2002. In 2005, NPA auditors concluded that continued operations
were not sustainable in the face o f its accumulated losses and weak cash-flow. It i s striking in
this regard that Sierra Leoneans pay some o f the highest tariff rates per kilowatt-hour in the
region, between US$O.19 and US$0.28, yet the revenue collected i s not sufficient to cover costs
o f US$0.37 per kilowatt-hour.60 The problem can be traced to several sources: inadequate
maintenance, technical line losses, under-investment, vandalism, a bloated workforce, and a
fixed tariff structure that does not automatically adjust for changes infuel costs.61 There are also
fundamental weaknesses in accounting procedures, particularly for revenue collection: some 40
percent o f energy generated inrecent years was not paid for.

5.67   Road Maintenance. The poor condition o f the road network i s another critical constraint
on growth and poverty reduction. Only 8 percent o f the approximately 5,200 mile (8,400 km)
road network i s paved and little more i s considered to be in good condition, with the
consequence that the remaining network i s under constant stress from annual rains and
deterioration from normal use. To promote better trunk road maintenance, the government has
established a Road Maintenance Fund financed by a fuel levy. Severe under-funding o f
programmed maintenance has resulted from massive government demands for unprogrammed
and emergency work and delays in adjusting fuel levy funding. The most recent adjustment in
the levy took place inMay 2006.

5.68   Ports. Low productivity and inefficiencies at Freetown port (SLPA) increase cost o f
imported and exported goods and act as constraint to the economic development o fthe country.

6.1      The design o f the programmatic series, including the key reforms supported by the

                                        B. POVERTYAND SOCIAL IMPACTS

6.2      The measures supported by the proposed GRGC-2 are expected to have a significant
positive direct impact on poverty reduction. First, the improvement in the investment climate
supported by the proposed operation should help sustain the present high GDP growth rate and
promote exports.          Second, the strengthening of public financial management would enhance
efficiency, transparency and accountability in public investments and service delivery. Such
measures would also increase the country's absorption capacity for external resources. Third, the
support provided to the decentralization o f public services should have the effect o f reducing
inequality. Indeed, the data from the 2003/04 household survey and pilot CWIQ show that the



                                                            49

 districts outside the Western Area, where the capital o f Freetown i s located, generally suffer
 higher levels o f poverty and reduced levels o f public services. Data from the 2207 and 2008
 Service Delivery Perception Surveys suggest there have been moderate gains in the quality of
 educational and health services in many districts. Yet, there i s clear room for improvement: the
 2008 Poverty Diagnostic finds that educational services remain pro-rich when adjusted for the
 quality o f teachers while cost remains an obstacle to health care for many o f the poor. The
 Poverty Diagnostic also finds that the proposed increase inthe electricity tariff could be pro-poor
 if it results in the release of resources that could be better targeted to the poor through other
poverty reducing programs.71 There are two reasons for this. First, the poor have very little
 access to electricity: most live in rural areas outside Freetown. Second, the current budgetary
 allocation for fuel for emergency power i s the single largest item in the 2008 budget largely
because the current tariff i s insufficient to cover operating costs. This denies resources to other,
better targeted, poverty reducing programs. The data also show that those households relying
primarily on income from artisanal mining are among the poorest of the poor.72 Thus, the
 support provided for decentralization (including the transfer o fbudgetaryhnds to local councils)
 and to the mining community, particularly cadastral and extension services, should help reduce
 inequality inaddition to reducing conflicts over property rights.

 6.3     The PRSP calls for another household survey (currently planned to begin in 2008/09) as
part o f its monitoring and evaluation strategy. The PRSP monitoring and evaluation strategy also
includes subsequent rounds o f the CWIQ. Both will be especially helpful in assessingthe impact
o fthe program.

                                        C. ENVIRONMENTAL              ASPECTS

6.4      IDA has determined that the specific actions supported under the proposed GRGC-2 are

mining sector which could have an indirect, medium-term impact if they lead to increased
investmentsinthe sector. A Strategic Environment and Social Assessment (SESA)conducted in

social priorities in the mining sector have the following weaknesses: the legal and regulatory
framework for the mining sector i s often lacking specificity, leaving interpretation to be
determined on a case by case basis, often by the involved stakeholders themselves - creating
conflict o f interest risks; laws pertaining to the sector are diffused across statutes emanating from
various ministries, leading to problems o f consistency and poorly defined responsibilities; and
implementation o f laws and regulations i s consistently weak. Given the above weaknesses, the
SESA recommended three areas o f focus to promote the sustainable development o f the sector:
(i)strengthening environmentalgovernance; (ii)enhancingbenefitsto communities; and(iii)
effectively incorporating the artisanal mining sector in the reform. The first area was addressed
by the July 2008 adoption of legislation establishing a new Environmental ProtectionAgency. A

                                  G. RISKS AND RISKMITIGATION

6.13    Several risks could jeopardize the expected outcomes and benefits o f this operation, but
measures are being taken to mitigate them.

6.14    The degree o f commitment by the authorities to sustain good macroeconomic
performance could affect the implementation o f reform policies and programs.            Failure to
maintain macroeconomic stability and move the reform program forward would result in a
shortfall in donor funding, on which the budget i s heavily dependent. This risk i s mitigated by
the ongoing intense Bank staffs dialogue with the government, the IMF and the multi-donor
budgetsupport group on macroeconomic policies andthe reformprogram.

6.15    Implementing the PRS could be made more difficult in the event that revenues or grants
fall significantly short o f targets or if substantial new and unavoidable spending requirements
arise. These risks are mitigated by the Government's manifested commitment to adhere to the
already established principle o f protecting priority expenditures through reductions in less
essential spending.

6.16    Weak institutional capacity or corruption could hamper the implementation o f the
reforms supported by the proposed operation or the broader Poverty Reduction Strategy. This
risk was evident in late 2007 and early 2008 when capacity in the central ministries and local
councils was temporarily reduced as a consequence o f the natural replacement o f various key
officials as a result o f the political transition. MOFED officials indicated they have largely
resolved the problem through intensive dialogue and monitoring with the implementing
ministries and local councils. In addition, inadequate training in procurement procedures could
slow the effective broadening o f procurement reform, weak accounting skills in district
governments could make it harder to account for the use o f resources transferred to local
councils, while weak capacity could slow the expansion o f the cadastral system for mining.
These risks are mitigated by the choice and design o f the supported measures which are
calibrated to existing capacity, the provision o f extensive technical assistancethrough ongoing or
planned projects (including IRCB support for training in procurement and accounting skills and
the World Bank technical assistance project for the mining sector currently under preparation)
and growing involvement o fcivil society inoversight activities.

6.17    Risks from exogenous shocks and external factors cannot be ruled out. These include
regional instability or a continued rapid rise in international commodity prices, especially food
and fuel. Although there is little that the Government can do to mitigate the risks from sub-
regional instability, conditions have improved considerably inrecent years, especially due to the
successful political transition in Liberia, and the recent peace agreement in C8te d'Ivoire.


                                                  53

Moreover, continued international support for post-conflict reconstruction in Liberia and the
peace process in CBte d'Ivoire would reduce somewhat the risk that political and economic
instability would spill over across borders. The Bankwill continue to monitor the situation. The
food price shock i s being mitigated through additional budget support (see para. 4.2) to help
protect the provision o f basic services andthrough the expansion o f safety net programs. Despite
these efforts, there i s a risk that social upheaval due to food price increases could distract the
Government from its macroeconomic and reform agendas. The Bank will continue to monitor
the food price situation and assess the need for further support ifneeded.

                            RecentEconomicDevelopments(2004-06)

4.       The Sierra Leonean economy recovered strongly duringthe post conflict period reflecting
continued buoyant activities in the key sectors-agriculture, mining, construction and services.
Real GDP growth averaged 7.4 percent during2004-06.

5.       Reflecting the slow growth in money supply and relative stability in the exchange rate,
endperiodinflation declinedto 8.3 percent in2006 from 14.4 percent in2004.

6.       External sector performance continued to improve, largely on account o f the strong
growth inmineral exports including diamonds, rutile and bauxite. Exports grew strongly to 24.9
percent o f GDP in 2006 from 13.1 percent in 2004. After recovering strongly during 2001-02,
imports slowed down during 2004-06 as reconstruction andhumanitarian activities reduced. As a
result, the current account deficit, including official transfers, narrowed to 3.5 percent o f GDP in
2006 from 5.8 percent in2004. Gross internationalreserves reached the equivalent o f 4.7 months
o f import cover in2006. The nominal exchange rate to the U S dollar depreciated during 2004 to
2005 but has been relatively stable since 2006.

7.       Government took corrective measures to address the fiscal shocks that occurred during
2004-06. The overall budget deficit, on commitment basis, and including grants moved to 2.7
percent o f GDP in2006 from a deficit o f 3.5 percent in2004.

8.      Monetary policy mirrored developments in the fiscal sector. Growth in reserve money
slowed down to 10.7 percent in 2006 from 12.6 percent in 2004 as the budget deficit declined.
The three-month Ttreasury bill rate declined from 28 percent in 2004 to 14.2 percent in 2006
reflecting inpart the fall ininflation rates. The Bank o f Sierra Leone and the Ministryo fFinance
established a Memorandum o f Understanding in October 2006 that sets out a schedule for
converting non-interest bearing securities heldby the BSL to interest bearingassets ina way that
minimizes adverse fiscal andmonetary consequences.

                              MacroeconomicPerformancein2007

9.      In 2007, domestic output continued to expand, reflecting increased activities in the
agricultural, mining, construction and services sectors. Real GDP i s estimated to have grown by
6.8 percent in 2007. After declining to single digits in late 2006, inflationary pressures re-
emerged in 2007. Year-on year inflation, as measured by the CPI for Freetown, rose from 8.3
percent inDecember 2006 to 13.8 percent inDecember 2007, due partly to shortage inthe supply
o fbasic commodities owing to social unrest inGuinea, a major trading partner, inearly 2007 and
also duringthe period leading to the general elections inAugust and September 2007.

10.     Exports continued to grow, albeit at a slower rate in2007 due mainly to the suspensiono f
operations by KoiduHoldings, the Kimberlite miningcompany. However, exports of bauxite and
other minerals increased during the period. Imports grew to 28.3 percent o f GDP in 2007,
resulting in a slight increase in the current account deficit, excluding grants, to 3.8 percent o f
GDP in 2007 from 3.5 percent o f GDP in 2006. Gross foreign reserves reached 4.5 months o f



                                                 56

import cover. The nominal and real effective exchange rates o f the Leone against the US dollar
remainedrelatively stable duringthe year.

 11.     Continuing the trend from previous years, the overall budget deficit, including grants was
further reduced to 2 percent in 2007. Inan effort to preserve macroeconomic stability inthe face
o f shortfalls in revenue and delays in the disbursement o f external budget support, Government
adopted a cash budget management system in April 2007. Consistent with the cash-basedbudget
management system, efforts were made to match expenditures to revenues collected during the
period. The cash-budget management system enabled the Government to meet the quantitative
performance criteria under the PRGF-supported programme for end June and end December
2007.

                Medium-termMacroeconomicObjectivesandPoliciesfor 2008-10

12.      The key medium-term macroeconomic objectives will include the maintenance o f
sustained high real growth, a stable macroeconomic environment and a revitalized structural
reform agenda. The macroeconomic framework projects real GDP growth o f 6 percent during
2008-2010 underpinned by broad based sectoral growth.               The planned investment in
infrastructure and improvements inthe business climate are expected to contribute to the growth
prospects o f the economy.

13.      Inflation will remain inthe lower double digits until2010, assuming the lagged effects o f
rising food and energy prices. In response to this situation, Government has reduced import
tariffs on rice, flour and sugar as well as import and excise duties on petroleum products.
Government is also examining options to expand social safety programmes to vulnerable groups.
Inthemediumterm, Government willincreasefoodproductionbyprovidingincreasedsupport to
the agricultural sector and by investing in infrastructure. The external current account deficit,
(excluding official transfers) is projected to narrow in the medium-term as imports growth slows
down. Gross external reserves are programmed to remain in the range o f 3.5 to 4.0 months o f
import cover.

                                            Fiscal Policy

14.     Macroeconomic stability in the medium term will be underpinned by prudent fiscal
policy. The main focus o f the policy framework will be to restore macroeconomic stability
through fiscal consolidation aimed at enhancing domestic revenuemobilisation.

15.     The medium-term fiscal objective will aim at reducing the domestic primary budget
deficit to contain the domestic debt, while reorientingpublic spendingtowards infrastructure and
poverty spending. Domestic revenue i s projected to increase from 10.8 percent o f GDP in 2007
to 13.4 percent of GDP in2010. This will be realized through the continued strengthening o f the
National Revenue Authority by operationalizing the NRA Modernization Plan. The NRA will
put in place measures to mobilize additional revenues including undertaking field audits,
strengtheningo f tax enforcement, intensificationof arrears collection and the stepping up o f anti-
smuggling activities. Government will introduce the Value Added Tax locally known as the



                                                 57

Goods and Services Tax (GST). The Government will also significantly narrow the scope o f
import duty and sales tax exemptions.

16.     Government will continue to strengthen public expenditure management, particularly in
containing the public sector wage bill while meetingtargets for poverty spending. The domestic
primary deficit i s programmed to decline over the medium term to no more than 1.5 percent o f
GDP by 2010. Government will strengthen budget execution procedures, especially commitment
controls. Inthis regard all ministries, departments, and agencies (MDAs) will be requiredto seek
prior approval from the Ministry o f Finance and Economic Development before entering into
contracts and or issuing Local Purchase Orders. Inaddition, expenditure commitments by MDAs
will be strictly controlled against the procurement planapproved by the Ministryo f Finance and
Economic Development and within the cumulative allocations for the year.

                               Monetary and Financial Sector Policies

17.     Monetary policy will continue to be conducted using market-based instruments o f
monetary policy, mainly open market operations, with the objective o f containing inflation.
Government will continue to strengthen the operational capacity o f the BSLby recapitalizing the
Bank and providing it with additional interest bearing securities to support monetary policy

18.     Sustaining the high GDP growth rates in the medium term critically depends on higher

IMFExecutive Board CompletesSecond Review Under PRGFArrangementfor Sierra Leone,
Approves Extension of Arrangement and US$7.2 Million Disbursement
Press Release No. OW166
July 7,2008

The Executive Board o f the International Monetary Fund(IMF) has completed the second review of
Sierra Leone's performance under a Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) arrangement.
The Executive Board also approved a one-year extension of the PRGF arrangement to 2010.The
completion o f this review allows for the disbursement o f SDR 4.4 million (about US$7.2 million),
which would bring total disbursements under the arrangement to SDR 13.5 million (about US$22.1
million).

The Executive Board also granted waivers for the non-observance o f three quantitative performance
criteria on the basis o f corrective measures taken and the temporary nature o f the deviations from the
quantitative targets.

The PRGF arrangement for Sierra Leone was approved on May 10,2006 in an amount equivalent to
SDR 31.1million (about US$50.9 million), to support the government's 2006-08 economic program.
The extension ofthe programfrom three years to four years should provide sufficient time to achieve
the program objectives (see Press Release No. 06/94).

Following the Executive Board's discussion, Mr. Murilo Portugal, Deputy Managing Director and
Acting Chair, said:

"Economic growth in Sierra Leone remained robust in 2007 and early 2008, led by solid agriculture
and miningproduction and buoyant activity inthe construction and service sectors. Inflation, though
rising, was relatively contained in the face o f surging global food and energy prices. Performance
under the PRGF-supported program was mixed in the second half o f 2006 through much o f 2007.
Significant revenue shortfalls, exacerbated by delayed external budgetary assistance, had
complicated budget execution. These developments contributed to a severe compression o f priority
spending and the accumulation o f domestic arrears. The strong commitment o f the authorities to
address the sources o f these setbacks and move forward with key structural reforms in the period
ahead is welcome, and the recent improvement inprogram implementation is encouraging."

"The proposed fiscal stance for 2008 seeks to recapture the momentum lost in mobilizing domestic
revenue, and strikes a good balance between expandingpoverty-reducing and infrastructure spending
and preventing the accumulation o f public debt. The authorities are committed to staying the course,
with wide-ranging measures to make the National Revenue Authority more efficient andbroaden the
tax base. Firm control over expenditure commitments by ministries, departments, and agencies will
provide critical support to the objective o f domestic arrears clearance while creating fiscal space to
address essential social needs."

"The Bank o f Sierra Leone will focus on strengtheningits management o f liquidity and monitoring
closely developments in the monetary aggregates, given the need to contain the second-round effects
o frisingimport prices for food and fuel."



                                                  102

"First steps have been taken to advance structural reforms, in particular in public financial
management and the financial sector. The authorities will work to ensure transparency in all public
procurement contracts, strengthen the Anti-Corruption Commission, and improve the financial
viability o f public utilities in order to promote good governance, accountability, and sustained high
growth," Mr.Portugal said.

The PRGF is the IMF'sconcessional facility for low-income countries. PRGF loans carry an annual
interest rate o f 0.5 percent and are repayable over 10 years with a 5%-year grace period on principal
payments.
