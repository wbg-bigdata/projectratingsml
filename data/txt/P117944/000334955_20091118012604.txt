                                 I.      INTRODUCTION

1.      This Program Document presents a proposed Economic Governance Reform
Operation (EGRO 111) for the Republic o f Haiti in the amount o f SDRS million (US$12.5
million equivalent). The proposed single-tranche operation is expected to be followed by a
fourth EGRO within the context o f a programmatic series. The series o f operations would
support policies and reforms aimed at: (i)    creating fiscal space for priority expenditures by
reducing inefficiencies in the electricity sector; (ii)
                                                      improving public finance management by
prioritizing allocations to key sectors in poverty reduction and growth, and improving
information system to enhance revenue mobilization capacity; and (iii)         strengthening the
framework for public procurement. The operation was designed in the context o f improved
donor harmonization on budget support agreed with the Government in April 2009.

2.      The grant would provide further support to the Government's reforms initiated
under the previous operations - EGRO I (FY2005) and EGRO I1 (FY2007) - and the
HIPC completion point. The previous operations supported reforms in governance, the civil
service, public finance management, and public procurement. These include: (i)improving
budget preparation and execution and monitoring through efforts to improve alignment o f the
budget with the DSNCRP, reduction in the use o f discretionary ministerial accounts (comptes
courants), and the establishment o f an automated system to track expenditures; (ii) improving
the control and reporting systems including the deployment o f financial controllers and public
accountants in line ministries, submission o f the Government accounts to the Court o f
Accounts and the Budget Execution L a w to Parliament; ( i )i iimproving the accounting system
with the adoption o f budget and accounting nomenclatures; (iv) rationalizing civil service
management through the completion o f the registry for state employees o f key line ministries;
(v) strengthening tax and customs administration through the introduction o f an automated
system (ASYCUDA) linking some o f the main customs offices; (vi) improving the state's
capacity to fight corruption and enhancing the accountability o f public employees; and (vii)
strengthening the financial situation, accounting system and management o f public utilities,
including the electricity sector. The design o f the operation incorporates key lessons learned
from these previous operations, thus representing continuity and consistency in the Bank's
support for the Haitian economic governance reform agenda

management and governance reform program, which includes (i)                 two Economic

                               11.     COUNTRY CONTEXT

POLITICAL AND SECURITY DEVELOPMENTS

5.     Haiti has long suffered from political instability and conflict. In early 2004, as
armed groups, including many former soldiers, advanced toward the capital, President Jean-
Bertrand Aristide resigned and left the country. A wave o f conflict l e f t thousands dead and
much public and private property, including infrastructure, destroyed or looted.

6.      The country has made considerable progress in restoring political and social
stability since 2004. After two years under a transitional Government, Haiti successfully
returned to constitutional order by holding presidential and parliamentary elections in 2006.
President Rene PrCval o f the Lespwa party won 51 percent o f the presidential vote. He
appointed a Prime Minister to head a six-party coalition Government, which took office in
M a y 2006. The 48th legislature was opened in May 2006, ending three years o f legislative
paralysis which strengthened the political process. Municipal elections were held in
December 2006 and April 2007. Recently, elections for a third o f the Senate went ahead on
April 19,2009, although delayed due to restructuring o f the electoral council and the passage
o f a new electoral code.

7.      Haiti has consolidated democracy and improved political stability since the
election o f President Rene Preval in 2006. There has been a relatively high turnover o f
Prime Ministers, with the recent appointment o f a third Prime Minister, but such changes have
been made using constitutional and democratic procedures, thereby preventing the country
from falling into major political crises as has been the case in the past. While the first Prime
Minister had to resign in 2008 as a result o f mounting discontent o f the population triggered
by the difficult economic circumstances following the food crisis, the second Prime Minister
was dismissed at the end o f October 2009 following a no-confidence vote by the Senate. The
third Prime Minister, who was Minister o f Planning and External Cooperation in the previous
Cabinet, was nominated shortly after the Senate no-confidence vote and was unanimously


                                                2
approved by the Senate on November 6, 2009. A new Cabinet i s expected to be in place
shortly.

8.       Although the overall security situation remains fragile, it has improved markedly
since December 2006. The Haitian National Police and the 9,000-strong UN peacekeeping
force (MINUSTAH) have dismantled or weakened major criminal gangs, largely returning to
state control the urban slums in which these groups had based their broad-ranging illegal
activities. Rates o f kidnapping and other violent crimes have fallen significantly but remain a
concern. In addition, the smuggling o f narcotics and weapons, amid weak border
control, continues to undermines Haiti's security and stability.

POVERTY TRENDS AND RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS

9.      Haiti i s by far the poorest country in Latin American and the Caribbean and
among the poorest in the world. 54 percent o f the country's population lives on less than
US$1 a day and 78 percent on less than US$2 a day, according to the 2001 household survey
data.' The 2009 United Nations Human Development Index ranked Haiti 149 out o f 177
countries. Income inequality i s high, with a Gini coefficient o f 0.59, among the highest in
Latin America and the Caribbean.

10.     The World Bank's 2006 Country Economic Memorandum (CEM)2 indicated that
poverty in Haiti i s associated with poor access to infrastructure, minimal ownership o f
assets, low education and literacy levels, and high levels o f child malnutrition. Haiti i s
also characterized by marked inequalities in access to productive assets and public services.
Little variation i s seen in the quality o f living conditions across the poorest 80 percent o f the
population. The richest quintile i s markedly better o f f along all dimensions, including
education levels and access to infrastructure. While data are inadequate, it appears that for the
country as a whole poverty and inequality rates have not changed substantially since 1986,
Many Haitians have avoided falling into complete destitution by receiving remittances from
abroad.

11.     I n 2008, Haiti experienced a series o f shocks that threatened macroeconomic
stability and diverted resources away from DSNCRP priorities. In addition to the political
stalemate following the food and fuel price riots, Haiti was hit by four back-to-back
hurricanes and tropical storms in August-September 2008, which caused damages and losses
estimated at nearly a billion dollars, or about 15 percent o f GDP. In order to help the
Government address the immediate impact o f the crisis, while safeguarding priority spending
for the longer term poverty reduction and growth agenda, donors pledged US$328 million in
new funds to support the Government's 18-month priority program3, in addition to the US$2-
3 billion already committed behind the DSNCRP, at a high-level conference in April 2009.

14.     Notwithstanding these challenges, macroeconomic stability has been maintained.
On the one hand, the decline in international commodity prices has reduced inflationary
pressures, leading to 12-month headline inflation o f 0.1 percent in April 2009, while core
inflation reached a l o w o f 7.1 percent, compared to 10.4 percent in September 2008. The
external current account deficit has been smaller than previously anticipated. Remittances
have been quite resilient, experiencing only a one percent decline between October 2008 and
April 2009, while lower import prices have more than offset the impact o f the increase in
reconstruction-related imports and the decline in services exports on the trade balance. As a
result, growth for Haitian FY2009 i s estimated to be around 2.5 percent.

15.    Nevertheless, significant uncertainty remains in the short term. Given the
magnitude o f the shocks to the Haitian economy experienced in 2008, output growth i s
expected to perform relatively well in 2009 and 2010, as infrastructure i s being rebuilt and
expanded, and economic activity recovers. Inflation i s expected to remain low, although the
recent recovery in commodities prices and the approval by Parliament o f a tripling of the
minimum wage6 could lead to renewed price pressures. Uncertainties to this short-term
economic outlook also stem from the possibility o f lower remittances, exports, and public
investment.

16.     Haiti's fiscal situation remains highly vulnerable. In 2009, l o w import prices and
weak economic activity have led to lower than expected tax and customs revenue, which i s
compensated in part by savings on wages and goods and services due to the delays in
approving the FY2009 budget. As a result, the authorities have decided to limit new domestic
investments for the second half o f FY2009 to a reduced l i s t o f priority projects in agriculture,
disaster preparedness and education. Lower external debt service thanks to debt relief also
created fiscal space. Despite these measures, the overall fiscal deficit i s estimated to widen to
4.0 percent o f GDP, up from 2.9 percent in FY2008. Fiscal performance could be further
affected by the increase in the minimum wage, which may translate into a proportional
increase in the public sector wage bill, increasing expenditures by as much as 0.5 percent o f

Fiscal and monetary policy

19.      The Government's macroeconomic policy framework remains adequate for the
purposes o f this DPL. Performance under the PRGF program is on track and negotiations for
a subsequent arrangement with the Fund are scheduled to begin in November 2009. The
authorities' medium-term policy stance aims for a gradual recovery o f growth and declining
inflation through: (i)  maintaining security; (ii)sustaining political and macroeconomic
stability; (iii)
               continuing progress on economic governance; and (iv) improving social and
economic infrastructure.

20.     The authorities' medium-term fiscal strategy i s to increase revenues and further
improve budget management, to allow for more poverty reducing spending and
investment. Revenue, which has been affected by the global slowdown, particularly through
lower import taxes due to the fall in commodity prices, i s expected to recover thanks to
measures to strengthen revenue administration, supported by donor-funded projects, and new
revenue measures, including a revision o f the external tariff and telecoms taxes (about 0.7
percent o f GDP). Domestic revenue is thus projected to grow gradually and reach 13.4
percent o f GDP in FY2012, while revenue from grants is projected to fall from 6.2 percent o f
GDP in FY2009 to 3.9 percent in FY2012. Given the country's vast needs in the social and
infrastructure sectors, revenue growth will not be fast enough in the short to medium run to
keep pace with the projected increase in expenditure. Assuming a sustained increase in capital
spending, from 7.1 percent o f GDP in FY2008 to 10.2 percent in FY2012, the fiscal deficit
would thus remain around 4 percent o f GDP.

24.     As identified in recent Government strategy documents, agriculture i s expected
to be an important driver o f growth in the short to medium term. Agriculture is the main
source o f livelihood for the 60 percent o f Haitians who live in rural areas. Agricultural
productivity has declined over time due to many factors,* but the sector has the potential to
contribute significantly to growth and poverty reduction. The Government's prioritization o f
investments in agriculture in FY2009 and FY2010 shows a strong commitment towards
reviving the sector, which was particularly affected by the 2008 hurricanes. In the medium
run, specific plans include strategic interventions in irrigation, agricultural inputs and
transportation infrastructure, along with a strengthening o f the public institutions to
coordinate such activities. These investments would also contribute to greater food security,
as well as increased agricultural exports, which currently include mangoes and essential oils.

25.     Another promising source o f growth in the coming years i s the textile sector,
which has a unique economic opportunity with the HOPE I1 Act. HOPE I1 gives duty-
free, quota-free access to the American market for the next nine years. Haiti is well placed in
some aspects to exploit this. Its labor costs are fully competitive with China, and there
remains some sectoral expertise and skilled labor in the sector, which has declined sharply
since the 1990s, but was previously strong. Haiti i s also located close to its main market, the
US. With support f i o m donors, including the IFC, the Government's program for this sector
focuses o n the expansion o f industrial parks, and the targeted infrastructure improvements
needed for these, including the rehabilitation o f three principal highways connecting major
population centers and the strengthening o f electricity supply. In addition, it envisions
complementary measures to improve the business environment, including proposals to allow
for multi-shift operation and greater speed and transparency o f customs procedures, and
enhancing training for workers.

32.     Despite the challenges and disruptions caused by a succession o f recent shocks,

33.     Under the growth pillar, there are a number o f notable achievements. In the
infrastructure sector, these include the construction o f new roads, the installation o f new
power plants, the creation o f an energy unit within the Ministry o f Public Works, and the

the rehabilitation o f irrigation systems, school reconstruction, and the building o f fords and
temporary overpasses where bridges have been destroyed. Although there has been some
progress towards the original DSNCRP goals, many o f the actions originally envisaged gave
way to emergency recovery actions that took priority.

34.     Progress was also made in improving basic social service delivery. I n education,
transfer mechanisms for tuition waivers (subsidies) to schools are now operational, covering
an estimated 80,000 children. A teacher training program has been implemented and the
number o f school inspectors has increased, covering ten departmental offices. Finally, more
than 20 percent o f the recurrent budget was allocated to the education sector, including
US$4.5 million committed by the Government to emergency school reconstruction and
rehabilitation. I n health, some health infrastructure was rehabilitated and equipment has been
improved. Vaccine coverage during the 2007 campaign reached 68 percent for Diphtheria,
Tetanus, and Pertussis (DTP) and 70 percent for tuberculosis, while the share o f medically
assisted births increased from 24 percent in 2006 to 30 percent in 2008. The APR reports
notable progress in reducing the incidence o f HIV/AIDS, which has fallen roughly by half
over the last decade although HIV incidence i s the highest national rate outside sub-Saharan
Africa.

35.     In the area o f economic governance, advances have been made to increase
transparency and efficiency in the use o f public resources through key reforms in public
finance management and procurement. Given the recent conflict situation, the political
instability, and the institutional weaknesses, implementing reforms i s challenging and results

    LESSONS LEARNED

    50.     Experience points to the need for adopting a simple approach in Haiti, as i s the
    case in most fragile countries. In such an environment, a multiple-tranche operation bears
    the risk o f delayed disbursement if the program implementation stalls due to exogenous
    factors as was the case in the previous operation. As a result, EGRO I11 has been designed as
    a single-tranche operation within a programmatic framework that promotes continuity and
    Government ownership.

    5 1.     Institutional strengthening i s key, and the shift from institution-building and
    support to legal and regulatory frameworks towards reforms supporting effective policy
    implementation should be gradual. For instance, reforms aimed at strengthening the
    Directorate o f budget and the internal control system have allowed for a significant reduction
    o f the Comptes Courants, which would have been difficult otherwise. Reforms supporting the
    CNMP have also facilitated the adoption o f the legal framework in procurement, the
    implementation o f which i s now foreseen in the near future. A key immediate challenge for
    the Government under the proposed operation i s to continue strengthening the legal
    framework for public financial management, building the capacity o f central Government
    agencies for revenue, expenditure, and procurement, and then gradually extending coverage to
.   sectoral ministries. Ensuring that these core institutions function appropriately and coordinate
    with each other i s crucial for improving service delivery, as well as for promoting a steady
    flow o f resources and their efficient use.

    52.     Reforms need to be backed up by pre-identified sources o f financing and
    technical assistance in order to maximize the chance for success and minimize the risk o f
    slow implementation. Post-conflict countries often lack sufficient resources due to weak
    revenue mobilization capacity and competing key demands, which range from security
    reinforcement, reconstruction programs to social needs &d debt service pressures. In
    addition, the Government's capacity generally needs to be significantly enhanced to sustain
    the implementation o f its reform program. This lesson i s confirmed by the experience with
    EGRO I: factor in its satisfactory implementation and the significant progress in meeting the
              a
    development objectives was the availability o f the LICUS Trust Fund for technical assistance.
    Similarly, EGRO I1 was accompanied by the EGTAG operations. The proposed operation has
    therefore been designed in close coordination with the ongoing EGTAG I1 operation, which
    will continue to lend technical assistance to areas crucial for the success o f this operation.
    Other donor-financed operations (see Table 4) further complement the technical assistance
    provided by the Bank.

                                                  17
53.     Close coordination among development partners on a commonly agreed reform
agenda could maximize the chances for a successful program. As institutions are relatively
weak and there are practical difficulties in post-conflict countries to cany out complex and
numerous reform programs operations should be sharply focused and sequenced with a
reasonable milestone framework to allow for reasonable progress in key areas. Post-conflict
countries' weak capacity i s likely to be mitigated by enhanced coordination o f development
partners' programs. Drawing from that lesson, the proposed operation i s based on the
Government matrix supported by a harmonized budget support framework, and closely
aligned with the budget support operations provided by the IADB, European Union (EU),
Caribbean Development Bank (CDB) and others donors.

R I S K S AND RISK MITIGATION

98.      The operation takes place in an environment o f high risk. There are four main
types o f relevant risks: economic risk, political risk, natural disaster-related risk, and
institutional capacity risk.

99.      The economic downturn in major international economies could have a serious
negative impact on Haiti, through reduced remittances, donor funding and investment.
There is evidence o f an impact in these areas already, which could jeopardize macroeconomic
stability, poverty reduction, human development and private sector development in Haiti.
Support from the Bank and other donors will be a mitigating factor for Haiti in these areas,
but a serious crisis would also reduce the overall effectiveness o f that assistance. In addition,
the country is extremely susceptible to external shocks. As in 2008, extreme poverty and
dependence o n imported food and fuel could exacerbate the effects o f the international
commodity markets on Haiti. Strengthening food security, including through raising
agricultural output, which has become a strategic priority for the Government and donors,
would help mitigate such a risk.

100. Haiti remains vulnerable to the political and social instability that has historically
undermined development and the implementation o f donor programs in the country.
Violent protests that led to the fall o f the first Prime Minister in April 2008 and the recent no-
confidence vote by the Senate against the second Prime Minister illustrate the challenges
towards achieving parliament democracy in Haiti. In the coming months, difficult socio-
economic conditions and multiple elections increase the risk o f the recurrence o f instability
and could contribute to political polarization, while absorbing Government's attention and
resources. However, the commitment o f President Preval to swiftly and constitutionally install
a new Prime Minister, and the broad support among political elites for Haiti's development
plan, the D S N C W , are positive signals. In addition, the recent appointment o f a new Prime
Minister, who was Minister o f Planning and International Cooperation in the previous
Cabinet, and as such has actively participated in the dialogue with development partners --
including the World Bank -- in the past, provides assurances that Haiti would stay the course,
including     continuing implementing key economic reforms supported by the proposed
operation. Lastly, the presence o f UN forces and the strong commitment o f the international
community, including the Bank Group, to supporting the Government and its reform program
are stabilizing factors.

Mr. President,

This Letter of Development Policy is intended to present the program that the
Government of Haiti intends to implement in order to continue the reforms started in
2004, and to strengthen the gains made in the area of economic governance. This
letter will present you with the broad outline of our medium term program, the primary
objective of which is to reduce poverty and improve the living conditions of the
population through job creation and the provision of access to basic social services.
It also outlines the reform measures planned in the context of this economic
governance program, for which we are seeking implementation assistance from the
World Bank.

1. Economic and Financial Situation in Haiti

The efforts made by the authorities to stabilize the macroeconomic situation in the
wake of the widespread social and political unrest in 2004 have produced satisfactory
results. These efforts are one component of the Government's program targeting the
establishment of a macroeconomic framework that facilitates economic growth and
poverty reduction.

Most macroeconomic indicators improved gradually in the 2004 - 2008 period. The
confluence of various internal and external shocks notwithstanding, the Haitian
economy managed to remain on a growth path despite the fact that this growth was
too weak to make a dent in poverty. The economic growth rate rose from 1.8 percent
in 2005 to 2.3 percent in 2006 and then to its highest rate, 3.4 percent, in 2007. In
2008, the growth rate fell to 1.2 percent. From an internal standpoint, this decline
was caused by the floods that occurred at the end of FY2006-07 and two hurricanes
that hit the country in the final two months of FY2007-08, causing widespread
devastation, estimated at 15 percent of GDP and, in the external sphere, the sharp

                                         46
    and unexpected rise in international oil prices and commodity and grain prices, as
    well as the sudden onset of the international financial crisis.

    In order to cope with the volatility and sudden increase in basic food prices, which
    sparked riots and led to the resignation of the Government, a series of measures was
    adopted, including: (a) subsidizing oil prices, which amounted to U S 4 7 million or 0.7
    percent of GDP; and (b) stabilizing the price of rice to its March 2008 level.

    Preliminary information on economic performance for 2008-09 indicates that the
    July/August 2008 flooding had a significant impact on economic activity, the scope of
    which was contained owing to the measures adopted. Based on preliminary
    estimates, the economic growth rate may be higher than the previous fiscal year, as
    a result of the following factors in particular: (a) implementation of the emergency and
    rehabilitation program; (b) higher agricultural production following the execution of
    programs and initiatives to strengthen food security through assistance aimed at
    boosting agricultural production; (c) the decline in international commodity prices,
    and; (d) higher consumption as a result of the stabilization of private transfers.

In the fiscal sphere, a series of reforms targeting gradual strengthening of the
efficiency of tax offices and curbing customs underinvoicing and tax evasion helped
blunt the impact of both the internal and external shocks on overall revenue trends.
As a result of these measures, revenue continues to trend upward. As of end-

At the same time, total expenditure increased, particularly in the context of the
emergency and rehabilitation program, as did implementation of various
infrastructure projects. Special concessional resources had to be mobilized in the

through donors' budget support and the HlPC debt relief, as Haiti reached completion
in June 2009.

Owing to a fiscal policy approach involving tighter expenditure control and avoidance
of monetary financing of the budget deficit, the Central Bank was able to strengthen
its net foreign exchange reserves, which stood at US$288 in September 2009
compared to US$294 million in September 2008. At the same time, the exchange
rate remained relatively stable, with a slight depreciation in the gourde (6 percent on
average).

II. The Government's Medium Term Strategy

The Government's medium term strategy is, for the most part, set forth in the GPRSP
(Growth and Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper), which was prepared in conjunction
with the international community and benefited from broad input from civil society
organizations, the private sector, and local authorities. The two main objectives of the
Government are (a) macroeconomic stability; and (b) sustained growth and poverty
reduction. The Government intends to achieve these objectives through the following
three pillars: growth vectors that include agriculture and rural development, tourism,
infrastructure, and science and technology (pillar 1); human development (pillar 2);
and democratic governance (pillar 3).

With the aim of achieving sustainable and lasting economic growth, the Government
views agriculture as one of the priority sectors, and with a view to boosting
agricultural ' production, the following activities have been identified: rural
development, with special emphasis on environmental protection, and the
diversification of revenue-generating activities.




                                           48
Tourism is another priority sector, given the country's natural potential. To stimulate
growth in this sector, the Ministry of Tourism has formulated a plan that accords
priority to the following activities: (a) development of tourist-related products; (b)
strengthening of the priority sectors; (c) identification and development of tourist
sites; and (d) development of qualified human resources through the establishment
and functioning of a national training institute.

The lack or poor condition of Haiti's infrastructure can stymie economic growth and
impede poverty reduction. For this reason, the transportation network needs to be
properly managed so as to reduce transaction costs. This can be achieved through a
road network which, when completed, will make it possible to strengthen economic
competitiveness and foster the development of regional potential, among other
things.

The Government also identified human development as a strategic priority in order to
enhance opportunities and thus improve the living conditions of the people. One of
its priorities is expanding access to and improving the quality of education. This pillar
includes such other important components as: (a) a wide range of health services
along with the improved quality of these resources; (b) greater access to potable
water and hygiene; (c) greater capacity to prevent physical impairments and greater
medical and other assistance for handicapped persons; and (d) greater focus on the
social and economic needs of young people, who account for more than 50 percent
of the population.

Democratic governance covers three important areas - the judiciary, security, and
state modernization. With regard to improvement of the judicial system, the
Government has identified the following five priorities: (a) restructuring and
modernization of the Ministry of Justice; (b) strengthening the judiciary; (c) access to
and effectiveness of the court system; (d) rehabilitation of the incarceration system;
and (e) updating of legislation.

Security encompasses the following three components: (a) improving working
conditions for staff and officers of the Haitian National Police (PNH); (b) restructuring
the PNH and strengthening the chain of command; and (c) reorganizing the institution
with a view to its deployment throughout Haiti.

Lastly, the GPRSP has as main axis, a cross-cutting theme which is economic
governance, including the strengthening of key institutions, the modernization of
public administration, and improving public finance management.

111. The Economic Governance Program for which World Bank assistance is
being sought

Since 2004, the Government has implemented a very extensive economic
governance program. The objectives of this program included enhancing
transparency in the management of public resources, building the capacity of public
                                           49
institutions to allocate and manage public resources, and combating corruption.
Implementation of this program led to the establishment and active functioning of
such institutions as the Unit to Combat Corruption (Unite deLutte contre la Corruption
ULCC], the National Public Procurement Commission (Commission Nationale des
Marches Publics, CNMP), and the General Finance Inspectorate (Inspection
Ggenerale des Finances, IGF]. The results obtained in this area should be
strengthened, hence the Government's desire to continue this program, composed of
the following three main areas: (i) fiscal policy; (2) public expenditure management;
and (3) public procurement.

   A. Fiscal Policy

Poor management of a number of public enterprises, reflected in a deterioration in
the quality of service provided to the population, has impacted public finances. The
Treasury has no choice but to make financial resources available to some of these
enterprises so that they can provide the most basic services to the population. In
2008-09, the Haitian Electricity Company (Electricife d'Hai'ti, EDH) received a subsidy
of close to G 4 billion, that is, more than half of the capital budget funded with internal
resources, thereby diverting resources from sectors key to poverty reduction and
growth. In order to address this situation with a view to reducing the amount of the
subsidy allocated to the EDH, a series of measures was adopted, such as the
auditing of accounts and the accounting system, increasing the company's
production capacity by rehabilitating and installing new power plants, and preparing a
draft law criminalizing the theft of electricity.

These measures would not yield the expected benefits unless supported by
additional reforms. Therefore, plans were made to review electricity rates, which have
been frozen for four years despite the very sharp increase in international petroleum
product prices. Additionally, improvements to the administrative and financial
management of the company were envisaged. Against this backdrop: (a) the EDH
has published and implemented new electricity rates. A new price mechanism was
implemented to take into account the fluctuations of oil prices. Rates will be revised
whenever international oil prices change by more than 15 percent; and (b) the EDH
concluded a contract with a supplier that will assume responsibility for the
establishment of a client management system and a technical service management
system. These two systems will facilitate closer monitoring of clients from the
standpoint of rates, collection, and the handling of client complaints.

Over the course of FY2009-IO, the Government intends to adopt measures to
enhance transparency in the use of the transfers consisting in strengthening the
mechanism that monitors key financial indicators for EDH, including the use of the
fiscal transfers, on a monthly basis, and commissioning a bi-annual audit of the
reports by an independent firm in order to ensure that the information is factually
correct.



                                           50
   B. Public Expenditure Management

Significant progress has been made in public finance management. With respect to
expenditure management, efforts have been made to submit the Budget to
Parliament on time as per the Law. The control systems have been strengthened,
mainly through the deployment of public accountants and financial controllers, which
has allowed for improving compliance with existing regulations and reducing the use
of off-budget expenditures. External control has also been strengthened through
regular submission of the Government accounts and the budget execution Law to the
Court of Account and Parliament respectively, although with delay.

Haiti's tax revenue has averaged 10 percent of GDP. The Government has launched
large reforms to strengthen tax and customs administration aimed at modernizing the
information systems to reduce fiscal evasion and maximize the existing tax assets.
For instance, the Government has started networking an information system
(ASYCUDA) in the main customs offices with the customs central office and initiated
the networking between the Taxes and Customs Directorates.

In this context, the Government has: (a) operationalized said system up and running
in 11 customs offices, namely, the Port-au-Prince port, the Port-au-Prince airport, and
the arrival area of the Port-au-Prince, Cap haltien, Gonai'ves, Saint Marc, Miragoane,
Malpasse, Ouanaminthe, Belladere, and Port de Paix airports. Installation of this
system should facilitate better management of imports and reduce the cases of fraud
and underinvoicing; (b) operationalized the networking of the information system
between tax offices at the departmental levels and the DGI in order to collect in a
timely manner information provided by existing modules, and (c) operationalized the
networking between the information systems of the General Customs Administration
(Administration generale des douanes, AGD) and Tax Administration Authority
(Direction Generale des lmpbts, DGI) in order to facilitate better management of tax
information, in particular relating to large firms and operators. Over the course of
FY2009-10, the Government plans to further strengthen the legal framework. In this
regard, the Government intends to submit the revised Fiscal Code to Parliament,
which will improve the environment for taxpayers and lay the foundation for a tax
framework in line with regional standards.

 With respect to expenditure management, the Government has increased the
number of inspectors assigned to the IGF with the aim of operationalizing said
institution and strengthening its analytical capacity to carry out the planned internal
audit validated by the Ministry of Finance. Over the course of FY2009-IO, the
Government plans to further strengthen the internal control system through the
dissemination of the procedural manual for internal audits and the completion of the
work plan for the IGF.

   C. Public Procurement

In-depth public procurement reforms have been implemented. In addition to the
establishment of the CNMP, these reforms have ushered in transparent public
                                          51
procurement mechanisms. This has all been facilitated by the organization of several
training seminars targeting public and private sector officials involved with
competitive bidding. The results of this work as a whole can be gauged by the
decrease in the number of sole source contracts. In 2004, such contracts were
estimated at 85 percent and in 2006, at 59 percent. Based on the most recent data
available, these contracts amounted to only 56 percent in 2008. These most recent
data suggest that work needs to be done in order to encourage ministries and
Government agencies to comply with the new procurement law.

In this regard, the Government undertook to apply the four implementing decrees to
the public procurement law. These decrees set forth competitive bidding thresholds,
organize the functions of the CNMP, stipulate general rules with respect to public
procurement and concessions, and lastly, set forth provisions pertaining to
procedures manuals for public institutions.

Over the course of 2009-10, the Government will encourage key public institutions to
draft and submit their annual procurement plans to the CNMP; and (c) ensure that
the CNMP database related to signed contracts is operational. This will help the
Government ensure strict monitoring of the dispositions of the new Code.

IV. Implementation and Monitoring of the Program

The Government plans to establish an entity within the Ministry of Economy and
Finance for ensuring implementation and monitoring of the program. Moreover, it
undertakes to provide World Bank staff, on a quarterly basis, with the information and
statistical data needed to monitor the activities for which the World Bank is providing
assistance.

V. Request for Financial Assistance

The Government has decided to adopt the measures necessary to reduce poverty
and improve the living conditions of the population. It intends to do so by rebuilding
the State and creating a stable environment conducive to wealth creation, and also
by forging ahead with economic governance reforms. To this end, the Government
has prepared and remains firmly committed to implementation of the program
described above. The Government wishes to obtain the technical and financial
support of the World Bank to assist it with implementation of these ambitious but
necessary reforms.

period (see Figure A 2 and Tables A4 and A5 for the evolution o f public debt ratios under the
baseline and alternativehhock scenarios). The P V o f debt-to-GDP ratio rises from 17 percent in
FY 2009 to 21.5 percent o f GDP by FY 2029, reflecting primarily an increase in external debt
over the period. The P V o f debt-to-revenue ratio has a similar profile, rising from about
99 percent in FY 2009 to about 119 percent in FY 2029. Debt service-to-revenue rises from
about 6 percent in FY 2009 to a high o f 7.7 percent in FY 2016 before dropping back to about
7 percent by FY 2029.

12.     Alternative and shock scenarios put public debt on a more sharply rising trajectory
during the projection period. Instead o f rising only gradually as under the baseline scenario, if
the primary deficit i s fixed at its relatively high FY 2009 level, the P V o f public debt-to-GDP
ratio would grow to 41 percent in F Y 2029 from 17 percent in FY 2009, while the PV o f public
debt-to-revenue ratio would reach 225 percent in FY 2029 compared to 99 percent in FY 2009.
Keeping the primary balance unchanged at the FY 2009 level would also raise debt service-to-


                                                 63
revenue from 6 percent in FY 2009 to 13 percent in FY 2029. Using historical values or the most
extreme shock (growth for the debt stock indicators and lower non-debt-creating flows for the
debt service measure) would also raise debt above the baseline scenario, although the
deterioration would be less pronounced than seen when holding the FY 2009 primary balance
constant.

V. Conclusions

13.     Haiti's risk of external debt distress remains high given a PV of debt-to-exports
ratio above the indicative threshold in the baseline scenario. Alternative and shock scenarios
highlight additional risks on the P V o f debt-to-exports, while other debt stock and service
indicators remain below the relevant thresholds in all scenarios. In terms o f public debt, there is a
risk o f steadily rising debt ratios under alternative and shock scenarios, particularly if the
primary balance is fixed at the FY 2009 level.

14.      Further steps are needed to safeguard debt sustainability following HIPC and
MDFU relief. H I P C M D R I relief bring the P V o f debt-to-exports ratio below the relevant
indicative threshold in FY 2009, but the ratio reaches the threshold by FY 2014, and would be
much worse in the likely event o f future shocks. Securing lasting debt sustainability will depend
on: maximizing grant assistance; a prudent borrowing strategy, for which efforts to strengthen
debt management should help; and steps to enhance Haiti's small export base, including through
improved security and infrastructure to boost trade, especially given preferential opportunities.
Better security and infrastructure could catalyze higher foreign direct investment flows. Greater
resilience to natural disasters would also help. Finally, sustained reform progress to bolster
institutions and policy implementation capacity would increase Haiti's ability to handle a higher
level o f debt.

Introduction

1.       Improving Public Expenditure Management (PEM) has been a major priority of
the reform agenda in Haiti over the last four years. Given the dramatic situation o f Haiti's
P E M during the first part o f the decade, the Government initiated in 2005 a comprehensive
series o f reforms aimed at establishing PEM basics and strengthening capacities and
institutions. While important results were achieved in terms o f the regulatory framework,
budget formulation, and budget execution, the most significant achievement i s probably the
competitive recruitment and the training o f professional human resources specialized in public
finance.

2.      The challenges remain significant, but the Government i s eager to pursue the
PEM reform process. There are three main medium-tern priorities: (i)    consolidating results
already achieved; (ii)  strengthening effectiveness and efficiency o f budget execution and
control through the integration o f the Government automated management systems; and (iii)
strengthening the strategic allocation o f resources through expanding budget
comprehensiveness, progressively introducing instruments o f budget planning, and further
linking between policy planning and budgeting.

Haiti's PEM challenges and progress during the period 2002-2008

2002-2004: Major systemic weaknesses and questionable PEM effectiveness

3.      The PEM regulatory framework was incomplete and ineffective. Significant legal
texts were still to be amended or adopted to ensure effective budgeting and PEM. First, there
was no organic budget l a w or equivalent defining the budgetary rules and the responsibilities
o f the executive and the legislature. This had an impact on the clarity o f roles and
accountabilities, the coordination o f decision making, and the possibility to prepare the budget
through a participatory process. Legislation and rules were also missing to clearly define the
mandate o f the Supreme Audit Institution (CSCCA), or even define some PEM functions such
as internal audit.

4.      The effectiveness and efficiency o f the budget formulation was hampered by its
dual nature, its absence o f medium-term perspective, and its lack o f timeliness. First, the
dual nature o f the budget39was generating a lack o f integration o f the different expenditures
contributing to the same objectives. Second, the budget was annual only, and based on
historical data. Third, due to the lack o f a clear calendar, important delays were experienced
in,ratifying the budget, generating up-front distortions for an effective execution. A s a result,
the budget was not a unified instrument aimed at controlling the use o f resources and planning
future allocations o f resources, and there were delays in ratifying the budget.

5.    The ability to control resource allocation and public spending was severely
constrained by significant unreported extra-budgetary expenditures. An important part o f
