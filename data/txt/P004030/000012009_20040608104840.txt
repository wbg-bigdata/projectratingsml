                                                EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Royal Government of the Kingdom of Cambodia has received a Credit from the International Development
Association (IDA Credit No. 3181KH) to rehabilitate National Road No 3 and No 6. Part of the construction work
was scoped for the restoration of ten ancient Angkorian bridges from PK 254+00 to PK 298+150.

The ancient bridge at Kampong Kdei (Spean Praptoes) is considered the most important Angkor period bridge
structure within Cambodia and is the gateway from the south into the Angkor region. The rehabilitation
proposals for this structure, as contained in the Contract, required that the bridge, after the provision of a
reinforced concrete deck slab, laterite block footpaths and certain minor repairs to one abutment, would
continue to serve as an integral part of National Road No 6. This turns out to be unrealistic given the large
increase in volume and speed of heavy goods vehicle traffic resulting from the NR6 rehabilitation.

As such, all concerned parties have agreed that a bypass of the bridge (Kampong Kdei bypass) is necessary to
safeguard this historic structure and to maximize its potential as a significant tourist attraction.

A study was carried out to identify alternatives to the bridge. The study proposed three alternatives ­ A, B, and
C ­ of which B has been chosen because of many merits it has. Four merits worth mentioning are (a)
minimization of resettlement costs and negative environmental impacts, (b) lowest relative cost that can be
taken up by the current Contract of the "National Road 6 Rehabilitation Project", (c) technical feasibility, and (d)
tourist attraction.

While the alternative B is selected, a series of consultation workshops with various stakeholders concerned
including local communities have been carried out, a baseline survey was completed, public information
campaign has been on-going, and a census and a socio-economic study of the affected households were
conducted to formulate this Resettlement Action Plan (RAP). The census and socio-economic study was
conducted in March and April 2004.

RAP is required not just by the Royal Government of Cambodia, but also by the World Bank (WB), which is the
donor of the Project. RAP is always required in the WB-assisted project/program that involves resettlement and
relocation. RAP is action-oriented, cost-specific, and time bound document, which is meant to achieve following
objectives:

    (i)      present a strategy that will help achieve objectives of resettlement policy;
    (ii)     give framework for implementing such a strategy;
    (iii)    provide a framework for entitlements to and eligibility for compensation and restoration measures
             that will help affected persons improve or at least maintain their living standard;
    (iv)     present a framework for implementing public participation and consultation process, and for
             grievance and complaint redress;
    (v)      identify and present an estimate of resources required to carry out RAP and all recommended
             measures; and
    (vi)     offer a framework for implementing both internal and external monitoring and supervision, and
             impact evaluation.

As part of RAP preparation, census, and socio-economic study was conducted in March 2004. The study was
dictated by the TOR, and based on a baseline survey done just in February 2004. Thus, the cut-off-date is
February 2004. All together 76 households will be affected by the Project. Since the size of this particular
population is small they were all included in both the census and socio-economic survey.

The affected persons rely mostly on market- and service-related sectors for their livings as can be reflected in
the share of income from different sources. Very few people depend on agriculture; and income share from
agriculture is relatively small. None of the households are unemployed, though on individual level the
employment rate is slightly higher than 11 percent. Average income per affected household is more than
US$300, but is highly variable among the households. Moreover, distribution of income among them is quite
uneven with the lowest 20 percent of households garner only just 5 percent of total income, and the top 10
percent gather 25 percent of total income. Though, poverty incidence rate in this population is slightly higher
than 10 percent relative to the poverty line set by the Ministry of Planning. More than 20 percent of the affected
households are dependent on female heads.

The 76 households that will be affected by the Project will lose some or all different assets, and structures such
as land, trees, and housing and other structures. Others that will be affected by the Project include local
Cambodia People's Party offices, government offices of information and telecommunication, and common
properties of community (a well and a drainage structure). These affected households, community and entities,
will be entitled to compensation and rehabilitation measures as set out in this RAP.

Overall, 47,701.5 m2 of residential land, 3,489.1 m2 of rice-land, 4,888.6 m2 of wooden and concrete
structures, 365 small non-producing trees and 2, 599 producing trees (of no environmental significance), 7
wells, 7 ponds, 2 gasoline tanks, one drainage structure, and 1,915 wooden fences will be affected by the
Project. All these losses will be compensated in accordance with the rehabilitation and restoration measures
specified in detail in this RAP. The compensation cost for these losses is estimated at US$556,680.2.

In addition, allowance for unforeseen losses of wealth and assets (including personal effects) not revealed
during census and socio-economic study is given to PAP. Also, allowance for dismantling and relocation
activities is incorporated in the compensation package. Each of the allowances is allocated as percentage of the
cost of principal structures such as houses, shops/stalls, and offices, based on experiences from previous
projects of RAP implementing and formulating agencies. The former is allocated 10 percent of the structure
cost, while the latter is given 20 percent. Both of these allowances are also considered as additional assistance
to off-set the loss of incomes that may incur on PAP households during disassembling and relocation activities.
The total cost for compensation, including the allowances amounts to US$614,018.23. Various measures for
rehabilitation and restoration of PAP households' living standards are also built in. Special attention will be
given to vulnerable groups at the Project site ­ female- and elderly-headed households ­ with regard to the
implementation of particular R&R measures such as facilitation and coordination for ownership titles of land,
skills/vocational training, and job placements.

Total cost estimate and budget for the whole RAP amounts to some US$731,121 covering implementation and
management cost, contingency on prices, and external monitoring and impact evaluation activities. The cost of
RAP management is estimated at US$30,701 and external monitoring and impact evaluation US$25,000. The
budget for RAP implementation will come from the national budget in the form of counterpart fund. Certain
amount of the counterpart fund has been released for the whole "National Road 3 and 6 Rehabilitation Project".
Additional counterpart amount will be given for RAP, if the allocated counterpart fund is not adequate.

RAP is expected to be implemented in June, i.e. two months after its submission; otherwise a new study shall
be required; and RAP will have to be updated accordingly. The implementing agency of RAP will be the
Resettlement Unit (RU) of WB-PIU1 of the Ministry of Public Works and Transport, which is the executing
agency of the Project. It will implement most of the works determined in the RAP, including internal monitoring
and supervision, in coordination with concerned institutions and stakeholders, and in consultation with PAP
households. To ensure that RAP strategy, policy and objectives are achieved major players will be directly
engaged, and new committee will be set up. These include National Ad-Hoc Committee with members from line
ministries, and the Ministry of Economy and Finance. The Sub-Committee at Project level, with representatives
from local authorities (such as provincial governor, district governor, commune councillors, and village chiefs),

                                              1 INTRODUCTION

1.1 PROJECT DESCRIPTION: THE KAMPONG KDEI BYPASS
The Royal Government of the Kingdom of Cambodia has received a Credit from the International Development
Association (IDA Credit No. 3181KH) to rehabilitate National Road No 3 and No 6. Part of the construction work
was scoped for the restoration of ten ancient Angkorian bridges from PK 254+00 to PK 298+150.

The ancient bridge at Kampong Kdei (Spean Praptoes) is considered the most important Angkor period bridge
structure within Cambodia and is the gateway from the south into the Angkor region. The rehabilitation
proposals for this structure, as contained in the Contract, required that the bridge, after the provision of a
reinforced concrete deck slab, laterite block footpaths and certain minor repairs to one abutment, would
continue to serve as an integral part of National Road No 6. As part of the site supervision services under the
Contract NR6, an examination of the condition of the fabric of this laterite block structure was undertaken. The
examination indicated that it would be most unwise to continue to subject this ancient bridge to the loading and
vibrations from the large increase in volume and speed of heavy goods vehicle traffic resulting from the NR6
rehabilitation. The Contract proposals are, therefore, no longer deemed appropriate and all concerned parties
have agreed that a bypass of the bridge (Kampong Kdei bypass) is necessary to safeguard this historic
structure and to maximize its potential as a significant tourist attraction.

In addressing this concern, Pacific Consultants International along with its partner, Khmer Consultant
Engineering Corporation Ltd. was asked to conduct a feasibility study to identify a feasible bypass. Three
alternatives (A, B, and C) of the bypass were considered and studied (Annex 1: map/drawing). In each case it
was assumed that the existing road through Kampong Kdei town would have limited refurbishment, with a
laterite (sub-base) running surface provided, to develop the areas adjacent to the bridge into a tourist-oriented
zone once the bypass had been constructed and the existing business adjacent to the bridge had been
relocated. The three alternatives were presented to and discussed with various stakeholders including Apsara
authority, and project-affected persons.

After a series of comprehensive consultations with the concerned stakeholders, and from the technical
viewpoint, the alternative B was chosen. The selection of the alternative bypass B was based on the following:

    (i)      Cost: The alternative bypass B costs US$615,000, which is 78.85%, and 51.24% of the cost of
             alternative A and C, respectively;
    (ii)     Government's requirement: The alternative bypass B satisfies the requirement of the Royal
             Government of Cambodia for the ancient structure to be clearly visible from the bypass so that
             tourists could be attracted that would stimulate local economic development;
    (iii)    Potential tourist development: It will release the area surrounding the ancient bridge for
             comprehensive re-development as a major tourist attraction of international significance;
    (iv)     Preservation of archaeological site of cultural and historic significance: The bypass could be
             constructed under the existing construction contract period thus ensuring that the existing historic
             bridge structure is relieved from heavy-goods vehicle loadings and vibrations. It would avoid
             additional costs and delays that would arise from the need for a landmark structure for the crossing
             of the river; and
    (v)      Minimal impacts: The alternative bypass B minimises impacts on existing properties while
             preserving the integrity of the existing businesses that will be relocated to positions along the
             proposed alignment.

The alternative bypass B ­ the Kampong Kdei bypass ­ will be located downstream from the site of existing,
ancient bridge. It is envisaged that either a curved two span, 48 meters overall length or a curved three span,
60 meters overall length pre-cast concrete beam/in situ deck structure will be required to carry the bypass over

the Chi Kreng River. Designs for (straight) structures of these span arrangements are already included in the
current Contract, and it is envisaged that there should be only minor problems in adapting the Contract designs
to a particular bridge layouts for the crossing structure.

1.2 SUMMARY DESCRIPTION OF IMPACTS
The Kampong Kdei bypass with the length of 1,300 meters will cross the Chi Kreng River of Kampong Kdei
Commune, Chi Kreng District, about 200 m downstream from the existing, ancient bridge at PK 254+00. The
bypass will cut through paddy fields of local farmers and part of the Chi Kreng market.                             Therefore, the
construction of this particular bypass will have certain impacts. These impacts can be summarized under four
main types as follows.

    (i)       Loss of land assets: The bypass will take away some of the paddy, chamcar and residential land of
              local population in Chi Kreng district. Part of the land in the marketplace where local businesses are
              operated will also be lost;
    (ii)      Loss of structures: Along with the loss of land will be the loss of fixed/standing structures on such
              land such as houses, shops, cattle/buffalo stables, chicken pens, hog pens, wells, ponds, etc;
    (iii)     Loss of tree assets: The trees standing on land the land that will be taken away by the bypass will
              be cut down and removed. These trees will be also lost; and
    (iv)      Interruption of livelihoods activities of local populace: Since majority of local people rely on
              agriculture, the loss of paddy and chamcar land1 will mean temporary interruption or permanent
              loss of their livelihoods. Livelihoods activities of businesspersons whose shops at the marketplace
              given up for the bypass will be affected.

In this regard, land acquisition and compensation will have to be done prior to starting the bypass construction.
For this purpose a detailed resettlement study was conducted in March 2004 to assess total costs of the
compensation, and resettlement, and to formulate an appropriate and acceptable Resettlement Action Plan
(RAP). The study was also based on the baseline survey conducted earlier by the WB-PIU1.

1.3 PRINCIPLE PROJECT STAKEHOLDERS
Project stakeholders can be defined as those individuals and/or agencies who/which have the stakes, and
interest in, and concerns about the project. Major stakeholders of the Kampong Kdei bypass are local
community's people, local businesspeople, local authorities (including village, commune, and district
authorities), provincial authorities, national institutions, and non-governmental agencies. The identification of
principle project stakeholders was done through a series of consultations at local, provincial, and national
levels. Among local stakeholders who will likely be affected include some of the vulnerable households
including female-headed, elderly-headed, and poor (see Section 2).

Major stakeholders within the national government who have been consulted with and informed are Apsara
Authority, Ministry of Public Works and Transport, and Ministry of Economy and Finance. Main stakeholders
from non-governmental organizations who have been involved and informed are UNESCO, and International
Committee for Restoration of Khmer Ruins.

      2 SOCIO-ECONOMIC SURVEY AND RESULTS, AND PROJECT IMPACTS

2.1 SURVEY PROCESS
The smallest sampling unit was the household. Since the size of population was small, 76 households2, census
was used in this resettlement study. The socio-economic survey was a necessity because many of the project-
affected persons will lose more than 10 percent of their productive assets.

Prior to the field data and information collection a set of questionnaires was developed in close collaboration
and consultation with the WB-PIU1. The questionnaires were formulated based on the TOR of the resettlement
study (Annex 2), and baseline information/report. Consultation with the World Bank's Involuntary Resettlement
Operation Manual was also made. The questionnaires were presented to the project proponent, WB-PIU1, and
revised. The revised questionnaires were then translated, and used during the training of well-trained and
experienced survey team. After the training, the questionnaires were pre-tested and finalized prior to the actual
field data and information collection. The questionnaires used to collect information for this resettlement study
consist of 9 different sets. For the detail content of each set, refer to Annex 3. Briefly they include the following:
(a) census, (b) land assets, (c) trees, (d) structures, and (e) market price survey for land, construction materials,
labour and transportation.

Prior to actual survey, i.e. during pre-testing, local people and authorities at the location of the bypass were
informed and public information leaflet (Annex 4) were handed out to them to further raise their awareness of
the bypass and the upcoming census survey. The fieldworks were performed for two weeks and data entry was
completed in early April. The results of the census survey are presented the sections that follow.

2.6 TENURE PRACTICE AND STATUS
Majority of rural population in Cambodia do not normally have ownership certificate of or titles to their
properties, especially land. All relevant documents before the 1970s were destroyed during the Khmer Rouge
and are not recognized at present. The ownerships are recognized by the de facto inhabitation on the
properties, while people of indigenous communities continue to follow their traditional recognition of ownerships.
Now, titles and ownerships are recognized by the new Land Law 2001, that replaces Land Act 1992 (see
Section 3).

At the Project-affected site, it is rarely observed that people have followed any sort of traditional tenure practice
for properties (Table 2.15). All households reported that legal tenure system follows the RGC's legal framework.
Also, there are some 14.4 and 15.3 percent of households that reported the practice of informal tenure systems
that depend on informing local authorities, and verbal agreements between / among parties of interests,
respectively about/on their occupation and use of the properties. Of all the 76 interviewees only 48 (63.2%) own

2.7 PRODUCTION SYSTEMS
Agricultural production system in the Project area is not different from the rest of Cambodia. Major agricultural
production found in the area includes crops production, livestock production, fishery and aquaculture. Many
people are aware of such production system practiced in their area (Table 2.17). In crops production people
grow mainly paddy rice, then vegetables, cash crops, and fruit trees. Paddy rice varieties found cultivated in the
area are importantly local and traditional. Vegetables generally grown are leafy, fruit vegetables and spices
such as salad, cabbage, Chinese kale, cucumber, yard long bean, gourd, bitter gourd, papaya, white radish,
morning glory, soropus, peas, amaranthus, ginger, chilli, tomato, lemon grass, taro. Cash crops found include
cashew, sesame, mung bean, soy bean, sugar cane, jackfruit, white yam, banana, pineapple, cassava, water
melon, and others. Common fruit trees are mango, milk fruit, santol, coconut tree, pomegranate, sapota,
longan, and so on.

For livestock, people always raise cattle and buffaloes mainly as traction power. Of course some of them are
sold out for cash. In addition to this, pigs, chickens, and ducks are found commonly kept. These animals are
part of the savings and cash income. Some of them are for household consumption as well.

Fishing is a normal livelihood activity for typical Cambodian farmers. They normally fish in rice field during wet
season and water bodies such as lakes, and rivers. In the Project area, they do fishing in Stung Chikreng, and
some times they go down to the Great Lake Tonle Sap. There are also few families who do aquaculture. They
raise mainly crocodiles and fish for sale. This is their main source of income. Very few reported that there are
villagers relying on non-timber forest products. They normally collect from the forest non-timber forest product
such as wild fruits, leafy vegetables.

2.8 VULNERABLE GROUPS
According to the World Bank's Policy on Involuntary Resettlement, vulnerable groups, among those to be
displaced, are those that are below poverty line, the landless, the elderly, women, the unemployed, children,
indigenous people, ethnic minorities, or other displaced persons who may not be protected through national
land compensation legislation.

Based on the information from the census and socio-economic survey vulnerable groups at the Project-affected
site include widow female headed households (7%), elderly-headed households (11%), and poor households
(10.53%). There is no household that was reported landless or unemployed. However, on individual level, there
are some unemployed persons within the households.

None of the Project-affected households is of any ethnic background, although there are some 1 percent of

                  3 COMPENSATION POLICY AND ENTITLEMENT CRITERIA

3.1 OBJECTIVES OF RESETTLEMENT POLICY
The Royal Government of Cambodia together with the civil society has started drafting a national resettlement
policy. It is not available at this stage. Therefore, the resettlement policy applied in this RAP is based generally
on the World Bank's involuntary resettlement policy. According the World Bank3, involuntary resettlement may
cause severe long-term hardship, impoverishment, and environmental damage unless appropriate measures
are carefully planned and carried out. For these reasons, the overall objectives of the Bank's policy on
involuntary resettlement are the following:

    (i)       Involuntary resettlement should be avoided where feasible, or minimized, exploring all viable
              alternative project designs.
    (ii)      Where it is not feasible to avoid resettlement, resettlement activities should be conceived and
              executed as sustainable development, programs, providing sufficient investment resources to
              enable the persons displaced by the project to share in project benefits. Displaced persons should
              be meaningfully consulted and should have opportunities to participate in planning and
              implementing resettlement programs.
    (iii)     Displaced persons should be assisted in their efforts to improve their livelihoods and standards of
              living or at least to restore them, in real terms, to pre-displacement levels or to levels prevailing
              prior to the beginning of project implementation, whichever is higher.

To ensure that the above resettlement policy objectives are met, effective public participation of and
consultation with all the key stakeholders, and project-affected persons (PAP) in particular, have been carried
out and will also be implemented at every stage of the project planning, design and implementation.

3.2 LEGAL FRAMEWORK AND ELIGIBILITY

3.2.1     Cambodia's Legal Framework
Cambodia had gone through a tragic political and regime change during the latter decades of the foregoing
millennium. During the Khmer Rouge reign of terror 1975 ­ 1978 all properties were nationalized and no private
ownership of properties was allowed, while all important relevant documents and legal frameworks were all, but
destroyed. After the collapse of the Khmer Rouge rule on January 7, 1979, people began to return to the urban
areas. Due to the lack of defined property rights, people started occupying vacant land and structures, whereby
a system based on usufruct rights was established. In rural areas, the government started mobilizing rural
families into a what-so-called solidarity group4, where properties and production factors, such as land, draft
animals, community structures, were placed under collective control and ownership supervised and managed
by village head. The practice was changed in the late 1980s when land was redistributed to individual
households for private ownership; but the legal framework that governed and ruled on the process and that
recognized such private ownership was only promulgated in October 1992. This legal framework became
obsolete, when a new Land Law was passed and promulgated in August 2001. This new Land Law replaces the
1992 Land Act, and is the only legal framework that defines and governs land ownership5. The provisions and
articles relevant to resettlement and relocation issues, which are the foundations for the drafting of a national
resettlement policy as referred to earlier, are quoted below for a quick reference.

3.2.2    Word Bank's Policy on Involuntary Resettlement
According to the Bank's BP 4.12, and OP 4.12 the basic guiding principle of the WB's Policy on Involuntary
Resettlement is that the adverse impacts in a project are avoided or minimized, and the resettlement measures
are conceived and executed as development programs and affected persons are given opportunity to share
project benefits. The objective is to assist displaced persons in their efforts to improve their former production
levels, income earning capacity, and living standards or, at least, to restore their income-earning capacity and
living standards to the level they are likely to have had without the project.

The Policy further stipulates that customary and formal rights are recognized equally in providing assistance
and in devising criteria for entitlements and procedures for compensation and other resettlement assistance.
The policy further states that the absence of formal legal title to land by some affected groups should not be a
bar to compensation and that particular attention should be paid to the needs of the poorest affected persons
including those without legal title to assets, female-headed households and other vulnerable groups, such as

3.2.3    Eligibility for Entitlements to Compensation Benefits
3.2.3.1    The Cut-Off Date for Eligibility
The baseline survey was carried out in February 2004 (Bang Somaly)?? by the WB-PIU1. Prior to and during
this baseline survey, a series of consultations with various stakeholders and representatives of the affected
communities were also conducted. Following the baseline survey, a full census of all households, whose assets
will be affected by the project, even minor, was executed during March 2004. The census also conducted a
complete inventory of would-be affected assets and structures of all affected households. The socio-economic
survey/census of the affected households was incorporated in the overall census in order to see further impact
on the households. However, for the purpose of entitlements, the cut-off date, which is one of the important
criteria for eligibility for compensations, is established at the date when the baseline survey of each affected
household was carried out, this is February 2004 (Bang Somaly)??.

3.2.3.2    Eligibility for Project-Affected Persons
Project-affected persons (PAP) include any person or persons who at the cut-off date of the project, i.e. at the
date of conducting the baseline survey referred to in the afore-mentioned section, are residing or deriving an
income from the Project area, and because of the Project would have their:

         - Standard of living adversely affected;
         - Loss of structures or any other movable or fixed assets acquired or possessed, in full or in part,
              temporarily; or
         - Business, occupation, place of work or residence or habitat adversely affected.

Project Affected Family (PAF) consists of all members residing under one roof and operating as a single
economic unit, who are adversely deprived off by the Project, or any of its components affected. For the
resettlement purpose, PAP will be considered members of the PAF. Some households may have more than
one family with separate income and kitchen. In this case, PAP will be taken as members of any one of the
families residing in the household with the legal status. The affected assets of each family are recoded
separately. PAF is also referred to project affected communities (PAC) that have assets located within the
Project area, e.g. pagoda compound, school complex, commune centre, trees. Community constitutes pagoda
association, farmers group, water user association, parental association, village development committee, local
business association, and so on.

3.2.3.3    Legal versus Illegal PAP
Under the Cambodian relevant legal frameworks presented earlier, people who use land or build structures
without permissions are not entitled to any compensation such as those who do have such permissions.
However, this will not prevent the PAP from entitlement to compensation benefits.

As indicated in the Bank's policy on involuntary resettlement, the lack of legal rights will not bar PAP from
entitlement to compensations for their lost assets and livelihoods. The compensations will cover improvements
of structures, houses, crops, trees and other fixed assets, and rehabilitation measures that ensure the
enhancement of the PAP livelihoods activities in amounts and levels sufficient to attain the resettlement policy's
objectives.

                        4 REHABILITATION AND INCOME RESTORATION
As discussed above the Project will cause the loss of assets, structures and livelihoods of the PAP. Therefore,
rehabilitation and income restoration measures are needed to ensure that the stated objectives of RAP are
satisfactorily met. These measures will ensure that living standards and conditions of the PAP during and after
the Project will be enhanced or at least will remain. The measures include (i) compensations as full replacement
costs for all assets lost identified above; and (ii) livelihoods assistance. The compensation measures will be all
in cash, unless specified otherwise. The measures are presented as follows:

4.1 REHABILITATION MEASURES FOR LOSS OF RESIDENTIAL AND PADDY LAND
    (i)      All PAP, but two, who will lose entirely their residential land, will receive full compensation for such
             loss. Those PAP who will lose part of their residential land, but the remaining land is not usable, will
             be entitled to full compensation. In such event they will not be allowed to come back or to resettle
             on that remaining piece of land as they expressed themselves during the socio-economic survey.
             To avoid any future conflict when the RGC may need such pieces of land for public purposes and
             interests, a letter of agreement will be prepared and signed/thumb-printed on compensation date,
             which will be maintained for and as proof for future handling of any relevant conflict.

    (ii)     The two PAP households that requested for only compensation of land of equal value and quality in
             the same district will be consulted. Their request will be considered, and plan to acquire land and to
             relocate the households will be drawn up in coordination with the Sub-Committee. This, however,
             will not rule out the possibility for compensation for the lost residential land in cash as applied to
             other PAP, provided that such arrangement is acceptable to them following appropriate
             consultations with them.

    (iii)    Those PAP whose residential land will only be affected in part, and the left-overt part is viable for
             continued use, the compensation at full replacement cost will be applied to only the affected part.
             They will have full rights to continue using their remaining residential land. Such rights will be
             guaranteed. And, if possible, facilitation for ownership titles to such pieces of residential land could
             be made.

    (iv)     The loss of paddy land is the loss of livelihood-based asset. Those PAP who will lose their paddy
             land entirely will be compensated in full. Fortunately, none of the affected households will lose
             entire paddy land. Those who will lose part of their paddy land, but the remaining part renders
             useless, will be entitled to full compensation like those who will lose the entire plot. In such event
             they will not be allowed to reuse the remaining pieces of their paddy land as they explained
             themselves during the socio-economic survey. To avoid any future conflict when the RGC may
             need such pieces of land for public purposes and interests, a letter of agreement will be prepared
             and signed/thumb-printed on compensation date, which will be maintained for and used as proof for
             future dealing of any relevant conflict.

    (v)      Those PAP who will lose only part of their paddy land, and the remaining part of it is utilizable, will
             be entitled to compensation of the affected part only. They will have full rights to continue using
             their remaining rice land. Such rights will be guaranteed. And, if possible, facilitation for ownership
             titles to such pieces of land could be made.

    (vi)     In all cases, appropriate development assistance will be provided to off-set the loss of the land-

                       5 PUBLIC PARTICIPATION AND CONSULTATIONS

5.1 OBJECTIVES OF PUBLIC PARTICIPATION AND CONSULTATION
Public participation and consultation is a process that ensures transparency for and improved understanding
about the Project. Experience shows that the Project or RAP with good arrangement for public participation and
consultation process always results in faster implementation of PAP, and thus the Project.

The specific objectives of the public information campaign and public consultation are as follows:
        (i)      To share fully the information about the proposed project, its components and its activities with
                 affected people;
        (ii)     To obtain information about the needs and priorities of the affected people, as well as
                 information about their reaction to proposed policies and activities;
        (iii)    To inform various options for relocation and rehabilitation measures available to people;
        (iv)     To obtain the cooperation and participation of the affected people and communities on activities
                 required to be undertaken for resettlement planning and implementation; and
        (v)      To ensure transparency in all activities related to land acquisition, resettlement and
                 rehabilitation.

5.2 CONCERNED STAKEHOLDERS
Concerned stakeholders of the Project were identified in previous section. These stakeholders will be fully
involved in public participation and consultation process. Those stakeholders who will be heavily involved in
public participation and consultation process will be local communities, PAP, and Sub-Committee members.
Their participations and consultations with them are very important for achieving RAP policy.

5.3 INSTITUTIONAL MECHANISM AND PROCESSES

5.3.1    Institutions
MPWT's WB-PIU1 as the Project executing agency and its RU will be responsible for the implementation of
RAP, and organization of public participation and consultation processes (see also Section 7). The RU of the
WB-PIU1 in coordination with the Sub-Committee will work to ensure effective public participation and
consultation process. Representatives of local authorities, communities and PAP as members of the Sub-
Committee will be able to ensure and further proper and active involvement of local people and PAP in public
participation and consultation process. Favourable and conducive environment will have to be established so
that local people can fully participate. They should be informed in advance of any activities of public
participation and consultation process. Timing will have to be flexible, and venues for such process must be
readily and accessible to them. The external monitoring agency, SBK, will also be part of the process. The
external monitoring agency will do this consultation and public participation process through its monitoring and
supervision activities (see Section 8).

5.3.2    Processes
The process of public participation and consultation will be in the form of meetings, workshops, small focus
group discussions that will have to be organized prior to and during the implementation of RAP. The issues for
this process will concentrate on RAP policy and entitlements, compensation rates and package, and restoration
measures. NAC at MEF will, before the actual implementation of RAP policy, visit PAP to verify and confirm
compensation rates and package as well as the income restoration measures. Where possible, visual and audio

                6 GRIEVANCE REDRESS MECHANISM AND PROCEDURES

6.1 GRIEVANCE REDRESS MECHANISM
It is envisaged that there will inevitably be grievances and complaints by the PAP, PAF, and PAC during the
implementation of the RAP. This is not uncommon. As such, a mechanism that will allow the grievances and
complaints of the PAP, PAF, and PAC to be heard and addressed will have to be in place prior to the actual
implementation of RAP, and all concerned stakeholders should be informed of the existence of such a
mechanism.

This is not the first RAP in Cambodia. Experiences of the project executing agency and concerned
government's agencies in previous RAP have prompted them to set up a mechanism that can deal with
grievances and complaints of the PAP, PAF, and PAC justly and effectively. The mechanism constitutes a
National Ad-Hoc Committee (NAC) at the national level, which is under the direction and chairmanship of the
Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF). The NAC has memberships from the Ministry of Public Works and
Transport (MPWT), and the agency/NGO external to the government system that conducted census and socio-
economic study and public consultations, and prepare RAP. In this particular case, it is the SBK.

In addition to the NAC, a Sub-Committee at local, project level will be created prior to the actual implementation
of the RAP. This Sub-Committee will consist of representatives of provincial, district, commune and village
authorities, and representatives of the PAP, PAF, and PAC whose interests will be taken away by the Project.
This Sub-Committee will be to facilitate the easy access of the PAP, PAF, and PAC to the lodge their
grievances and complaints and timely and fair dealings of their grievances and complaints, unless such
dealings are acceptable, they can refer their cases to the NAC for appropriate adjudication. Other duties of
these committees are to review and approve the project design, planning and implementation and other
components of the project deemed necessary, compensation package, and monitoring of the implementation of
RAP.

6.2 GRIEVANCE REDRESS PROCEDURES
The grievance redress procedures as have been introduced and practiced constitute three steps. The PAP,
PAF, and PAC who want to have their grievances and complaints heard and addressed will follow these steps
which are elaborated below.

          (vi)   Step 1: To simplify the accessibility of the PAP for his/her grievances and complaints a
                   Working Group (WG) under the RAP implementation team will be set up. The WG will
                   evaluate the compensation rates and package, and present such rates and package to the
                   PAP. The PAP will have full right to review, reject or agree on the compensation rates set by
                   the WG or on any aspect of the resettlement program. Grievances and complaints of the PAP
                   shall in first instance be lodged verbally or in writing to the WG directly. They shall be settled
                   through an informal or amicable meeting between the PAP and the WG or the response of the
                   WG in writing within five (5) days from the day they were received, to the PAP.
          (vii)  Step 2: If no understanding or acceptable solution can be reached or the PAP does not receive
                   any response from the WG after five (5) days from the day the grievances and complaints
                   were filed, he/she can appeal to the Sub-committee of Grievance Redress and the WB-PIU1.
                   The PAP will be supported by field staff of the WB-PIU1 and local authorities (village,
                   commune, and district) to express, and prepare and file his/her grievances and complaints.
                   The WB-PIU1 field staff will help the PAP produce documents to support his/her claims. The
                   Su-Committee and WB-PIU1 together will be responsible for addressing the grievances and
                   complaints within ten (10) days from the day they were filed.

                           8 MONITORING AND SUPERVISION OF RAP
Monitoring and supervision of the RAP implementation will be conducted to ensure that RAP is executed in
accordance with its plan and that its stated objectives are being met. Monitoring and supervision is also
important to ensure that anything that was overlooked or underestimated during the design of the RAP can be
corrected in a timely manner, and appropriate actions can be taken.

Monitoring and supervision process per se is as important as its results and findings. The results and findings of
the monitoring and supervisions must be able to reflect the reality of RAP design and implementation. These
findings and results must not cover up any flaws that may be due to either the design or the implementation of
RAP. They shall be able to provide technical and professional viewpoints. For this purpose, personnel and
agency specializing and with experiences in RAP monitoring activities or in social sector works will be appointed
and responsible for conducting the monitoring and supervision of the implementation of RAP. The monitoring
will be carried out in two phases.

8.1 PHASE 1: ON-GOING MONITORING
This type of monitoring will be conducted regularly during the implementation of RAP, and will be divided into
two distinct categories, namely internal and external monitoring.

8.1.1     On-going Internal Monitoring
This monitoring will be conducted by the RAP implementing agency. The responsibilities of the internal
monitoring will be assigned to the Resettlement Unit (RU) of the WB-PIU1. Overall monitoring and supervision
activities will possibly be carried by the NAC and Sub-Committee as a follow on activities of the RU's internal
monitoring works to ensure that the policies and procedures specified in the RAP are adhered to properly and
effectively.

The on-going internal monitoring shall start two weeks, the latest, after the commencement of the RAP
implementation, and shall have provided at least following information to the RAP and Project management
teams: (a) background information of RAP, (b) design and progress of implementation of RAP and lessons to
be learned, (c) cooperation and awareness among concerned stakeholders, (d) fund disbursement for RAP
implementation, and (e) problems and challenges facing the implementation and remedial/corrective measures
to deal with them.

The internal monitoring shall be conducted regularly, and the report will be prepared and submitted to WB-PIU1,
NAC, and Sub-Committee on monthly basis, and made available to interested parties/stakeholders upon
request. Although, the monitoring report will be prepared in English for the consumption by WB-PIU1, NAC, and
other stakeholders knowledgeable of English, it is necessary that its Executive Summary be made available in
Khmer version so that members of Su-Committee, and PAP could have access to.

External monitoring will be needed to get independent views and objective assessment of the [design and]

8.3 PROPOSED METHOD FOR MONITORING
Monitoring agency needs to be familiar with the situation of the Project site, and RAP. This can be achieved

8.3.1    Desk Study
This will involve reviewing existing information and documents that are relevant to the Project and RAP. The
executing agency shall make all available information and documents of relevance accessible to the monitoring

disposal. It shall also coordinate for collaboration for the monitoring agency to conduct their works properly and

8.3.2    Agency Visits
Following reviews of existing, relevant documents, the monitoring agency shall visit certain, relevant agencies
such as MPWT's WB-PIU1, NAC, MAFF, MEF, MRD, MOE, MOC, and Sub-Committee to discuss and collect
information about the Project and RAP. Discussion could centre around the topics indicated in the sections on
external monitoring, and impact evaluation above.

8.3.3    Observation and Participatory Impact Appraisal
To get full picture of situation on the field level of the RAP implementation, and impact of the Project and RAP,
for the external monitoring, and impact evaluation, respectively, the monitoring agency will conduct field/site
visits. During site visits the monitoring agency will make observations using field observation matrix; and
photographs will be taken as deemed necessary and relevant.

Participatory impact appraisal (PIA) will be conducted. This will involve discussion with groups of local
authorities, especially district authority and commune councils, and of PAP population. At least one PIA will be
conducted, respectively, with district authority, and commune councillors and/or village chiefs. Two or three PIA
will be facilitated with PAP population.

                                   9 RAP COST ESTIMATE AND BUDGET
Total cost and budget for RAP covers three important item lines, namely: (a) compensation costs and package,
(b) income restoration measures, and (c) operation/implementation, management and overhead costs.

The major costs which need to be added are operation/implementation, management and overhead and
contingency costs. Based on previous experiences in similar projects, the operation/implementation,
