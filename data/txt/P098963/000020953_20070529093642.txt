I. Introduction

1.     The proposed Second Rural and Social Policy Reform Grant (RSRC-2)' for SDR 33.1
million (US$50 million equivalent) will support the Government o f Niger (GoN) inits efforts
to improve service delivery in priority sectors, while ensuring adequate public expenditure
management. This RSRC-2 i s a one-year budget support operation to be disbursed in two
tranches. It will address policy and institutional bottlenecks inpublic sector management, as
well as rural and human development, where it is closely linked to ongoing investment
operations.
2.     Followingthe approach o f the previous RSRC, the areas o f support o f this operation are
closely aligned with the Government's medium-term reform program and will aim at: (i)
improving management o f public resources; (ii)             establishing an environment more conducive
to economic growth through the development o f both agriculture and the private sector; and
(iii)improving the coverage and quality o fbasic social services.
3.     The RSRCs have been built around a medium-term results framework which has
identified the proposed reforms in the rural and human development areas, as well as
subsequent steps to further support the growth agenda. This approach is aligned with the
Government's strategy, reflected in the PRSP-II, which identifies agriculture as the engine of
growth o f the economy, in the short-and medium-term. To further strengthen reforms in
support o f shared growth, the next reform operation (RSRC-3) will build upon the RSRC-1
and -2 and begin to address policy bottlenecks in private/financial sector development and
infrastructure, while completing the ongoing reforms in rural and human development. Key
issues in rural and human development sectors will continue to be addressed through the
Bank's investment portfolio which is expected to be enhanced, thus maintaining the
complementaritybetween the RSRC series andinvestment operations.


11. CountryContext

A.      Political and social context

4.     Consolidatingdemocracy and better leadership. Niger has undergone a fundamental
political transition during the last six years. General elections, presidential and legislative,
took place on November 16 and December 4, 2004, respectively. This was the first peaceful
and democratic transition between civilian governments in more than a decade. President
Tandja was comfortably re-elected, with 65 percent in the second round. Moreover, Niger's
first local elections were successfully completed on July 24, 2004, as part o f a program o f
political decentralization. It is expected that the successful consolidation o f democracy will
have positive implications for governance, commitment to reforms, poverty reduction and
economic prospects.

5.      Although Niger remains a very poor country with a limited natural and human
resourcebase, there are improvementsin economic and social performance. The country
i s ranked the lowest inthe world (177th out o f 177 countries in 2006) according to the United
Nations Development Program (UNDP) HumanDevelopment Index and had a GNIper capita
o f US$260 in 2006. However, the improved growth performance over the 2001-06 period i s
encouraging and reflects both favorable weather conditions in 2001 and 2006 and the impact
o f measures to promote growth, including increases in capital expenditures on infrastructure
and improvement infiscal management. Several social indicators have improved over the past
six years, in particular for education and health. The gross primary enrolment rate increased
from about 29 percent inthe early 1990s to 52 percent in2006, and the ratio o f girls to boys in
primary schools also increased from 25 to 40 percent over the same period. The under five
mortality rate has decreased from 320 to 198 per thousand'between 1990 and 2006. The
prevalence o f HN/AIDS i s at less than 1percent, which is low for Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA).
Inspite ofprogress, however, gender differences persist. Data from end-2005 indicate that
prevalence among young women (15-24) is, at 0.8 percent, four times higher than prevalence
among males o f the same age (0.2 percent), suggesting that young women remain especially
vulnerable to HN, even when overall prevalence rates are low.
6.      Despiteimprovementsinvarious social indicators,Niger remainsbehindon most of
the MilleniumDevelopmentGoals(MDGs) and if currenttrends persist,it will not reach
most of them by 2015. The recent Country Economic Memorandum (CEM),* notes that
Niger is currently o f f track inachieving the MDGs, and that to change this prospect requires a
forward-looking and comprehensive public investment program in priority sectors. Niger's
government has already begun implementing a Policy Action Program targeting the
achievement o f the MDGs. However, given its current financial resources, the country cannot
finance the required investment program, and therefore substantial external assistance is
needed.

Fiscal developmentsand challenges

12. Niger is a member country o f West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU)
and shares a common currency (CFA.F) with the other member countries. The Central Bank's
(BCEAO) monetary policy is driven by a fixed exchange rate arrangement, since the CFAF is
peggedto the Euro. The fixed peg and convertibility arrangements o f the CFA Zone require
fiscal discipline from all member countries.                     The arrangements were spelled out in the
Regional Pact o f Convergence, Stability, Growth and Solidarity adopted by the Conference of

                                                     - 5 -

Heads o f States o f WAEMU in December 1999.                     In 2006, Niger met six out of nine
convergence ~ r i t e r i a . ~Inflation i s in general low in the sub-region due to the discipline
imposed by the monetary framework o f the CFA zone and has been kept around 3 percent in
Niger over the last six year period, except for 2005, when it averaged 7.8 percent following the
food crisis and the oil price hikes. Inflation i s expected to decline to an average o f 2 percent
over 2007-09 compared to an average o f4 percent between 2005 and2006.
13.    Niger's revenue/GDPratio of 12.9 percentof GDPin2006 remainsthe lowestinthe
WAEMU sub-region. Porous borders with Nigeria and continued nominal depreciation
between Nigeria Naira and the CFAF reinforce informal trade, which leads to a loss o f
revenues for Niger. While government revenue increased from 9.3 percent o f GDP in2001 to
12.9 percent in 2006 as a result o f revenue-enhancing measures introduced since 2001, a
challenge remains for attaining WAEMU criteria o f 17 percent, given the narrow tax base and
the geographic constraints o fNiger.
14.    Government has taken measures to improve revenue mobilization. The core
measures taken by the authorities for improving revenue mobilization include: (i)                  petroleum
tax reform in August 2001; (ii)           adoption o f monthly performance indicators for the main
customs offices and consistently tracking compliance with these indicators; (iii)             establishment

Debt sustainability, MDRI, and the fiscal space issue

16.   The recently completed (December 2006) joint IMF-World Bank debt sustainability
analysis (DSA) shows that, while Niger's debt outlook improved significantly in the short-
term, the country needs to sustain its debt management efforts to ensure that debt distress
issues do not become a concern. The risk o f debt distress has declined with the granting o f
assistance under the Highly Indebted Poor Country (HIPC) and Multilateral Debt Relief
(MDRI) initiatives. The Net Present Value (NPV) of debt-to-exports ratio fell from 136
percent in 2005 to 46 percent in 2006 and i s forecast to increase steadily to 107 percent in
2025, due to Niger's high financing requirements to sustain investment levels required to
increase the economy's growth potential. The ratio would remain well below the 150 percent
threshold, however. Nevertheless, debt management needs to remain cautious, given the
economy's vulnerability to external shocks. This vulnerability will be reduced if efforts to
broaden the export base are successful. Thus, the government is expected to focus on growth-
enhancing measures and investment, while continuing to seek grants and highly concessional
loans.

Agriculture and ruraldevelopment

45.    Despite unfavorable agro-climatic conditions and recurrent natural disasters, agriculture
and rural development continue to play a key role for the growth o f Niger's economy. The
rural sector contributes to about 40 percent o f GDP, a declining trend since 1980, with
agriculture accounting for about 52 percent and livestock about 30 percent o f rural output.
Agro-pastoral products account for about 85 percent o f export revenues excluding uranium,
with the livestock sector alone accounting for 56 percent (mostly live exports). Overall, the
country's agricultural trade balance remains heavily in deficit, and imports o f foodstuffs
represent a third o f the country's import bill. Subsistence farming and animal husbandry,
based on low-productivity and traditional techniques, are pursued by the majority o f the rural
population. Almost 85 percent o f the country's population live in rural areas, most o f them
below the poverty line. With increasing population concentration in the most productive
zones, the sustainable management o f productive natural resources, particularly land and
water, has become essential to Niger's agricultural growth andrural poverty reduction.

Key issues inagriculture andrural development

46.    The country's slow recovery from the 2005 food crisis has been accelerated by a
favorable agricultural year in 2006. Despite a difficult start inthe current season due to late
and unevenly distributed rainfalls, 2006-07 has been an exceptionally good year for rain-fed
agricultural production. The Ministry o f Agricultural Development has reported production
figures in all major crops to be well above early projections. With the irrigation season well
underway, it i s likely that the 2006-07 agricultural season will be well above average.
Nevertheless, shortages and difficult access to agricultural production inputs (high yielding
seedvarieties, fertilizer), are likely to prevent again growth inirrigated agriculture this year.

                                               - 15 -

47.    With an average annual sectoral growth below 2 percent over the past 40 years, well
below the annual population growth rate, the challenges for the rural economy as an engine o f
growth are daunting. The biggest challenge is to achieve rapid and sustainable modernization
of the agriculture and livestock sectors conducive to rural growth and poverty reduction. In
view of weak market access opportunities, insufficient land tenure security, poor access to key
services (rural finance, agricultural research, knowledge and information systems, veterinary
services), low private investment for the expansion of irrigated agriculture, for the
improvement o f rain-fed fanning systems, and for the intensification o f livestock production,
much o f Niger's rural growth potential remains untapped. As an example, the estimated
irrigable land covers 270,000 ha, o f which less than 100,000 ha have so far been developed,
andeven less actually beingirrigated.
48.    Food insecurity and vulnerability. Food insecurity, affecting 80 percent o f the
population, i s chronic inNiger. It i s aggravated by highincidences of rural poverty, translated
at the household level in low purchasing power and lack o f access to food by vulnerable
groups. Following a locust invasion and an extended drought, the 2004-05 agricultural season
was marked by a 25 percent decline inproductioncompared to the average o f the five previous
years. Because o f the low institutional capacity in the sector agencies, the agricultural crisis
(cereal and fodder shortage) quickly deteriorated into a severe food crisis, illustrating the

65.   Despite considerable improvement over the last 10 years, progress in the health sector

Key issues inhealth

66.   As in most countriesof West Africa, Niger's health sector suffers from inadequate
financing, limited supply of basic health services and weak management capacities.
Indeed, the share o f the health budget in the national budget averages about 7 percent,
equivalent to an annual per capita health expenditure o f about US$6, which i s far below some
o f the global health care financing benchmarks. Furthermore, there is a disconnect between
financial resource mobilization and availability and schedules governing the purchase o f inputs
needed to control diseases. For instance, the budgetary allocations for priority activities are
not always aligned with the procurement timing o f primary drugs and vaccines. The budget
execution rate is low and budget transfers to the regions are inadequate due to the lack o f

resource availability. As a result, most regions are facing deficit in essential generic drugs,
vaccines, andother medical supplies.
67.    Access to health facilities is inequitable while the health services delivered are
seriously inadequate in terms of quality. More than 50 percent o f the population is still
more than 5 kilometers from a health facility providing basic curative andpreventive services,
and one out of six lives more than 15 km from the nearest health center. The quality o f
available health services and their coverage are both severely limited. Given the poor quality
o f services, the average per capita usage rates have been declining from 42 percent in 1996 to
26 percent in2006. Utilization o f basic health services i s similarly very low, andi s worse than
inmany other countries at a comparable economic level. For example, coverage of routine
immunization, one o f the cheapest and easiest basic health interventions, i s particularly
problematic, as only 29 percent o f one-year-old children are fully immunized.
68.    The shortage of health personnel and its uneven distribution is a key problemin
resource managementin the sector. Staff allocation among regions and between rural and
urban areas is inadequate. Niamey, and to a lesser extent other urban centers, attracts health
professionals with better opportunities for additional income and career growth and better
living conditions than rural areas. Niger has only one physician for 46,290 people, however,
while the WHO norm is one for 10,000.               A similar deficit exists for midwives, while
availability o f nurses i s better. These ratios are significantly worse inpoor andrural areas than
inricher urbanareas (e.g., Niameyconcentrates 33 percent ofthe total health staffwith only 6
percent o f the nation's population). These issues are compounded by poorly motivated staff

and support systems, leading to delays in financial flows, procurement, and personnel and
payroll management, and co-ordination between departments and levels. As a result, there are
no incentives for already low paidworkers to operate inrural areas.
69.    While Government has increased hding to the sector, including the private-not-for-
profit (PNFP) sub sector, facilities are still under-funded.           Many health facilities are

health services for the most vulnerable groups (pre and postnatal care for children under five

Key issues indemographics

76.   The high rate of population growth (3.d percent per annum) impedes iiger's
abilityto reducepoverty and meetthe MDGs. Inthe 2006 Demographic andHealth Survey

literacy, early age at marriage, andpoverty. Marriage is universal and early, especially inrural
areas: one intwo women i s marriedbythe age of 15. Highfertility rates have also contributed
to a very young age structure: close to 50 percent o f Niger's population i s under the age of 15.
This inturnfuels demographic growth as manyyoungpeople enter their reproductive age.

D.         Lessons learned

104. The following lessons are drawn from the experience o f implementing RSRC-1, and are
taken into account inpreparing RSRC-2:
105. StrengthenGovernmentownershipand commitmentto the reformprogram. Based
on previous budget support as well as RSRC-1, to ensure success inmedium term reforms, it
i s necessary to establish and maintain support within the administration, including better
communication among the various sectoral ministries. Although the main components o f the
RSRC series are derived from the Government o f Niger's own medium-term reform program
and are in line with PRSP objectives, efforts in implementing reforms are slow and there is
need to strengthen commitment to a mediumterm reform vision. Therefore, there is a need for
continuous awareness raising with MEF and line ministries on the need to adopt a medium-
term vision, to monitor progress on indicators, and ensure that the multisectoral RSRCs
Monitoringand Implementationcommittee carries out its mandate.
106. Raise awareness on difference betweeninvestmentoperations and budget support,
in order to emphasize the necessary articulation of the reform program with the State
budget. Budget support operations are different from project support and accordingly there is
a need to change the way teams operate. It i s necessary to continue to raise awareness on key
differences between project and budget support among the Government counterparts, in order
to ensure a common understanding and smoother implementation. It is also crucial that

                                             - 33 -

sectors see that the budget support brings financing o f priority programs, so as to secure the
continuous involvement o f sectoral ministries.
107. Address weak institutional capacity through close coordinationwith the ongoing
sectoralinvestmentoperations. The reform program supported bythe RSRC series is based
on a full-fledged medium-term strategy agreed upon with the authorities and targeted at the
central ministries. Such medium-term approach needs to be firmly grounded inthe findings o f
a key diagnostic study on public expenditure management (PEMFAR), as well as specific
sectoral studies (rural development strategy, education and health sector work, and
demographic study) that were preparedjointly with the Government and development partners.
Experience during the implementation o f the RSRC-1 has shown that technical assistance for
capacity building needs to be strengthened through ongoing investment operations. Since
budget support has limited access to short-term consultancies or technical assistance from the
Bank side during preparation, closer collaboration among teams working on investment
operations as well as coordination with other donors i s a prerequisite to enhance institutional
capacities. Inthis context, the core pillars o f the reform program supported by the RSRC
(public finance, rural development, and human development) are closely coordinated with the
ongoing investment operations as well as other donors support (IMF, EU, AFD, etc.).
108. The two-tranche approach used in the RSRC-1 has helped the Government
implementreforms. The RSRC-1 has shown that beingbased on two tranches, the operation
was successfully implemented, requiring only one partial waiver.            More specifically,
continuous dialogue, close supervision and technical support have helped the Government to
implement reform measures. In this context, the proposed RSRC-2 will also include two
tranches.
109. Align budgetsupport operationswith the Government'sbudgetarycycle. Given the
political and economic environment o f Niger, however, all adjustment operations for Niger
(including this RSRC-1 and the proposedRSRC-2) are prepared around June o f each calendar
year, while the Government budget, presented to the Parliament duringthe third quarter o f the
previous year, already has incorporatedBank financing.

VI. Operationimplementation

A.      Poverty andsocial impactsand environment


Poverty and social impact

146. The RSRCs would provide incremental resources to finance government PRSP
priorities, which should translate into improved services to the poor. Specifically, achieving
faster poverty reduction and progress towards the MDGs, paying more attention to public
expenditure management (Le., better linkages o f the PRSP to the budget) i s often the most
important reform that a government can implement. The proposed operation is focused on
these issues, with practical proposals for the country to move forward.            Apart from
strengthening public expenditure management, the measures in the proposed RSRC-2will be
instrumental in increasing the impact of public spending on the poor inkey areas. The focus
on rural development and human development (education and health) targets the priority
sectors that are arguably the most crucial for poverty reduction.
147. The proposedRSRC-2will continue supporting Niger inits effort to alleviate poverty in
a number o f ways. First, it aims at promoting agricultural growth through implementing the
programs o f the rural development strategy such as improving land tenure security, increasing
efficiency o f national system to manage food crisis, andpromoting local governance o f natural
resources, which are viewed as important means for agriculture development andprotection of
vulnerable. The expansion of rural development will fuel economic growth, which in turn i s
expected to helpthe poor.
148. Second, the program seeks to improve the effectiveness o f public spending.             By
supporting budgetary and fiduciary reforms, the RSRC program will help to increase the
efficiency inthe operation and thus inthe allocation o f public expenditures insectors such as
agriculture and social services, which are directly linked to the growth and poverty alleviation
agenda. The emphasis on decentralization also responds to the needto strengthen the delivery
o f public services inthe rural regions and local communities, where the highest percentage o f
poor households can be found.
149. Third, the operation improves the stock o f human capital through actions that should
lead to better access and quality o f health services, particularly among women and children,
while curbing population growth.
150. Fourth, it aims at improving directly the living conditions o f the most vulnerable groups
such as women and children by providing them fully subsidized services, such as pre-natal
consultations and health care for children under 5, contraceptives, and caesarean services.

BACKGROUND,STRATEGYAND GOALS


1.      With an estimated population o f 12.6 million inhabitants, Niger is a landlocked
Sahelian country with the nearest seaport more than 1000 km away. It i s one o f the poorest
countries in the world. The most recent poverty survey conducted in 2006 based on the Core
Welfare Indicators Questionnaire (CWIQ) estimates Niger's incidence o f poverty at 62
percent. The very limited resources available to the economy, fluctuating growth (0.8% in
2004, then 7.2% and 4.8% in 2005 and 2006 respectively) and the high sociopolitical
instability inthe 1990s added to highpopulation growth (3.3% in2005), are the major causes
o fpoverty.

2.      Despite improving social indicators, poverty remains widespread inNiger both inrural
andurbanareas:
    0   per capita income fell from 391US Dollarin 1975 to 270 US Dollar in2006;
    0   life expectancy at birthi s among the lowest inthe world, 46 years in2005;
    0   infant death rate and under five death rate are respectively 198 per thousand (274 per
        thousand in 1992) and 81 per thousand in2005 (123 per thousand in 1992);
        the gross enrolment rate in2006 was 53.5% (59% for boys and41% for girls);


3.      Inan effort to tackle widespread poverty and the low level of basic social indicators,
Niger elaborated in January 2002 a Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS). The PRS that was
implemented provides a framework for coordinating Government and donors policies and
defines the strategic orientations for resource allocation. Its strategic objectives are: (i)  to
create a stable macroeconomic environment so as to accelerate economic growth andpreserve
economic stability in the medium and long terms; (ii)    to accelerate the growth o f productive
sectors particularly in rural areas so as to reduce food vulnerability and increase income
generation; (iii)to improve access to quality basic social services for the poor; and (iv) to
encourage good governance and to strengthen human and institutional capacities. The strategy
is undergoing revision and the PRS 2 i s currently being finalized. A workshop was organized
inearly April 2007 to validate its technical aspects andit is expected that it will be adopted by
the Government o fNiger inMay 2007. The policy areas o f PRS 2 do not conflict with those o f
PRS 1. The revised strategy specifically addresses cross-cutting thematic areas like population
growth, decentralization and gender.

4.     Niger's economy experienced high growth inthe last two years (an average o f almost
6% for 2005 and 2006). The estimated growth rate for 2007 is 4.5%. Inflationwas contained in
2006 (0.1% at an annual rate whereas it attained 7.8% in2005). Fiscal policies implementedin
2005 and 2006 ledto significant reduction o f budget deficits, with the total balance improving
from -9.4% o f the GNP in2005 to -5.7% in2006. Furthermore, the current balance (excluding
grants) improved from -11.9% in 2005 to -10.6% in 2006. However, it should be noted that
financing requirements in 2005 and 2006 were entirely met through concessional loans and
grants. The third Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) was approved in December
2006 by the Executive Board o f the International Monetary Fund. This underlines the positive
macroeconomic and financial results obtained in recent years. The fourth review was
conducted inMarcWApril2007 and its conclusions included inthe 2007 project memorandum

                                             - 85 -

on economic and financial policies that will be submitted for approval by the IMF's Executive
Board inJune 2007.

5.       The 2007 Finance Law elaborates the PRS priorities, allocating over 51% o f resources
to key sectors (agriculture and rural development, education and health) with support from
various World Bank programs - Rural and Social Policy Reform Credit (RSRCs), investment
projects and investment programs -to finance the fight against poverty.

6.       This Letter o f Development Policy (LDP) is in line with the major elements in the
PRS. It discusses recent changes in the implementation o f reforms inthe key areas and offers
mid-termperspectives. It pays particular attentionto reforms inthe priority sectors o fthe PRS
(management o f the public sector, social sectors and rural development sector), challenges to
bemet, results achieved particularly inimplementing the first reform support program for rural
development and RSRCl social sectors, RSRC2 ongoing reforms and the potential triggers
identified for RSRC3.

BUDGET    PREPARATION

9.       The main goal is to ensure budget policy coherence with the PRS. This goal was
clearly underlined inbudget circulars sent to ministries inpreparation o f the 2007 budget. This
will be done in future years by aligning Finance Law allocations with PRS priority goals and
the Medium Term Expenditure Frameworks (MTEFs) for the education, health and rural
development sectors in addition to rigorous monitoring o f poverty reduction expenditures, the
list o f which has been revised and codified in 2007 (RSRC2). The same list will be used for
the preparation o f the 2008 Finance Law (RSRC3). The Government will increase the level o f
expenditure devoted to fightingpoverty from 49%'in 2007 to 53% in2008 and 66% in2011.

10.      To bring2008 budget allocations inline with major concerns o f the sector ministries as
defined inthe revised PRS, from a budget baseline o f 7.3%,17.7% and 25.3% in2005 for the

BUDGET   EXECUTION

11.     The Government hopes to improve budget execution by increasing appropriations from
one year to the next and by securing priority expenditures. In this regard, the available
instruments, particularly budget regulation and cash management planning, are used in
collaboration with the technical ministries incharge o f priority sectors. Thus, the execution o f
the Cash Management Plan at the end o f December 2006 was handled by the Ministry o f
Economy and Finance. The expenditures for priority sectors were protected. The Government
will increase the level o f spending inpriority areas (education, health and rural development)
to at least 90% in2008.

12.     The 2007 cash management plantook the same approach andwas inline with the goals
identified above. In the annual cash management plan (including that for 2007), the
Government will adjust estimates to priority expenditures effectively achieved.

13.     Integrating the 2008 entry balance in accounts o f the overall treasury balance i s a
necessary measure and should be completed by June 30, 2007 as agreed with IMF. The
implementation o f this measure i s the final step to normalizing the State's final accounts and
tracking budget execution.

14.     To improve the efficiency o f its services and comply with regional directives adopted
by the Government, the Ministry o f Economy and Finance was reorganized by Decree
N02006-322/PRN/MEF on December 13, 2006. Decree No2006-323PRN/MEF o f December
13, 2006, relative to the organization and missions o f the Treasury and Public Accounts
Directorate (DGTCP))       transforms the Treasury into a Treasury and Public Accounts
Directorate. Decree The decree also creates the Financial Control Directorate (DGCF) andthe
Procurement Directorate (DGCMP).

15.     The next step o f the reorganization process will be (i)the nomination o f the managers
o f the DGTCP (Director, Central Accountant, National Treasurer and Receiver General
(RSRC2); and (ii)  the recruitment o fhighlevel managers to strengthen the capacity o fMEF.

32.     Niger has written a 10-year education development plan(PDDE2003-2012) inline with

Strategy. The planenabled Niger to be among the first countries chosen inthe Educationfor All -

36.     The regulatory framework for Ecoles Normales d'Instituteurs - ENIs - (Teacher

elaborated in April 2007) aimed at improving the quality and efficiency o f educational
spending.      The Government will introduce a more coherent approach to planning and
budgetingfor recruitment o fnew teachers andother staff following an actionplanbased onthe
recommendations from the review.

37.     To improve on staff management and posting, an action plan was adopted in 2006

Onthe basis o fthis final evaluation, the actionplanwill be revisedby2008 (RSRC3).

38.     To encourage good governance and responsible management by sharing information

HEALTH

39.     In accordance with the PRS and following the introduction of the Strategic
Orientations for the health sector for 2002-2011, a HealthDevelopment Plan (PDS 2005-2010)
was adopted by the Government in February2005. This plan provides the basis for assisting
the health sector. Among the problems it strives to tackle are: high maternal and under-five
death rates; low population access to quality treatment; insufficient staff both in terms o f
quantity and quality; insufficiency o f essential medicines and quality consumables; and the
institutional weakness o f the MSP. Its overall goal is to reduce maternal and under-five death
rates by improving on the efficiency and quality o f the health system given the level o f
currently available resources.

                    NIGER: Assessment Letterfor the World Bank

                                       April 20,2007

Background

1. The government o f Niger has adopted a Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP I)
which aimed at enhancing macroeconomic stability; increasing public investment,
especially inirrigation infrastructure; deepeninghumancapital formation; andbuttressing
economic governance.        The IMF supports this strategy throughout a three-year
arrangement under the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) as well through
technical assistance.    The Bank supports the strategy through Development Policy
Lending and Sectoral InvestmentProjects coupledwith technical assistance.

StaffAssessment of MacroeconomicdevelopmentsandStructuralReform

2. Since 2000, the Nigerien authorities have maintained a strong commitment to prudent
policies and reforms. The policy and reform agenda was underpinned by a poverty
reduction strategy (PRS) adopted in 2002, which i s being updated. A large number o f
reforms have been introduced since 2000 to improve public expenditure management,
strengthen domestic revenue, and enhance the economy's supply reforms.

3. However, over the past five years real GDP growth has not been sufficient to improve
per capita income, and social indicators remain weak. Inthis regard, Niger remains one
o f the poorest countries in the World and is far from achieving the Millennium
Development Goals (MDGs).            Economic growth has been constrained by weak
productivity. Inparticular, inadequate irrigation infrastructure and limited use o f modern
inputs have undermined agricultural performance. Also, economic diversification has
been hampered by infrastructure bottlenecks.
4. In 2004-05, Niger's economy was hit by a severe drought that had significant
socioeconomic costs.      In late 2004, the drought significantly reduced agricultural
production, triggered a food crisis, andraised inflation during the first half o f 2005. The
external current account deficit remained stable because o f a significant increase in
humanitarianassistance. However, the situation improved in late 2005, with a bumper
harvest pushing real GDP growth for the year as a whole to 6.8 percent, and easing
inflationary pressures.
5. In 2006 macroeconomic performance was broadly satisfactory. Real GDP growth
slowed down to 3.4 percent. Growth in the agriculture sector moderated, with cereal
production remaining close to the record level o f late 2005; performance in the mining
and services has been strong. Average inflation declined to 0.3 percent on average in
2006. In2006, the external current account deficit (including grants) remained close to
2005 level as lower food imports offset the high cost o f fuel imports. Niger's external

                                          - 109 -

debt burden was further reduced by debt relief under the Multilateral Debt Relief
Initiative (MDRI) totaling SDR 638 million (US$956.7 million) from the Fund, IDA and
the African Development Fund. This reduced the NPV o f debt-to-export ratio from
136percent in2005 to 46 percent in2006.
6. The implementation o f macroeconomic policies thus far in2006 has been satisfactory.
Inthe first halfof 2006, the basic fiscal deficit was significantly lower thanprogrammed
because o f higher revenue and lower spending.        Improvements in tax and customs
administration (achieved mainly by tightening control o f import valuation and transit
operations, and reinforcing the auditing o f large- and medium-sized enterprises) boosted
revenue collection. Good harvest and strong external assistance facilitated a significant
buildup o f the strategic grain reserves. For the year as whole, the fiscal deficit and
domestic financing were lower than originally envisaged. The Central Bank of West
African States pursued a prudent monetary policy.
7. The authorities continued to implement reforms to strengthen revenue, enhance public
expenditure management (PEM), and increase the supplyresponse o f the economy. They
prepared and started implementing an action plan with a focus on strengthening tax and
customs administration.    On PEM, they have advanced the computerization o f the
operations o f the treasury and budget departments, completed a comprehensive
assessment o f Niger's public financial management systems, and adopted medium-term
expenditure plans for key sectors. Other key reforms included the adoption o f new
mining code, steps to streamline business regulations, and the removal o f duties on
imports (manufacturing, animal products and vegetables) from all members o f the
Economic Community o f West African States.


Status of FundRelations
8. The Executive Board o f the International Monetary Fund (IMF) completed on
December 20, 2006 the third review o f Niger's economic performance under a three-year
Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) arrangement. The completion o f the
review enables the release o f a further SDR 5.9 million (about US$8.9 million), which
will bring the total amount disbursed under the arrangement to SDR 23.5 million (about
US$35.4 million). Niger's PRGF arrangement was approved on January 31,2005 for a
total amount of SDR 6.58 millions which was increased to a total access under the
arrangement to SDR 26.32 million (about US$37.5 million) inNovember 2005.

9. The Board waived Niger's non-observance o f the quantitative performance criterion
on the reduction in domestic payment arrears on government obligations and the
continuous structural performance criterion on the application o f the petroleum pricing
mechanism.

10. In concluding the third review, IMF Executive Directors stressed the need to
persevere on implementing the program o f strong macroeconomic policies and economic
reforms in2007 andbeyondinthe following areas:

                                   -110-


The 2007 fiscal program calls for shifting expenditure toward priority outlays. It
also emphasizes continued improvements inthe tax and customs administrations
aimed at broadening the tax base by tightening exemptions and simplifjmg tax
procedures.    Steadfast implementation o f reforms in public expenditure
management will be critical for enhancing the effectiveness o fNiger's pro-growth
andpro-poor programs.

The debt relief under the Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative (MDRI) has reduced
Niger's external debt burden significantly. The authorities are committed to using
the resources freed up by the MDRI effectively, so as to advance toward the
Millennium Development Goals (MDGs).        To preserve debt sustainability, it i s
important that the authorities strengthen debt management and rely only on
highly concessional resources, preferably inthe form o f grants, to finance highly
productive investment.

The authorities plan to adopt in 2007 a revised Poverty Reduction Strategy for
2008-2012.    In the face of the significant challenges that lie ahead as Niger
advances toward the MDGs, the strategy will outline policies and reforms to help
sustain high economic growth and reduce poverty.         Increased investment in
humancapital andinfrastructure will be required, and government investment in
key sectors will need to be accompanied by reforms to promote private sector
development. Other priorities are to deepen financial intermediation, including
through the development o f a viable microfinance system; to reform the
regulatory framework; and to enhance the efficiency o f keypublic utilities.
