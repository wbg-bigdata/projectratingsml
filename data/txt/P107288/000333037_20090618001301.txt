                                      1. INTRODUCTION

1.1     This programdocumentproposes a PublicFinanceSupportCredit for the Republic
of Senegal in the amount of SDR40.4 million (US$60 million equivalent). This is a stand-
alone Development Policy Operation, following a series of three Poverty Reduction Support
Credits (PRSCs) successfully completed over 2003-2007 and ahead of a new PRSC series
expectedto be initiated by FY10. The operation aims to support the authorities' efforts to bring
their fiscal stance to a sound and sustainable track, mitigate fiscal risks emanating from public
sector entities and protect critical developmental objectives at a time when the authorities must
maintain a tight fiscal policy and cope with the impact of the global crisis. Both the urgent need
for financial assistance in a highly uncertain global context and the current focus of policy
makers on fiscal issues make a stand-alone operation a more appropriate instrument for Bank
support to the authorities at thisjuncture than a new series of Poverty Reduction Support Credits.
This approachwould also provide time to the authorities for the design and implementation of a
medium-termreform agenda that could be supported under the next series of PRSCs. Policies
and reforms supported by this operation are well aligned with Senegal's Poverty Reduction
Strategy Paper. The proposed operation i s also fully consistent with the objectives of the Bank's
FY07-10 Country Assistance Strategy.

1.2     Senegal, which had long prided itself on the strength of its macroeconomic
management,had an unusuallyweak fiscalperformancein 2008. Budget slippages emerged
early inthe year, reflecting the interplay of external shocks and internal policy factors-the fiscal
impact of the oil and food price shocks, difficulties inaccessing financing inthe regional money
markets, and weaknesses in fiscal policy and public financial management. In response, the
authorities adopted a plan of fiscal and structural corrective actions that was endorsed by the
IMF Board inlate December. There has beenstrong progress inlate 2008 and early 2009 inthe
implementation of these corrective actions, and a clear demonstration of resolve to avoid a
recurrenceof budget slippages.

1.3     The country is trying to emerge from its 2008 fiscal problems at a time of
extraordinary stress in the global environment. The major channels of transmission of the
global economic recession to the Senegalese economy are exports, remittances, tourism and
foreign direct investment. Being a very open economy, Senegal's exposure to the global crisis i s
significant. However, the already evident strength of the authorities' response to their 2008
fiscal problems is turning out to provide a critical boost that, together with a prudent
macroeconomic management going forward, should help Senegal weather the global crisis, at
least through 2009.

1.4     It is in this challengingcontext that the proposedoperation aims to support a core
set of critical actions to restore budget discipline and avoid a recurrence of slippages by
accelerating public financial management reforms, mitigate against fiscal risks emanating from
weak corporate governance of public sector entities and protecting key developmental objectives

decentralization.  These measures are complementary to critical actions being taken by the
authorities under their IMF-supported Policy Support Instrumentprogram, whose third review i s
slatedto be discussedat the IMF Board on June 19,2009.

1.5     The Credit is beingprocessed under the IDA FinancialCrisis Response Fast-Track
Facility to support the Government's efforts to meet urgent financing needs and mitigate the
impact of the global crisis. As a result of the crisis, growth for 2009 is projected at 3.1 percent,
down from an earlier projection of 5% percent, and revenues are expected to fall short of the
budget's original targets by about 10 percent. The Government has requested that IDA delivery
be accelerated in order to meet urgent needs of the population. Inaddition to this credit, whose
amount exceeds by US$20 million equivalent the amount originally plannedfor a fourth PRSC,
IDA is helping to mitigate the impact of the crisis through the Rapid Response Child-Focused
Social Cash Transfer and Nutrition Security Project (US$18 million) to reduce the risk of

numberor size of future operations.

                                 2. COUNTRY CONTEXT

                            A. RECENT     POLITICAL DEVELOPMENTS

2.1     Senegalcontinues to enjoy a broadly stablepoliticalclimate. The Senegalesepolitical
system continued to show a degree of democratic maturity with the recent holding of local
elections, on March 22, 2009, that were widely viewed as fair and transparent, and saw the
victory of opposition parties in several key urban centers. On April 30, the Prime Minister
resigned and his successor rapidly nominated a new cabinet. Key Ministers, including the
Minister of Economy and Finance and the Minister of Budget, kept their post, thus ensuring a
measure of policy continuity.       Nonetheless, the victory of the opposition in important
municipalities suggests that policies and reforms on which a broad consensus has not been
forged could be more difficult to implement. The need for an effective and timely response was
a critical lesson that the authorities have learned from recent crises, but in the new political
context they should be expected to still grapple with issues of consensus building as they
confront the impact of the current global crisis on Senegal.

                            B. RECENT    ECONOMIC      DEVELOPMENTS

2.2     From the mid-90s until2005, Senegal had one of the best economic performancesin
Sub-Saharan Africa. Though there were brief episodes of low growth, real GDP grew on
average by about 5 percent annually, marking the first sustained increase in average per capita
growth since independence in 1960. Poverty declined rapidly-the         fraction of the population
that fell below the poverty line declined from 68 percent in 1994/95 to 51 percent in 2005/06.
The regional monetary arrangement-a       currency union, managed by the supranational Central


                                                2

Bank of the West African States, with a peg to the French franc until 1999, and the euro since
then-provided       a framework of monetary and price stability. On the fiscal policy front, the
authorities pursued a broadly stable track, enhancing revenue mobilization and maintaining
control on current expenditures, while allowing for a gradual increase in capital and pro-poor
spending. The achievement of the HIPC completion point in 2004 and, in 2005, MDRI, and
prudentconcessionalexternal borrowingallowed a sharpreduction inthe stock of debt.

2.3      In 2006-07, however, a series of shocks buffeted the Senegalese economy. The runup
inoil prices and, starting in2007, the surge infood prices weighed down on the economy and
drove end-of-period inflation to 4 percent in 2006 and 6 percent in 2007 from an average of %
percent over the previous three years. Escalating oil and food prices also contributed to a
widening of the external current account deficit-Senegal              imports all its oil (which powers most
of its electricity), and respectively 80 and 100 percent of its rice and wheat consumption.
Sharply higher oil prices and a policy-mandated incomplete pass-through to retail electricity
prices compounded internal inefficiency inthe electricity parastatal, SENELEC,resulting inhigh
costs and blackouts, which hurt economic activity.' Other shocks were internal. Poor rains in
both 2006 and 2007 ledto a fall of agricultural output of about 15 percent in each year. Despite
the sector's small share in GDP-about                  7 percent in 2 0 0 6 , falling agricultural output
contributed to trim at least one percentage point in overall GDP growth in both years and to
exacerbate the impact on the population of escalating food prices. Deep financial difficulties in
the largest exporting firm, the phosphate mining and phosphoric acid production firm ICs which
i s majority foreign private-owned, ledto a sharp slowdown inits activity, and another loss of one
percentagepoint of GDP growth in2006 (the company was recapitalized inearly 2008).

2.4      Nonetheless, growth, while falling below trend, managed to sustainsome momentum
over 2006-07. Real GDP growth slowed to 2% percent in 2006, but recovered to 4% percent in
2007. Mitigating the slowdown in agriculture and industry, sectoral performance was strong in
construction and the services sector. The telecom sector in particular continued to register
double-digit growth-the        number of subscribers to fixed or mobile phone lines stood at 88 per
100people in2007, comparedto 44 in2000. Importantly for Senegal's medium-termprospects,
an expansion of investment underpinned these developments: domestic investment expanded
from 24 percent of GDP in2003 to 31percent of GDP by 2007, with an estimated 1/3 increasein
both private and public investments. The investment expansion itself was fueled by the
continued robust growth in remittances (20 percent a year in nominal terms),2 steady expansion
o f public works, and increasing foreign direct investment in the telecom, port, and mining
sectors.

2.5      Macroeconomic performance weakened again in 2008 as a result of additional
shocks. Real GDP growth i s now estimated at 2% percent, while inflationremained above trend
(about 6 percent year-on-year at end-2008), as the prices of imported fuel and food surged
through the mid year. Favorable rains helped agriculture output return to its trend level, but
industrial production registered a small decline-the            expected recovery of ICs i s now set to take
full effect only by 2009. Construction and commercial activity slowed sharply inthe second half
of the year, with the combined effect of substantial payment delays to domestic suppliers that

2.10    On the strength of these responses, the IMF approved the second review of the PSI
program at end-December 2008. On that occasion, the IMF Board also approved drawings

food and fuel price crises. With the completion of the PSI program review, the government was
able to secure financing from various sources to effect a rapid repayment of domestic arreais

secure about % of the amounts needed to fully normalize its relations with domestic suppliers

2.11    The non-concessionalloan from France was contracted by Senegal at end-2008 to
finance its corrective fiscal action plan, notably the clearing of the domestic supplier
arrears. As Senegal is a MDRI recipient, it is subject to IDA'Snon-concessional borrowing

acknowledges that under certain circumstances non-concessional loans with high rates of return

can be granted for a non-concessional loan if there is a favorable assessment at both the loan-
specific level and country-specific level. Inthe particular instance of the non-concessional loan
from France, the waiver was justified by the specific combination of the following five factors:
(i) significantmacroeconomicbenefitsfromcontractingtheloanbyrestoringthecredibility

government arrears clearance program and its financing, including this loan, by the IMF Board

2.13    Growth for 2009 is now projected at 3.1 percent, down from an earlier projection of
5% percent on account of the estimated impact of the global crisis on Senegal. Being a very
open economy, Senegal's exposure to the global recession is significant. The nature o f this
transmission i s not financial but economic. On the downside, the main transmission channels
include possible declines in export demand and prices, tourism, remittances and foreign direct
investment (FDI). The current forecast assumes projected drops in merchandise exports (14
percent), tourism and other service exports (2 percent), remittances (18 percent) and FDI (15
percent). The operation o f most o f these transmission channels i s contingent on developments in
the European economies to which Senegal is most exposed. The global slowdown also has an
upside for Senegal, with the recent sharp fall in fuel and food prices, but it i s an upside tainted
with high uncertainty. Agriculture and modern services (banking, telecom, business services)
appear set to continue to grow, while the sectors that had most suffered from payment delays by
the State in 2008, especially construction and public works, are being supported by the
authorities' corrective policies. Lower imported fuel and food prices should also contribute to
return inflation faster to lower levels (2.2 percent y-o-y).

2.14    The response to the new global conditions of a few large and critical private foreign
investment projects augurs well for Senegal's ability to weather the immediate impact of
the global crisis, but uncertainties remain. Three large scale FDI projects had been expected
to be initiated by 2008-09-an     iron ore miningproject by Mittal, a PPP-structured toll highway,
and Dubai Ports World (DPW)'s planned modernization o f the container port of Dakar. O f
these, the Mittal project i s being clearly postponed. The IDA-supported PPP project to develop a
toll highway i s expected to proceed broadly as planned, though its multi-year phasing and modes
o f financing were revised to account for the impact o f the global crisis. So far, DPW i s not
revising its current plans, and i s expected to continue to pursue its port expansion project.
Overall, the slowdown in the overall scope o f FDIprojects may amount mainly to the loss o f a
source o f short-term growth acceleration for Senegal, rather than contribute to a growth decline.

2.15    The prompt and early completion of the authorities' domestic arrears clearance
plan is proving to be an important elementof the policy response to the crisis. Inaddition to
substantial support from donors at the end o f 2008, the government managed to mobilize
additional short-term domestic bank financing in early 2009, as liquidity conditions eased
somewhat in the regional money market in the wake o f BCEAO's more forceful liquidity
injections in January and February. As a result, by the end o f February 2009 (the two-month
period for closing previous year's budgetary operations), the stock o f domestic arrears had been
reduced from 3% percent o f GDP to less than % percent (FCFA 50 billion). The repayment o f a
large fraction o f domestic arrears constitutes a significant injection o f liquidity inthe economy-
equivalent to about a sixth o f total bank credit to the private sector-that       should contribute to
strengthen private sector balance sheets, increase bank liquidity and provide stimulus to the local
economy.

2.16    With the transmission of the global crisis being felt increasingly in Senegal, the
authorities have already had to revise their budget plans for 2009. The budget had been
prepared before evidence o f the impact o f the global crisis and a resulting domestic growth
slowdown began to emerge. The original 2009 Budget envisaged an overall deficit (including
grants) o f 3 percent o f GDP, with moderate growth in revenues and the suppression o f fuel and
food subsidies creating fiscal space in part for a resumption o f capital spending, while leaving
room to finance the repayment o f residual arrears carried over from 2008. However, the original
budget assumptions for 2009-including          GDP growth at 5% percent-are     no longer realistic.
While revenues are expected to fall short o f the budget's original targets by about 10 percent,
leaving overall spending targets unchanged and allowing a widening o f the deficit i s a more
desirable option at the current juncture-an        option that avoids imparting on fiscal policy a
contractionary stance at a time when the economy can least afford it and when developmental
goals are most vulnerable. The authorities have prepared a Supplemental Budget for 2009 that
leaves original spending broadly intact, and targets the fiscal deficit at 4% percent o f GDP. The
budget i s fully financed by additional recourse to domestic financing, and additional expected
donor support.

2.17    Overall, Senegal's macroeconomicframework provides an adequate basis for the
proposedoperation. The authorities are fully aware o fthe need to remain cautious and monitor
ongoing developments as the global crisis unfolds. Short-term adjustments to the authorities'
policy stance, if needed, would focus mainly on fiscal policy-the       sole macroeconomic policy
instrument clearly under their direct control. Developments in 2008 underscored the need to
ensure a credible, fully funded and sustainable fiscal stance. With this in mind, the authorities
are committed to revising their fiscal stance and cut spending, ifthis proves necessary, inanother
Supplemental Budget later in the year, after a careful review o f developments and prospects for
revenues, budget execution, domestic financing, and budget support.           Recent measures to
enhance control o f budget execution should help ensure that the authorities will be in a better
position to carry through possible revised spendingplans.

2.18    While transmissionchannels are broadly identifiedat the macro level, there is also
significant uncertainty and limited knowledge about their distributional impact. Current
monitoring tools do not have enough resolution to assess micro-level distributional or regional
effects with any significant precision, nor are the government's targeting instruments and
programs developed enough to use distributional information in well targeted mitigation
interventions. Thus, for practical policy purposes, mitigating cuts in the government budget
remains one o f the main policy steps against the negative impacts o f the global crisis, along with
efforts to develop better targeting such as the pilot cash transfer system supported by the IDA
Rapid Response Child-Focused Social Cash Transfer and Nutrition Security Project that aims to
reduce the risk o f nutrition insecurity o f vulnerable populations.

2.19    Though risks of direct financial contagion from the global crisis to the Senegalese
banking sector appear limited at the current juncture, enhanced monitoring by regional
supervisors remains in order to guard against unforeseendevelopments. The tightening of
liquidity inthe local and regional money markets in2008 largely reflects internal developments,
and in response, the regional central bank intervened more forcefully in early 2009 to inject
liquidity. Senegalese banks, even foreign-owned ones, appear to be broadly insulated from
global or parent banks. Their balance sheets do not appear to be exposed to toxic assets, sharp

revisions in foreign asset valuations and deleveraging. Nonetheless, with a deepening of the
global crisis' impact on Senegal, adverse shifts in depositor confidence or a sharp worsening of
lacal economic conditions could negatively impact liquidity or solvency of local banks, even as

3.1     The second Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP-11)' through which the
Authorities offer their vision for the next five years, was adopted by the Government in
July 2006.' The PRSP-I1 builds on lessons learned during the implementation o f the 2003-05
Senegal Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRSP-I). It pushes the poverty reduction agenda forward
so as to meet the objective o f raising economic growth to about 7 percent per year, the level
estimated to be needed to halve poverty incidence by 2015, inline with the targets set out inthe
Millennium Development Goals (MDGs). The poverty strategy was prepared in a participatory
manner with extensive consultations both at the national and district levels with a broad range o f
stakeholders, including representatives from the Parliament, central and local governments,
donors, the private sector, think tanks, trade unions, and civil society organizations. Its vision i s
well aligned with the MDGs agenda.

building and the provision o f basic social services; (iii) protection o f vulnerable groups; and

3.3      As the authorities candidly conceded in their last PRSP Annual Progress Report
(APR) in 2007, progress in the implementationof the strategy has been uneven. On the
upside, the APR underscored the significant decline in poverty incidence during the 1995-2005
decade. The poverty headcount fell from 68 in 1994/95 to 57 percent in2001/02 and 51 percent
in2005/06. However, the last Poverty Assessmentpreparedthe World Bankteam' andbasedon
the last household survey (ESPS 2005) showedthat the declining trend inpoverty incidence may
have stalled in the recent context of surging oil and food prices crisis, possibly leading to an
increase inpoverty incidence by 2% points from 2005 to 2007.

3.4      The APR acknowledges that performance on the first pillar has fallen short of the
strategy's ambitiousgoals. As noted above, a series of shocks has hurt economic growth over
the last three years.      On the policy front, Senegal has made advances in improving the
environment for business-procedures        for starting a business were simplified, the timeframe for
transferring property was reduced, and customs regulations were simplified and automated.
However, progress inother areas-especially access to finance, investor protection, and contract
enforcement-is      lagging. A more recent update of the APR would also highlight the role of
government payment delays that have negatively affected the private sector in2008.

3.5      Concerningthe second pillar, the APR pointedto progress in healthand education.
The allocation of resources to social sectors has grown over the last five years relative to total
public spending and to GDP, and key indicators of school enrollment, health care, and access to
water and sanitation have improved. Senegal has successfully increased access to primary
education from a gross enrollment rate o f 79 percent in 2004 to 86 percent in 2007. The grade
one intake rate improved from 92 percent in 2004 to 103 percent in 2007. The gender gap is
effectively closed at the national level, with girls' GER at 89 percent against 83 for boys in2007.
Similarly, in the health sector, positive results were achieved-an          increase in the DTC3

3.6      Despite clear progress, many MDGs will be difficult to achieve without targeted

The authorities' effort to develop medium-term expenditure frameworks for several key sectors

sectors, They have also embarked on a series o f public expenditure reviews which will be
prepared in collaboration with the Bank and other donors, starting with reviews o f spending in
health and education.

3.7      Regarding the protection of vulnerable groups, the APR recognizes that
performance on this strategic pillar has been weak. The APR recommends that efforts be
stepped up to strengthen safety nets and better monitor the impact o f existing or future targeting
policies. Inthe short term, now that the food and fuel subsidies have been eliminated, there i s a
need for an appropriate targeting mechanism, especially since food and fuel prices, despite their
recent decline, remain elevated relative to their historic levels. This could ultimately take the
form o f a conditional cash transfer program-an        IDA-supported project i s launching a pilot
operations that could rely on the operational infrastructure o f the successful nutrition project and
target young children and their mothers inpoor areas, a highly vulnerable group-,         and could be
based on indicators correlated with poverty and living conditions drawn fiom household survey
data. More fundamentally, there remains a need to improve the identification o f vulnerable
groups: currently, the definition o f vulnerable groups i s based on a multiplicity o f diverse criteria
that include age (children, youth and elderly), gender (women) and other categories
(handicapped and refugees). Such a wide net does not allow for efficient targeting, and leads to
interventions such as subsidies and tax exemptions that are ineffective, inefficient and financially
unsustainable.

3.8     Perfprmance was also mixed on the fourth pillar, economic governance. The
improved procurement framework was a major achievement, but the 2008 fiscal slippages
represent a significant setback. The authorities intendto restore the integrity and transparency of
the budget framework and put in place appropriate measures to strengthen public financial
management. The medium-term expenditure frameworks recently introduced in some sectors
represent steps in the right direction to enhance the predictability of budgetary allocations.
However, for this medium-term planning effort to succeed, the consistency between the
macroeconomic framework and the budget needs to be reinforced. The APR also notes that
modest progress was made in the area of judicial governance but that, despite greater efforts
spent inmodernizing the judicial sector, access remains relatively complex and costly for a large
part of the population. Implementation of decentralization reforms i s also moving slowly, and
this, together with the ongoing need to buildcapacity for financial and fiscal managementat the
regional level, has hampered the delivery of basic public services in remote areas. While
transfers to local governments have in principle become more predictable thanks to new
legislation on sharing the VAT, they remain small (accounting for less than 2 percent of the
central government budget) and delays in actually transferring these financial resources were
frequent in2007 and 2008.

                                     B. REFORMPRIORITIES

3.9     The country is emerging from its 2008 fiscal problems at a time of extraordinary
stress in the global environment. The authorities have made good progress already in
implementing their plan of fiscal and structural corrective actions to deal with the 2008
slippages. However, the evolving global crisis creates an entirely new context for the pursuit of
their broad PRSP-basedpolicy agenda. This new context makes it more pressing to bring the
fiscal policy stance to a sound and sustainable track, mitigate fiscal risks emanating from public
sector entities and protect critical developmental objectives at a time when the authorities must
maintain atight fiscal policy and cope with the impact ofthe global crisis.

3.10    The Government of Senegal has been pursuing a program of public financial
managementreformssince the early 2000s. The mobilizationofthe Ministryof Economy and
Finance(MEF) around the modernization of the public finance management system has made it
possible to make substantial progress with the support of the technical and financial partners,
including the World Bank. This modernization of public finance management constitutes the
cornerstone for successful efforts to implement the poverty reduction strategy (PRSP 11) and i s
an essential component inthe dialogue with donors on the management of external assistance in
the spirit of the Paris Declaration. The modernization of public finance management is also an
important plank o f the current economic program supported by the International Monetary Fund
(IMF) within the framework of the Policy Support Instrument (PSI) approved in November
2007.

3.11    Recent diagnoses performedby or at the requestof the authorities haveidentifieda
range of weaknesses in Senegal's PFM system. Over 2002-08, PFM reforms drew on the
Bank's assessment of the public finance management system (the 2002 CFAA, Country

Financial Accountability Assessment, and the 2003 CPAR, Country Procurement Assessment
Review), with an inter-ministerial committee established to monitor their implementation. More
recent diagnoses, in particular the 2007 PEFA and a 2008 IMF technical assistance report,
confirmed progress and identified weaknesses. The main findings o f these latter diagnoses were
consistent with the analysis o f budgetary slippages reported by the Finance Inspectorate audit in
July 2008 which identified several deficiencies inbudget execution procedures including (i)        an

autonomous agencies, public universities and public hospitals; (iv) ponderous, slow and
excessively centralized budget execution procedures; and (v) a need to strengthen accounting

3.12    Nonetheless, Senegal's PFM system has several strengths. Senegal now has (i)                a
coherent legal framework consistent with good international practices; (ii)   a rational institutional
organization; (iii)good capacity for economic forecasting; and (iv) a commitment to develop
budgeting methods on a multiyear results-based approach. The country has a solid and well-
structured legal framework, anchored in the Constitution and the transcription innational law o f
WAEMU Directives on the budgetary and accounting framework-Organic               Law on the Budget
Law, Decree on Government Accounting, the Budget Nomenclature, State Chart o f Accounts,
and Transparency Code. The institutional framework i s also broadly satisfactory. There i s a
clear role assigned to the Minister o f Economy and Finance inthe area o f budget preparation and
execution, to Parliament responsible for approving budget laws, and to an Audit Office (Cow
des Comptes, CC) for external control.

3.13     A strong point in budget preparation is the general reliability of revenue forecasts,
notwithstanding the inherent complexity of assessing revenue prospects in a highly
uncertain context, as has been the case in recent years. Nonetheless, the links between
forecasts, the macroeconomic framework, and new measures should be strengthened, especially
with the recent increase in the overall cost o f tax incentives for private investment. Inview o f
this, the authorities intendto conduct a complete reviewoftax expenditures (that is, the effective
cost o f various departures from general tax code provisions, including tax incentives, exemptions
or exclusions, whether statutory or discretionary) to obtain a better estimate o f the overall cost o f
these tax provisions, their impact on the economy, and their alignment with the government's
economic and social objectives, thereby also enhancing transparency inthe use-even implicit-
o f public resources. Going forward, the review should also enable the government to assess the
relevance o f maintaining such `exemptions inits tax policy framework.

3.14     Budget preparation modalities have a number of weaknesses that have a negative
impact on budget quality and execution. Onthe expenditure side, weaknesses on expenditure
estimates undermine the credibility o f the budget and complicate budget execution.              Poor
expenditure estimates often create the need for many subsequent adjustments through shifts and
line transfers o f budget appropriations, and may result in budget overruns, as well as cash flow
problems. To address these problems, a new budget preparation decree was issued in February
2009. This decree should introduce greater rigor and realism inrespect o f the budget preparation
timetable, with a budget ceilings circular sent to the spending ministries at an earlier stage o f
sectoral ministries' budget programming. Additional measures must be taken to improve these
forecasts, in particular by developing adequate techniques for revising approved funding,

estimated appropriations, personnel expenditure, and unforeseen expenditure.                        As part of an
overall effort to improve ex ante budget planning, the Parliament should also be able to rely on
technical advice, including from the Audit Office with respect to the initial draft budget laws and
amended or supplemental budget laws, and the Audit Office should be able to voice its opinion
on compliance with budgetary principles and the accuracy o f forecasts at the time these draft

3.15     The implementationof the Medium-TermExpenditureFramework(MTEF) should
also be pursued. Assessing public policies is made more difficult by a budgeting methodology
based largely on resources, by the absence o f an appropriate program-based budget classification
system, and by a budget steering debate that could easily be improved.                           The Senegalese
authorities have made major efforts in recent years to modernize budget law programming and
preparation. These efforts have been aimed primarily at evolving from a procedure inspired by
budget forecasting principles based on agencies' resources toward expenditure frameworks in a
multiyear context. MTEFs were prepared in twelve ministries in charge o f priority sectors.
However, at the current stage, MTEFs and program budgets o f the ministries are merely annexed
to the budget law. The reform o f budget preparationthrough a program approach i s an ambitious
project that will require, among other things, a new organic law on budget laws, and a
strengthening o f the units responsible for preparingMTEFs to ensure that they fully master this
budgeting technique.       An overarching METF, covering the entire budget, should also be
improved over time.

3.16     The budgetary slippages in 2008 exposed weaknesses in budget execution
procedures. First, there was an overly broad and frequent recourse to simplified expenditure
payment procedures ("treasury advances"), normally reserved for emergencies. Inresponse, the
authorities adopted revised regulations that now prohibit "treasury advances", and strictly
confine derogatory expenditure procedures to the cases envisaged by the regulations (a positive
list o f emergencies or special cases)--regulations which have effectively restored the standard
expenditure procedures. Second, the principle o f circumscribing budget execution operations
strictly within the annual budget cycle was found not to be strictly followed, with budget
operations taking place well after the end o f the budget year, and large carry-overs from year to
yearsg In response, the authorities restated by decree the budget-closing schedule, and issued a
decree by end February 2009 that strictly limits carry-overs to no more than 5 percent o f current
year appropriations, with residual carry-overs applied against (and substituting for) current year
appropriations. To make this mechanism fully operational, an inventory o f all active public
procurement contracts was needed.

3.21    Following the 2003 CPAR, the Government showed a clear commitment to
modernize its public procurement system. In June 2006, the Parliament adopted a new
Procurement Law (revision of the Code des Obligations de 1'Administration) which complies
with the WAEMU's Procurement Directives adopted in December 2005, and international
standards. The new law provides for principles of large competition, separation between
regulatory and control functions, handling of complaints by an independentbody which includes
the private sector, regular procurement audits, and appropriate mechanisms for preventing and
detecting fraud and corruption and applying sanctions. In February 2009, in order to assess
progress made and level of quality of the new public procurement system, the Government
conducted an assessment using the OECD/DAC benchmarking tool. This assessment provided a
score of 2.46 (the maximum score is 3) for the quality of the system-a   good overall rating.

3.22    The procurementregulatoryand institutionalframeworkand capacity management
have considerably improved. Following the adoption of the new Procurement Law, a new
procurement code was approved by the Government in April 2007, according to the provisions
of the law.     Several key implementing regulations have been prepared, including national
standard bidding documents (NSBDs) for works, goods and consulting services, which were
drafted on the basis of the Bank's standard bidding documents. The Public Procurement
Regulatory Authority (ARMP), an autonomous and independent structure, is operational and
responsible for policy, handling complaints from bidders, carrying out independent audits and
designing national capacity development strategy. The Regulatory Council of the ARMP is
composed of representatives from the private sector and civil society, in addition to those from
civil services. To ensure appropriate and reliable collection and monitoring of procurement
statistics and the dissemination of procurement information, including regulations, procurement
plans, tender invitations, and requests for proposals, complaints, audits and contract award
information, the Government has set up an integrated procurement information system
(SIGMAP) which includes a open-access website (the Senegal procurement portal).           l1   This

would require the Ministry of Economy and Finance to strengthen the monitoring of agencies'
budget execution, and these organizations to submit quarterly budget execution statements and

3.30    Additional fiscal risks emanate from Senegal's public hospitalswhich, following a
10-year old reform, are in a difficult financial situation. In 1998, the Government started a
reform of its hospital system. The first phase of the hospital reform, between 1998 and 2006,
covered institutional, managerial and administrative aspects, with a focus on regional and central
hospitals. The objective was to enhance hospital autonomy. At the same time, however, very
limitedaccountability was put inplace. For instance, no health master plan (carte sanitaire) has
been prepared to define the strategic role of each referral hospitals within the health system.
Also, no mechanism was implementedto ensure fair access of poor patients to hospital care.
Furthermore, human resource issues (notably allocation of hospital staff among services and
control on recruitments and remuneration, including incentives) were not addressed by the
reform.

3.31    To date, the results of this reform are mixed and raise critical issues of
sustainability. Hospital use has significantly increased(inpatients: +30 percent in 10years), but
equity in hospital access has decreased (the poor represent only 3 percent of inpatients but 51
percent of the population), while efficiency has drastically fallen, as a result of large recruitments
of unskilled workers and salary increases which were not commensurate with hospital
productivity increases. More fundamentally, most hospitals are near bankrupt and expect that
the Government will bailthem out.l3 The situation thus creates apotentially large fiscal burden
on the government, which, ifleft unaddressed, may only increasewith time.

3.32    A Bank technical assistance program has reviewed the deep financial problems of
the public hospital sector and proposed a set of recommendations. Among corrective
measures that should be taken, a first priority is to rebuildthe oversight function of the hospital
system by (i)  reinforcing the Ministry of Health's Hospitals Directorate and strengtheningjoint
financial oversight by the Ministry of Health and the Ministry of Economy and Finance; (ii)
instituting performance evaluation mechanisms for hospital managers; (iii)           strengthening control
mechanisms over hiring and personnel spending; and (iv) introducing a hospital "bankruptcy"
mechanism that could place de facto bankrupt hospitals under temporary judicial oversight to
carry out in-depth financial reorganization. A second priority would be to restore hospital
efficiency by (i)realigning financing mechanisms (comprising user fees and government
budgetary allowances) with actual costs; (ii)reducing unskilled personnel (with retrenchment
packages); and (iii)  rationalizing the hospital system through the adoption and enforcement of a
health map (Carte Sanitaire) and the restructuring of the hospital network in Dakar.

Decentralization

3.43    The Government has pursued a progressive, yet cautious, decentralization policy.
The first phase, started in 1972, saw the creation of municipalities and regional development
committees, includingmunicipal and rural councils. The secondphase, started in 1996, aimed at
increasing the responsibility o f local governments inthe management of local affairs. In2004, a
law on local taxation was adopted by the National Assembly and the Government created the
Ministry of Decentralization and Local Governments, whose headhas the rank of State Minister,
with a mandate to pilot the decentralization policy. Inspite ofthese early efforts, more could be
done to ensure adequate support to local authorities to help them actually fulfill their new
managementresponsibilities, and to simplify and update the institutional framework.

3.44    The legal framework for political, administrative, and fiscal decentralization in
Senegal has resulted in the creation of three non-hierarchical levels of local governments,
with a significant devolution of government competencies from the center to lower levels.
Senegal counts 14 regions, 116 communes, and 369 rural councils. Local governments are legal
and moral entities with financial autonomy and management responsibilities.              They are
administered by elected officials and primarily responsible for local development management
within their respective jurisdiction and the delivery of quality basic social services to their
population. In law, the central government has devolved to local governments the management
of public programs innine competencies, namely: environment and natural resources, education,
health, population and social action, youth, sport and leisure, culture, local development
planning, urban development, and housing. Land tenure, water and sanitation are also under
consideration for devolution.

3.45    In practice, however, the transfer of resources has not yet been aligned with the
transfer of responsibilities. The tax base i s highly centralized-with      the bulk of revenues
raised on a tax base largely centered in Dakar. For the foreseeable future, resource transfers
from the central budget to the local level will remain the most significant, and often the sole,
source of local government revenues.         However, the share of financial transfers to local
governments as a percentage of the government budget (excluding debt) has remained well
below 5 percent. Overall, the flow o f fiscal transfers to local governments is managed from the
central budget through two dedicatedfunds, a local government operating fund (the FDD, Fonds
de Dotation de la De'centralisation) and a local government investment fund (the FECL, Fonds


                                                26

d'Equipement des Collectivitks Locales). The ongoing dialogue with the Government on a
reform of the FECL led, in 2007, to an agreement in principle to increase the amount of the
FECL from the equivalent of 2 percent to 10 percent of total VAT proceeds by 2010, with
limitedprogressmade inimplementation so far.

3.46    Weak local government capacity remains a significant constraint in Senegal's
decentralization process. The government has developed a broadly satisfactory institutional
program for supporting the gradual improvement in local government capacity. A two-track
approach-with      one track for the urban sub-sector and another for rural government-has               been
put in place with Bank and donor support, and now covers the entire spectrum of local
governments in the country.'*            These programs aim at strengthening the financial and
organizational management of local governments and at harmonizing heretofore scattered donor
involvement in decentralization and local development programs. In the urban sub-sector, a
Municipal Development Agency (MDA) was created under the IDA-financedUDDP and was
instrumental inimplementing the project, as well as gathering and disseminating comprehensive
information on each urban municipality.              Investment programming for individual local
governments is being carried out to ensure that priority needs are in line with their capacity to
finance and maintain them. In the rural sector, a multi-donor program supports the National
Program for Local Development (PNDL), a conduit through which all activities towards
participatory local development initiatives are channeled. Implementation is coordinated by an
executive secretariat under the ministry incharge of decentralization. Overall, resource transfers
from the center have remained hesitant, and more needs to be done to ensure that the devolution
o f responsibilities is accompanied by sufficient resources to equip local governments with
appropriate financial, technical and humanresourcesrequiredto carry out local development.

3.47    In the short term, therefore, a central challenge remains that of providingsufficient
central government transfers. Addressing the deep political, institutional and operational
constraints to an effective decentralization program will be a long term undertaking, well beyond
the scope of the proposed operation. For the short term, it is important that funding both for
direct local governments and for government agencies responsible for technical assistance to
local governments be protected and increased.

the poor, which remain a majority. Also, financial support under the proposed operation will

6.3      The road user charge is not an additional tax, with potentially direct negative
distributional impacts on petroleum products consumers, but a restructuring of existing
indirect taxation on gasoline and gasoil products. Retail petroleum products prices are thus

reduce on net the average fiscal costs of expanding and maintaining the road network and
improve its quality, and benefit the rural areas, which bear most of the incidence of shortfalls in
road maintenanceand where most of the poor reside.

                                        I. Introduction

1.        Ina context marked by global economic and financial crisis, the government of Senegal
i s still determined to sustainably strengthen the bases o f economic growth as well as ensure a
more rapid progress towards the achievement o f the Millennium Development Goals. To that
end, it will continue to maintain macroeconomic stability while pursuing the implementation of
structural reforms with a view to enhancing the competitiveness o f its economy and improving
the populations' living conditions.

                       11.   RecentEconomicand SocialDevelopments

2.        2008 was marked by a difficult international environment illustrated by the food
andenergy crisis which impactednegativelyon the economy. The government reacted swiftly
to try to contain the shock and limit the impacts on households, but could not prevent the
economic slowdown. Real GDP growth slowed significantly, from 4.7 percent in 2007 to only
2.5 percent in 2008. The primary sector registered a strong performance with 12.7 percent
growth, thanks to a more active agricultural policy meant to boost production and favorable
rainfall.    However, the secondary sector declined by 2.7 percent because of the delayed
resumption o f ICs activity and the negative effect o f the delay on the State's payment to the




                                                40

domestic private sector. The tertiary sector, which i s the traditional source o f         economy,
continues to grow (3.5 percent) but at a pace that i s 50% less thanin2007.

3.      Beyond the slowdown in growth, the shock slightly eroded the stability of the
macroeconomicframework that the country was able to build up during precedingyears.
Consumer prices registered an annual average increase o f 5.8 percent, reflecting the high
increase in the prices o f the oil barrel and food products. However, the inflationary pressure
declined before the end o f the year following the drop in the oil barrel and food prices. The
external current account deficit reached 12.3 percent of GDP, a 0.5 point decline compared to
2007. As regards public finance, the 2008 budget management was affected by the impact, at the
end of the year, o f pending payments and minus-value in tax revenue collection. The overall
fiscal deficit (including grants) stood at 4.3% o f GDP.

4.      With respectto public finances, 2008 was marked by difficulties encounteredin the
implementation of the budget. The execution o f expenditures higher than the resources
available, notably the payment o f subsidiesto absorb the oil and food price shock, translated into
an accumulation o f pending payments worth about 3% percent o f GDP owed to the domestic
private sector in late October 2008. Extra-budgetary expenditures to the tune o f 1% percent o f
GDP as well as an excessive use o f cash advance were observed. However, harsh measures
including budget cuts through a budgetreconciliation act were rapidly adopted and implemented
to correct budget difficulties. An audit i s also in progress to take stock o f the extra-budgetary
expenditures. At the end o f the complementary budget execution period (28 February 2009), late
payments to the private sector were reduced to less than 1 percent o f GDP and no expenses based
on the exceptional cash advance procedure was executed between September and December
2008.

5.      Despitethe difficultbudgetsituationwhich ledto budgetcuts of about 3% percentof
GDP, the government continued to maintain priority expenditures, notably in the
education and health sectors. However, the situation o f hospitals is still disturbing. The share
o f the social spending in the budget increased from 32% to 36 percent between 2007 and 2008.
The performances in the education sector continued to improve in 2008. Actually, the rate o f
admission to first grade and the gross primary school enrolment rate increased respectively from
110 percent and 87.5 percent in 2007 to respectively 113 percent and 90 percent in 2008. Girls'
access to education was stepped up since their rate o f admission to first grade and their gross
primary enrolment rate are higher than those o f boys. The parity ratio i s thus higher than one for
the second consecutive year. However, the primary school completion rate which improved by 3
points compared to 2007, reaching 58.5 percent, i s still below the government's objectives.

6.      Conversely, performance in the health sector declined in 2008, reflecting the
difficulties of the sector, notablyin hospitals,which worsened during the year. The global
primary consultation rate (preventive and curative) dropped from 60 percent in 2007 to 58
percent in2008, i.e. a decline o f 2 points. At the same time, the proportion o f deliveries assisted

A three-year Policy Support Instrument (PSI) was approved in December 2007 and its first
review was completed in June 2008. The second review was completed in December 2008,
together with the approval o f a one-year Exogenous Shocks Facility amounting to
SDR 48.54 million (30 percent o f quota), of which halfwas disbursed immediately.

Fund staff is recommending completion of the third review o f the PSI and first review of the
ESF, for which a June 2009 Board meeting is scheduled. Staff will also recommend that the
Board approve the authorities' request for an augmentation o f the ESF to 75 percent o f quota
(SDR 121-35million) and an extension o fthe arrangement from 12to 18 months to help Senegal
address the impact o f the global crisis. The undrawn balance o f 60 percent o f quota (SDR 97.08
million) would be disbursed inthree equal tranches at the time o f the third, fourth, and fifth PSI
reviews.

Macroeconomicprospects

Growth outside agriculture slowed sharply in 2008 due to the government's payment delays to
the private sector and the effect on household consumption o f high food and fuel prices during
most o f the year. In late 2008/early 2009, the global economic crisis began to affect the
Senegalese economy. Remittances, export prices, and foreign direct investment (FDI) appear to
be the main transmission channels. As a result, the economic rebound in 2009 is projected to be
weak, even as government payment delays are eliminated and the phosphate company is moving
to full-capacity production after its successful rehabilitation. Similarly, projected medium-term
growth, at around 5 percent, i s lower than previously foreseen due to a more constrained external
environment.

Inflation should be low on average in 2009, given the sharp decline in food and energy prices
and absence of domestic demand pressures. Inflationis poised to return to historical levels over
the medium term. The external current account deficit is projected to narrow slightly in 2009
(though less than previously foreseen) and more strongly over the medium term, as subdued


                                                63

economic growth and FDI will contain imports. Reflecting more depressed financial inflows,
especially FDI, reserves should hoverjust above three months o f imports.
The financial sector has so far weathered the impact o f the global economic crisis. It does not
appear to have been directly affected by toxic assets or difficulties in foreign parent banks, but
indirect effects could arise as a result o f declining credit quality or solvency and liquidity
problems. The regional authorities have stepped up their monitoring o f the sector.

Macroeconomic and structural policies

In line with program commitments, the government's unpaid bills have been substantially
reduced and far-reaching public financial management (PFM) reforms launched. The program's
focus in 2009 will be on helping Senegal weather the impact o f the global economic crisis while
completing the normalization o f the government's financial relations with the private sector. All
payment delays are intended to be settlid by mid-year, with the help o f external and domestic
financing that will become available in the next few weeks. In the meantime, budget
implementationi s beingtightly managed.

Fiscal policy will continue to be aimed at preserving medium-term debt sustainability while
providing sufficient resources for priority social sectors and infrastructure investment. This
supports macroeconomic stability, progress toward meeting the MDGs, poverty alleviation, and
raising growth. The updated joint Bank-Fund DSA confirms that an overall fiscal deficit o f 4
percent o f GDP over the medium term preserves debt sustainability. Following tighter
macroeconomic policies in 2007-08, scope exists for a temporary easing in 2009, to an overall
fiscal deficit o f 4% percent o f GDP relative to the earlier program target of 2% percent o f GDP.
The higher fiscal deficit is feasible as progress has been made in settling unpaid bills and lining
upthe necessary financing.

The PSI program continues to aim at deepening structural reforms inthree areas: (i)       improving
fiscal governance and transparency; (ii)encouraging private sector activity; and (iii)limiting
financial sector vulnerabilities and raising the sector's contribution to the economy. The focus
will continue to be on measures to strengthen PFMand tax administration, with a particular view
to further improving budget preparation, monitoring, and execution and safeguarding the
traditionally strong revenue performance. The PFM measures are based on technical assistance
recommendations from the Fundand other development partners and are being implemented ina
concerted effort. This will also help lower the risk of a recurrence o f the past budgetary slippages
that had resulted inthe government's payment delays to the private sector.

Overall, if effectively implemented with a strong political commitment, the authorities'
macroeconomic and structural policies, together with support from development partners, should
help enable Senegal to weather the impact ofthe global crisis
