A. STRATEGICCONTEXTAND RATIONALE
1. Countryandsectorissues

Country and sector background:

Poverty in Mexico remains widespread. Poverty indicators demonstrate that in 2000, about 53 percent of
its national population suffered from income poverty (defined as those with a per capita consumption
level below that which is neededto meet basic food and non-food needs). In 2002, 35 percent of the rural
population (between 8 and 10million people) was extremely poor.

Inthe last decade, Mexico has undertaken major efforts to transform its rural and agricultural sectors, and
in particular the social sector,' which represents nearly 70 percent of the rural population and where
poverty rates are the highest, to try to make it an integral component of a more dynamic economic
environment.* However, these efforts have been limited due to the following factors:

          0    As a consequenceof legal and administrative restrictions combined with a lack of economic
               incentives, the social sector has been characterized by a disproportionately low level of
               investment, often unsustainable resource use, atomization of land, and a high level of poverty.
               A 2000 survey of ejidutarios (ejido beneficiaries) and private producers found that the
               average capital endowment in ejido lands i s significantly lower than among neighboring non-
               ejido producers (Procuraduria Agraria, 2000). The 2001 VI1Ejidos Census indicates that only
               5 percent of the ejiduturios have their own motor vehicle for productiveuse.


          0    There is a situation in the ejidos where most land rights (usufruct rights) belong to senior
               farmers, many of whom are unable to make efficient use of their land. Young households-
               who are more educatedand potentially more innovative farmers-suffer from significant land
               shortages or highly insecure land tenure. As a consequence, the social sector i s getting older,

' Social sector overview
The social sector includes social lands classifiedas "ejidos" and indigenous"communal lands" representinghalf of the country's
land. These lands resulted from the massive land distribution process set forth in the 1917 Mexican Constitution, under which
indigenous andnon-indigenousbeneficiaries receivedrights to a house plot, sole usufructrights for a parcelof land, and access to
communal lands subject to collectivemanagement decisions. Ejidos are those social lands whose tenure regime i s regulatedby
the AgrarianLaw, while "communal lands" are lands regulatedby customary-basedland tenure regimes.
The population living in ejidos and comunidudes includes ejidutun`os and comuneros, those with clear usufruct rights over a
parcelof land, access to communalland, and the right to ahouse plot, posesionarios, those with possessionof plots, but no other
rights, uvecindudos, members of the community who have lived in the ejido urban area for more than a year, and are so
recognizedby the usambleu, alongwith their childrenand spouses.
In 1992, Constitutionalreforms focused on modificationsof the legal environment, institutional changes, and the implementation
of a programof landregularization(PROCEDE). The 2001 ESW highlighted the positiveimpacts of these legal and institutional
limitations not only on govemance, including a significant reductionof conflicts within and between ejidos, but also on activity
in rentalmarketsandhouseholdwelfare (Deininger 2001).
The central legalchangesapprovedin 1992are threefold:
          -    Strengthening of self-govemance of the ejidos which have legal personality and can autonomously regulate

               lands may be parceledout to individuals and whether landholders can movetowardfull privateownership; and
          -    Endof the landdistributionprocess (known in Mexico as repurto agrurio).

The remaining legal and administrative restrictions in the social sector continue to be major obstacles to
the sustained development of ejidos and communities, together with the lack of access to credit, lack of
property right (addressed by PROCEDE), asymmetry of information (on legal framework, land prices,
input/output markets) and technical assistance (which are still skewed in favor of medium-size farmers
rather than the poorest producers). While further changes in the legal framework of ejidos remain
politically very sensitive and difficult to bringabout at the present time, the project will address the other
barriers to the development of the social sector, by supporting the development of a land market and the
ability to engage in land transactions and productive projects (through the provision of credit, information
and technical assistance), which will eventually promote more efficient allocation of land and possibly
enhance a broader access to credit. Annex 10 of the PAD includes a detailed analysis of land market
distortions and actions to address them through the project.

GOM priorities and commitment

GOM and particularly the Secretariat of Agrarian Reform (Secretaria de la Reforma Agraria-SRA) are
highly conscious of the aging problem and the opportunities for enhancing rural development represented
by young rural workers. They have decided to make an effort to help rejuvenate and revitalize the
countryside and invest in this more dynamic young segment of the population endowed with more human
capital.

The intention is to operate simultaneously on various fronts. This includes: (i)facilitating the
intergenerational transfer of land rights through the creation of a land fund and the provision of incentives
to senior landholders who choose to transfer their land rights to young farmers; (ii)                   providing technical
and financial support to young farmers to invest in off- and on-farm activities; (iii)studying possible
measures to stimulate land markets in the social sector within the existing constitutional framework; (iv)
studying the introduction of social welfare schemes or plans for senior landholders; and (v) to the extent
possible, using the above mechanisms of access to land to consolidate holdings so as to promote the
formation of viable farms in the hands of the new generation.

Action on these fronts has already started. In 2004, the Secretariat of Finance and Public Credit
(Secretaria de Hacienda y Crkdito Pdblico, SHCP) budgeted an amount of MXN270 million for an
Access to Land for Young Farmers pr~gram,~to be implemented by SRA, that would allow young
farmers (between the ages of 18 and 39) from the social sector to have access to underutilized but
potentially more productive lands and implement productive on- and off-farm activities on these lands.
The intention is to use the allocated budgetary resources to carry out pilot experiences flexible enough to
test different models of access to land and investment resources.




 The purpose of PROCEDE (Programa Nacional de Certificacih de 10s Derechos Ejidales y Solares Urbanos, the National
CertificationProgram of Ejido Rights and Urban Lots) i s to resolve boundary conflicts, regularize land tenure, delineate ejidos
and individual parcel boundaries, and issue property rights in ejidos and communal lands. This program, started in 1993, i s
demanddriven and free. It allows beneficiariesto choose their propertyrights regime, andeventually documentsrights to a house
plot, sole usufructrightsto a parcel of land, and access to communal lands subject to collective management decisions.

 The official name of this program i s Progrumu del Joven Emprendedor Rural y Fondo de Tierru (Young Rural Entrepreneur
and LandFundProgram) which is operatingunder rules of operation approved by SHCP and COFEMER and publishedin the
Official Gazette (last version datedFebruary 18, 2005).

Linkages to CPS

The recently-approved CPS (Country Partnership Strategy) for 2005-2008 focuses on reducing poverty
and inequality, increasing competitiveness, strengthening institutions, and promoting environmental
sustainability. The CPS specifically intends to support GOM programs in rural areas, mentions the need
for a more integrated rural strategy, and identifies rural development through the improvement of land
markets and access to land as one of the key pillars for reducing rural poverty and inequality. To achieve
these objectives, the CPS includesa US$lOO million loan to support the Secretariat of Agrarian Reform's
(SRA) Access to Land for Young Farmers program.

2. Rationale for Bank involvement

InOctober 2003, SRA, through SHCP, sent a letter to the Mexico Country Director asking for Bank
assistance. InMarch 2004, SHCP sent a second letter of interest to the Country Director asking for PHRD
funds to support project preparation. The PHRD was approved in June 2004 and helped to prepare the
project, including hiring a project preparation team and carrying out a baseline survey and background
reports.

The Bank i s uniquely placed to assist the GOM given its experience in land policies and programs
worldwide. Project design will take into account lessons from three countries in the region where Bank-
supported access to land programs is under implementation-Brazil,               Guatemala, and Honduras-and      also
from experiences outside the region, such as inMalawi and India.

The proposed project i s the natural and ideal continuation of the Bank's involvement in land and rural
poverty reduction in Mexico. The project builds on a body of analytical work carried out by the Bank in
Mexico, which started long before the 1992 Constitutional reforms (see footnote 1 above) and whose
more recent pieces include: (i)       the 2001 Land Policy ESW; (ii) international conference in December
                                                                          an
2003 to review access to land in different countries; (iii)       an ongoing analytical study based on field work
in selected ejidos and indigenous comunidades to better understand barriers to better functioning land
markets and incentives for improving access to land; (iv) a review of rural poverty in Mexico as part of a
programmatic AAA carried out under the 2005-2008 CPS; and (iv) plannedassistanceand a possible loan
for urbanproperty rights (FY06).

Finally, the Bank's Land Acquisition Committee has authorized5the use of project funds to acquire land
as per the Bank's new expenditure eligibility policy (OP/BP 6.0) approved by the Board inApril 2004.

The project will also contribute to the GOM objectives as stated in the Plan Nacional de Desarrollo,

inclusive policies for elderly citizens and indigenous peoples. The proposed project addresses these
strategic issues since it will train and build the capacity of young rural workers, including those in
indigenous areas, and pilot mechanisms to improve their access to land and other necessary,
complementary assets. In parallel, it will facilitate the access to social welfare schemes of senior
landholders willing to transfer their lands. Likewise, the project directly supports SRA's strategy to

The new Country Partnership Strategy (CPS) for Mexico, covering the period 2005-2008, supports the
four main pillars of the Government's agenda: (a) reducing poverty and inequality; (b) increasing
competitiveness; (c) strengthening institutions; and (d) promoting environmental sustainability. The CPS
recognizes that expansion of labor income in the rural agricultural and non-farm sector will requirerobust
economic growth and a revitalized, competitive private sector. Furthermore, it identifies access to assets,
markets, and finance as the key to reducing poverty and inequality and promoting sustainable growth. The
project will directly contribute to three of the CPS objectives. By supporting access to land and other
productive assets, the project will facilitate private investment and createjobs. This will contribute to the
reduction of poverty and inequality. Furthermore, by addressing land market issues in the social sector
(ejidos), home to the poorest segment of the population, the project will directly improve the livelihoods
of those most in need. Second, the project will seek to build the capacity of young farmers to carry out
productive investments, thereby increasing the long-term competitiveness of the rural sector-a           key
sector for the country's development. Finally, by supporting environmentally sound productive
investments, through a detailed screening mechanism, the proposed project will contribute to promoting
the mainstreaming of environmental concerns and the implementation of environmentally sustainable
production activities.

4. Lessons learned and reflected inthe project design

One key lesson learned from other land-based rural development projects and highlighted in a recent
World Bank report7is that distributing land alone, unaccompanied by credit and technical assistance, does
little to increase rural income. This was reflectedin the project design whereby young rural entrepreneurs
will receive financial support to purchase or rent (usufructrights over) lands as well as for complementary
training, technical assistance, and productive investment.

Another lesson learned from Oportunidades, Mexico's cash transfer program to rural areas, i s the
importance of an efficient and sophisticated M&E system. This i s important in the context of a pilot
project which has a learning-by-doing nature. Therefore, specific attention has been paid to the design of
the project's M&E system, including the participation of an expert who will help to design and implement
the Oportunidades' M&E system, and the establishment of a national baseline to be carried out in the first

D. APPRAISAL SUMMARY
1. Economicandfinancialanalyses

An economic and financial analysis of seven illustrative models was conducted to examine their
profitability and provide recommendations for the improvement of the reglas de operacidn in 2006 and
thereafter. Ingeneral, the analysis shows that these subprojects are profitable and imply an improvement
in the allocation of resources in the economy. However, the models examined in the analysis can be
separated into two clusters based on their financial returns and investment costs: (i)agro-
industrialhtensive agricultural production subprojects, and (ii)   traditional mixed cropping subprojects.
The former tend to be more profitable than the latter but require higher investment costs. To achieve the
goal of income generation of these subprojects, the project would need to take different actions to meet
the specific needs of each cluster. For the agro-industrialhntensive agricultural production subprojects, the
following recommendations are made: (i)     encourage young farmers to associate in groups to share costs
and risks; (ii)require training, such as the Proyecto Escuela, for subprojects that involve technical
innovation; (iii) favor subprojects that create vertical and horizontal clusters in the supply chain; and (iv)
allow for a flexible allocation of credit between land acquisitionh-ental and productive investment
components.For the traditional mixed cropping subprojects, the following recommendations are made: (i)
implement subsidies for productive investments at the beginning of the subproject cycle to reduce the
negative impact of land purchases on the income stream; (ii) subsidies for productive investments in
                                                               grant
land rental schemes to encourage this type of contracts; (iii)consider providing subsidies under a
matching subsidy scheme where beneficiaries would be required to provide counterpart funds to reduce
the risk of giving out loans that cannot be repaid; and (iv) consider a scheme with progressive debt
repayment. Given the pilot nature of the project, more scenarios could be analyzed to continue providing
recommendationsfor the improvement of the reglas de operacidn in subsequent years of the project.

3. Fiduciary

Financial Management: The Bank carried out a Financial Management Assessment (FMA), which
involved ensuring that project design allows for an appropriate level of transparency, facilitating oversight
and control while also supporting smooth implementation. The regional financial management team
(LCOAA) concluded the following: (i)       the main financial unit (under the Ojkiali'a Mayor) within SRA
lacks sufficient capacity to manage project financial matters, but can handle some basic functions as it
does with normal Secretariat activities; (ii)  the planning and policy unit (DGPPA) in SRA will keep
accounting records and prepare financial reports for the project, using existing staff and resources, with

Procurement: The Bank carried out an assessment of the capacity of SRA and FIFONAFE to handle

should participate in decisionmaking on project design and implementation; (b) identify their form of

identify the possible risks and social impact, and make recommendations to compensatefor such risks and
impact.

The assessment was based on quantitative and qualitative methods including: (i)semi-structured
interviews; (ii)participatory field activities, including case studies; (iii)focus groups with experts and
relevant institutions; and (iv) the use of secondary data such as those contained in the program's current

rules of operation, together with a review of social analyses carried out during the preparation of other
Bank-financed projects in the country and government statistics.

The Social Assessment identified project beneficiaries/stakeholders as: (i)young sujetos ugrurios
(ranging from 18 to 39 years of age); (ii)senior sujetos ugrurios; and (iii)specific social groups,
includingsenior landholders, women, and indigenous comunidudes.

The maximum authorities on the use of agrarian rights are ejido and comunidud assemblies. In order to
actively participate in these assemblies and have voting rights, ejido members must have agrarian rights.
Due to their lack of such rights, young sujetos ugrurios have little participation in ejido and comunidud
assemblies. As a result, they view access to land as a means to achieve full participation in local and
regional decisionmaking groups and organizations, and thus attain independence and social status.

The project will promote the inclusion of young people and their projects in locally agreed development
processes. Through training activities, the empowerment of local committees, and the participation of the
local authority in program promotion and training, the program seeks to have a favorable impact on a
process of local support for productiveprojects, thus contributingto their sustainability and viability.

Inorder to ensure the participation and well-being of senior sujetos agrurios, the project will include
incentives for senior landholders to sell or rent their land, by facilitating their access to social welfare
schemes that guarantee their basic needs, including food, health, and life insurance. These schemes will
be inline with the national strategy for the elderly.

As the project will benefit indigenous populations-one             of the principal beneficiary groups-a
participatory Indigenous Peoples Development Plan (IPDP) was prepared. The IPDP addresses specific
issues related to the consultation, capacity building, and participation of indigenous groups through the
project.

With the progress of PROCEDE, the risks that the Program might work on lands under open agrarian
conflicts are nearly nonexistent. The project specifically excludes "units or parcels under litigation or with
social or agrarian conflicts." However, there are cases of latent andor potential agrarian conflicts. To
limit the risksof conflicts that may arise in land transactions, the project will call for promoters to receive
training to detect this type of circumstances and prevent possible risks. It will guide those involved to
agencies such as the Procuruduriu Agruriu, which has developed specific tools for conflict resolution.

One of the most important risks identified by the Social Assessment i s the potential excessive
indebtedness of young people, a situation that would have an unfavorable effect on the living conditions
of the families involved. The project includes administrative training and technical advice for the
preparation of productive projects, which will take into account the characteristics of the family economy
in order to prevent the risk of excessive indebtedness. Additionally, reliable information on the incomes
of young rural entrepreneurs before and during the operation of their projects will be generated in order to
verify the eventual improvement of living standards of the families involved, which i s one of the project's
key objectives.

The social impact of the project will be closely monitored, with mitigation and preventive measures
introduced to address adverse impacts.

trigger OP 4.12, as the policy requires that people who are forced to lose their land or land-based assets,
or who lose access to livelihoods as a result of land acquisition, be entitled to compensation and
assistance. The Social Assessment determined that the project does not trigger OP 4.12 as the policy
requires action only when land transfedtaking i s involuntary; in the case of the project, transfer of land
(through rental or purchase) from older to young farmers is entirely voluntary.

Specifically, the Social Assessment found that in the case that a parcel, owned by a (senior) person under
the ejido's or comunidad's rules, was rentedto a tenant or other user, and the owner chose to sell that
parcelto a third party through the project, this would be a voluntary decision by the owner, and would not
constitute an involuntary taking of land from the renter by the project. Rather, as their relationship i s a
private arrangement that could and would be addressed under the local law, it would be a private issue
between the rightful owner (who is voluntarily choosing to sell his or her land) and the user or occupant.
Moreover, these cases could and would clearly be identified through the screening process, which will
exclude the eligibility of any lands with conflicts as indicated inthe reglas de operacidn.

7. Policy Exceptions and Readiness

The project does not require any exception to IBRD policies. The project meets the regional criteria for
readiness for implementation. Fiduciary arrangements are in place. Adequate monitoring and evaluation
capacity is already inplace. The Environmental Analysis and the IPDP were disclosed in country and sent
to IBRD's Infoshop before appraisal. This project complies with all applicable Bank policies.

Country and sector background:

Poverty in Mexico remains widespread. Poverty indicators demonstrate that in 2000, about 53 percent of
the national population suffered from income poverty (defined as those with a per capita consumption
level below that which i s neededto meet basic food andnon-food needs). In 2002, 35 percent of the rural
population (between 8 and 10millionpeople) was extremely poor.

Inthe lastdecade, Mexico hasundertakenmajorefforts totransformitsruralandagriculturalsectors, and
in particular the social sector which represents nearly 70 percent of the rural population, to try to make it
an integral component of a more dynamic economic environment." However, these efforts have been
limited due to the following factors:

        0    As a consequence of legal and administrative restrictions combined with a lack of economic
             incentives, the social sector has been characterized by a disproportionately low level of
             investment, often unsustainable resource use, atomization of land, and a highlevel of poverty.
             A 2000 survey of ejidutarios (ejido.beneficiaries) and private producers found that the
             average capital endowment in ejido lands i s significantly lower than among neighboring non-
             ejido producers (Procuraduria Agraria 2000). The 2001 VI1Ejidos Census indicates that only
             5 percent of the ejidutarios have their own motor vehicle for productive use.

        0    There is a situation in the ejidos where most land rights belong to older farmers, many of
             whom are unable to make efficient use of their land. Young households with more educated
             and potentially more innovative and entrepreneurial farmers suffer from significant land
             shortages or highly insecure land tenure. Today, the social sector i s getting older, young
             people are migrating to large cities and the US. Ejiduturios who are beneficiaries of the
             PROCEDE are largely (50 percent) older than 50, and 29 percent older than 65, whereas only
             25 percent are 18to 40.

             Participatory diagnostics carried out in 2004 and 2005 in the states of Oaxaca, Guanajuato,
             Guerrero, Sinaloa, Sonora, and Michoach (also pilot states of the proposed project) indicate
             that due to their age and health conditions, senior ejiduturios often have difficulties in
             cultivating their lands. Lack of investment and working capital severely constrains
             technological upgrading or the hiring of paid labor and perpetuates low productivity and
             profitability in agricultural and livestock production. Remittances from family members are
             mainly usedfor consumption, health, and education.

The remaining legal and administrative restrictions in the social sector continue to be major obstacles to
the sustained development of ejidos and communities, together with the lack of access to credit, lack of
property right (addressed by PROCEDE), asymmetry of information (on legal framework, land prices,
input/output markets) and technical assistance (which are still skewed in favor of medium-size farmers
rather than the poorest producers). While further changes in the legal framework of ejidos remain
politically very sensitive and difficult to bring about at the present time, the project will address the other
barriers to the development of the social sector, by supporting the development of a land market and the

The project will provide significant investment resources to increase productivity of the land acquired

GOM commitment

models of access to land and investment resources.

GeographicalCoverage

SRA will be executing the project in the following ten states: Aguascalientes, Guanajuato, Guerrero,
Michoachn, Oaxaca, San Luis Potosi, Sinaloa, Sonora, Yucath and ZacatCcas, or any other state for

Within the above mentioned ten states, a total of about 800 ejidos have been selected for
participation in the project. It i s estimated that approximately 10 percent of these are indigenous
peoples' ejidos.

Target Group

The program targets the sujetos agrarios living in ejidos and comunidades (including indigenous
ones), including ejidatarios and comuneros (those with clear usufruct rights over a parcel of land,
access to communal land, and the right to a house plot), posesionarios (those with possession of
plots, but no other rights), avecindados (members of the community who have lived in the ejido
urban area for more than a year, and are so recognized by the asamblea) along with their children
and spouses. An age limitation (between the ages of 18 and 39) i s included in the rules of operation
so as to promote transfer of landfrom the older ejidatarios to young farmers, considered to represent
an underutilized and more efficient source of productive capital. About 40 percent of the total
beneficiaries are indigenous peoples. By facilitating the access of young sujetos agrarios to land, the
project gives a relatively poor target group the chance to pursue productive projects and settle down
on lands which would otherwise have been rented or acquired by wealthier private operators from
outside the ejido sector. This will also contribute to maintaining the social cohesion of the ejido
sector-a   goal proclaimed by the GOM.

Target lands include underutilized and potentially more productive individual plots (tierras
parceladas).

Subcomponent 1: Promotion of the project and selection of participating young rural entrepreneurs
(US$ 3.0 million)

Through this subcomponent the project's services will be disseminated in the ejidos and
comunidades (including indigenous ones) that have been selected for participation in the project.
The dissemination will be carried out by promoters hired by SRA. If they fulfill the administrative
requirements laid out in the rules of operation of the program, eligible young ruralentrepreneurs can
take part in a series of workshops where (i)the objectives, services and rules of the project are
explained in greater detail, and (ii)  where various options for productive projects in the respective
regions are identified and discussed. The latter workshops include the provision of the trainers and
facilitators as well as of local resource persons who can help the young rural entrepreneurs to
identify viable productive projects in the respective region. In this context SRA i s placing special
emphasis on identifying "clusters" of business opportunities, e.g., those organized along the lines of
product chains (includingmarketing opportunities) that might in the future allow groups of young
rural entrepreneursto associatethemselves injoint productive projects.

Subcomponent 2: Capacity Buildingof Young Rural Entrepreneurs (US$35.5 million)

After participating in Subcomponent 1workshops the young people have two options (1. "Proyecto
Escuela," 2. Fast Track):

     1. They can either form a group which will receive intensive training (on average three
         months) on a specific productive project identified in the local workshops. This option,
         which in the further text is referred to as "Proyecto Escuela," also includes training in
         technical and administrative issues.
    2. Alternatively, SRA will identify and qualify young rural entrepreneurs who, without the
         requirement of undergoing the time-intensive "Proyecto Esucuela " training, can proceed
         directly to presenting a productive project linked to landto FIFONAFE. This i s the so-called
         "fast track" option. However, these potential "fast track" beneficiaries can also receive more
         selective, less intensive training on general issues such as project administration or financial
         matters in the context of Subcomponent 2 as well as specific technical assistance to prepare
         their project proposal, as offered in Subcomponent 3.
In time-intensive option 1 (Proyecto Escuela) a group of 25 to 30 young people is trained on a
specific productive project (e.g., establishment of a greenhouse, fish breeding ponds) identified as a
potentially interesting productive option in the local workshops. In this case, the land for the
establishment of the Proyecto Escuela will be provided by one of the participating ejidos or
comunidades, and the training will be carried out by a trainer or training institution identified by
SRA and its collaborating partners. SRA will finance the training (up to MXN100,OOO per group) as
well as the requiredProyecto Escuela installations (up to MXN300,OOO per group), and will provide
the participatingyoung people with a grant for the time of their participation in the training activity.
After the termination of the Proyecto Escuela, the income generated from this exercise can be

distributed among the participating youth, while the installations and their further use will be turned
over to the ejido that provided the land for the Proyecto Escuela.

The selection criteria and procedures for the young rural entrepreneurs eligible for participation in

Why andhowdoes the projectsupport a Lineof Credit?

Why a LOC?

Currently, people in the social sector (sujetos agrarios) have practically no access to credit. Out of
the few credits received, 85 percent are used to cover current expenditures for production, while
only 14 percent are used for productive investments. Sujetos agrarios have almost no access to
credit usingland since banks are reluctant to provide credit becausecollateral, inthe social sector, in
the form of usufruct rights on land are difficult to foreclose. Moreover, it i s essentially private
lenders who lend money to people in the social sector at a very high rate (50% or more for short-
term micro-credit), given their higher risk and weak (or nonexistent) collateral guarantees. The
purpose of that LOC is to help create a credit and repayment culture inthe social sector. The project
will thus make use of targeted subsidies to jumpstart young entrepreneurial sujetos ugrarios, who
would then become creditworthy and have access to regular credit.

At the same time, the GOM has several ongoing complementary programs that intend to reform the
underlying institutional infrastructure problems that affect the rural finance market. These include
two Bank interventions: (i)          a US$5OOM adjustment loan to strengthen Financieru Rural and the
rural financial market inMexico, and (ii)US$64project (BANSEFI)for strengthening the Savings
                                                     a
and Credit Institutions sector. In addition, the GOM is implementing a national ejidos and
community land rights certification program (PROCEDE, USD1B total) that has already and will
continue to help securing property rights and improving land tenure security inthe social sector.

LOC model

Through the project, beneficiaries from the social sector will have access to credit at the CETES rate
(around 8 percent in February 2005) for the purchase of land and associatedproductive investments.
SRA through FIFONAFE will offer credits to final beneficiaries for the purchase of land at the
CETES rate and will take the credit risk. In addition, SRA will guarantee about 30-50% of FI's
lending to final beneficiaries for complementary productive investments at the CETES rate. It will
also compensate FI for interest rate differences (CETES vs. the FIs' market rates at about 12
percent). This will ensure not only that FI's equity i s not affected, but also that the subsidies
provided through the project do not distort the market.


''TheProcuraduriu       Agruriu (PA) was created in 1992 as a decentralized institution with juridical autonomy and independent
endowment with offices in 32 states and 123 local offices. Its basic mission i s to defend the legal rights of people living in the
social sector. It acts as the ombudsman of the social sector and has a very local presence. The Registro Agrurio Nucionul (RAN)
i s a decentralized institution with three main areas of responsibility: (i)to register transactions, (ii)to provide cadastral
assistance, and (iii)to archive and preserve historical documentation on land entitlements and on PROCEDE.

Appropriate use of publicfunds

Subsidies are considered an appropriate use of public funds in the context of this pilot project to be
implemented inthe social sector. As per OP 8.30, inthis specific case subsidies:

(a) Are transparent, targeted (beneficiaries are sujetos agrarios from the social sector), and capped
(credits are capped: MXN150,OOO for productive projects and MXN250,OOO for land purchase). The
interest spreadthat the project i s subsidizing will be indicatedinthe agreement to be signed between
S W I F O N A F E and the FA and will also be capped.

(b) Are funded explicitly through the government budget, subject to effective control and regular
review.

(c) Guarantee long-term sustainability. The pilot project's learning-by-doing nature will allow
improvements to be introduced during the process (beneficiaries' contributions, reduction of
debtlequity requirements, lower caps, etc.) to improve sustainability and debt repayment. M&Ewill
focus on (i)  beneficiaries' repayment level, and (ii)    successful beneficiaries' capacity to request
additional short- and medium-termcredits.

(d) Do not give an unfair advantage to the FA because once the pilot project has successfully tested
access to credit mechanisms utilizing the FA, financing schemes will be opened up to other viable
financing agencies, public or private.

(e) Are economically justified to achieve the project's poverty reduction objectives:

As already mentioned, in ejidos the market is still thin and needs to be activated. This is why the
project is looking for options to encourage investments and structural change, by investing in the
young workers. Young entrepreneurial sujetos agrarios (i)      lack access to credit for land, as they are
associatedwith highrisk and no guarantees, and (ii)     only have access to high-rate, short-term credits
essentially to finance current expenditures for production.

Introduction

The Access to Land for Young Farmers Pilot Project is a framework programfor small investments
to purchase (or rent) land and other assets and inputs for productive subprojects. The specific
investments to be carried out under the project will be decided by the young farmers in the
beneficiary communities over the life span of the project. It is thus not possible to know a priori how
available resources will precisely be allocated. Therefore, an ex ante estimate of the project's rate of
returnand fiscal impact is not possible. However, there are certain ceilings and other restrictions for
eligible investments. On the basis of these and of a field work enquiry with young farmers about the
potential demand of productive subprojects carried out during preparation we have constructed
illustrative activity models for potentially eligible investments.

Seven illustrative productive subprojects have been constructed based on situations in five different
states in the project area. The sample of productive projects includes on- and off-farm activities, as
well as projects with different investment costs. The sample was chosen to include situations
illustrating the wide variety of productive subprojects that could be financed considering different
agro-ecological zones, market situations, and investment densities. Two of the activity models
illustrate agro-industrial activities: a small plant for dairy processing, producing cheese and yogurt,
and another small plant for the production of jams and syrups. Because of the size of the investment
required and the income and employment generated, these projects would preferably be for small
groups of young farmers. Two other models illustrate intensive agricultural production: a
greenhouse for tomato production using a hydroponic system, and a greenhouse for the production
of cut flowers, especially roses. One model illustrates a small livestock operation: the semi-intensive
production of chicken for meat. Finally, the last two models illustrate mixed cropping operations in
small irrigated farms. The first farm has been assumed to be of 4 hectares used to grow corn (1/2
hectare), tomatoes (1/2 hectare), wheat (1 hectare), and onions (2 hectares). The second farm i s
assumed to have 3 hectares where tomatoes (112 hectare), feed corn (1/2 hectare), soybeans (1/2
hectare), wheat (1/2 hectare), and lemons (1 hectare) are grown. Details of the activity models are
available inthe project file.

FinancialAnalysis

Since the main purpose of the project i s to generate employment and income opportunities for young
farmers we first carry out a financial analysis of the above illustrative models to see how attractive
they are for the potential beneficiaries under the conditions offered by the project. We investigate
two scenarios. Under Scenario 1 the entire project cost i s recovered from the beneficiary, the
maximum loan i s MXN400,OOO (of which MXN250,OOO are for the land and MXN150,OOO for the
investment subproject), and recovery i s over ten years with a one-year grace period. The interest
charged is the CETES rate for 28 days, currently at 8.6 percent. Scenario 2 is like Scenario 1 but
there i s a 20 percent subsidy of subproject costs when the cost of the subproject i s below
MXN200,OOO and 10 percent if it i s above MXN200,OOO. In the case of the two mixed cropping
models we also investigate the case of land rental instead of land purchase. We only examine this
under Scenario 1. For the purpose of the present exercise the purchase price of land (irrigated) has
been estimated at MXN50,OOO per hectare and the rental price at MXN3,OOO per hectare and per
year. Inall models it is assumed that at least 1hectare of land i s bought, with the exception of course
of the cases of rental. The financial profiles of the illustrative models are inTable 1.

Conclusionsand Recommendations

The pilot nature of the Access to Land for Young Farmers Pilot Project provides the flexibility to
include changes in the reglas de operacidn for 2006 and thereafter based on the findings in this
study. Probably more important are the findings of the financial analysis, since it explains how the
young farmers would be affected. The financial analysis shows that there are mainly two clusters of
subprojects: agro-industriavintensive agricultural production models and mixed-cropping models.
Each cluster i s affected differently by the two scenarios presented inthe analysis. Therefore, specific
recommendations for improving the reglas are drafted based on how they would impact each of
these clusters.

Agro-industrialhntensiveagriculturalproduction models. Ingeneral, these subprojects have high
profitability. The problem is that these subprojects require costly beneficiary contributions.
Specifically, in the case of the cheese factory the beneficiary contribution-MXN860,OOO-is        very
high. Considering that young farmers in the ejido sector are the target group of this project, it is
unlikely that they would have enough liquidity to make these investments becausethey involve new
and more complex production processes. Some measures could be taken to ensure that agro-
industrial and intensive agricultural production subprojects are more accessibleto young farmers:

             Encourage young farmers to associate for the subprojects: High subproject costs can
             decrease significantly if they are divided among different associates. In general these
             subprojects produce enough income to support more than one beneficiary. For example,
             the cash flow analysis for the cheese factory shows that it can support up to seven
             partners without their incomes falling under the poverty line duringthe first year.


        0    Make training a prerequisite for the approval of subprojects that involve a significant
             level of technical innovation. Although the financial profitability of agro-industrial and



                                                     57

            greenhouse agricultural production i s high, the probability of failure could be high
            considering that they require technical innovation by beneficiaries and technical
            assistance is limited. To avoid the risk of subproject failure due to the lack of technical
            knowledge, training could be requiredfor this type of subproject. The Proyecto Escuela
            could offer intensive training that the subprojects would need in order to be successful.
            Another advantage of the Proyecto Escuela i s that these young farmers could use these
            spaces to network with others and find partners for the associations.

            Favor subprojects that create vertical and horizontal clusters in the supply chain: The
            consolidation of subprojects in vertical clusters within a supply chain can help reduce
            the risk of changes in prices and costs because the outputs of some subprojects would be
            used as inputs in others. On the other hand, the consolidation of horizontal clusters can
            help expand the supply base and amount of resources available to address common
            challenges such as marketing. Farming and agro-industrial activities have the potential
            to create these links among subprojects. Proyectos Escuela can be installed in adjacent
            ejidos to provide young farmers with the training they need to create these vertical and
            horizontalclusters.

            Allow for a flexible allocation of credit between land acquisition and productive
            investment components: Since these are investment-intensive subprojects, the amount of
            credit available is limited because there i s a ceiling of MXN150,OOO for productive
            investments. There would be a number of benefits from more credit to productive
            investments. First, the size of beneficiary contributions would be reduced and the cash
            flow analysis shows that these subprojects are able to generate enough income to repay
            a higher debt service. Second, productive investments would be used to increase land
            use and productivity in places where land cost i s low. Third, productive investments
            would be used to generate high revenues from small plots in areas were land cost i s
            high. Fourth, productive investment would be used injoint projects between older and
            young farmers where the land is considered a contribution from older farmers in the
            joint venture.

Mixed-croppingproductionmodels.The financial analysis shows that mixed-cropping production
models struggle with profitability. Specifically, those models that involve land purchase show lower
financial profitability than land rental. Some measurescould be taken to improve the profitability of
these subprojects:

            Implement a subsidy scheme such as that proposed under Scenario 2: The cash flow
            analysis for mixed-cropping models 1 and 2 under Scenario 2 shows that the subsidy
            during the first year of the projects has a great impact on the models' income stream.
            Net income at full development remains the same under both scenarios, but the internal
            rate of return increases in Scenario 2. More important i s that a subsidy of 20 percent
            allows the producer to have positive income as early as the third year of the project,
            which under Scenario 1could be as late as the sixth year. A larger subsidy (for example
            50 percent) would help young farmers assure a positive income earlier in the project
            cycle. This larger subsidy could be restricted to those projects with a cost below
            MXN200,000, as the cash flow analysis shows that the subsidy does not have a
            significant impact on more expensive projects which generate higher revenues. It i s not
            recommended that the subsidy be attached only to the productive investments because
            the size of the subsidy would be reduced dramatically given that these projects are land
            intensive.




                                                     58

e Include subsidies for land rental: For mixed-cropping models, the financial analysis
  shows that land rental schemes prove to be a good alternative to land purchase. This
  type of project should be encouragedby providing subsidies for productive investments.
  Long-term rental will also have the advantage of overcoming the reluctance of older
  ejidatarios to sell their land and encouraging the rentalkoncession of common land in
  natural resource-rich ejidos.

e Consider granting incentives for counterpart funds in a matching subsidy scheme:
  Mixed-cropping models 1 and 2 for land purchase show that beneficiaries may be
  eligible for credit packages that they would have difficulty repaying. The use of
  personal funds could make young farmers more risk averse. A mechanism to encourage
  them to use their own funds for the subprojects i s to establish a matching subsidy
  scheme where counterpart funds would be required to obtain subsidies. The problem is
  that beneficiaries with severe liquidity constraints, and who need the subsidy the most,
  may not be able to have access to them.

e Consider a scheme with progressive debt repayment: A repayment mechanism in which
  the debt burden increases throughout the years would reduce pressure from the first
  years of operation in mixed-croppingmodels. Given that mixed-cropping models show
  a narrow profit margin throughout their life, it i s recommendedthat the subsidy scheme
  still be implemented under this modality.

As part of project preparation, a study of land markets in the social sector is being prepared. The
study draws on a review of existing literature, case studies prepared by a team of national
consultants in early 2004, and some exploratory field work that was carried out between December

the eight states participating in the pilot phase of the Program. Focus group interviews were
conducted with ejido and comunidud authorities, members of the Youth Committees (Comite`s de

1.Legalframeworkfor landmarkets

Land transactions in the social sector are framed by the 1992 Agrarian Law. This law, following a
reform of Article 27 of the Constitution, marked a major policy change away from land
redistribution toward securing property rights and stimulating land markets in the social sector. The
law has confirmed the property rights of ejidos and comunidudes on their land and strengthenedtheir
autonomy in defining their internal property rights regime by allocating their land into three main
categories: Land for urban settlement, rural land under individual tenure, and rural land under
communal tenure. While property rights on urban plots (solures urbunos) are recorded in the public
registry, rights to rural plots are registered inthe newly createdNational Agrarian Registry (RAN).

According to the Agrarian Law, only those persons recognized as agrarian subjects can acquire land
in the same ejido, which implies that land cannot be sold to pobl~dores,'~      members of other ejidos,
and private farmers. Thus, through its faculty to recognize uvecindudos, the ejido assembly acts as a
filter in land sales markets. Land held under common tenure and land of comunidudes cannot be
sold. Individual parcels certified through PROCEDE cannot be divided for sales or inheritance
purposes.

These restrictions on land sales can be circumvented by converting ejido land into private property
(dominio pleno). Only certified ejidos can adopt dominio pleno which must be endorsed by 75
percent of all ejiduturios. No ejiduturio can possess more than five percent of the total land of the
ejido or than that established as ceilings for private property.18 The legal recognition of a sales
transaction of individual land only requires a letter of intent signed by both parties and two
witnesses, and its registration in RAN. The seller's spouse and children have pre-emptive rights
(derecho del tanto) to be exercised within thirty days of notification of the intended transaction.

No restrictions apply to land rental markets or other forms of temporary or partial transfer of
property rights, such as sharecropping arrangements or the formation of joint venture companies.
Ejiduturios can rent their plots or parts thereof to any person or company residing within or outside
the boundaries of the ejido. Moreover, ejidos can engage injoint ventures (sociedudes mercuntiles)
with outside investors on common land, in which case the land must be converted into dominio
pleno. The usufruct rights of individual and communal ejido land can be pledged as a guarantee to
creditors such as financial institutions and persons engaged in a commercial relationship or joint
venture with the ejidolejiduturios.

2. Dynamicsand characteristics of landmarkets

High levels of informality: Evidence from field work suggests a high level of informality in land
sales transactions, even in certified ejidos. This endangers the sustainability of PROCEDE, because
data may become obsolete if the RAN is not updated. Although this topic merits further
investigation during the land market survey, previous field work points to the following causes: (i)
lack of knowledge and awareness of legal provisions and administrative procedures; (ii)high
transaction costs in relation to the value of the transaction and the perceived incremental benefits of
formalization; and (iii) legalprohibition on subdividing plots.

Dynamics of sales and rental transactions:Most observers conclude that PROCEDEhas stimulated
land sales and rental markets, although its impact has been lower than expected. The informality of
land transactions makes it difficult to assess their magnitude and limits the possibilities of
monitoring land market activities. Thus, apart from the surveys conducted by De Janvry (1997) and
Deininger (2001), evidence of landtransactions is mostly anecdotal or based on case studies.

According to Deininger (2001), the activity of land sales markets has not significantly altered since
the implementation of PROCEDE. Out of a sample of 24 ejidos, 3 percent of the ejiduturios in
certified ejidos bought and 5 percent sold land between 1992 and 2000. The respective figures for
non-certified ejidos are 4 percent and 3 percent. In turn, 11percent of the surveyed private farmers
had purchased land in the same period. The literature points to a higher activity of land rental
markets. A survey conducted by PA (1998) in all certified ejidos found that one-third of all
ejiduturios were engaged in a contractual arrangement regarding their land. Most of these
contractual arrangements were informal and only included verbal agreements between the parties;
very few contracts are registered with ejido authorities or public registries. Sharecropping, land
rental, and land loans are the most frequent forms of transactions. Deininger (2001) found higher
levels of rental activities in certified ejidos than in non-certified ejidos, but equal to private farmers
after 1992. This suggests that PROCEDE has increased rental market activities by improving the
legal security of usufruct right holders who no longer had to fear the loss of their land rights when
renting.

Types of land markets: Land markets have different dynamics and characteristics depending on the
predominant production system, land quality, access to infrastructure and water, distance to centers
of demand such as towns and processing enterprises, local history and culture, and ethnic
composition.

Landmarkets are most active in ejidos that are located in areas with a predominance of commercial
agriculture or that are integrated in commodity chains. The dynamics of land markets and the
formation of prices are determined by the profitability of the main crops. This type of land market
can be found inregions with large-scale production of seasonalcrops on irrigated landsuch as in the
Northwest, but also in other parts of the country with important production of fruits and vegetables,
sugarcane, coffee, or tobacco. Given that most ejiduturios lack access to working capital and have
disadvantages with regard to machinery ownership, access to output and factor markets and
information, the demand for land comes mainly from the private sector. Only a minority of most



                                                       61

entrepreneurial and affluent ejidutarios is able to rent or purchase land. Generally, land i s sold or
rented to those offering the highest price and no preference is granted to family or ejido members.
Land i s sold either through conversion into dominio pleno or recognition of the buyer or related
persons as uvecindudos. These land markets are largely confined to irrigated land which commands
highprices inthe range of MXN50,OOO to 150,000 per ha, depending on the region.

Agribusiness and commercial farmers prefer to rent land in order to avoid high costs and possible
administrative and political problems related to land purchase. Short-term rental of land provides a
high level of flexibility in shifting production in case of phytosanitary problems, declining soil
fertility, or access to water. InSonora and Sinaloa, more than half of the irrigatedejido land i s rented
to outsiders of the ejido sector and rental prices reach MXN4,500 per ha and year. In some cases
(e.g., vegetable production), where investments in fixed assets such as greenhouses or irrigation
systems are required, investors engage in long-term rental contracts with ejiduturios.

Speculative influence in land markets can be found inperi-urban areas and where public investments
such as roads, airports, or industrial parks are planned. Land is acquired at relatively low prices by
agents with privileged access to information on these projects, often indirectly through connected
individuals (so-called prestunombres). Private investments in tourism, aquaculture, or housing also
increase land values and thus the opportunity costs for agricultural use. In coastal or peri-urban
areas, ejido land i s sold to private investors by ejiduturios seeking windfall profits. Transactions
occur through conversion into dominio pleno, the recognition of buyers as uvecindudos,or informal
transactions.

In areas with a predominance of subsistence agricultural systems where production is primarily
aimed at local consumption or regional markets, most land transactions occur among members of the
same ejido or comunidud. Sales transactions on rural land are less frequent than rentals,
sharecropping, and land loans, although an active market exists for housing plots. Prices for
agricultural land are significantly lower, in the range of 5,000 to 20,000 for rainfed land and
MXN30,OOO to 50,000 for irrigated land. Most sales are informal or semiformal (with written
evidence inthe minutes of ejido assembly meetings or in ejido registry books), with the exception of
those ejidos that have experienced land conflicts in the past. In comunidudes, informal land sales
among comuneros can also be observed, although mostly disguised as permanent transfers of land
with compensation for improvements made on the land. Most land transactions are part of asset
(de)accumulation processesat different stages of the families' life cycle, or are relatedto migration.

Other determinants of land markets: Land quality, access to water, and site-specific conditions
such as distance to markets, roads, and places of residence play a major role in the development of
sales and rental markets and the formation of prices. Important differences exist with regardto these
conditions, both within and across ejidos. Most rental and sales transactions occur on irrigated land
or on plain rainfed land with sufficient humidity and good accessibility. Poor rainfed land
(ugostudero) i s mainly used for extensive livestock production and i s often held under communal
tenure.

Land markets are further shaped by the specific history of the ejido as an important determinant of
local identities, cultural attachment to land, and strength of the ejido institutions. This i s reflected in
the extent to which ejidutarios try to retain land and their ejiduturio status as part of the family
patrimony and how the ejido assembly uses its faculty to recognize non-ejido members as
avecindudos as a filter for land market transactions. Ejidos that emerged as a result of a long period
of struggle for land and consist of relatively homogeneous members usually maintain a stronger
local identity, and ejiduturios are more attached to their land (especially where the first generation of
ejiduturios is still alive). Land markets are less active and mainly confined to local boundaries and



                                                      62

family ties. Inturn, ejidos that were founded as part of colonization or settlement processesand that
consist of ejiduturios from different regions have a higher fluctuation of ejido members. In these
ejidos, new settlers are easily recognized as uvecindudos or informal buyers as posesionurios or
ejiduturios, and land markets are more active and open.

Ingeneral terms, however, the social segmentationof land markets tends to erode as the commercial
value of ejido land increases, e.g., due to public investments in infrastructure or the advent of
commercialfarming opportunities.

Degree of underutilization of land: No statistics exist that would allow one to assess the degree of
land utilization in relation to its productive or market potential. Earlier studies indicate that levels of
technology use and intensity of production have declined since the early 1990s following structural
adjustment policies. Major constraints for increasing land use intensity are the low profitability of
most farming activities, and poor access to input and output markets and to agricultural support
services. Collective action problems have prevented most ejidos from establishing forward and
backward linkages that would allow them to overcome poorly developed input and output markets.
Moreover, most ejido land is rainfed and often of poor quality (ugostudero), and access to water i s
often the critical determinant of its potentialfor agricultural use. As outlined in the previous section,
land markets function reasonably well in those areas where commercial farming opportunities exist.
However, in those areas land markets tend to shift land to private farmers and better-off ejiduturios,
due to their better access to capital, markets, and information.

As a consequence, only a minority of ejidos, primarily those located in less remote areas with
sufficient access to water, has been able to diversify into more profitable high-value crops. Most
ejiduturios are engaged in subsistence farming with occasional sales of surplus production in local
or regional markets and depend mainly on off-farm income, remittances, and temporary migration.
Another important trend contributing to the underutilization of land i s the expansion of extensive
cattle production on agricultural land. The attractiveness of cattle can be explained by its low labor
and working capital requirements (under extensive production systems) and its function as an in-
kindsavings instrument. However, some ejidos have been able to tap important niche markets, often
with external support. Examples include organic coffee, non-wood forest products, and ecotourism,
especially in naturalresource-rich ejidos with limited agricultural potential.

Access of ejido youth to land: National statistics on the age structure of certified ejidos show that
only 22.6 percent of all ejiduturios are below the age of 40, while 27.5 percent are older than 65.
These figures reflect that inheritance is the main form of permanent access to land which conveys
the status of ejiduturio. Although in some areas children of ejiduturios or comuneros receive small
plots upon marriage, the dominant inheritance pattern consists of the transfer of the entire land first
to the spouse and eventually to one child, usually a son. This reflects the central role of land as a
livelihood asset for elderly ejiduturios in the absence of a pension system. Since women access land
as ejiduturius mainly after the death of their husbands, most ejiduturias consist of elderly widows
who often lack the physical capacity and working capital to farm their land. Although the
predominant inheritance pattern further delays the intergenerational transfer of land ownership, it
protects elderly widows from falling into absolutepoverty.

Other factors contribute to the late intergenerational transfer of land. Elderly ejiduturios retain land
rights to ensure continued support from those family members who are interested in eventually
inheritingthe land. Moreover, many elderly ejiduturios have a strong cultural attachment to their
land and consider farming as part of their identity. They also express doubts about the real interest of
the younger generation in farming and in preserving the family's assets, in view of their increased
educational level and the "culture of migration." This is exacerbated by fears that after ceding the



                                                       63

land, the heirs might sell it in order to finance migration and abandon their parents (even though
only a few concrete examples of this could be found). Temporary transfers of land use rights to
children are a way to test their ability and interest in farming before inheritance decisions are taken.
Finally, the legal prohibition on subdividing plots further constrains an earlier intergenerational
transfer of land, especially in case of ejiduturios who possess only one plot." However, this
provision is frequently violated inpractice.

Despite the late inheritance, the ejido population in the age range between 18 and 39 has temporary
access to land through a range of informal mechanisms such as rental, sharecropping, and land
loans, either within or outside family ties. Most of these arrangements only extend to one cropping
season or year which creates uncertainty and tends to undermine incentives for investments and
sustainable land use practices. However, in practice these arrangements are often renewed several
times, especially if they occur among family members. Land of elderly ejiduturios or widows is
often farmed with the support of children or other family members. Insome cases, part of the land i s
loaned or rented to children or other ejido members. Further field work would be required to assess
to whether such intrafamily arrangements involve a de facto transfer of farmmanagement decisions,
to which extent control i s retained by the formal owners, and how the costs and benefits are shared.

Overall, temporary access to land through the mechanisms outlined above seems less problematic in
ejidos with a predominance of subsistence agricultural systems and limited integration in
commercial farming activities. However, access can be more restricted in ejidos with high
population pressure, especially with regard to irrigated land, and where land i s highly fragmented. In
ejidos that are integrated into the production of commercial crops, strong outside demand for land
rental or purchase raises the opportunity costs for customary land transfers within ejidos. Youth are
mostly excluded from rental and sales markets due to lack of working capital and highcosts of land.
Inless commercial ejidos, remittances are an important driver in land markets. Several cases were
detected where young or middle-aged persons have used the proceeds from migration to purchase
land. This i s also reflectedin the significant number ofposesionurios between the ages of 30 and 50,
as reflectedinPROCEDE data.

Potential demand for land by ejido youth: Field work revealed that younger generations have a
more pragmatic relationship to land. Access to land i s no longer regarded as an aim per se, but its
attractiveness depends on its potential as an income-earning asset and the scope for developing
attractive land-based income generating activities in the ejido. The option to migrate raises the
opportunity costs of land-related projects, especially among young people under the age of 25 who
do not yet have children. Migration is mainly due to limited productive and employment
opportunities in ejidos especially duringthe lean season, low profitability of agriculture, and the lack
of working capital to engage in farming activities and rent land. The difficulties in obtaining secure
and permanent access to land and delays and uncertainties related to inheritance further fuel the
propensity to migrate. In most rural areas (including small private farmers), migration has become
an integral part of the life cycle of rural youth.

The long-term interest in land-based projects may increase with age and family size. Migration
becomes less attractive for middle-aged persons, given the risks and uncertainties, long periods of
absence from family members, and the harsh realities inthe labor markets involved. Especially those
who did not manage or desire to establish their entire family elsewhere often return to their home
communities and constitute an important target group for the Program. These persons often have
accumulated some savings, have invested in housing, and may now seek to consolidate their
patrimony through land purchase or land-based productive activities. A second target group i s young

4. Implicationsfor projectdesignandimplementation:

In view of the diversity of situations regarding land market characteristics, land quality, resource
endowments, and socioeconomic and cultural conditions, the Program will adopt a flexible
approach. Several instruments will be piloted in order to respond to diverse local and regional
conditions, some of which are already contemplated in the existing reglus de operucion. Others will
be includedinthe revision of the rules of operation for 2006 and onward.

Different modes of land access: The purchase of land i s most feasible in areas where underutilized
land i s available at low prices and land-use intensity and productivity can be increased through on-
farm investments. The supply of land is likely to come mainly from those ejiduturios who possess
several plots but lack the physical conditions or working capital to use all of them productively.
Moreover, the possibilities of purchasing land are higher in ejidos with active land sales markets,
such as those located in former colonization zones, peri-urban areas, and areas with commercial
agriculture. Given the high land prices in the latter cases, purchase might only be feasible for small
plots to be used for high-value crops, intensive livestock production, or processing. In some cases,
where small plots are not available, conversion into dominio pleno might be encouraged. Landrental
or joint ventures with older ejiduturios would be preferable under those circumstances. Rainfed land
with sufficient humidity and good access to markets and infrastructure would be another source of
supply.

Long-term rental has the advantage of overcoming the reluctance of elderly ejiduturios to sell,
especially in cases where land i s scarce and ejiduturios only possess a single plot. It also avoids the
high costs of purchasing land which could underminethe financial viability of projects, especially in
case of imgated land or projects requiring larger plots. Long-term rental can be used to test the
feasibility of the projects and the long-term interest of young farmers addressing respective doubts
from the elderly. The lower costs for land may enable young farmers to save which would allow
them to purchase land at the end of the rental period.

Joint ventures between youth and the elderly are a third option, especially in those cases where
landholders are reluctant to sell or rent. They may be used to capitalize underutilized land and
combine the skills and experience of different generations. They could be structured in different
ways, involving different members of one family, landless youth, and ejiduturios from different
families, or even private investors as third parties. Joint ventures imply a more gradual transfer of
farm assets and could be structured in a way that would allow young shareholders to gradually buy
out the elderly. Joint ventures with private investors could be established on common lands, if the
ejido assembly agrees to its conversion into dominiopleno.

Innatural resource-rich ejidos or indigenous comunidudes,youth could either rent common landor
receive a concession to use specific natural resources over a longer time frame. Examples include



                                                     65

forest use (both timber and non-wood forest products), ecotourism, or other biodiversity products.
Projects must be embedded in existing natural resource management plans. This option, not yet
included in the current program, will be further studied during project implementation, to be
includedin a follow-up operation.

Legal assistance: The project will provide legal assistance in all aspects related to the transfer of
land rights and the formation of joint enterprises under the modalities outlined above. This includes
the formalization of existing rights on the land involved in transactions (especially in non-certified
ejidos), the registration of the transaction in RAN, etc. Assistance will also be provided for contract
design, especially incase of long-termrental, joint projects, and concessions.

Funding mechanisms: Different financing mechanisms will be piloted to ensure the financial
viability of projects through incentives for minimizing costs of the land acquisition component and
maximizing the on-farm investment component, thereby increasing the productive potential of the
land. Mechanisms include (a) funding decisions based on a reverse auction mechanism giving
preference to least costly, viable proposals within the available budget; (b) funding decisions based
on a quality- and cost-based selection mechanism; (c) flexible allocation of financing packages
between land acquisition and on-farm investment components; (d) matching grants up to a certain
amount for on-farm investments while land acquisition i s being financed through loans; and (e)
equity contribution of beneficiaries to project costs, in combination with a savings scheme for
resource-constrained youth. Options (c), (d), and (e) are not yet included in the current reglus de
operucidn but the study as well as lessons from other similar programs in other countries strongly
recommend introducing these mechanismsin the future rules of operation as mentioned in the PAD
section, "Lessons Learned."

Diversification of productive activities: The Program will encourage the formation of clusters of
similar productive products in ejidos or subregions to facilitate diversification into high-value crops
and intensive livestock production and the establishment of forward and backward linkages. This
approach makes it possible to achieve economies of scale in marketing and processing, purchase
inputs, and contract support services. Clusters may also allow some young people to specialize in
processing and marketing in response to their different skills, experiences, and inclinations. In more
remote and natural resource-rich ejidos, projects will focus on niche markets. The Program will
provide ongoing support and mentoring with regard to production, marketing, management, and
conflict resolution.

I.SocialAssessment

1.1Social Assessment Objectives

The principal objectives of the social assessment were to: (a) identify project beneficiaries; (b) identify
forms of organization and decisionmaking at community level and beneficiaries' organizational level; (c)
identify management levels and based on this propose a typology of organization and productivity for
project beneficiaries, in order to ensure their participation; (d) identify the principal productive activities
to be developed and propose schemes that assure the participation of the different stakeholders in the
project's area of influence; (e) identify the possible risks that could arise during the process of project
implementation, and propose actions to mitigate them; and (g) identify the indigenous population in each
of the proposed states and develop an action plan to assure the full participation of indigenous peoples.

1.2 Methodologyof the Social Assessment

The social assessment process comprised three relevant phases, including:

(1) Review of Literature. Extensive review of the literature, beginning in 2004 and continuing in 2005.
The following activities were carried out: review of documentation, including reports such as social and
environmental assessments of other projects (e.g., PROCYMAF I1and COINBIO, the Brazil Land Fund
Pilot Project. Relevant publications from universities and NGOs were reviewed, as well as unpublished
documents from SRA's archives andthe program's rules of operation.

(2) Field work and participatory consultations. For the field work phases, the following activities were
carried out: (a) semi-structured interviews, questionnaires given to families in selected communities for
purposes of field sampling; (b) focus groups with qualified informants and institutions involved; (c)
participatory sessions and consultation workshops with groups from other government agencies, NGOs,
and community representatives; and (d) case studies that reflect the experience and interpretation of the
problem according to the perceptions of potentialbeneficiaries; and

(3) Systemization of data. The Social Assessment team carried out an extensive systemization of data,
including the review of literature as well as information collected in the field work phase. Different
reports were obtained. These are included in the final social assessment document and in the Indigenous
Development Plan.

1.3 Principal results of the social study

Direct beneficiaries of FTJER

    (a) Typology of young people. Three age ranges are distinguished, reflecting young people's types of
    preferences. (a) 19-25 years, (b) 25-35 years, (c) 35-39 years. Before the age of 25, a young person i s
    concerned with buildingup his own capital to start a family, while between the ages of 25 and 35, he
    requires a larger income due to the increasing costs of supporting his family. This situation causes
    him to assign greater value to activities that are different from those offered in rural areas, and he




                                                      80

    often migrates. After age 35, the decision to establish one's roots sets in and the option to remain in
    one's community of origin becomesa reality.

    (b) Youngpeople as agrarian subjects represent 41.3 percent of the total ejido sector p,opulation in
    the regions which are the subject of the Program. According to INEGI (2001), this percentage i s
    equivalent to a population of approximately 3 million young people, distributed among the 10
    federative entities (states) selected. Ten percent of these young people are ejiduturios with land and
    thus have access rights to common lands and have decisionmaking power inthe ejido assembly. Sixty
    percent of them are children of ejido settlers without their own land, while 30 percent are landholders
    with the right to use land. One of the implications of this situation i s that 90 percent of the young
    people have limited possibilities for participatingin agrarian sector decisionmaking processes.

    (c) Participation of young people in community-level decisionmaking mechanisms. There is a
    disconnection between access to land and access to decisionmaking processesin ejidos. The principal
    authorities on the use of agrarian rights are ejido and community assemblies in which most young
    people, because they lack the agrarian and voting rights required for participation, are not part of the
    collective decisionmaking process. Thus, young people seek the possibility of having full
    participation in local and regional decisionmaking groups and bodies that are different from social
    ownership of land. The agrarian production model i s not attractive to them and, deprived of
    technological development options and opportunities to establish themselves on the land, they prefer
    to emigrate and to seek in cities or other countries the meansto obtain better salaries and services.

    (d) Presence of ZndigenousPeoples. Nearly 30,000 agrarian units are registered in Mexico, of which
    38 percent are located in the 10 states covered by FTJER. In addition, slightly more than 7,000
    agrarian units have been identified with indigenous populations whose proportion ranges from 10 to
    100 percent. According to the Inter-Institutional Catalogue of Agrarian Units (CINA),*l 439 of the
    2,278 communities identifiedare located inOaxaca.

    (e) Typology of participation and organizational capacity. Young people's participation in
    community and ejido decisionmaking bodies may be considered from different points of view and in
    different spaces. Spaces for decisionmaking involve the community or ejido, the organization of
    producers, family life, and the municipal and regional context. The types of decisions made may have
    an effect on the social-cultural, economic, and political scheme. The study develops a typology of the
    participation and organizational capacity of communities and ejidos.

1.5 PotentialrisksofFTJER
    (a) Latent and/or potential agrarian confEicts that may be related to land markets or linked to
    specific situations such as areas on the outskirts of urban areas, tourism activities, or the increased
    pressure of delinquency or drugs. To identify these risks, ongoing linkage and communication
    between public and social agrarian organizations will be important. FTJER promoters will play an
    important role in providing informationthrough their reports and ongoing monitoring.

    (b) Excessive indebtedness in relation to young people's ability to pay. This situation will also have
    an unfavorable effect on the living conditions of the families involved. Most young people lack their
    own capital and familiarity with credit, and thus administrative training, monitoring by promoters,
    and ongoing monitoring will serve as basic strategiesto verify and address this possible risk.
    (e) Possible involuntary resettlementor displacementof sustainablemeans of living (OP 4.12). The
    Program would not have this type of social impact as a direct result, because these are not involuntary
    or forced transactions but rather voluntary agreements between ejidatarios and young people
    interested in the lands. To avoid indirect impacts, FTJER will implement measures to guide
    ejidatarios and their families on the management and use of resources obtained from the sale of their
    parcels. The Agrarian Law also establishes specific procedures, such as the right of pre-emption and
    legal time periods to carry out transactions for the purchase and sale of parcels, so that relatives as
    well as users of these lands are protected.

    (d) Exclusion. Various categories of exclusion are identified: (a) young men and women who were
    not elected by ejido assemblies (EA), to be designated as "settlers" and subject to participation in
    FTJER; (b) women (i)wives of agrarian subjects, (ii)without relatives in the ejido, (iii)who livefrom
    the ejido seat; (c) older adults who are agrarian subjects, without access to technical-legal advice; and
    (d) indigenous peoples interested in participating. To avoid creating such situations, special emphasis
    will be placed on efforts of promotion and direct work with EAs so that there i s gender equality in the

11.3 Landtenure

Three types of land tenure coexist in indigenous regions: (a) communal assets; (b) indigenous ejidos; and
(c) indigenous ejidos that operatein accordance with the regulations of the Agrarian Law.

Communal lands encompass a territory that may belong to one or several communities (associated
ownership; for example, Associated Peoples in Oaxaca's Sierra Norte) and land control i s exercised and
governed by the General Assembly of Comuneros (GAC), which i s elected by traditional authorities
(governors, principals, tatamandones, councils of elders, etc.). The GAC i s the community's chief
authority, and it in turn elects a board of directors consisting of a president, a secretary, a treasurer, and
their alternates, including a surveillance council.

Communal assets are divided into parcels that are usedtemporarily in accordance with community uses
and customs. Lifelong use of several parcels is also permitted, to benefit comuneros and their families,
who may inherit them or exchange them among members of the community, but these parcels are not
considered private property.

Indigenous ejidos consist of lands that were recognized for communities which did not possess
documentation from the colonial era to prove their ownership but which in practice operate and are
organized under the rules of communal lands. Thus, we find indigenous ejidos that operate in accordance
with regulations of the Agrarian Law, representing a minority of lands inthe hands of indigenous peoples
and parceled out individually; they may opt to remain as ejidos or become private property, as stated in
Article 27 of the current Constitution.

11.5 Migration

Duringthe social assessment, and specifically while analyzing the indigenous population, migration in
indigenous communities was identified as quite acute and growing. At first this was a rural-urban
migration by peasants who were expelled from rural areas and were attracted by large cities and by poles
of nationaldevelopment, or who sought better living and working conditions on the mythical "other side."
Soon, however, the origin and destination of migration became more complex and their impact for the
country was greater. Since the post-revolutionary era, the indigenous population has joined national
waves of migration and imposes its unique characteristics both on their places of destination and on the
placesthey leave, and on the processitself. Duringproject implementation, specific studies will be carried
out to analyze the problem of migration in indigenous zones, but also to analyze the impact of remittances
and how these may be reoriented to productive aspects.

11.6 Legalframework

Article 106 of the Agrarian Law states that "lands corresponding to indigenous groups must be protected
by the authorities, under terms of the law that regulates Article 4 and the second paragraph of the seventh
section of constitutional article 27."

Notable constitutional changes were made in 2001 which frame a new relationship between the state and
indigenous peoples, including: (a) the right of free determination for indigenous peoples and
communities; and (b) deciding on their internal forms of social, economic, political, and cultural
coexistence and organization, as well as other relevant forms relatedto land tenure, the policy of equality
at three levels of government, and support for productive activities.

The Law of the National Commission for the Development of Indigenous Peoples was published in2001.
This law transformed the National Indigenist Institute (NII),consolidating it into a new institution whose
mission i s to orient, coordinate, promote, support, encourage, monitor, and evaluate public programs,
projects, strategies, and actions to achieve the integrated, sustainable development and full exercise of the
rightsof indigenous peoples and communities.

Therefore, CDIfocuses its actions, on the one hand, toward providing protection for individual rights, and
on the other, toward the recognition and effectiveness of the rights which, as a collective entity,

 indigenous peoples demand, such as legal status, free determination and autonomy, full access to state
jurisdiction, and respect for their culture.24

 11.7 Agrarian conflicts

 There i s a lack of clarity regarding the number of conflicts that exist in the country's indigenous regions.
 However, many agrarian conflicts remain unresolved in Mexico. For its part, the Mexican Government
 affirms that it has pinpointed those conflicts related to land problems, concentrated in states with larger
 indigenous populations.

 Despite the existence of hundreds of agrarian conflicts, their principal causes have been identified, e.g.,
 (a) problems caused by land boundaries between different population centers; and (b) donations linkedto
 land. Other types of agrarian conflicts range from Govemment-issued expropriation decrees to
 intercommunity problems.

The social assessment identified a series of actions to be carried out duringproject implementation, which
 will be coordinated by the Secretariat of Agrarian Reform with other government agencies dealing with
this type of problem.
