                                        1. INTRODUCTION
1.1     This program document proposes a Third Economic Governance and Recovery
Grant (EGRG 111) for the Republic of C8te d'Ivoire in the amount of SDR58.8 million
(US$90 million equivalent). This stand-alone, one-tranche development policy operation
supports the Government's efforts to pursue the implementation of governance reforms initiated
under the first and second EGRGs approved by the Board in April 2008 and March 2009,
respectively, and detailed in its Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP) adopted in February
2009.' Specifically, the EGRG I11 focuses on continuing and deepening policy reforms to
improve public financial management (PFM), and governance, transparency and efficiency in
three key sectors - energy, cocoa and finance, in the challenging context of an exceptionally
unfavorable global environment.

1.2       The Grant is an integral part of IDA'S proposed Country Partnership Strategy for
C8te d'Ivoire for the period FY10-FY13 and is closely coordinated with assistance provided to
support the program of Government's reforms through the ongoing Governance and Institutional
Development Grant (US$13 million equivalent), the Enhanced HIPC Initiative, and the IMF-
supported Extended Credit Facility (ECF) program. The reforms supported by this grant are
aligned to the government's PRSP and reflect a continuation of focused support to the Republic
of C8te d'Ivoire at a critical stage of its transition from post conflict recovery to long-term
sustained growth and development. However, as the country attempts to make this transition, its
open and still fragile economy has been affected by the succession of the food and fuel price
crisis in 2008 and the global financial crisis in 2009. The nascent growth has been hampered
through the decline in the confidence of private investors, the fall in some commodity prices, and
the economic slowdown in the sub-region, although these have been in part mitigated by the
overall economic rebound from the socio-political crisis and high cocoa and oil prices.

1.3       The operation would provide financial support to the government in the context of
the unfavorable global economic environment and the fact that C8te d'Ivoire's annual debt
service to the World Bank will remain considerable until attainment of the HIPC completion
point expected for the first half of 201 1. Thus, the amount of this grant has been increased from
SDR49.0 million (US$75 million equivalent) to SDR58.8 million (US$90 million equivalent)
through US$15 million from the Crisis Response Window to help mitigate the ongoing effects of
the global economic slowdown on key priorities reflected in the Poverty Reduction Strategy
Paper. Since C8te d'Ivoire is at high risk of debt distress, IDA resources are provided on grant
terms, per the stipulations of the grant allocation system agreed with IDA donors for the IDA 15
period (FY09-FY 11). C8te d'Ivoire is also eligible for annual exceptional post-conflict

Partnership Strategy (CPS), further DPOs would be considered to support the Government's

1.4     CGte d'Ivoire's political situation continues to stabilize. The Government is making
progress towards the reunification of the country and preparation of presidential elections, with
voter registration completed. The Government is also pursuing efforts started in 2007 to restore
public financial management processes and procedures throughout the country, effect a visible
improvement in the governance and management of the energy and cocoa sectors, the country's
two main export and public revenue sources, and restructure the financial sector to ensure its
soundness.

1.5     A rapid improvement in public financial management and governance and
transparency in the energy, cocoa and financial sectors is critical to sustain the ongoing
peace process and recovery of the economy. Specifically, the Government needs to ensure that
scarce public resources are utilized efficiently and transparently to provide essential public
services, that resources raised in the cocoa sector are effectively used to rebuild the sector, and
that the overall tax burden is reduced to ensure that the farmers obtain a higher share of the world
price. The Government has already implemented a number of governance reforms, and intends
to pursue and deepen these efforts as it continues its post-conflict recovery.

       2. C ~ T D'IVOIFWS DEVELOPMENT CHALLENGES AND PROSPECTS

2.1      CGte d'Ivoire, a medium-size country with a population projected to reach 20
million in 2010, is the largest economy in West Africa after Nigeria and is critical to the
overall development of the sub-region. CGte d'Ivoire has an important economic influence
over the rest of the region, given its size (accounting for 40 percent of GDP in the WAEMU), its
relatively high income per capita (US$1,026 in 2009 as per the Bank's Atlas methodology), and
its role in transit trade for landlocked neighboring countries. With its large immigrant population,
CGte d'Ivoire has also been an important source of worker remittances for other countries in the
sub-region. The country is the largest exporter of cocoa and raw cashew nuts in the world, and
its level of total merchandise exports is the fourth largest in Sub-Saharan Africa (after South
Africa, Nigeria and Angola). It has a relatively diversified agricultural economy as well as
significant manufacturing and services sectors.

2.2     However, the civil war in 2002-03 and subsequent years of stalemate in the peace
process took a heavy toll on the economy and social outcomes. During the years of crisis,
growth was very weak, and the incidence of poverty rose from 38.4 percent in 2002 to 48.9
percent in 2008.2 The rural poverty ratio is 63 percent, rising to 85 percent in the north. The
contribution of agriculture to GDP increased, reflecting a decline in the manufacturing and
services sectors. CGte d'Ivoire is now ranked 166th out of 179 countries according to the 2008
UNDP Human Development Index. While average per capita income is the highest in mainland
West Africa, it is very unevenly distributed, with a Gini coefficient of 0.42. Health and
education indicators have also suffered from the political crisis, with mortality of children under
5 rising fiom 157 in 1990 to 192 in 2007, and life expectancy falling from 49 in 1995 to 47 in

The authorities are taking steps to give top priority to interventions in agriculture and rural

2.3     Political instability during the conflict resulted in, and was fueled by, a deterioration
of governance, transparency and budget allocations. The public expenditure management and
financial accountability review (PEMFAR) conducted in 2008 revealed a deterioration in
budgetary discipline, financial management, and procurement processes and procedures. Budget
allocations in the social sectors (health and education) have significantly declined since 2000 and
resources were diverted away from poverty-reducing sectors, such that the delivery of services
has suffered. Significant extra-budgetary expenses occurred and government procurement laws
were not respected for some major public works. In the cocoa sector, vested interests have
undermined the effectiveness of the sector's institutional set-up and held back the development
of the cocoa supply chain. Corruption and lack of legitimacy associated with the cocoa
regulatory and promotional agencies, combined with inadequate producer incentives, have
limited the sector's contribution to rural growth. Several smaller banks were also created with
weak governance structures, while the microfinance sector suffered from lack of oversight after
the withdrawal of foreign partners.

2.4     The Government has started efforts to reverse the deterioration in basic service
provision by redeploying public services throughout the country and progressively rehabilitating
education, health, and village water supply infrastructures. Beginning with the 2007/08 academic
year, the education system has returned to normal throughout the country. With the
redeployment of teachers and education administrators, enrollment rates are up nationwide at all
educational levels. In the health sector, a mission to assess requirements in the CNW zones
identified urgent needs which the Government has begun addressing. As a result, 2,417 new
health officials have now been redeployed, while approximately 1,700 additional health officials
were recruited in 2007 and 2008. The rehabilitation of health care facilities is in progress and
vaccination programs have resumed with help from multilateral agencies. The Government
cleared the supplier arrears of the public health pharmacy in late 2009 in order to re-establish full
access to necessary medicines.

2.5     Due to the severe constraints to budget allocation and execution in the social sectors
during the crisis, achievement of the MDGs by 2015 continues to be a major challenge. The
crisis had a considerable negative effect on the implementation of poverty targeted activities,
with security and defense spending taking priority, while demobilization, re-integration, and
election preparation have required significant resources in the "crisis-exit" period. As a result,
service quality remains well below pre-crisis levels. Problems in the education sector which
require urgent attention include: (a) incomplete re-deployment of qualified educational
personnel; (b) delay in resolving the question of volunteer teachers in the CNW zone; (c) lack of
transparency in teacher recruitment; and (d) the rising wage bill. Gender equality suffered during
the conflict with massive human rights violations and abuses, and while the situation has
improved, gender-based violence continues to be a problem. With gradual political and economic
recovery, the Government has increased social and pro-poor spending as a share of GDP.

                                           DEVELOPMENTS
                                   POLITICAL
                           A. RECENT

2.6      Under the Ouagadougou Political Accord of March 2007, important progress has
been made in re-instating the democratic process. In May 2009, the Council of Ministers
adopted a decree confirming the eligibility of all signatories of the Linas-Marcoussis Agreement
to stand in the upcoming elections, and setting 29 November 2009 as the date for the first round
of the presidential election. However, this date proved unrealistic given the difficulties in
finalizing the voter registration process and all parties agreed to a further postponement. A
voters' list was finally published in December 2009 and the process of handling disputes began.
However, in February 2010 an apparent case of fraud was detected in the Independent Electoral
Commission (IEC) and it was subsequently confirmed through an investigation by the state
prosecutor. Faced with the refusal of the IEC President to step down, President Gbagbo dissolved
the IEC and the Government. A new Commission and Cabinet were announced in the first week
of March and, in both cases, the former principle of inclusive, multi-party membership was
respected. Consequently, the electoral calendar has been further delayed. However, the
facilitator of the peace process, President Blaise CompoarC of Burkina Faso, has urged all parties
to aim for elections by early May 20 10.

2.7     It is important that the,Ivorian parties sustain the progress achieved so far and
complete, in a timely manner, the remaining stages of the peace process. The progress made
in the identification and voter registration operations resulted in the enrolment of more than 6.4
million Ivorians by the end of 2009. The events of February were worrisome but the parties
were able to resolve their differences and move forward. All parties need to rebuild trust and
complete the final stages of the electoral process, while the international community tries to
mobilize the remaining funds necessary to support this process' implementation.

2.8     Achievement of the reunification agenda remains a challenge. To date, some key
tasks set out in the Ouagadougou agreement and supplementary accords have not advanced as
expected. Activities of the national structures in charge of the disarmament and reintegration of
former combatants and militias remain stalled. Progress regarding the reunification of the fiscal
administration remains slow. Also, there has been limited progress in the crucial transfer of
authority from the Forces Nouvelles (FN) zone commanders to the prefectures, and in the
deployment of joint police and gendarmerie units which combine the Forces Nouvelles and
Ivorian defense and security forces to secure the electoral process. Reform of the security sector
is another key issue that needs to be addressed in order to ensure durable peace in CBte d'Ivoire.
On the other hand, the redeployment in the northern and western regions of the first group of
civil servants has been completed while the second wave is ongoing, and progress is being made
in the re-establishment of the judicial institutions.

                                    ECONOMIC
                            B. RECENT

2.9     The economy is now rebounding from a prolonged period of civil conflict and
economic hardship. Progress toward peace and reunification has been essential for domestic
consensus building and improved private sector confidence. Government's macroeconomic
performance since mid-2007 has been broadly satisfactory despite the adverse effects of the food
and oil price increases in 2008 and of the global economic slowdown in 2008 and 2009. Growth
picked up to 2.3 percent in 2008 from 1.6 percent in 2007, helped by high export prices. Output


                                                4
growth in 2009 is estimated to have strengthened to 3.7 percent due to strong cocoa and oil
prices. With food and fuel prices moderating, inflation is estimated to have fallen to -1.6 percent
by end-2009. The 2008 external current account balance (excluding official transfers) turned
into a surplus of 1 percent of GDP and is estimated at 2.1 percent in 2009 as a result of high
cocoa prices and oil exports.

2.10 However, the global economic slowdown and political uncertainty have delayed the
recovery in private investment and held down the expected rebound in GDP growth, which
remains significantly below what would normally be expected and what is required to reverse
poverty trends. Foreign direct investment has fallen from a projected level, pre-global crisis, of
US$480 million to an estimated US$400 million in 2009, and is expected to remain lower than

gasoline to the national market. For this and other reasons, fiscal revenues fell short of IMF
program targets by US$50 million in 2009.

2.12 Extra-budgetary spending on the transfer of the capital and other large public
works threatened the agreed macro framework in 2008. The authorities have since
implemented corrective measures including tax measures and expenditure cuts. The adopted ECF
includes a continuous performance criterion requiring zero extra-budgetary spending. The
Government agreed to limit spending on large public works in 2009 to one-half the level
incurred in 2008, in order to favor pro-poor spending consistent with the PRSP and HIPC debt
relief. Performance on these measures was considered to be on track after the first IMF ECF
review, and preliminary results from the second review are also encouraging. Pro-poor spending
has grown from 5.9 percent of GDP in 2007 and 6.9 percent in 2008 to 7.7 percent in 2009.
Much of this increase has taken place in the education sector whose share has grown from 3.6
percent of GDP to 4.9 percent over the three years.

2.13 The authorities have adopted and started implementing a domestic arrears
clearance plan for 2009-1 1 to bring down the stock from a level of 2.7 percent of GDP at end-
2008. During April-June 2009, most of the proceeds of the first tranche of the ECF (US$120
million) as well as IDA'S development policy operation (the Second Economic Governance and
Recovery Grant of US$l50 million equivalent) was used to settle domestic arrears even faster
than called for in the adopted plan. The modalities of the domestic arrears repayment were
discussed between the authorities and the Fund and IDA, with a view to ensure transparency.
These modalities included: prioritization first by size for the lowest claims then by maturity;
consultation with creditor suppliers; and publication of monthly lists of who will be paid. In the
first half of 2009, the stock of float plus domestic arrears was reduced by almost 60 percent. It is
estimated that the stock was reduced by 0.6 percent of GDP by end-2009, and will be lowered by
similar amounts in the next two years, so as to limit the stock to a reduced normal float.

                  c. MACROECONOMIC
                               OUTLOOK AND DEBTSUSTAINABILITY

2.14 The medium-term macroeconomic outlook is based on the assumption of a steady
post-conflict recovery. Real GDP growth is projected to fall to 3.0 percent in 2010, before
rebounding to average 4.8 percent over the period 201 1-14, as the global economic slowdown
ends, the political environment i.mproves, and productivity and investment return to their pre-
crisis growth path. Growth during the recovery phase would come from renewed private sector
confidence and investments, the return of capacity utilization in industry to normal levels,
normalization of the situation in the central, northern and western parts of the country, the
rehabilitation of public infrastructure, and expected reforms in the cocoa, energy and financial
sectors. Rehabilitation of public infrastructure (especially roads to the North) will be particularly
critical to help domestic and external trade and re-launch nontraditional exports and agricultural
processing industries. Restoration of capacity of the electricity sector will also be critical for the
country's economic recovery. Longer-term growth would be sustained by diversification of the
economy, increased investment, and expanded trade to regional and global markets. CPI
inflation is expected to remain at 2.5-3 percent during 2010-2014.

2.15 The trade balance is expected to remain positive but decrease and turn negative in
the next few years. Export and import volumes are projected to grow at similar rates but the
terms of trade are expected to deteriorate after 2009. Cocoa sales will likely decline in the
medium term due to aging plantations and declining prices. Oil exports will gradually expand
but so will imports, such that CGte d'Ivoire will remain only a small net exporter. With


                                                  8
significant outflows for service payments and private transfers, the current account balance is
therefore expected to turn negative.

2.16 Fiscal policies aim at further fiscal consolidation and prudent debt management
under the ECF-supported program for 2010-2011. The program targets primary basic
surpluses of 0.2 and 0.9 percent of GDP for 2010 and 2011, respectively. Total revenues
(excluding grants) are projected to remain in the range of 19.3 to 19.7 percent of GDP in 2010
and 201 1. The reduction in cocoa tax rates will require compensatory measures in the coming
years. A drop of 1 percent in cocoa taxes results in annual revenue losses of about US$15-20
million; however this has been more than offset in the last year by high world prices.
Expenditures would be held at 21.7 percent of GDP in 2010 before rising to 23.3 percent in
201 1. The authorities approved the 2010 budget in November 2009. Bank recapitalization and
electricity sector subsidies have been important components of past spending, but the authorities
have committed to avoiding the former and reducing the latter in the context of the IMF ECF,
and with technical support from IDA.

2.17 The 2010 budget emphasiz'es pro-poor spending together with post-conflict
expenditures (disarmament, demobilization and reintegration, redeployment of the
administration, elections and reunification). Pro-poor spending is planned to increase from 34
percent of the national budget in 2009 to 37.7 percent in 2010. Whereas total spending
(excluding debt service) will increase by only 6 percent, spending on health and education is
planned to rise by 10 percent and 13.4 percent, respectively. The smaller amounts allocated to
agriculture and water and sewage will increase more dramatically, by 50 percent and 100
percent, respectively. The government is having some difficulty containing the wage bill due to
additional hiring following reunification and pent-up demand for wage increases after many
years of a wage freeze. Wages and salaries, which had fallen somewhat to 40.6 percent of tax
revenues in 2009, are projected to grow again to 41.2 percent in 2010. However, an agreement
has been reached with public servants to postpone payment of half of their negotiated pay raises
until after the HIPC completion point. The authorities plan to gradually reduce the wage bill to
38.0 percent of tax revenues by 2014, thereby approaching the WAEMU target of 35 percent. A
census of civil servants and the implementation of an integrated payroll information system will
help control the future wage bill. Expenditures on large public works have been held to the same
level as in 2009 in absolute terms, thereby falling as a share of the total budget to 2 percent.

2.18 In the medium-term, the Government plans continued primary basic surpluses
above 1 percent of GDP, which would help it to remain current on debt service. Fiscal space for
pro-poor spending should come from a modest increase in the revenue/GDP ratio, containing the
wage bill in line with the WAEMU criterion, reducing sovereignty and other non-essential
spending, and an increase in concessional project financing. Total revenues (excluding grants)
are projected to reach 21 .O percent of GDP in 2015. Government expenditures are projected to
increase to 26.3 percent of GDP in 2015, due primarily to a recovery of public investment to 6.7
percent of GDP (from 3 percent in 2008). This will be funded fairly equally from domestic and
foreign sources. External grants, mainly for projects, are expected to average 1.2 percent of
GDP over the period.

cleared outstanding arrears with multilateral creditors or reached an agreement to clear them
during the program period. Possible HIPC Initiative debt relief is estimated at US$3 billion in

end-2007 NPV terms. C8te d'Ivoire has already received about 55 percent of its estimated HIPC
debt relief including pre-2002 excess debt relief provided by the Paris Club and London Club
creditors, and the concessional arrears clearance operations undertaken by IDA and the AfDB
Group. The May 2009 agreement with the Paris Club is expected to reduce debt service to
bilateral donors by 92 percent (US$4.3 billion) during the IMF program period. Upon reaching
the HIPC completion point, C8te d'Ivoire would qualify for relief under the Multilateral Debt

2.20 Nonetheless, C8te d'Ivoire is at a high risk of debt distress, according to the latest
Debt Sustainability Analysis conducted for the HIPC Decision Point document, and the
medium-term fiscal challenges remain large. External debt service is projected to rise in
2009-1 1 to an average of 3.6 percent of GDP, in order to stay current on outstanding obligations.
Support from multilateral agencies will help meet the financing gap but the authorities will need
to maintain a fiscal surplus and higher economic growth if they are to remain current on external
and domestic debt service. This challenge will remain in the years immediately following the
HIPC completion point as minimal repayments of rescheduled bilateral and commercial debt will
need to resume. In order to strengthen the authorities' capacity to manage their debt, a Debt
Management Performance Assessment was conducted by the Bank in June 2009. It found that
C8te d'Ivoire generally meets the normal requirements for debt management, and exceeds them
in some cases. However, several recommendations were made, including the preparation of a
proper debt strategy which includes a rigorous analysis of costs and risks.

2.21 The original 2009 financing gap amounted to 25 percent of GDP and was filled
mainly by debt restructuring. Interim assistance from multilateral institutions and the debt
relief granted by the Paris Club amount to 20.9 percent of GDP. The World Bank, AfDB, and the
IMF provided budget support of 2.3 percent of GDP in fresh inflows, while the remaining
financing needs are expected to be met by domestic/WAEMU borrowing. The liquidity situation
on the WAEMU market remained tight and the Government could not raise the expected
amounts in the first half of 2009. However, a special IMF SDR allocation has enabled the
Government to borrow from the BCEAO at more favorable rates than typically prevail in the
regional market.

2.22 In summary, C6te d'Ivoire's macroeconomic framework, which forms the basis for
its ECF support from the IMF, provides an adequate basis for the proposed operation. The
Government has generally demonstrated a broadly satisfactory track record in maintaining
prudent macroeconomic policies that are sustainable over the medium term. However, this
framework is subject to significant domestic risks (political and security) as well as external
shocks. Under a scenario of a worsened political and security situation, GDP growth would
decline to a modest 1-2 percent per year, driven mainly by oil production, with per capita
incomes falling. This could result from further delays in the election process, creating frustration
in parts of the population which feel disenfranchised, and a fall in investment, as the private

2.23 The international economic environment poses other significant risks. While the
strong cocoa and oil prices have helped mitigate the impact of the global crisis on the country's

vulnerable to terms of trade or other exogenous shocks. A drop in oil and cocoa prices would
reduce government revenue and the fiscal space for reconstruction spending. If cocoa and oil

with possible delays in moving to the HIPC completion point, these developments would raise
the risk of renewed arrears. Mitigating the above risks are CGte d'Ivoire's recent track record in

and support provided by donors, including through the proposed operation.

3.15 Sustained by growing demand for electricity and high oil prices, the energy sector
has experienced strong growth in recent years. C8te d'Ivoire is a modest oil exporter, while
relying on a combination of imported oil, domestic natural gas and hydropower to satisfy internal
energy demand. It has been one of the three largest providers of electricity in West Africa,
servicing Mali, Burkina Faso, Benin and Togo in the recent past. However, rising domestic
demand and a lack of investment in supply capacity is resulting in reduced exports, while the
declining share of hydropower as a source of energy has changed the cost structure of the sector.
An efficient and adequate provision of electricity will be critical for the country's own economic
growth and could again become a significant source of export revenues.

3.16 However, the electricity sector is threatened by years of minimal maintenance and
an investment freeze, which have resulted in growing technical losses due to overloaded
equipment and increasing outages. The lack of investment and maintenance during the crisis
period has given rise to the risk of major technical breakdowns and an inability to face rising
domestic power demand. As a consequence, C8te d'Ivoire began to suffer power shortages in
early 2010, despite cutting exports sharply. New generation projects will not be commissioned
before 2012/2013 due to difficulties faced by some private project developers in raising the
needed funds. Furthermore, the transmission and distribution system, which is the responsibility
of the state, also needs an extensive overhaul, but this is not likely to happen in the short term
because of the sector's financial deficit. Due to rising fuel costs, greater reliance on thermal
power, limited power tariff adjustments, technical losses, fraud and difficulties in billing in the


                                                17
areas formerly held by the Forces Nouvelles, the electricity sector has suffered significant
financial losses in 2007-09, amounting to CFAF63 billion in 2008 and an estimated CFAF47
billion (0.4 percent of GDP) in 2009.

3.17 Many of the sector's problems are related to major institutional changes
undertaken in the late 1990s. These resulted in the creation of three state bodies, Autorite'
Nationale de Re'gulation du Secteur de 1'Electricite' (ANARE) the regulator, Socie'te' de Gestion
du Patrimoine du Secteur de 1 'Electricite' (SOGEPE) which is intended to be the asset-holding
entity responsible for major investments, and Socie'te' d 'Ope'ration Ivoirienne d 'Electricite'
(SOPIE), which is responsible for project management and technical supervision of power
operations. These changes were introduced by decree but the 1985 Electricity Law has never
been amended. This institutional arrangement has proven to be unwieldy, costly and increasingly
ill-adapted to the real needs of the sector. SOGEPE did not legally own the assets in the sector,
which have continued to be held by Energie Electrique de la Cote d'Ivoire (EECI), and thus
could not use its balance sheet for borrowing purposes. The division of responsibility for
investment versus maintenance has been unclear, while there are overlapping areas of
responsibility between the three state bodies. ANARE has had a purely advisory role on tariff
matters and the Ministry of Energy, while retaining most decision-making authority, has lacked
adequate skills to provide proper leadership. Investment strategy and planning, as well as
oversight of the operator contract were under the collective responsibility of the Government and
the state bodies leading to an inefficient planning and decision-making process, which goes a
long way to explain the current insolvency of the sector and the inability to resolve the situation.

3.18 The sector is therefore structurally unable to generate the resources needed to
renew its aging asset base. Under the current institutional arrangements, responsibility for asset
renewal lies entirely with the Government, which lacks the necessary resources, while sector
entities are unable to mobilize funds through long-term borrowing because they are not the legal
owners of sector assets. Consequently, the sector has been depleting its capital stock over the
past 15 years and faces a looming crisis as equipment reaches the end of its operating life.

3.19 Gas prices increased dramatically in the past two years since they are directly
linked to international oil prices under existing supply contracts. These contracts do not
include any cap on the level of potential increases, and prices are based on the last 12 months. In
2009, the gas bill of the electricity sector is estimated at CFAF138 billion, or about 70 percent of
total electricity sales revenue, which is an unsustainably high proportion. Gas prices will remain
high in line with the rising trend of oil price forecasts. In order to cope with the situation, the
government foregoes its share of the gas revenues to which it would normally be entitled as a
part owner of the gas production operations, which is only partly compensated by arrears
accumulated by the state on its electricity bill. Such arrangements are clearly suboptimal and
need to be resolved quickly.

3.20 Progress has been made in increasing revenues. The electricity company plans to
regularize billing in the northern zones of the country, thereby earning an additional CFAF25
billion in 2009, though only 17 percent of this amount had been collected by end-August 2009.
After a five-year tariff freeze, prices were increased by 16-18 percent in mid-2008 for industrial
and commercial clients and by 10 percent in early 2009 for better-off residential customers
accounting for 60 percent of residential consumption. However, electricity prices remain below

Current issues

3.35 The cocoa sector, which provides the main source of income for one fifth of the
Ivorian population, has been saddled with excessive taxation and governance problems.
CBte d'Ivoire is the world's largest cocoa producer, with about 40 percent of total world
production; cocoa accounts for one quarter of its exports and roughly 17 percent of public
revenues. Approximately 700,000 small holder families (about 4 million people) depend on the
sector for their main source of income. However, inadequate producer incentives and poor
management of the sector's agencies have limited cocoa's contribution to rural growth and
poverty reduction. Between 2001 and 2007, it is estimated that a staggering CFAF3.2 trillion
(US$6.4 billion equivalent) were extracted from the sector as fiscal (US$5.0 billion) and quasi
fiscal levies (US$1.4 billion). In relative terms, overall taxation represented about 30-40 percent
of the CIF price leaving a small share - 35 to 40 percent of the CIF price - to farmers. It is
estimated that about half of the investments financed through these revenues have been used to
acquire companies unrelated to the sector and substantial amounts were also diverted to the
Government for security related expenditures. As a result, roughly 60 percent of cocoa growers
now live below the poverty line according to the PRSP, and these households accounted for



                                                23
some 28 percent of the poor nation-wide. The audits, and the reduction in taxes and levies,
supported by this operation aim to directly address these problems.

3.36 The cocoa sector has been remarkably resilient during the recent political crisis.
Despite mounting difficulties, cocoa has continued to provide badly needed incomes in the
Southern region of the country as well as critical revenues for the Government. Cocoa output
remained relatively stable throughout the crisis, fluctuating between 1.3 and 1.4 million tons, but
declined to 1.1 million tons in the most recent year with emerging signs of productivity erosion
from aging trees, spreading disease and farmer replanting with more remunerative alternative
crops. This productivity erosion is compounded by prevailing adverse conditions of recent
years: (i) insecurity/violence and the forced displacement of a significant portion of the labor
force due to land and ethnic disputes exacerbated during the conflict; (ii) the widespread
prevalence of rackets at roadblocks on the main transport routes; and (iii) the compression of
farm-gate prices due to the very high level of taxation, beginning to be addressed, which
undermines production incentives and drives Ivorian cocoa to be smuggled into Ghana and other
neighboring countries where prices are higher.

3.37 The negative impact of the current adverse institutional environment has so far
been mitigated by favorable prices on the international market, but productivity erosion
threatens future sector prospects. Cocoa prices surpassed US$3,000/tonne in June 2008 for the
first time in over 20 years, and returned to this level in November 2009. International prices are
projected to remain high over the medium term, only gradually declining thereafter as future
global supply is expected to increase more rapidly than demand. However, the longer term
prospects of the sector in CBte d'Ivoire are a source of growing concern. Yields are low (400-500
kgha), due to poor maintenance of plantations and the steady deterioration in the quality of
cocoa beans (due to problems in ferrjnentation, drying and storage at farm level), resulting in a
lower price on the world market. Also, plantations are rapidly ageing as plantinglreplanting
incentives are dwindling and competition from other crops, such as rubber, is increasing. An
estimated 50 percent of total plantations is more than 30 years old. It is thus urgent to address the
major constraints - agronomic, economic, institutional and social -- that threaten the sector's
future development.

                                      SECTOR SOUNDNESS AND TRANSPARENCY
             D. STRENGTHENING FINANCIAL
Current issues

3.45 The financial sector was hard hit by the crisis and is undergoing restructuring.
Banking activities in the CNW zone ceased after the crisis began. Institutions with heavy
exposure to small and medium-sized enterprises and weak sectors, such as transport, were the
hardest hit. Credit risk increased but after peaking at 26 percent in 2004, the share of
nonperforming loans to total credit fell to about 19 percent in 2008. In aggregate, the sector is
relatively well capitalized due to the strong performance of foreign private banks which account
for about two-thirds of total banking assets. However, government-owned and private domestic
banks are undercapitalized. The overall capital adequacy ratio has fallen from 12.2 percent in
2002 to 9.3 percent at end-2008 due to a rapid increase in lending and greater risk-taking. Since
March 2007, banks have begun to reengage in the Center, North and West of the country and are
gradually increasing their activities. A new Nigerian bank, United Bank of Africa, has started
operations in C8te d'Ivoire and a second one, Access, has acquired 75 percent of the capital of an
Ivorian bank. The entry of these new foreign banks, significantly more capitalized than the
Ivorian banks, will further improve the banking landscape. Overall, liquidity levels are adequate
and the banking sector is profitable.*

3.46 Key challenges in the sector are: (i) a mixed performance in the banking system, with
some large commercial banks in good financial standing, and other smaller ones not in
compliance with prudential standards; (ii) a microfinance sector in challenging conditions (net
worth is negative for the sector as a whole); (iii) a savings bank (CNCE) in a difficult financial
situation; (iv) the National Investment Bank (BNI), which depends for its liquidity on significant
payments from the State and needs to redefine its objectives; and (v) heavily overdrawn pension
schemes, with high deficits both on a cash and actuarial basis. In addition, the judicial system
does not contribute to the respect of contracts or debt recovery, and there are weaknesses in the
supervision of financial institutions.

3.47 The recapitalization of small commercial banks which had negative equity in June
2008 is moving more slowly than expected. Four of them submitted recapitalization plans to
the Banking Commission of the BCEAO but only two of these plans were approved in 2008.
These two banks implemented these plans and regained a position of positive net worth by
January 2009. A third bank has also attracted sufficient new capital to eliminate its deficit
situation, but a legal case launched by one of the shareholders has delayed approval of its
recapitalization plan by the Banking Commission. The two remaining banks have been
recapitalized by the state, on the grounds that their specialized mandates which focused on small
and medium enterprise and agriculture represented strategic priorities. One of these banks
(Versus) had its restructuring plan approved in December 2009. This intervention is temporary as

3.48 The microfinance sector is in crisis and requires urgent reform. The sector has been

technical and financial support, while the National Microfinance Commission has been unable to
properly supervise the sector and control its expansion. Given its dominant role, UNACOOPEC
represents a systemic risk to the sector and requires immediate attention.

3.49 The Caisse Nationale des Caisses d'Epargne (CNCE), the dominant savings
institution in the country, also demands immediate attention. The commercial banks are not
keen to expand their services to small towns and rural areas in light of the significant costs
involved and the limited volume of activities. CNCE, because of the size of its network (180
branches and service outlets across the country), could fill that gap. The CNCE inherited a
difficult financial position at the time that postal and financial activities were split, and has been
accumulating losses since then.

                                                LEARNED
                                       E. LESSONS
4.12 The design of this operation has benefited from the guidance provided by OPCS,
lessons from other development policy operations, and the specific lessons from the previous two
Economic Governance and Recovery Grants. These lessons are discussed in detail below:


                                               32
       It is important to be selective in the choice of policy and institutional reforms to
       undertake at this particular stage of the country's transition. This is particularly true in
       the case of a government of national unity where it may be more difficult to obtain a
       consensus across a wide range of issues. While the technical capacity of the Ivorian
       public sector is not weak, the political context remains fragile and requires a tightly
       focused agenda.
   0   Concentrating on high priority sectors which are key elements of the government's
       strategy will improve the degree of national ownership and the likelihood of developing a
       consensus. The sectors covered by this operation are key pillars of the economy as
       confirmed in the Poverty Reduction Strategy.
       Deepening reforms within the same sectors from one operation to another is preferable to
       changing the sector focus. Only gradual progress can be expected between any two
       operations, especially in a fragile, post-conflict situation, and the needed reforms
       typically require several steps over a medium-term time frame. Prior actions should be
       chosen which open the door to other important policy and institutional reforms.
       The effectiveness of Bank-supported development policy operations can be enhanced if
       complemented by other Bank-financed operations and the efforts of other donors and
       lending agencies. This operation benefits greatly from the parallel activities implemented
       under the Governance and Institutional Development Grant which focuses on most of the
       same sectors. Close coordination with the IMF-supported ECF and the HIPC completion
       point reform agenda also serve to maintain a consistent and focused dialogue with the
       authorities.

was recapitalized by the Government which then submitted its plan to the Banking Commission
as called for in the original prior action. That restructuring plan was approved in December 2009.
In a second case, the bank had a recapitalization plan ready and a new investor purchased shares
in the bank. However, the Banking Commission indicated that they had reservations about the
quality of the new investor, so the major shareholder had a change of heart and decided not to
release its shares even though the funds from the new investor had been transferred. Legal
proceedings were launched by the new investor and the case is now in court. It is therefore
impossible to submit a restructuring plan until the court case is resolved. In the third case, the
Government decided to recapitalize the bank in late 2008 because its focus on agricultural credit
was considered to be of strategic interest. However, the bank has continued to lose money and is
once more in a negative equity position. The Government has chosen to try to recover enough
non-performing loans to stabilize the bank and then attract private investors.

                                           OWNERSHIP
6.1      The proposed operation is supported and owned by the authorities, as indicated in
the Government's Letter of Development Policy (LDP). The specific reforms it supports are
critical elements of the Government's strategy to re-launch growth and enhance governance and
transparency in public sector resource management, and the energy, cocoa and the financial
sectors. There is widespread support for these reforms which were designed with the broad
involvement of the Government's relevant ministries and agencies under the championship of the
Minister of Economy and Finance. They also emerge as key elements in the recently-adopted
PRSP, which has provided an opportunity to foster national consensus across the Ivorian society
and among Government representatives on a medium-term reform agenda.

6.2     The economic governance reforms supported by this operation will only be
sustainable if ownership goes beyond the government to include the many beneficiaries and
private stakeholders affected by the changes. The increased transparency and accountability
that will accompany or result from many of the reforms are one aspect of improving
communications, understanding and ownership of reforms. The Bank has also supported an
iterative and open consultation of the reform program through its dialogue with stakeholders and
the authorities. Additional strategic communication activities are planned to further garner
broad-based input for the program and strengthen the sustainability of reforms.

6.3     Specifically, the Governance and Institutional Development Grant provides
technical assistance to help implement the reform program, including a component to
support a communications program. The project is being supplemented by a State and Peace-
building Fund (SPF) grant signed in November 2009 to increase communications around the
reform program. The broader objective of the grant is to help build the communications capacity
of government, media and civil society organizations (CSOs) in regard to promoting
understanding of the economic recovery and reform program. The grant is financing the
preparation and implementation of a government communications strategy, to build on activities
started with the TA operation (newsletters, web site). The SPF grant has three components: (1)
assisting government with a communications program around three themes: a) PFM, focusing on
the transparency of budget formulation and execution, trackinglreporting on pro-poor
expenditures, and revenues and expenditures from natural resources; (b) the petroleum sector,
including supporting implementation of EITI; and (c) the coffee/cocoa sector - monitoring
improved governance of the sector and producer revenues; (2) strengthening non-
government/CSO capacity to participate in and monitor economic governance reforms; and (3)
strengthening the capacity of the media to report on issues related to PFM, oil and gas,
coffee/cocoa, corruption, civic participation and the PRSP implementation. The Bank is carrying
out a governance diagnostic in early 2010, which is expected to strengthen the knowledge-base
on the quality of public services and perceptions of corruption. The findings of the diagnostic



                                              39
will be useful in determining what demand-side governance activities the Government and Bank
could support in the future.

6.4     Separately, the Country Office in Abidjan is implementing a communications and
outreach program with a broad array of stakeholders to further support a peaceful and smooth
political transition and economic recovery during and following the electoral period. These
efforts seek to raise awareness and understanding of the objectives of the reform program on the
part of public opinion leaders while also providing a platform for exchanging views on the
country's priorities. In addition, the Bank has prepared a communications strategy for its lending
and non-lending activities to strengthen broad-based understanding and support for its activities,
as well as to strengthen two-way channels of communication with stakeholders

                                               ASPECTS
                                    B. FIDUCIARY

6.5     Strengthening Government systems. The fiduciary standards in C8te d'Ivoire have
suffered from the crisis but remain strong relative to those in other WAEMU countries in some
respects, notably in the preparation of budget execution bills and in financial management
systems. Nevertheless, there is the need for further improvements in fiduciary standards. This is
a central objective of the public financial management component of the proposed operation and
the Government has made encouraging progress in implementing measures to enhance public
resource management and transparency. These early measures provide a platform for deeper
reforms in the context of the proposed operation and complementary ongoing and planned IDA
and other donors' interventions (see Section 4).

6.6     These measures include the full reinstatement and application of normal budget
preparation and execution procedures; regular publication of budget execution reports;
clearance of the backlog of outstanding draft budget execution bills to the Chamber of Accounts;
and implementation of public procurement reforms. Building on the PEMFAR findings and
recommendations, the authorities have recently adopted and are implementing an action plan to
further strengthen budget formulation, implementation, reporting, cash management and control.
These measures are accompanied by ongoing efforts to enhance transparency and efficiency ih
the energy sector, notably through the follow-up of the recommendations of the financial,
technical and operational audits of the energy sector (completed during 2008), including
financial relations between the Government and oil/gas producers and other agencies. The
authorities have already joined the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI). They
have also reduced quasi-fiscal levies and other taxes in the cocoa sector, increased the allocation
of levies to rural investment, and strengthened government oversight of such resources.

6.7     In 2005, the IMF staff carried out an on-site safeguards assessment of the Central
Bank of the West African States (BCEAO), the common central bank of the West African
Economic and Monetary Union, which includes C8te d'Ivoire. The assessment found that
progress had been made in strengthening the BCEAO's safeguards framework since 2002 when
the last safeguard assessment was undertaken. The BCEAO now publishes a full set of audited
financial statements and improvements have been made to move financial reporting closer to
International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). IMF staff noted that the BCEAO has
improved the explanatory notes to the financial statements and further changes are scheduled for
the next fiscal year, with a view toward a gradual alignment with International Accounting


                                                40
Standard, as adopted internationally by other central banks. Furthermore, an internal audit
charter has been put in place, mechanisms for improving risk management and risk prevention
have been established and follow-up on internal and external audit recommendations has been
strengthened. The assessment identified a number of areas where further steps would help
solidify the progress made in recent years. The main recommendations relate to: (i) improving
the external audit process by adopting a formal rotation policy and further enhancing the
transparency of the financial statements by adopting IFRS in full; and (ii) enhancing the
effectiveness of the internal audit function by further strengthening the reporting to management
of the BCEAO. The status report of the implementation of recommendations, received from the
Bank in March 2007, indicates that some progress was achieved.

                                 G. RISKS AND RISK MITIGATION

6.17 In the current environment of the country emerging from conflict, the proposed
operation presents significant country and fiduciary risks. The Bank's active engagement in
C8te d'Ivoire during this period of recovery is a high-risk, high-profile and potentially high-gain
enterprise. The greatest risk, however, is failing to support the peace and recovery process and
missing a window of opportunity for helping to stabilize a key country in an unstable region.
Thus, while there is no guarantee that conflict will not erupt again or that the process of inter-
Ivorian dialogue will not lead to a Government that has limited commitment to development, the
risks of inaction are much greater. The specific risks that could jeopardize the expected outcomes
and benefits of the proposed grant relate to political, governance, macroeconomic performance
and fiduciary matters.

6.18 Political risk. Possible political tensions related to the upcoming elections could lead to
delays in implementing the authorities' reform program and the reforms supported by the
proposed operation, jeopardize the attainment of program objectives, dampen donor support, and
provoke a resurgence of political instability. Specifically, resurgence of new rebel activity not
controlled by the current, unified government could undermine the peace and reengagement
process. Also, slow progress in addressing the most pressing social and economic problems
could lead to instability in volatile areas and adversely affect security conditions, the business
climate and macroeconomic management. The Government is pursuing efforts to improve
security. Notably, it is implementing a disarmament, demobilization ahd reintegration program
and "audiences foraines" to address the identification problem underlying the conflict. While
mitigation of the security risk falls outside of the direct scope of this operation, it is expected that
continued international support for the peace process, including through the ongoing World Bank
Post-Conflict Assistance Project (US$120 million), would help mitigate this risk. Elections are
planned for 2010. When they do take place, and regardless of the results, changes may
subsequently occur in some government institutions, in particular the Ministry of Economy and
Finance, Mining and Energy, and Agriculture, who are directly involved in the activities and the
reforms supported by the proposed grant. The fact that there is an inter-ministerial coordinating
committee, and that this committee has been involved in the design of the operation since its
inception, has facilitated the buy-in of key governmental entities into the objectives of the
supported reforms and will help ensure continuity and commitment.



                                                  44
6.19 Governance and commitment to reform risk. Vested interest and political intervention
in the cocoa and energy sectors remain strong and could stall the reforms supported by the
proposed operation. The coalition nature of the Government also means that it is difficult to
develop a consensus on some reforms. However, through the actions it has already implemented,
the Government has expressed its commitment to pursue and deepen the reforms launched.
There is now a broad-based constituency for substantive reform in the cocoa sector, including
external and domestic stakeholders. This has prompted Government to remove the leadership of
the four entities responsible for managing the sector and to launch a process of fundamental
reform. The proposed grant, as well as other IDA and donors' interventions, focus on advancing
and deepening reforms in the key sectors of the economy (PFM, energy, cocoa and financial
sector). Also, this risk can be mitigated by the continued intensive attention of the international
community to budget processes and frequent broad-based consultations with stakeholders,
including civil society organizations, to strengthen the case for reforms and Government's
commitment to them. In this respect, intensive communication efforts on the Government's
reforms and activities in the above areas will be supported by the Governance and Institutional
Development Grant to better inform the public and help build demand for governance and
transparency reforms. Trust funds have been obtained from the State- and Peace-Building Fund
to finance demand-side governance activities which will aim to strengthen the capacity of the
media to cover economic reforms and conduct investigative reporting. A Governance Diagnostic
Survey Initiative is also planned in FY 10 to provide government officials and other stakeholders
with a framework and tools to assess governance for policy purposes.

6.20 Macroeconomic risk. The country's macroeconomic performance represents another
important risk to the reforms supported by the proposed operation. Economic recovery will
depend critically on the Government's adherence to the medium-term macroeconomic
framework elaborated in collaboration with the IMF and the World Bank. In addition, while the
strong cocoa and oil prices have helped shield the economy from the global financial crisis, the
country's incipient recovery remains vulnerable to a slower than expected recovery from the
global crisis, and terms of trade or other exogenous shocks. A slower than expected world
recovery could adversely affect foreign direct investment and other capital flows necessary to
rebuild the economy. A drop in oil and cocoa prices would reduce government revenue and the
fiscal space for reconstruction spending. Together with possible delays in moving to the HIPC
Completion Point, these developments would raise the risk of renewed arrears. Mitigating the
above risks are C6te d'Ivoire's recent track record in macroeconomic management, the
Government's focus on growth enhancing structural reforms and support provided by donors
(including through the proposed operation), its commitment to a rapid attainment of the HIPC
completion point and to adjust the macroeconomic program to address possible exogenous
shocks. The IMF and IDA will continue to closely monitor the Government's macroeconomic
performance.

6.21 Fiduciary risk. The financial management assessment has concluded that fiduciary risks
are substantial mainly because of political instability and governance issues. This risk is
mitigated by the focus of the proposed grant on the improvement in fiduciary standards and
continued IDA assistance in public financial management and public administration which will
help to improve budget execution processes over time. In this respect, the PEMFAR has
identified areas in particular need of remedial actions and technical and financial assistance



                                                45
provided under the ongoing Governance and Institutional Development Grant is expected to
facilitate the amelioration of fiduciary standards.

6.22 In IDA's assessment, the potential benefits of the proposed operation outweigh the
residual risks and warrant IDA's assistance for implementing critical reforms and policy actions
in a coordinated fashion with other donors, while supporting risk mitigation actions to maximize
the sustainability of the reform agenda.

I.     Political and Economic Context
2.      Since 1999, CBte d'Ivoire has been experiencing a political crisis that led to armed
conflict in 2002 and the partitioning of the country. The socioeconomic impact has been
significant: real GDP per capita plummeted; basic social services deteriorated; particularly in the
northern part of the country, and over one million people were displaced. However, in recent
years, security in the country has gradually improved and economic growth has recovered
moderately, owing to the sound fundamentals of the economy. The agreement in March 2009 of
an economic and financial program supported by the Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility
(PRGF), and the achievement of the Decision Point for the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries


                                                 48
(HIPC) Initiative, reinforce the basis for this recovery. Uncertainties regarding public finances
stemmed from technical and administrative problems encountered in the Customs Department.
To avoid excessive pressure on the treasury, budget controls helped maintain the fiscal deficit at
1.6 percent of GDP, compared to the goal of 1.3 percent.
3.     The Ouagadougou Political Accord signed on March 4, 2007 by His Excellency,
President Laurent Gbagbo and Mr. Guillaume Kigbafori Soro, Secretary-General of the New
Forces and the current prime minister, and its supplementary accords, contributed to the gradual
normalization of the sociopolitical situation.
4.      The progress made in the crisis exit process, through the implementation of the
Ouagadougou Political Accords, is expected to lead to the organization of the first round of
presidential elections in 2010. The voter registration process covered 75.49 percent of the target
population, registering 6.5 million persons. Verification of the electoral list by crosschecking
data from the registration with historical files led to the preparation of the provisional list of
5,467,000 that included 1,033,000 persons who are currently the subject of a judicial appeal. The
appeals processing phase, which began in November 2009, was completed in January 2010.
However, parallel processing by the CEI (electoral commission) with respect to 429,000 persons
triggered a crisis that led to the dissolution of the CEI and the Government on February 12,2010.
The top priority of the newly formed Government and the newly installed CEI is the finalization
of the process and the staging of the first round of presidential elections as soon as possible. In
addition, a permanent solution has been found to the military issues that were hindering the
peace process. Against this backdrop and with a view to consolidating the peace, a joint force of
8,000 persons from the Integrated Command Center (CCI) comprising 4,000 members from the
Defense and Security Forces (FDS) and 4,000 members from the Armed Forces of the New
Forces (FAFN) has been redeployed to ensure the safe conduct of the elections. Demobilization
of ex-FAFN combatants is effectively underway.
5.       The Government reestablished economic and financial relations with all partners and
intends to benefit from their support to ensure a permanent exit from the sociopolitical crisis and
the resumption of economic activities. It has cleared all of its arrears to all multilateral
institutions, primarily the World Bank and the African Development Bank. It also adhered to the
debt servicing schedule in 2009.

11.    Progress Report on Reforms in 2009
6.      The 2009 program sought to step up the pace of reconstruction and growth, combat
poverty, and progress toward achievement of the MDGs in a reunified country and a climate of
sustainable peace. These objectives, which are in line with those of the PRSP, have served as a
benchmark for coordination of the program with civil society and development partners.
Implementation of the 2009 program helped (i) achieve real GDP growth of 3.8 percent; (ii)
reduce the annual inflation rate to 1 percent; and (iii) stabilize the overall budget deficit
(including grants; excluding reductions in external arrears) at 1.6 percent of GDP (compared to
the goal of 1.3 percent), despite the loss in customs revenue of CFAF 139.8 billion
(approximately 1.4 percent of GDP). In 2009, the Government undertook to adopt measures in
the following areas: public finance, the financial sector, the energy sector, the cocoa-coffee
sector, public administration, and the business environment.

                   B.     Governance, transDarency and structural reforms


Governance and transparency
35.     The Government has placed good governance and transparency at the center of its
economic program. It intends to implement its action plan for public financial management.
Measures focus on harmonizing tax and public financial management systems with WAEMU
directives, strengthening controls, and systematically publishing reports on budget execution and
the use of other State resources, including from the cocoa-coffee and energy sectors. The
Government also intends to adopt a national plan to combat corruption and illicit enrichment, as
well as a ministerial code of conduct.

36.     During 2010, the Government will focus on the implementation of structural measures
that have a direct impact on the economic environment and the transparency of public finances.
These measures should, in the long term, help lay the foundations for deeper and more far-
reaching structural reforms. The Government will pay particular attention to (i) building the
capacity and increasing the accountability of managers of financial and tax administrations and
the parastatal sector; (ii) strengthening controls; and (iii) improving communication (including
publication) of the results of public policies.

    Public financial management reforms

37.    The 2010 fiscal framework seeks to continue consolidating public finances while
addressing crisis exit needs, in a context of normalizing financial relations with external partners.
A primary basic surplus of roughly 0.2 percent of GDP to facilitate progress toward debt
sustainability and reduce domestic arrears is therefore expected for 2010. In addition, the 2010
budget will continue to include the quasi-fiscal revenues of the Rural Investment Fund from the
cocoa-coffee sector and all dividends from State-owned companies.

38.    Budget management and the accounting systems (SIGFIP and ASTER) will be fully used
to control and monitor budget execution and issue reports on budget execution at least once
every quarter. To achieve this objective, current budget execution procedures will be strictly
adhered to, and the use of treasury advances will be limited. The Government has adopted
measures to continue the training of credit administrators. Expenditure processing times will be

          Cocoa sector reforms

45.     The Government intends to continue its efforts to achieve the institutional reform of the

legal framework. The strategy will focus on improving the marketing and intermediation system
so as to ensure that the projected decline in cocoa-coffee taxation will largely benefit the farmers.
In this regard, the strategy will seek to strengthen the marketing capacity of farmers (through

into an ad valorem tax at a rate that does not exceed 20 percent of the CIF price.

Assessment Letter by IMF Staff for the World Bank
March 8,2010

Overall performance since early 2009 under the Extended Credit Facility (ECF) has been
broadly satisfactory, although structural reforms have been slow. The IMF is currently
reviewing with the authorities the 2009 program outcome and the economic program for
2010. The second review under the ECF arrangement i expected to be considered by the
                                                      s
IMF Board in the coming months.

1.      C6te d'Ivoire has achieved significant progress in restoring macroeconomic
stability, strengthening growth, and normalizing relations with the international
financial community. In 2009, per capita income increased for the first time since 1998.
Economic activity was little affected by the global financial crisis and benefitted from high
world cocoa and oil prices, as well as from an abundant harvest and strong oil extraction.
After rising in 2008, inflation declined during 2009, led by food, energy and transport prices.
Fiscal policy has been restrained despite large spending needs to overcome the internal
conflict and prepare elections, enhance investment, and clear large external debt service
arrears. The external current account surplus and imputed reserves at the BCEAO increased.

2.       The IMF has supported the authorities' economic policy and reform program
since March 2009 with a three-year arrangement under the ECF (formerly Poverty Reduction
and Growth Facility, PRGF). Reforms aim to achieve macroeconomic stability, remove
impediments to growth, particularly in the cocoa and energy sectors, improve the business
climate, create fiscal space for pro-growth and pro-poor expenditures, strengthen public
financial management, bolster the financial system, and restore fiscal and external
sustainability. In November 2009 the IMF Executive Board approved the completion of the
first review under the ECF arrangement.

3.     On April 1, 2009, the Executive Boards of the IMF and the World Bank
approved C6te d'Ivoire's decision point under the enhanced HIPC Initiative. Both the
Fund and World Bank are providing interim assistance. The granting of final debt relief is
conditional on triggers to reach the completion point under the Initiative.

4.     Performance under the program during 2009 was broadly satisfactory.
Preliminary data indicate that the program's fiscal targets for the year as a whole were
broadly met, despite unexpected shortfalls in customs revenue and the need for increased
support to the electricity sector. Progress in the implementation of structural reforms has
continued to be slow in the pre-election environment.

5.       Fund staff is currently assisting the authorities in reviewing the design of their
economic program for 2010. An IMF staff mission is currently in the field. The goal of the
mission is to review program implementation through December 2009 and agree on policies
to achieve the 2010 program objectives established at the time of the first review and update
the structural reforrn agenda.



                                             82
    .6.      C8te d'Ivoire's public external debt remains unsustainable notwithstanding
     good progress on debt restructuring. In addition to the debt relief provided by Paris Club
     creditors in May 2009 and the financial support provided by multilaterals and bilaterals,
     further relief is needed for CGte d'Ivoire to achieve a sustainable external debt position. A
     debt exchange with Brady bond holders is expected to be launched in March 2010 and would
    provide significant debt and debt service relief. At the same time, the authorities are
     committed to reaching debt-restructuring agreements with other private commercial creditors
     and official non-Paris Club bilateral creditors, and discussions are ongoing on terms
     consistent with the Paris Club's comparability and HIPC Initiative requirements.

    7.     The second review under the ECF arrangement is expected to be considered by
    the IMF Board in the coming months. Board consideration would be based on CGte
    d'Ivoire having satisfactorily performed under the ECF-supported program and agreement
    with the authorities on forward-looking policies and reforms consistent with the medium-
    term objectives. Completion of the review will also be subject to CGte d'Ivoire obtaining
    financing assurances for the program.

    8.      There are risks related to program implementation. Success in conducting open
    and democratic elections and achieving political stability and reunification would provide a
.   firm platform for the implementation of the authorities' economic and structural reform
    objectives. It would also bolster private sector confidence and economic activity. At the same
    time, there is a risk of spending overruns related to the elections and other crisis-exit
    programs, which could lead to curtailing pro-poor spending and to domestic arrears. The
    domestic consensus to pursue structural reforms needs to be strengthened so as to ensure that
    reforms proceed apace in public financial management and key sectors (coffee/cacao, energy,
    banking, and the judiciary). Also, a drop in output and/or world prices of cocoa and oil could
    worsen the growth outlook and lower government revenue. On the upside, further
    international support could accelerate the recovery and boost reforms.
