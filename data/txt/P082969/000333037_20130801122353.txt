1.      Project Context, Development Objectives and Design

1.1     Context at Appraisal

1.     The Gambia is a small country with an area of 11 300 km2 situated on the
Atlantic Coast of West Africa. Its population is estimated at about 1.7 million for 2009,
making The Gambia one of Africa’s most densely populated countries (150 persons per
km2). Economic growth was stable with an average real GDP growth of 6.2% between
2003 and 2006, after a brief contraction in 2002 due to low rainfall and poor
macroeconomic management. Over 70% of the labor force works in the informal sector,
mostly in low productivity smallholder agriculture which contributed 30% to GDP in
2005. In 2007, GDP growth rate was 7%. Despite this achievement, poverty was
pervasive and worse in rural areas. Two thirds of Gambians live below the poverty line
while almost 40% of the households are food poor.

2.       At appraisal, the Government’s Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP) II was
just approved. Although some modest achievement were recorded during implementation
of PRSP I, poverty and hunger continued to be widespread and was aggravated by poor
health, especially in rural areas. For example, in 2005, 83% of the population lived on
less than US$ 2 a day, and 60% had less than one dollar a day. The probability at birth of
not reaching the age of 40 was 21% compared to 17% in nearby Senegal. Concerned
with low outcomes and widening disparity in access to basic social and economic
activities, the PRSP II was designed to build on PRSP I and defined the Government’s
strategies for reducing disparities in access to sources of income and empowerment,
while emphasizing on decentralizing the public and directly intervening at the community
level sector within the framework of Local Government Act of 2002.

3.      In order to address the multiple determinants of poverty in rural areas, and in
consideration of the limited size of country, the government requested that the Bank
support a multi-sectoral project based on the community-driven development approach.
By supporting communities and local governments, the Community Driven Development
(CDD) Project was envisaged to be a single mechanism for working across all sectors.
Doing so would allow the project to efficiently tackle some of the priority issues affecting
rural areas, particularly related to past weaknesses associated with a centralized, supply-
driven approach to poverty reduction. This CDD approach would transfer the requisite
skills and resource to decentralized administrative structures in order to effectively
address poverty issues on the ground.

4.     The Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) supported three of the five pillars1 around
which the objectives of PRSP were built. The CDD project aimed to contribute to the

1.2        Original Project Development Objectives (PDO) and Key Indicators

5.       The Project’s development objective was to support benefiting rural communities
in planning, implementing and maintaining their priority social and economic
investments, in partnership with Local Government Authorities. The project was
designed as a Specific Investment Loan (SIL) 2 , co-financed by a PHRD grant 3 , and
implemented over a 5-year period with project interventions phased at village and ward
levels 4 . The phasing of the implementation was designed to allow for continuous
improvement in key facilitation over the course of the project.

6.         The following Key PDO indicators were agreed:

      i.   At least 70 percent of Community Subprojects’ results are achieved;
     ii.   Satisfaction with the results of the Community Subprojects increases by 10
           percent each year in sample targeted rural population up to 90 percent by Year 5;
           and
    iii.   At least 70 percent of Community Subprojects are fully functional two years after
           their completion.

1.4    Main Beneficiaries

8.       The primary beneficiaries of the project were the rural population living in the
600 villages and 88 wards in the six rural Local Government Areas (LGAs) covered by
the project (estimated at about 435,000 people, representing about 50 percent of the rural
population). Given that strengthened capacity of the decentralized administrative
structures including that of the community organizations (e.g. Kafos) and representatives
matter for promoting participatory community development , capacity building for the
Local Government Authority(e.g. councilors)5, and government Ministries, Department
and Agencies(MDAs) responsible for local development were undertaken. This capacity
building program targeted internal organization and procedures of these government
institutions as an important step in building a functional and well administered local
council.

9.      Since participatory development is not possible without community engagement,
Civil Society Organizations (CSOs) and Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs)
capacity to mobilize communities to engage in deliberative decision making, to demand
better service and to hold local government more socially accountable was also a key
capacity building activity for the project.

10.     The multi-sectoral nature of the project interventions across a range of sectors
saddled the project with important coordination role at different levels of the
decentralized administrative structures including national levels, and expanded the
project beneficiaries to include central government agencies responsible for health,
agriculture, education, water and sanitation, and transport sector, as well as the National
Nutrition Agency, National Environment Agency and the Gambia Public Procurement
Agency.

1.5    Original Components

11.   The project comprised three components: (A) Community Development Facility;
(B) Capacity strengthening for community development; and (C) Project coordination,
monitoring and evaluation.

                                             3
Component A: Community Development Facility (CDF)-US$10.8 million (of which
IDA US$9.2 million and PHRD US$0.5million)

12.     This component aimed to provide grant resources to communities for financing
priority, demand driven village and ward levels sub projects (social and economic
infrastructure, including capacity building and matching grant for income generating
activities) identified through participatory processes. The component financed a mix of
productive and social subprojects (with details provided in Annex 2).

13.     To foster local governance at the local level, and build an educated and aware
citizenry as a prerequisite for local accountability within the decentralization framework,
about five percent of the CDF was earmarked for a Good Governance Facility (GGF).
The GGF financed activities aimed to strengthened social accountability and transparency
in the use of project funds.

Component B: Strengthening Capacity for community Development-US$6.3 million
(of which IDA US$ 2.1 million and PHRD US$ 3.35million)

14.       This component was geared towards            building the technical, fiduciary,
organizational and institutional capacity to plan, prioritize, implement and monitor local
development activities envisaged under the project, including the preparation and
implementation of Strategic Development Plan (SDP), the organization of the rural
population into socially cohesive rural communities/councils and implementation of
decentralization processes. In particular, the component aimed: (i) to develop the capacity
of villages and wards to undertake participatory planning and resource allocation based
on a simple, results-oriented approach grounded in the principles of community
empowerment, transparency, and ownership; (ii) to strengthen the capacity of de-
concentrated staff to facilitate community-level development planning and investment;
(iii) to strengthen capacities of Local Government Authorities to effectively monitor local
development plans and to manage and account for funds provided to villages and wards
for investment activities; (iv) to provide selected support for capacity building for key
sectoral government agencies, geared toward strengthening sectoral capacities to support
community level social and economic activities; (v) to support implementation of the
project’s public outreach campaign; and support strengthening of environmental
management capacity.

Component C: Project Coordination, Monitoring and Evaluation -US$1.65 million
(of which IDA US$ 0.4 million and PHRD US$ 0.95 million)

15.     This component sought to ensure sound and efficient management of the project
including the overall coordination of project activities, monitoring and evaluation of
project results for decision making and impact evaluation and fiduciary matter (flow of
funds). Some of the envisaged results included: (i) setting up of an MIS and M&E system
for the project; (ii) hosting of project website; and (iii) establishment of project database

1.7      Other significant changes

17.    The only important change that occurred during the implementation of the project

2.      Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes

2.1     Project Preparation, Design and Quality at Entry

18.      The project preparation was broadly participatory and involved extensive
stakeholders’ consultations to generate the local commitment and sense of ownership that
are critical for implementation success. Its design benefitted from strong analytical
underpinnings6 and government commitments to policy reform. The project had a two
year preparation period (2005-2007) up to Board approval, and replaced two operations
initially foreseen in the FY 2003-2005 CAS: a Community-Based Rural Development
Project, and a follow up operation to the Participatory, Health, Nutrition and Population
Project. The project design was adequate for laying the foundation for achievement of the
Government of the Gambia (GoTG)’s long-term development goals of poverty reduction,
and building the capacity of relevant actors/institutions in the implementation of the
decentralization and poverty reduction agenda, while addressing key focus of the CAS.
The project design equally paid appropriate attention to targeting of the poor and the
vulnerable that are often geographically isolated and easily excluded from participatory

2.2     Implementation

25.     The project was implemented as designed and represents the first Government
effort to roll out a major national reform on decentralization. This implementation period
of the CDDP project benefitted from major reforms in the area of budget support
operations and civil service reforms (2008-2012). This budget support also known as
Development Policy Lending (DPL) supported policy and institutional reforms in
macroeconomic and public finance management. The reforms in the public financial
management which is essential for laying the foundation for fiscal decentralization
included updating the legislative framework for budget management and public
procurement, the roll out of the Integrated Financial Management Information
System(IFMIS), which was completed in January 2011 and the formulation of the
“Public Financial Management Reforms Comprehensive Strategy (2010-2014).
Government commitment to the implementation of the project was also demonstrated
through increases in the network of CDO and CDAs9. Progress in these overall legal,
fiscal and administrative reforms provided the right policy and regulatory environments
for decentralization and drove much of the project's own actions that redefined central-
local authorities’ relationships towards creating an efficient public service delivery at the

2.4    Safeguard and Fiduciary Compliance

36.    Safeguards. The project was assessed as a category ‘B’ project, and triggered
three safeguard policies including (i) Environmental Assessment (OP 4.01), Pest
Management (OP 4.09) and Involuntary Resettlement (OP 4.12). The appropriate
safeguards instruments namely ESMF with PMP and RPF were prepared to address any
negative environment and social impacts and disclosed in The Gambia and in the
InfoShop.

37.     All subprojects identified for funding passed through mandatory screening using
agreed safeguard checklist and forms to ensure compliance with Bank social and
environmental safeguard policies, and environmental focal person (EPA) identified at the
local within the MTDF. However, the first batch of subprojects that were implemented
without complete screening were subjected to post review and audited on environmental
issues. These subprojects were within the predicted parameters in the ESMF that
subprojects would have minimal easily remediable impacts. Lack of sufficient capacity of
the environmental focal persons at the local level was a responsible factor.

38.     Initial problem with the management of biomedical waste at the community level,
lack of insertion of environmental guidelines for contactors and increased use of
pesticides for horticulture were experienced. Adequate capacity building for EFP at the
MTDF level, use of the National Biomedical waste management plan, and PMP helped
addressing the problems.

39.     The project commissioned a study to screen subprojects in order to determine
whether or not specific sub projects need to undergo a separate EIA. Findings from the
study confirmed recommendations in the ESMF. The collaboration between the CDDP
and UTG was particularly useful in training the MDFTs and EFP on basic EIA process
and procedures to enable them to properly screen CDDP village sub-projects. Similarly,
partnership with the National Environmental Agency (NEA) for the monitoring of the
implementation of mitigation measures ensured compliance. Despite this initial
difficulties to fully implement the safeguard policies due to lack of capacity, overall, the
project management of safeguard issues was good and compliance with environmental
and social safeguard management plans was rated moderately satisfactory.

2.5     Post-completion Operation/Next Phase

42.      CDDP has no doubt promoted the two most important dimensions of people’s
oriented development that is critical to promoting sustainability. They are the vertical
(decentralization) and horizontal (participation) dimensions. The mainstreaming of the
design of the project in the Local Government Act of 2002 and its implementation
through line ministries and decentralized structures at the central and provincial levels
have enhanced capacities related to technical, fiduciary, social, institutional aspect of
localizing development. Similarly, the participatory planning adopted in the preparation
of SDP has tremendously capacitated local level institutions including elected community
representatives (VDCs and WDCs) in the management of development at the local level
and increased the likelihood of internalizing community needs, perspectives and
ownership. The functionality of the community representatives and level of livelihood
improvement are plausible indicators that can be used by the Bank to monitor
institutional strengthening and economic activities that can be attributed to the project
capacity building programs and investment in the project intervention areas.

43.    In fact, there is evidence of wide acceptance of community driven development
approach with a number of the Local Council authorities integrating it into their programs
and funding it from their statutory allocation. Other donors have also strengthened their
support for decentralization and designed program using the CDDP approach. An
example is the Community-Based Infrastructure and Livelihood Improvement Project
(CILIP), a US$18.02 million operation sponsored by Islamic Development Bank (IDB).

support to priority subprojects across many sectors contributed to addressing the
multidimensional determinant of poverty by reducing disparity (to improved coverage) in
access to social and economic infrastructure needed to engage in economic activities. The
pro-poor impact of these productive and social investments of the project came from

(b) Institutional Change/Strengthening

58.      CDDP has contributed substantially to longer term capacity and institutional
development of the decentralized structures/architecture through its capacity
strengthening program. Its contribution to legal, fiscal and administrative dimensions of
decentralization is invaluable. Undoubtedly, CDDP has strengthened all the decentralized
institutions providing a strong basis for the successful implementation of the
decentralization framework whose orientation is poverty reduction. The project helped
the government to operationalize decentralization. Through CDDP, LGAs were given
greater role in strategic planning, oversight over the use of funds and implementation of
development programs. The success recorded by CDDP in this area is connected to the
other initiatives by AfDB and World Bank such as the budget support operations and the
institutional support projects which contributed towards macroeconomic and fiscal
stability. The reforms in the legislative framework for budget management and public
procurement and the rolling out of the Integrated Financial Management Information
System (IFMIS) in January 2011 have had positive impact on the decentralization agenda
by completing the LGA Act 2004 and Finance Audit of 2004.

59.      In particular, CDDP promoted a paradigm shift that changed the orientation of
project implementation from top-down approach to a bottom up which aligns with the
GoTG decentralization agenda. In addition, CDDP has led to a dramatic change in the
relationship between communities and local authorities. During the ICR mission, a local
government chairman testified that the project has created a challenge for them;
communities are now more aware and demanding better services thereby putting them on
the spot. The capacity of administrative decentralized structures and local level
institutions such as kafos, VDCs, WDCs were strengthened to initiate their own local

(c) Other Unintended Outcomes and Impacts (positive or negative)

60.    The project has had positive impact on time allocation for both men and women.
Before project intervention, women spent disproportionate time in pressing/processing
millet which affects how much time is available for other domestic activities. Improved
access of women to farm power/processing machineries freed more time for domestic as
well as economic activities. During the ICR, a member of one of the beneficiaries’
communities confirmed that level of intra-households conflicts over use of time has gone
down and attributed to project intervention in labor saving technologies. This has
implications on social development.

61.      In addition, the project recorded second order impacts. The project successfully
mobilized savings, promoted a strong saving culture among the Kafos/group and above
all, relaxed liquidity constraint. This positive outcome was also noted in a study31 which
identified CDDP as a microfinance intermediary funder in the Gambia.

62.    The results of CDDP after five years of operation have surpassed expectations.
Based on this positive outcome and the impact of the project in delivering results, a new
IDB supported project, CILIP was modeled after CDDP, and its preparation benefitted
from skill transfer from CDDP PIU. Furthermore, the spillover effect of the project is
unquantifiable; equipment procured as a result of the project benefitted non-participating
communities and so did infrastructure such as the health facilities which served
neighboring villages.

4.     Assessment of Risk to Development Outcome

Rating: Low

67.     The overall risk to development outcome is Low. At appraisal, slow
implementation of the decentralization process, weak technical and fiduciary capacity at
all institutional level (DoSGL, MDTFs, LGAs, wards, villages) and politicization of
project activities were identified as substantial risk to development outcomes. The project
support to provisions of the Local Government Act and other donor strengthened support
to the decentralization contributed to building political will for the decentralization
framework. The government maintained its commitment to promoting decentralization by
passing and implementing a number of complementary policy reforms. As discussed in
Section 2.5, CDDP built technical and fiduciary capacities at all levels thereby being
instrumental to empower community organizations and LGAs in a manner that it difficult
to see being reversed. In particular, M&E at the LGA focused on fiduciary aspect of the
project to mitigate fiduciary risk. Throughout project implementation, all technical and
financial audits carried out were unqualified. As noted in earlier section, the project
participatory approach created substantial social capital in the beneficiary villages,
mainstreamed gender and social equity in the local development agenda and developed a



                                            22
strong constituency and local ownership. This coupled with the involvement of the NGOs
and traditional structures in project implementation promoted good governance and social
accountability and were sufficient deterrent against politicization of project activities.
68.    Risk related to sustainability of the subproject is low. The subprojects supported
under the project are viable in the medium term because of positive ERR which was
estimated at 23 percent opportunity cost of capital and about 4 times higher than what
was predicted ex-ante. The acquisition of additional equipment by communities from
own group savings suggest that the O&M arrangement is functional. Further, the
introduction of user fees rules out the absence of fund for operation and maintenance of
equipment acquired through the project.
69.     Further, there is also low environmental risk to development outcomes. The
Project was classified as a B Category, implying that potential environmental and social
impacts will be less adverse, easily remediable and site-specific. Following the initial
steep learning curve, the project implemented mitigation measures that ensured
continuous satisfactory safeguard performance. However, there is need to develop further
the agricultural production system so that it is resilient to climate and weather shocks.
70. Finally, the risk that decentralization will not always achieve the goal of making
local governments more responsive and accountable is moderate. The moderate risk is
related to visible results of the good governance subcomponent of the project manifested
in the growing demand to making development planning accountable. However,
government continued commitment to policy and administrative reforms would help to
maintain the momentum.

6. Lessons Learned

Investment in capacity building at all levels is essential for localizing development and
promoting local governance.

79.    Capacity building is essential to translate the notion of decentralization into
operational reality. CDD involves transfer of fiduciary, technical and management
responsibilities to communities. Without adequate capacity building on procurement and
FM, management of funds would be impossible and could be fraught with fiduciary risk
such as mismanagement of funds. Also, capacity building on community based
procurement methods promoted transparency, and accountability and raised level of
awareness of communities on their rights building communities that demands for social
accountability.

Commitment to reforms and strong political will to decentralization is fundamental to
projects that seek to support its implementation.

80.  CDD project is not complete without a legal framework to fiscal decentralization.
The CDD project could not have succeeded if the local government authorities and
community representatives are not empowered by law to management development.

Realism and candor is essential to avoid creating implementation bottlenecks

81.      The plan to have one M&E specialist at the central level to monitor project
activities in 600 villages and wards was overly ambitious. Even though the country is
considered small, navigating through these numbers of villages and wards without
support staff could be problematic.




                                           26
Full dedication of government staff to project is required to reduce risk of absenteeism

82.     Admittedly, mainstreaming project implementation through government
structures is important for skills transfer and long term capacity development agenda.
However, maximum benefit cannot be realized without full dedication of government
staff and accountability to the PCT. It is important that there is agreement upfront that
project activities would be part of the normal work schedule of government staff,
otherwise, whenever assignments from line ministries clash with critical project activities
there is a risk that attention to project activities only takes second priority.


Transfer of responsibilities requires strong oversight by decentralized structures.

83.    It is not enough to just transfer management of resources to beneficiaries, strong
oversight is needed to guard against elite capture, political hijacking or deviation from the
bottom up approach.

Limited geographical scope and selectivity is required for greater impact.

84.     The project coverage was ambitious, even though this was meant to extend the
coverage of the benefit to many communities as possible, may result in lower per capita
benefit. Also, there are many unmet needs in the approved SDPs which could have led to
greater impact in case the project would have covered less villages and wards. The way it
is, impacts were spread rather thinly.

Component A: Community Development Facility (CDF)

2.      This is a key component of the project with 85 % of IDA allocation, 4% of PHRD
and 10% beneficiaries’ contribution totaling US$9.2 million. The component has two
subcomponents: (i) CDF that funds village (75%) and ward (25%) level social and
economic subprojects; and (ii) Good Governance Facility (5% of CDF) which financed
CSOs activities to strengthen social accountability and transparency. Six indicators were
specified for this component, including: (i) percent of annually budgeted IDA funds for
component A disbursed (target 100); (ii) Average replenishment time at LGA, WDC and
VDC level reduced (Target 20 %) of 14 days (11 days); (iii) 80 % of surveyed citizens in
villages who know the amount of funds disbursed; (iv) 60 % of surveyed citizens in
Wards who know the amount of funds disbursed; (v) percent of funds utilized in
accordance with PIM Financial and procurement guidelines; and (vi) 100 % of
subprojects screened for Environmental Impact.

partially. For the first indicator, the target was met and surpassed. In all, a total of 1428
subprojects over the target of 1098 were financed with a total cost of GMD
243,302,664.20 (US$ 9,357,794.78), over the allocated amount of GMD 240,995,069.90

2.     A financial and economic analysis of the CDDP was done both at project
appraisal (PAD) and at completion (ICR). However, only result of economic analysis was
presented in PAD. The aim was to determine whether the community driven development
subprojects financed under the project were financially and economically profitable and

3.      In addition to secondary data collected by reviewing project documents and data
set which helped to identify the needed additional information, primary data that was
collected through the design and administration of questionnaire to capture the
subprojects’ information on community investment contributions, financial revenues
generated and the various relevant dimensions of the investment was also used. There are
about 143 randomly selected sample sub-project activities used for the analysis. Sample
from the agriculture subproject investments covered; farm implements and equipment,
processing equipment, production inputs, draught animals, vegetable gardening, animal
fattening and farm produce such as rice and millet. Data on fishing covered out-board

4.     The estimation of the economic benefit was based on the financial cash inflows
(revenue) for subprojects for the project period 2008 to 2011. Each sample sub-project
was analyzed by quantifying the incremental benefits and costs, which are discounted at
20 % opportunity cost of capital with 10 years as the average project life. Each subproject
was assumed to generate a net stream of incremental benefits over 10 year (2008-2017).

5.      The survey had income information for 2008. Income for subsequent years was
projected on the basis of an average annual growth rate of 2.5%. Annual recurrent
maintenance cost of the rehabilitated infrastructure is kept at 5%. Both income and
Operating expenses are expected to grow by 2.5% annually in line with the average
annual rate of inflation in the economy. A standard conversion factor of 0.8 was used to
convert financial costs to economic costs. An exchange rate of 1 US$ is equivalent to
Gambian Dalasi GMD 30 was used.
6.      The results of the analysis show that subprojects have positive NPV and overall
ex-post ERR of 23 percent. Although, the ERR is not too high, it is within the range for
demand driven type of investment. This overall economic rate of return of 23 percent is
higher than the assumed 20 percent cost of capital, so the subprojects are economically
viable. From the sectoral distribution of subprojects in table 3.1, it was clear that there
were preference for the most profitable activities. This coupled with the training provided
by the project with respect to the elaboration and implementation of subproject activities
may explain why the ERR is positive.

Cost-effectiveness Analysis for Social investment

10.     An overall cost effectiveness analysis was done instead of cost effectiveness in
incremental terms. This is due to lack of data. Even the comparative data used for the
analysis is indicative, so results should be interpreted with caution. That said, economic
benefits of social infrastructure is enormous and investment in a country like the Gambia
where there is access disparity is justified on equity and welfare grounds. For example,
provision of adequate and safe water within distance from homes will free up time for
girls and women and result in increased attendance in schools and women will use their
time for income generating activities. Also, access to portable water will result in

Annex 5. Beneficiary Survey Results

1.      A beneficiary assessment was conducted in January 2013 during the ICR mission.
The objective of the consultation was to obtain beneficiary perceptions of different
stakeholder group on the impact of as well as their satisfaction with the activities of the
project. While the findings are qualitative, it represents stakeholders’ views about project
impact. The assessment took place in all the six rural LGAs where the project intervened
in the form of Focus Group Discussion and interaction with opinion leaders, authorities at
the local level, NGOs and policy implementers.

2.       The main conclusions from this assessment are as follows:

        The project is about the most popular project in the Gambia even in places where
         it is not currently intervening because of good results it has generated.
        The project was relevant to the context and issues in communities and greatly
         help GoTG in implementing its decentralization agenda.
        Beneficiaries acknowledged the fact that CDDP is the first project, that
         empowered people technically and financially to take responsibility of their
         development with adequate capacity building and significant oversight.
        The project showcased the usefulness of the decentralized structures at the local
         level and provided opportunity for collection action.
        Participatory approach is novel and promoted inclusive community development.

3.      Beneficiaries perceived the project has contributing to improving their standard of
living and connecting them to more economic opportunities. One interesting thing across
the different stakeholders is that there was convergence of views on the positive impact
of the project. Specific reference was made to contribution of the project to livelihood at
the instance of shocks experienced in 2011 and 2012. Despite the drought expected in
2011 and the flood in 2012 which prevented tractor from working on the farms, the
beneficiaries of the projects that had power tillers were able to work and had a good
harvest. Equally, the community benefiting from animal fattening subprojects had good
business.

4.      Through project support, VDCs, WDCs, PEC, TAC, MDFTs have become very
vibrant bringing everybody together around development opportunities of the village and
wards. The project has improved the capacity of decentralized institutions including
legislative arms of the local government to plan and exercise oversight over development
issues.

5.     Beneficiaries liked very much the targeting of women. This category of
beneficiaries felt that they benefitted more from the social infrastructure such as water,
and health than their male counterpart.

6.       All the stakeholders wanted a second phase for the project.

Annex 7. Summary of Borrower's ICR and/or Comments on Draft ICR

Apart from issues leading to the Economic Recovery Programme (ERP) in the 1980s and
shortly after the military coup in 1994, when most donor partners on whom the country
depended for aid to sustain and perpetuate economic growth temporarily suspended
disbursement to pressure her into returning to democratic leadership, The Gambia has
had a good history of macroeconomic management that ensured stability and relative
favorable growth.

In spite of this history of The Gambia in sustaining economic growth, poverty has not
caught up, and instead worsened. Further macroeconomic study is still needed to
determine the reasons behind this mismatch between economic growth and poverty
reduction in The Gambia. Anecdotally, this might be explained by such as mismatch
between the component contribution to economic growth and its link with employment
and this impact on distribution efficiency, the narrow economic scope and inefficient
policy structure of the government and its relevance to poverty reduction growth, lack or
inadequacy of systemic and physical infrastructure and human skill and knowledge to
undertake required human development planning and execution and etc.
In this understudied development environment, initiatives such as the CDDP was
undertaken to address poverty reduction that was based on identifying community
poverty reduction needs and addressing them through building community capacities to
identify, plan and manage the implementation of their development needs; the outcomes
of which will be enhanced socioeconomic situations at both community and individual
levels in the intervention areas.

The project covers 600 villages and 88 wards in the 6 rural Local Government Areas
(LGAs) in the country. The project development objective is for rural communities, in
partnership with Local Government Areas (LGAs), to plan, implement and maintain their
priority social and economic investments.
Specifically, the project sought to:
1.      Establish a system of channeling funds to rural communities to finance
        subprojects identified and prioritized on the basis of participatory planning
        processes,
2.      Strengthen the capacity of communities and LGAs to plan, implement and
        manage their own development, and
3.      Support accountability activities conceptualized and implemented by Gambian
        CSOs to promote the fostering of good governance at decentralized levels.
The project sought the following critical outcomes:
        1.      Functional system for channeling funds to rural communities to finance
                subprojects,
        2.      Strengthened capacity of communities and LGAs to plan, implement and
                manage their own development, and
        3.      Fostering of good governance at decentralized levels through support for
                accountability activities conceptualized and implemented by Gambian
                CSOs.




                                           56
To determine achievement or otherwise of the intended outcomes, the project established
the following key Project Outcome Indicators for assessing the progress in
implementation of the project:
That by the end of the project, the following shall be evident in the subprojects identified
and invested in:
1.      70% of economic and social development subproject results achieved,
2.      Satisfaction with the results of community subprojects would increase by 10%
        each year in sampled rural population, and up to 90% by FY05, and
3.      70% of community subprojects shall be fully functional after two years from their
        completion.
This report is the result of an evaluation contribution to the project completion report to
provide fresh evidence on the cost effectiveness and changes in the quality of life
indicators for the population as a result of the project in its six local government areas.
The evaluation specifically emphasized five aspects of the subprojects in the six LGAs:
4.      The project’s relevance to the beneficiary communities,
5.      Its effectiveness in meeting predefined goals,
6.      Its efficiency in terms of cost,
7.      Its consistency with local development plans, and
8.      Its sustainability in terms of maintenance.

The study sought to prove that as discerned at project design, that upon implementation
of this project, there was evidence of changes in the quality of life of project beneficiaries
and provided conclusions from the study of its cost effectiveness and sustainability at
both LGA and subproject levels.

The study took the following approach:
       1.     Secondary progress reports review and the review and analysis of
              subproject data from the CDDP database,
       2.     The administration of a questionnaire to a sample of subprojects stratified
              across LGAs and intervention areas and sensitive to gender to capture
              community counterpart contributions to the project, maintenance costs,
              revenue generation and data from FGDs that enabled the assessment of the
              following subproject issues:
                      The project’s relevance to the beneficiary communities,
                      Its effectiveness in meeting predefined goals,
                      Its efficiency in terms of cost,
                      Its consistency with local development plans, and
                      Its sustainability in terms of maintenance.

Based on a discounted flow cash flow analysis method to determine both the financial
and economic investment viability of the project and questionnaire data to assess its
relevance and effectiveness criteria, the study showed the following critical results:




                                             57
Financial and Economic Impacts of the CDDP

1.      The financial analysis of project investment show a net project loss over the
project implementation and cash flow project period of (D175.3 million) and a -28%
IRR., meaning that as a profit venture, no one would have re-invested money in the
projects’ activities.

9.     However, based on the GDP multiplier and its resulting net economic cash flow,
       the situation is different. The CDDP does not yet show on the basis both of the
       NPV and IRR that the project has been or shall continue to be viable in all the
       LGAs. Instead, it shows that it is extremely viable in Brikama Area Council and
       Janjanbureh Area Council.

10.    At subproject level and based on the intervention areas, the following were
       critical conclusions from the study:

             More than half (animals and ruminant fattening) of the interventions in
              agriculture generate positive NPVs and IRRs both financially and
              economically, whilst interventions in such areas as vegetable gardening
              even though easier, are both not financially and economically viable. Seed
              stores and roads have not been found to be both financially and
              economically viable. The results show that any intervention in animal
              husbandry in any form, significantly changes lives because of its
              profitability and economic impacts on the lives of the beneficiaries.
             Vegetable gardening is really not an economically viable intervention in
              rural areas especially in small communities like Bano Kunda (study
              sample) due to its seasonality and glut in the market due to over-
              production of vegetables during its high season amidst low prices and lack
              of processing facilities.
             From a general perspective, the results confirm the financial and economic
              viability of agricultural interventions and inform the wisdom of its
              criticality in reducing poverty at the rural level. However, the specific
              intervention must be informed by the ability to change lives as has been
              shown by the animal husbandry interventions.
             Interventions in education, health, water and sanitation and environment
              have been very difficult to assess, just as in road works, and the negative
              result of the analysis must be handled with caution. However, outcomes of
              investments both in infrastructure and education take a very long time to
              generate both financial and economic value and therefore, the results in
              this study estimated to 2017, is too short as a basis for judgment on the
              viability of these investments. The same conclusions can be made for
              health, trade (which is more a capacity building intervention) and water
              sanitation.



                                          58
             Easily commercialized activities like operation of an outboard engine
              evidently result in both financial and economic viability even in the short
              term, to 2017.
             Finally, it may be concluded that using the NPVs and IRR alone to
              determine the viability of the CDDP interventions would be misleading.
              These outcomes must be seen in conjunction with the results of the FGD
              assessment by beneficiaries about how the interventions changed their
              lives in the relevance and effectiveness section.
Effectiveness and Usefulness of the CDDP in the Various Intervention Areas such as
Agriculture, Education and Trade

The overall result of the survey in measuring the usefulness and effectiveness of the
CDDP across all its intervention areas comes out at above 70% on average in all LGAs,
meaning that the beneficiaries perceived the project as a good investment in terms of its
contribution to agricultural productivity, in enhanced health status, sound environmental
and educational objectives(planning, managing and implementing their development) and
that the project was perceived to have significantly reduced poverty and all its
ramifications.

Lifesaving Institutional Capacities Impacts of the CDDP on the beneficiaries

 The perceptions of the prospect of life without the project in hindsight have been
discovered to be grim. Over 80% of all respondents in all intervention areas and LGAs
agree that life without the project would have been unbearable. Testimonies of
beneficiaries on this are evident in the boxes in the analysis section of this report.
