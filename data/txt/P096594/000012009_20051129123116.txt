                                  I. INTRODUCTION

1.      The proposed Indonesia Second Development Policy Loan (DPL 2) for $400
million seeks to help the Government sustain the reform momentumthrough its dual
political and economic transitions. The proposed DPL 2 is the second ina series o f three
annual, single tranche loans. Itbuilds on reforms supported bythe first DPL- a $300 million
loan approved by the Board on December 21, 2004 - and deepens cross-cutting reforms in
the core policy areas o f (i)macroeconomic stability and creditworthiness; (ii)improved
investment climate; and (iii)    improved public financial management and anti-corruption.
These remain the defining areas for Indonesia's economic and social development, and ones
that DPL 2 will continue to support. The operation also marks the starting point for policy
dialogue on pro-poor service delivery - a policy area that the Government i s keen on making
an integral part of the DPL program - starting with DPL 3. This new policy area i s in line
with the Government's focus on reducing poverty and reaching ambitious medium-term
development goals linked to the MDGs, as articulated in the Government's medium-term
development plan, or RPJM.

2.      The continuation of the DPL program is based on the Government's overall
progressin implementingits reform agenda. This is evidenced by Indonesia having met
the FY04-07 CAS highcase lendingscenario and having satisfied the pre-defined triggers for
DPL 2. It must be notedthat progress has been unevenacross the range ofpolicy areas, with
substantial progress in some areas, such as macroeconomic stability, and slower progress in
others, such as procurement reform and private participation in infrastructure. However, on
balance, the government's reform program remains broadly on track.

3.      DPL 2 also comes at a criticalcrossroadinIndonesia's politicaltransition. Much
progress has been made inthe past year to consolidate the democratic process, with peaceful
and free elections for president - the first in the country's history - and for regional
governments.     President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono moved quickly to articulate an
ambitious reform agenda under the medium-termdevelopment plan for 2004-09, or RPJM,
which builds on and deepens the reform agenda o f his predecessor.               Early policy
pronouncements and continued strong economic growth raised market confidence and stock
indexes to historic post-crisis highs. However, euphoria was short-lived, with the devastating
December 2004 tsunami claiming at least 150,000 lives in Aceh and North Sumatra, and
leaving more than 500,000 homeless. The disaster prompted an outpouring o f financial
support by the international community and a massive rescue, rehabilitation, and
reconstruction effort bynational and local governments.

4.      The recent reduction in fuel subsidies and consequent reallocation of public
spendingto the poor marks one of the most ambitious and politically difficult reforms
undertaken by any developing country in 2005. It represented a concerted effort by the
central government to execute a strategy that balanced economic prerogatives with a concern
for the welfare o f the poor.     It also effectively addressed lingering concerns over the
Government's commitment to a stable macroeconomic framework - recently tested by the
short-term bout o f currency volatility. Overall, Indonesia's underlying economic indicators

                                              2

 remain stronger than at any time since the financial crisis. Broad progress has been made in
 the governance agenda, where there is a notable difference in the tonality and actions taken
 bythe new administration intackling embedded corruption. The Government has also made
 steady progress on decentralization,              the investment climate, and public financial
 management. While there i s substantial room to accelerate progress in specific areas, the
 reforms undertaken during the past year warrants continuation o f the DPL program.
 Together with Indonesia's other main donors - the Government o f Japan and the Asian
 Development Bank (ADB) - the Bank views the proposed DPL 2 as an effective vehicle for
 sustained policy engagement with the Government and as an important endorsement o f the
 Government's pace, depth, and commitment to reform.

          11.      COUNTRY CONTEXT AND DEVELOPMENT CHALLENGES

    A.      POLITICAL,ECONOMIC,            AND SOCIAL CONTEXT

 5.      The tremendous growththat Indonesiaexperiencedin the decades leadingup to
 the regional financial crisis lifted millions out of poverty. From the mid-1960s through
 the late-l990s, growth inIndonesia's income per capita kept pace with that o f its neighbors,
 significantly reducing poverty incidence from 54.2 percent o f the population in 1976 to 11.3
 percent in 1996.' The economic growth strategy pursuedby the Suharto regime during this
 period was spearheaded by well-connected industrial groupings, financed in large part by
 directed lending from state-owned banks. Growth was severely interruptedby the economic
 crisis which set in motion a period o f political change, culminating in the fall o f the New
 Order regime and characterized by a transition from an autocratic, centralized state to a
 democratic, decentralized one. The political and economic instability that accompanied this
 transition severely hampered private investment, which had already suffered from continued
 institutional weaknesses in Indonesia's regulatory framework for key sectors, and in its
 judiciary and tax administration. The country's relatively slow recovery reflects this; until
 recently, Indonesia's economic growthhad been driven byprivate consumption, with exports
 in both manufacturing and petroleum sectors hampered by lack of investment and falling
 behind other countries.

 6.      Despite challenges and risks on a number of fronts, Indonesia's dual political
 andeconomic transitionremainsbroadlyon track. The previous government didmuchto
 restore macroeconomic and financial stability, and initiated structural reforms needed to
 boost investment and growth. Fiscal consolidation helped to bolster confidence and lower
 interest rates, laying the basis for economic recovery and combining to reduce public debt.
 Meanwhile, important strides were made to stabilize and re-privatize intervened banks and
 strengthen bank profitability. The completion o f both the IMF-program at the end o f 2003,
 and the transitional White Paper implementation a year later provided a backdrop to last
 year's historic presidential and regional elections. Universally acknowledged as peaceful,
 free and fair, the elections did much to consolidate the democratic process inIndonesia, with
 a burgeoning civil society, relatively free local media, and the academic community
 increasingly engaged in the policy debate. The elections also more firmly established the
 dominance o f secular-nationalist parties inIndonesianpolitics.

 7.        As the country's first directly elected president, President Yudhoyono won a
 strong mandate to maintain economic fundamentals and deepen the reform agenda.
 Assuming office in October 2004, he quickly articulated a broad-based development vision
 which reinforced the country's shift away from a post-crisis, stabilization framework to a
 longer-term growth and poverty reduction agenda. Among other priorities, the Government
 has focused on conservative budget management, made strides against corruption, and taken
 steps to enhance the investment climate and to improve service delivery.                            These
 developments have encouraged continued strong economic growth, resembling the levels o f
 growth prior to the financial crisis. Last year, Indonesia reached historic highs inexports and
 imports, and has seen a turn around in FDI, with net FDIpositive at US$l.O billion for the
 first time since the crisis. Amidst the positive news, poverty levels have also declined to
 below pre-crisis levels.

 8.        Sadly, the past year will also be remembered for the tragic tsunami that
 devastated Aceh in December 2004. Total damage in Aceh was extensive, particularly in
 human terms, with approximately 131,000 people having died, 37,000 still missing, and
 500,000 left homeless, with virtually the entire population havingsuffered the loss o f friends,
 relatives, livelihood, or trauma. The emergency relief effort i s widely credited for having
 minimized greater suffering through rapid provisions o f food, water, shelter, and medical
 care.     Post-relief activities have also succeeded in getting approximately 90 percent o f
 children into temporary school facilities; resuming basic medical systems in most areas; and
 enrolling about 14,000 people in cash-for-work programs.                       Despite these successes,
 reconstruction has generally made less progress than expected or needed, reflecting the
 complexity o f reconstruction, the scale o f destruction, and challenges in planning,
 coordination, and implementation. The GDP in the affected area has shrunk approximately
 14 percent and the Government's Master Plan estimates reconstruction costs at US$5.1
 billion. Against this estimate, the donor community has pledgedmore than US$2.5 billion in
 official aid for reconstruction, US$550 million for broader development programs, and an
 offer o f a debt moratorium by the Paris Club creditors. In addition, NGOs have raised an
 estimated $2 billion. Fortunately, the economic recovery and macroeconomic stabilization
 achieved inthe last few years, andparticularly the political and economic milestones in2004,
 have added resilience to the economy, positioning it to better absorb such shocks.

 9.        One of the Government's major achievements in its first year in office - with
 impetus provided by the tsunami response - was a successful peace deal with the
 province's separatist rebels, the Free Aceh Movement (GAM), on August 15,2005. The
 incumbent administration had been continuing informal negotiations with GAM in Helsinki
 -evenbeforethetsunami-withaimoffindingaresolutiontothethreedecade-oldproblem.
 With the signing of the MOU, GAM agreed to cease all separatist activities. Inreturn, the
 Government agreed to permit special autonomy in the Province o f Nanggroe Aceh
 Darussalam while upholding the principle of the Unitary State o f the Republic o f Indonesia.
 The Government has requested Parliament to consider granting amnesty to former GAM
 activists. The President, in his State o f the Nation address, has promised that the MOUwill
be consistently implemented, called on former GAM activists to also abide by the MOU, and

       B.      KEY DEVELOPMENT             ISSUES

 10.         Despite progress in recent years, poverty reduction remains a critical challenge
 for Indonesia. The 2004 household survey indicates that the poverty incidence has fallen
 significantly from a peak in 1999 at 23.4 percent, or a headcount o f 48 million people, to
 16.0 percent or 35.1 million in 2005 (see Figure la).3 However, the total number o f poor
 continues to be high. While 7.4 percent o f the population live below $1 a day, over 53
 percent - or 115 million people - live on less than $2 a day, with many remaining highly
 vulnerable to falling under Indonesia's poverty line. Eastern Indonesia continues to lag far
 behindthe rest ofthe country, but there are also pocketsofpovertywithin all regions, andthe
 highpopulation density of Java and Sumatra means that most o f the poor, in fact, live on
 these islands. Comparisons to other Southeast Asian countries show Indonesiacontinuing to
 under-perform its neighbors in access to quality health, education, and other basis services.
 For example, Indonesia's maternal mortality rate i s twice that o f the Philippines and five
 times higher than that o f Vietnam (see Figure lb.)

                                  Box 1. Oil Prices and the IndonesianEconomy

The second half o f 2005 witnessed widespread market concern over the government's economic management,
centering on the policy response to - and the impact o f - the soaring global price o f crude oil o n macroeconomic
stability, and the broader challenges facing the country's energy sector.

Indonesia has significant energy resources. It is the only Asian OPEC country and remains, marginally, a net
exporter of energy when oil and gas are combined (adding coal would make the net export position greater).
Nevertheless, the positive impact o f higher oil prices on the economy has beenrapidly declining and as noted, the
balance moved temporarily to negative inthe second quarter and is thought to be significantly negative by many
market participants. Reasons for this perception are rooted inthe country's production and consumption o f oil,
with gas output (mostly for export) largely stagnant. Production continued to decline in 2004 to a low o f 1.1
million barrels per day from a higho f 1.5 millionbarrels per day ten years ago. This decline is largely a result o f
under-investment inexploration and a similar lack o f investment inrefinery capacity since the crisis.
Moreover, in recent years, the increase in the price o f Indonesian crude has not kept pace with international
prices (the price o f Indonesian crude used to average US$1-2 per barrel below West Texas Intermediate but now
averages US$3-4 a barrel less). In addition, the price o f crude overall has fallen relative to the price o f refined
products, due to worldwide refinery shortages. Both o f these trends work against Indonesia's oil terms o f trade,
as Indonesia exports relatively more crude and imports relatively more - and increasingly expensive - fuel, with
domestic fuel production requiring the import o f relatively more expensive non-Indonesian crude.
Meanwhile, consumption levels have been rising rapidly, reaching 1.2 million barrels per day, spurred by faster
growth and subsidized prices. Before the recent price increase, gasoline was approximately U S 0 . 2 4 a liter (now
about US$0.45 a liter), which enabled Indonesians to enjoy some o f the cheapest fuel in the world, but also
encouraging wasteful and excessive fuel consumption and a lack o f investment inconservation. L o w fuel prices
have also contributed to very inefficient energy usage inanother way: fuel oil now accounts for nearly half o f the
country's energy needs w h l e other sources o f energy, such as natural gas and coal - in which Indonesia is very
well endowed - remain under-utilized. Finally, fuel subsidies disproportionately benefit the wealthy: fewer than
6 percent o f Indonesianhouseholds have cars, and only a thirdhave motorcycles.
Apart from these distorting effects, fuel subsidies also represented a drain o n the national budget. Before the
October fuel price increase, it was estimated that each US$l/bbl increase in the international oil price added
Rp.0.9 trillion to the deficit, with the cost o f subsidies running close to U S 1 5 billion (roughly a third o f central
government expenditures), an amount better spent on infrastructure and pro-poor spending priorities.
Although fuel prices were increasedby 29 percent inMarch 2005, the markets were looking for further fuel price
hikes and subsidy reductions as a defining structural reform issue. These expectations were met when, on
October 1, 2005, the government enacted fuel price increases o f 88 percent for gasoline, 105 percent for diesel
and 186 percent for kerosene. It is too soon to evaluate the impact o f the price increase o n Indonesia's energy
picture but anecdotal evidence i s already accumulating. For example, Pertamina (the national oil company) has
announced delays for the scheduled fuel imports, especially gasoline, for October due to decline in fuel
consumption volume, and there are newspaper accounts indicating rapidly increasing demand for natural gas for
residential use. The adjustment o f the economy to changed (and more appropriate) prices will take some time,
but Indonesia is well positioned to improve the efficiency o f production (through conservation) and shift its
domestic energy mix to gas and coal where it i s well endowed.

Government and development partners have agreed to enhance and intensify their policy
engagement structured around the RPJM,both at the macroeconomic and sector levels.

23.         Key to implementing the program is an annual work plan, or RKP. For 2006,

         Cl. Reducing inefficient spending

 31.      Since the crisis, macroeconomicstability and fiscal consolidationwere achieved,
 in part, by low levels of governmentspendingon investmentand service delivery, while
 poorly targeted subsidies mounted. Public sector investment has declined considerably
 since the mid-1990s as the government was focused on restoring its fiscal position. Further,
 despite being an oil producing and exporting country, Indonesia has not gained from the
 current favorable global oil market due to lack o f investments and mounting subsidies (see
 Box 1).

 32.      The fuel price increases in 2005 dramaticallv reduced inefficient spending and
 opened the opportunity for an improved allocation of government resources. The
 March 2005 increase in retail prices o f fuel by 29 percent was followed by the government's
 move to raise the price o f fuel sales to industrial users to international levels inJuly-August.
 Remaining administered fuel prices were then increased by a weighted average o f 114
 percent in October 2005 (an increase o f approximately 90 percent if all fuel products
 including those previously increased were included). The net effect o f these increases has
 been to reallocate up to U S $9-10 billion in spending from subsidies to other programs and
 transfers to the regions (at an assumed price o f US$57 dollars a barrel, as used inthe budget)
 in 2006. In order to mitigate the social impact of these increases, starting in March the
 government put in place programs allocating an additional Rp. 11trillion (US $ 1.1billion)
 to pro-poor spending on education, health, and food (rice).                         In October, an additional
 program was instituted that provides cash transfers o f Rp. 300,000 per family to 15.5 million
 poor and near poor families on a quarterly basis (this will involve close to US$2 billion in
 spendingannually).

 33.      With success on the reduction of inefficient spending, the critical policy issue
 moves to how well these funds are spent to address Government service delivery and
 poverty goals. Macroeconomic stability can never be taken for granted and the size o f the

                                                            21

he1price increases could lead to problems, including risks o f inflation and fiscal contraction.
Furthermore, the lack o f market-based pricing continues to cause concerns in the event o f
either dramatic increases or decreases in international oil prices.            Nevertheless, the
expenditure agenda around the funds freed up from the reduction in subsidies are sufficiently
important that the Government has requested that this topic be a new core policy area, to be
referred to as "Making Services Work for the Poor" (see section F).

        C2. Making tax administration more efficient

34.     The government continues to progress on its tax agenda. To provide increased
and predictable revenues, the government's strategy has been to improve compliance and
broaden the tax base while reducing rates to improve investment sentiment. To improve the
efficiency o f the tax system, the tax department has two areas o f focus. First, the government
i s changing the orientation o f the tax department with the introduction o f modem tax offices
(see below). Inaddition, the tax department has other short-term tax generation measures. In
particular, the government i s registering additional tax payers; increasing the filing of tax
returns (by new and existing taxpayers); recovering tax arrears; and expanding audit
coverage. For example, in a widely publicized move, the government in October issued
individualtax numbersto 10milliontaxpayers, up from 3.5 million last year. This was done
bylinkingincome taxes to other databases, especially onproperty taxes.

35.     The governmenthas proposedto Parliamentamendments to three key tax laws:
income taxes, value-added taxes, and tax administration. Among other things, the
income tax law unifies the corporate tax rate and reduces its highest rate from 30 percent to
25 percent by 2010, the individual income tax law reduces the highest marginal rate from 35
percent to 30 percent over the same period and reduces the rate on dividends from 20 to 10
percent.   Not yet introduced, but under discussion, i s another law providing for a tax
amnesty. Analysis indicates that the net effect of the proposed changes could reduce taxes by
up to 0.5 percent of GDP. The history o f tax amnesties has not been good. Nevertheless, the
government argues that improvements to tax administration (including the expansion o f the
modem tax offices), and the proposed tax amnesty will off-set the lower rates. The tax
administration law i s particularly controversial with the business community, especially as
regards increased powers to the tax department, includingthe right to use criminal sanctions
against taxpayers.

36.     While changesto the tax system are debated, moderntax offices have expanded.
InOctober 2004, alltax officesinJakartaweremodernized, withthebuildingsupgraded; the
staff organized by function; account representatives created in line with the changed focus;
offices fully computerized and linked (including the addition o f built-in controls); tax payer
bill of rights and tax official code of conduct instituted; and remuneration increased. In
December 2004, a pilot small taxpayer office was opened. In addition, e-registration o f tax
payers was introduced inDecember 2004, as was e-filing inJanuary 2005. In2005, the first
of the modem tax offices, the Large Taxpayers' Office (opened in2003), had an independent
satisfaction survey completed. Eighty-one percent o f respondents indicated that they were
satisfied with the overall level o f services received; 84 percent say filing taxes i s easier; and

  37.        Despite progress on extending the modern tax office system, the tax system is
  still characterized by: relatively low revenue generation, by poorly defined and overly
  complicated taxes (Le., creating excessive compliance costs), as well as by poor and
  inconsistent enforcement (Le., creating uncertainty and abuse). In part, the problem
  requires better oversight o f tax officials, and in part stems from a systematic lack o f
  information and capacity for tax (and customs) analysis outside o f the respective tax and
  customs offices. The lack o f capacity has ledto: an inability to accurately assess and forecast
  the implications o f the proposed tax and customs changes; reduced checks and balances; and
  to inability to balance taxpayer and government requirements.

          C3. ImprovingDebt Management

  38.      In the aftermath of the crisis in 1997, government strategy has focused on
  improving its fiscal, and especially debt, situation. The government was left with large
  amounts o f debt - approximately 50 percent o f GDP each indomestic and foreign debt. The
  domestic debt was short-term and initial efforts focused on re-profiling and improving the
  functioning o f the domestic bond market.                 Debt buy-backs continue but re-profiling is
  complete and regular domestic bond offers that began in2003 are continuing (see Annex 5).
  Inthe last two years the government also movedto diversify financing sources and is now
  issuingbonds ininternational markets. The government has issued bonds three times inthe
  last two years, including most recently in October 2005, when it issued US$900 million in
  10-year maturity and US$600 million in 30-year maturity, at an average o f 329-basis points
  and 406-basis points over U S treasuries o f the same maturity, respectively. The agreement
  between Parliament and the Government to provide increased flexibility (to tap either
  domestic or international markets) as long as overall borrowing limits are respected is a step
  toward improved debt management.

  implementation of a debt management strategy. The government prepared and presented

  finding in the background assessment is that Indonesia's public debt is highly exposed to
  changes inexchange rates and interest rates; the strategy calls for a gradual reduction o f these
  risks. The Bank and AUSAID are supporting the government in the development o f sound
  debt management practices, including governance, risk management, and staff capacity, as
  well as providing policy guidance.              The government has had a system for publishing

         C4. ClarijjingI nter-governrnentalBorrowing

  40.     Indonesia has embarked on an ambitious program of administrative and fiscal
  decentralization. Preparation for decentralization has its genesis intwo laws promulgatedin
  May 1999, including Law 25/99 concerning fiscal and finance issues. While the
  decentralization program was carried out more smoothly than expected, a number o f
  problems became apparent. For example, the regulatory framework for on-lending and on-
  granting was not clear and uncertainty regarding the legal framework led to a near halt in
  sub-national borrowing and a decline in sub-national government investment in public
  infrastructure.

41.      In response, the government revised Law 25/99. Now designated Law 33/04, the
revised law lays the groundwork for regional governments to borrow from a wide range o f
domestic sources. These can include the issuance o f revenue bonds, thereby potentially
reducing sub-national governments' dependence on the central government to finance capital
development, at least in the long-run.      Relevant regulatory issues, including work-out
procedures, are yet to be addressed. Inthe mediumterm, sub-national government borrowing
i s likely to remain restricted to the central government and, in particular, the on-lending
mechanism. Major government regulations required for implementation o f Law 33/04 have
been drafted and were sent to the President, who i s expected to sign them by mid December
2005. Assistance inthis process has been provided by the World Bank andthe ADB through
their technical assistance programs, the DPL, and the ADB's Local Government Finance
(LGF) and Governance ReformSector Development Program.

42.      To operationalize on-lending, Minister of Finance Decree 35 - or KMK 35/03 -
needs to be revised. This decree sets out the critical procedures for on-lending, including
such things as the specific process for project submission and review, the treatment o f local
government arrears, and the types o f projects that can be lent for (the definition o f "revenue
generation").    However, revised KMK 35/03 is, in turn, based on three higher level
government regulations related to regional borrowing, regional grants, and on-granting / on-
lending of foreign funds to sub-national governments that lay out definitions, institutional
assignments, and broad procedures. These government regulations have been drafted and
checked for consistency. The regulations on regional borrowing and regional grants have been
cleared at the Ministryof Law and Justice and are now ready for signing by the President. A
third regulation is undergoing interdepartmental hearings at the Minister o f Law and Justice
and i s also expected to be signed by the President by mid-December. Based on these draft
regulations, the Government has drafted a revision to KMK 35/03 on on-lending which will i s
expected to be signed by the Minister (also in mid-December) when the three regulations are
signed. Three other ministerial decrees will still need to be revised before the system is
completely operational: (1) on project submission and project appraisal; (2) on on-granting;
and (3) on loan disbursement, administration, and monitoring/evaluation. The Government
has doubled the size of its commitment to conditional regional grants inthe 2006 draft budget,
increasingthe DAK to Rp 11.6 trillion, from Rp 4.8 trillion inthe 2005 budget.

 53.     Indonesia's infrastructureinvestmentsneeds are estimatedto be betweenUS$12
 and US$15 billion per year to be able to sustain a 6 percent annual growth rate. To
 achieve this, Indonesia needs to increase its overall investment in infrastructure. As public
 funds alone are insufficient to cover all aspects ofinfrastructureinvestments, Indonesianeeds
 to develop a comprehensive Infrastructure Development Plan that can encourage effective
 public-private partnerships (PPP).    Key elements o f the Government's strategy include a
 policy framework for involving the private sector, sectoral policies for market structure and
 regulation, and implementationo f private sector transactions. Progress is being made, but to
 restart PPIinIndonesia, coordination and implementation issues will have to be addressed in
 earnest.

 54.     In January 2005, the Government organized an Infrastructure Summit to
 attract private investors to participate in the sector, and committed to reform
 applicable laws, policies, and guidelines to create the investment climate necessary to
 attract them. This was a major event, and sent a strong signal concerningthe Government's
 determination to make a break from prior practices in the infrastructure sectors,          The
 proposedpolicy framework for involving the private sector includes two central elements: (i)
 a cohesive PPP framework that provides clear rules and procurement principles, a level
 playing field for bidders, and transparency and accountability in the process for awarding
 PPP transactions; and (ii)a risk management framework that ensures that risks are
 appropriately allocated between the public and private sectors and that limits the public
 sector's overall exposure to risks posed by mechanisms for providing public support to
 private projects.

55.      There has been progress on the PPP framework. Recent government regulations
establish an inter-ministerial infrastructure committee (KKPPI) responsible for coordinating
across ministries, accelerating investment projects, and ensuring consistent and transparent
treatment o f bidding processes (Perpres 42) and set out the procedures for procurement o f
private and public infrastructure (Perpres 67). The key to making KKPPI work, however, i s a
competent, funded, secretariat to ensure that individual transactions comply with rules and
international best practice. Development o f such a centralizing institution has posed a
challenge to traditional practices inwhich sectoral ministries were free to develop transactions
as they wished.

56.      There has also beenprogresson the riskmanagementframework. The Minister o f
Finance has issued a decree that establishes a Committee in the MOF to formulate a risk
management framework for infrastructure transactions. The framework would set out the
Government's policy stance and modalities for risk sharing aimed at facilitating infrastructure
development. Going forward, the Government plans to provide specific budgetary allocations
for public support to PPP projects.

       04. Promoting the Development of Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs)

58.      SMEs play a criticalrole in reducingpoverty and spurring economic activity in
Indonesia. Data from 2003 indicates that SMEs contributed nearly 57 percent o f the
country's GDP. The tangible impact o f SMEs includes employment generation, enhanced
productivity, and lowered income and regional disparities. However, SMEs confront many
challenges: they typically have limited access to credit from banks or other financial
institutions; to markets, market information, and new production and distribution
technologies; and to modern management and human resource skills. At the institutional
level, SMEs also face legal and regulatory challenges: the lack o f coordination among
government agencies charged with different aspects o f the SME sub-sector; conflicting
national and sub-national regulations; and an overly burdensome regulatory environment.

59.      The Governmenthas looked to address some of the most salient issues affecting
SMEs. InFebruary2005, the Ministry o f Cooperatives and Small and MediumEnterprises
publishedthe "SME Empowerment Action Program 2005-2009" which has a medium-term
action plan and effective framework to coordinate the development o f SMEs.              It has
developed a comprehensive government policy on micro-finance regulation; undertaken
reforms to develop a consumer credit bureau; improved tracking o f key investment climate
indictors to help identify and track investment climate reforms; and reviewed policies that
impact procurement regulations for goods and services. While there is some progress on a
comprehensive set o f national SME development guidelines, many ministries are working
within their authorities to further address SME development issues. The Government's focus
i s primarily on job-creation. Through the new action program, it is estimated that about 6
million new micro and small enterprises will be created by 2009, up from the 40 million
estimated registered and unregistered enterprises operating at present. The program will also
help strengthen the network o f 70,000 cooperatives servingover 20 millionmembers.

70.       The Government, through its medium-termand annual plans, has laid out an
ambitious poverty reduction agenda linked to the achievement of the MDGs. Driving
this agenda is an overarching emphasis on improving pro-poor public service delivery,
specifically in the priority areas o f health, education, rural infrastructure, and social
protection. With this objective in mind, the Government has undertaken enhanced action
plans, established new programs, and increased budget expenditure for public service
delivery. The latter is exemplified by the 2005 reallocation o f about US$1.3 billion in fuel
subsidy funds to programs tied to the four priority areas. This is in addition to the US$2.0
billion for the cash transfer scheme.         The programs tied to public service delivery are
expected to benefit poorer households by increasing access and reducing or eliminating fees
to primary health and basic education services. Along with increased public expenditure in
other areas, these programs should also result inemployment generation.

incorporate the policy dialogue on improving public service delivery for the poor,
starting with DPL 3. The Bank's engagement with the Government will revolve around
efforts to ensure the effective design, implementation, and monitoring o f public service
delivery programs. In particular, as the programs are largely supply-side driven, it will be
important to ensure that the needed provision o f additional funds to fi-ont-line providers

its stated development goals.      A CCT program may also provide the opportunity to
consolidate some o f the supply-sidedriven programs, such as those in place, with demand-
side interventions. This would enable the concurrent removal o f supply and demand side
constraints when either i s binding, while also increasing the accountability o f service
provision units to clients who hold additional funds.

72.     Moving forward, the Government aims to effectively reallocate fuel subsidy

subsidy, to more targeted interventions to benefit the poor will require a fine-tuned targeting
system.    While an important step in this direction has been taken in 2005 with the

         B.      LOAN    ADMINISTRATION

 81.      Borrower and Credit Amount. The borrower is the Republic o f Indonesia and this
 operation i s a single-tranche loan for US$400 million that would be made available upon

 prior to Board presentation. The Government has confirmed that Indonesia would borrow
 this amount as a Fixed Spread Loan (FSL).                  In the past, Indonesia has selected Variable
 Spread Loans (VSLs) mainly due to cost considerations (e.g., the current VSL lending rate is
 about 0.08% lower than that of the FSL). However, the FSL offers a fixed spread over
 LIBOR, flexible repayment terms, and embedded conversion options to change the loan
 currency and fix the interest rate. Inparticular, if the Government would like to retain the
 flexibility o f borrowing in U S dollars, but potentially subsequently undertaking swap
 transactions and repaying the loan in local currency, this i s possible only by taking FSL.16
 The Government's debt management strategy aims, over time, to increase the proportion of
 local currency liabilities inoverall liabilities. Therefore, the flexibility offered by FSLs i s in
 line with this strategy. FSLs also offer the flexibility to customize loan repayment terms, fix
 the interest rate on the entire loan amount, and convert the currency o fthe loan.

94.     Continuedstrong performanceby the governmenton its reform agenda and the
achievement of medium-term objectives are subject to several risks. These include: (i)
rising interest rates; (ii)
                          increased social tensions may result when government increases fuel
prices; (iii)deteriorating external environment; (iv) growing fears o f a bird-fluepidemic; (v)
heightened security alert. These are described inmore detail below:

   (i)   Increased economic volatility. The bout o f short-term volatility experienced in
         August-September has provided a strong reminder that sound fiscal and monetary
         policies - with timely policy actions in line with key global developments - are
         needed in order to provide assurance to financial markets.          The key problems
         confronting macroeconomic management inthe near term are that o f rising inflation
         and fiscal contraction - as the full effects o f the fuel price hike feed through the
         economy. The DPL program faces risks in the event that the policy responses to
         these issues are inadequate. Bank Indonesia needs to stay ahead o f the curve - as it
         has since the h e 1 price hike - in ensuring that appropriate monetary policies are in
         place to be able to address the spike in inflation- and to manage a smooth transition
         back to a lower inflation environment. The fiscal authorities, on the other hand, will
         have to ensure that the additional revenues from the reduction in subsidies are
         quickly, but efficiently, reallocated to avoid a fiscal contraction.

   (ii)Rising    interest rates may result in slower growth and greater difficulties for the
         financial sector. The increase in fuel prices will certainly bring higher inflation
         which will trigger higher interest rates and may slow borrowing and reduce

                                                43

     investment depending on market expectations. If inflation expectations buildhigher
     interest rates will be needed impacting on investment sentiment and lengthen the
     adjustment. Banks are also likely to have larger non-performing loans with adverse
     impacts on profitability. While this will clearly hurtbank balance sheets inthe short
     run,giventhe current strong capitalpositionofmuch ofthe banking sector, this risk
     is manageable - especially if a high interest rate regime i s temporary.          Further
     problems are also likely in debt markets - where yields have been sharply higher

Impact of the proposed DPL on the overall debt

Government debt management i s centered on the trade-off between cost and risk. From a pure
risk perspective, a loan indomestic currency i s more attractive than a loan inforeign currency,
as it will insulate the government's budget fiom changes in the loan payments caused by
fluctuations in exchange rates. Similarly, a fixed rate loan i s attractive compared to a variable
rate loan, as changes in market interest rates will have no effect on the loan payments, and
therefore the government's budget. However, a low-risk choice typically comes at a higher
cost. A loan in domestic currency under current market conditions will come at a higher
interest rate, and a fixed rate loan will normally have a higher interest rate than a variable rate
loan.

11.       Debt Sustainability

Debt sustainability analysis shows that that both external and public debt dynamics are robust
to possible shocks in exchange rates, interest rates, and GDP growth. Inthe baseline scenario,
the debt to GDP ratio i s projected to decline by from its 2004 level (54 percent) to under 30
per cent of GDP by 2010 (Figure 2). Evenin a extreme shock scenario - up to a 30 per cent
depreciation in the exchange rate, a 250 basis point rise in interest rates, and slow down o f
GDP growth to 3 percent - the public debt ratio would increase by 10 percentage points o f
GDP, but the debt ratios would still be sustainable, and the path o f consolidation would be
expected to continue.35

Economic Conditions and Outlook


0  Following a period o f increased volatility inAugust and September, Indonesia's financial
   markets have stabilized following action taken by the authorities to address the underlying
   imbalances in September and October. Monetarypolicy has beentightened significantly and
   the government announced sharp cuts in domestic fuel subsidies, effective October 1, to
   address pressures on the budget and the balance o f payments associated with high
   international prices. Breaking a policy o f gradual and modest rate increases, BIraised its one-
   month policy rate by 125 basis points to 10 percent in late Augudearly September and by an
   additional 100 basis points in early October. The last hike came after the sharp increases on
   October 1in domestic fuel prices-88     percent on gasoline, 200 percent on kerosene, and 105
   percent on diesel. The fuel price increase was accompanied by a compensation package,
   whereby 15%million families will receive about US$120 annually to help offset the impact o f
   rising fuel costs on their income. Markets have reacted positively to the interest rate and
   domestic fuel price increases, and both the rupiah and the stock market have strengthened
   since.

0  The macroeconomic situation i s weaker but the outlook for growth i s still favorable.
   Real GDP grew by 5.1 percent in 2004 and i s envisaged to increase by 5-5% percent in2005.
   This pace is somewhat slower than the 6 percent growth registeredearlier this year, reflecting
   the impact o f the recent financial market volatility on consumer and investment spending,
   as well as higher oil prices. Inflation i s expected to remain in the double digits in the near-
   term, following the bigjump inOctober to 18 percent (year-on-year) that largely reflected the
   impact o f domestic fuel price increases but also a more general pickup inprice pressures. To
   contain underlyinginflation, BI raised its policy interest rate by another 125 basis points on
   November 1, and stated its commitment to take further action, as needed, so as to bring
   inflation back down to single digits bythe end o f 2006.

0  Fiscal policy remains prudent and the revised 2005 budget deficit target of about one
   percent of GDP is achievable. The revised 2005 budget approved by parliament in
   September entailed a slight increase in the deficit target by 0.1 percent o f GDP to 0.9
   percent o f GDP (1.3 percent o f GDP under IMF definitions). With fuel subsidy payments
   this year now expected to reachjust over 3 percent o f GDP, compared with over 4 percent
   estimated prior to the hike and given the slower-than-expected pace o f capital
   expenditures so far, the deficit target should be met. The 2006 revised budget, reflecting
   more updated macroeconomic developments, including the cut indomestic fuel subsidies,
   was just approved by Parliament. The deficit target has been kept at 0.7 percent o f GDP in
   line with the authorities commitment of continued fiscal consolidation and a further
   reduction inthe public debt burden.

0 The international reserve position is expected to recover again over the medium
  term. Gross internationalreserves have increased from their lows in September, helped in
  part by the successful $1% billion sovereign bindissue inOctober, and remain adequate at
  $33 billion (135 percent o f short-term debt), although they are down from their peak of
  $37 billion in early April. The increase in domestic fuel prices combined with tighter
  monetary conditions should help reduce pressures on the balance o f payments that had
  emerged over the course o f the year, leading to significant losses in foreign official
  reserves. That said, the current account surplus i s expected to narrow further over the
  medium term as imports rise faster than exports, financed in large part by private sector
  capital inflows.


0 The overall performanceof the bankingsector has improved,butrisksremain.Inrecent
  years, banks were able to improve earnings as net interest margins improved due to lower
  interest rates. Asset quality has also improved at several banks, although some banks recently
  showed higher NPLs largely due to stricter classification rules enforced by BI but also, in
  part, due to a deterioration intheir lending portfolios. Going forward, rising interest rates will
  increase credit and interest rate risks, and squeeze profit margins.

  governance. However, the operating agreement between Pertamina and ExxonMobil, which
  will help boost oil production, i s yet to be completed. Progress has also been limited in the
  areas o frevenue administration, labor market reforms, andjudicial and legal systems.

ChallengesAhead

      Recent events illustrate that vulnerabilities remain, and appropriate and timely
      policies will be critical to maintaining macroeconomic stability and achieve the
      economic objectives o f high growth and reduced unemployment and poverty. The
      immediate policy priority for the authorities is maintaining market confidence through
      careful monitoring of inflation developments and firm monetary restraint to contain the
      risk that the recent fuel price increase lead to a generalized surge in underlying inflation,
      while implementing effectively the fuel price compensation scheme for the poor. These
      actions will help to ensure that the fall-out from the volatility in financial markets in
      earlier months does not jeopardize the achievement o f the authorities' objectives o f high
      and sustained growth and low unemployment and poverty. Assuming that fiscal and
      monetary policy remain prudent and progress continues to be made on structural
      reforms to improve the investment climate, w e believe that the authorities can achieve
      their broad economic objectives.

A. Description

The Government's investment climate program is focused on: i)streamlining investment
procedures and simplifying licensing requirements to encourage business entry; ii)improving
tax and customs administration; iii)enhancing labor market flexibility; iv) improving
infrastructure; and v) reducing corruption. The overall objective i s to reduce transactions
costs for business. In statements to international investors, the President has repeatedly
stressed the importance o f bureaucratic reform inthese areas, and has specifically promised to
reduce the start-up time for new businesses from 151 days to 30 days. The Government's
anti-corruption campaign has seen senior officials tried for corruption, including two
governors and police officials, sending a messagethroughout the bureaucracy.

China, Malaysia, the Philippines, Thailand and Vietnam interms o f the ease o f doing business
(see charts below).             The time and cost to start a business exceeds every major regional
economy, contract enforcement i s weak, the process o f paying taxes is more complicated and
time consuming than the regional average, effective tax rates are high, and labor regulations
are rigid. The new government has initiated programs to address these problems.

2) Taxation
Three new tax laws -- on general tax administration, income tax and value added tax -- were
signed by the President and sent to Parliament in early September. The laws reduce the
administrative burden o f taxation on businesses by: i)cutting the statutory limit for tax
assessment from 10 years to 5 years; ii)extending filing deadlines for corporate income tax
and allowing automatic extensions, and iii)removing the requirement to audit every refund
request. The income tax law reduces the top corporate rate, cuts taxes on dividends, reduces
the tax rate for low income earners, andraises thresholds so that minimumwage workers will
no longer be subject to income tax. The VAT tax law exempts unprocessed agricultural
products, electricity, and water from V A T tax.

While the tax laws include definite improvementsintax administration, they did not meet the
expectations o f the business community. In particular, businesses have complained that the
laws treat taxpayers harshly but do not provide for similar harsh treatment o f tax officials who
abuse their authority. This i s a major concern given the perceived arbitrary nature o f tax
assessments, arbitrary handlingof appeals and refund requests, and pervasive corruption. Tax
officials continue to have the power to arrest and detain a person suspected o f having
committed a tax crime; vaguely defined "negligence" in filing a return is a criminal offense
subject to imprisonment; and 100% o f any assessment must be paid immediately even if the
assessment i s under appeal.

Based on these concerns, the Indonesian Chamber of Commerce has begun lobbying to have
the laws revised in Parliament.      The head o f the Chamber has written to the President
complaining that the "principle of equal treatment isn't taken into account" in the draft laws.
Pending the outcome o f discussions between the Government, Parliament and the business
community, any assessment o f the current drafts shouldbe neutral.

Indonesia's infrastructure investments needs are estimated to be between US$ 12 and US$ 15
billion per year. To achieve this, Indonesia needs to increase its overall investment in
infrastructure. As public fimds alone are insufficient to cover all aspects o f infrastructure
investments, Indonesia needs to develop a comprehensive Infrastructure Development Plan
that can encourage effective public private partnerships (PPP). Key elements o f the
Government's strategy include a policy framework for involving the private sector, sectoral
policies for market structure and regulation, and implementation o fprivate sector transactions.
Progress i s beingmade, but for the moment outcomes remainmixed.

Actions taken by the Government during thepast year

InJanuary 2005, the newlyelected Government organized an Infrastructure Summit to attract
private investors to participate inthe sector, and committed to reform applicable laws, policies
and guidelines to create the investment climate that i s necessary to attract them. This was a
major event, and sent a strong signal concerning the Government's determination to make a
break from prior practices inthe infrastructure sectors.

    a

                     ANNEX9: TECHNICALNOTEONDECENTRALIZATION


Description

Indonesia has embarked upon an ambitious program o f administrative and fiscal
decentralization. Preparation o f the effort had its genesis in two laws, both promulgated in
May o f 1999, one on administrative matters (Law 22/99) and the other concerning fiscal and
finance issues (Law 25/99) In December o f 2000, an additional and essential piece o f
decentralization legislation, on regional government taxation (Law 34/00), was passed by
parliament. Implementation o f the three laws beganin2001.

Challenge

The initial comprehensive implementationo fIndonesia's large scale decentralizationprogram
was carried out more smoothly than many observers expected might be the case. Two million
central civil servants were transferred to sub-national governments and paid in a timely
manner. And regional public services continued to be delivered to citizens without major
incident.

Still, a number o f problems with the new decentralization framework soon became apparent.

The assignment of expenditures across levels of government was not clearly laid out in law.
Among other things, this ambiguity led to conflict and confusion among central departments
and sub-national governments regarding responsibility for service delivery in many sectors.
Central governments have continued to spend a large share o f their resources inthe regions on
tasks that have become decentralized.

The new decentralization legislation did not devolve appropriate tax bases to sub-national
governments. Inadequate decentralization o f taxes has constrained sub-national governments'
ability to gauge demand for local services and has weakened accountability linkages. Instead
of decentralizing typically local taxes (such as the property tax), the center allowed sub-
national governments to create their own new taxes and charges, under certain conditions.
Regional governments have created an abundance o f new taxes and charges, many o f which
appear, inpractice, to be either nuisances or economically harmful.

Sub-national governments gained access to significant shared revenues from natural resources
and the personal income tax under the new decentralization framework. But given the unequal
geographic distribution o f these resource bases, the allocation o f attendant revenues across
regional governments proved very unequal.

The vast bulk o f funding for most sub-national governments under decentralization derived
from the new general allocation fund (DAU). The DAU was intended to equalize fiscal
capacities across sub-national governments. But the prior inequitable distribution o f shared
revenue, a number o f faulty design features o f the DAU itself, and parliament's insistence on
instituting a "hold harmless" provision prohibited it from doing so very effectively. As a

                                               83

result, fiscal inequalities among regional governments have become exacerbated under
decentralization.

The objective o f the Government's newly designed special purpose grant, DAK, was not
clearly articulated and DAK funding was and continues to be limitedinmagnitude. The DAK
constitutes an underutilized component of the intergovernmental transfer system.

Sub-national borrowing came to a near halt during the initial years o f decentralization. For the
most part, this was due to the collapse o f the Government's traditional on-lending mechanism,
which resulted from uncertainty regarding its legal status as outlined in Law 25/1999. As a
direct result, sub-national government investment in public infrastructure has declined
precipitously.

Bank's AssessmentRecommendations

The two new decentralization laws have only recently come into effect and they are not yet
fully operational, as noted. As such, it is too earlyto make firmjudgmentson many important
matters. A number o f issues integral to the revisions are at least worthy o f note, however.

Most positively, the new legislation provides for the direct election o f sub-national
government executives. This will surely help support the development o f greater
accountability at the local level, inthe longrun.

Law 32/2004 outlines broad areas o f obligatory regional government service responsibility but
does not actually identify specific service responsibilities across levels o f government.
Confusingly, the general expenditure assignments for provinces and kabupatedkota indicated
inthe law are nearlyidentical. The assignment ofexpenditures willreceive further attentionin
a forthcoming government regulation.

Sub-national governments were not granted authority over additional local tax bases under the
new decentralization legislation. However, in an attempt to curtail the proliferation o f
nuisance and harmful taxes and charges, Law 33/2004 insists that regions should not employ
revenue instruments that cause a "high cost economy" and/or that constrain the mobility o f
goods and factors between regions. Inaddition, Law 32/2004 stipulates that regions will now
be requiredto obtain approval from the center before authorizing new revenues. Revisions to
Law 34/00 are expected to further curtail regions' authority to create nuisance and
economically harmful taxes. The development o f monitoring and enforcement mechanisms
will be integralto the success ofthe newregulations.

Law 33/2004 extends revenue sharing somewhat, by adding a new source o f shared revenue-
geothermal mining-and      by setting slightly higher regional shares for oil and gas revenues.
More positively, the Government has developed a mechanism to distribute windfall gains in
the gas and oil sectors across all sub-national governments, as opposed to just allocating those
revenues to producing regions. This should help to equalize fiscal capacities across local
governments to a moderate extent.




                                                84

Law 33/04 provides for the possibility o f a more equalizing DAU, by making provisions for
removing the hold harmless condition, for example. This and other envisioned improvements
to the distribution mechanism are not slated to be carried out until 2008, however. In the
meantime, the DAU will, first and foremost, be used to fund the full regional wage bill,
thereby creating unfortunate incentives for overstaffing and leaving little space for fiscal
equalization.

The new legislation stipulates that current sectoral department development spending on sub-
national tasks will be turned into increased DAK funding. The law notes that this will be done
ina gradual manner andthat mechanisms to operationalize the policywill be developed later.
This i s to be the subject of forthcoming government regulationon deconcentrated spending.

Law 33/2004 lays the groundwork for regional governments to borrow from a wide range o f
domestic sources, including via the issuance o f revenue bonds, thereby potentially reducing
sub-national governments' dependence on the center to finance capital development, at least
in the long run. Relevant regulatory issues, including work-out procedures, have yet to be
addressed in detail. Inthe meantime, sub-national government borrowing i s likely to remain
restricted to that from the central government and, in particular, the center's on-lending
mechanism. Inorder to operationalize on-lending, KMK 35/03 will have to be revised.

BACKGROUND

The World Bank has conducted a fiduciary assessmentfor this SecondDPL. The assessment
included a review of the progress made inimplementingthe first DPL approvedinyear 2004,
andthe progress inpreparing for implementation o fthe.World Bank-financedGovernment
Financial Management andRevenue Administration Project (GFMRAP)that was approvedby
the Board inJune 2004, and inputs from the ADB, AusAID and the IMF.

Past deficiencies in Indonesia's PFM systems have been well documented. Various donor
diagnostic studies led by the CFAA in 2001 and IMF reports over the years have outlined
deficiencies in some detail. These were acknowledged to a large degree by the Government in
their White Paper on Public Financial Management reforms that was prepared in 2002. The
major acknowledged deficiencies have included:
    e  An outdated legal environment for PFM
    e  An opaque and inefficient budget formulation process, including the separation of
       recurrent anddevelopment budgets
    e  Difficulties in implementing the budget, due to an inefficient payments system and
       weak monitoring arrangements
       Fragmentedcash management and government banking arrangements
    e  Fragmented and unreliable accounting andreporting systems
    e  Inadequate auditing o f government sectors, and overlaps between external and internal
        audit agencies, reflecting unclear roles and responsibilities.


The Government has articulated an objective o f having a "state o f the art" PFM system in
place by year 2008. The reform agenda i s thus now clearly being ledby the Government, and
received good support from the previous Government as well as the current one that has been
inoffice forjust about oneyear.

The preparation and successful negotiation o f the GFMRAP Project in 2004 in some ways
marked a key transition inthe country - a transition from the completion o f analytical work by
various donors on the content and sequencing o f needed financial management reforms and
the development a comprehensive reform program, to the completion o f important initial steps
to put the reforms into effect and lock in the future path o f these reforms. The completed
analytical work included studies such as the CFAA and the Government's White paper laying
out the road map for PFM reforms, and the important initial steps taken include passage o f a
troika of laws (on State Finances, State Treasury and State Audit) in2003 and 2004 providing
an adequate architecture to modernize public financial management and oversight and the
reorganization o f the Ministry o f Finance. The successful negotiation of the GFMRAP project
also provided a vehicle to lock in the reforms under a wider umbrella that funds the required
investments as well as technical assistance. A summary o f the specific recommendations
made underthe CFAA and their current status are attached.

CURRENT STATUS OF PFMREFORM-A SUMMARY

The most notable achievements recently accomplished are summarized below.
As stated above, major progress has occurred in establishingthe legal framework for
improved PFM, and in the restructuring of MOF. Other important changes in the
classification and structure o fthe budget also occurred in2005 budget.

TheLaw on State Finances (2003), although at a highlevel o f generality, provides for the first
time for such basic budget processes as developing a macro-economic and fiscal framework,
establishing a budget timetable, setting out the budget information to be supplied to
Parliament and the internationally recognized classification standards to be used indeveloping
the budget. Reforms beingundertaken in the budget formulation area include the unification
o f the previous two separate budgets, the development o f an MTEF system and the
introduction o f performance based budgeting

The State Development Planning System Law (2004) provides the legal basis for the national
development planning process, and for the linking o fplanning with budgeting.

The State Treasury Law (2004) provides the basis for modernizing budget execution and
reporting. Treasury reforms being undertaken include steps to rationalize the current
proliferation o f government bank accounts and to centralize cash management, simplify and
speed up the payments system, and to improve reporting on budget execution. Government
financial reporting will also be improved through the implementation o f government
accounting standards on a modified accrual basis, but with an eventual move to full accrual
accounting.

The State Audit Law (2004) lays out the broad legal framework for the operation on the
country's supreme audit institution, BPK to reinforce its position and mandate as an external
audit institution reporting to Parliament.

Procurement Decree (2003) introduces many improvements in the procurement regime in
Indonesia and lays out a timetable for establishing a national policy formulation and oversight
agency. An omnibus procurement law is envisaged for consolidating, clarifying and
simplifylngthe numerous procurement rules andregulations.

All o f these new laws are required to be supported by detailed regulations to guide their
implementation. Considerable work has been done in developing these regulations, in
particular the regulations under the Finance Law, although hrther work i s required to develop
and issue implementing regulations in a number o f areas under treasury management and
audit.

Key regulations finalized include ones for Work Plan and Work Plan and Budget, Special
Purpose Agencies, Government Accounting Standards, Budget Execution (Ministerial decree
for 2005 Budget). Those under preparation include on Procurement, On-lending, Cash
Management, Grants andEmergencyFunds,MTEF and Government auditing standards.

Another major development has been the restructuring of MOF following Presidential
decrees issued in2004, inparticular to create a new DGBudget and Fiscal Balance and a new
DGTreasury, broadly inline with previous IMFrecommendations. Appointments were made
at the beginningo f 2005 to DG and director positions. Both the budget and treasury functions
have been strengthened by these changes. Inparticular DGTreasury now oversees all treasury
functions including cash and debt management, fixed asset management and government
fiscal reporting.

The key challenge is to move beyond the development of appropriate laws and
regulations to their effective implementation.The FMRC will also need to develop some
priorities for this implementation to avoid reform overload and effectively involve spending
ministries and agencies and Parliament in the PFM reform process, which does not appear to
be well understoodoutside o f a small group o fM O F and Bappenas officials.

The current position concerningmajor PFMreform activities are discussed below:

BudgetReforms

These new measures that have been introducedthrough the new State FinanceLaw are
expected to significantly improve fiscal transparency. Budget documents will become
more comprehensive and informative. This will include among others: (i) new economic

envisaged under performance budgeting, will also be required.


Treasury Reforms

Budget execution and public resource management are being modernized. The new legal
framework for comprehensive management o f public resources will allow the Government to
manage its financial assets, debt and cash more efficiently and effectively. A functional
reorganization o f the M O F i s under way.      This will strengthen accountability, eliminate
overlap and duplication o f functions, and enable the Ministryto focus more effectively on its
core functions. The consolidation o f budget development activities in the new DG Budget
and Fiscal Balance, and consolidating budget execution functions (including cash
management and fiscal reporting) in the new DG Treasury, has clarified mandates and
strengthened accountability. The complementary ongoing reengineering o f business processes
i s envisaged to introduce checks and balances, and diminish the potential for rent-seekingthat
hitherto characterized virtually all stages o fbudget formulation and execution inIndonesia.

Some very necessary organizational rationalization has been undertaken to support
rationalization of the treasury system. A new Directorate of Treasury has been established
under the reorganization o f the Ministry of Finance. Regional accounting offices (KAR) and
local verification offices (KASPA) have been merged with regional administrative offices
(KANWIL) under DG Treasury, with treasury payment and accounting field offices (KPPNs)
coming under KANWILs. However progress so far on the ground in the simplification o f
payments processing has been somewhat limited so far. While there has been training for
M O F staff this has not yet extended to spending ministrystaff, who thus lack awareness o f the
proposed changes. New processing procedures have yet to be fully developed and tested.

The shortcomings in the implementation arrangements for these changes have been reflected
inthe chronic delays that have beenexperienced inthe issue by the Treasury Department of
Budget authorization documents (DIPA) government wide this year.

Going forward, the introductionof a Treasury Single Account (TSA) and automationof
the treasurypayment systemare key reforms.Progress inthis areahas been slow, although
a detailed work plan for this has now been developed, especially for the establishment o f the
Treasury Single Account (TSA) which involves the progressive rationalization o f the vast
number o f separate government bank accounts. Substantial idle cash balances continue. As
part of improving cash management there i s a need to reduce delays in revenue remittances
into government bank accounts. An IMF peripatetic advisor will be working with DG
Treasury in MOF over the next three years to facilitate progress. Treasury cash management
and payment modernization will be supported by the planned computerized treasury
information management system (SPAN) underthe World Bank GFMRAPproject.

In the area of fiscal reporting,the requirementof the State Treasury law of the complete
introduction of accrual accounting by 2008 is very ambitious. The transition to the
accruals based GFS 2001 needs to be gradual, recognizing its dependence on an operational
TSA, a double entry ledger system allowing for the regular reconciliation o f accounting and
bank information and on the new SPAN system.

The developmentand adoption ofnew GovernmentAccounting Standardsis a good step
forward to improvefinancialreporting. The Accounting Standards Committee (KSAP)has
been inoperation for a few years now duringwhich time they have put inplace a due process
for development o f Government accounting standards that are well aligned with comparable
international standards (IPSA'S).An initial set o f 11 government accounting standards have
been formally adopted through the issue o f a Presidential Decree (No. 25/2005). These
standards are based on a modified accrual approach and cover a budget implementation report,
overall cash flow statement and supplementary information on various assets and liabilities, to
be elaborated in a balance sheet. Each ministry and agency i s requiredto prepare a financial
accountability statement, based on these standards. Implementing these new standards across
government will require extensive training as well as some re-engineering o f related business
processes in line ministries and agencies. This task will impose challenges, given inherently
weak capacities inMinistries.

Audit Issues

The BPK - the country's supreme audit institution now has a stronger mandate. The

new Audit Law strengthens the role o f BPK and clarifies its role across all three levels o f
government. The BPK has independence in determining the scope o f audits. It can audit all
government entities and operations as it deems appropriate. In addition, public disclosure o f
all BPK audit reports i s now mandatory. It appears that overlap and duplication on audit
mandates between BPK and BPKP and Inspectors General remains to be resolved
satisfactorily. Inthe past BPK has received technical assistance under a Bank financed project
financed  that closed in 2004. More attention i s required to improving the resources and
capacity o f BPK, which has not in the past had adequate resources to fulfill its mandate. On
the other hand, BPKP's mandate has diminished inboth at the national and sub-national levels

of government, and its relationship with BPK remains unclear. Overall, audit arrangements
remain weak and a program for State Audit Reform (STAR) financed by ADB i s under

KEY AREAS FORFUTURE ATTENTION-PUBLIC FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT

While progress in implementing reforms has been slow, to some degree that has been due to
the natural disaster in Aceh and North Sumatra. Coping with the Tsunami distracted the
attention o f many parts o f the national government, including the Ministry o f Finance. Inthe
period ahead, progress o f the following key on-going PFM reforms will need to be closely
watched inthe period ahead, to update the fiduciary environment inthe country:

        Ensuring greater integrity and more effective execution o f public expenditures,
        through the issue and implementation o f regulations for the State Finances, Treasury
        and Audit laws, establishing a Treasury Single Account, automating the treasury
        payments system;

        Streamlining public procurement through the establishment o f a National Procurement
        Policy Office (NPPO);
        Strengthening public expenditure oversight by enhancing the capacity o f the SAI,
        strengthening legislative capacity for oversight o f budget execution, and eliminating
        the overlap ininternal audit entities' mandates;

        Increasing revenue collection and eliminating revenue leakages through governance-
        and accountability-enhancing reforms to the tax and customs administrations;

        Implementation of a government financial management information system and
        successful implementation o f Government Accounting Standards.

        The Government has agreed to lock inmany o fthese reforms with support from
         donors, under the umbrella o f the World Bank-financed GFMRAP. While the first
         phase will focus on strengthening the public financial management system,
         subsequent phases are envisaged to support both public financial management and
         revenue administration.


The following additional issues should desirably be addressed by the authorities as part o f the
PFMreformprogram:

a) Integrating off-budget operations into the budget.

Budget comprehensiveness is still inadequate. While the full extent and magnitude of off
budget operations are not known the institutions involved include various semi government
foundations, activities o f the Housing Fund and the Petroleum account, RDI and RDA and
militaryenterprises. A first step will be for MOFthrough DGBudget to establish an inventory
of these institutions and then to develop a phased plan for their integration into the
consolidated budget.


                                               91

b) Improving Fiscal Reporting by Sub-National Governments (SNGs).

SNGs now account for about one third o f total fiscal activity. Three problems prevent M O F
from compiling adequate consolidated fiscal reports on SNG. SNG do not inall cases provide
the required information (their approved budgets and quarterly budget execution reports) to
MOF (and spending ministries), what information is provided is often o f poor quality and
there are two separate and different reporting requirements, promulgated by MOF and M O H A
respectively. The reporting requirements need to be rationalized and mechanisms for
enforcement developed.

c) Improving Financial Monitoring of State Owned Enterprises

A special program o f SOE audits is being carried out by BPK. However there is a need to
develop a systematic approach by MOF and relevant sectoral ministries to have SOEs operate
to financial and other performance targets, with reliable, timely and transparent reporting o f
their results.

A modernized State Treasury and Budget System (SPAN) would be the central element o f
financial  management and accounting operations inIndonesia. The SPAN will:

    a) Include core functional requirements for the execution components o f government
         budget through an integrated accounting system. In general, this would include
         recording of revenue collections, appropriations, commitments, verifications, and fund
         allocations leading to payment (covering accounts payable, accounts receivable,
         general ledger operations, and report generation);
    b) Record and process all state budget transactions and incorporate aggregate data from
         other elements o f general government as rapidly as possible;

    c) Record all relevant stages of the payments and receipts processes and enable reports to
         be made on key variables, such as commitments, funds available, bills outstanding and
         payments and receipts (both cash and accruals basis); for individual agencies and at
         various levels o f aggregation;

    d) Incorporate controls on any specified variables as necessary, for instance to ensure that
         commitments do not exceed available funds;

    e) Generate standard monthly reports and provide query facilities to determine the
         budgetaryposition o f agencies, the state budget and the general government budget;

    f) Maintain data according to the budget classification for as many past years as required,
         and allow projections o f up to three years;
    g) Facilitate reconciliation between treasury system accounts and banking data by
         generating chronological lists o f transactions in a form comparable to those generated
         bythebanks; and
    h) Ensure compatibility of the system with other systems, such as those for tax and
         customs administrations. The treasury system will also support the budget preparation
         process.

improving these programs and designing increased effective poverty reduction
programs. While the launching o f these new programs aimed at the poor are well-
intentioned, it will be important for Government to closely monitor the early implementation
of these programs. In particular, as the programs are largely supply-side driven, it will be
important to ensure that the needed provision o f additional funds to front-line providers

are well-known in Latin America (e.g. Mexico, Brazil) could (i)      supplement income o f the
chronic poor while also (ii)  fostering key human capital enhancements needed for Indonesia
to achieve its stated development goals. A CCT program may also provide the opportunity to
consolidate some of the supply-side driven programs, such as those in place, with demand
side interventions. This would enable the concurrent removal o f supply and demand side
constraints when either are binding, while also increasing the accountability o f service
provision units to clients who hold additional funds.

99.      Moving forward, the Government aims to rise to the huge opportunity and

social protection policy that better matches programs to actual risks and vulnerabilities faced
by the poor. Related to these strategic actions, the Government is aware that moving from a
generalized regressive subsidy, such as the fuel subsidy, to more targeted interventions to
benefit the poor will require a fine-tuned targeting system. While an important step in this
direction has beentaken in2005 with the development o f a "poverty database," there is scope
for improving the accuracy o f this database over time, and the Government intends to assess
the current targeting scheme and improve on the system. Furthermore, as noted above, a
critical first step in improving the effectiveness o f its current but new programs to achieve

The 2006 budget i s likely to be approved on October 27-28.37 The main issue i s how to
allocate financial resources, especially saving from fuel price increase in October, efficiently
across key expenditure items. The government aims at further achieving fiscal consolidation
with budget deficits are 0.7 percentage points o f GDP from 0.9 percentage points in the
second revision o f 2005 budget.

international and domestic macroeconomic situations (Table 1). Oil prices (Indonesian crude
oil prices) are set at US$57/bbl from US$40/bbl in the proposal inAugust. Also, due to he1
price increase in October, inflation projection i s increased to 8.0 percent from 7.0 percent.
Accordingly, SBI 3 month interest rates are revised up to 9.5 percent from 8.0 percent.
Growth rate projection i s almost unchanged at 6.1 percent.
