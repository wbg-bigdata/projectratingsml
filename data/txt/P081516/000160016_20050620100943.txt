Project developmentobjective:
The proposed Honduras Judicial Branch Modernization Project will support implementation of
the Judicial Branch Modernization Plan 2004-2009. The project aims to improve the capacity
and performance of the Judicial Branch in three areas: (a) greater efficiency of case processing,
judgments and appeals, (b) enhanced transparency and accountability and (c) better access to
justice, especially for the most disadvantagedgroups.

accountability and transparency standards.
C.PromotingEquitableAccess toJustice to address shortagesanddisparities injustice services,
especially for the disadvantaged.
D. Project Coordination, Monitoring and Evaluation to strengthen institutional capability,
mobilization for change, oversight of the modernization program process, and donor
coordination.

A. STRATEGICCONTEXTAND RATIONALE

1.CountryandSectorIssues

GeneralContext

Honduras has a populationof 7 million, of which 45 percent are less than 15 years of age. Its social
indicators are among the worst in the region, especially in the areas of poverty, corruption and
crime. Two-thirds of the population subsist on less than 1 US$ per day. The economy has
experiencedslow growth andthe cost of doing business is among the highest inthe region.

To help redress these problems, the Government of Honduras (GoH) has targeted the need for a
more effective judicial system, which it views as an essential element of its Poverty Reduction
Strategy (PRS). It recognizes that Honduras' ineffective legal framework and judicial institutions
negatively impact the investment climate and worsen conditions for the poor.

The Government has already initiated measures aimed at relieving these problems. Past judicial
reform efforts (largely funded by other donors) have largely focused on the promotion of human
rights, the strengthening of democratic institutions, the provision of justice infrastructure, and
reforming the structure and content of criminal procedures. It also recently introduced oral trials in
criminal courts to expedite case processing.But other areas (civil, commercial, family and labor-
which constitute about 60 percent of the caseload) have received little attention.

These reform efforts have resulted in uneven improvements and suffered from a lack of integrated
vision and focus on users. Institutionalcapabilities remain weak, with disparity in the provision of
judicial services between rural and urban areas and between criminal and civiljurisdictions, and a
lack of attention to the specific needs of users, especially vulnerable groups. Citizen demand,
availability of information, and confidence are low. The proposed Project aims to target some of
these institutional deficiencies.

The Government has therefore begun to move more towards judicial modernization by building
ownership, developing skills, encouraging transparency and participation, and bringing justice
closer to the people. It sees the needto strengthenits role as the neutral arbiter of social andprivate
disputesandthe guarantor of the rule of law.

Strengthening the administration of justice is now a sharedpriority of the government andjudicial
policy makers, the business community, and civil society groups, due largely to a new dynamism
for change led by the recently appointed Supreme Court and supported by the other branches of
government and civil society. The Supreme Court has prepared a Judicial Branch Modernization
Plan (JBMP) 2004-2009 with Bank support, adopting a participatory methodology actively
involvingjudges, court staff and inputs from NGOs and user surveys, so as to expand ownership,
reduce resistanceto change and provide a soundtechnical basis for judicial reform.

The country's PRS highlights actions to address the needs of the most vulnerable groups and to

Weak Governance Transparency. Honduras ranked 114 out of 145 countries in Transparency
International's "Corruption Perception Index" for 2004.6 Twenty percent of all Hondurans believe
that they have been victims of corruption, and many believe that corruption is one of their most
important national problems. The PRS emphasizes improving governance and participatory
democratic processes, including guaranteeing the transparency of public administration having a
legal system that guarantees the protection of human rights, security for investment and the
equitable application and enforcement of the law.

Constitutional reforms in 2002 promoted judicial independence, and strengthened government
accountability and checks and balances. However, the extent of corruption in the public sector is
such that it i s seen as an `institutionalized' program, deeply ingrained in the functioning of the
state.7 A lack of transparency in governance undermines the credibility of state institutions and
fosters the perception amongst the population that, in order to safeguard their rights and interests,
they have to go through illegal channels.

JudicialBranchChallenges(seeAnnexes)

General Organization. The Judiciary employs 616 judges, and about 3300 support staff. Of the
judges, 335 are justices of the peace, of whom about half are non-lawyers. There are about 71,000
new cases per year (2004), of which approximately 30 percent are criminal, and 50 percent civil
andfamily. About half of the cases are concentratedinSanPedro Sula andTegucigalpa.

User Profile. Menrepresent 64 percent of those involvedin labor and criminal disputes. Women
represent 62 percent of the plaintiffs infamily courts. Women experience problemsof all types-
domestic violence, family related problems, violations of their labor rights and as victims of
criminal offenses. In2004, child support and divorce cases comprised the majority of family court
claims, makingup 40 and 36 percent of total family court cases, respectively.

1.Weak institutionalcapabilities

Operationalweaknesses are indicated inthe courts' inabilitiesto resolve cases ina timely manner.
The majority of civil, family and labor cases take over two years to resolve. Significant backlogs
are also apparent in criminal cases. It is estimated that over 70,000 cases are still pending from
before the criminal procedure reforms of 2002. Rigid procedures for handling filings or inquiries

Court quality, decision preparation, and enforcement. Two studies in 2003 of the processing
times and satisfaction levels of users in civil, labor and family cases showed that the longest
processingtime inthe sample was almost 9 years. The shortest was just over 2 months; the median
was below 3.6 years. Family cases are typically resolved the fastest, whilejuicios ejecutivos and
ordinary civil cases take the longest. The procedural phase shown to take the longest i s
sentencing,with the criteria for scheduling sentencing hearings are not clearly established. Judges

decisions suffers from a lack of supervision and coordination with other institutions. In cases of
asset seizure, the study showed an average delay of 7 months between the filing of a claim andthe
notificationof seizure, and of 20 months betweenthe filing and decision.

Judicial statistics. Honduras' courts are behind the international curve in the use of statistics to
monitor and evaluate performance. While the judiciary captures statistics with a high amount of
detail on courtroom workflow-more         than in more highly automated judiciaries-it     relies on
manual submissions. A preliminary review suggests problems with accuracy, completeness, and
consistency of entries.

Judicial careers and oversight. Hiringmechanismsneedimprovement. Current training programs
for judges are inadequate, with entry-level training aimed solely at keepingjudges up to date with
reforms in criminal procedural code. There is no systematic training of administrative or support
staff. Judges have limited knowledge of issues related to business resolution, corruption or
mismanagement in government procurement, violation of property rights, labor and environmental
laws. Moreover the Judicial Branch i s unprepared to deal with needs arising from international
trade agreements.

Disciplinary oversight and inspection systems are inadequate for accountability. Current
procedures of the Court Inspections Office are cumbersome. Many inspectors lack basic audit
skills.

Weak infrastructure. The physical infrastructure i s inadequate. Its spaces are poorly distributed or
insufficient, especially for the needs of oral procedures in courts and accommodating information
and communication technologies. Areas that especially suffer include the judicial library,
document and citizen information centers, the judicial school for judges and staff, the
consolidation of family and youth courts in Tegucigalpa, public defender services, and offices for
the administration and oversight of the judicial career. There has been some limited progress in
technology andinformationsystems.

statistical capabilities. The Judiciary also has no integrated program of orientation for users, and

2. Weak transparency and Accountability

Corruption.*Perceptions of corruption in the judiciary system are widespread. In a 2001 survey
approximately two-thirds of respondents who had contact with the courts reported that they had
been asked at some time to pay a bribe. Ten percent said that they knew someone who had paid a
bribe in the courts. Fifty percent of firms and 63 percent of users considered the judicial system
unfair andjudges not to be ~redible.~ overwhelming majority of respondents to a more recent

80 percent of users agreedthat briberyis neededfor a speedyjudicial process.

Citizen Orientation. Inrecent years the Judicial Branch has taken steps to promote greater public
dialogue aboutjustice administration. A Coalition for StrengtheningJustice has beenestablished in
partnership with NGOs and business and labor associations, among others. The Coalition has
participated in dialogues over recent reforms such as the new Criminal Procedural Code, the
Judicial Career Law and the Judicial Council Law. Postponement of the approval of the latter by
Congress resulted from a consensus among the Coalition to provide broader public discussion of
the proposed law. There i s a need to create institutional mechanisms to support still greater
dialogue aboutjustice administration issues, and to informusers aboutjudicial procedures.

Inter-institutional Coordination. The weaknesses in coordination of information among sector
institutions are affecting the fight against corruption within andoutside the sector.

3. LimitedAccess to Justice

The weaknesses in the capabilities of the Judicial Branch, inadequate geographical coverage in
rural areas, low citizen confidence, economic and cultural barriers, and a lack of information about
rights constitute significant barriers to effective access to justice. Vulnerable groups at the justice
of the peace andfirst instance levels suffer the most from these deficiencies.

Justice ofthe Peace Courts. Between January 2002 and December 2003 the proportion ofjustices
with legal training (Zetrudos) increased from 33 to 48 percent, but these courts continue to fall
short of meeting the demand and although civil procedural norms dictate orality in procedures, in
practice justices report that these tend to continue to be written.1° The need to improve court
productivity i s made all the more imperative by the fact that justices of the peace represent 57
percent of the total number of judges inthe country. These courts in 2004 handled 12,462 cases of
a total caseload of 71,000, meaning that 57 percent of the country's judges handle about 17 percent

Alternative Dispute Resolution Mechanisms. Honduras i s implementing a Conciliation and
Arbitration Law that, promotes the creation of mediation centers in the chambers of commerce.
However, the law has not had a significant impact. Conciliation i s used with some frequency in
labor cases and the new Criminal Procedural Code includes provision for conciliation, but it i s too
early to gauge their impacts. The use of conciliation and mediation in civil cases is limited to
nonexistent, even though the legal framework is in place. A lack of support mechanisms is one
major constraint: there are no formal procedures or trained personnel to guide parties through the
process. This situation i s exacerbated by a widespread skepticism among professionals about the
benefits of ADR mechanisms.

Public Defenders. According to available data on 2004, there are 230 public defenders nationally
(133 women, 97 men) supported by 73 staff. Their mediancaseloadi s relatively low at around 33.
However, since the distribution of demand is highly variable across the country, public defenders
are assigned as needed to every criminal courthouse resultingin uneven workload. Additionally,
public defenders services must attend to active cases still governed by the previous Criminal
Procedural Code. Due to insufficient numbers, the Public Defender's Office cannot provide
services to all defendants who lack the means to obtain a lawyer. This situation points to a need to
expand and increase the specialization of the Public Defender's Office. There i s also a need for
specialized traininginparticular areas of the law, such asjuvenile justice.

Gender and Justice Services. Domestic violence i s generally perceived in Honduran society and
byjustice sector operators as the most important legal problem affecting women. In 2002, nearly
16 percent of Honduran women between the ages of 15 and 45 years of age reported having
suffered domestic violence at some point. Judicial statistics for 2003 show that 8,687 cases of
domestic violence entered first instance courts and 2,216 more were presented to justices of the
peace. The Law Against Domestic Violence has existed since 1998 but has not been enforced
effectively.

Family related conflicts rank as the second most important legal problem affecting women in
Honduras. Women represent 62 percent of plaintiffs in family courts. In 2004, child support and
divorce cases comprised the majority of family court claims. While both men and women are
plaintiffs in divorce proceedings, women constitute the majority of plaintiffs in child support
claims. Women are also active litigants inpaternity recognition cases. And while menare the main
users of labor courts (64 percent), women experience serious violations of labor rights for which
neither the courts nor the government provide adequate service.

Indigenous Populations and Justice Services. In a recent survey, 75 percent of ethnic minorities
expressed distrust in the justice sector. Local communities prefer to resolve civil disputes where
possible by "informal" justice. The majority of court cases involving ethnic populations are

therefore criminal cases. Consultations with judges in Departments with ethnic populations
identifieda lack of information on the operation of the courts inlocal languages and the need for
cultural sensitivity trainingfor judges and staff inthese areas.

Youth and Justice Services. Youth crime and violence and the protection of the rights of minors
are growing problems. Increasing crime and public insecurity are typically blamed by the media
for the growing youth violence phenomenon, (although statistics indicate that crimes committed by
minors are comparatively few.)" A popular perception i s that judicial and law enforcement
services are mishandling the situation. Reports of extra-judicial violence against Mum members
are prevalent, and there i s a growing recognition that the enforcement of existing legislation does
not adequately protect the rightsof youths or gang members. Legalaid and public defense services
are inadequate, as are the social services attachedto the courts.

Small Businessess. According to an Investment Climate Assessment, 39 percent of firms in
Honduras do not believe that the courts will upholdtheir contract and property rights in a dispute.
According to the survey, 50 percent of SMEs would rather handle disputes outside the courts.
Micro and small firms-which              bear the brunt of crime and ineffective judicial practices-trust              the
Judiciary less than their larger counterparts. New legal frameworks and regional trade agreements
may continue to widen the gap between the Judiciary's ability to deliver quality services and the
increasing specialization of businessusers' needs.12

2. Rationalefor Bankinvolvement

The Government recognizes that its ineffective legal framework andjudicial institutions negatively
impact its investment climate and worsen conditions for the poor. The GOH and Supreme Court
seek Bank assistance in its judicial modernization efforts in order to bring the Bank's global
experience to bear on these problems. The authorities seek to leverage the Bank's know-how in
designing and implementing successful judicial reform strategies in Central America and
elsewhere. Members of the Honduran Supreme Court have expressed their desire to emulate these
efforts.

Another part of the justification for Bank involvement i s the needto balance and rationalize overall
external assistanceto thejudicial sector. Other donors continue to provide sizable resources.These
have largely focused on the promotion of human rights, strengthening democratic institutions,
judicial infrastructure and on criminal cases. These efforts have been important, but they have
produced uneven results-including              a lack of development of the civil law side-and                have been
insufficient to improve institutionalcapabilities or satisfy citizens' demands. Simultaneously, there
i s no Judicial Branch machinery in place to avoid overlapping or conflictinguses of these funds.

4. LessonsLearned

Integral view and targeting matter. Experience has demonstrated that piecemeal analysis i s not
suited to the complexities of judicial reform. The challenges faced by Judiciaries are crosscutting
and institutional needs are typically vast in scope. The very nature of justice systems and the
cumbersome institutions that administer themmean that any modernization initiative will therefore
need to be comprehensive. While the design and planning of modernization programs need to be
targeted in focus, the bigger picture needs to be considered. In the Honduran context this has
meant ensuringcomplementarities with ongoing reform activities, and targeting results where they
are needed and where real dividends can be felt by end users of the system.

Participatory analysis and reviews matter. The participatory methodology of project development
reflects the fact that stakeholder consultation i s a process and not an event. Reforms affect many
stakeholders with different perceptions, objectives and expectations. As a consequence, there i s a
need for an evolving consensus on objectives and how to reach them. The proposed project has
benefited from a participatory assessment that has considered institutional needs and stakeholder
priorities, perceptions of users and vulnerable groups, past institutional reform activities
undertaken by various agencies in the sector, as well as past and current assistance from
international agencies. These reviews have resulted in the development of the ModernizationPlan
Participatory assessments have included stakeholder workshops with judges and judicial
administrators and analysis of the perceptions of users, especially vulnerable groups. (see Annexes
10, and 15,19). The Project has also benefited from ICA 2004 and WBI governance assessments,
andWorld Bank report "Poor People's Knowledge: PromotingIntellectual Property inDeveloping
Countries,'' as well as the ongoing LandAdministration, the Economic andFinancial Management,
and the Poverty Reduction Support Technical Assistance projects. The proposed Judicial Branch
Modernization Project plans to continue this collaboration during implementation so as to build
synergies, poolresourceswhere possible, andenhance results on the ground.

Implementation and mobilization support matters. Past reform efforts have shown that the best
intentions, the right resources and sound project design will not deliver the desired results if




                                                   14

implementation capacity is not adequate or taken into account in the sequencing of activities and
the planning of short and medium term results. Thorough assessments during project preparation
of conditions on the ground have enabled the proposed Project design to capitalize on existing
strengths, make accommodations for weaknesses and incorporate measures to strengthen
implementation capacity, and help mobilize stakeholders. Component of the project includes
assistance for resource mobilization and development of core staff skills injudicial modernization
to directly help address capacity needs. (See Annex 6.)

5. Alternatives considered and reasons for rejection
Development Policy Loan (DPL) versus TechnicalAssistance Credit (TAC). There are still some
weaknesses in Honduras' judicial system which could be overcome by further legislative changes
andpolicy decisions. However, the constitutional reforms of 2002 andrecent policy declarations of
the Supreme Court have already produced substantial improvements in the country's legal
framework. B y comparison, the Judiciary faces far more pressing difficulties to satisfy the public's
needs and demands for improved services. Inorder to help meet these requirements, the proposed
Credit i s more oriented to helping the Judicial Branch to accomplish these service improvements
on the ground, rather than to further refine its policy framework. This mainly calls for technical
support and assistancefor institutionalimprovements.

Focus on demand side or supply side of justice. A mixed approach is needed. Therefore the
proposed Project contemplates combining capacity buildingand providing better access to justice.
An emphasis inproject design on strengthening transparency and anti corruption mechanisms will
help lay the foundation for building confidence of users. A demand side focus ensures that
measuresto strengthen the delivery ofjudicial services are focused on the needs of end users of the
system, in particular vulnerable groups. In order to address the problems of individual
communities, the Government recognizes that establishing ADR mechanisms and reorganizing the
operation of the justice of the peace courts is needed with some urgency. The proposed Project
would support identifying current and future institutional supply and demand of ADR while
simultaneously strengthening existing services and human resources.

Judicial Branch versus other Justice Sector Institutions. The proposed Project would
simultaneously be conducted with a number of other donor activities addressing other deficiencies
inthe sector. The approach is to complement resources and efforts to ensure improvement inthe
sector with an integrated vision.

Anti-corruption Prevention versus Control. The proposed Project contemplates mobilizing citizen
information as a preventive anti-corruption tool through greater accountability and transparency.
While eliminating corruption requires a hybrid (public sector wide) approach that includes
strengthening control mechanisms and checks and balances in the system, the design of the
proposedProject focuses on prevention as key to the sustainability of the strategy. This approach is
in line with GoH anti-corruption strategy and is founded on the belief that fostering greater
openness and accountability in the judicial system will help restore credibility and serve as a
deterrent to corrupt practices inthe long term.

 Economic Benefits: The improved delivery of judicial services and other Project outputs
 would help lower the costs of doing business inHonduras, as well as reduce the time now required
 for carrying out litigation and clearance of cases. These outputs are expected to result in the
 country's laws being interpreted and enforced in a more predictable and uniform manner.

 strengthen deterrents to illegal practices elsewhere in Honduras' governance. These and the
 attendant enhancementof businessconfidence would improve the investment climate. They are
 likely as well to encourage participants in Honduras' large informal economy to move closer
 towards regulated formal economic activity.

 Fiscal Impact: Typically, judicial development activities are not subject to conventional financial
 analysis since a substantial part of their results are related to improved social outcomes. These are
 difficult to quantify and to relate to financial benefits, especially over precise periods. But despite
 these limitations, the fiscal impact of the Project i s considered to be relatively modest and
 sustainable. In the short run, the Project requires counterpart financing which should be covered

2. Technical
The technical design of the proposed Project is a combined strategy of institutional capacity
building, improved transparency, and targeted measures for enhanced access for users.
International experiencesinjudicial modernization have shown that such a combined approach can
reap the greatest benefits for end users of judicial services. Strengthening operational capacities
will require integral measures to increase the efficiency of the courts and of institutional
management. These measures will involve scaling up capacities, including technological
infrastructure, for a long-term vision of sustainable modernization. Enhancing transparency
mechanisms would include better checks and balances in the system and greater civil society
participation. This strategy has been designed in line with the Borrower's judicial modernization
needs and other donor activities in the sector, and i s supported by policy makers in the Executive
and Judicial Branches. Its fulfillment i s not expectedto entail substantial difficulties.

                        Annex 1:CountryandSectorBackground
               HONDURAS:JUDICIAL BRANCHMODERNIZATION

This Annex is divided into two sections. The first provides a brief introduction to the
socio-economic context of Honduras and a description of the institutions and functioning
of Honduras's justice system and Judicial Branch. The second describes the broad
challenges and issues faced by the Judicial Branch, including the needs of specific user
groups andhow these relate to the Judiciary's modernization program.

1.CountryandSectorContext

Honduras has a population of 7 million, of which 45 percent are less than 15 years of age.
Its social indicators are among the worst in the region, especially in the areas of poverty,
corruption and crime. Two-thirds of the population subsist on less than 1US$ per day.
The economy has experienced slow growth and the cost of doing business is among the
highest inthe region.

To help redress these problems, the Government of Honduras (GoH) has targeted the
need for a more effective judicial system, which it views as an essential element of its
Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS). It recognizes that Honduras' ineffective legal
framework andjudicial institutions negatively impact the investment climate and worsen
conditions for the poor.

The Government has already initiated measures aimed at relieving these problems. Past
judicial reform efforts (largely funded by external donors) have largely focused on the
promotion of human rights, the strengthening of democratic institutions, the provision of
justice infrastructure, and reforming the structure and content of criminal procedures. It
also recently introduced oral trials in criminal courts to expedite case processing. But
other areas (civil, commercial, family and labor-which       constitute about 60 percent of
the caseload) have received little attention.

These reform efforts have resulted in uneven improvements and suffered from a lack of
integratedvision and focus on users. Institutionalcapabilities remainweak, with disparity
inthe provisionofjudicial services between ruraland urbanareas andbetween criminal
and civil jurisdictions, and a lack of attention to the specific needs of users, especially
vulnerable groups such as low-income women, businesses and youth. Citizen demand,
availability of information, and confidence are low. The proposed Project aims to target
some of these institutional gaps for the improvement of dispute resolution services and
equitable enforcement of rights.

The Government has therefore begun to move more towards judicial modernization
through building ownership,        developing skills,     encouraging transparency and
participation, and bringing justice closer to the people. It sees the importance of
accomplishing gains in the judicial system to strengthen its role as the neutral arbiter of
social and private disputes and the guarantor of the rule of law.




                                             24

Strengthening the administration of justice i s now a shared priority of the government
andjudicial policy makers, the business community and civil society groups, due largely
to a new dynamism for change led by the recently appointed Supreme Court and
supportedby the other branches of government and civil society. The Supreme Court has
prepared a Judicial Branch Modernization Plan (JBMP) 2004-2009 with Bank support,
adopting a participatory methodology with active involvement of judges, court staff and
inputs from NGOs and user surveys, so as to build ownership, reduce resistance to
change and provide a soundtechnical basis for judicial reform.

CountryChallenges

Weak Investment CZimate.Economic growth in Honduras has remained persistently low
since at least the 1960s, a record which has persisted despite economic reforms in recent
years.' Average per capita growth was only 0.3 percent over 1999-2004, less than half the
average for Latin America and one of the lowest in Central America. While investment
levels have not been particularly low, the productivity of investments and firms has
lagged due to constraints in the investment environment (including red tape, weak
property rights, uncertainty, corruption and crime), weak infrastructure services, low
innovation and labors skills, and limited access to and costs of credits.2 The informal
economy accounts for more than half of output inHonduras, resulting in lost revenue for
the state and vast proportion of economic activity operating without the protections and
guaranteesof regulation.

These constraints have impeded firms from exploiting the opportunities providedby trade
agreements and regional integration, and affected national competitivene~s.~Honduras's
recent approval of the Central American Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA) provides a
signific~topportunity to reinforce the export-led growth strategy and attract foreign
investment. The latter will depend on improving the investment climate and promoting
the diversification of trade. These are now heavily dependent on agriculture and the
maquila industry. Also needed are increasedprivate sector productivity and growth, and
increasedefficiency of investments.

Government's strategy (PRS) i s to improve the investment climate for economic growth,
increasejob opportunities, and achieve overall poverty reduction. Strengthening the rule
of law and the effectiveness and efficiency of the judicial system are identified by the
PRS as key to the sustainability of that strategy. In particular, more effective judicial
institutions would help diminishthe costs of doing business, improve the enforcement of
laws, increasetransparencyas well as investor confidence.

Poverty and Exclusion. Honduras faces serious challenges in addressing poverty and the
social and economic exclusion of vulnerable groups. Seven out of 10 households live
below the poverty line. Social inequality and poverty are linked to, among other things,
informal economic activity, crime, corruption, lack of transparency and limited access of
the poor to democratic participation and basic service^.^ The country's PRS highlights

risk factors and to strengthen social protections for children, adolescents, women, senior

enforcement of rights.

Weak Governance Transparency. Honduras i s ranked 114 out of 145 in Transparency
International's "Corruption Perception Index" for 2004.' Twenty percent of all
Hondurans believe that they have been a victim of corruption, and Hondurans share the
belief that Corruption i s one of their most important national problems, along with
poverty and violent crime. The PRS also emphasizes improving governance and
participatory democratic processes, including guaranteeing the transparency of public
administration by strengthening government auditing agencies and social audits and
generating conditions and a legal system that guarantee the protection of human rights,
legal security for investment and the equitable application and enforcement of the law.
Highlevels of corruption in Honduras result in high economic costs, inefficiency in the
use of public resources, and delays in bureaucratic processes that can drive away
investment, all of which reduce the resources available for social programs targeting the
poor.

The Constitutional reforms in 2002 have promoted judicial independence, and
strengthenedgovernment accountability and checks andbalances. However, the extent of
corruption in the public sector is such that it i s seen as an `institutionalized' program,
deeply ingrainedinthe functioning of the institutions, norms and procedures of the state.6
A lack of transparency in governance undermines the credibility of state institutions and
fosters the perception amongst the population that in order to safeguard their rights and
interests they have to go through illegal channels. A better functioning judiciary with
social participation should mitigate corruption in the justice system and increase its
capacity for deterringit elsewhereinHonduran society.

Sector Profile: The Justice System and the Judicial Branchof Honduras.

The Judicial Branch is the branch of State charged with the administration ofjustice and
i s part of Honduras's justice system. The institutions that comprise the justice system in
addition to the Judicial Branch are: the Public Ministry (Ministerio Pu'blico), the body
charged with investigating crimes, the enforcement of sentences and ensuring the lower
courts' adherence to the Constitution and laws. It has the power to initiate cases to
address infractions by judicial employees, and enjoys complete functional,
administrative, technical, financial and budgetary independence. The Ministry is also
responsible for the Special Anti-narcotics Police (Policia Especial de lucha contra el
Narcotrdifico),criminal investigation andthe Forensic Medicine Services, among others.

Other institutions of the justice system are the Ministry of Security (Secretaria de
Seguridad)responsible for the national police forces and the penitentiaries; the Attorney
General of the Republic (Procuraduria General de la Repu'blica), responsible for the
legal representationof the State; the Superior Tribunalof Accounts (Tribunal Superior de
Cuentas) which oversees the administration of public resources, and is charged with
establishing a transparent system for the management of public sector employees; the
Ministry of Governance and Justice (La Secretaria del Estado de Gobernacidn y
Justicia); the National Human Rights Commission (ComisionadoNacional de Derechos
Humanos); and the General Directorate of Public Defense (Direccidn General de la
Defensa Pu'blicu), charged with representing, assisting, and providing information in a
free, opportune, and efficient manner for defendants who do not hire their own defense
counsel. Although the Constitution mandates public defender services in all cases and in
particular for the poor, the public defender system currently only operates for criminal
cases and does not yet provide either uniform services for the poor, or specialized
services for young offenders.

The General Directorate of Criminal Investigation (Direccidn General de Investigacidn
Criminal) is charged with investigating crimes, and depends hierarchically and
administratively on the Ministry of Security, but acts under the technical and legal
purview of the Public Ministry inthe judicial investigative process. The Inter-institutional
Commission on Criminal Justice (Comisidn Interinstitucional de Justicia Penal) i s
responsible for coordinating activities relating to the criminal justice system and for
formulating State policy on criminal justice. The Commission i s comprised of
representatives of the Supreme Court of Justice, the General Directorate for Public
Defense, the Attorney General of the Republic, the Public Ministry, the General
Directorate for Administrative Probity (Direccidn General de Probidad Administrativa),
the Ministry of Security and the Ministry of Governance andJustice.

The Judicial Branch i s comprised of the Supreme Court of Justice, the Appeals Courts
and national courts. The Supreme Court of Justice, the highest court, hasjurisdiction over
the entire country. The normative framework of the organization and operation of the
courts and tribunals is set out in Chapter XI1 of the Constitution and by the Law of
Organization andAttribution of the Tribunals (Ley de Organizacidny Atribuciones de los
Tribunales).These laws establish a hierarchical and centralized organizational structure



                                            27

to the courts, the pinnacle of which is the Supreme Court of Justice. The hierarchy then
descends to the Appeals Courts, the Trial Courts and Tribunals, courts of first instance
(Juzgados de Letras), Sentencing Tribunals, Courts of Sentencing Execution (Juzgados
de Ejecucidn) and Courts of the Peace. The hierarchy depends on auxiliary bodies such
as the General Inspectorate of the Courts, the Program of Civil Defense, and the
Administration of the Judicial Career. The Law of Organization and Attribution of
Tribunals (La Ley de Organizacidn y Atribuciones de 10s Tribunales) has not been
modifiedto reflect recent changesto the Constitution, and is therefore out of date.

The Supreme Court of Justice (Corte Suprema de Justicia), the highest court, i s
independent and responsible for directing all the units that comprise the Judicial Branch.
The Court has 15 Justices, elected by the national Congress, whose rulings are
determined by majority vote. Each Justice serves a seven-year term with the possibility of
re-election. The Court i s mainly divided into four chambers: Criminal Court (three
Justices), Labor Court, which also reviews cases relating to administrative disputes (three
Justices), Civil Court (three Justices), and Constitutional Court (five Justices). Pending
the approval of the Judicial Council and Judicial Career Laws, administrative
responsibilities for the Judicial Branch fall under the aegis of the President and Plenary
Chamber of the Supreme Court.

The Court of Appeals (CortedeApelaciones)i s responsiblefor guaranteeing the integrity
of the legal process at the appeals level. It reviews arguments presented by the parties
against judicial rulings of trial courts. Appeals Courts are each comprised of three
principal Judges and two substituteJudges, and are governed by a president who is also
one of the three principal Judges. There are currently ten Appeals Courts, distributed
throughout the country.

SentencingTribunals (Tribunalesde Sentencia)handdown rulings incriminal trials. The
tribunals are comprised of three judges, and decisions are made by majority vote. The
sentencing tribunals initiated their work on February 20, 2002, after the introduction of
the new CriminalProceduralCode. There are currently seven sentencing tribunals.

Courts of Sentence Execution (Juzgados de E'ecucidn): The courts responsible for
promoting the rights of the guilty and accused, and achieving the effective enforcement
of their sentences. These courts also initiatedtheir work upon the implementation of the
new Criminal Procedural Code. The courts are comprised of one judge, one secretary,
and auxiliary personnel. Their jurisdiction i s determined according to the location of
criminaljustice centers throughout the country.

Specialized or First Instance Courts (Juzgados de Letras): Specialized first instance
tribunals are concentrated around the two largest urban centers: Tegucigalpa and San
Pedro Sula. They are comprised of one or morejudges, basedon populationanddemand.
Where there is more than one judge, one of them i s appointed to coordinate and oversee
the court's activities. The jurisdiction of the courts is determined by the level of
specialization and territorial distribution. The specialized courts can be found in the

larger cities, while the mixed jurisdiction first instance courts can be found in less
populated areas. Areas of specialization are as follows:

    Civil Courts (Juzgados de Letras de lo Civil): The courts responsible for hearing
    disputed cases and those of voluntary jurisdiction Gurisdiccidn voluntaria).

    Administrative Dispute Courts (Juzgados de Letras ContenciosoAdministrutivo): The
    courts responsible for hearing and resolving, at the trial level, cases relating to public
    administration, subject to administrative law.

    Landlord-Tenant Courts (Juzgadosde Letras de Inquilinato): The courts responsible
    for processing and resolving cases involvinglandlord-tenant law.

    Family Courts (Juzgadosde Letras de Familia): The courts responsible for resolving
    cases involving relatives andinstitutions involvedwith family issues.

    Family Courts Specializing in Domestic Violence (Juzgados de Letras de Familia
    con Especialidad en Violencia Dombtica): The courts responsiblefor resolving cases
    involving infractions of the Law for the Eradication and Punishment of Domestic
    Violence (Ley para la Erradicacidn y Sancidn de la Violencia Dombtica), and to
    protect victims' rights.

    Labor Courts (Juzgados de Letras de lo Laboral): The courts responsible for hearing
    and resolving labor disputes according to the rights and guarantees established inthe
    Constitution, the Labor Code (Cddigo de Trabajo)and other laws.

    Juvenile Courts (Juzgados de Letras de la NiAez y la Adolescencia): The courts
    responsible for providing minors inconflict with the law and minors at-risk with legal
    attention in strict accordance with the International Convention on the Rights of the
    Child, with emphasis on the protection of humanrights.

    Criminal Courts (Juzgados de Letras Penales): The courts responsible for hearing
    criminal cases within legal timeframes and guaranteeing respect for human rights and
    legal reliability.

    Mixed Jurisdiction Courts (Juzgados de Letras Mixtus): The courts responsible for
    hearingall categories of cases within established legal timeframes and guaranteeing
    legal reliability.

Justice of the peace courts include both mixed and specialized courts with first instance
jurisdiction to handle civil and small claims cases (not to exceed 50,000 Lempiras, or
approximately US$ 2,777) and minor criminal offenses (misdemeanors). There are 335
justices of the peace, with one in each municipality. The working methods of the courts
are determinedby their mixed or specialized nature. Specialized courts exist for civil and
criminal matters only, and these tend to be concentrated in urban areas where demand i s
higher.Mixedcourts are more prevalent inrural areas, where the demanddoes notjustify

specialization. Data for 2003 indicates that out of the then total of 331 justices of the
peace, only 159 are specialized (letrados), meaning that they have legal training - an
increase in 15 percent from the previous year as a result of an initiative to increase the
professionalization ofjustices of the peace.

The NationalElections Tribunal(Tribunal Nacional de Elecciones) i s an autonomous and
independent body that oversees elections throughout the country. The members are
nominated by the Executive Branch, through the Ministry of Governance andJustice. The
Supreme Court and each of the legally registered political parties nominate one member
andone alternate. The lower courts also hearelection-related cases.

The Judicial School i s responsible for planning, developing and evaluating training
programs for Judicial Branch personnel, in both entry-level and continuing training.
Entry-level training i s a recent introduction at the Judicial School. To date it has
concentrated on keeping jurisdictional staff up-to-date on the new Criminal Procedural
Code. Judges from sentencing and enforcement courts received training over a four-
month program, with the collaboration and coordination of the Public Ministry, the
Ministry of Security and the Judicial Branch as part of the drive to facilitate the criminal
procedural reform. Current continuing education programs lack any follow-up
mechanisms to provide continuity and consistency to the skills acquisition of judicial
personnel, and to ensure that changes in legislation or operational procedures are
accompanied with the requisite skills training for jurisdictional, administrative and
support staff. There i s thus a need to broaden and strengthen both the entry level and
continuing programs provided by the School, and to strengthen the organizational
structure of the School to enable it to respond to the demand for training across the
Judicial Branch and nationally. This need is exacerbated by the fact that Law School
graduates do not possess the requisite skills for entry into the Judicial Career. The
Judicial School also suffers from inadequate infrastructure, facilities and equipment.

There are 616 judges in the courts system of which about 35 percent are women. In
addition, there are about 3300 professional, technical and support staff in the Judicial
Branch. The annual budget in 2002 was 494 million Lempiras (about 1.81 percent of the
national budget, thereby falling short of the mandated 3 percent). Overall, courts receive
about 71,000 cases per year (of all types), of which about 30 percent are criminal cases
andthe rest pertain to civil, family, labor and administrative and other-matters.

Judicial Branch administration involves the operation of many different units or agencies
including: the Plenary Chamber of the Supreme Court, President of the Supreme Court,
Secretary General of the Supreme Court, Accounts, Human Resources, Administrative
Directorate for Procurement, General Services, Internal Audit, buildingmaintenance, and
information and communication technology services. Inaddition to the operation of the
courts and administration of their various departments, sector wide operational
responsibilities of the Judicial Branch include: policy development, budgetary planning,
and the oversight and management of the General Inspectorate of Tribunals, (Inspectoria
General de Tribunales), the Electronic Documentation Center (Centro Electrdnico de
Documentacidn), the Judicial School, Judicial Services for Inter-institutional Relations,

Administration of Public Notaries (Control de Notarios Pziblicos),and Criminal Records
Offices (AntecedentesPenales).

Honduras has undertaken a number of legal andjudicial reform initiatives inrecent years.
Important changes were introduced to Article XI1of the Constitution in2001, providing a
normative framework and paving the way for significant institutional changes. Among
these changes was the increase in the number of Supreme Court Justices (Magistrados)
from 9 to 15, and the establishment of new guidelines for their election with civil society
participation. Specifically, these new guidelines establish that a Nominating Committee
(Junta Nominadora) is to submit nominations to Congress, which in turn elects the
Justices by means of a two-thirds vote. The Nominating Commission i s currently
composed of representatives of the Supreme Court of Justice, the Bar Association
(Colegio de Abogados), the Commission on Human Rights, the Honduran Council on
Private Enterprise (Consejo HondureAo de la Empresa Privada, COHEP), law
professors, civil society organizations and workers' confederations (Confederaciones de
los Trabajadores).

The Constitutional reforms also created the Judicial Council (Consejo de la Judicatura)
to supervise the judicial career and exercise disciplinary authority over judicial
employees. This reform responds to the need to separate the administrative and
jurisdictional functions of the Judiciary. Members of the Judicial Council are to be
designated by the Supreme Court of Justice and cannot be suspended, transferred,
demoted, or retired except for reasons stipulated by Law. Establishment of the Judicial
Council i s pending approval of the Judicial Council and Judicial Career Laws by
Congress.

Another important aspect of the reform process has been the creation of the
Constitutional Chamber ( M a Constitucional) of the Supreme Court. The role of the
Constitutional Chamber i s to hear cases relating to habeas corpus, personal defense
(exhibicionespersonales) and appeal (amparo). The Court also hears disputes between
the branchesof government, the National Elections Tribunals, and other entities indicated
by law.

The new Criminal Procedural Code (Cddigo Procesal Penal) went into effect inFebruary
2002, after being approved by Congress in 1999. The Code represents a radical change
in the criminaljustice system, from an inquisitive, written process to one based on oral
argument. The new Code also confirms other rights, including the right to legal defense
and the right to a speedy trial, among other things. The Supreme Court of Justice
developeda Transition Plan (Plande Transicidn) to facilitate the adoption of the reforms.
This Plan encompasses both specific activities and the general formulation of policy
relatingto reformof the criminaljustice system.

11.BroadChallengesandIssuesof theJudicialBranch

The challenges faced by the Judiciary are categorized below according to three strategic
areas requiringprogrammatic intervention: Weaknesses inthe institutional capacity of the
Judicial Branch that affect its efficiency and effectiveness, transparency and
accountability in the administration of justice, and limited access to justice for the
population.

1.Weak institutionalcapabilities

Operational weaknesses are indicated in the courts' inabilities to resolve cases in a
timely manner. The majority of civil, family and labor cases take over two years to
resolve. Significant backlogs are also apparent incriminal cases. It is estimated that over
70,000 cases are still pendingfrom before the criminal procedurereforms of 2002.

Rigid procedures for handling filings or inquiries cause administrative personnel and
judges to spendabout 60 percent of their time on clerical tasks. Duplication of procedures
and inadequate capabilities to meet demands contribute to inefficient and ineffective
performance, causing backlogs in the system. This illustrates the need to update the
normative framework (Ley de Organizacion y Atribuciones de 10s Tribunales of 1904)
that regulates case processing as well as the organization and administrative functions of
the Judicial Branch.

2. Transparency and Accountability

Transparency and accountability in justice administration depend fundamentally on the
efficient collection, use and accessibility of information, whether for internal use to
support the implementation of effective oversight mechanisms and policy decision-
making or for external use by stakeholders to assist in social audits and civil society
participation. While the lack of adequate information mechanisms is a crucial dimension
of the ineffectiveness of justice administration in Honduras, it also creates opportunities
for corrupt practices and contributes to the lack of transparency of the system as a whole.

Corruption.10 Perceptions of corruption in the judicial system are widespread. Ina 2001
survey approximately two-thirds of respondents who hadcontact with the courts reported
that they had been asked at some time to pay a bribe. Ten percent of the respondents said
that they knew someone who had paid a bribe in the courts. Fifty seven percent of firms
and 63 percent of users considered the judicial system unfair and judges not crediblell.
The overwhelming majority of respondents in a recent survey stated that the Judiciary i s
influenced by economic and political pressures. Also, more than 80 percent of users
agreedthat bribery is neededfor a speedyjudicial process12.

participated indialogues over recent reforms such as the new Criminal Procedural Code,

the latter by Congress resulted from a consensus among the Coalition to provide broader

3. Limited Access to Justice

The aforementioned weaknesses in the capabilities of the Judicial Branch, inadequate
geographical coverage in rural areas, low citizen confidence, economic and cultural
barriers, and a lack of information about rights constitute significant barriers to effective
access to justice. Vulnerable groups at the justice of the peace and first instance levels
suffer the most from these deficiencies. There is a need to broaden and diversify these
opportunities for the population, by providing targeted services and educational and
outreach campaigns and supporting the dejudicialization of non-contentious cases (e.g.
uncontesteddivorce).

                         Annex 4: DetailedProjectDescription
              HONDURAS: JUDICIAL BRANCHMODERNIZATION


This annex describesthe project objectives, components, andimplementation scope.

ProjectDevelopmentObjective(PDO):

The proposed Project will support` implementation of the government's Judicial Branch
Modernization Plan 2004-2009. The project aims to improve the capacity and
performance of the Judicial Branch in three areas: (a) greater efficiency of case
processing, judgments and appeals, (b) enhancedtransparency and accountability and (c)
better access tojustice, especially for the most disadvantagedgroups.

The progress and the outcomes of the project would be measured against a set of overall
indicators, of which the main ones are: clearance rates for noncriminal cases; progressive
improvement inusers satisfaction withjudicial performance; number of women, poor and
other disadvantaged groups accessing the courts or related services. Overall the Project
would deliver a set of solutions inthese three areas that can be scaledup and expandedas
judicial performance gradually improves over the medium term with the implementation
of the Judicial Branch Modenrization Plan. Over the life of the project, M&E would be
conducted with measurement of intermediate results at subcomponent level using
corresponding indicators. Initial base line data i s generally available and updates are
being prepared (including disaggregated by gender) with assistance from the Project
PreparationFacility (PPF).

Total IDA financing US$15.00 million. Total Project cost US$17.27

ProjectComponents

PDO will be achievedthrough these four inter-related components:

A. Improvement of the Efficiency of Judicial Services.

Operational capabilities of the Judicial Branch to more efficiently process cases, issue
judgments and review appeals would be strengthened through (a) development and
implementation of streamlined judicial processes, through: (i)the design and
implementation of model systems aimed at modernizing the organizational and functional
capabilities of the courts; (ii)the design and installation of automated information
systems aimed at improving the quality of judicial management, the courts' enforcement
functions, and the registration systems for court appointed experts; and (iii)
                                                                            the upgrading
and rehabilitation of selected existing courts buildings and infrastructure; (b)
improvement of the courts' capacity to program and monitor the utilization and
management of resources, through: (i)       development of administrative procedures to

                 Annex 6: Implementation Arrangements and Strategy
              HONDURAS: JUDICIAL BRANCHMODERNIZATION

Implementation Agency and Arrangements. The Supreme Court of Justice through its
PCU. PCU i s already set up for implementation of the PPF (and coordination of donor
activities) which will be scaled-up to help implementthe proposed Project.

Implementation Strategy. Institutional transformation i s a learning process that
requires participation of stakeholders and their change in attitudes for good and
sustainable results. Preliminary steps in that direction have already been taken by the
Judicial Branch in the recent past with IDA (PPF) and donor support, as well as its own
resources. These included consultation withjudges and staff on institutionalpriorities and
needs, development of an integral, medium-term Judicial BranchModernizationPlanthat
provides a blueprint for needed actions, agreements on contracting an international
consulting firm for the evaluation of all judges and staff to assess their skills and identify
gaps against required profiles, and implementation of initiatives to introduce oral and
transparent procedures.

Also the design calls for concentration of activities for a manageable geographic and
technical coverage of the Project. As noted above, efficiency improvement (Component
1) under the project would basically target Judicial Branch operation in four cities-
Tegucigalpa, San Pedro Sula, La Ceiba and Choluteca. Access to justice pilots
(Component 3) would focus on rural areas, and target disadvantaged groups such as
women, and poor citizens. The Project work on improving citizen information
(Component 2) on the performance of the courts would be focused on promoting a
culture of service and transparency and testing their impacts. For this purpose, eight
locations where public information centers would be set up are being considered for
impact measurements.

Based on the lessons of the past, and to fully address the capacity needs of the Judicial
Branch, the Project would be implemented in three phases over a five year period. In
parallel, on an on-going basis, there will be monitoring and evaluation of the Project
activities, periodic consultation with donors and facilitation for participation among
stakeholders. This will help pave the way for gradual but firm consensual basis for
attitudinal change, and deployment of workable technical solutions that help build
confidence inthejudicial systemby showing positive results.

1. Mobilization and building of capacities (yearl). This refers to the continuation of the
   creation of institutional readiness (initiated under the PPF) for the promotion of
   participatory solutions of institutional changes. The activities are principally oriented
   towards the evaluation of the performance of the judicial staff, and their training in
   topics related to modernization, with the use of distance education and links with
   universities. This will include identification and training of a core group ofjudges and
   staff who would be responsible for designing and developing pilots for improved
  judicial services. Similarly, a new model of organization and competencies of the
  justice of the peace system would be designed, incorporating alternative dispute


                                             58

   resolution mechanisms, and mobile justice, initiating its operation in a pilot
   experience. In parallel, there would be contracting of consultancies for the
   development of the organization model andoperations of the chambers of the Supreme
   Court, appeal and first instance courts; automation ofjudicial, administrative and court
   management; andinitiationof space refurbishing and rehabilitation.

2. Development of change and demonstration of pilot results (year 2 and 3). This will
    consist of stock-taking and an initial evaluation of access to justice pilots; launch of
    citizen outreach; and approval and consensus building and implementation regarding
    the new organizational model andautomatedsystems. Also, support will beginfor the
    introduction of career and staff training, and the provision of ICT platform for
    regional and local network for e-learning. Court rehabilitation and space remodeling
    will continue as inthe previous phase.

  3.Sustainability of institutional change and development of future goals (year 4 and 5).
    This refers to continuation of the preceding phase, with further emphasis on training
    functionaries and users on the new work methods, modern tools, and inthe evaluation
    of the achievement of the objectives and the impacts of the new service delivery
    system. This also impliesthe development of future modernization activities.

Financial Allocations, Disbursement and Procurement Sequence by Implementation
Phase. In accordance with the lines of action established in the Judicial Modernization
Plan 2005-2009, the Project seeks to improve the capacity and the performance of the
Judicial Branch through a series of pilot activities inthree strategic areas: efficiency in
case processing, transparency, and access to justice, particularly for vulnerable groups.
As noted above, the Project will be implemented over 5 years, with a first phase of 1
year, and two subsequent phases of 2 years, during which the project will advance
towards its statedobjectives. The implementation strategy i s basedon the development of
pilot programs and their subsequentevaluation and expansion. As such, the largest phase
of disbursement will be the second phase in which the expansion of pilot programs will
be developed, and the third phasewill entail smaller disbursementsfor training and social
communications.

The cost of the first phase is estimated between US$2-2.5 million, the second phase
between US$9-12.5 million, and the third phase between US$3-1.2 million (see

consultancy andtechnical assistance, 14%to training and related services, 33% to goods,

                      Annex 9: EconomicandFinancialAnalysis
               HONDURAS: JUDICIALBRANCHMODERNIZATION


Economic Benefits: The improved delivery of judicial services and other Project outputs
would help lower the costs of doing business in Honduras, as well as reduce the
time now required for carrying out litigation and clearance of cases.            These
outputs are expected to result in the country's laws being interpreted and
enforced in a more predictable and uniform manner.             Better guarantees of
property    rights and   collections  of  financial   obligations   should   thus be
facilitated.    Moreover, the Project should help to some degree to mitigate
corruption in the judicial system and strengthen deterrents to illegal practices
elsewhere in Honduras' governance. These and the attendant enhancement of
business confidence would improve the investment climate, thereby strengthening
incentives for entrepreneurs. They are likely as well to encourage participants
in Honduras' large informal economy to move closer to operating within the
protection and guarantees of regulated formal economic activity, which could
increaseGovernment revenues.

Fiscal Impact: Typically, judicial development activities are not subject to
conventional financial analysis since a substantial part of their results are
related to improved social outcomes. These are difficult to quantify and to
relate to financial benefits, especially over precise periods. But despite these
limitations, the fiscal impact of the Project i s      considered to be relatively
modest and sustainable.

In the       short   run,  the   Project  requires   counterpart    financing    which
should be covered by the Judicial Branch's anticipated appropriations               (or
supplementary Government funds if need be). Debt service on the proposed IDA
Credit would be comparatively low and extended over a considerable period.
These constitute reasonable assumptions about the Project's fiscal impact on two
accounts: There is a constitutional mandate which obliges the Executive Branch
to provide at least 3% of its annual appropriations for financing the judicial
system's current budget. Also, the Judicial Branch has virtually no heavy debt
burden. In this connection, it is       expected that the Judicial Branch will
simultaneously undertake other investments than those involved in this Project
from additional external sources. However, the aid flows from the Government of
Spain and USAID are expected to be in the form of grants, while IDB's projected
loan is     estimated to require relatively minor local cost financing and debt
service obligations.

In the      medium    term, efficiency  gains    in court     operations    and their
management will allow Judicial Branch resources to be used in a more
cost-effective    manner,   possibly   permitting    reductions    in   administrative
expenses. Greater automation of their processes will allow more cases to be
resolved in less time, permitting public resources to be better used for the


                                        71

required services. These together with the anticipated improvements in the
Branch's personnel practices could reduce budget needs.       So too could the
increased promotion     of Alternative   Dispute Resolution mechanisms,      greater
rationalization of    the  Justices  of    the   Peace,  the de-judicialization  of
non-contentious cases and improvedjurisdictional mapping.

Background. Honduras is one of the poorest countries in the region. According to the
PRSP (GOHPoverty Reduction Strategy) 57 percent of households inurbanareas and 75
percent in rural areas live below the poverty line, and 37 and 65 percent, respectively,
live inconditions of extreme poverty. Indigenous and afro-descendent peoples have been
recognized as the poorest of the poor in the region. Social indicators such as health,
education and access to basic services are lowest for these groups inthe region. Measures
to support poverty alleviation and equitable access to social and economic opportunities
now uniformly recognize that the sustainability of these policies for indigenous peoples
depend on their appropriateness to the historical and cultural specificities of these
populations. Inmany cases, as in Honduras, this has resulted in the need for a delicate
balancing act for policy makers and communities alike between increasing access to
markets and social capital, andmaintaining distinct cultural identities andheritage.

The major factors related to the incidence of poverty among ethnic groups cited in the
PRSP are lack of secure landtenure; little or deficient coverage of basic services such as
health, education, potable water, sanitation, electricity androads; highlevels of infectious
diseases (AIDS i s a serious problem among the Garifuna population); and little support
for productive employment programs and for preserving these groups' cultural heritage.
The geographical isolation of many of these communities also contributes to their limited
access to services and opportunities. Improving the efficiency, effectiveness and
accessibility of justice administration in Honduras is identifiedby the PRS as key to the
sustainability of poverty reduction. The proposed project would support measures for
broadening and diversifying access to justice, particularly for vulnerable groups. Inareas
with local ethnic populations, access to justice measures would seek culturally
appropriate solutions to enhanceaccess to dispute resolution mechanisms.

Inthe last few years, particularly in the aftermath of Hurricane Mitch, Honduras has
benefited from a number of participatory assessments that have significantly contributed
to the availability of information about Honduras's ethnic populations, the factors that
impact their social and economic exclusion, as well as information about their socio-
cultural characteristics and traditions.22 The proposed project has benefited from the
insightsprovided by the PRSP, the World Bank Indigenous PeoplesProfile, andNuestrus
Rakes Program. Other Bank supported projects in Honduras such as the Health System
Reform Project and Honduras Sustainable Tourism Project also provide important social
assessment insights into the conditions and development priorities of these groups. The
project has also benefited from the results of a survey: "Voces de 10s grupos vulnerubles
para el ucceso de la justiciu en Honduras" which collected quantitative and qualitative
data on the opinions of vulnerable groups, including ethnic minorities, about the justice
administration system inHonduras.

Some Access to Justice Issues. Ina recent survey, "Voces de 10s grupos vulnerable para
el acceso de lajusticia en Honduras," 75 percent of ethnic respondents expressed distrust
of the justice sector. Local communities instead prefer to resolve civil disputes where
possible by community justice. The majority of court cases involving ethnic population
are therefore criminal cases. Consultations with judges in Departments with ethnic
populations identified numerous shortcomings in addressing the needs of these
communities. The courts lack bilingual information services and staff. Public defender
services are also critically inadequate (1 in Gracias a Dios, 2 in Colbn, for e.g.). The
remoteness of many of these areas, lack of coordination betweenjustice sector agencies,
as well as language barriers make the collection of evidence and speaking to witnesses
almost impossible. A lack of information about justice services and widespread distrust
contribute to a general perception that the formal justice system merely exists as an
instrument of repression, rather than for the protection of rights.

Judges report a need for information and training in the application of international
conventions about the rights of indigenous and tribal peoples. They also point to an
urgent need to educatejudicial operators, includingjudges, public prosecutors and public
defenders about ADR mechanisms and the culture and traditions of these communities to
better address their needs and improve the quality of service they receive in the courts.
Community or customary justice remains a way of life for many communities. There i s a
need for judicial operators to be aware of the cultural values enshrinedin these practices,
and for indigenous communities to be informed about their legal rights. A Miskito
translation of E O Convention 169 was recently published and disseminated, but the
Conventions still fall shorts of being effectively enforced, and more systematic outreach
and legal education is required. Strategies that seek to improve access to judicial services
for Honduras's ethnic minorities also need to incorporate gender sensitivity in outreach
and social empowerment initiatives.

   AdditionalAnnex 15: ConditionandPlanfor Improvementof JudicialStatistics
               HONDURAS: JUDICIALBRANCHMODERNIZATION

The Placeof InformationinJudicial Reforms: As experts inthe matter are coming to
realize, ajudicial reform, modernization, or development program without empirical data
i s a proposal couched in a vacuum. When the round of Latin American reforms began in
the early 1980s, plannershad to rely on conventional understandingsof system problems
and their causes, as nojudiciary inthe region kept good statistics on its own performance.
Over twenty years later, we are beginning to see that much of this conventional wisdom
was misleading when not completely wrong. Presumably overworked judges turned out
to have relatively light caseloads; delays are not as exaggeratedas the usualcollection of
anecdotal evidence indicates and may be less problematic than the large number of cases
which either never are resolved, or reachjudgments that are not enforced; "underfunded"
judiciaries are found to receive a higher than average proportionof the national budgets;
and muchjudicial workload may be composed of cases which arguably should not reach
the courts.

These and many other "discoveries" have been aided by the availability of better
collections of management statistics within the region and internationally. Much more
could be known if the region's databases were further improved and better utilized.
Contrary to the view that justice is a quality, not a measurable quantity and that statistical
systems are of little help, in fact may distort efforts to improve its delivery, numerical
data on judicial performance have applications far beyond the basics of judicial
productivity (i.e. whether judges are resolving a sufficient number of cases within
prescribed time limits). They can be used to guide code reform efforts by indicating
where the real bottlenecks or irregularities occur, can help plan programs to expand
access to marginalized groups, can help judicial governing bodies defend legitimate
requests for more resources and determine where to place them, and can identify where
alternative mechanisms (including improving administrative dispute resolution) might be
most effective. They can also help courts determine where poor performance by other
sector organizations impedes their own output, as well as evaluating the success of
internalreforms.

Despite the large investments in ICT, very few judiciaries have used this equipment to
enhance their ability to track and evaluate their own performance. The countries with
relatively adequate management statistics (e.g. Costa Rica, Chile, Colombia, Argentina
andMexico at the federal levels, Brazil, for the federal, labor and some state courts) are a
small minority. Most of them do not use these data as a means of monitoring output,
identifying problems, and setting reform goals. What data are collected are rarely
checked for accuracy and consistency, usually reach the central offices only in the most
abbreviated form (Le. annual filings and dispositions, at most broken down by major
legal areas); and are often used only as input to annual reports on judicial activities. A
few countries do use them to evaluate the performance of individualjudges, but virtually
no one attempts more sophisticated analysis, for example matching distributions of
workload and resources, tracking differences in outputs among types of courts or cases,
looking for anomalies or bottlenecks in processing, or comparing their own data with the


                                              90

increasingly available international statistical compilations. It is not unusual to find that
national judiciaries are unaware of the potential for comparative analysis, do not in fact
know that these other databases exist.

Thus while Latin American judiciaries are beginning to catch up in the areas of
automation, budgetary funding (where they even surpass international averages), ratio of
courts andjudges to population, career andprofessionaldevelopment, and the adoption of
access-enhancing and alternative dispute resolution programs, they are far behind the
international curve as regards the introduction of mechanisms, including but not limited
to management statistics, to monitor, evaluate, and develop empirically basedmethodsto
improve their performance. Their bilateral and multi-lateral partners share part of the
blame, but are now attempting to incorporate this missingelement intheir own assistance
programs. Given the frequent use of external resourcesto fund automation programs, the
IT way to do this is to ensure that automation incorporates a statistical capability. This is
not hardto do, and only requires ensuring that data entry at the courtroom level (for case
management) i s done in a fashion that allows its subsequent extraction in a satisfactory
form. This means some care must be taken with format, consistency, and accuracy, and
that secondary users (those managing aggregatedatabases or doing direct analysis of raw
data) give sufficient thought to the kind of information they need, and have the skills for
further analysis.

The Situationof the HonduranJudiciary: Honduras' Judiciary is no exception to these
general observations. It does collect statistics and those available would be of some use
to judicial planning. However, there i s little indication that they are yet utilized for this
purpose or that the Judiciary currently has the capacity to do much of its own analysis.
Comparisons with international databases, as a means of interpreting national results, are
not yet attempted.

Itdoes bear noting that following recent changes, Honduras' current system is better than
many others, including those using automatic generation of submissions. Data for the
central "management statistics system" are provided by each court office using manual
entry on standardized formats The information submitted to the central office captures
considerably more than the usual input-output (entradas y salidas) figures, and while the
forms are complex (and appear to generate some errors and confusion), they do give the
central planners more to work with - including a breakdown of cases by legal area, a
limited number of basic proceedings, types of disposition, and a differentiation between
cases decided in the year covered and those originating in earlier years. While a good
statistical system can be developed without automation, the relatively low endowment of
informatics equipment in Honduras' courts (as compared to others in the LAC region)
and the apparent failure to use what exists to generate statistics automatically probably
contributes to some of the problems noted - incomplete or untimely submission of data,
probable inaccuracies in what i s provided, some indications of double counting, and
likely inconsistenciesinhow data are categorized and entered. Central offices keeptrack
of personnel andbudgetary figures separately, and inrecent annualreports court analysts
have attempted to cross them with the caseload figures, to derive costs of processing




                                             91

cases and the like. This itself is an indication of greater sophistication than shown in
reports collected from countries with far more expansive statistics.

The manual process could be further improved, but probably has reached the limits of
what can be handled in this fashion. As it i s likely to remain the means of gathering data
on the mass of rural courts (justices of the peace and judges of mixedjurisdiction) over
the medium term, there might be some benefit in refining it further - not to add more
categories, but to ensure that those used offer the most value and that those generating
most errors are either redefined or eliminated. The more immediate needs are to ensure
the accuracy, consistency and completeness of data entry (at both the courtroom and
central levels)24, and to improve the analysis of the results. On the first point, it bears
mentioning that data fromjustices of the peace appear very incomplete. As these courts
seemingly have a very low caseload, it should not be a problem for them to provide all
the data requested. Training and monitoring are thus inorder. As for analysis, the recent
annualreports incorporateconsiderableworking of the data, with graphs andpie charts in
addition to summary lists. Conspicuously missingis an interpretation of the results, not
only for the purposes of the report, but also for judicial planning. Technical assistance to
core staff would be a practical way of moving the statistics from a descriptive to an
analytic focus, and also of exploring some of the oddities in the results (e.g. the few
courts which dismiss en masse an enormous proportion of their backlog - this may be an
error in reporting or it may be a reflection of reality, but it certainly merits further
investigation).         Assistance should also focus on the use of international databases for
interpretive purposes.

Similarly more work could be done with the elementary judicial map the analysts have
already created, thus advancing a major input to determining the distribution of new
resources and the redistribution of existing ones. Such further analysis would help
discourage the usual claims that more of everything i s needed, might reduce political
interference in the creation of new work units, and could also aid in identifying areas
where other types of analysis might be needed - for example, samples of case files or
interviews with actual and potential users as a means of better understanding problems
appearing in the aggregate review. These might be problems related to performance of
individualjudges or courts, in handling certain types of cases, or inrestricted access and
thus nonuseby logical client groups. One already apparent example is the low workload
of many of Honduras' justices of the peace. When a large portion of the judiciary i s so
severely underutilized (as the aggregatedata already make apparent), there are a series of
possible explanations, each lending itself to a different type of solution. Even more
sophisticated statistical systems may not provide the information needed to identify the
most important explanations, but can help narrow down the possibilities and so make the
additional work easier.

Points of Progress

For the first time in Honduran history, the Supreme Court Magistrates were elected from
a list of candidates that were proposed by seven sectors, representing both governmental
and nongovernmental agencies and organizations, and by a board with the same
representation. Previously, it was common to hear that a given magistrate "was the
magistrate of a politician X or Y." A far more participative process may reverse that
perception.

The new code of criminal procedures has made the criminal justice system more
transparent and expeditious through the introduction of oral trials (see below for
discussion of civil cases). Previously for example, a case file could be easily "lost" as a
means of delaying procedures to illegally benefit either one of the parties. Bribery of
court officials i s facilitated by a non-oral system.

A merit-based system was used to select new "jueces de sentencia" and "jueces de
ejecuci6n" needed for the implementation of the new code of criminal procedures. And
for the first time in Honduran history, two members of Congress were convicted of
crimes (one for drug trafficking). The other had his congressional immunities lifted in
order to permit his arrest. The current Supreme Court has provided more resources to
public defense and tripled the number of public defenders. Most judges and judicial
personnel are still not selected on the basis of merit in an institutionalized and systemic
manner. The Supreme Court was scheduled to contract an international firm to audit the
human resources of the Judicial Branchin order to determine who should stay be let go,
who should stay, andwhat skill needs the latter group has.

Currently, civil cases are still resolved in a non-oral system, characterized by secret,
manipulatory and non-transparent practices. There i s a significant case backlog. (See

practices. Case management in and of itself, once modernized, will become more
efficient, result in reduced backlogs and easier access to information, thus reducing the
propensity for corrupt practices inthe courts.

problems, with particular attention to the Judiciary; (iv) a summary of the supply and
demand side issues which shape women's access to justice in Honduras; and, (v) a
description of operational recommendationsfor the project's design.

A judicial modernizationeffort that addresses gender inequalities can accrue major
benefits for Honduran society and can contribute to poverty reduction.               Justice
sector institutions have an important role to play in achieving gender equality by
protecting men and women's legal rights and enhancing their access to justice. As the
Bank acknowledges in its Gender Strategy, gender inequalities lower a country's
productivity, inhibit prospects of reducing poverty and weaken governance generally.
Thus, supporting the efforts of justice institutions to enhance their responsiveness to the
distinct needs of female and male users can strengthen governance as well as contribute
to the fight against poverty inHonduras.

Honduras's recent signing of the Central America Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA)
requires that justice services be able to respond to new, fast-changing economic
situations, while still protecting the labor conditions of workers and consumers'
rights. Efficient and qualityjustice services are fundamental for a healthy economy and
for the creation of employment opportunities through providing an attractive climate for
investment. Modern judicial services are also essential in guaranteeing the very basic
economic needs of households and individuals, such as non-discriminatory working
conditions (gender or otherwise) or the fulfillment of obligations related to the
livelihoodsfacing families (e.g. child support, debt collection, eviction proceedings, etc.).
Thus, efforts to increase access to justice will help resolve important legal problems
for workers, as well as users in general of the labor courts, and can facilitate their
incorporation into the labor force; contribute to securing the income of poor families;
and, ultimately spur socio-economic progressinHonduras.

As part of project preparation, a Gender Assessment (GA) was conducted to
identify male and female specific needs as well as limitations to their effective
satisfaction through the justice system in Honduras. The objectives of the Gender
Assessment were to: (a) identify critical legal problems affecting men and women in
Honduras based on a review of pertinent laws and legislation; (b) assess the response of
justice sector institutions to users, especially poor women; and (c) design operational
recommendations to increase access to justice and enhance the response of sector
institutions to disadvantaged groups, particularly poor, rural women. The GA is also
based on: (a) a desk review of Honduras laws and relevant legislation andjustice sector
reports; (b) analysis of judicial statistics; and (c) in depth interviews with justice sector
operators (including criminal, labor, family, and domestic violence judges, judicial

LEGALPROBLEMSINHONDURAS

Men and women in Honduras experience different legal problems, and consequently,
have different legal needs. While notably affected by domestic violence and family
related problems, women in Honduras experience legal problems of all types, including
violations to their labor rightsand as victims of criminal offenses. Menare predominantly
involved in labor and criminal disputes.

Domestic violence i s generally perceived in Honduran society and by justice sector
operators as the most important legal problem affecting women. Overall, nearly 16
percent of Honduran women between the ages of 15 and 45 years of age have reported
having suffered domestic violence at some point intheir lives in 200142.Judicial statistics
for 2003 show that 8,687 cases of domestic violence entered first instance courts and
2,216 more were presentedto justices of the peace. Tegucigalpa, San Pedro Sula, and La
Ceiba represent the highest concentration of domestic violence claims, with almost 70
percent of the country. Although a Law Against Domestic Violence has been in effect
since 1998, deficiencies inits design (namely the non-punitive character of the law43,and
the unclear distinction between "domestic violence" and "intra-family violence")44, as
well as a lack of resources to fully implement it, preclude the effective protection of
women inthese cases.

Family related conflicts are, after domestic violence, the most important legal problem
affecting women in Honduras. Women generally represent 62 percent of plaintiffs in
family courts. In 2004, child support and divorce cases comprised the majority of family
court claims, making up 40 and 36 percent of total cases, respectively. While both men
and women are plaintiffs in divorce proceedings, women constitute the majority of
plaintiffs in child support claims. Women are also active litigants in cases of paternity
(requiring legal recognition or paternity of a child).

The Judiciary

Weak organizational capacity of the judicial system in Honduras affects poor users'
ability to effectively resolve their legal problems. In particular, inefficient courts with
long procedures, unqualified judicial and administrative personnel,        costly services,
together with a lack of adequate legal aid services and corruption all work to limit poor
menandwomen's ability to accessjustice to satisfy their legal needs.

The inefficient use of resources increases the gender gap in access to justice. An
inefficient use of resources in the justice sector affects women users negatively at a
variety of levels in the administration of justice. First, the unbalanced allocation of
resources between civil and criminal justice increases gender disparities in access to
justice, as men tend to be the offenders incriminal cases and women the victims; as well
as mostly the plaintiffs in civil cases51. The current lack of public defenders for civil
cases has a clear gender impact, while free legal assistance is guaranteed by law to
defendants incriminal cases -the majority of which are male. Women, particularly those
from low-income households, who are mainly plaintiffs in civil courts or victims of
criminal offensesdonot receive the benefit of free legal counsel providedby the State.

Secondly, the inefficient distribution of resources at the level of first instance courts
leads to insufficient specialized domestic violence courts. For example, while there were
8,687 cases of domestic violence filed in Honduras in 2003, verses 2,364 labor cases,
there are only four specialized domestic violence courts, compared to five specialized
labor courts which deal with a significantly lower caseload. Domestic violence courts
were created to reduce the backlog of Tegucigalpa's already overloaded family courts.
Although domestic violebece courts are independent in theory, they remain attached to
the family court system and require compliance with many of its procedures. Also, no
new judicial positions have been created nor has any additional budget been allocated to
these courts to assist them with their heavier share of family cases. Domestic violence
courts have, however, obtained additional auxiliary staff on occasion. Finally, given the
number of domestic violence cases in the judicial district of San Pedro Sula, many
experts believe that a new domestic violence court is required there, and should be
separatedfrom the family courts.

The shortage of social support services in the justice sector i s most damaging to
women. There is only one psychologist and one social worker to assist the entire Judicial
Branch in Honduras. These professionals produce the socio-psychological reports for
domestic violence cases as well as for family cases inthe country, thus clearly noting the
impact of an inefficient allocation of resourcestowards this important service.

Honduran law limits the involvement of justices of the peace to appropriately deal
with domestic violence, which i s particularlyharmfulto poor, ruralwomen. Peacejudges
are somewhat unequally distributed throughout the country, suggesting that they are "an
underutiZized resource" at the present time. While there are about 330 justices of the
peace throughout Honduras, these individuals could play a fundamental role in providing

Inefficient court operations leads to long procedures and low quality services,
especially in the family and domestic violence court systems. Delays in family and
domestic violence courts are acute and affect women disproportionately as the primary
users of such courts. As shown in Figure 4, the clearance rates of family and domestic
courts are extremely low when comparedto other civil courts. In2004, only 16percentof
domestic violence cases and 18 percent of family cases nationally reached judgment,
compared to 53 percent in labor courts. According to judicial statistics in Honduras for
2004, in the family courts, child support claims represent the poorest clearing rates, with
only 4.5 percent of cases beingcleared by the courts verses 30 percent of divorce cases.

One possible reason behindinefficiencies in the family courts i s the absence of adequate
procedural systems, as well as the lack of Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)
mechanisms, which could help reduce the backlog of cases. No specific procedural
guidelines for family courts exists inHonduras inthe area, and current procedures follow
civil rules that do not always adapt to the complicated nature of family disputes which
need faster, less burdensome processes. Additionally, ADR mechanisms, such as those
currently being used in labor courts, could be applied to certain cases in family courts
(e.g. child support, divorce, paternity claims), thus contributing to speeding up a
resolution of these cases.

The Public Prosecutor's Office (Ministerio Pliblico o Fiscalia)

The aforementioned imbalance of resources between criminal and civil justice in
Honduras affects not only the operations of civil courts, but also those of the Public
Prosecuro's Office. While an office for Women's Issues (Fiscalia de la Mujer) exists,
which is responsible for representing victims of domestic violence,           its staff has
decreased from 12 to 2 over the last few years. Thus, currently there is not even one
prosecutor per domestic violence court - there are 4 domestic violence courts in
operation, leading to delays in the processing of such cases or/and inadequate attention
by prosecutors in each case. As an indication of the limitations prosecutors endure, and
inturn, of the quality of the service that a victim might expect, arecent petitionfrom the

inbetween cases so that prosecutorscould at least read the file of their client. Similarly,
while the Law Against Domestic Violence establishes that cases need to be seen by a
judicial authority within 24 hours of the filing of a claim, delays are common with cases

Other institutions involved withJusticeIssues

The police and municipalities. Again, a lack of resources and of gender awareness
among the police and municipalities influence the quality of           public services that
domestic violence victims receive. The police are one of the principal public servants
involve in domestic violence cases. Although a gender unit has been recently created
within the police force (and coordinates trainingwith INAM), their responseto domestic
violence i s still notably weak. According to users, the police often do not take their
claims seriously and, according to other sector representatives, they even fail to pass the
complaints on to the Public Prosecutor's Office and the courts at times. Also, according
to Honduran law, the police are responsible for enforcing the sanctions establishedby the
courts, but they are often reluctant to get involved in `family matters' and often neglect
the monitoring required to oversee the implementation of sanctions. Municipalities also
fail to properly monitor aggressors who may have to comply with domestic violence
sanctions imposed by the courts. While socio-cultural issues and a lack of gender
sensitivity may be to blame, training by INAM and other organizations have had some
positive impact inaddressing the root of the problem.

The Departmentof Labor (Secretaria de Trabajo).The role of the Department of Labor
i s essentialto protecting the rights of workers, particularly in the maquila industry. Inthe
case of the maquila sector, workers must follow administrative procedures first (before
taking their cases to courts), if requesting protection from the Departmentof Labor. The
Department of Labor then sends an inspector to the workplace to document the claim,
and must act as a mediator between the worker and the employer. Inspectors encounter
numerous obstacles in carrying out their jobs (e.g. employers deny them access to the
workplace). The Department of Labor does include a Women's Program that deals with
cases involving women workers. They provide legal advice, and act as inspectors and
mediators. However, limited resourcespreclude a more effective action on their part or a
wider responseto the problems of maquila workers (as they only follow a limitednumber
of cases).

Civil Society. The role of civil society in Honduras has been inthe past, and continues to
be, fundamental in facilitating access to justice services for poor users, particularly
women. Civil society organizations (CSOs), including women's rights NGOs, have been
instrumentalover the last two decades inbringingabout changes inHonduran legislation
to make it more gender responsive. During the last decade, in parallel to promoting
legislative reform, the role of CSOs in the justice sector reform process has evolved to
include the provision of gender training and advice, counseling, and legal assistance
services to system users (see Box 2). Their role as legal aid providers was particularly
noteworthy during the nineties, declining inthe last few years due to lack of international
and/or state financial support.

International Donors. International donors play an important role in supporting the
process of judicial reform in Honduras. As part of their programs with different sector
institutions, donors are supporting the justice sector in Honduras through several
activities to enhance women's access to justice, especially with regard to domestic
violence. The main actors include the following bilateral donors: Spanish, Danish, and
Swedish cooperation agencies, which are financing: (a) an evaluation of the
implementation of the Law Against Domestic Violence, and incorporation of gender
issues in the reforms of training programs of the Judicial Academy; (b) a review of
gender issues in Honduran legislation and; (c) support to the NationalWomen's Institute
(INAM)to provide advice andassistanceto the Judiciary on gender.

  ZV. GENDERAND ACCESS TOJUSTICE IN HONDURAS

  Access to justice, or the lack of it, i s caused by both supply and demand factors. The way
  justice sector institutions operate affects the system's users, as mentioned in the section
  above.   In addition, cultural and socio-economic dynamics that escape the control of
  justice sector institutions shape citizen's demand for services as well. The following
  section summarizes the supply or systemic (internal to the justice system) factors that
  limit women's access to equitable justice and analyzes non-systemic or demand factors
  which influence access.

  Supplyside obstacles

  (a) Justice system inefficiencies. Poor performance of justice sector institutions deters
  people from using the system. In the case of women, gender insensitive judicial
  decisions; long procedures in family courts; corruption in the handling of notifications; or
  inconsiderate treatment from justice sector institutions, police, and local authorities to
  domestic violence victims, are just some examples of how the system's shortcomings
  may discourage women from accessing the justice system and limits their access to
  equitable justice services.

(b) Costly services. The cost of services of legal representation in civil cases (with the
exception of domestic violence), as well as the associated expenses, such as informal
payments for        notification^:^    negatively influences men and women's use of the system.
This is further aggravated by the almost total absence of legal aid service providers,
whether public or NGO based. While the lack of legal aid is damaging for the poor in
general, the lack of legal aid incivil cases58i s most harmfulto poor women as mainusers
of certain civil courts.

(c) Gender inequity in legislation. The lack of clearly articulated rights i s a deterrent to
using the judicial system to resolve conflicts. In Honduras, ambiguities in the laws, as
well as in their interpretation, notably affect women in cases of rape, labor rights
violations, or domestidintra-familyviolence (see previous sections for details).

Demand side obstacles

(d) Legal illiteracy. A lack of awareness and knowledge about rights also results in a low
use of the justice system. Women's generally higher rates of illiteracy, puts them at
greater disadvantage to understanding their rights, and how to use the system to exercise
them. The lack of knowledge about rights and laws also tends to be more of a problem in
rural than urban areas--justice sector operators in rural Honduras are themselves hardly
familiarized with such laws as the Law Against Domestic Violence (passed 5 years ago),
let alone common citizens. NGOs representatives interviewed reported an acute lack of
legal literacy among women inrural areas regardingtheir most basic rights, including, of
course, more gender specific laws such as the Law Against Domestic Violence or the
EqualOpportunities Law.

(e) Cultural barriers. The lack of access to justice services might be on occasion less
about a lack of legal education and awareness,                              than about culturally entrenched
principles which keep women and men from challenging discriminatory (or illegal)
practices which are perceived as the "norm." For women, cultural prejudices lead to low
demand of justice services in cases of domestic violence, sexual assault, etc. For
example, the traditional practice of early marriage in rural communities in Honduras
leadsjustices of the peace to condone cases that the country's laws define as `statutory
rape.' Widely held social views preclude women from questioning such decisions and
taking their cases to court to challenge such views. (see box 2 for more examples of
culturally biasedjurisprudence).

      AdditionalAnnex 18: ParticipatoryConsultationsinProjectDevelopment
              HONDURAS: JUDICIAL BRANCHMODERNIZATION

The participatory consultation strategy during project preparation includedconsultation
and strategic planning workshops with jurisdictional and administrative staff of the
Judicial Branch, to identify key problems injustice administration and develop strategies
for addressing them in the short, medium and long term. A series of workshops were
conducted with the participation of over 200 judges, administrators and judicial
operators. The methodology, findings and conclusions of these workshops are described
below. Participants included the 15 Supreme Court Justices, appellate judges, first
instance court judges, sentencing court judges, justices of the peace, Judicial Branch
administrative    directors,  office  coordinators,    information and      documentation
administrators, court inspectors, Public Defenders, and others. (A list of the participants
i s provided in Annex 11.)

Project preparation has also benefited from consultations with vulnerable groups about
perceptions of, and confidence in the justice administration system, including its
accessibility and public awareness of its functions and services. The findings of the
survey "Voces de 10s grupos vulnerables para el acceso a la justicia en Honduras" are
provided below.

Consultation and Strategic Planning Workshops for Jurisdictional and
AdministrativePersonnel

Methodology. A series of three workshops were each conducted over two day periods,
bringing together judges and judicial administrators from all over the country and from
all spheres of thejustice administration system. Althoughjudges and administrators were
present at all events, the workshops were designed to provide judges and administrators
an opportunity to be consulted separately about the problems and deficiencies affecting
the administration of justice. The first two events were principally for judges, and the
thirdfor judicial administrators. As might be expectedjudicial administrators andjudges
ranked perceived problems and challenges with differing orders of priority. However,
they coincided in many respects in the diagnosis of problems, and in the solutions they
proposed to strengthen the performance of the courts and the justice administration
system. The findings and conclusions are summarizedbelow.

The workshops were conducted using the METAPLANmethodology, led by an external
team of facilitators who guided working sessions with small groups. The methodology
employs brainstorming techniques to identify a broad variety of issues and problems,
which are then clustered thematically then ranked in order of priority by the participants.
The groups then discussed and proposed solutions to the key problems identified, with a
focus on priorities areas where improvements couldbe achieved inthe short term, and the
identification of sustainable actions to reach long-term goals. The METAPLAN
methodology i s particularly well suited to facilitating working sessions with stakeholders
of large institutional structures where vertical hierarchies predominate (top-down
management) as it maximizes the opportunity for contribution from all working group



                                            119

participants through a collective approach to problem solving. The findings of each group
were then presented and discussed in a plenary session attended by all participants
including senior administrative andjurisdictionalpersonnel.

The summaries of the findings outlined below represent the conclusions of one working
group of judges and one of administrators. While the conclusions of these working
groups do not reflect all of the issues raised by all the working groups, they have been
selectedfor the purposes of this summary becausethey are felt to be representative of the
conclusions as a whole.

The findings summarized here are those of judges of various categories andjurisdictions
(Workshop I,   Group 2) and include a diagnosis of key weaknesses and recommendations
to address them and to strengthenthe administration ofjustice.

I.FindingsandRecommendationsofJudges

Strengthen independence of the Judiciary:               External political and economic
interference in the operations of the Judiciary was identified as a key issue affecting the
performance and operations of the justice administration system. The problem was seen
as related to two factors: an institutionalculture that tolerates and enables pressures to be
broughtto bear onjudicial operators, and alack of mechanisms for the proper application
of the judicial career law (see the next item), including inadequate hiring, evaluation and
oversight mechanisms, insecurity injudicial tenure, and inadequately qualified personnel
due to the lack of standardized evaluation criteria or procedures for hiring candidates to
the judicial career. The recommendations for addressing these issues included the
implementation of the judicial career law andjudicial tenure to eliminate the motivation
or the opportunity for interference in the judicial process, and the criminalization of acts
of political or economic interference in the operations of the Judiciary. Public
information campaigns to publicize these measures would be necessary to help change
negative public perceptions about corruption inthe Judiciary.

Implement the Judicial Career: Judges identifiedthe inadequate implementation of the
judicial career as a key-contributing factor to weaknesses in the effectiveness of the
courts and the administration of justice. This situation derives from the fact that the
Judiciary lacks the institutional capabilities to fully apply or enforce the judicial career
law. As a result, there is a lack of uniformity inthe criteria used for hiringand appointing
judicial personnel. Likewise no standardized evaluation, oversight and discipline
mechanisms are in place, and there is a lack of career incentives. The recommendations
suggested included the rapid approval by Congress of the Judicial Council Law (the
autonomous body that will be responsible for judicial hiring, oversight, and discipline),
the institutionalization and publicizing of the policies and procedures of the judicial
career, and the implementation of standardized salary and incentive packages and
corresponding manuals.

Promote vocational and professional identification of judicial personnel: In addition
to the insufficient number of personnel to meet the demands on the courts, judges
identifieda generalized culture of detachment among alljudicial operators with regardto


                                             120

professional tasks and responsibilities, and a lack of vocational identification or
motivation. Resistance to change was also identified as institutionally pervasive.
Recommendations included the creation of incentive and motivational programs to help
develop a sense of vocational purpose and motivations for performance, of which
monitoringand evaluation mechanisms would be an integral part. Introducing aculture of
service and awareness of the social role of the Judiciary was also felt to be important.
Hiring appropriately qualified individuals for each post could also be supported by
standardizedevaluationprocedures for hiring.

Improve distribution of the judicial caseload (jurisdictional mapping): Judges
identified the poor distribution of the judicial caseload across the national territory as an
important issue affecting the efficiency of the justice administration system as a whole.
Poor jurisdictional mapping results in an uneven workload among the courts, with some
courts and judges handling a disproportionate burden of cases while others remain
relatively unoccupied. Judges recommended a comprehensive review and revision of the
jurisdictional mapping of the courts.

Improve distribution of technological support services nationwide: While courts in
Tegucigalpa and San Pedro Sula have benefited from the introduction of basic ICT
services, courts nationwide suffer from a lack of technological infrastructure, including
case automation capabilities and communication technologies. Case tracking,
administrative support services and the collection of statistics are handled manually,
precluding the possibility of up-to-date or quality information for institutional planning
and oversight. While judges recognized that equipping the judiciary with adequate
technological capabilities i s a long-term objective, requiring review of existing
capabilities and a scaling-up of technological support through the introduction of ICT and
training, they identified this as a priority issue for strengthening the performance of the
courts.

Improvephysical infrastructure: Judges identifiedinadequatephysical infrastructure as
a major deficiency in the Judiciary's ability to deliver effective and efficient services.
Although only one third of Judiciary buildings are currently rented, the majority of
physical spaces are poorly distributed and insufficient to enable the judicial offices and
courthouses to runeffectively. Buildings are also rundown which contributes to the poor
public image of the Judiciary. Measures to address this problem were identified as long-
term priority, though rehabilitation and the redistribution of spaces were considered to be
a feasible short andmediumterm approach.

Reduce judicial backlog and delays: Delays are widespread across the justice
administration system. Inefficient working methods, duplication of tasks, the replication
of functions, lack of integrated planning, inconsistencies in the normative framework that
regulates courts operations all contribute to delays in the administration of justice. The
new Criminal Procedural Code has introduced greater efficiencies and more timely case
resolution in criminal cases, although a significant backlog also remainsfrom prior to the
introduction of the new Code. Increasing the efficiency and timeliness of case resolution




                                              121

will require an integrated approach to strengthening the capabilities of the courts,
includinginsome cases updating the normative framework.

Strengthen logistical support: Inadequate logistical support means that judges are
impaired in fulfilling their responsibilities, including meeting with witnesses and court
transport for issuing summonses. While this is a widespread problem, it i s particularly
acute inmore remote areas.

Improve budget use and allocation: Judges identified deficiencies in the use and
allocation of resources as a significant issue, including a disproportionate allocation to
administrative functions as compared to jurisdictional tasks and personnel. Judges
recommended conducting an evaluation of resource usage and a national study of
institutional functions (job responsibilities) and a revision of current allocations
according to the findings of the study.

11.FindingsandRecommendationsofJudicial Administrators

The findings summarized here are those of judicial administrators (Consultation
Workshop Group 1) and include a diagnosis of key weaknesses, and recommendations
for the short, medium and long term for improving the administrative efficiency and
effectiveness of the Judiciary.

Improve communication and administrative coordination: Among the chief obstacles
to efficiency, administrators cited poor communication between administrative offices
and personnel. Compounding this problem they reported a general lack of awareness of
administrative functions, leading to confusion and overlap of functions and
responsibilities. Inthe immediate short term, they recommended training and information
to fill the knowledge gap and inform all administrators of the roles and responsibilities of
administrative offices, and the respective procedural functions performed by each. The
publication of administrative bulletins, it was suggested, would help over the mediumto
long term to allow for continual updates in administrative reform and to ensure the
continuity and quality of administrative improvements. Over the long term, an integrated
IT communication system for administrative offices was identified as a prerequisite for
efficiency both within the administrative function and for effective communication
betweenjurisdictional and administrative staff.

Improve change management and professional motivation: Another deficiency that
was identified as a key obstacle to the efficient administration of justice was related to
institutional culture. The problem was defined as low motivation and a generalized
resistance to change. These were attributed to inadequate or unclear job definitions,
resultingin low motivation or sense of purpose, the absence of institutional mechanisms
for evaluating and acknowledging professional contributions, and lack of oversight to
ensure feedback in administrative processes and change management. Training
workshops were recommended as a short-term approach, including motivational seminars
to identify institutionalvision and goals, as well as training inethics. Over the mediumto
long term, establishing clear internal guidelines for procedures was recommended to



                                             122

facilitate change management and improve performance; and the introduction of
mechanisms for the recognition of performance and responsibilities was suggested to
improve motivation, and individuals' personal investment in the quality of their
performance. Over the long term, a system of monitoring and evaluation was
recommended as a necessary step for measuring the impact of changes and enabling the
adjustment of strategies, where necessary.

Modernize administrative support procedures: The current administrative system was
perceivedas out of date, heavily bureaucratic and prone to delays. Specific problemareas
included an inefficient and chronically slow payroll system, inefficiency and lack of
clarity in human resources benefits system, obsolete procedures that are neither
standardized nor systematic, and lack of parity in the support systems provided to
jurisdictional and administrative staff. Recommendations over the short term included
the development of procedural manuals and training (including quality control). Over the
medium and long terms it was recommended that the Judiciary overhaul its
administrative support system, including streamlining and automating bureaucratic
procedures.

Strengthen governance and decision-making: This problem area was identified as a
crosscutting issue for administrative efficiency and effectiveness. Inaddition to a need
for institutionalre-engineering of the administrative functions, it was felt that a key area
of concern was the decision-making capabilities of administrative offices. Short-term
recommendations for strengthening governance included a review and redefinition of
administrative    management, and training        in management       effectiveness. The
deconcentration and delegation of responsibilities was recommended as a medium term
goal, and upon completion of institutional reviews and assessments, the restructuring of
management functions with an emphasis on the simplification of procedures and the clear
definitionof institutionalroles andresponsibilities.

111. Citizen Stakeholder Consultations: "Voces de 10s grupos vulnerables para el
acceso de lajusticia en Honduras"

A survey was conducted to obtain both qualitative and quantitative data regarding the
opinions of vulnerable groups about access to justice in Honduras. Qualitative findings
were obtained on the basis of focus group workshops, which also aided in the design of
the questionnaire for the gathering of quantitative data. The socio-demographic profile of
the respondent groups was as follows: a total of 1614 individuals were surveyed,
including youths (252); Indigenous peoples (130); representatives of small and medium
enterprises (SMEs) (22); Afro-descendents (146); and individuals of marginaleconomic
means (497). In the analysis of results, the survey groups were also cross-indexed in
terms of age, educational background and gender (810 men, 804 women). A
commonality between all groups was economic hardship, which was found to have a
bearing on perceptions of the majority of respondents about access to opportunities and
public services. The overwhelming perception of all groups was that the poor are less
likely to be treated with fairness andrespectinthejustice systemthan the wealthy.




                                            123

The most salient finding across all socio-demographic groups was a lack of information
about judicial institutions and services, including for e.g. how to obtain the services of a
lawyer. There was also a generalized lack of knowledge about laws and rights,
particularly as regards protections against domestic violence, and the rights of children
and adolescents. The majority of respondents however expressed awareness about the
institutions responsible for protecting workers' rights.

A lack of confidence in the courts was reflected in the opinion ofjust over 40 percent of
respondents that they would rather resolve disputes on their own, than turn to the courts.
This was also true of 50 percent of SME respondents, and 47 percent of indigenous
respondents (42 percent identifiedthe courts as the best means for resolving disputes, 11
percent gave no opinion), Moreover, 85 percent of respondents expressedthe opinion that
only somejudicial operators (judges, lawyers and police) are honest. When asked if they
felt confidence in judicial institutions, overall scores were low: 11 percent expressed
strong confidence in the first instance courts (Juzgudos de Letrus), 12 percent in the
Supreme Court of Justice, 14 percent in the justice of the peace courts, and 21 percent in
the Appeals Courts. Respondents reported a perception of discrimination in the courts,
particularlywith regard to sex, ethnicity andeconomic means.

The respondents that expressed greater awareness about the courts were representatives
of SMEs; a result that is proportional to the higher educational qualifications attained by
this group, although the result is not matched by a greater likelihood to use the courts,
suggesting that information needs to also be coupled with greater confidence. A
widespread culture of fear was also reported by most respondents with regard to a general
reluctance to turn to law enforcement agencies to file a civil or criminal complaint. Fear
of reprisal was cited as the reason for this reluctance.

The findings of the survey point to the need for the Judicial Branch to provide
educational outreach and public information about the services of justice administration.
This applies equally across all groups, from the needfor information in local languages
for indigenous groups, to specialized information for SMEs, and greater awareness about
alternative dispute resolution (ADR) mechanisms. The need for steps to promote greater
confidence in the courts i s also apparent, particularly as regards restoring confidence in
the effectiveness ofjudicial mechanisms.

Origins of the Maras: The origin of the Maras stems from the 1960s as neighborhood
groups formed for the young to congregate and socialize. However, fairly innocent
conflicts surrounding the domination of a neighborhood quickly turnedextremely violent
once drug- and arms trafficking arose as a societal problem. Inthe 1990s, especially since
1997 and following the 1998 hurricane Mitch, the Maras are considered to have become
instruments of organized crime, attracted by quick money and power and increasingly
involved with organized crime that provides them with illegal arms. The Maras are now
accused of a wide array of crimes, such as drug trafficking (reportedly from Colombia to
the US), human trafficking, pornography, arms dealing, killings on demand and armed
robberies. According to the humanitarian organization, "Save the Children", an estimated
10% of the Mara members has been members of violent gangs in Los Angeles and/or
New York City and "import" these practices back to Honduras when returning or
escaping deportation from the US. Another aspect possibly affecting the rise inMaras i s
the disarmament process from former civil wars in Central America and the easy access
to weapons for organized crime..

The causes of Maras may be related to risk factors, including increasing levels of
poverty and social exclusion. Two of the prevalent reasons for the existence of the
Maras are the high levels of poverty (more than 50% of Hondurans live under the
poverty line, 33% of children are malnourished) as well as drop-out rates among youth,
including high unemployment for those over 18.                        For example, the only statistics
available indicate that in 1997, only 22% of the youngsters between 11-25 years of age
attendedmiddle school.

Other possible risk factors may include the consumption of alcohol and drugs, which
often lead members of Maras to commit crimes. The public's lack of trust in Government
and high social and economic inequalities have affected the perception of institutions in
Honduras. For example, many perceive there to be a lack of respect for humanrights, a
dysfunctional justice system, diminished greater civic engagement and stronger
polarization and increased crime generally in Honduras. The break-up of traditional
family structures and social capital, increased domestic violence, the migration of men
and more women working in the maquilas are also frequently cited as factors
contributing to greater violence since young people are left without supervision or not
involvedin any structured activity.

The legal framework and access to justice are insufficient to protect vulnerable
youth. Even though Honduras has ratified most international human rights treaties
aiming at the protection of children and youth, domestic legislation does not sufficiently
protect their rights.Most youth who are at-risk of being involvedinlegal problems do not
have access to legal counsel and must depend on public defenders, which are not
specialized inthese types of cases.

3. GovernmentResponsesto the Mara phenomenon

     Challenges of Implementation of the Legal Framework: In response to rising
crime attributed to the Murus and their rapid expansion, President Maduro has called for
a "Zero Tolerance" policy. As part of the recent criminal code reform, the government
approved in 2001 the so-called Anti-Mum Law.62This law makes membership in a gang
illegal and stipulates penalties for up to 12 years for gang membersfleaders. The harsh
application of the Anti-Mum Law in Honduras has been subject to strong criticism, as
often seemingly irreverent aspects (such as a tattoo) have been considered sufficient
proof of gang membership, and have resulted in arrest.                          In accordance with the
toughening of laws regarding the Muras, the government haspassedother laws to prevent
the escalation of further violence, such as a law prohibiting the possession and use of
powerful and dangerous arms. The enactment of a Law on Juveniles as well as the Codes
for Children and Adolescents, have also heightened awareness about the rights of youth
and have helped reduce police abuses. The combined efforts by the Government, under
the leadership of the President, the Justice Sector, the Public Ministry and the Security
Ministry have generated great support inthe generalpopulation and elicited support from
some experts. Civil society organizations also have noted the recent positive changes, but
stressed that youth violence still constitutes a prevalent problem in Honduras and one
which needs more investigation to be appropriately resolved.

        The Need to Evaluate Preventive Mechanisms: Some donor-supported
capacity-building activities are now taking place to educate the police about its rights and
responsibilities towards youth. These capacity-building activities have shown good
results so far, and there appears to be widespread support for their expansion. The justice
sector also has expandedcitizen's education and social awarenessprograms on youth and
justice and promoted community initiatives. These initiatives need to be evaluated before
the Bank and other international financial institutions consider supporting such
interventions. Government agencies have tried to foster educational, recreational and
employment opportunities for youth and created volunteer community-service programs;
these too require a thorough evaluation, since many experts have placed more attention
on the issue of street children and their plight, rather than those involved in organized
programs.

        Broader preventive approaches: In order to prevent juvenile crime the
government has sponsored pilots initiatives, such as the "Si, se pede" project. Also, the
government has established initiatives to work with vulnerable groups in poor urban

4. The Needfor Researchon the Role of the Courts and other PublicInstitutions

    The following areas broadly define the scope of a study on youth violence and its
potential for impact and responsivenessin the Justice Sector. The study should consider
a variety of issues or risk-factors which may contribute to youth or Mara-related
violence, as well as evaluate the differentprograms on-going inHonduras. Basedon this,
the Judicial Branch will be able to better understand the issues involved and how it
should modernize its services to respond to social development and youth in Honduras.
The basic terms of reference of the Study on the Prevention of Youth Violence and Risk
Factors Contributingto Maras would include:

a)      The Need for an Integrated Approach: Preventing youth violence and
improving their access to justice and a fair trial mandates an integrated strategy. Such a
strategy goes beyond the justice sector and includes broader governance and anti-
corruption reforms, including inthe education and social protection sectors. Factors such
as strengthening of civic education in the current context of government services the
provision of special support to families, especially to female heads of families, are vital.
A reform movement in this context should involve the participation of relevant
government agencies, human rights institutions, non-governmental organizations, civil
society and especially community-based organizations andthe media.

b)      The Need for Preventative Mechanisms: The recent reforms and the
corresponding decrease in crime have built growing support from the population and
political parties. Therefore there i s some momentum for reform, which should be used
constructively. In particular, a criminalization approach tends to reinforce criminality
amongst youth and also promotes recidivism. Hence, preventive approaches should be
emphasized, as they are more cost effective, both socially and economically. Efforts are
also neededto offer alternatives to youth so that they do not resort to violence by offering
them better educational opportunities, including civic education as well as recreational
and social work opportunities. Other options which should be assessed include possible
victim-offender mediation and compensation mechanisms for minor offenses.. A media
campaign that would entail training ofjournalists to gradually change the public image of
both the Maras and the police dealing with them may offer other alternatives to the
Judicial Branch and should be studied as well.

c)      Capacity Strengthening Targeted Towards Particular Actors: Other donors
have financed the training of prosecutors in the justice system to provide more
specialized services, such as the Fiscalia de la Ninez y del Menor. In order to improve



                                            128

access to justice by youth, it would be constructive to consult with the Bar Associations
andpromote continued education onjuveniles and anti-corruption. Such a program would
promote professionalism and sensitivity towards youth and the special challenges they
face in the Honduran society. The study should also evaluate the possible inclusion
"Clinicas Juridicus Universitarius" (university legal clinics), which have been purported
to be successfulinmany Latin American countries and inthe US.

e)      Coordination and Participatory Approach: Violence prevention activities
within the Judicial Branchalso needto be linkedmore closely to other public institutions,
particularly the Public Ministry, the Ministry of Governance and the Ministry of Security
have all engagedin such programs. The study should examine the possibility of a multi-
sectoral program which would promote networks that further sensitize the sector's
stakeholders to youth problems and educate the Judiciary about juvenile rights. Finally,
donors need to coordinate in the area of youth andjustice andjointly focus on supporting
the Judiciary in order to deal with the problem of youth violence and Maras, and the
study should include concreterecommendations on this aspect.

f)      Urbanvs RuralFocus: Measuresthat seek to address youth violence and access
tojustice should not only target reforms at the central level, but ought to also focus on the
local and community levels, especially in rural areas. Projects such as inter-institutional
justice centers as well as community-level or "street-law" programs have been successful
and could be applied in Honduras, but need to be appraised as to the best practice most
relevant to the country. To complement on-going reforms of the justice sector, it may be
necessaryto pass specific laws that further provide access to information and
transparency, as well as encouragecitizenparticipation. The study should consider these
aspects andpossible recommendationsfor updating the legal framework, where
necessary.
