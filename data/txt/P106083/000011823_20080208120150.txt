Description          The proposed Poverty Reduction Support Grant (PRSG) is the fourth for the country, and the first in
                     the second series o f grants designed to assist the Government o f Rwanda (GoR) to implement key
                     policy actions that will form part o f its second Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP), called the
                     Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS).                The EDPRS highlights the
                     increased attention to growth, and was prepared in a participatory manner, with strong country
                     ownership. The proposedPRSG-4 continues to support the GoR as it implements the next steps in its
                     reform program to support growth, now being undertaken in the context o f enhanced
                     decentralization reforms. Within this context, PRSG-4 is expected to help the GoR: (i)maintain a
                     stable macroeconomic framework, put in place reforms to ease the human capital and infrastructure
                     constraints to growth, with the aim o f creating a strong basis for private sector-led economic growth,
                     driven by agricultural transformation, promotion o f exports, deepening of the financial sector, and
                     the use o f information communications technology; (ii)strengthen further the linkages between
                     strategic planning and the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF), by broadening the focus
                     on service delivery to include more strongly the agriculture, transport, and financial sectors, while
                     continuing to maintain and deepen the progress made in the education, health, water, and energy
                     sectors; and (iii)continue improving governance and transparency through capacity support to
                     enable implementation o f completed reforms to financial management, and the budget and
                     procurement frameworks, with a particular emphasis on strengthening the process o f fiscal
                     decentralization.
Benefits             Expected benefits are as follows: (i)    increased access to infrastructure services while continuing to
                     consolidate and improve access to education and health services to increase the supply o f skilled
                     labor in the workforce; (ii)  improved efficiency o f spending by supporting reforms to strengthen the
                     MTEF and public procurement process; (iii)      strengthened capacity to implement through support to
                     civil service reforms to improve ministerial structures and provide incentives to staff, increase skills
                     o f the civil service, and reduce turn-over. The series builds upon the successes o f the first series o f
                     PRSGs, particularly the performance-based contracting o f high-impact health services via local
                     health centers and per student transfers in the education sector to support increased hiring and
                     incentives for teachers at the district level. The second PRSG series will also aim to support GoR in
                     its attempt to replicate this success in the infrastructure and agriculture sectors. The objective is to
                     develop a basis for sustained growth through supporting measures to improve the efficiency o f
                     spending and use o f aid funds.       PRSG-4 preparation capitalizes on the much improved, fully
                     government-led donor coordination, a direct result o f the budget support program in Rwanda. It will
                     also support the development o f a Common Performance Assessment Framework (CPAF) for
                     monitoring the EDPRS that will help to reduce the transaction costs to Government related to their
                     reporting on the use o f aid funds, to donors.
Risks                Though international and regional efforts to mitigate instability in the Great Lakes sub-region are
                     continuing, the risk o f flare ups in the eastern Congo i s not negligible. The Bank acknowledges
                     efforts aimed at fostering peace and security between Rwanda and its neighbors.               Internally

                     particularly the size o f World Bank disbursements, is an issue, though alignment o f donor

                                      I. INTRODUCTION

 1.        The Fourth Poverty Reduction Support Grant (PRSG 4) represents the first in the
second series of PRSGs to support Rwanda's second poverty reduction strategy the Economic
                                                                                        -
Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS). Given the strong focus o f the EDPRS on
growth, this proposed second series o f PRSGs will scale up support to growth-oriented measures, with
an emphasis on improving the delivery o f economic and infrastructure services. Beginning with
PRSG 4, this series o f PRSGs will continue to support reforms in the social sectors, to sustain the
progress made under the first series o f PRSGs and provide the framework for dialogue and action on
governance and social protection issues. The consolidation o f the reforms in the social sectors is
needed to improve the skills o f the labor force, which the recent Country Economic Memorandum
(CEM) noted as critical to sustained long-term growth.           The C E M also found that the low
infrastructure base, coupled with the high cost o f delivering related services, currently pose the most
immediate constraints to growth and poverty reduction. Furthermore, the Diagnostic Trade Integrated
Study (DTIS) found the poor state of infrastructure to be the most binding constraint to the sustained
growth o f exports and trade.

2.        The first series o f Poverty Reduction Support Grants (PRSG 1 to 3) supported Rwanda's
first Poverty Reduction Strategy Program (PRSP) by laying the foundation for improved service
delivery for human development and growth. Overall, the series supported: (i)      improvements in the
quality, coverage, and equity o f basic service delivery to support human development, with a focus on
outcome-oriented spending; and (ii)an enhanced overall public expenditure management and
economic governance process, with emphasis on results and expanding the voice and participation o f
citizens and (iii)policy measures to improve the investment climate and development o f the private
sector, with a focus on export promotion.

3.        Implementationover the course of PRSG 1to 3 has been satisfactory, and has resulted
in substantial achievements. According to the two household surveys (EICV), primary school net
enrollment rose from 72 in 2002 to 95 percent in 2006, and completion rates increased from 30 to 52
percent.   According to the 2005 Demographic Health Survey (DHS), immunization rates have
improved considerably with a coverage rate o f 85 percent for DPT3, over the period, which i s now
among the highest in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). The use o f insecticide-treated bed nets increased
from 4 to 40 percent o f the population between 2004 and 2006, and i s expected to reach 60 percent by
end-2007. Access to health insurance was scaled up from 7 to 55 percent o f the population between
2003 and 2006, leadingto increased use of health services (e.g., assisted deliveries have increased, and
under five mortality decreased by 25 percent from 2000 to 2005, bringingrates back to pre-genocide
levels).  Infrastructure services are also malung gains: through local-level and private operator
schemes, an additional 600,000 people inrural areas gained access to potable water in 2005; and over
the past three years, an additional one million people (14 percent o f the population) gained access to
safe water. In the energy sector, the GoR was able to pass through the cost o f oil price increases in
2005, and i s now aggressively pursuingthe development o f Lake Kivu gas to ease energy costs and
increase supply and access for the population inthe future.

4.        With the post-conflict economicgrowth rebound over, GDP growthwas inthe range of
4-6 percent during the period 2004-06 in contrast to the average annual growth rate of 9.3
percent between 1996 and 2001. Ifthe growth rates remain inthe range o f 4 to 6 percent inthe long
term, the GoR's 2020 poverty reduction targets are unlikely to be met. Inaddition, the recent poverty

5.           This PRSG series will therefore support the GoR's increased focus on growth as
outlined in the EDPRS while consolidatingthe progress made in the social sectors under the first
PRSP. Starting with the proposed PRSG 4 for an amount o f SDR 44.1 million (equivalent to US$70
million), and continuing with PRSG 5 and 6, each for an amount o f SDR 38.4 million (equivalent to
US$60 million), there will increased emphasis on the transport infkastructure and services to the
agriculture sector, as well as policies to improve the business and investment climate with continued
support to decentralized service delivery, particularly in education, health, and water.                      In close
collaboration with the International Monetary Fund (IMF), the PRSG will continue to support the
maintenance o f a stable macro-environment particularly to contain inflation and maintain competitive
exchange rates in the context o f scaled-up external assistance. Support for fiscal decentralization will
focus on improving monitoring and reporting on district spending, to ensure alignment o f fiscal and
monetary policies and sound use o f resources by decentralized entities. PRSG 4 will also support
ongoing refoms to strengthen the integration and the results orientation o f the Medium Term
Expenditure Framework (MTEF) and the budget process, at both the national and district levels.
Overall, the operation will emphasize support for refoms that promote increased spending efficiency,
to lay the foundation for increased absorption and effectiveness o f higher aid inflows. This series o f
PRSGs will also continue to support Rwanda's efforts on good governance, particularly in regard to
buildinginstitutionalcapacity inthe areas o fprocurement andpublic financial management.

Socio-Political Developments

6.           Since 1994, the Government has taken actions to promote reconciliation and peace,
resettle and reintegrate refugees and displaced persons and ex-combatants, and bring justice to
the thousands of people suspected to have participated in the genocide. A National Unity and
Reconciliation Commission has been working to redress Rwanda's legacy o f divisive politics through
wide national dialogue and civic education initiatives. Further, about 3.5 million refugees have been
repatriated and resettled,2 and more than 37,000 ex-combatants have been demobilized, o f which about
15,000, including the former commander-in-chief o f the (FDLR),3 have been integrated into the
Rwanda Defense Force at various command levels. A system o f gacaca courts, modeled after a
traditional approach to settling disputes, was established as a means o f engaging citizens in the
genocide trials. The current estimate i s that around 78,000 people remain to be tried in 12,100 gacaca
courts throughout the country. A new constitutionpromulgatedin 2003, provided the legal framework
for managing, controlling, and resolving social conflicts, and for power sharing at the top level o f
government. According to the constitution, the president cannot appoint more than 50 percent o f the
cabinet from hisher own party, and the speaker o f the parliament has to come from the minority party.
The decentralization process provided an additional framework for improving political governance
throughmechanisms for enhancing transparency, accountability and voice.

 8.         The first phase of decentralization (2000-03) established elected community
 development structures, and initiated the process of building their capacities. The policy and
 institutional frameworks were adopted to specify the roles and responsibilities o f central and
 decentralized structures and the mechanisms for the financing o f services and accountability. The
 second phase (2004-08) i s aimed to consolidate and deepen decentralization through efforts to
 strengthen decentralized service delivery to communities within an integrated accountability
 framework. There have been efforts to promote the participation and involvement o f communities in
 the planning and management o f their affairs; and to mainstream a "bottom-up" approach to
 developing sectoral strategies and plans, most notably through the District Development Plans. As part
 o f this second phase, a Territorial Reform Law that came into effect in January 2006 reduced the
number o f provinces from 13 to 5 (including Kigali City); the number o f districts from 106 to 30; and
the number o f secteurs from 1,545 to 418 (the number o f cellules, the lowest level o f local
 administration, remained at 9,165). Local elections for district mayors and district councils were held
in February and March 2006. The Government prepared and adopted the Rwanda Strategic
Decentralization Framework to guide the third phase o f the decentralization that will involve a
continuous process o f improving, supporting, and sustaining achievements o f the previous phases.

9.          The decentralizationprocessalso encompassessystems of accountabilityandvoice that
are basedon traditionalRwandaninstitutionsand customs. For example, the Umudugudu process
i s being used to elaborate an integrated system o f social protection with an emphasis on employment
and income generation. Imihigo i s a performance management contract signed between the President
o f the Republic and the district mayors on behalf o f their constituents. The contract includes a set o f
development targets backed by specific performance indicators over a period o f one year. This system
has helped to further galvanize the process o f decentralization but needs to be more closely aligned
with the overall budget preparation and monitoring process. The monitoring process is already
building on Ubudehe, a traditional system o f consultation being used to actively engage local
communities inthe EDPRS process, to facilitate bottom upplanning and monitoring o f programs.

 10.       The second phase of decentralization has highlightedthe need to quickly put in place
reporting mechanisms and elaborate a framework for oversight. Although the decentralization
process aims to help districts develop their own financing sources, intergovernmental transfers will
remain the main source o f revenue for local governments for the foreseeable future. The design and
monitoring o f these transfers i s o f crucial importance for the success o f the decentralization program.
The current system o f intergovernmental transfers i s based largely on a recurrent block grant to
finance administrative costs (including salaries), and a development grant through the Common
Development Fund (CDF) to fund capital project^.^ In addition to these flows, a significant level o f
international funding contributes to local development priorities on either an on- or off-budget basis.
The Fiscal and Financial Decentralization Policy, adopted by the Government in 2006, sets the
objectives and guidingprinciples for the future reform work.

Monetary and Financial Sector Policies and Performance

17.        With scaled-up aid, the Government has taken measures to diffuse pressure for
appreciation of the Rwandan franc. Since 2004, the Rwanda franc has appreciated moderately,
following a depreciation o f the real effective exchange rate, despite increased aid flows. Rwanda's
exchange rate regime i s a managed float, and it has been able to control and manage the exchange rate
and liquidity effects o f increased aid flows. The central bank relies mainly on foreign exchange sales
to sterilize liquidity in the market, limiting the use o f domestic debt instruments and minimizing the
crowding out o f the private sector. Since June 2007, the central bank i s selling foreign exchange
directly to banks at a predetermined reference rate, the previous auction-based system has been
abolished.

18.        Other sources o f liquidity in the economy arise from the growth of the microfinance sector,
which in turn has contributed to the growth in broad money. The increase in domestic credit, mainly
from the private sector, has significantly contributed to broad money (M2) growing, on average, by 14
percent from 2002 to 2005, and then by 32 percent in 2006. The growth in domestic credit was, in
turn, driven by growth inprivate sector credit, while net credit to government decreased during the
whole period due to the accumulation o f deposits originating from external aid. In terms o f external
aid inflows, in the form o f budget and project support, net foreign assets increased continuously,
averaging a growth rate of 37 percent between 2002 and 2006. Gross international reserves remained
inthe range of 5 to 6 months of imports. The growth rate of reserve money, the operational target
used to monitor inflation, averaged 12 percent.

 19.       To control inflation, the central bank uses various instruments, including setting the
level of reserve requirements, money market interventions, as well as an interest rate corridor
and a policy rate. In 2005, an overnight deposit facility was introduced to aid in sterilizing
excess liquidity of commercial banks. The rate for the overnight facility forms the lower limit o f an
rate interest corridor, while the upper limit i s the rate the central bank applies as lender o f last resort. A
new policy rate i s fixed within this corridor, reflecting market conditions and guiding interest rates in
line with the level o f inflation as well as other monetary parameters. Overall, the financial sector
indicators attest to the soundness o f the sector and the GoR has recently adopted a Financial Sector
Development Plan (FSDP) to promote the growth and efficiency o f operations o f the sector. The
central bank intends to strengthen supervision and regulation o f micro-finance institutions to improve
stability inthe sector.

Debt Sustainability

20.        The external debt stock was reduced substantially after Rwanda reached the HIPC
(Heavily Indebted Poor Countries Initiative) completion point in 2005. External debt as a percentage
of GDP fell from 91.7 percent in2004 to only 19.2 percent in2006. As the recent DSA update shows,
Rwanda's external debt situation in2006 was more favorable than envisaged in the last DSA, due to a
stronger than expected export performance, especially in the coffee, tea and tourism sectors. Despite
these favorable developments, the current D S A concludes that Rwanda remains at highrisk o f debt
distress, because o f its small export base.

21.        Rwanda reached the completion point in April 2005 and qualified for the MDRI in January
2006, which substantially improved its debt indicators. External debt amounted to 19 percent o f GDP
at the end o f 2006, down from 70 percent at the end o f 2005. Domestic debt6amounted to 14 percent
of GDP at the end o f 2006. Interms o f the compositionby creditor, about half o f the public debt at the
end o f 2006 was with multilaterals and 10 percent with bilaterals while the remaining 40 percent were
domestic debt. The NPV o f external debt-to-exports ratio was 55 percent at the end o f 2006, which i s
lower than the estimate inthe last DSA.

22.        It is assumed that most o f the increased spending, which is necessary to address Rwanda's
severe infrastructure constraints and advance more rapidly towards the MDGs, would be covered by
higher revenues and grants, but with some increases in external loan financing. Within the baseline
scenario, similar to the previous DSA, external and public debt servicing bothremain manageable over
the projection period through 2027 at below 10 percent o f exports and 3-5 percent o f revenue,
respectively. The NPV of the external debt-to-GDP ratio as well as the NPV o f the debt-to-revenue
ratio remain below their thresholds throughout the forecast period. However, despite one third o f
financing coming from highly concessional loans, the NPV o f external debt-to-exports ratio would
breach the policy-dependent threshold o f 150 percent by 2011, peaking at about 210 percent in 2020
and declining to 192 percent in2027.

                            A.      RWANDA'S       POVERTY        REDUCTION

32.        Rwanda's first Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP-1) laid the foundation for
sustainable peace and sustained social and economic recovery and poverty reduction. The PRSP-1
covering the period 2002-2006, outlined six priority action areas:              (i)rural development and
agricultural transformation; (ii)human development; (iii)economic infrastructure; (iv) good
governance; (v) private sector development; and (f)institutional capacity building. It also stressed the
need to immediately address the issues o f HIV/AIDS, shelter for the homeless, the demobilization o f
ex-combatants and the resettlement and reintegration o f all refugees. A new poverty reduction
strategy, the Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS), refocuses
Rwanda's priority on growth and human development, with emphasis on decentralization, and a
greater role for the private sector. The EDPRS provides a road map for the next five years (2008-2012)
to government, development partners, the private sector and civil society indicating where Rwanda
wants to go, what it needs to do to get there, what the journey i s going to cost and how it will be
financed. The strategy provides a medium-term framework for achieving the Millennium
Development Goals, in the context o f the country's long term development aspirations embodied in
Rwanda's Vision 2020.

33.       The preparation of the EDPRS involved extensive consultation over a period of more
than one year with a wide range of stakeholders. This process was overseen by a National Policy

                                                            10

Steering Group (NPSG), comprised o f central government ministers and provincial governors. The
NPSG was supported by a Technical Steering Group, made up of secretary generals o f ministries,
provincial executive secretaries, and representatives of donors, civil society, faith-based organisations
and the private sector. In addition, nineteen sector worlung groups (SWGs) were involved in setting

small-holders from a liberalized fertilizer market. Nevertheless, there are signs that commodity based
cooperatives are now playing increasing roles, to address the micro-level constraints, by improving
access of small-holders to fertilizers, with bulk purchases at wholesale prices, credit guarantees for
farmers and associated extension services.

47.        Some important institutional and policy measures taken under PRSP-1 augur well for
the future performance of the agricultural sector. These actions include the enactment o f the land
law, the privatization o f some o f the tea factories and estates, the development o f a time bound and
monitorable Export Promotion Action Plan, the adoption o f the PSTA and the development o f an
MTEF in line with it, and the operationalization o f Animal and Resources Development Authority

water management and conservation with the aim of reducing the heavy reliance on rain-fed
agriculture, and the significant negative impact rainfall variability has had on agriculture GDP.

Economic governance

51.         Reforms have focused on improving financial management, decentralization of
decision making and delivery of public services, strengthening the civil service and capacity
building. Several measures have been taken to improve budget management, including the
introduction o f the medium term expenditure framework (MTEF) to enhance the alignment between

Flagship2: Vision 2020 Umurenge

67.        As a flagship program, the Vision 2020 Umurenge program (WP) aims to emphasize
poverty reducing interventions at grassroots levels. The VUP is a pro-poor growth and export
oriented strategy aimed at releasing the productive capacity o f the poor and extremely poor who
comprise 56 and 37 percent respectively o f the population. The 2005-06 EICV2 household survey
showed that the number o f poor households had grown since 2001, because population growth had
more than offset a fall in the proportion o f families that were poor. The Government concluded that
the relatively high rates o f economic growth o f 5 percent per annum were not yet translating into
stronger growth in rural employment and incomes. Accordingly, it developed the new Umurenge
flagship program for poverty reduction, accelerated growth, and social protection that augments
central guidelines for socio-economic transformation (i.e. economic growth, job creation, and extreme
poverty eradication) with local participatory mechanisms. The VUP will promote off-farm
employment, with incentives to promote this aspect at the local level; it will aim to accelerate the
process o f monetizationand formalization o f the economy; and finally it will redirect social protection
to the neediest, including the landless and those who are unable to work. The W P i s currently in the
pilot phase, and i s targeted to the poorest imirenge in each o f the 30 districts, and operates in the rural
areas, to complement the nation-wide social protectionprogram.

68.        The W P has three components: The first component consists of planned public works
based on community-based participation approaches (i.e. ubudehe), with the aim o f building
community assets, and creating off-farm employment. These could be in agriculture or livestock or
off-farm manufacturing or service activities. Assets produced will need to benefit the community at
large, to provide the incentives for communities to maintain and sustain them. The secondcomponent
aims to develop cooperative and small- and medium-sized enterprises (SME), with credit packages to
tackle extreme poverty and foster entrepreneurship and off-farm employment. These packages will
involve the private financial sector and micro-finance institutions, in the form o f formal contracts that
describe the use of credit inpre-specified activities, agreed upon and endorsed by the community and
approved by the VUP Management Team. The third component o f the VUP will support improved
access to social services or provide for landless households with no members qualifying for public
works or credit packages. The aim will be to expand support to education and health and the
development o f appropriate skills, handicrafts, or social service activities.       For those individuals
unable to participate in public works or who lack the productive capacity to take on credit packages,
direct support through grants will be provided. These individuals would include the most vulnerable
and those who do not have sufficient and reliable support from sonddaughters or remittances from
relatives. However, beneficiaries will be expected to engage in relevant skills acquisition activities,
handicraft, and social service activities. Since the VUP will initially be adopted on a pilot basis, it will
not substitute for social protection programs. Instead, it provides an opportunity to evaluate the
possibility o f enabling socially protected individuals or households to progress to appropriate
productive activities.

                                             D.       LESSONSLEARNED

LessonsLearnedduringImplementationof PRSG 1-3

87.          Several lessons have emerged in the course of implementation of PRSG 1-3. First,
PRSG 1-3 demonstrated that substantial outcomes can be achieved with strong commitment on the
part o f Government and with Bank support designed to develop the needed capacity for
implementation. This i s particularly evident in the education, health, water and agriculture sectors.
PRSG-1 started off with substantial focus on capacity support for development o f sector strategies and
MTEFs, with attention to the resource constraint and an assessment o f trade-offs and various options
to reach targets in the education and health sectors. Support provided by the Bank (working closely
with key donors), in these areas greatly facilitated the move toward a SWAP in the education sector,
and i s now leadingto discussions o f similar arrangements inthe health and water sectors. Discussions
to move to a SWAP are also being held in the agriculture, transport, and energy sectors.                           The
experience with the social sectors indicates that substantial improvements in access to services in the
infrastructure sectors will require substantial capacity support toward sector strategy development,
planning and MTEFdevelopment.

                                                          27

88.        Another lesson learned is that in the context of low capacity, the design of
decentralization and civil service reforms should be such that they pose no further tax on
already constrained capacity. This is particularly evident in the experience with decentralization
reforms. GoR should be commended for the successful way inwhich it has carried out the first phase
o f its comprehensive civil service reform program (with support from DFID), including large
retrenchments. However, in the short-term it has further diminished the already taxed capacity o f
central and local government ministries and agencies. This i s particularly evident inthe infrastructure
sector. In the second phase o f its reforms, the GoR, while it competitively recruits and trains
candidates for new positions, i s workmg with its donor partners to ensure that the capacity building
needs o f the ministries and agencies are met. The PRSG, inparticular, will focus support on the areas
o f financial and expenditure management, procurement, monitoring and evaluation.

89.        A third lesson learned pertains to the importance of sound analytical work in
informing policy dialogue and reforms, as conveyed through the response from development
partners, as part of the Bank country office consultations on conditionality and Bank policy
operations (that took place inKigali inJuly 2007). However, development partners stressed the need
to ensure that all stakeholders are consulted and own the outcome together. Thus, this second series o f
PRSGs will continue to support and encourage joint analytical work with other donors and with
government.

90.        Finally, the consultations on conditionality and Bank policy, noted that the Bank's
support (and that of other donors), still entailed transaction costs and a move toward a lean and
harmonized accountability framework based on the EDPRS will significantly lessen the problem
and improve ownership. Results o f the consultation indicated the need to have more focused,
streamlined and enforceable benchmarks. It was suggested that a Common Performance Assessment
Framework (CPAF) was needed around the EDPRS. The consensus was that ensuring that the CPAF
i s government-driven, widely consulted on, endorsed by key stakeholders, and focused on the critical
success indicators, which would translate to a significant reduction intransaction costs, ensure smooth
implementation and sharply increase the strength o f dialogue between Government and development
partners. Therefore support to the elaboration o f a CPAF will be one key area o f focus inPRSG-4.

Box 2: GoodPracticePrincipleson Conditionality

Principle 1: Reinforcecountry ownership
The Government o f Rwanda has a strong track record in committing to and implementing reforms. The PRSG
supports the Government's program, the Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS),
which was developed ina participatory manner involving extensive consultation o f a wide range o f stakeholders.
The process was led by a National Steering Committee, comprised o f Ministers and Governors, providing high
level guidance. The Bank has actively supported the Government in this process. The Government has
furthermore completed an Aid Policy, articulating the Government's vision for improved aid management and
policies.

Principle 2: Agree up front with the government and other financial partners on a coordinated
accountabilityframework
The Government and the budget support donors have agreed on a common assessment framework for public
financial management (PEFA). In a further step, the Government is currently in the process o f elaborating a
common performance assessment framework (CPAF) in consultation with all development partners. The CPAF
will be basedon the policy matrix o f the EDPRS and represent a time boundand monitorable action plan. Given
the timing in completing the EDPRS and the Government's urgent need for the financing offered through PRSG-
4, it was agreed with Government and discussed with donors, to proceed with preparation o f PRSG-4. Initial
discussion o n priority areas o f support that could from part o f the CPAF would be used in preparation o f the
PRSG-4 operation thereby enabling Government to have access to support for the 2008 budget year and also
provide room for adequate consultation on the development o f a CPAF. PRSG-5 onward would therefore be
fully aligned with the CPAF.

Principle 3: Customize the accountability framework and modalities of Bank support to country
circumstances
The PRSG series i s fully aligned with the EDPRS and has been developed through extensive consultations with
the Government and the lead development partners for the key sectors. The PRSG should be seen as the overall
umbrella for Bank support and policy dialogue, which i s complemented by specific projects providing focused
investment assistance. Rwanda has established a track record in budget support and project implementation,
therefore budget support i s seen as the ideal instrument to address a broader range o f issues with reduced
transaction costs while at the same time strengthening the Government's systems.

Principle4: Chooseonly actions critical for achievingresultsas conditionsfor disbursement
The PRSG focuses on a few selected triggers and prior actions, representing critical steps for implementing the
Government's program. They are based on the EDPRS policy matrix and evolved from extensive discussion
with the Government and other relevant stakeholders. Ten prior actions are included for PRSG-4 and ten
indicative triggers for PRSG-5. A higher number o f indicators are included inthe PRSG policy matrix to be able
to monitor the sectors covered under the program indetail to assess their performance.

Principle 5: Conduct transparent progress reviews conducive to predictable and performance-based
financial support
A full review o f the program takes place in line with the reviews agreed inthe Partnership Framework between
the Government o f Rwanda and the budget support donors. Joint budget reviews take place twice a year, aligned
with the budget cycle. A secretariat inMJNECOFINhas taken over the lead role incoordination and preparation
of the reviews. A harmonization calendar i s inplace, providing the time line for key reviews. Inaddition, sector
working groups conduct sector reviews, aligned with the budget cycle.

                                A.       POVERTY AND SOCIALIMPACTS

127.       Overall, the reforms and measures to be supported under this operation are geared
toward improving access of rural households to services to support their income generating and

assessment o f the performance financing approach in the health sector will also be used to assess the
impact on outcomes and strengthen design o f the system. In addition, further examination o f the
reasons for differences in educational attainment between boys and girls will inform development o f
the strategy for improving access for both girls and boys.

F.       RISKS AND RISKMITIGATION

 136.       There are four main risks potentially associated with the program as follows: i)
macroeconomic and financial sector risk; ii)regional/country risk; iii)program risk; and iv) fiduciary
risk.

 137.       Macroeconomic and Financial Risk. This is rated as a low risk given the GoR's record in
macroeconomic management. Overall GoR's record has been rated as satisfactory as evidenced also
through the satisfactory completion o f reviews under the PRGF. Mitigation o f this risk i s achievable
as long as GoR continues to implement reforms to increase investmentto support growth.

 138.       Managing large injections of liquidity into the economy i s challenging and may present
significant dilemmas for the Government and the macro-economic situation. Despite the higher aid
flows, the level o f need continues to outweigh the available amount o f aid. There may be a need for
Government to take on some concessional loans, on a selective basis, to enable the necessary
investments to generate growth.        The World Bank and the IMF will continue to support the
Government in managing macroeconomic reform and investing in infrastructure development to
ensure continued growth and productivity.          In the context of the low income country debt
sustainability framework, the Bank and Fundwill continue to monitor the evolution in Rwanda's debt
indicators which are currently substantially below set thresholds. In terms o f any appreciation o f the
exchange rate and the likely effects on exports, it will be critical to put ineffect policies to improve the
supply response, ease the constraints to productivity and support increased absorption. This is the
main area o f focus o f the PRSG operation. In addition, Government has demonstrated through its
management o f previous injections o f liquidity into the economy, its commitment to use market based
instruments to ensure that inflation i s contained and any potential exchange rate appreciation i s well
managed. Furthermore, the level o f gross reserves in months o f imports has been between 5 and 6,
providing adequate import coverage and underlining the government's commitment to prudent
macroeconomic management o f aid inflows.

139.        RegionaYCountry Risk. One continued risk factor to Rwanda's political and economic

international community. The signature on November 9, 2007 o f a Joint Communique between the
Governments o f Rwanda and the Democratic Republic o f Congo with the objective o f resolving the
question o f the presence o f combatants o f Rwandese armed groups on Congolese territory in a
collaborative manner i s an important step forward. At the international level, both the United Nations
and the African Union have continued to support the establishment o f peace inthe region. Inaddition,
the Tripartite Plus Joint Commission continues to work toward identifying measures to counter the
negative forces operating in the region. The recent high-level meeting in Addis Ababa hosted by the
U S and attended by Rwanda, Uganda and Burundi i s further evidence o f the diplomatic efforts o f the
international community to finda lasting solution.

140.        Currently, Rwanda's internal security and political situation remains stable. However
continued effort i s needed to address the remaining political effects o f the 1994 genocide. Constraints
surrounding the Gacaca system have given rise to contentions both by victims and perpetrators o f the
genocide, and highlighted the need to strengthen ongoing efforts to build a strong judiciary.
Government efforts to continue to open the political space also need to continue. As part o fthis effort,
the law on NGO groups i s being substantially revisedin line with internationalexperience.

141.        Program Risk. Predictability o f donor funding, the ability to mobilize donor funding and
debt sustainability continue to be the main program risks. DFID's three-year rolling program within
an overall ten year commitment provides a good model for enhancing the long-term predictability o f
donor funding. There i s a general move toward more budget support as evidenced by support from the
Netherlands and Germany. It i s also encouraging that other donors, such as Canada, have expressed

                                                 43

interest in providing budget support to the GoR. Improvements in frontloading by donors will also
help the GoR to plan and implement its budget in a more sustainable manner. In the short-term, the
Bank has worked to help the GoR access the Catalytic Fund for Africa. However, over the long-term,
the GoR will needto continue to focus on mobilizing additional grant financing, which i s critical ifthe
GoR i s to remain within prudent debt sustainability limits.

142.       Fiduciary Risk. The rapid decentralization undertaken bythe GoR has heightened potential
procurement and public financial management risks since only limited accompanying fiduciary
measures were included. However, the GoR has committed to accelerating its efforts to address these
risks. The GoR i s drafting an easy-reference guide for procurement, along with a technical assistance
"hotline" for procurement officers. It i s also accelerating the implementation o f the Rwanda Expertise
Scheme that i s training accountants, to be deployed at decentralized levels, in the Rwanda public
accounting system, and sponsoring the best among them for professional accountancy qualification.

1.      The Government of Rwanda (GoR) has continued to implement an ambitious
reform program for growth and poverty reduction. Rwanda's Economic Development and
Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS) refocuses the country's priority on growth and
human development with an emphasis on decentralization and a greater role of the
private sector. It i s built on the foundations laid by the implementation of the first
Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP), covering the years 2002-2006, which was
elaborated in a post-conflict environment and focused primarily on sectors that would
yield immediate growth benefits and build the human resource and infrastructure base.
The government has made progress in establishing the institutions and legal framework
needed for successful implementation of the PRSP. Reforms have included trade
liberalization and privatization, improved public financial and expenditure management,
and decentralization, as a means to increase voice and accountability. Structural
reforms have helped to maintain both good macro-economic performance and
substantial improvements in the social sector indicators. In addition t h e Government's
program was supported by the close alignment of public spending to the priority areas of
the PRSP. We expect that the PRSG-4 will help u s in implementing the next steps in the
reform process and we will rely o n the Bank's future support in the form of this second
PRSG series to help u s implement the EDPRS.

2.      The EDPRS provides a road map for the next five years (2008-2012) to
government, development partners, the private sector and civil society indicating where
Rwanda wants to go, what it needs to do to get there, what the journey is going to cost,
how it will be financed and how it will be monitored. The strategy provides a medium-
term framework for achieving the Millennium Development Goals, in the context of the
country's long term development aspirations embodied in Rwanda's Vision 2020. The
preparation of the EDPRS involved extensive consultation over a period of more than one


                                          46

year with a wide range of stakeholders. The priorities of the strategy are embodied in
three flagship programs: (a) Sustainable Growth for Jobs and Exports, (b) Vision 2020
Umurenge and (c) Governance.

3.      The EDPRS assigns highest priority to accelerating growth to create employment
and generate exports. It aims to achieve this through an ambitious, high quality public
investment program aimed at reducing the operational costs of business that would
create strong incentives for the private sector to increase its investments. With two
thirds of the population aged less than 25 years, particular emphasis will be placed on
creating jobs for young people. To facilitate accelerated reduction in poverty, we have
elaborated a highly decentralized integrated rural development program, called Vision
2020 Umurenge. The aim is to pilot the approach in thirty of the poorest sectors
(imirenge) of the country. We intend to strengthen governance by building on Rwanda's
reputation as a country with a low incidence of, and zero toleration for corruption. In the
next five years, Rwanda plans to develop a regional comparative advantage in 'soft
infrastructure', understood as those aspects of governance, related to well-defined
property rights, efficient public administration, transparency and accountability in fiscal
and regulatory matters.

4.      Overall, Rwanda's macroeconomic performance continues to be on track. Real
GDP growth averaged 5.1 percent between 2002 and 2006, mainly driven by the
agricultural sector and improved performance of the manufacturing and services sectors.
In the area of private sector led growth substantial progress has been made in the
privatization of major companies and it is planned to privatize the remaining tea
factories in the near future. We will also continue the implementation of the export
promotion strategy, which has already produced good results, which i s especially
important in the context of debt sustainability and the resulting financing envelope that
is available to Rwanda. Export receipts have been increasing for almost all sectors and
strategies have been developed or are under development for the key export products to
align the activities of the different stakeholders and facilitate the implementation of the
national export strategy. We are also implementing our Strategic Plan for Agricultural
Transformation (PSTA). The key agricultural institutions, the Rwanda Agricultural
Development Authority (RADA), the Rwanda Animal Resources Development Authority
(RARDA) and the research and extension agency (ISAR) are operational and will help to
ensure access to inputs and agricultural services. Regarding the improvement of service
delivery, we have been focusing o n the key sectors of education, health, water and
energy. Decentralized service delivery has been one of the main successes of the PRSG
support, and we look forward to further achievements in this area as we continue our
decentralization program.

Education


5.      In order for Rwanda to make the transition to a knowledge-based economy and
society, it i s essential to improve access to quality, equitable and effective education. The
Education Sector has a range of policies in place to achieve this and to ensure that the
country attains the Millennium Development Goals, Education for All, and Rwanda
Vision 2020. Policy and planning documents have been developed include an Education
Sector Strategic Plan (ESSP 2007-2011), a ten year education plan and a Long Term
Strategy and Financial Framework (LTSFF 2007-20 15). These have been endorsed by
development partners. The acceptance of Rwanda into the Education For All Fast Track
Initiative (EFA-FTI) o n the basis of the LTSFF, means that an additional US$70 million
has been promised for 2007 and 2008 to help Rwanda reach the MDGs.

6.      Following the substantial increases in primary n e t enrollment (estimated at 95
percent), the main challenge has been to accommodate the increasing number of students

                                             47

while improving quality. Higher primary enrollment rates have also translated into
expanded demand for post-primary education as increasing numbers move through the
full nine-year basic education cycle. At the primary level, improving quality requires
more teachers with better qualifications. For secondary education the current framework
will need to be restructured to accommodate a larger number of students and a diversity
of abilities and needs. A rapid increase in enrollment could exacerbate the differences in
learning outcomes for boys and girls. Boys still outperform girls o n national exams, and
in terms of completing primary education and transitioning to secondary and higher
education.

7.      The rapid expansion in primary education has not been matched by an increase
in the number of teachers, classrooms and learning materials. The pupil-teacher ratio
increased from 5 1 percent in 2001 to 69 percent by 2005 and 71 percent by 2006,

address the issue of rapidly meeting the need for teachers. The needed expansion of

the population density and national targets-i.e. school mapping, as well as careful
planning and coordination between the Ministry of Education (MINEDUC) and the

education institutions to facilitate the increased supply of skilled labor, in particular, by
targeting loans and grants to priority areas of economic development such as science
and technology.

Science, Technology and Innovation

9.      The GoR's policy in STI will complement and align efforts to improve quality and
relevance of the education sector to the needs of the economy, by facilitating stronger
links between skills development, higher education and the private sector. In July 2005,
the Cabinet approved a National Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) Policy that
puts emphasis on promotion of knowledge acquisition, creation, transfer, and a culture
of innovation. For the implementation of the policy, the Government created the Ministry
of Science, Technology and Scientific Research under the President's Office in March
2006 and, in partnership with DFID, is in the process of developing a legal and
regulatory framework and governing institutions for science and technology. The
Ministry also began work with the African Development Bank o n a science-based Higher
Education Strategy and has also developed a comprehensive cross-sectoral STI capacity
building technical assistance program in partnership with the World Bank and DFID.
This latter program i s designed to build STI capacity to diversify exports, increase
wealth, reduce poverty, boost economic development and improve living conditions.

10.     Rwanda's commitment to building science and technology capacity also forms an
essential pillar of Vision 2020. Specifically, the national policy on Science, Technology
and Innovation aims to build a well-rounded, balanced capability in science and
technology in order to:

            Promote sustained growth of GDP;
            Advance the quality of life and standards of living for the citizens of Rwanda;

                                            48

            Improve skills and knowledge among the population;
            Maintain viability of and enhance opportunities for growth in ruralareas; and
            Integrate technical education with commerce, industry and the private sector.

 11.    STI needs are evident in areas related to poverty reduction and growth. Much of
the technical knowledge that Rwanda needs to improve the well-being of farmers and
diversify the economy is already in use in other countries. Rwanda needs to build the
STI capacity to acquire, adapt, and utilize this knowledge. In the case of poverty
reduction, the needs are very much centered on improving productivity in the rural
areas, and increasing value added production. For example, water management is one
area where straightforward adoption of existing technologies could greatly increase
productivity. Even though the rains come twice a year, some regions in the country may
get more precipitation than others, and sometimes the rains may be delayed, which in
turn significantly impacts production. Yet, there are no simple technologies to harvest
and preserve water, whether it is in the rural or urban areas. In the rural areas, there
are limited storage facilities. As a result, surplus food rots due to the lack of storage and
processing capacity, while many of the people who produced these crops do not have the
security of a year-round stable food supply. At the same time, productivity of such staple
crops as rice, beans and cassava is below that of neighboring countries. Also, shortage of
electricity and overdependence on biomass adversely affects productive sectors, schools,
health centers and households.

 12.   To accelerate economic growth, the focus needs to be on developing skills to
support technologies that increase value added production. The STI capacity building
challenge is to train farmers, entrepreneurs, engineers, technicians, scientists and
teachers to find appropriate technologies, import them and adapt them to local
conditions to solve local problems and produce and market higher value, more
knowledge intensive goods and services. This, in turn, means that Rwanda will need to
improve the quality of its applied engineering and scientific research institutes as well as
its technical and vocational education. Skills development will also be needed to build
the work force to adopt and adapt these technologies. Last but not least, enterprises will
not be able to exploit the competitive opportunities generated by appropriate
technologies if their workers do not have the requisite skills to use new technology or
perform more complex tasks, or if local enterprises do not have t h e organizational and
managerial skills and technical competence to invest, innovate, and compete o n the
basis of quality rather than cheap labor. Unfortunately, Rwanda currently suffers from a
shortage of skilled technicians and craftsmen. At the same time, graduates from the few
schools that do exist are having difficulty finding jobs, apparently because graduates do
not receive appropriate technical training. STI capacity building, therefore, needs to
encompass innovative ways of delivering and financing technical and vocational
education.

13.    Inmid-2006, the Government of Rwanda and World Bank, with financial support
primarily from the Norwegian Post-Primary Education Trust Fund, embarked on a two-
stage STI capacity building program. The first stage involved assembling teams of
Rwandan and international experts to prepare a series of STI capacity building needs
assessments and action plans. This stage has been completed. The second stage will
involve financing and implementing the specific policies and programs outlined in the
Needs Assessments and Action Plans. Funding for the second stage would be provided
primarily by bilateral donors and the Government's budget with support from the World
Bank through the PRSG-4, and a parallel science, technology and innovation capacity
building project.

15.      The main challenges in the health sector are to increase access to basic health

16.      The delivery of health services has experienced many innovations in recent years.
The contractual approach to health service delivery, linking transfers to performance of
the service providers, has contributed to strengthening the whole system. This approach

2006 and put fully o n the government budget. Similarly, community health schemes
transfer resources directly to the municipalities that use these resources to enter into

years. In 2006, the GoR transferred funds to cover premiums for the poorest people in

tertiary care by the poor.

 Electricity and Urban Water Supply

 19.     Rwanda needs to diversify its sources of power generation to meet growing
demand. In the short term, the only option for increasing power supply is high cost
thermal generation. With the country's main hydro potential already developed (with
exception of Nyaborongo hydro power project), Rwanda will have to adjust to a mixed
hydro-thermal system and build capacity to plan and manage its water resources.
Improving water management with the aim of mitigating the effects on the hydro power
 system due to the variability in rainfall will be key to improving electricity management
and generation from the existing and planned hydropower plants. Building capacity at
all levels of water management and improving the hydrological information network will
increase the capacity to deliver base and peak energy. Potential energy resources under
development are the Lake Kivu methane gas reserves for new thermal generation and
regional hydro projects such as the Rusumo Falls Hydropower project shared with
Burundi and Tanzania. Although, mini-hydro schemes will not fill a substantial part of
the power supply gap, these mini schemes could provide important regional
complements to the existing system, and could pave the way for broader private
participation in the sector and further decentralization of service delivery. The reduction

government subsidies, consisting of grant-financed investments, debt arrears, tax
exemptions, and outright subsidy payments. Furthermore, the existing single, flat tariff
is independent of energy consumed or customer type and will be reviewed and modified
as necessary. Despite two substantial tariff adjustments since 2004, Electrogaz'
electricity operations remain far below full cost recovery but since the tariffs are
relatively very high, the focus will be on reducing the costs of power production and
distribution.

Information Communications Technology (ICT)

2 1.    The Government sees ICT as having potential to transform society. At less than 5

Water Resources, Supply and Sanitation

25.     Rwanda is on track to reach the water and sanitation MDGs. In 2006, an
estimated 63 percent of Rwanda's total population had access to safe drinking water.
Spending on the water sector tripled in 2005 compared to 2004, and increased by one
third again in 2006, to about US$ 30 million. Access to clean water increased to an
additional 600,000 people in both 2005 and 2006. The introduction of private
participation into the operation of piped, rural water supply systems has been an
unprecedented success. The target of 20 percent of about 850 systems to be privately
managed by November 2007 is expected to be reached. O n the sanitation side, in 2005,
85 percent of the population had traditional household sanitation facilities, of which
only 38 percent were considered by the 2004 UNICEF/WHO JMP report as hygienic on-
site sanitation. All district performance contracts signed in 2006 committed to expand
their respective household sanitation coverage to 100 percent.

26.     However, the challenges ahead remain considerable. To reach the globally agreed
upon MDG objectives, sector funding will need to be further increased to about US$ 32-
35 million per year, and maintained at that level until 2015. Furthermore, the districts
need technical support to enable them to implement water and sanitation activities.
Effective cost recovery and private participation also will be necessary to ensure sector
sustainability. While Rwanda is relatively water rich, the sector's infrastructure suffered
from considerable destruction during the period of civil war and the genocide,
accompanied by years of underinvestment and poor management. In addition, rainfall
variability and the resulting floods and regional droughts place severe strains on the
country's economy. As the population increases, the availability of fresh water, both
spatially and temporally, i s becoming an important constraint on the water supply,
hydropower generation, and industrial and agricultural production. The added stress of
water quality deterioration, due to heavy siltation,         has led to environmental
deterioration, intensifying competing demands and disputes among users.

27.    The Government strategy for the water sector encompasses: (a) strengthening of
the decentralized approach, with rural districts fully in charge of the planning,
execution, and management of their water supply systems, and with the support of local
private operators; (b) increasing water production capacity, particularly in Kigali, to
improve the reliability and quality of services; (c) continuing the rehabilitation and
extension of networks in secondary towns; and (d) promoting the necessary paradigm
shift from the traditional water and sanitation approach towards integrated water
resources management and development (IWRM&D) for the sustainable multipurpose
use of water through (i) building the non-existing capacity to manage water resources for
growth by implementing the new water resources law and (ii)implementing
comprehensive river-basin planning, development and management of the water
resources in the country. The Government will also undertake specific actions under the
water supply and sanitation program: (i)   coordinate with donors to establish a sector-
wide basket fund in 2009 to finance investment and facilitate donor harmonization and
alignment with the country system, as recommended in the 2006 Paris Declaration; (ii)
endorsing the national sanitation strategy including the preparation of a sanitation
strategic plan by at least 25 percent of the districts by 2009; and (iii)  implementing a
nationwide action plan for the protection of the 25,000 spring catchments, with at least
90 percent of the catchments protected according to national standards by 2010.

Flagship 2: Vision 2020 Umurenge

Social Protection


35.     Discussions of SP policy that adopt the social risk management perspective, as
Rwanda has done, focus on three distinct challenges: reducing the major risks to which
households are vulnerable; reducing the consequences of those risks when they occur;
and helping households that experience the risk to deal with the consequences. For
Rwanda, the major systemic risks include famine and hunger from food shortages,
seasonal shortages of water for agriculture and of clean water year-round, land
degradation, pressure of population growth rates on land supply, disease, natural
disasters, large fluctuations in crop prices for agricultural producers, loss of traditional
markets for agricultural output, shortages of non-farm employment opportunities, and
social conflict. None of these systemic problems can be dealt with through social
protection policies alone. However, national policies that reduce these systemic risks or
the severity of their consequences will substantially reduce the severity of the challenges
facing social protection policy-makers.

36.     Specifically, Vision 2020 Umurenge identifies 4 categories of vulnerable groups to
whom specific programs will be targeted:


    0  For those considered unable to work at all, it will offer cash or in-kind social
       assistance, together with low-intensity work where feasible, and/ or training and
       microfinance options to facilitate self-sufficiency.

       For those able to undertake labor-intensive work but unable to find such work, it
       will offer paid labor-intensive employment either in a publicly financed public-
       works program or in agricultural employment. The income package could
       potentially include deferred elements to facilitate subsequent investments that
       could make these people economically self-sufficient.

       For those with land that provides at least a minimally adequate living standard,
       the program will offer opportunities for improved credit to allow financing of high-
       input, high-output modes of agriculture, while also seeking to promote improved
       options for social risk management (including management of risks from
       fluctuations in rainfall and in crop prices through improved rainfall retention,
       water-management, and crop-storage systems, and through expanded forward
       markets for post-harvest crop sales at pre-agreed prices).

       For all groups, the program will seek more efficient ways to offer appropriate
       forms of credit so that unsubsidized credit is available at lower cost and on more
      favorable or flexible terms than at present.

37.     Coordination mechanisms will be designed to ensure that activities are reflected
inthe policy framework of relevant ministries.

Fiscal Decentralization

45.    Rwanda adopted its National Decentralization Policy (NDP) in May 2000. In the
context of post-conflict Rwanda, with limited financial resources and capacity, the
decentralization program was a bold venture. But the Government decided to embark on
it and make adjustments along the way. Territorial reforms were completed in 2005
reducing the number of provinces, districts, sectors, and cellules, and thus the number
of local administrations. This set the stage for the phase of decentralization from 2006 to
2010 focusing on deepening the process. To this end, the Government developed the
Rwanda Decentralization Strategic Framework (RSDF) in July 2007 that provides a
framework of the coordination of technical and financial resources in support of
decentralization. It builds on and links to key national strategy documents -namely, the
Vision 2020, MDGs, EDPRS, and the Aid Development Strategy.


46.    A Decentralization Implementation Program (DIP), to cover the years 2008-20 12,
is currently under preparation. The DIP would provide the road map toward phasing out
the provincial level of Government, which was set up in the design phase as an interim
administrative level to coordinate the work of the new district administrations. At the
conceptual phase of decentralization, fiscal decentralization was seen as crucial to the
success of the decentralization process.


47.    Since both the revenue potential and capacity for collection of revenues of local
governments were limited, transfers from the central government were critical for the
local governments to carry out their mandates. The present system of intergovernmental
transfers is based largely o n block grants to finance administrative costs of local
governments, earmarked grants for specific services (education, health), and a
development grant through the Common Development Fund (CDF) to fund capital
projects. Inaddition to these flows, a significant level of international funding contributes
to local development priorities on either an on- or off-budget basis. Moreover, there i s
greater emphasis on monitoring performance of districts by devising output indicators
and also drawing on indicators included in the Imihigo contracts.

48.    When the MTEF was introduced in the central administration, the GoR's decided

                                           58

to simultaneously extend the approach to local governments. Officials from local
governments participated in the MTEF sensitization and training sessions with those from
the central governments. Progress has been in the development of local government
budgets and the incorporation into the MTEF process. To streamline and strengthen
district financial management, MINECOFIN is drafting new planning and budgeting
guidelines that will cover the various phases in the local planning and budgeting
process. The second phase of decentralization highlighted the need for reporting
mechanisms and framework for oversight. As intergovernmental transfers will remain
the main source of revenue for local governments for the foreseeable future, a regular
and detailed monitoring of these transfers and outcomes is critical for the success of the
decentralization process. The monitoring of local government spending i s being

MINECOFIN whose role, among others is to report on local government finances. Local
governments are required to make monthly reports of financial transactions and the

unused balances or have not been compliant with monthly requirements. A consolidated
report showing transfers and budget execution per major line items per district and per
province is to be regularly published on the website of MINECOFIN.

Civil service reform

50.     Rwanda has carried out significant civil service reforms, however, public sector

responsibilities; that the capacity in decentralized and delegated entities constrained their
ability to meet public service delivery objectives; current ministerial structures were not
appropriate for meeting their missions; fundamental systems and processes for managing
a decentralized public service were inadequate or even non-existent. The audits also
observed a lack of clarity of roles between agencies and their parent ministries which
hampers the latter's capacity to manage agencies and evaluate their performance.
