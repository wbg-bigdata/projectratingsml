      A. STRATEGICCONTEXTAND RATIONALE

1. Country and sector issues
1.           The cotton sector plays a critical role in the economies o f the eight West
African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) countries,' where 10 million
households rely on cotton for their livelihood. As such, it is an important element in
determining the rate of economic growth and the level o f poverty within the region. The
cotton producing countries have been able to gain market share in cotton over the past
three decades, but falling prices and increasing market pressures make it imperative that
they find ways to improve their competitiveness while seeking more favorable trade
conditions.
2.           For the past three years, driven primarily by insect resistance to chemical
pesticides, Burkina Faso has engaged in regulate$ field testing o f transgenic cotton, in
partnership with the science plant industry. Scientists and government officials in Mali
and Senegal, andto some extent inBeninand Togo, would also like to start field trials for
transgenic cotton, as well as for other transgenic food and cash crops. Inaddition to being
a potential tool in the control o f insect infestation, agricultural biotechnology in West
Africa also has the potential to reduce the use o f pesticides, increase agricultural
productivity, improve food security, and expand regional competitiveness ininternational
trade. However, the use o f transgenic organisms carries important risks, not just for the
countries who conduct field trials, but for the entire space due to WAEMU's common
market and custom o f seed sharing across national boundaries. Moreover, in the cotton
sector, technology fee negotiations will be key to determine the magnitude o f producer
surplus. Consequently, all parties have expressed a need for strong biosafety regulatory
and safeguard mechanisms in order to ensure that transgenic organisms, also known as
Living Modified Organisms (LMOs), are used safely, inlinewith the requirements o f the
Cartagena Protocol andother international standards, andthat they benefit farmers.

3.           The Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety (CPB)3 is a part o f the Convention on
Biological Diversity (CBD) andwas created to coordinate international efforts to provide
for the safe transfer, handling, and use o f LMOs, with a strong foundation in the
precautionary principle. In order to comply with the CPB, signatory countries must
establish or maintain means to regulate, manage or control the risks associated with the
use and release o f LMOs, which include risks to biodiversity and human health (Article
8).
4.          Six o f the WAEMU countries have ratified the CPB4, and all eight have
participated in programs implemented by the United Nations Environment Programme
(UNEP) to develop National Biosafety Frameworks (NBFs)~. o f the countries (Benin,

2. Rationale for GEF/Bank involvement
5.            Although seven o f the eight WAEMU countries have completed NBF
development programs, which were financed by GEF, only Burkina Faso has an
operational biosafety legal framework and regulatory system inplace, despite the risks to
the entire region. Technical and institutional capacity in biosafety remains weak in the
region, especially in the key areas o f risk assessment, monitoring and regulation o f
biotechnology research and LMO field trials, public awareness and participation, and
laboratory equipment (see Table 1, Annex 1for more details). The countries also lack the
capacity to negotiate technological fees related to intellectual property rights for
transgenic crops. Without further support from development partners, the countries are
not likely to become fully compliant with the CPB or to maximize their potential benefit
from the technology.
6.            GEF seeks diversification o f implementing agencies and has welcomed the
World Bank6to finance this regional Project. The Bank, acting as implementing agency
for the GEF has already financed two capacity-building projects related to NBF pilot
programs in Colombia and India. It i s also considering the provision o f a financing, as
implementing agency for the GEF, to the proposed Regional Biosafety Project under
preparation in the Latin America and Caribbean Region, with participation from Brazil,
Colombia, Costa Rica, and Peru.
7.            The World Bank, with its role as a facilitator and neutral party, and its broad
experience in bringing together the full range o f partners in providing expertise and
technical supportY7i s the appropriate financing institution for this Project. The World
Bank's approach i s to enable countries to take necessary measures to minimize
environmental and health risks and meet their treaty obligations; to promote capacity
buildingamong all stakeholders to allow informeddecision-making; andto tailor pro ects
to address country needs and priorities for long-lasting and sustainable results                       $`.Its
environmental and social safeguards and its fiduciary frameworks will enable it to
support the stakeholders, address the ongoing LMO development dynamic, and
mainstream sustainable arrangements for implementing risk assessment and management
processes inline with the Cartagena Protocol.

3. Higher level objectives to which the Project contributes
9.           Because o f its focus on CPB implementation, the Project will contribute
significantly to the goals and strategic priorities o f the GEF operational program by
improving the participating countries' capacities to handle issues concerning the safe and
sustainable import and use o f transgenic crops and products. Thus, the Project is aligned
with the GEF focal area on Biodiversity and the GEF Operational Program (OP) on
Conservation and Sustainable Use o f Biological Diversity Important to Agriculture (OP
13). The proposed regional approach inthis Project i s also consistent with the draft GEF
biosafety strategy'        and the GEF evaluation report,12 which promote sub-regional
cooperation as an effective means o f enabling information sharing and harmonization o f
legal frameworks to maximize the use o f institutional, financial, technical, and human
resources inthe region.

10.          Inaddition, the Project fits within GEF Biodiversity Strategic Priority No. 3
on capacity building for CPB implementation and within Strategic Priority No. 4 on the
generation and dissemination o f best practices for addressing current and emerging
biodiversity issues (see Component A). The Project will contribute to the latter by
identifying innovative approaches andtools inrisk assessment, developing database tools
for knowledge generation and sharing in biosafety, and creating models for capacity
buildingand institutional strengthening.

2. Program and Project development objectives and key indicator
14.          The Program development objective i s to establish and implement a Regional
Biosafety Regulatory Framework that will enable the WAEMU member countries to
meet their obligations under the CPB and put in place the necessary social and
environmental safeguards.

15.          The Project development objective is to establish an operational institutional,
legal and regulatory regional biosafety framework for the regulation o f living modified
organisms.

16.          The key performance indicator for the achievement o f the Project
Development Objective is the percentage o f applications received by the national
competent authorities for field trials and commercial releases that concern crops of high
priority for the WAEMU's territory. These are defined in accordance with the
WAEMU's agricultural policy, the applications are reviewedI6, using the science-based
risk assessment and management procedures set up by the national reference biosafety
laboratory with a regional dimension established as part o f the Project (see Annex 3 for
details).

3. Global environment objective and key indicators
17.          The global environment objective (GEO) to which the GEF financing would
contribute i s to protect regional biodiversity against the potential risks associated with the
introduction o f LMOs into the environment. This will be achieved through the
development o f common science-based risk assessment and management methods for the
approval or rejection o f LMOs, in compliance with the CPB and other international
standards. The Program will initiallybenefit the WAEMU region but offers the potential
to scale up to the larger Economic Community o f West African States (ECOWAS)
region. ECOWAS will be involved from the beginning o f the Program to ensure
synergies and consistency across the entire ECOWAS region.

18.          The performance indicator for the GEO is the percentage o f risk assessments
incorporating measurements o f (i)         presence or absence o f contamination through gene
transfer to wild species and the concentration level; and (ii)the appearance o f new
organisms or the disappearance o f non-target organisms (see Annex 3 for details). These
measurements will be performed by the national reference biosafety laboratory with a
regional dimension in Burkina Faso, in collaboration with the network o f national
biosafety laboratories.

19.          Other performance indicators are listed in Annex 3. Because o f the intricate
relationship between the GEF-IDA financed Project and the other part o fthe Program not
financed from World Bank funds, the Result Framework is based on the whole Program,

6. Alternatives consideredandreasons for rejection
37.          Inlinewiththe NBFdevelopment initiativesundertakenbyUNEP, the Project
could have proposed an exclusively national approach to implementing the CPB.
However, this was not considered cost-effective or efficient in the context o f the
WAEMU countries due to their size and capacity constraints and due to the WAEMU
common market. For these reasons, and to conform to the conclusions and
recommendations o f the GEF Secretariat and COP/MOP3, a regional approach was
chosen. Inaddition to being more cost-effective and efficient, this approach will provide
for a stable regulatory environment and will facilitate information-sharing among
partners and stakeholders throughout the sub-region.

1. Economic and financialanalysis
79.          Without the Program o f which the Project i s a part, it i s anticipated that the
WAEMU regionwill progressively adopt transgenic cotton andprobably other transgenic
crops as a result o f activities financing capacity building o f scientists and LMO
acceptance by end users. This approach will not guarantee transparent and objective
regulation at regional level, technology fees negotiated in the interests o f farmers and
adequate risk assessment and management safeguards use.

80.          With the Program, economic benefits to the WAEMU region will be o f three
kinds: (i) efficiency gains from regional regulation compared to national ones; (ii)    lower
level o f technology fees from enhanced negotiations capacity; (iii) lower risks o fregional
biodiversity contamination andlower impact o f pesticides on the environment andhealth.

81.         Inthe with-Program situation, countries willbenefit from the establishment of
a regional biosafety agency that will work with the NBAS (with now reduced
responsibilities) and from reduced costs o f adopting risk assessment and management
procedures that will be designed and implemented in the national reference biosafety
laboratory with a regional dimension and in the network o f national laboratories, instead
o fbeingdesigned ineach country.

                                          22

82.         Technology fee negotiations will be key to determine the magnitude o f
producer surplus. Under the Program, farmer organizations will be better informed. As a
result o f negotiations capacity buildingactivities, the technology fee will be lower than in
the without-Program scenario and, thus, the introduction o f LMOs in the cotton sector
will benefit farmers more.

83.         The economic analysis results (see Annex 9) shows that the expected
economic benefits, based on several assumptions about the counterfactual situation, the
efficiency gains inregulation, andthe lower technology fee, will be o f the same order o f
mamitude than the economic costs, even if environmental benefits (lower biodiversity
contamination and lower impact o f pesticides on environment and health) are not
captured in the economic benefits stream. Therefore the proposed investments by the
Program are likely to be economically justified. The two main beneficiaries o f the
Program would be the government that will save on their NBAs and research budget and
the farmers that will likely pay lower technology fee ifthey decide to adopt Bt cotton.

2. Technical
85.         If successful, this Program of which the Project is a part will allow the
beneficiary countries to implement the Cartagena Protocol and safely field test and
commercialize transgenic cotton and other crops in the future, should they choose to do
so. Risk assessment and management i s a fundamental tool in Program implementation.
Riskassessmentis the process o f identifying, evaluating, and choosing actions to prevent
or mitigate risks associated with the use o f LMOs. This leads to procedures that must be
followed in a scientific manner, based on recognized risk assessment techniques, taking
into account advice and guidelines developed by relevant international organizations
along with lessons learned through practical experience developed worldwide.

86.         The Program will produce operational, regionally harmonized methodologies,
consistent with international standards, such as guidelines, technical documents, forms,
and checklists for risk assessment and management o f LMOs and LMO products. The
existing tools in the countries and in the sub-region will be assessed and strengthened,
consistent with international standards on risk management developed by specialized
organizations like OECD and UNEP. These tools will be disseminated at the national
level through workshops and specific trainings for regional andnational stakeholders.

87.         Among many other activities to ensure its technical soundness, the Program
will finance and seek accreditation for a national reference biosafety laboratory with a
regional dimension and a network of national laboratories. The proposed reference
laboratory would act as the `executive arm' o f the regulatory framework to be developed
by WAEMU, providing objective and transparent biosafety risk assessment support for
its member countries.

88.         These environmental safety assessments must be done on a case-by-case basis
inthe environment where the LMO will be released. WAEMU countries need dedicated
                                          23

personnel and facilities for this purpose, but currently have only limited human and
laboratory capacity. In this case, pooling expertise in a national reference biosafety
laboratory with a regional dimension will be the most cost-effective way to provide for
adequate laboratory capacity and to reach sufficient critical mass for biosafety risk
assessment and capacity building. Furthermore, the GEF policy on biosafety emphasizes
the need to develop regional approaches to make the most o f limitednational resources.
Similarly, the regional IDA calls for the generation o f a regional public good through its
support.

89.         The reference laboratory must be linked with the national laboratories charged
with LMO detection, monitoring, and reporting, in order to prevent or mitigate the
uncontrolled movement o f L M O seeds from Burkina Faso (which i s likely to
commercialize Bt cotton within the next two years) to its neighboring countries.

90.         These laboratories will provide direct scientific inputs to a regional
observatory on modem agricultural biotechnology that will monitor the impact o f such
technology, should it be adopted by the countries, on the environment and on
socioeconomic issues, in accordance with a set o f key indicators developed by WAEMU
and all o f its stakeholders. The observatory will recommend appropriate actions for
decision makers at regional and national levels. Additionally, the observatory will be
responsible for monitoring food and feed safety, although the capacity for this will not be

                        Annex 1: Country and Sector or Program Background
                           AFRICA: West Africa RegionalBiosafetyProject
Agricultural productivity in West Africa generally remains very low, and growth has
been slow or stagnant inthe past four decades. Researchers and Ministries o f Agriculture
of Benin, Burkina Faso, Mali, Senegal and Togo (the five GEF grant beneficiary
countries) believe agricultural biotechnology provides a new opportunity to increase the
productivity and competitiveness in the agricultural sector, particularly in cotton
production (the region's major commodity).

Bt and Vip cotton, developed by Monsanto and Syngenta, respectively, are transgenic
varieties of cotton that reduce insecticidecosts because the plantsare resistant to the main
insect pest, the cotton bollworm. Both Bt and Vip cotton contain a gene from BaciZZus
thuringiensis (BO, which parasitizes the caterpillars o f harmful moths and butterflies. The
Vip gene produces a protein that kills the insect pest larvaewhen it tries to eat the cotton
plant. Inthe West Africa cotton belt, Burkina Faso began field trials o f transgenic cotton
in 2003 (see section on Burkina Faso, below). Mali may begin similar activitiedfield
trials. Senegal has a policy to increase its cotton production to 100,000 metric tons per
year and, at the research level, is the clear leader inbiotechnology. Fieldtrials will begin
as soon as the biosafety law i s in force. Benin and Togo are poised to follow this
economic trend, inan effort to prevent the loss of competitiveness that could arise iftheir
regional neighbors begin commercialization o ftransgenic cotton.

The regulatory framework developed inBurkina Faso is the most advanced inthe region
and was driven by the launching o f transgenic cotton field trials in 2003. In 2004,
intermediary legislative measures were adopted by decree37(i.e. without debate in the
National Parliament); with the support o f the UNEP/GEF financed project, however, the
government has prepared a law that has been adopted by the Parliament and has been in
force since April 2006. Burkina Faso established a National Biosafety Agency (NBA)
within its Ministry o f Environment in February 2005 with a mandate to coordinate
biosafety activities among government agencies and private organizations so as to ensure
safety in the use o f LMOs (production, imports and exports, and commercialization). In
order to accomplish this mission, the agency will rely on the support o f other agencies,
such as customs for import and export control and Ministries ofAgriculture andResearch
to regulate LMO field trials and post-commercialization monitoring. The NBA will also
collaborate with other committees established by law. The same kind o f regulatory and
institutional mechanisms are expected in the other countries, especially in Mali and
Senegal, where the process has already started.

While all GEF beneficiary countries have released their final National Biosafety
Frameworks, most o f them still have to be discussed and approved through national
stakeholder workshops before adoption by national parliaments. Within these
frameworks, a National Competent Authority (NCA) would be established in each
country and would be in charge o f biosafety coordination and final decision-making on
LMO applications. A National Biosafety Committee would provide scientific expertise to

Country and sector background
Agricultural production-particularly        sorghum, millet, maize, cotton seed, and lint-and
livestock remain the dominant sources of revenue for rural households in Burkina Faso.
However, this source of income is undermined by pests and diseases, periodic drought,
and unpredictable water supplies. To improve the agricultural economy, the government
has prioritized the application o f technological solutions to mitigate environmental risks,
increase production andpromote diversification.

The cash crop for BurkinaFaso i s cotton, which has increased inproduction bymore than
8 percent per annum inthe period 1998-2003, reaching 750,000 metric tons in2005. This
comprised two thirds o f the country's export earnings and prompted the government to
introduce genetically modified seeds, including better-yield Bt cotton. In 2003, an
agreement was signed between the government, Monsanto, and Syngenta for
experimentation with Bt cotton and Vip cotton. As a result, the National Research
Institute in Agriculture (INERA) conducted two field trials on these two genetically
modified cottons. Independently from this endeavor, the World Bank has confirmed its
intention to continue support cotton sector productivity and competitiveness (CAS,
2005).
SOFITEX, the former state cotton enterprise, is responsible for most o f the sector
activities (purchasing o f seed cotton, sale of inputs, processing, and marketing). In 1999,
the Union Nationale des Producteurs de Coton du Burkina Faso purchased a 30 percent
share in the company (Dagris, 34%; government, 35%). Two other cotton enterprises
exist inBurkina Faso: FASOCOTONandthe SOCOMA.
Confinedfield trials of transgenic cotton in Burkina Faso
Burkina Faso i s the first country in West Africa to engage in field testing o f transgenic
crops. In 2003, driven by insect resistance to chemical pesticides and keen interest from
the cotton-processing company SOFITEX to experiment with insect-resistant transgenic
cotton, INERA started field trials o f transgenic cotton in two research stations: one near
Bobo-Dioulasso and another near Fado N'Kourma. Two transgenic events, both
conveying resistance to insect damage, are being tested: Monsanto's Bollgard I1
                                               31

(expressing two Bacillus thuringiensis insecticidal proteins against bollworm and
nontarget insects) and Syngenta's Vip Cotton (expressing another gene from Bacillus
thuringiensis called vegetative insecticidal protein, Vip3, which has a similar mode of
action to cry genes and is also toxic to cotton bollworm). To achieve reproductive
isolation o f the Monsanto's Bollgard 11, it i s surrounded by a 15m guard row o f
conventional cottonplanted at the perimeter o f Cry1Ac and Cry2Ab).

The field area o f the transgenic trial at the INERA field station in Bobo-Dioulasso i s
about 0.6 ha, laid out in a randomized block design with three treatments: (1) transgenic
variety, unsprayed; (2) local variety, unsprayed; and (3) local variety treated with a 6-
spray regime o f organophosphate and synthetic pyrethroid-based broad spectrum
insecticides. Insect pressure and populations are monitored by frequent observations
using insect traps for both cottons on trial. After harvest, all plant material o f the
transgenic cotton and the guard row i s destroyed by burning, except for grain samples
taken for toxicological testing.

According to INERA,pesticide applications can be reduced by approximately 80 percent,
andthe yield o f the transgenic variety is between 20 and 30 percent better than the yield
o f a non-transgenic variety. The yield data have not yet beenpublished. These field trials
are used as venues for farmers and farmer organization representatives to observe the
performance o fthe transgenic cotton and for training farmers inthe technology.

The variety presently used in the trials i s an American variety, Coker. However,
integration o f the insect-resistance trait into a local variety i s being sought through two
methods: crossing and transformation. A local cotton variety has been transformed with
cry genes in Monsanto laboratories in St. Louis. A cotton breeder from INERA went to
Monsanto to select the best transformants, which are currently being field-tested in
Burkina Faso.

In2002, initial presidentialapproval was received for the 2003 field trials. InSeptember
2003,    an interministerial      committee     was    formed      to   develop   a   national
biotechnology/biosafety policy and regulatory framework. That committee oversaw the
conducting o f the trials until February 2005, when the Ministry o f Environment's
Biosafety Agency began operation. The Agency has already made one request to INERA
to test the impact o f the transgenic variety on bees. As a response, the INERA team
brought beehives to one o f the experimental fields in Bobo-Dioulasso and to one o f the
experimental fields inFadaN'Gourma.

Country and sector background
Senegal's agriculture has a weak productive base (CAS, 2003). This weakness limits
Senegal's agriculture potential; it is largely attributed to low soil fertility, insufficient
rainfall, low productivity, vulnerability to drought, price fluctuations for raw materials,
and inadequate marketstructure to stimulate competition inthe supply sectors.

The PRSP and CAS consider agricultural growth a key feature o f the poverty reduction
strategy and therefore promote the cultivation o f highvalue-added crops, the application
o f irrigation, and agricultural diversification into horticultural products, fresh fruits, and
cotton. In addition, the strategy emphasizes the creation and strengthening o f producer
organizations as full partners in rural development policy, the decentralization o f the

marketing informationnetworks.

Cotton i s grown on about 70,000 farms innearly every region, and is mostly concentrated

mechanization.

                                Annex 4: DetailedProgramDescription
                            AFRICA: West Africa RegionalBiosafetyProject

ProgramObjective,Outcomes andComponents
The World Bank financed Project i s an important part o f the larger WAEMU Program adopted
byRgglement inApril 200745anddescribed inthis Annex.
The Program development objective is to establish and implement a Regional Biosafety
Regulatory Framework that will enable the WAEMU member countries to meet their obligations
under the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety (CPB) and put in place the necessary social and
environmental safeguards. The Project development objective is to establish an operational
institutional, legal and regulatory regional biosafety framework for the regulation o f Living
Modified Organisms (LMOs).

The expected Program results include: (i) set o f operational and regionally-harmonized tools
                                                   a
and methodologies for the WAEMU countries, consistent with international standards; (ii)                   an
operational Regional Biosafety Framework (RBF) for the WAEMU space, including a regional
biosafety observatory; (iii)     a strengthened institutional capacity to implement the RBF at the
national level; (iv) an increased public awareness o f LMOs/transgenic crops, and a strengthened
country capacity inIntellectualPropertyRights (IPRs) negotiations on transgenic crops.

The Global Environment Objective (GEO) is to protect regionalbiodiversity against the potential
risks associated with the introduction o f LMOs into the environment. The expected results are:
(i) risksofcontaminationthroughgenetransfertowildspeciesandtheconcentration
   limited
level; and (ii)  control o f the appearance o f new organisms or the disappearance o f non-target
organisms.

The Program beneficiary countries are the eight WAEMU member states (Benin, Burkina Faso,
Mali, Senegal, Togo, Cote d'Ivoire, Niger and Guinea Bissau). The countries eligible for the
GEF grant arethe countries havingratified the CPB, i.e. Benin, BurkinaFaso, Mali, Senegal, and
Togo. Guinea Bissau, CGte d'Ivoire and Niger will be financed by other donors and/or WAEMU
resources as they are not eligible for GEF fimding, having not ratified the CPB.

The Program concept was promptedupon demand o fthe beneficiary countries to assess the costs
and benefits o fthe adoption o fLMOs, field trials being underway inBurkinaFaso. The Program
will support them by providing scientific and institutional capacity building at the national and
regional levels, accompanied by broad-based stakeholder participation. The Program will also
build a regional observatory that will host a regional Biosafety Clearing House (BCH)
mechanism on modem biotechnologies and monitor country compliance with the CPB and the
impact o f any L M O introduction. Followingthe adoption o f the RegionalBiosafety Framework,
the regional observatory has the potential to play a decision-making role for LMO approvals in
the WAEMU space.

The Program components and activities were defined through a set of preparatory studies
undertaken between pre-appraisal and appraisal and financed by the GEF/PDF Block B Grant.

Risk assessment is the process o f identifying, evaluating, and selecting actions to prevent or
mitigate risks associated with the use o f LMOs. This leads to procedures that must be followed
in a scientific manner, based on recognized risk assessment techniques, and taking into account
advice and guidelines developed by relevant international organizations along with lessons
learned through practical experiences developed worldwide, including the developing world,
such as South Asia and LatinAmerica.

Component A will produce regionally harmonized methodologies, consistent with international
standards, such as guidelines, technical documents, forms, and checklists to be used to conduct
risk assessment and management o f LMOs and LMOs products.            At this initial stage, the
Program will focus on transgenic cotton, extending to other crops in the future according to the
priorities and outputs o f the biotechnology research institutions. The existing tools in the
countries and in the sub-region will be assessed and strengthened, consistent with international
standards on risk management developed by specialized organizations, such as the Organization
for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) and UNEP.                The products of this
component will be disseminated via workshops and specific trainings to the regional and national
stakeholders. The four subcomponents relatedto risk assessment are:

A. General
Background-ProcurementReform
The WAEMU Commission, based in Burkina Faso, will be in charge of procurement for the
main part o f this Project (financed by GEF and IDA). Activities financed by IDA'Sfunds
allowed for Burkina Faso will be carried out by the National Biosafety Agency (in collaboration
with PAFASP for fiduciary aspects).

All WAEMU countries are currently undergoing the procurement reform and harmonization
process supported by IDA and the African Development Bank (AfDB). The reform is led by the
Economic Policy Department o f WAEMU and will influence the procurement procedure of the
WAEMU Commission inthe coming years.

The regional procurement reform has been divided into two phases:

Phase 1was financed jointly by the Bank through an IDA Grant (closed on December 3, 2005),
inthe amount of US $497,000 equivalent; by AfDB, in the amount of US$97,000; and by the
Commission (and indirectly the EU), in the amount o f US$ 150,000. Phase 1was successfully
completed with the following main outputs: (a) regional harmonized directives were adopted by
the Council o f Ministers in December 2005; (b) the instruments and functions that need to be
located at the Commission in order to fulfill its mandate (promotion, regulation and Monitoring
andEvaluation) were identified; and (c) an updated assessmento f procurement reform needs in
eachmember state was made.

Phase 2 i s currently under preparation. Its objective i s to implement the Reform Program created
inPhase 1.More specifically, itwillhelp set up a Procurement Unit(for reform follow up) at the
Commission to promote and monitor the application o fthe Regional Directives, the development
o f procurement capacity in the member states, and support the member states in the integration
o f the Directives into their nationalprocurement codes.

DuringPhase 2, a regional procurement standard document will be prepared for member states,
as the WAEMU Commission has no official procurement standard document. Until this i s
completed, IDA'Sprocurement standard document will be used during implementation o f the
proposed Project.

Conclusion of economic analysis
The first rough calculations show that the expected economic benefits (of approximately
US$20.4million - see Table D), based on several assumptions about the counterfactual situation
and the efficiency gains o f regulation and lower technology fees, are o f the same order of
magnitude than the economic costs (of approximately US$22.2 million - see Table C or D).

Financial analysis
The two main beneficiaries o f the Program in terms o f the financial analysis will be (1) the
governments that will save on their NBA research budgets and any long-term risk management
and mitigation costs; and (2) the farmers that will likelypay lower technology fees ifthey decide
to adopt Bt cotton. The main loser from this Program i s WAEMU - and consequently the
governments o f each member country - as they are responsible for implementing the various
components o fthe Program.

The Program has a recurrent cost o fUS$ 500,000 per year: US$ 100,000 for the maintenance o f
the laboratories (Component A), US$ 300,000 for operation costs o f the regional biosafety
agencies (Component B) andUS$ 100,000 for the nationalbiosafety agencies (Component C).

The stocktaking assessment suggests three possible solutions (or a combination thereof) to
finance the recurrent costs o f the biosafety agencies, notably: (i)  imposing a market levy, (ii)
charging license and application fees, and (iii)engaging inpublic-private partnerships.
A market levy would place a fee on the production, sale, or export o f Bt cotton, generating
revenue that would be used to finance the recurrent Program costs. During the consultations,
however, farmer groups expressed their concern that such a market mechanism would place an
undue burden on farmers. Selling licenses to biotechnology research institutions and charging
fees for field trials could provide additional resources, but this may create disincentives for
innovation and would be unpopular with scientists. The stocktaking assessment fbrther notes that
while public-private partnerships could provide funding, preliminary discussions with potential
participants did not reveal a strong interest from the private sector. Furthermore, NGOs have
voiced their concern that such partnership could skew the regional biosafety agency towards
commercial interests.

The stocktaking assessment identifies a market levy as imposing the least direct costs to donors
and countries, though considerable uncertainty exists concerning Bt cotton adoption rates,
exchange rate volatility, and price volatility and trends. Higher market levies and Bt cotton
adoption rates inparticular will reduce the number o f years necessary for Program self-financing.
The model predicts that a low market levy o f 0.25% o f the Bt cotton market value will lead to
sustainable financing within five years o f the levy's introduction, a moderate value o f 0.5% will
lead to sustainability in the third year, and a high value o f 0.75% will lead to sustainability in
only the second year.

                                 Annex 10: SafeguardPolicyIssues

                         AFRICA: West Africa RegionalBiosafetyProject
The Project being an important part o f the larger Program has been included in the entire
assessmento fthe Program, for purposes o f environment and social safeguard policy issues.

The main objective o f the Program is to establish and implement a regulatory fi-amework on
biosafety that will ensure prevention and management o f environmental risks related to the use
and handling o f LMOs on the national and regional levels. All but two WAEMU countries are
Parties to the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety, and therefore have an international legal
obligation to comply with the protocol requirements in areas such as risk assessment, public
participation, socioeconomic considerations, and monitoring and evaluation. The forthcoming
regional framework will meet the requirements o f the Cartagena Protocol, and resources will be
provided to help the countries comply with these requirements.

The Program has been screened and classified as a category Bytaking into account type, location,
sensitivity, and scale, as well as the nature and magnitude o f its potential environmental impacts.
The Program will not promote or finance the introduction o f LMOs. However, the potential
introduction o f LMOs by the beneficiary countries may pose an environmental threat or
engender adverse socioeconomic impacts, and the Program takes this into account in the
biosafety regulatory framework.

As a result o f the potential risks, the Program triggers the policies on environmental assessment
(OP 4.01) and on pest management (OP 4.09). The main instrument used to address these risks is
an Environmental and Social Framework (ESMF, described inthe following section). This annex
summarizes the key issues related to the two safeguard policies trigged by the Program and the
actions taken to address these issues. During the consultation process, it was determined that
there existed a risk o f public misperception o f the Program. To address this risk, a series o f
consultations and disclosures were held; the results o f these processes andtheir changes upon the
Program are described in this annex. The comments o f GEF council members and six
international NGOs (Sierra Club, GRAIN, ETC Group, the African Centre for Biosafety, and
Red por una Amkrica Latina Libre de Transgknicos, and the Coalition pour la Protection du
Patrimoine Gknktique Afiicain -COPAGEN) have also been addressed. Finally, the annex
examines the processes for monitoring and evaluation o fthe Program.
Choice of the EA instrument,content and disclosure
Duringthe screening process, it was determined that an ESMF would be the most suitable EA
instrument for the proposed Program. The ESMF for the Program, first preparedby WAEMU in
July 2006, spells out the environmental and social safeguard actions, institutional arrangements
and capacity required to identify and mitigate potential environmental and social safeguards
issues o f each Program activity.

The first o f the ESMF's three central elements is its risk assessment framework, which
establishes analysis methods and riskmanagement guidelines inline with international standards.
The ESMF also defines a participatory framework, describing various requirements for
information disclosure and public participation indecisions relating to LMOs. The ESMF's third
element i s a framework for monitoring and evaluating the Program outcomes and impacts,
including guidelines for assessing the possible impacts o f LMO introduction on consumer and



                                               99

producer welfare. Finally, the ESMF outlines a series o f principles to follow as the regional
regulationis put into place. The framework allows the Program to adapt to any possible risks and
contingencies.

The proposed ESMF followed a broad-based consultation process involving relevant stakeholder
groups inthe client counties. These included the various government agencies o f the Ministries
o f Agriculture and Environment, National Agricultural Research Institutes (NARIS),producer
organizations (POs) and civil society o f the eight countries. The ESMF has been discussed
during Program preparation with the aforementioned stakeholder groups, including NGOs - to
encompass critics and other interested parties - with the aim o f seeking their input, hearing and
publicizing their concerns, and fostering ownership on their part.

InAugust 2006, the ESMFwas disclosed by WAEMU, with the request that it be submitted to
relevant stakeholder groups in all eight countries. The ESMF was also released to the World
Bank Infoshop on August 24, 2006 for international public disclosure; it is available both online
and in hard copy. In order to guarantee access to information about the Program, national
consultations were organized inNovember-December 2006. The July 2006 version o f the ESMF
was modified in February 2007 to reflect the stakeholder feedback from the November-
December 2006 national consultations. It was then released to the West African public through
WAEMU, and to international civil society via the World Bank's Infoshop. All fbture revisions
to the ESMF will be made public in the same manner and will likely lead to continued
improvements o fthe ESMF, just as past consultations have.
Actual risk
Considering the pace o f development o fnew biotechnologyproducts and the pressure to test and
use them inthese countries, there i s a risk o f local regulations lagging behindthe realities on the
ground. The regulatory framework established by the Program must contain the necessary
mechanisms to ensure WAEMU can satisfactorily control what i s happening on the ground and
speedily react to the newest biotechnology issues. Otherwise, there is a real risk o f introducing
unsoundor inadequately tested LMOs into West Africa.

The Program has a number o f elements to mitigate this risk. The establishment o f an effective
Coordination Unit within WAEMU to oversee Program functions during preparation and
implementation will enhance the speed and ease o f the recipient countries to manage andcontrol
Program development and deal with technical issues (such as risk assessment). Furthermore, the
Programwill builda regional observatory to monitor the member countries' compliance with the
CPB and the possible environmental and socioeconomic impact o f agricultural biotechnology (in
accordance with a set o f key indicators developed by WAEMU and its stakeholders). The
observatory will host a Biosafety Clearing House, which will disseminate information through
trainings, workshops, and outreach Programs: the feedback based on this information
dissemination and the Regional Coordinator's public quarterly, semiannual and annual reports
will be processed by the Steering Committee (see below), which will allow any necessary
improvementsto berapidly made.

The environmental risks identified inthe ESMF are addressedby Component A, which includes
the consolidation and further development o f a common regional approach for risk assessment,
management, and inspection, as well as monitoring and evaluation o f LMOs (in line with
internationally recognized standards). The proposed laboratory would be the `executive arm' o f
the regulatory framework to be developed by WAEMU, providing objective and transparent


                                               100

biosafety risk assessment support for its member countries. Because Burkina Faso i s likely to
commercialize Bt cotton (or import LMOs into the region) within the next two years, the
reference laboratory will be linked with competent national laboratories charged with LMO
detection, monitoring, and reporting, in order to prevent or mitigate the uncontrolled movement
o f LMO seeds from Burkina Faso to neighboring countries. Uncontrolled movement and
production o f LMOs can endanger the quality o f the seed and thus the technology itself, which
would be counterproductive for the entire region's agricultural sector.

The social risks identified in the ESMF are that gains from the cultivation o f transgenic plants
could be offset by the countries' inability to negotiate on intellectualproperty rights (IPR) issues.
Iffarmers are overly dependent on seed suppliers with a monopolistic holdonprices, and ifthe
gains from increased yields and reduced pesticide costs are not sufficient to overcome these
higher seed prices, farmers' incomes and livelihoods will suffer. Two studies were
commissioned to evaluate these risks: an economic impact study and a needs assessment for IPR.
The recommendations from these studies have been taken into account by the Program, as
described below.

The International Food Policy Research Institute (IFPRI) researched the potential impacts o f
introducing Bt cotton in the West Africa cotton belt. The most recent version o f the study was
released on December 20th, 2006, and analyzed the current evidence from other countries
regarding the range o f potential economic benefits o f Bt cotton for farmers and the likely
consequences on seed supply and product channels inWest Africa. These analyses were used to
identify factors that could influence the success or failure o f Bt cotton introduction and to serve
as background for future regional policymaking. A summary o f this study i s annexed to the
ESMF.

The needs assessment for IPR was carried out in January 2006 and identified the most urgent
areas o f intervention. These included: (1) assistanceto National Agricultural Research Institution
(NARIS)to register extant varieties before the end o f the grace period under the African
Intellectual Property Organization Plant Variety Protection (AIPO PVP), (2) multistakeholder
dialogues on IPRs in agriculture, (3) training activities for AIPO concentrating on technical
issues in DUS (distinctness, uniformity and stability) testing, application and renewal fees, and
enforcement, (4) consultative workshops between AIPO, international patent offices, and WIPO,
(5) training o f policy makers, (6) training workshops for NARIs, and (7) external legal expertise
to NARIs. Points (1) and (2) were identified as priority activities.

On the basis o f these studies, IPR issues are addressed in subcomponents B 2 and C3: the
Program will (1) put in place a regional IPR legal framework, (2) build the capacity o f
governments and farmers' organizations for negotiations with seed companies, (3) organize
yearly series o f training workshops for producers, managers, and researchers, (4) fund
scholarships to regional universities for specialization inIPR and negotiations, (5) hire experts to
assist inthe negotiationprocess, and (6) bringjudicial expertise to improve AIPO's capacity.

The consultations with the representatives o f civil society groups chiefly revolved around the

specifically to the Program's regulatory framework. The representatives voiced their
apprehensions about LMOs, citing fears they would damage their local heritage and way o f life,
induce farmer dependency on multinational firms, and bring significant health risks. They also
expressed their perception that the Program, and therefore the World Bank, was trying to
promote and "push" LMOs into WAEMU.

The consultations with researchers and institutes, on the other hand, revealed a favorable
reception to and better understanding of the Program. The representatives recognized the need
for a strong regulatory framework and concentrated on their current lack o f equipment and
training. Other significant issues that arose were the Program's over-emphasis on cotton - at
least as perceived by the participants - and the difficulty o f organizing public educational events
about LMOs. The participants recommended expanded informational campaigns about
biotechnology and the Program.

The discussions with government agencies also brought up the need for improved
communication with civil society so as to clearly separate the Program from the promotion o f


                                                  102

LMOs in the public eye. While the representatives were clearly able to make this distinction,
they were concerned it hadbeen less apparent to the general public.

The different groups made varying claims about farmer attitudes towards the Program and
LMOs: some o f the participating farmer organizations claimed farmers would reject LMOs and
oppose the Program, contrasting with researchers' belief that adoption would occur if LMOs
were shown to be superior to conventional crops. Because the consultations elicited these
differing claims on possible farmer reception o f the Program, the communications team
recommended an additional consultation with private farmers, independent o f farmer
organizations, to more accurately assess their needs and concerns. These were carried out on
November 17,2006, and are discussed below.

The only environmental and social concerns raised by participants at the consultations were the
potential economic risks to farmers and the risk o f the Program being misperceived as actively
supporting and financing LMO introduction into West Africa.
National consultations on the updated ESMF (November-December 2006)

The aims o f these consultations were to raise awareness about the revised Program and ESMF
and to further gather stakeholder perceptions, opinions, and suggestions for improvement. The
Program had beenmodified in response to the May-June consultations: its focus on biosafety -
not biotechnology - was emphasized, as the previous consultations had revealed
misunderstandings as to the Program objectives. Also, the focus o f the Program shifted away
fromcotton, inaccordance with stakeholder requests.

These consultations occurred in November and December 2006, with day-long conferences
organized in each WAEMU country and moderated by the communications specialist. Each o f
these eight conferences consisted o f fifty people on average and mixed all backgrounds together.
The communications specialist was present at each consultation: he moderated the discussions
and ensured that all stakeholder groups were fully cognizant o f the scope and objectives o f the
ESMF, and o f their roles inits implementation.

Breakdown of national consultationsby country and participant category




This round o f consultations was characterized by candid and constructive debate and a marked
increase in popularity o f the Program. Government representatives remained supportive o f the


                                              103

Program, while civil society groups, previously opposed to it as identified inthe May-June 2006
consultations, now declared themselves in its favor. Representatives from research institutes,
however, regretted what they saw as a shift in the Program's focus from biotechnology to
biosafety, as they had initially hoped the Program would fund their research. After the
presentation o f the updated ESMF, all participants agreed it was an adequate instrument with
which to manage the potential risks surrounding LMO introduction into WAEMU.

Certain concerns remained about the Program, and participants developed a set o f
recommendations for improvement. Interms o f risk management, the participants recommended
(1) a systematic approach to risk assessment and management, (2) havingclear guidelines for the
measures to be taken after a risk assessment, (3) re-evaluating LMOs two years after any
introduction, and (4) a clause to urge emergency food aid donors to respect regional biosafety
regulations. Component A o f the Program incorporates these first two recommendations by
providing for the development o f a common regional approach for risk assessment, management,
andinspection, as well monitoring and evaluation o fLMOs.

Other concerns were more results-based: participants believed the Program needed to construct
objectively verifiable indicators to judge the results o f the ESMF and to verify that all WAEMU
states obeyed the CPB and implementedtheir National Biosecurity Frameworks. The participants
also proposed the formation o f defensive measures inthe event o f an illicit LMO introduction or
o f a violation o f the CPB by its West African signatories.

As relates to socioeconomic matters, recommendations were made to account for community
rights and the preservation of the local genetic heritage (including the "Farmer's Right" to save
seed), and to consider the impacts o f IPR on farmer communities.

Itwas concluded that (1) the risk o fmisperceptiono fthe Program hasbeenattenuated as aresult
o f the consultations and the various disclosure processes, and (2) the various representatives to
the meetings support the Program.
Farmers consultation (November 17, 2006)
Although the May-June consultations included a number o f representatives from producer
organizations, the communications study recommended an additional consultation with private
farmers, separately from producer organizations. This took place on November 17, 2006, during
a regional meeting o f West African agricultural producers in Bobo-Dioulasso, Burkina Faso. A
group of sixty people attended, including twenty farmers and forty representatives o f government
agencies, civil society, and seed firms.

The farmers, whose attitudes towards the Program had remained ambiguous in the May-June
2006 consultations, were in favor o f the revised Program at this later date. They indicated their
desire for continued future involvement and consultation throughout the Program's duration,
and, when asked what role they could play in implementation, volunteered to help educate their
peers about biosafety and the Program and integrate them into the consultation process - if they
were properly trained to do so. The farmers' other expectations included information sessions
about the contents o f the CPB, regular radio updates about the evolution o f biosafety activities,
institutional capacity building, and educational exchange programs.

Also o f note, civil society groups such as COPAGEN-BF - previously hostile to the Program -
were this time noticeably more supportive o f its aims, reflecting a shift o f opinions similar to that

o f the above-mentioned ESMF national consultations o f November-December. This observed
change inattitude further supports the conclusionthat public misperceptions have diminished.
GEFCouncilandNGOQuestions
Several questions regarding the Program have been addressed to the World Bank by GEF
Council Members and six NGOs (Sierra Club, GRAIN, ETC Group, the African Centre for
Biosafety, and Red por una AmCrica Latina Libre de TransgCnicos, COPAGEN). The main
issues raisedbythe Council andNGOs are summarized below.
Program Objectives
A strong concern is that the Program seeks to rapidly push LMOs into West Africa by
implementing regulation at the WAEMJJ level, thereby overriding national democratic
processes. However, the Program does not finance field trials or any introduction o f LMOs,
commercial or otherwise, into West Africa, and the Bank itself does not make any value
judgment regarding the ultimate desirability o f agricultural biotechnology in the region. Rather,
the Programwill enable the beneficiary countries to make these valuejudgments themselves.
Stakeholder Participation

The aforementioned NGOs have alleged the Program does not provide for adequate stakeholder
participation. This annex shows the extent to which stakeholder participation and local
ownership are, in fact, central to the Program. To recapitulate, the Program has at this date
engaged in 22 consultations since the Program's inception, hearing from over 500
representatives o f all possible stakeholder groups (with a particular emphasis on fanners) in all
eight WAEMU countries. Additionally, Program preparation workshops included representatives
o f these stakeholder groups, includingNGOs.

The Program has also established a Steering Committee to oversee its implementation; its
members consist o f the eight Ministers o f the Environment from each WAEMU country, as well
one representative from NGOs, three producer organizations, one from ECOWAS, one from
WAEMU, and the president o f the regional scientific committee respectively. The Committee's
presidency will be occupied at all times by the Minister o f the Environment o f the country
presiding over the Council o f Ministers o f WAEMU. The Committee will meet a minimum o f
twice a year, but may meet additional times if judged necessary by at least one third o f its
members. These meetingsshall yield publicly available minutes.

The Steering Committee will also cooperate with the Regional Coordination to develop
intermediate results indicators in order to track progress toward the desired outcomes. All data
will be shared with stakeholders through the Committee. The Committee and the Regional
Coordination will produce quarterly reports charting the stages o f Program implementation,
which shall be made public through the regional BCH.

Moreover, a permanent Secretariat has been established in the Coordination Unit to handle all
complaints and suggestions, serving as a link between the Program and civil society. Its input
will be reviewed by the Steering Committee during their biyearly meeting. However, if the
Secretariat finds a matter deserving immediate attention, an extraordinary Committee meeting
will beheldto address it. Quarterly reports will be producedbythe Regional Coordinator and the
Steering Committee, outlining the progress inimplementing the Program and the input received
through the Secretariat. To continue stakeholder engagement and ensure Program transparency,
there will also be continuous information dissemination through trainings, workshops, and


                                              105

outreach programs.
Implementing Agency
Although there appears to be concern regarding the choice o f WAEMU as implementing agency
for the Program, the World Bank and all participating countries believe WAEMU i s the agency
best able to implement the Program. The beneficiary countries decided from the outset that going
through an international body was preferable to carrying out the Program at a national level, due
to the countries' common market, the efficiency o f international collaboration, and the cross-
country externalities that could arise from LMO testing.

Furthermore, having WAEMU in charge o f the Program's execution will not erode the
sovereignty o f each country. The decision to adopt regional regulation concerning LMOs lies
entirely with representatives o f the member states. Each state holds veto power, such that none
will be forced to adhere to regulations it deems unnecessary or unwise, or to adopt transgenic
crops ifit has found this to be undesirable. Inthis sense, the adoption o f LMOs i s not a foregone
conclusion, but rather a decision that rests with the WAEMU member states.

Operating through a regional body, as opposed to national governments, has not led to the
"sidestepping" o f public debate. WAEMU allows for local ownership o f the Program in its
frequent public consultations and the creation o f the Steering Committee. Already, suggestions
and concerns from stakeholders have shaped the structure o f the Program, and this involvement
will continue throughout its term.

Another concern raised about WAEMU is its alleged lack o f qualifications to implement such an
environmentally sensitive program as this; there have been claims this role should be assigned to
a body with greater expertise inbiosafety. However, WAEMU is the region's strongest and most
effective actor, and has the legal mandate to promote the coordination o f agricultural and
environmental policy among the states. Furthermore, WAEMU will benefit from the
environmental expertise o f the Steering Committee, where eight o f the twelve seats belong to the
Ministers o f the Environment and the leadership role belongs to the Minister o f whichever
country heads the Council o f Ministers o f WAEMU.
Participation of theMinistries of the Environment

The limitedrole o fthe Ministries o fthe Environment inProgramplanninghas beenbrought into
question. However, the Ministers have all been consulted duringthe various preparationmissions
and have expressed support for WAEMU as the main implementingagency, which they view as
effective and well respected. The involvement and key role o f the Ministers o f Environment in
the Program was clearly demonstrated in the regional Ministers' signing o f the Bamako
Declarationon November 15th, 2006, which reaffirmed their commitment to the Program (see the
end of Annex 1). These Ministries will also be kept active in the Program by their prominent
involvement inthe Steering Committee.
Safeguards
The objectives o f the Program have been questioned on the grounds that it is not possible to
introduce transgenic crops inan entirely safe manner. Such a concern reveals a misunderstanding
as to the aims of the Program. LMO testing in West Africa has been taking place since 2003,
which may have undesirable environmental and social consequences if the region remains
without the strong regulatory framework and monitoring institutions the Program seeks to
provide. Regardless o f whether WAEMU countries choose to conduct field trials or


                                              106

commercially release LMOs, the Program provides them the regulatory framework to make
informed decisions about each proposed introduction.
Economic andfinancial analyses
There has been concern regardingthe economic and financial analysis o f the program in that it
was not considering opportunity costs and because the appraisal was deemed favorable to GMOs
bypresenting recovery mechanisms.

The program cost o f approximately $24 million represents the costs o f defining and applying
biosafety regulations for five years ineight WAEMU countries. This sum includes a network o f
laboratories to conduct the requisite tests and provide benchmark information that can serve as a
basis for sound decision-making by sovereign governments whether or not to use GMOs. As an
initiative first proposed by West African countries, the WARBP was created to assist the region
manage possible environmental risks inlight o fthe testing o f genetically modified cotton already
undertaken by one country in the region. Inthe absence o f GMO testing, the region would not
have had this urgent need to develop biosafety regulations as requiredby the Cartagena Protocol
on Biosafety (CPB). Furthermore, WARBP has distinct goals and does not duplicate existing
efforts or overlap with completed GEF-financed projects for developing national biosafety
frameworks in individual West African countries. On the contrary, it will build upon the GEF
projects to assist countries to hrther implementthe CPB, inaccordance with guidance under the
Conventionon Biological Diversity.

Implementing an environmental regulatory framework does not preclude the application o f the
"polluter pays" principle which has been incorporated in the regional environment policy
recently approved by the WAEMU Council o f Ministers. The situation i s similar to imposing an
emissions tax to curb carbon emissions which raises revenue even though this was not the
primary goal o f the policy. In the case o f biosafety, it i s normal to tax firms that intendto test
GMOs; this does not mean the policy encourages these tests or the commercialization o f GMOs
in any way whatsoever. In this context, the economic and financial analysis is an exercise
seeking to understand whether the costs associated with the implementation o f a prudent
regulatory framework (that i s to say, including the network o f laboratories so that data provided
by the private sector can be independently tested) could be offset by the hnds raised by a
taxation o f firms undertaking GMO testing. The analysis conducted here simply concludes that
underreasonable assumptions, it shouldbepossible to offset the costs.
Relationship with USAID, USDA
The NGOs and GEF Council Members have expressed uncertainty about the relationship
betweenthe Program andongoing USAID-sponsoredbiotechnology support inthe region. These
other programs, stemming from USDA- and USAID-sponsored biotechnology conferences, are
perceived as actively promoting the introduction o f genetically engineered crops inWest Africa.
At this time, the task team has made efforts to be aware o fongoing regionalprograms related to
biosafety and to keep these donors informed o f Program developments, and has met with a
number o f their representatives (most recently with UNEP, CILSS-INSAH, ECOWAS, FA0 and
CORAF on September 20 - 22, 2006 and with USAID on November Sth,                          2006). All
collaboration efforts with other donor agencies reflect only a desire to avoid inefficiency and
duplication o fpast efforts, and do not indicate agreement with these agencies' views on LMOs or
any other issue.

Role of theprivate sector
The role o f the private sector in the Program has also come under scrutiny. NGOs and GEF
Council Members have articulated their concern that (1) the Program will primarily benefit
biotechnology companies to the detriment o f farmers and (2) the private sector has become a
main Program partner - an approach not supported by the CPB. Ambiguities within the text
relating to the private sector's hnction have been revised to clarify that the private sector i s only
one among many stakeholder groups. To prevent any conflict o f interests, the private sector will
not be a part o fthe Steering Committee.

Monitoring,Evaluation,and SupervisionFramework
Component B o f the Program provides for the establishment o f a regional observatory that will
monitor adherence to the CPB and evaluate environmental and socioeconomic impacts in the
eventthat LMOs are commercially introducedinthe region.

The Regional Coordinator and the Steering Committee will develop intermediate regional and
country indicators (in accordance with the results framework in annex 3) and monitor progress
towards achieving the desired outcomes. This will result in public quarterly, semiannual, and
annual monitoring reports, to be shared with all stakeholders through the regional B C H and
discussed duringthe meetingso fthe Steering Committee.

Every year, the World Bank will organize two supervision missions to evaluate the Program's
progress and to identify and assist with any problems. One o f these missions will include major
Programpartners.

Furthermore, a mid-term review will      OCCUT  before December 30th, 2009, during which all

                              Annex 15: IncrementalCost Analysis
                        AFRICA: West Africa RegionalBiosafetyProject

The Project being an important component o fthe Program, the Incremental Cost Analysis o fthe
GEF financing covers the overallProgram.
1.       BroadDevelopmentGoals andthe Baseline
The development goal o f the Program is to establish and implement a regional biosafety
regulatory framework that will enable the eight West African Economic and Monetary Union
(WAEMU) countries to meet their obligations under the CPB for the regulation of LMOs. The
countries have chosen to reach this development goal through technical and institutional capacity
building at the national and regional levels, accompanied by broad-based stakeholder
participation. The Program will also build a regional observatory that will host a regional
Biosafety Clearing House (BCH) mechanism on modem biotechnologies and monitor country
compliance with the CPB and the impact o f any LMO introductions.

Under the baseline scenario, it i s anticipated that the WAEMU region will progressively adopt
transgenic cotton and probably other transgenic crops as a result o f activities financing capacity
building o f scientists and LMO acceptance by end users. However, this approach will not
guarantee adequate risk assessmentand management or adherence to international standards.

Thus, the baseline scenario would result in a potentially high risk o f contamination o f local
biodiversity by genes originating from LMOs, due to the absence o f adequate safeguard
guidelines, regional coordination, and monitoring and evaluation tools at the different stages o f
LMO development. In the baseline scenario, the socioeconomic impacts on farmers o f LMO
introductions are also not likely to be monitored.

Total expenditures under the baseline scenario are estimated at approximately US$1.4 million,
dominated by already-existing and forthcoming investments from the private sector in field
trials, LMO acceptance campaigns, and from bilateral donors incapacity buildinginthe general
field o fbiotechnology and biosafety.
2.      Globalenvironmentobjective
The global environment objective o f the Program is to protect regional biodiversity against the
potential risks associated with the introduction o f LMOs into the environment. This will be
achieved through the development o f common science-based risk assessment and management
methods for the approval or rejection o f LMOs, incompliance with CPB and other international
standards.
3.      Alternative
Under the Program, the WAEMU member countries will be able to (i) common risk define
assessment and management procedures in line with international standards in order to mitigate
environmental and foodfeed safety risks associated with transgenic cotton, and eventually other
crops such as maize, tomatoes, cassava and cow pea; (ii)    undertake application reviews using
these procedures under a harmonized legal biosafety framework; and (iii) the impact o f
                                                                             monitor
the introduction o fLMOs on biodiversity, and socio-economic issues.

Under this alternative, the overall Program beneficiaries will be able to (i)reach their
development objectives (that is, to safely test modem biotechnologies), at a lower economic cost



                                              119

compared to the baseline scenario, because they will build and use a regional legal framework;
and also (ii) the global environmental objective because they will have developed and
              reach
adapted regional science-based risk assessment and management methods, in compliance with
international standards.

The domestic benefits o f the proposed alternative and the baseline will differ; under the
alternative, farmer organizations will be better informed and also more closely associated with
anypotential commercial negotiations betweenthe cotton industry andthe plant science industry
regarding the level o f the technology fee that would be paid each year for the transgenic seeds.
As a result o f negotiations capacity building activities, the technology fee would probably be
lower - if WAEMU approves an LMO for commercialization - in the alternative scenario o f
Program implementation than inthe baseline scenario. Thus, farmers will benefit more from the
Program-implementation scenario than from the baseline scenario, which might be neutral from
the farmer's point o f view (paying as much for the transgenic seed as for the conventional seed
plusthe pesticide).

Total expenditures under the Program scenario are estimated at US$24 million.
4.      Scope of the analysis
The activities related to the development o f common science-based risk assessment and
management methods inthe approval process o f LMOs, the setting up and implementation o f a
Regional Biosafety Framework to meet the requirements o f the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety
(CPB), and the construction o f the regional observatory would not take place without the
Program. The capacity building and public outreach activities are largely baseline activities and
GEF will allocate limited funding for these, focusing on the activities designed to create the
regional planand strengthen regional collaboration.

Domestic benefits, additional to those in the baseline, include reductions in risks o f damage to
the agro-biodiversity that provides employment, foreign exchange, and food for country
nationals through the export and subsistence agriculture sectors. Additional domestic benefits
will also come from the increased efficiency o f national review processes. Countries will also
benefit from the reduced cost o f adopting risk assessment and management procedures that will
no longer be designed at national levels but at the regional level, thereby avoiding the financing
o fthe same activities inthe five World Bank financing beneficiary countries.
5.      Incremental costs
The difference between the cost o f the baseline scenario (US$1.3 million) and the cost o f the
Program (US$24 million) is estimated at US$22.7 million. This represents the incremental cost
for achieving the global environmental objectives. Of this, about 24 percent, or $5.4 million, i s
requested from GEF. The remaining support will come from beneficiary country governments
and WAEMU (primarily in the form o f budgetary support contribution from the French
Treasury), from national andregional IDA allocation, and EC (inthe form o f grant).

General comments

Overview of Program

The proposed activities focus on developing an approach to regional capacity building and
participatory processes regarding risk assessment and risk management (and to some extent
communication) linked to introducing an L M O (in this case transgenic cotton) into cotton
producing countries (or potentially cotton producing countries) in West Africa. The approach is
presented as a future model for the subsequent introduction o f LMOs into this region.

The introduction o f any L M O into a new region i s associated with potential controversies (for
example, relating to environmental or human and animal health impacts, or effects on the rural
economy). Given the potential controversies associated with L M O introduction, there are
different views presented by a broad range o f stakeholders and end-users. The process thus
demands a systematic and unbiased risWcost benefit analysis, with credible data relating to
potential benefits and risks (environmental, health, and impact on regional economic factors).
Systematic, transparent and independent evaluation o f decision-making and participatory
processesi s essential ifthis credibility is to be developed andmaintained.

A primary objective o f the proposed project relates to localized capacity buildingregarding the
development o f regulatory policies designed to optimize both environmental protection and food
safety, and mitigate socio-economic impacts (including those related to the potential effects o f
PR and new transgenic products) on regional rural areas. Thus, the potential controversies
associated with the introduction o f LMOs into a new region are particularly addressedwithin this
framework.

Abbreviations and acronyms

My understanding is that LMO, as defined by the Cartagena Protocol, generally refers to "living
modified organism." Clearly there are profoundly different implications for environmental
release o f LMOs to GMOs interms o f the impact on biodiversity, particularly ina transboundary
and other potentially controversial factors. Inthe abbreviation glossary, L M O is described as a
"genetically modified organism". While the CPB states that LMOs are usually considered to be
the same as GMOs (Genetically Modified Organisms), inthis case clarification is needed. O f less
importance is the use o f the abbreviation "PPP", which may refer to public/private partnerships,
or Public Participation Panels. Iwonder if the latter is meant here. Ifthe former, please note the
broadly accepted abbreviation.




                                                 122

Interms ofpotentially problematic issues regarding environmental introduction of any LMO, it
is important to consider risk assessments related to health (human and animal) and potential for
negative environmental impact. Socio-economic risks relate to the potential impact on local and
national economies (including compromised export capacities) and, increasingly, bioethical
issues. Localisation o f an effective risk assessmentprocess i s contingent on the identification o f
local expertise in key areas, with local knowledge (for example, regarding the vulnerability o f
the local ecology to the toxic effects o f increased pesticide use). Capacity buildingis contingent
on both the training o f such expertise and the discussion regarding what local issues need to be
addressed.

The major issues for community discussion relate to potential transboundary issues associated
with LMO release, potential impacts on biodiversity and the environment, and the possible
socio-economic or socially transformative consequences o f L M O introduction (IPR and rural
farmingpractices, or adoptiono fnovel farming practices to include effective risk management o f
LMOs). Humanhealth effects mayberelatedto the introduction o fnovel food allergens into the
human-food chain, or potentially allergenic pollens into the environment. The inclusion o f
antibiotic resistance marker genes in transgenic cotton plants i s also the focus o f international
debate regardingpotential development o fmicrobial resistance.

The benefits o f adoption relate to implementation o f more effective farming practices in the
cotton producing countries (or potential cotton producing countries) involved in the Program,
which, according to the proposal, should ultimately lead to reduction in poverty in these
countries involved. The literature is somewhat equivocal regarding potential environmental
impacts o f transgenic cotton. A systematic evaluation o f potential positive impacts (for example,
reduced use o f pesticide ensembles) needs to be made, and evaluated against potential negative
effects (increased total usage o f pesticides). Due consideration o f these data should be made as
part o f the stakeholder consultation. Iam not able to comment on potential impact on local
biodiversity in the area, but Iassume this information would be suppliedby regional research
institutes with relevant expertise inthis field resulting from capacity buildingactivities.

Scientific andtechnical soundness o fthe Program

Scientific and technical soundness refers not only to the question o f whether the Program has a
sound "natural science" basis but also to the social science "technical" issues. Usually, social
science issues, such as tenure systems, local technical knowledge, local leadership on
conservation measures, enforcement, and monitoring, are as important as ecological aspects o f
biodiversity conservation in sustainable use programs and therefore deserve the same amount o f
attention intechnical reviews. Some o f the social science issues can also be dealt with under "the
degree o f involvement o f stakeholders."

Questions that could be raisedunder this issue are:

  1. I s there sufficient ecological and technical information available to give the Program a
      sound scientific base?




                                                123

There is a reasonable set o f data available regardingthe introduction o f LMO cotton inother
regions which can contribute to the local development o f a risk assessment and management
strategy. However, regional factors may be influential (for example, variations in local
biodiversity and / or farming practices). Iassume that these factors will be systematically
analysed as part o f the proposed activities. Potential transfer to other crops (inparticular non-
