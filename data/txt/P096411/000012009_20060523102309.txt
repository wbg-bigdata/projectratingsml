I.INTRODUCTION

1.      The Rural and Social Policy Reform Credit I(RSRC-l), the first in a series o f two to
three operations, i s designed to support the Government o f Niger's (GoN) medium-term reform
program to strengthen performance in service delivery and increase transparency and
accountability in public sector management. This medium-term approach builds on the
achievements and lessons o f three previous budgetary support operations (the Public Expenditure
Adjustment Credits Iand II,   and the Public Expenditure Reform Credit and Grant). It i s closely
linked to ongoing investment operations inrural and human development, and will set forth the
medium-term sequencing o f policy reforms for future budgetary support operations. The RSRC-
1, for a total amount o f US$50 million equivalent, will consist o f a one-year, two tranches
operation to support the national budget.

2.      The objective o f this first operation i s to help the Government o f Niger to overcome
specific policy constraints and institutional bottlenecks in selected sectors to improve service
delivery while ensuring adequate public expenditure management. It will focus on three pillars:
(i)public sector management; (ii)growth and agriculture development; and (iii)human
development, including population growth and gender. These pillars are closely aligned with the
Government's medium-term reform program that focuses on:           (i)improvingmanagement of
public resources; (ii) establishing an environment more conducive to economic growth through
the development o f both agriculture and the private sector; and (iii)  improving coverage and
quality o fbasic social services.


11.     COUNTRY CONTEXT

A.      Political and social context
3.      Niger has undergone a fundamental political transition over the last six years. The
military junta that assumed power after the April 1999 coup handed power over to a civilian
government following orderly presidential and legislative elections in December 1999. The
coalition government, headed by President Mamadou Tandja, has been able to improve political
and economic governance, to restore sufficient discipline in public finance, and successfully
implemented a reform program supported by the Bank and the IMF. Local elections were
successfully completed in July 2004 as part o f a program o f political decentralization, and
presidential and legislative elections were held inNovember-December 2004. Although Niger's
economic situation remains difficult and fragile, for the first time in more than a decade there
was a peaceful and democratic transition between civilian governments.

4.      The continued implementation o f the PRSP has resulted in progress in social indicators
(see detail in Section III).  Nevertheless, Niger remains a very poor country with a limited
natural and human resource base. Gross National Income (GNI) per capita was around
US$230 in2004 (nearly the level in 1976 and about half that o f 1980). The country i s rankedthe
lowest in the world (177th out o f 177 countries in 2005) according to the United Nations
Development Program (UNDP) Human Development Index. Poverty remains widespread and
social indicators compare poorly to the average in Sub-Saharan Africa.          The share o f the
population living in poverty, estimated at 64 percent in 2004, has remained at the level o f the
 early 1990s (63% in 1993), mainly due to the high levels o f population growth, recurrent
 droughts, and a famine in 2004. As in other African countries, poverty and extreme poverty are
 even more severe in rural areas where close to 80 percent o f the population still lives. Although

important progress has been achieved, the illiteracy rate in2003 was about 83 percent and infant
mortality was 153 per 1,000 live births, compared to 35 and 94 respectively for Sub-Saharan
Africa. Average life expectancy i s only 46 years, which i s exceptionally low for a country that
has been barely touched by AIDS. Several social indicators have improved over the past five
years, in particular in education and health. Yet because these efforts start from a low base,
progress to date invarious social indicators suggests that Niger may not reach most of the MDG
targets by 2015. The gross primary enrollment rate increased from about 29 percent inthe early
1990s to 52 percent in2005, and the ratio of girls to boys inprimary schools also increased from
25 to 40 percent over the same period. With the exception of the prevalence of HIV/AIDS (still
at less than l%),outcomes are extremely modest and are mostly comparable to those of
                   health
conflict-affected countries. Progress has been limited for infant, child, and maternal mortality;
the child mortality rate decreased only from 320 to 262 per thousand between 1990 and 2002.
Moreover, only about 15 percent of women benefit today from medically assisted child delivery
(the same as in 1990). Finally, limitedprogress has been achieved in terms of access to safe
water andbasic infrastructure services.

5.       Economic development has been constrained by lack of natural resources,a degrading
natural environment, high population growth, and low institutional capacity. Moreover, the
economy lacks diversification and i s highly exposed to exogenous shocks. This includes droughts
and locust invasions, changes inpolicy and political developments inneighboring countries, and
volatile aid flows, making it difficult for Niger to sustain productive activity.


B.       Economic context and recent macroeconomic performance

6.       Macroeconomic performance over the last four years (2002-2005) was generally
satisfactory despite the recurrence of domestic and regional sociopolitical tensions, drought,
and difficulties in securing timely and adequate external assistance. Real GDP grew on
average 4.4 percent in the 2001-05 period, slightly higher than the population growth rate,
compared to the average increase of 1.2 percent during 1999-2000. In2005 growth i s estimated
at 7 percent despite the economic slowdown in 2004 caused by the drought and locust attacks
(see Table 1). The strong growth performance over the 2001-05 period reflectedboth favorable
weather conditions and the impact o f measures to promote growth, including improvement in
fiscal management and increases o f capital expenditures on infrastructure (including
telecommunications and in the water sector through a World Bank-funded project). This
improved macroeconomic environment has increased investor and consumer confidence. On the
demand side, private investment increasedby 3.4 percent o f GDP between 1999 and 2005, rising
to 7.1 percent of GDP in 2005. Similarly, private consumption grew faster in nominal terms
during2002-2005 than inthe previous three years.

8.         Inflation (annual basis) averaged about 1.6 percent from 2000-2004, below the
WAEMU convergence criteria of three percent, and mainly as a result of continuedprudent
BCEAO monetary policies and good agricultureproduction. Inflation rose rapidly, however, in
early 2005 following a food crisis and the oil price shock, eventually averaging 7.8 percent for
the year. Food prices increased by 26.4 percent in 2005 with a peak o f 53.7 percent in August
2005, and transport price inflation rose by 4.4 percent on average during the year. Despite these
2005 increases, inflation remained broadly in line with the WAEMU convergence criteria,
reaching an average 3.2 percent between 2000-05.

9.         The external current account expanded from an average o f 7.7 percent o f GDP between
2002 and 2003 to 10.1percent o f GDP over the 2004-05 periods, reflecting the increase inthe oil
import bill, increased food imports resulting from the food crisis, and border closing with
Nigeria.


Fiscaldevelopment and challenges

10.        Niger's fiscal stance generally has been satisfactory since the new elected civilian
Government took office in early 2000. Government reform policies are focused on bringingthe
fiscal position to sustainable levels and complying gradually with WAEMUconvergence criteria.
Although important challenges remain, Niger has been making progress toward meeting the
WAEMU convergence criteria; particularly those related to fiscal performance; by 2004 Niger
had met five criteria compared to only one in 1999 (See Table 2).

11.        Government revenue increasedfrom 8.2 percent of GDP in 2000 to 10.4 percent in
2005 as a result of revenue-enhancing measures introduced since 2001. These measures
include: (i)     petroleum tax reform in August 2001, (ii)              strengthening tax and custom directorate,
(iii)  revenue measures introduced inthe 2002 budget laws to improve tax collection and broaden
the tax base', (iv) control over the granting o f tax exonerations and revision o f tax incentives
under the investment code, and (v) other specific revenue measures introduced in 20042. Still,
Niger's revenue/GDP ratio remains the lowest in the WAEMU region, below Benin and Mali's
ratios o f 14.6 and 15.1 percent, respectively. Achieving the WAEMU convergence criterion to
raise fiscal revenue from its current level o f 10.4 percent of GDP in 2005 to 17 percent will be
extremely difficult. Further increase inrevenue will depend on the ability o f authorities to tackle
the issues o f weak tax collection and excessive granting of tax exemptions and a narrow tax base.
Expanding the tax base will require the "re-fiscalization" o f the agriculture sector (i.e., finding
ways o f bringingagriculture into the fiscal net).

12.       Government expenditure increased slightly at an annual average of 18.4 percent of
GDP over 2003-2005 compared to 17.0 percent over the 2000-2002 period. This increase
partially reflects the rise incapital expenditure by about 1.0 percent of GDP, from 7.7 percent of
GDP in 2002 to 8.6 percent of GDP in 2005, largely as a result o f the implementation of the
Poverty Reduction Strategy and supported in part by resources freed by the HIPC Initiative.
Meanwhile, current expenditure declined from 10.7 percent of GDP in 2002 to 9.1 percent in
2005, notwithstanding government's efforts to clear domestic arrears. The overall budget deficit

B.       Key issues and sectoral strategies

26.      Accelerating economicgrowth, managingpopulation dynamics, and ensuring that the

likelihood of reaching at least some of the MDG targets. Improving Niger's living standards

Agriculture and rural development

36.      Despite unfavorable agro-climatic conditions and recurrent natural disasters,
agriculture and rural development continue to play a key role in Niger's economy. The rural
sector contributes to about 40 percent of GDP, a declining trend since 1980, with agriculture
accounting for about 52 percent and livestock about 30 percent of the rural output. Ago-pastoral
products account for about 85 percent of export revenues ex-uranium, with the livestock sector
contributing to 56 percent (mostly live exports). Overall, the country's agricultural trade balance
remains heavily indeficit, and imports of foodstuffs represent a thirdof the country's import bill.
Subsistence farming and animal husbandry, based on low-productivity and traditional techniques,
are pursued by the majority of the rural population.          Almost 85 percent o f the country's
population lives inrural areas, most of them below the poverty line. With increasing population
concentration in the most productive zones, the sustainable management o f productive natural
resources, particularly land and water, has become essential to Niger's agricultural growth and
rural poverty reduction.

Key issues inagriculture andruraldevelopment
37,      I n rural development, the biggest challenge is to achieve rapid and sustainable
modernization of the agriculture and livestock sectors conducive to rural growth and poverty
reduction. Inthe absence of market access opportunities, sufficient land tenure security, access
to key services (rural finance, agricultural research, knowledge and information systems,
veterinary services), private investment for the expansion of irrigated agriculture, for the
improvement of rain-fed farming systems, and for the intensification o f livestock production,
muchofNiger's ruralgrowthpotential remains untapped. As an example, the estimated irrigable
land covers 270,000 ha, o f which only 85,000 ha have been developed, with even less actually
irrigated.

38.      With an average annual sectoral growth below 2 percent over thepast 40 years, well
below the annual population growth rate, the challengesfor the rural economy to remain an
engine of growth are daunting. Above all, overcoming these challenges requires adequate
policies and effective institutions to capitalize on the existing potential, and to provide a strategic
framework conducive to much-needed modernization o f the country's agriculture and livestock
 sectors. This framework exists and i s defined by the 2003 Rural Development Strategy (RDS),
whose full implementationi s scheduled in2006.

 39.     Food insecurity and vulnerability. Food insecurity, affecting 80 percent o f the
population, i s chronic inNiger. It i s aggravated by highincidences of ruralpoverty, translated at
the household level in low purchasing power and lack o f access to food by vulnerable groups.

K e y issues inhealth
47.      The key issues affecting performance are as follows:

0        Inadequate financing and limited supply of basic health services.               As in most
         countries o f West Africa, Niger's health sector suffers from chronic under-funding. The
         weak capacity o f the Government to mobilize internal resources has been a major
         constraint, aggravated by the instability o f external aid. Currently, public expenditure on
         health (US$S per capita and year) represents 1.9 percent o f GDP and 7.2 percent o f
         Government budget (2004).       Lack o f sufficient funding has led to serious imbalances
         between investment and recurrent budgets. As a result o f insufficient public funds, the
         most cost-effective interventions are still in short supply, particularly in the poorest
         regions. In addition, regional disparities in the allocation o f resources have caused a
         deficit in essential generic drugs, vaccines, and other medical supplies in most regions.
         Major gaps also exist in the financing o f recurrent costs for basic health care and major
         public healthpriority programs. Cost-recovery schemes represent a major impedimentto
         access to basic services by the poor because they are not equitable as no fully subsidized
         schemes are set up.

e        Inequitableaccess to basic health services. Access to the health system suffers from
         inequities o f many types - geographic, structural, financial, and cultural - while the
         health services delivered are seriously inadequate in terms o f quality. Overall, due to
         limited supply o f basic health services, less than half o f the population has easy physical
         access to a health facility, and one out o f six live more than 15 km from the nearest
         health center. Inaddition, gaps between rural and urban areas as well as between socio-
         economic groups are very large, both interms o f health outcomes and access. Given the
         poor quality o f services, the average usage rates per capita have been declining from 42
         percent in 1996 to 26 percent in2003.

0        Inadequate human resource management. One o f the most urgent problems in the
         health sector i s the serious deficit o f human resources which i s inadequate both interms
         o f number and geographical distribution. With 6,000 employees, the Ministry o f Health

         only 6% o f the nation's population) and there are no incentives for already l o w paid
         workers to operate inrural areas. Poor management o f humanresources limits efficiency
         and efforts to match basic training to needs and to promote career growth. As a result,
         the levels o f health service consumption are affected not only by physical and financial
         access barriers, but also by insufficient numbers o f qualified medical staff.

0        Private financing of the health system is putting additionalburden on vulnerable
         groups. While the supply o f health services is largely public inNiger, its financing is
         mainly private, through out-of-pocket payments made by households.               Households
         finance an estimated 61 percent o f all health care expenditures (amount equal to about
         $3.60 per person per year); the Government contributes about 32 percent ($1.95) o f total
         health spending, while donors contribute the remaining 7 percent ($0.36). The strong
         participation of households in health care financing, while not rare in the regional
         context, responds inpart to the existence o f a national cost-recovery policy, begun inthe
         early 1990s with the adoption o f the Bamako Initiative. Although cost recovery has
         helped to secure resources for the purchase of essential drugs, the financial contributions
         requested of patients put a significant burden on the poorest and most vulnerable
         population groups, and may even prevent them from access to health care.

Key issues in demographics and gender
51.     Niger's ability to reduce poverty levels and to meet the MDGs is constrained by its
high rate of population growth, estimated at 3.3 percent per annum. The total fertility rate was
estimated at above 7 children per woman in 2000-05, making it the highest in the world. If
demographic growth i s not contained, Niger's population would reach 50 million in 2050, or
about four times its current size. With such a population, Niger would then become the second
most populated country inWest Africa, after Nigeria. The rapid increase inNiger's population i s
primarily explained by very high fertility levels coupled with decreasing mortality rates. The
continued high fertility rate i s the result o f multiple social factors that determine the current
reproductive behavior o f Niger's population.         These factors include pro-natalist attitudes,
religious beliefs, gender roles, low female literacy, and l o w rates o f contraceptive use, early
marriage age, and poverty. Marriage i s universal and early, especially inrural areas: one in two
women i s married by the age o f 15 at the latest. High fertility rates also have contributed to a
very young age structure: close to 50 percent o f Niger's population i s under the age o f 15, and
about 70 percent o f the populationi s under 25.

e       At the institutional level, the newly created Ministrystill has a weak capacity andhas no
        authority to influence the broader regulatory framework.

0        At the supply side, improved survival prospects for infants and children as well as the
         provision o f family planning services are crucial to bring about the behavioral changes

0        At the demand-side, the issues are more complex, because it involves changing attitudes
         and behavior that are entrenched in Nigerien culture and norms. Demand for lower
         fertility i s at the heart o f the issue, and supply intervention will not be effective unless

E.     Lessons learned

64.    The design o f this DPLhas been informed by lessons learned from previous adjustment
operations (PEACI, PEACII, PERCG) as well as by experience with sectoral investment projects:


e      The first lesson i s the importance o f Government ownership and commitment to the
       reform program. To ensure success, it i s necessary to establish and maintain support
       within the administrationand including better communicationamong the various sectoral
       ministries. The main components o f the RSRC series are derived from the Government
        o fNiger's own medium-termreformprogram and are inline with PRSP objectives.

e       Second, addressing the problem o f weak institutional capacity cannot be limited to a
       piecemeal approach via different operations, but rather requires a full-fledged medium-
        term strategy agreed upon with the authorities and targeted at the central ministries. The
       reform program supported by the RSRC series i s based on such a medium-term approach
        and i s firmly grounded in the findings o f a key diagnostic study on public expenditure
        management (PEMFAR) as well as specific sectoral studies (rural development strategy,
        education and health sector work, and demographic study) that were prepared jointly
        with the Government and development partners. The reformprogramfocuses on priority
        policy measures that are challenging but necessary for medium-term reforms and can be
        implemented in the next two to three years, taking into account capacity constraints
        withinthe ministries and other Government institutions.

e       Third, reform o f public expenditure management and social service delivery i s a
        medium-term process that requires sustained effort, continued policy dialogue, and
        strengthened institutional capacity. The core pillars o f the reform program supported by
        the RSRC are public finance, rural development, and human development.

e       Fourth, experience shows that operations exceeding two tranches often fail, incur
        delayed disbursements, require waivers, and need restructuring.           Multiple-tranche
        operations are best confined to a closed set o f reforms that can achieve their objectives
        over a series o f pre-defined actions. Given the need for medium-term incremental
        reforms, the RSRC series will be based inprinciple on a set o f single-tranche operations,
        with completed actions embedded in a medium-term framework. Given the time
        constraint, the RSRC-1 will be based on two fixed tranches.

e       Fifth,preparationo fbudget support operations shouldbe alignedwith the Government's
        budgetary cycle. Given the political and economic environment o f Niger, however, all
        adjustment operations for Niger (including this RSRC-1) have been prepared around
        June o f each calendar year, while the Government budget, presented to the Parliament
        during the third quarter of the previous year, already has incorporated Bank financing.
        To address this shortcoming, preparation o f subsequent RSRCs i s expected to be aligned
        with the Governmentbudget cycle.

Rationalefor medium-term approach

65.     The RSRC series seeks to provide support to the Government o f Niger to pursue the
reform agenda inpriority sectors defined in its PRSP. The series provides budget support to help
the Government to overcome bottlenecks identified as constraints to policy and institutional
reforms in Public Expenditure Management, Agriculture and Rural Development, Health,
Education, and Demographic growth and gender issues. The complexity o f the Government's
reform agenda (e.g., public expenditure under PEMFAR), however, represents a considerable
challenge for the country, particularly in light o f the institutional capacity-building needs. This
i s apparent in the delays incurred in implementing triggers identified under PERCG (see Annex
2). As a result, the proposed series o f RSRCs will help accompany the Government to implement
its reform agenda within a medium-term framework, and through related annual budget support
operations over the next two to three years. These operations will focus on the same priority
sectors listed above, gradually introducing others, as appropriate, based on the results o f ongoing
and upcoming analyticalwork (e.g., private sector development, infrastructure).

66.      The medium-term approach o f the RSRCs offers several advantages to Niger. Above all,
it allows anchoring o f the measures to be implemented in support o f the reforms in a results
framework; these results, coupled with indicators, are expected to contribute to the strategic
objectives defined by the Government in the PRSP, PEMFAR, and sectoral strategies.
Furthermore, continuity in budget support operations will secure predictability for concerned
sectors which will benefit from the policy dialogue around the PRSP-updating exercise.
Moreover, this predictability will result from the fact that with this medium-term approach, the
triggers agreed upon in RSRC-1 will become the priority actions for RSRC2. Also, with
continuity in the priority sectors, it i s expected that implementation and monitoring o f specific
measures to support reforms will be more efficient and thus will allow a gradual alignment o f
budget support with the country's budget calendar (i-e., earlier disbursement in the fiscal year),
contributing to improved budget execution.

67.       The slow pace o f PEMFAR reform often i s explained by the Government because o f a
very ambitious agenda with a tight calendar. Maintaining RSRC focus on the same priority
sectors will allow donors to provide technical assistance and institutional capacity buildingto the
Government. More specifically, the Bank will mobilize trust funds or grants (e.g., PRSP trust
fund, IDF grant, statistical capacity building trust fund) that can be made available to the
Government for specific areas o f focus under the RSRCs (e.g., for the PRSP update, for
strengthening technical capacity o f INS, etc.). Finally, at the sectoral level, the medium-term
approach o f the RSRC allows differentiation among sectors regarding the nature o f reforms
sought, taking into account the existing variety among sectors in the level o f reform
advancement. More specifically, in agriculture and rural development, for example, the program
will be able to focus first on helping the Government to complete the transition towards a
program approach by implementing a program-based sectoral strategy backed by an MTEF
(which already exists for Educationand Health), and then phase incore policy reforms.

Introduction


1.      Niger's development i s severely constrained by drought, highpopulation growth as well
as limited natural resources. The landlocked nature o f the country also adds cost to its imports
and exports.

2.      To address these challenges, several adjustment and stabilization programs were
implemented between 1982 and 1999 but yielded mixed results due mainly to a socio-political
environment that was hardly conducive to structural reforms. Authorities o f the FifthRepublic,
with the support o f Niger's development partners, implemented a three-year program (2000 -
2003) under IMF's first Poverty Reduction and GrowthFacility (PRGF).

3.       These reforms and recovery measures helped reduce internal and external economic
imbalances. Furthermore, with the support o f the Bretton Woods Institutions, ADF and other
partners, Niger adopted and implemented in January 2003 a Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS).
National institutions and the international community regard the PRS as a reference framework
for macroeconomic and sectoral policies o f the country as well as interventionsby development
partners.

4.       In view o f positive results achieved and the Authorities' firm commitment to carry on
with the initiated reforms, Niger's development partners agreedto grant the country external debt
relief under the Highly Indebted Poor Countries Initiative (HIPC).        Niger attained the HIPC
completion point on April 12,2004. It was also among the first group o f countries to benefitfrom
the recent multilateral debt cancellation by the G-8 countries. The country has therefore
benefited from substantial debt relief with all gains channeled to poverty reduction and
development o f priority social sectors through the President's Special Program.

5.       Notwithstanding several years o f initiatives and reforms, the challenges facing Niger are
still huge. These include: (i)accelerating economic growth to levels that can substantially
alleviate poverty, (ii) creating jobs through economic diversification, (iii)  establishing a legal
and institutional framework that i s conducive to private sector development, (iv) achieving
Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) and reducing the endemic poverty ;(v) promoting key
structural reforms to guarantee higher budgetary performance. To ensure that these reforms
improve the living standard o f the people, there i s the need to slow down population growth.

6.       In spite of the progress made, key reforms are yet to be implemented to achieve
sustained economic growth that will help significantly reduce poverty and attain the MDGs.
Consequently, Niger requires increased financial support to build human and material capacity
and to harmonize its development partners' procedures to achieve sustainable development.
Other factors are also needed to win the battle against poverty. These are, among others, a
greater involvement by all workers especially the elite, as well as the general public.

  7.     This Letter of Development Policy reflects the government's 2005-2007 Financial and
Economic Program drawn from the Poverty Reduction Strategy. It therefore hinges on public
 finance management to sustain groutth and reduce poverty. It also takes into account lessons
 learned from the assessment and conclusions o f the Priority Action Plan that came out o f the
PEMFAR exercise conducted from November 2003 to February 2005 as well as the need to
 start implementing far reaching policy reforms in sectors such as health, basic education and
rural development.

8.       The last five years were marked by serious economic and political reforms that have
yielded positive results despite problems stemming from a difficult international and regional
environment (crisis in Cote d'Ivoire and high petroleum prices), harsh climatic conditions in
2004 and the damage done by the invasion o f locusts.

9.       In the political arena, following the period of instability that prevailed between 1996
and 1999, free and fair democratic elections held at the end of 1999 paved the way for the
restoration o f political and social stability. The government born out of these elections has since
been able to maintain a peaceful social and political climate inthe country under the leadership
o f President Tandja. This was achieved through constant dialogue between the Authorities and
the entire political class and social partners, as well as the regular payment of salaries in arrears.
The President's Special Program, which directly benefits the most deprived in society, has also
helped maintain this stable social climate. President Mamadou Tandja's first term in office
therefore contributed to the consolidation of national democratic institutions. This was
evidenced by the good conditions under which 2004 local, presidential and parliamentary
elections were organized using methods consistent with democratic principles, according both
international andnational observers.

10.      On the economic front, government policies have stimulated growth and restored
budgetary discipline after many years o f economic stagnation and budget deficits.
Macroeconomic performance duringthe period 2000 - 2003 was satisfactory, with an average
GDP growth rate of 5% corresponding to a 2% growth rate of per capita income. In 2004,
performance was less satisfactory than expected due to drought, a locust invasion in the Sahel
region and an upsurge in world oil prices. The growth rate dipped to minus 0.6% as against an
initial projection of 4.1%. There was an upward trend in economic activity with a growth rate
estimated at 7.1% as a result of increased growth inthe agricultural sector (20.2%). Inflationfell
from 4% in 2001 to -1.5% in 2003 owing to higher agricultural production during the period as
well as prudentmonetary policies of BCEAO that eased pressure on internal prices. It settled at
3.7% in December 2004 with an annual decline o f 0.2% as a result o f low agricultural
production recorded in 2004. The effects o f poor agricultural production coupled with high
petroleumprices pushedinflationto 7.8% in2005.

11,      Regarding public finance, with the improvement in budget management during 2001-
2003, the basic budget deficit was maintained at 2.4% of GDP compared to 3.4% duringthe last
five years. However, social expenditures increased slightly from 5% of GDP in2001 to 5.2% in
2003. Meanwhile, the government was able to reduce internal arrears payments from 10.6% of
GDP at end o f 2003 to 3.1% of GDP at end o f 2004. The budget management trend for the
period 2000-2003 was maintained in 2004-2005 in spite of the difficult situation marked by
social unrest opposing fiscal measures contained inthe Supplementary Budget o f 2005, the food
crisis and the hosting of the 5* Francophonie games. Consequently, as at 3lStDecember 2005,

19.      On the social front, significant efforts were made to improve access to basic social

2.3 Populationand Gender

31.     The rapid population growth in Niger i s a major constraint to the success o f any
development initiative in the country. His Excellency the Prime Minister and Head o f
Government strongly acknowledged this fact in the Government's General Policy Statement for
2005 which called for a major shift inpopulation concepts and their related gender dimension. In
fact, the rapid population growth poses a serious obstacle to improving the living conditions in
Niger.

32.      The government has already taken some measures, including the establishment o f a
Ministry of Population and Social Action to address this major challenge. The Government has
also asked for the World Bank's support to prepare a Multi-Sector Population Program
(PRODEM).

Certainly, these population and gender issues remain delicate as they touch on core values and
social relations. It i s for this reason that the Government intends to use PRODEM to intensify
educational campaigns and organize a national Forum on these issues. The aims o f this Forum
are to create the necessary conditions for Niger to ratify the Protocol o f the African Charter on
Women's Rights and proceedwith implementation. This huge challenge requires urgent attention
and the Government has therefore established a time table o f interventions to be implemented

during the preparatory stage o f PRODEM. These interventions will be part o f triggers o f the

2-6-    Health

49.     Major progress has been achieved inthe health sector as actions planned inthe PEMFAR
action plan have been completed, inparticular improving the financing and access to basic health
services. The health budget has been increased innominal terms, from CFAF 24 billions in2004
to CFAF 26.8 billions in 2005 and to CFAF 31 billions in 2006, and health sector budget
preparation i s now governed by a medium term expenditure framework (MTEF) translating into
a program budget. To improve the management o f human resources, government has issued a
decree decentralizing health staff recruitment (April 2005), and intends to implement it during
the next recruitment campaign. The government has also intensified the fight against major
diseases. As a result, health outcomes from the implementation o f priority programs have
improved.     Vaccination coverage has recently increased; making drugs and impregnated
mosquito nets available free o f charge to the population have boosted the results of tuberculosis
and malaria programs. However, inthe area o f mother and child healthadditional efforts are still
needed to ensure that progress in corresponding indicators i s consistent with achieving the
MillenniumDevelopment Goals by 2015.

50.     The government intends to pursue its reform efforts in the health sector with the view to
scale up the availability o f health care to the population. In the next three (3) years, it will
implement specific measures to achieve objectives for the sector in the PRS and in the Health
Development Plan (PDS 2005-lo), which i s being supported by the Institutional Strengthening
and Health Sector Support Project (PUSS) recently approved by the World Bank in January
2006. Specifically, the P U S S will help: (i)   improve access and use o f essential health services;
(ii)improve efficiency and efficacy o f mother and child health services: (iii)     strengthen health
services, in particular in the area o f reproductive health, and the anti-malaria program; and (iv)
improve sector management and develop its organizational capacity. The 2006-2008 program

Humanresources management

51.     The most urgent and persistent problems to be addressed in the health sector are the
insufficiency and inadequate allocation o f its humanresources. Humanresources management in
the health sector i s confronted with financial and organizational constraints. Staff allocation
among regions and among rural and urban areas i s inadequate: most (63%) qualified healthcare
professionals prefer to work in urban areas. Despite Government's efforts in recent years to
recruit staff and improve the standards o f living o f healthcare providers-all        doctors and
pharmacists now receive housing allowances and other benefits-, the level o f staffing in 2006
remains grossly inadequate. In support o f designing a reform in this area, the government has
conducted a study on health sector staff motivation in 2005. The study uses the opinions o f
health service providers and trainees to look at alternative ways to provide financially sustainable
incentives to qualified personnel to work in rural areas as proposed by MSP/LCE.                  It
recommends an efficient and fair system blending salary and non-salary incentives.              The
determination o f salary incentives require an extensive review and clarification o f current
allowances and bonuses (including special bonuses paid to staff posted in areas o f the desert and
housing allowances). The government will assess the financial impact o f such measures on the
MSP/LCE budget. Non-salary incentives will be integrated in the new career plans designed to
improve the quality o f life and employment o f staff working in remote and difficult areas. The
Government also plans to modernize the management o f healthpersonnel, and to that effect, will
introduce, inter alia, fonvard-career management, regionalized budget management and

53.      The availability o f adequate healthcare to the population also suffers from inadequate

Furthermore, there i s a disconnect between financial resources mobilization and availability and
schedules governing the purchase o f inputs needed to control diseases. For instance, the
allocation o f budget to priority activities i s not always in line with the procurement timing o f
primarily drug and vaccines. To improve the availability o f healthcare to the population and to
ensure a decentralized budget execution and resource allocations, the government's program
includes measures to (i)improve the utilization o f budget credits, (ii)regionalize budget
execution to support the decentralization o f health services and to strengthen front-line health
services, and (iii)  ensure greater coherence between budget execution and seasonal health

Improving the access to health services through a reform of cost recovery

54.      Inequality o f income has translated into an unequal access to the health system among
population.    While health facilities and services cover only 48% o f the population, health
services are less utilized in areas markedby poverty and lack o f inputs needed for healthcare. To
measure the actual utilization o f health services, the Government has conducted a study in 2005
to evaluate the cost recovery system. The study has concluded to (i)     the adequacy o f resources
available to the health system to finance its recurrent costs, and (ii)     the existence o f major
bottlenecks preventing some programs to achieve success, in particular programs targeting
women and children. The Government has prepared an action plan to implement the study
recommendations, which, inter alia, exonerates some vulnerable categories (in particular

consultations.    Government's program will pursue the reform o f the cost recovery system,
revising current statutory instruments and adopting new legislation relative to the provision o f
free healthcare to targeted groups and to the marketing o f free contraceptive products in a
country where the current prevalence in contraceptive use i s 4% (DHS, 1998). The government
expects that enforcing these measures will significantly increase the demand for healthcare
among the population. This, in turn, will require improvements in the supply and quality o f
health services, and continued support by the government and by its technical and financial
partners.

     Annex 6: Debt SustainabilityAnalysis BaselineScenario Assumptions
                                                -

The baseline and alternative scenarios for 2005-2025 are based on the following
assumptions, which are broadly consistent with those used in the HIPC Completion Point
document:

   Over the length of the projection period, real GDP growth i s expected to rise from its
   historical average of 3.4 percent to an average of 4.3 percent inthe mediumterm (2005-
   OS), continuing to about 5.0 percent by 2025. This results from investments in irrigation
   and infrastructure, which would raise productivity in the agriculture sector. These,
   combined with efforts to diversify the economy, including through the development of
   tourism, would reduce vulnerability to macroeconomic shocks.

   Average inflation, as measured by the implicit GDP deflator, would average at about
   2.0 percent.

   The revenue to GDP ratio is projectedto rise gradually from 10.4 percent in2005 to 12.2
   percent by 2008 and to about 17percent by 2025, reflecting the gradual convergence of
   the tax revenueratio to the 17percent WAEMUnorm.

   Public expenditures, currently at 19.6 percent o f GDP, would stabilize at about 19
   percent by 2008, reflecting the authorities' budgetary efforts to prioritize expenditures.
   Over the 2009-2025 period, total expenditures remain at about 20 percent of GDP, with a
   slightly higher proportion spent on capital expenditures, with the aim of enabling the
   authorities to make progress towards meeting the Millennium Development Goals
   (MDGs).
   Total exports of goods and services, which benefited from a temporary boost in gold
   exports in 2005, are projected to grow at an annual average rate o f 6.2 percent, spurred
   by the strengthening of non-mineral exports over 2009-2025. Imports are projected to
   grow at an annual average of 5.8 percent, broadly in line with nominal growth. These
   assumptions lead to an improvement in the non-interest current account deficit from an
   average of 7.0 percentof GDP in2006-2010 to about 5.0 percent of GDP by 2025.

   The baseline scenario assumes that 50 percent of total external financing needs
   (including project assistance) will contain a 60 percent grant element excluding the
   borrowings from the IMF, while the remaining financial requirements are met by grants
   and net-FDI.Two sensitivity analyses are included (alternative 1 and 2), and the third
   alternative scenario reflects the effect o f reducing the grant element to 50 percent
   (alternative 3). Finally, the last scenario (alternative 4) takes into consideration the
   effect of the Multilateral Debt Cancellation Initiative applied on top of the alternative 3
   scenario.

and rehabilitation of the power system. The privatization o f the whole sale petroleum product
distribution company (SONIDEP) has failed in part because o f difficulties in finding sound
foreign private partners interested in investinginNiger. With Bank's assistance, the authorities
revisited their objectives and approach in private provision of infrastructure. The restructured
project seeks to enhance corporate governance agenda for selected state owned companies
(NIGELEC,     SONIDEP) and on the design o f a Government unit to implement it. The Fund i s
also a key partner in this policy dialogue and has included elements of the public enterprise
reform agenda, such as the continuous implementation o f apetroleumpricing system.


3.       Rural development.The Bank has provided support for developing and implementing a
comprehensive rural development strategy, which aims at mitigating vulnerability and
stimulating income generation, especially in rural areas. A rural development strategy was
completed in November 2003 and i s centered on three pillars: (i)  improving the access of rural

                                              - 110-

populations to economic opportunities; (ii)protecting rural populations against risks and
improving food security and managing natural resources sustainability; and (iii)     enhancing the
capacity o f public institutions and rural organizations to improve the management of the rural
sector. Bank assistance in this sector i s provided through three ongoing operations: Private
Irrigation; the Emergency Locust Project aiming to reduce Niger's vulnerability to ' future desert
locust infestations by supporting improved strategies for prevention, early warning systems,
reactions, and mitigation at both the national and regional levels; and Community Action
Program. Decreasing the dependence of the vast majority o f Niger's population on rain-fed
subsistence agriculture i s a key objective of this strategy. Bank support in this area emphasizes
the promotion of small-scale irrigated agriculture, livestock, farm and non-farm income-
generating activities and cereal bank construction.


4.       Social sector reforms. The Bank has assisted the Government in the preparation of a
ten-year development plan in the education sector (PDDE) that i s based on the PRSP and
includes a medium-term expenditure framework for the basic education sector. It has also
provided assistance and played a key role inNiger's eligibility for participation inthe Education
for All-Fast-Track    Initiative. Inaddition, a post primary education study has been completed.
Improvement o f access of the poor to social services i s one of the strategic pillars in the PRSP.
The Bank supports this objective through ongoing lending operations, which combine project
assistance with program support, as well as analytical work, notably on population, gender, and
poverty. There has also been close collaboration between the Bank and the Government in the
design and implementation of reforms in the education and health sectors, such as the
introduction o f decentralized recruitment of teachers ("volunteer teachers") and health workers
on a contractual basis. This contractual recruitment program has helped address issues of human
resource shortagesand supply-side constraints while keepingpayroll costs sustainable


5.       Inthe health sector, the Bankhasworked with the Government on the preparationof the
Strategic Orientations for Health Sector Development. In2002, the Government adopted a ten-
year health policy strategy (2002-2011). Its main objectives are to further improve access to
health services (from 47 percent in 2000 to 80 percent in 2011) and to reduce the incidence of
infectious diseases, by promoting new approaches, including preventive action. With a view to
make this strategy more operational, the Government has adopted the five-year development plan
for the sector in January 2006. The Bank approved in January 2006 a health SWAP o f U S $
35million which includes a reproductive health and malaria components. .The RSRC 1will help
the government consolidate and enhance these reforms by: (i)     improvingpersonnel management
and allocations in both sectors; (ii)promoting governance and accountability through
information sharing and empowering the community in the education sector; (iii)increase access
and provision o f quality service in both sect0rs.A multi-sector demographics operation i s also
under preparation.


6.       Poverty monitoring. The Bank worked closely with the Government in preparing a
poverty profile that served as the basis for the PRSP poverty diagnosis. While this diagnosis has
been judged as thorough and comprehensive by the joint staff assessment (JSA), it i s based on
household survey data from 1993. Updating the existing database i s therefore an important
concern of the Government. A nationwide census was recently completed, and preparations for a
new household partially funded by the government which launch is scheduled in the second
semester of 2006. A Demographic and Health survey and the 2005 Bank financed Core Welfare
Indicators Survey (CWIQ) which should be completed by end June 2006 will provide updated

                                              - 111-

social indicators most needed for the PRSP update and an assessment o f progress towards PRSP
targets (together with the Country Economic Memorandum under preparation). The Bank,
together with other donors, i s also advising the authorities on strengthening institutional
arrangements for the monitoring and the evaluation o f poverty in the context o f the PRSP. In
addition, the Bankhas financed the revision o f the 2001 Participatory Poverty Assessment (PPA)
as a contribution to the Government's efforts to update and strengthen the knowledge base on
poverty and social development.

Areas where Bank and Fund share the lead


7.       Poverty reduction strategy. Together with other external development partners, the
Bank and Fund have jointly provided assistance to the Government in the preparation o f its
PRSP. Since its completion, both institutions have jointly advised the authorities on the
refinement and implementation o f the strategy. The first and second progress reports on the
implementation o f the PRSP have been prepared with the assistance o f the Bank and the Fund
and bothprovided comments and advice for the elaborationo f the thirdProgress Report expected
inJune 2006.


8.       Debt sustainability. The Bank and Fund supported the Government's efforts to reach
the HPC completion point inApril 2004 with additional relief ("Topping-Up") granted to Niger.
The country is still at debt distress as evidenced by the last D S A completed in September 2005
conjointly by Bank and Fund staff on end-2004 debt data. Hence, the authorities need to pursue
sound macroeconomicpolicies and reforms and prudent debt policies (essentially, respecting that
all new loans have a minimum grant element o f 50 percent). Niger also benefited o f the
Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative (MDRI), but the effect will be short-lived as the NPV o f debt
to exports ratio will raise rapidly from 58 percent in 2006 to 124 percent by 2025 due to limited
internal revenue mobilization capacities and huge financing requirements. The need to build
technical and institutional capacity for managing Niger's external debt has been stressed by the
Fund and the Bank. Several measures to strengthen the external debt unit were implemented as
structural benchmarks under the previous PRGF. Bank and Fund staffs have also supported the
external evaluation o f the use o f HIPC resources under the President Special Program. As agreed
in the Bank's Public Expenditure Reformoperation (PERCG), the government integrated HPC
resources into sectoral programs o f HD sectors in 2006 Budget Law and intends to expand the
measure to rural development ministries upon completion o f the rural sector MTEF (scheduled
for June 2006) and the elaborationo f sector ProgramBudget.


9.       Budgetary and public expenditure reforms. Strengthening public finances i s a
prerequisite for success o f Niger's broader reform agenda. The Bank and Fundshare the lead in
this area. Bothinstitutions haveplayedkeyroles inhelping the Governmentreduce domestic and
external arrears. While the Fund i s leading the dialogue on revenue-enhancing measures, the
Bank i s concentrating its efforts on budgetary reforms, in particular in the area o f public
expenditure. The Fund i s also making key contributions to improving budgetary processes: a
number o f important measures, such as preparation o fbudget review laws and computerizationo f
budgetary expenditure, have been included as structural benchmarks in the previous PRGF
arrangement. In2004, the Bank prepared jointly with the Government and EU a comprehensive
assessment o f Niger's Public Expenditure Management Systems and Capacities (Public
Expenditure Management and Financial Accountability Review: PEMFAR). The Bank has
 supported budgetary reforms through four adjustment operations (PEAC I, PERCG
                                                                            PEAC II, and

                                                 - 112 -


the upcoming RSRC I).       Consolidating and deepening these reforms (based on PEMFAR priority
action plan) i s a critical objective of the ongoing RSRC Iwhich should be presented to the Board
inJune 2006. The CountryProcurement AssessmentReport (CPAR) was updated in2004.


10.       Financial sector reform. The Government launched a comprehensive financial sector
reform program in 2002. Supported by a World Bank project, the program covers the regulatory
and legal environment, the banking sector, micro-finance, postal financial services and social
~ecurity.'~Some progress has already been realized. Two commercial banks, B C N and BINCI
and three insurance companies were restructured and recapitalized. Audits o f all major micro-
finance institutions have been completed. Restructuring plans for the post office have been
designed and staff reduction has started. Inthe next two to three years, measureswill be taken to
improve the legal and judicial environment (modification of legislation for the issuing of land
titles, improvement of the legal framework for the taking of guarantees, training o f magistrates,
etc.). The Housing Bank (CDN) will be either privatized or liquidated by end December 2006;
the Community Lending Bank (CPCT) will be restructured. Micro-finance institutions will be
restructured on the basis of the results of the audits, and the supervisory unit at the Ministry of
Finance will be strengthened. The post office was split into two entities in2005, Niger Post, for
pure mail transactions, and FinaPost, a financial services affiliate. Finally, an actuarial audit was
conducted o f the CNSS, the social security institution.


11.      Civil service reform and decentralization. The reform and modernization o f the civil
service i s an important element of Niger's PRSP, yet there has been little progress inthis area so
far. The authorities are currently making an effort to put in place an integrated civil service
database. By allowing a more transparent and effective management of the civil service, this
database should improve control over the wage bill. Controlling the wage bill i s important for
maintaining fiscal balance, as recognized by the previous PRGF arrangement, which had set
quantitative benchmarks for the wage bill. Planning for the implementation o f the legal
framework for political decentralization of 1996 has recently gained momentum. The first local
elections took place on July 24, 2004. However, important concerns regarding this reform
remain, such as the lack of capacity at the local level and the fiscal implications o f
decentralization. To help the Government address some of these concerns, the Community
Action Program will help build capacities in rural communities in planning, implementing, and
monitoring micro-development projects as well as the upcoming Local Urban Infrastructure
DevelopmentProject.

Areas in which the Fundleads


12.      Macroeconomic management. The main objectives o f Niger's macroeconomic
program, as stated in the PRSP, are to ensure economic and financial stability while promoting
sustainable and robust growth. The Fundi s supporting this programthrough its PRGFframework
by providing financial and technical assistance, as well as through dialogue on macroeconomic
policy reforms. The program has made satisfactory progress since approval o f the first PRGF
arrangement in 2000 by achieving most o f its benchmarks and overall positive fiscal
performance. A second PRGF was approved in January 2005 and two reviewswere successfully
completed to date. In the context o f the macroeconomic framework underlying the PRSP, the
Bank has provided technical assistance in building capacity in the Ministry o f Finance and

sectoral ministries (Education, Health adrural Development); and macroeconomic modeling.
13.      Fiscal policy. Fiscal consolidation i s a key objective o f the PRGF and i s supported by a
number of performance criteria and benchmarks. Increasing budgetary revenue in order to
progressively lower the Government's reliance on external assistance is particularly important,
given Niger's low level o f revenues, compared with regional partners in WAEMU. Interms o f
expenditures, the Fund i s mainly concerned with overall budget envelopes, while the Bank

Education, Health and Rural Development at budget execution phase.


14.      Monetary policy. The Fundleads the policy dialogue on monetary policy, which is set

World BankGroup strategy


15.      The Bank's new Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) for the period 2003 to 2005 was
approved by the Bank Board in January 2003. It supports the implementation of the PRSP. A
newCAS is underpreparation.


16.      As of April 30, 2006, the World Bank lendingportfolio inNiger consisted of nine IDA
active operations, with a total commitment of US$ 255 million, o f which $131.5 million i s
undisbursed.IDA assistancehas helpedreduce the volatility inODA by compensating short-term
declines in assistance from other partners. IDA has also been responsive to exogenous shocks.
For instance, an Africa Emergency Locust Project (AELP) aiming at fighting the locust's
infestation in West Africa was delivered in FY05. A Health sector SWAP and a Development
Policy lendingwere delivered inFY 06 (, Board date the DPL which covers more than 41 percent
ofNiger's 2006 financing gap i s scheduled for June 13,2006).

17.      The AAA program helps the Government in areas such as poverty analysis, gender,
population growth, and sources o f growth and the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs). The
AAA programalso aims at reinforcing public sector capacity inpursuit of the PRSP's objectives
and in preparing Niger for the transition to consolidated programmatic lending. In support of
these objectives, sector work on population, rural development, Public Expenditure Management
and Financial Accountability Review (PEMFAR), CPAR, and Participatory Poverty Assessment
have been completed. A Country Economic Memorandum (CEM) focusing on accelerating
growth and achievingthe MDGs inNiger i s under preparation.

18.      The Bank is committed to enhancing external partnerships in the framework of the
Government's      current  efforts to mobilize and coordinate donor support for PRSP
implementation. Besides the strong partnership with the Fund, the Bank i s collaborating with a
number of donors in different areas, including the European Union, the African Development
Bank (AfDB), the United Nations Development Program (UNDP), and key bilateral donors
involved indevelopment issues inNiger.
