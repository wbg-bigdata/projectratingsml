A. STRATEGIC CONTEXTAND RATIONALE
This document describes a proposed US$2.0 million Second Economic Governance Technical
Assistance Grant (EGTAG 11) to the Government of Haiti (GoH) for strengthening its economic
governance, thereby enhancing its ability to use domestic and external resources to advance its
national development programs efficiently, effectively, and transparently. The operation follows
the first EGTAG (approved by the Board in May, 2005), working in the same general areas and
building on its expected results. Activities under both grants derive from the economic
governance section of the Government's Interim Cooperation Framework (ICF). The ICF was
prepared b y the Government, with donor participation, in April-June 2004 as a means of
identifying the country's priority development interventions and related funding within a donor
coordinated framework. The ICF also informed the preparation of earlier IDA operations -
including the Economic Governance Reform Operation (EGRO) in the amount of US$61
million, approved b y the Board in January 2005 and LICUS Trust Fund Grant, approved in
November 2004 in the amount of US$6 million, of which US$1.5 million supports economic
governance reforms.
The proposed Second Economic Governance Technical Assistance Grant (EGTAG 11) i s
intendedto build on and support existing IDA operations and to coordinate closely with technical
assistance activities of other donors. It follows from the indication in the TSS and the
presentation of EGTAG Ithat IDA assistance would be structured as a progressive package of
incremental support and would include another technical assistance operation if conditions in
Haiti for economic governance reforms remained favorable. EGTAG I1will thus continue work
begun in EGTAG I,although with relatively greater attention to ICF priorities which were not
covered under EGTAGI.      Additionally, as EGTAGIwas closely coordinated with the Transition
Government's priorities, the new Government's ownership of both operations would be
enhanced by giving it an opportunity to program and execute these follow-on actions.

It should be stressed, as laid out in greater detail below, that the various components of IDA'S
economic governance assistanceprogram are organized as steps in a longer term process and that
the immediate focus of EGTAG 11, like EGTAG Ibefore it, i s to help the Government
operationalize its new legal framework for managing public resources in an efficient, effective,
and transparent fashion. Specifically this means supporting the central economic governance
agencies in the reengineering of their structures and operating procedures to match their
redefined mandates and in their elaboration and provision of guidance, instructions and oversight
as regards resource management throughout the public sector.               Here IDA support i s
complemented by that of other donors (and especially the IDB) who are focusing on buildingthe
relevant capacities within the sector ministries thereby enhancing their ability to work to the new
rules.
1. Country andsector issues
 a. General Background: Situated on the western third of the island of Hispaniola, Haiti i s the
poorest country in the Western Hemisphere.          An estimated 76 percent of its 8.3 million
population lives below the poverty line (less than US$2 a day) while approximately 56 percent
lives in abject poverty (below US$1 dollar a day). At almost three times the regional average,




                                                  1

Haiti's poverty level can only be compared to that of Sub-Saharan Africa,'                   and income
inequality i s amongst the highest in the world.2 Haiti ranks 153`d (out of 177 countries) in the
UNHumanDevelopment Index. Lifeexpectancy is 53 years. Infant andmaternalmortality rates
remain among the highest in the world. Fully half of the population i s illiterate. Malnutrition
affects a third of all children, and ineffective public health and sanitation systems together with
widespread poverty provide fertile ground for the rapid spread of such infectious diseases as
HIV/AIDS3, tuberculosis, polio and human rabies. The widespread lack of solid waste
management and access to potable water also contributes to many waterborne illnesses and
preventable diseases and childhood deaths.
Haiti's history has been fraught with political and economic instability. Since the mid 20th
century, the country has endured a 30-year dictatorship characterized by corruption, repression,
and the pillage of its wealth; followed by a series of short-lived governments also plagued by
corruption; and the election, overthrow, reinstatement, and ultimate resignation in February 2004
of President Jean Bertrand Aristide. In the wake of Aristide's forced exile, a Transition
Government was established in March 2004 with a mandate to create the conditions necessary to
hold democratic elections in end-2005 and hand over power to a new government inearly 2006.
Faced with the formidable challenges of deep-seated political instability, weak institutions,
inefficient resource allocation and usage, and the resulting mistrust of its citizenry, the
Transitional Government joined forces with the donor community in April 2004 to prepare the
Interim Cooperation Framework (ICF). The ICF identifies priority interventions and
associated financing needs to support Haiti's path to economic, social and political recovery
throughout the July 2004-September 2006 transition period. It also constitutes the foundation
upon which a future medium-term Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP) will be prepared.
The ICF i s based on 4 pillars: (i)       strengthening political governance and promoting national
dialogue; (ii)   strengthening economic governance and contributing to institutional development;
(iii)promoting economic recovery; and (iv) improving access to basic services. The ICF also
takes into consideration cross-sector issues, notably crisis prevention, human rights, gender, and
HIV/AIDS. The newly elected Government and the donors have established a steering
committee to coordinate the extension of the ICF to December 2007. President-elect RenC
PrCval has confirmed his commitment to continue the ICF focus on economic governance as a
key priority.
Progress in Haiti over the past two years has been beset by challenges - natural disasters, rising
energy prices, and escalating criminal and politically-motivated violence. Nonetheless, the
Transition Government, on its own and with donor support, made some significant advances. In
an effort to fight corruption, build links with civil society, and manage resources in a more
transparent manner, it created an anti-corruption office, conducted an in-depth diagnostic study
on the state of governance and corruption, and established mechanisms for civil society
monitoring. It prepared its 2005 and 2006 budgets in accordance with rules set out in a new
Organic Law and the ICF priorities and disseminated key budget execution data. It drastically cut
the use of "comptes courunts" (unprogrammed funds given to ministries to finance
"unanticipated expenses" and frequently used in a non-transparent manner). It set up a central

Haiti's implementation of a longer-term program of incremental capacity-building.
b. Sector Specific Issues: For a variety of reasons, not the least of which were the weaknesses
of its institutional base, Haiti's economic governance deteriorated severely over the years leading
to the departure of the second Aristide government. Donor dissatisfaction with management of
the funding they provided was a principal cause of their withdrawal of Government-executed
programs at the end of the last decade, after which conditions continued to worsen. That donors
remained present as long as they did was partly due to their ability to rely on Haiti's non-
governmental organizations andor to channel funds outside the Government's normal financial
management system. But, as the GoH has continuously remarked, that strategy did not resolve
and arguably may have compounded existing institutional weaknesses. Services can indeed be
provided through direct payments to NGOs, but the practice i s neither sustainable nor a
substitutefor a well-functioning set of public institutions.
This section reviews the situation and trends inthe area of economic governance, looking first at
the existing problems and then at progress made in addressing them over the past two years.
Although institutional weaknesses extend to all levels and sectors o f government, attention here
i s focused on the functions and organizations most directly related to the programming and use
of public resources - financial management in its most ample sense. The Bank has identified this
area as critical to the success of its interventions as well as to Haiti's sustained recovery and
future development.
Budgetary Process as o f k t e 2003: Haiti's Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) has the
responsibility for overall budget formulation, execution, and control, sharing programming and
oversight of public investment with the Ministry of Planning and External Cooperation (MPCE).
However, the 1985 Budgetary Law (replaced in 2004) defining MEF's basic procedures, was
never fully implemented, and critical positions and offices were simply not created. Offices that
did exist were often understaffed and under-equipped. Parameters for budget analysis and data
for budget preparation were weak, and the budget did not incorporate all expenditures or
revenues. Its contents were not linked to programs or higher-level government goals (e.g.
poverty reduction), but solely presented in line item form, and the MEF did not have, and still
lacks, means for tracking the quantity and quality of outputs produced through budgetary
outlays. The Treasury, which should manage liquidity and program disbursements in line with
available funds, had become little more than a mechanism for endorsing checks to employees
and suppliers and processing accounts on an accrual basis. Typically, deficit control was
accomplished by tapping Central Bank reserves or accumulating internal and external arrears.

Overall, the cash management system was weak and did not allow for proper control and
synchronization between disbursements and receipts.
From 1997 through 2001, the government operated without an approved budget. The three
following budgets were approved late into their fiscal years. Between 1997 and 2003, an
increasing share of public resources (reaching 60 percent in 2002-03) was channeled through
comptes courants and/or largely assigned to a catch-all category called "autres interventions
publiques." While it was not expected that funds so assigned be programmed beforehand, the
requirement for subsequent documentation and reporting of their uses was routinely ignored.
Public Znvestment as ofLate 2003: On the investment side, officially managed by the MPCE,
expenditures were not identified and planned in a coherent framework. Like the current
expenditure budget, investment projections were limited to a single-year timeframe. Sector
ministries' planning offices (Unite`s de Programmation) played little part in the process; many
had simply disappeared. B y the mid 1990s, public sector investment had come to be driven by
donor-funded projects. Donors typically set up special accounts with the Central Bank, but
without an automatic transfer of information to the MEF or bypassed the Government altogether.
The MEF eventually received information on these accounts and disbursements, but aid supplied
in kindwas not registered at all. Although the FY2004-05 public investment budget rose to 6.2
percent of GDP (over the 2002-03 average of 2.1 percent), limited planning and execution
capacity, as well as a lack of sufficient transparency, pose concerns about vulnerability to
misuse. However, the recent increase was donor funded and thus followed proper procurement
procedures.
Control as of Late 2003: Internal controls were affected by the lack of a well-structured
accounting system, fragmentation of the accounting function, and the extensive use of comptes
courants. External audits of the government budget had not been done for several years. The
supreme audit institution, the Cour Supe`rieure des Comptes et du Contentieux Administratif
(CSCCA) focused nearly exclusively on ex-ante control, duplicating the functions of the
Ministry of Economy and Finance's Directorate of Budgetary Control (DCB).            The posts of
several of the CSCCA's ten members had remained vacant for several years. The CSCCA also
lacked sufficient staff and equipment as well as funds for training. Under the 1985 budgetary
law, the CSCCA's role in ex-ante and ex-post control, and its simultaneous functioning as an
administrative court presented potential conflicts of interest. Another usual source of external
control, the Parliament, was not in operation between early 1999 and mid-2000 or after early
2004. Effective review of the budget law and its execution will be a challenge for the newly
elected Assembly, given the poor quality of information managed and provided by the Executive
and the legislature's own weaknesses.
Procurement as @Late 2003: Untilrecently, public procurement operated under a 1989 decree
which had never been fully implemented. The law had several flaws, including its failure to
regulate certain types o f public procurement, overregulation o f some areas (e.g., by establishing
membership of evaluation committees -jurys de selection - and the thresholds for the use of
different procurement methods), and the provision for the CSCCA's prior approval of contracts,
a clear conflict with its other roles. The system was decentralized, but the Commission Nationale
des Marche`s Publics (CNMP), which was to oversee its implementation, had not existed since
the early 1990s, and the departmental commissions were never created. The ad hoc arrangements
used to fillthis void were neither efficient nor transparent and gave rise to accusations of
corruption. Reduced operating budgets and salaries drove qualified procurement specialists to


                                                  4

seek employment in the private sector. Sole-source (grk-u-grk) contracts and unadvertised
bidding (uppels d'oflrks restraints) had become the norm; there were no standardized bidding
documents or manuals; and information on proposed or awarded contracts was not publicized or
centrally collected. Chronically slow payments to contractors, combined with a lack of legal
recourse, resulted in higher prices, liquidity problems for vendors, and the potential for
additional abuses.
Corruption, Vertical Accountability, and Transparency as of Late 2003: Since its initiation,
Transparency International's Corruption Perception Index (CPI) has placed Haiti among the
lowest rankings worldwide. While a study by TI'SHaitian branch indicated the private sector's
belief that corruption i s the leading constraint on economic growth and investment:            it also
showed corruption to be widely tolerated. Government's limited control of financial data and the
lack of any law requiring information to be made public meant that citizens had little or no
knowledge of how public resources were used. Media coverage remained uneven, and civil
society groups were few, tended to be concentrated in the capital city, and were relatively
unprepared to deal with the information that was available. Within government control
mechanisms - the judiciary, public prosecution and police, as well as those already mentioned -
remained weak, little trusted, and plagued by their own internal problems. The offices of Fiscal
and of Financial Control had yet to be established to provide the required internal checks on
revenue collection and expenditures.
Human ResourceManagement as of Late 2003: One of the greatest impediments to successful
economic governance in Haiti i s the inadequate quality and quantity of human resources - the
absence of a sufficient number of qualified personnel, within the MEF and the sector agencies, to
make the system work and the lack of a good incentive system to attract, maintain, and motivate
the public service. The legal framework (a 1982 law and the Constitution) called for a modern,
reliable and competent public service based on equal access to employment and selection on the
basis of merit. However, the cabinet-level office charged with overseeing the system was never
created and the necessary ArrCtk (a detailed set of specific regulations to govern personnel
administration) was never drafted. A series of donor-sponsored studies indicate that a majority
of appointments were made on the basis of political and personal connections. For those that did
get a job, by whatever means, incentives for good performance were scarce. Public sector
salaries lost fifty percent of their value between 1995 and 2000, and dropped more rapidly
afterwards. There was little funding for training and limits on the possibilities for promotion for
good performance through a formal career system. Donors, NGOs, and the private sector often
attracted the most qualified staff.
Human resource inadequacies took many forms. Highly technical offices (the statistical and
economic studies offices, procurement personnel in ministerial and independent project units),
had some qualified professionals, but their number was often too small to accomplish their
mandate. In a few cases (e.g., commissionaires de douanes5),redundancies ("a useless layer of
extra semi-officials") were also a problem. The actual quality of existing personnel was never

c. RecentImprovements: The conditions described above were those confronting Haiti in early
2004 when the Transition Government launched an economic governance reform program with

Decree (published in February 2005) are among the most important. The former, in addition to
establishing the outlines of a new budgetary process, also mandates the creation of a new
accounting      system,     distinguishes between          authorizers     (ordonnateurs)       and    approvers

control) and a new internal auditing office (the Inspection des Finances), and removes the Cour

eliminating one potential conflict of interest. The Procurement Decree creates a Commission
Nationale des March& Publics (CNMP) with a revised mandate and full-time members selected
transparently with input from both the public and private sectors. The decree requires that
competitive bidding practices replace direct contracting and includes new provisions governing
the procurement o f intellectual (consulting) services. A new civil service law and a new law for
the organization o f the central administration were drafted and enacted by decree in July 2005.

regulations to permit the implementation of their revised functions.                  A 2003 law created the

1. Lendinginstrument
The choice of a Technical Assistance Grant follows the LICUS Task Force recommendations
and their emphasis - as well as that of other donor programs - on technical assistance for
institutional strengthening. To ensure GoH ownership, EGTAG I1supports ICF priorities and

2. ProgramObjectiveandPhases
The operation proposed herein i s part of an assistance package described in the TSS. The overall
objective of both the package and the proposed project i s to strengthen the GoH capacity in
economic governance by developing its ability to: (i)      produce and execute a budget, track its
implementation, and reduce irregular uses and expenditures; (ii)     manage human resources in a
more effective, efficient, and transparent fashion; (iii)monitor public service delivery as a means
of improving strategic tracking of sector policies; (iv) strengthen anti-corruption and civil society
monitoring mechanisms; and (v) tighten coordination of donor contributions and foment broader
citizen participation in and understanding of the reforms. Support in all these areas i s intended to
move the process along by measurable increments; at the end of EGTAG 11, the GoH should
have fully established the legal and structural framework required for satisfactory economic
governance although further work will still be required to ensure its adequate functioning.
Additional economic governance objectives (e.g., attention to departmental government) are not
yet included in this package, but might be considered for possible future operations. This
program i s based on the ICF, subsequent discussions with the Government, and information
provided by other donors on their support in the area. As should be clear from the preceding
description o f the situation, an effective, sustainable institutional change program will require
several years of concerted, continuous effort. In this connection, the institutional strengthening
initiated by the LICUS i s continuing under EGTAG Iwhich began disbursingin February 2006
and has a Closing Date of December 31, 2007. However, the ICF's objectives cannot be
accomplished quickly. For several of them, experience in other countries suggests that a five-
year time frame i s reasonable, assuming no setbacks. Thus, both sustained government
commitment and a substantial injection of donor resources will be needed. IDA'Smedium-term
assistance program in the area of economic governance would be complementary to those
provided by other donors. Hence, the following description o f IDA'Sprogram i s subject to
variation, depending on future donor commitments. It i s also likely to evolve in greater
specificity from discussions with the new Government.

A. Country Overview
Situated on the western third of the island of Hispaniola, Haiti i s the poorest country of the
Western Hemisphere. An estimated 76 percent of its population of approximately 8.3 million
lives below the poverty line (less than US$2 a day), and roughly 56 percent lives in abject
poverty (below US$1 dollar a day). At almost three times the regional average, Haiti's poverty
level can only be compared to that of Sub-Saharan Africa,I3 and income inequality i s amongst
the highest in the w0r1d.l~Haiti ranks 153rd (out of 177 countries) in the UN Human
Development Index. Life expectancy i s 52 years. Infant and maternal mortality rates remain
among the highest in the world. Fully half of the population i s illiterate. Malnutrition affects a
third of all children, and ineffective public health and sanitation systems together with
widespread overty provide fertile ground for the rapid spread o f such infectious diseases as
HIV/AIDS1{ tuberculosis, polio and human rabies. The widespread lack of solid waste
management and access to potable water also contribute to many water-borne illnesses,
preventable diseases and childhood deaths.
Between 1960 and 2004, Haiti's real income per-capita fell by an average of 1.3 percent per
year, resulting in a per capita real GDP reduction of 45 percent over the entire period. Positive
growth rates occurred only during the 1970s, achieved through the interaction of private sector
dynamism in exports of agricultural crops and assembly manufactures, and public sector
investment in infrastructure, basic services, and tourism. Over 90 percent of Haiti's non-
agricultural labor force works in the informal sector in some type o f micro-enterprise activity.
Nearly 70 percent of the population lives in rural communities and depends on small-scale
subsistence farming. Demographic pressures have led to the cultivation of increasingly marginal
lands and accelerated deforestation and soil erosion, resulting in declining agricultural
production and contributing to food shortages.
The small size and capacity of Haiti's government contrast starkly with the daunting
humanitarian and social development challenges it faces. Government expenditures represent
about 10 percent of GDP, compared to an average of 21 percent in other developing countries.
Although both the number (roughly 48,000 as of 2004) and salaries of public employees are low,
most revenues, excluding those from donors, go into personnel. The vast majority of public
servants works in metropolitan Port-au-Prince despite the overwhelmingly rural population
distribution. The weak performance o f key public institutions has contributed to a diminishing
quality of life for many Haitians. The government spends an estimated 2 percent of GDP on
education, compared to 5 percent or more in other low-income countries. The private sector
currently provides over 80 percent of education services, much of it unregulated and of uncertain
quality. B y 2004, a number o f key government agencies and offices had been closed or
substantially downsized. A small police force has been overwhelmed b y increasing crime levels

B. Political Context
Haiti's history has been fraught with political and economic instability. The 1986 termination of
the 30-year dictatorships of Franqois Duvalier and his son, Jean Claude, noted for their
corruption, repression of dissent, and pillage of the country's wealth, was followed by a series of
short-lived, but equally abusive administrations. The 1990 election of President Jean Bertrand
Aristide was initially hailed as a break with past patterns. The seeming return to civilian rule
was interrupted seven months later by a military coup that prompted an international embargo
and the suspension of all but humanitarian aid. These actions further crippled Haiti's economy
and produced a severe decline in the already dire living standards of its population. Activity in
the textile and export-oriented assembly industries (representing over three quarters of export
earnings as well as a considerable share of formal sector employment) virtually came to a halt;
tax collection and expenditure control systems collapsed; and maintenance of the country's
economic and social infrastructure was all but abandoned.
The return to constitutional rule, introduced via the U.S.-led reinstatement of Aristide's
presidency in 1994, allowed Aristide to finish out his term and the democratic election of his
successor. The adoption in 1995 of an Emergency and Economic Reconstruction Program
(EERP) appeared to place Haiti on a path of economic recovery. However, by 1997 internal
power struggles, weak institutions, and poor governance again took their toll on the economy.'6
Aristide's 1997 break from the Organisation Populaire Lavalas (OPL) that had backed him
since 1990 and his establishment of the Lavalas Family (FL) unleashed a severe institutional
crisis and a deadlocked parliament - ultimately suspended in 1999.                         In the absence of a
legislative body, no major legislation (including budget laws) could be approved, ongoing reform
programs were halted, and ratification of new loans from international creditors was rendered
impossible.
Aristide's landslide victory in the 2000 elections represented Haiti's first constitutional transition
of government between a full term president and his elected successor. However, the contested
results spurred civil unrest that continued to escalate over the next three years. This provoked an
armed rebellion that seized the northern half of the country. Aristide's resignation on February
29, 2004 was followed by the establishment o f a Transitional G~vernment'~empowered to create
the conditions necessary to hold democratic elections in late 2005 and hand over power to a new
government in early 2006. (In effect, because of delays in the registration program, the first
round of elections was held in February, 2006 and the congressional run-offs in April of that
year.)

C. The Interim CooperationFramework
Faced with the formidable challenge of deep-seated political instability, weak institutions,
inefficient use o f already scarce resources, and the resulting mistrust of its citizenry, in April
2004 the Transition Government joined forces with the donor community to prepare the Interim

D. EconomicGovernance-Overview of Progressto Dateand PrevailingChallengesin
CriticalGovernanceAreas
Although it has had to contend with natural disasters, rising energy prices, and escalating
criminal and politically motivated violence, the Transitional Government has adopted a number
of economic governance reforms throughout the last two years critical to increasing the overall
transparency and efficiency of the use of public resources. What follows i s a summary of the
challenges and corrective actions as detailed in the ICF and of the progress attained in each of
the 5 areas.
                                 1. BUDGETARYPROCESS
a. Backgroundand ICF Findings. Upon preparation of the ICF, the Transitional Government
was confronted with a chronically inefficient budget structure and processes. Although Haiti's
1985 Budgetary Law (replaced in 2004) gave responsibility for overall budget formulation,
execution, and control to the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), and provided for shared
programming and oversight of public investment with the Ministry of Planning and External
Cooperation (MPCE), the MEF's basic procedures as defined in the law were never fully
implemented, while critical positions and offices were simply not created. Offices that did exist
were often understaffed and under-equipped. Parameters for budget analysis and data for its
preparation were weak, and the budget did not incorporate all expenditures or revenues. Its
contents were not linked to programs or high level government goals (e.g. poverty reduction),
but solely presented inline itemform, and there were no means for tracking outputs or results of
budgetary outlays. The Treasury, which should manage liquidity and program disbursements in
line with available funds, had become little more than a mechanism for endorsing checks to
employees and suppliers and processing accounts on an accrual basis. Deficit control was
typically accomplished by tapping Central Bank reserves or accumulating internal and external
arrears. The overall cash management system was weak and did not allow for proper control and
synchronization of disbursements and receipts.
Likewise, public investment was not identified and planned within a coherent framework. The
planning offices of the sector ministries (Unitks de Programmation) played little to no role in
investment programming, as many of them had disappeared. The MPCE had also lost staff and
thus the capacity to carry out its functions. As a result, by the mid 1990s, public investment had
come to be driven by donor-funded and NGO projects that made use of special accounts
deposited in the Central Bank or bypassed the Government altogether, and did not provide for
the automatic transfer of account activity to the MEF. While the MEF eventually received
information on these accounts and their disbursements, aid supplied in kindwas not registered at
all.
Between 1997 and 2001, Haiti operated without a budget. In 2002-03, approximately 60 percent
of government expenditures were spent through the ministries' comptes courants - in effect,
discretionary accounts - or through a catch-all category called "autres interventions publiques."
Both effectively evaded programming and tracking mechanisms, thereby considerably reducing
the transparency and control of public expenditures.
b. ICF Recommendations. On the basis of the above analysis, the ICF found that the overall
efficiency of the budget process was undermined by a combination of: (i) capacity of the
                                                                              weak
planning, monitoring and assessment structures throughout the Ministries o f Finance, Planning
and all other sector ministries and agencies; (ii)
                                                 delays inbudget ratification; (iii) deficiencies in


                                                26

both the parameters for budget formulation and data underpinning budget proposals; and (iii)  the
pervasive use of comptes courants to circumvent a lengthy and cumbersome expenditure
execution process. The latter were found to be particularly conducive to mismanagement and
corruption given their discretionary nature and the fact they were not subject to ex ante or ex-
post budgetary scrutiny.
The ICF proposed a number of corrective measures to address these structural problems, calling
for: (i) modernization of the State's automated financial management system (SYSDEP); (ii)
        the
the creation    o f a unified accounting system; (iii)the establishment of a board of public
accountants, (iv) strengthening of budget preparation; (v) reinforcing the Ministry of Planning's
ability to plan and coordinate public investment and its project planning capacities; and (v)
strengthening the role of the Treasury inmanaging all expenditures.
c. Progress to Date. Over the past two years, important headway was made toward establishing
the foundations for a more efficient budgetary structure. Significantly, a new Organic Law on
Budget Preparation and Execution was enacted in 2003 and published in August 2004. It (i)
establishes the outlines of a new budgetary process; (ii) out the mandates and responsibilities
                                                        sets
of each of the agencies involved in the public financial management process (many of which
until now had either served purely perfunctory roles or had been circumvented entirely); (iii)
calls for the creation of a new accounting system and the establishment of a board of public
accountants, and (iv) creates the position of contrSZeurfinancier (internal ex-ante control) and a
new internal auditing office (Direction General de Inspection Financidre, DGIF) staffed by a
corps of inspecteurs financiers       Unfortunately, due to financial restrictions progress in
implementing the new positions and functions has been restricted to retitling existing employees,
and the DGIFhas yet to materialize.
There has been progress as regards budget preparation. While in earlier years the State was left
to function either without budgets or with significant delays in their approval, both the FY'04-
 `05 and FY'05`06 budgets were approved in a timely manner and in accord with the steps
dictated by the 2004 law. The absence of a Parliament meant they were approved by decree.
Prepared in consultation with donors and civil society alike, these budgets were elaborated in
accordance with the Organic Budget Law and the ICF and disseminated widely, as was
information on the associated budget execution for these years. Moreover, there has been an
attempt to move toward results budgets (also mandated b y the 2004 law) in a few ministries.
The products are still rudimentary, but a shift to a results framework i s a longer-term iterative
process.
Haiti's self-developed automated financial management system, SYSDEP, was recently installed
in seven ministries. While it so far only tracks expenditures, and then incompletely, its
 development has greatly improved the timely input o f financial information. The MEF i s aware
 of the limitations of the present system and has sought donor assistance to expand its coverage
 and add modules to include budget preparation, investment, contracting/purchasing, accounting
 (a General Ledger), human resources (especially salary management), and reporting. It has also
been willing to entertain suggestions that SYSDEP be replaced by one of the several integrated
 financial management systems available commercially.
 Ongoing USAID and IDB grants are supporting the roll out and upgrading of SYSDEP. IDA'S
 LICUS grant supported a study tour to review other integrated financial management systems as
 well as a consultancy to help the MEF develop a plan to implement the Organic Budget Law.

                                 2. FINANCIAL CONTROL
a. Backgroundand ICF Findings.The new Organic Budget Law delineates and differentiates
the responsibilities of the agencies charged with the control and oversight of public finances [Le.,
CSCCA; MEF's Directorate of Budgetary Control, (DCB); the Ministry of Planning's Evaluation
and Control Directorate (DEC), and the Direction General de Inspection Financiere]. During
preparation of the ICF, it was found that oversight and control of Haiti's public finances were
seriously undermined by a number of structural deficiencies. Those identified included the lack
of a well structured accounting system, coupled with the fragmentation of the accounting
process, duplications in functions among the various control bodies, and the pervasive use of
comptes courants.      It was also noted that all existing control agencies lacked sufficient,
adequately trained personnel to carry out their functions and also had important shortfalls in
financial and material resources.
As regards the audit function, while the Cour Supe`rieure des Comptes et du Contentieux
Administratif (CSCCA) was responsible for oversight of the public finance audit functions, it had
focused nearly exclusively on ex-ante control, thereby duplicating the actions of the Ministry of
Economy and Finance's Directorate of Budgetary Control (DCB) and rendering it unable to
undertake its primary duty as the supreme audit institution. The CSCCA's overall ability to carry
out its mandated functions was seriously hampered b y vacancies in the court membership itself,
as well as insufficient staff, systems, equipment, and training funds. As a result, external audits
of the government budget had not been done for several years. The CSCCA's role in ex-ante and
ex-post control, and its simultaneous functioning as an administrative court also presented
potential conflicts of interest.
With respect to expenditure control, the Directorate of Budgetary Control (DCB) is responsible
 for enforcing compliance with investment and operational functions. To this end, the DCB's
 duty i s to systematically track the execution of budget expenditures, generate periodic reports
 that identify gaps between expenditures programmed and executed; and formulate strategies for
 corrective actions. According to the ICF's finding, the overall efficiency of this agency's
 operations and therefore its ability to carry out its mandate as described in the new budget law,
 were seriously hampered by its (i)      inadequate organizational structure; (ii) designed
                                                                                    poorly
 control methodologies; and (iii)  insufficient personnel and office infrastructure. The new budget
 law also mandates the creation of a corps of inspecteurs financiers to perform the ex-post
 internal control funcion. At the time o f the ICF they had still not been created, nor had their
 relationship to the CSCCA or the DCB been articulated. There was thus a potential problem of
 redundant controls as well as a lack of clarity as to lines of responsibility. Both agencies were
 also impeded by their inability to inspect comptes courants which in turn undermined the
 Government's capacity to evaluate the economic, financial and/or social impact of such
 expenditures.




                                                  28

As regards fiscal oversight, the Direction de 1'Inspection Fiscule (DIF) exerts permanent control
over the collection and recovery of duties, taxes, fees and other charges and revenues on behalf
of the State. A key impediment to its efficient functioning stems from poor record keeping by
the tax and customs agencies and the failure of other agencies receiving income from fees and
user charges to transmit information on their collections. Furthermore, as i s the case for the other
control agencies, the DIF's overall efficiency i s compromised by inadequate and/or insufficient
staffing and training; ineffective control methodologies, as well as outdated systems technologies
and infrastructure.
b. ICF Recommendations. Based on these findings; the ICF proposed a number of measuresto
correct prevailing structural deficiencies. Specifically, the ICF called for: (i)
                                                                                the establishment of
the Regulatory Framework for the new Organic Budget Law, including provisions on the
responsibilities of ordonnateurs and financial comptrollers, and enabling legislation for the
creation of a board of public accountants; (ii)  the provision of support aimed at bolstering the
National Treasury, CSSA, DCB and DEC's respective abilities to efficiently carry out their
functions in accordance with the Organic Law; (iii)      strengthening of the Directorate of Fiscal
Inspection; and (iv) an evaluation of the feasibility of creating an Inspectorate General of
Finance.
c. Progress to date. Throughout the past two years, the Government has implemented a number
of corrective measures as prescribed by the ICF. For one, the CSCCA's ex-ante control functions
were fully removed and transferred to the DCB per the new Budget Law, allowing it to focus on
ex-post auditing and thereby eliminating some duplication of functions and potential conflicts of
interest. Additionally, the vacant positions on the CSCCA were filled. As a result, government
accounts are now being audited more regularly in accordance with internationally accepted
standards. Furthermore, the use of comptes courants has been drastically reduced to less than 10
percent of cumulative non-salary current public expenditures since the beginning of FY 2004-
2005.
With support of the LICUS TF, a decree laying out the internal organization andbasic operations
 of the CSCCA was prepared, enacted and published in the national gazette. The LICUS and
 EGTAGIare supporting the training of CSCCA auditors intheir new ex-post control functions,
 the evaluation of their initial performance, related equipment and training needs, as well as the
 elaboration of a medium term plan to further develop external and internal control functions.
 EGTAGIand IDB projects are working with the Government to define and create the inspecteur
financier function.
                                   3. PUBLIC PROCUREMENT
 a. Background and ICF Findings. As of 2004 actions were governed by the Public
 Procurement Decree of 1989 which called for a decentralized national procurement system.
 Sector ministries, administrative units and public enterprises were to manage their own
 procurement processes under the supervision of a national public procurement commission
 (Commission Nutionale des Murcht?sPubliques - CNMP). Ex-post control in theory lay with the
 CSSCA. However, the enabling legislation required to implement the Public Procurement
 Decree had not been enacted, the CSCCA was still focused on ex-ante control, and the CNMP
 had not functioned since the early 1990s. This latter situation was due to the institutional void
 caused by the absence of a Ministry of Civil Service, which according to the decree, was
 supposed to coordinate the CNMP's functions. As a consequence, ministries and agencies were



                                                  29

virtually free to adopt their own methods for procuring goods and services. The results were at
best, inefficiency and waste, and at worst, corruption. The situation further deteriorated as ever-
decreasing public sector salary levels prompted qualified procurement staff to seek better paying
a1ternative employment.
b. ICFRecommendations. To rectify these deficiencies the ICFproposed: (i) establishment
                                                                                 the
of an interim public procurement commission under the authority of the Prime Minister; (ii)    the
recruitment of fixed-term procurement specialists to supervise procurement and provide training:
(iii) creationofanAnti-corruptionUnitwhichamongitsotherresponsibilitiescouldprovide
     the
an independent check on alleged abuses; (iv) the passage of a professional code of ethics; (v)
widespread dissemination of information/data on procurement processes; (vi) strengthening of
the private sector's capacity to tender bids for Government contracts and procurement; and (vii)
the consolidation and improvement of the legal framework via the enlistment of a legal
consultant to assist in the preparation and implementation of a new procurement law.
c. Progress to date. In accordance with the ICF's recommendations a number of important
measures have been taken to strengthen the public procurement system. Most notably, an Interim
National Commission for Public Procurement (Commission Nationale des Marche`s Publics -
CNMP)was created and its five full-time commissioners named with input from the public and
private sectors, and a new Procurement Decree was published in February 2005, strengthening
the commission's mandate and incorporating many elements of internationally accepted
procurement practices including, most importantly the stipulation that competitive methods were
the norm and not the exception. The Commission i s now operational and as an important first
step, has produced a preliminary version of national Standard Bidding Documents to be used in
the procurement of civil works. It has also begun a series of contacts and workshops with sector
procurement officials to publicize the new decree and the Commission's role in implementing it.

As agreed to under the ICF, donors are supporting this effort by financing technical assistance,
training, and equipment. Under the LICUS TF Grant and EGTAG I,IDA has initiated an
iterative institutional strengthening process focusing largely on the CNMP while other donors
(mainly the IDB) are addressing the development of capacity within the sector ministries and of
a software system for tracking procurements. The CNMP has used EGTAG Ifunds to hire an
international procurement consulting firm which will provide it with procurement management
and oversight training, assist in the further development of standard documents and training
programs for sector staff, and help in the drafting of an improved law to replace the present
decree.
These early steps have provided significant advances in meeting the ICF recommendations,
setting up the basic organizational and legal framework to improve procurement practices.
Remaining needs to complete the basic program involve the fleshing out of the procedural details
and of the Commission's ability to enforce them, the designation and training of additional
CNMP staff and sector procurement officials, and the creation of mechanisms ensuring that the
CNMP i s fully informed of all intended procurements.          Additionally, the Commission has
expressed concerns about how the training of state enterprise procurement offices will be
handled and how soon its own operations can be decentralizedto the departments.
Important progress has also been made toward increasing the overall transparency of the
procurement function. The LICUS TF and EGRO were instrumental in supporting the recent
establishment of the Anti-Corruption Unit (Unite` de Lutte Contre la Corruption - ULCC) as an


                                                 30

autonomous entity under the Ministry of Economy and Finance, and both supported the creation
of a mechanism for civil society to monitor the Government program of economic governance
reforms. This mechanism i s now operational and i s generating reports on the government's
progress. Likewise, the CNMP launched a website that publishes government contract awards as
well as a supplier database. Further progress in enhancing transparency and civil society
monitoring i s being supported under the EGTAG Ithrough technical assistance geared to
supporting inter alia: the development of the ULCC's anti-corruption programming capacities;
the drafting of a freedom of information law; and an early performance evaluation of civil
society's new economic governance M&E mechanisms.

                       4. STRENGTHENINGHUMANRESOURCES
a. Backgroundand ICF Findings. The 1987 Constitution and the prevailing legal framework
underscore the importance of building a modern, reliable and competent public sector firmly
grounded in the principles of equal employment opportunities and merit-based recruitment and
promotion practices. Nonetheless, the ICF recognized that Government's performance was
seriously undermined by the insufficient number of qualified civil servants and the lack o f an
incentive structure capable of attracting, maintaining and motivating them. Although earlier
legislation called for the establishment of a cabinet-level office to oversee human resource
management, the latter was never created nor had the Arr2tis (a detailed set of specific
regulations to govern personnel administration) ever been drafted. Intheir absence, a majority of
appointments were routinely made on the basis of political and personal connections. Incentives
for good performance, including both adequate salaries and routine evaluations, remained scarce.
As a result, Haiti's public agencies have been particularly vulnerable to highturnover associated
with changes of administration and political crises which in turn undermines efforts to
professionalize career civil servants.
Haiti has a relatively small corps of public employees, and their territorial and sectoral
distribution does not correspond to actual needs. On the one hand, there i s an absolute shortage
of personnel outside of Port-au-Prince, but even in the capital city many offices lack the
technical staff required to do their work. On the other hand, certain agencies (most notably the
public enterprises) seemed to have a surfeit of under qualified staff. Estimates from a study done
under the ICF indicated an urgent need for nearly 4,000 additional employees, most of them in
technical positions. The level of technical expertise throughout all ranks o f the public sector i s
limited. As of the early 2000s (i)senior officials comprised 5.6 percent o f public service
employees; (ii)  technical personnel makes up between 35 and 40 percent; while (iii) percent
                                                                                         65
of the public sector was comprised of support staff. Funding shortages meant little opportunity
for training and limits on the possibilities for promotion for good performance through a formal
career system. A further disincentive to pursuingcareer civil service lay in the fact that public
sector salaries, already low to begin with, began a downwards spiral in 1995. As a result,
donors, NGOs, and the private sector have traditionally attracted the most qualified staff at the
expense of the public sector. Unsurprisingly, wide scale insufficiencies in staffing play a key role
inunderminingthe overall quality and efficiency of the public sector.

b. ICF Recommendations. In order to address these deficiencies, the ICF called for: (i)          the
hiring of critical staff at the decision-making, conceptual and execution levels for ICF
implementation in the short term; (ii)    the training of existing staff over the short and medium



                                                  31

term; and (iii) adoption of an incentives-based system (including salary increases) to retain
                  the
qualified civil servants.

c. Progress to date. Important, albeit initial steps have been taken toward setting in place the
foundations for a modern, merit-based, career civil service. Significantly, both a new civil
service law and a law for the organization of the central administration were drafted and enacted
by decree in July 2005. Furthermore a Coordination Unit was set up in the Prime Minister's
Office with a mandate to oversee human resource functions, as a first step to promoting the
implementation of a new civil service law. With support from EGTAG I, unit has drawn up
                                                                             this
an action plan for meeting short and medium term human resource needs. It has also initiated
contacts with existing training institutes and with donors interested in providing training to
develop a coordinated program aimed at meeting the most urgent needs and ensuringthat this i s
done in the most efficient fashion. Finally, civil service salaries were increased by 30 percent in
2004, in consultation with the LMF, while an additional 15 percent increase i s under
consideration.
                            5. PUBLICENTERPRISEMANAGEMENT
a. Backgroundand ICF Findings.Historically treated as sources of fundingor employment for
political followers, Haiti's public enterprises have fallen prey to a legacy of mismanagement that
ultimately underminedtheir service delivery capabilities and profitability. Their management has
traditionally been opaque and far removed from the norms of good economic governance, and
they were found in urgent need of restructuring, financial and management auditing, and
modernization to end the pattern of operating deficits. The ICF singled out the situations of
EDH(electricity) and TELECO (telephone) as particularly urgent because of "their poor record
of customer account cost recovery, unethical practices (illicit connections and use, theft and
fraud) and inefficient human resource management." It further indicated that a "combination of
structural deficiencies, financial insolvency and the lack of asset maintenance has exacerbated
the problems of EDH," paralyzing the production and distribution of electricity and raising
resistance from users with no interest in paying for a substandard or nonexistent service.
However, even the relatively "better" performers were found to have serious problems in all
areas, generally providing poor quality services in insufficient quantities at excessive operating
costs.
b. ICF Recommendations. Within the context of the ICF, five state-owned enterprises (SOEs)
were chosen to spearhead a strategy geared to improving their contributions to national economic
recovery and growth. The five selected were the national electricity (EDH); telephone
 (TELECO); airport authority (AAN); ports (APN); and potable water supply (CAMEP)
companies. In general, the ICF calls for a package of interventions in auditing and management
consultation in each, aiming at (i)        increasing the overall transparency of public enterprise
 management and (ii)preparing the five for increased private sector participation in their
 management structures and recapitalization to ensure their technical and financial viability. To
 this end, the ICF proposed inter alia that (i)    external audits be undertaken of AAN's, APN's,
 CAMEP's, TELECO's and EDH's accounts, financial statements and financial position; (ii)
 evaluations of these agencies' management capacities be conducted and/or options, costs and
 schedules for private sector participation in their management and/or recapitalization be
 provided; (iii)   international certification of the Port au Prince Port be obtained; and (iv) the
 institutional, financial, technical and operational viability of the Jacmel Port be undertaken on a
 pilot basis.

c. Progress to date. Significant headway has been made in implementing the ICF
recommendations. To date, international audit firms have been hired on a competitive basis to
do financial audits of APN, TELECO, and EDH. The selection of an international firm to
undertake financial and administrative audits and a technical review of CAMEP i s close to
completion. Progress made within the telecom sector has proven particularly noteworthy.
Following a comprehensive accounting rehabilitation and major management reorganization of
TELECO, the Government recently concluded a sector-wide review and i s in the process of
finalizing a comprehensive action plan aimed at improving sector performance. A newly licensed
mobile private operator has begun operations and has negotiated an IFC investment which was
recently presented to the Board. Finally, as regards the electricity sector, the Government, EDH,
and donor community signed a Memorandum of Understanding in October 2005. The M O U
commits all parties to a series of measures to strengthen the governance and increase the
efficiency and transparency of investments in the electricity sector. This represents an important
step towards furthering progress in this important sector. The IDA-financed US$6.0 Electricity
Loss Reduction Project, which i s expected to be presented to the Board in parallel with the
EGTAG11, will provide pivotal support to improve the quality of electricity services inPort-au-
Prince and to strengthen the financial and operational performance of EDH.

    6. SUMMARY OF ICF-BASEDPROGRESSTO DATE AND NEXT STEPS
Over the two years it has served as the basis for donor cooperation, the ICF has seen important
progress made on its recommendations. President-elect, RenC PrCval, has indicated his wish to
continue with its implementation and to extend it b y one additional year, in the economic
governance and other areas. Ineconomic governance, the steps already taken have significantly
improved the organizational and legal framework. The remaining challenge i s ensuring that
institutional capabilities are fully up to the task of implementing it. More dedicated Government
and donor involvement in the area have not altered the fundamental ICF priorities or initial
diagnosis; they have helped the parties better understand the remaining obstacles, the
opportunities and alternatives for overcoming them, and the levels of investment and time that
will be required to do so. Progress made to date and the new government's endorsement of the
ICF and specifically of its economic governance pillar lie behind IDA'Sdecision to continue
with its plan of incremental assistance in the area, and its close coordination with programs of
other donors.    Within this scheme, over the medium run, IDA assistance will focus on
strengthening central agencies' capacity to carry out their new mandates, gradually opening the
way to incorporate additional ICF recommendations (e.g. IDA'Sproposed new focus on human
resource management) and as progress i s made, and GoH priorities themselves expand, possibly
adding new elements.

     Arrangementsfor resultsmonitoring

     Institutional issues: As this i s a project with multiple secondary counterparts (aside from the
     prime counterpart, the Ministry of Economy and Finance), most monitoring will be carried out
     by the agency directly responsible for a specific set of results. Thus the CSCCA will keep its
     own records on the number of audits carried out, while the ULCC will likewise keep records of
     its own investigations, complaints registered by individuals and the like. The only exception,
     information on public reactions, will either come from separate surveys or from more informal
     consultations.     The indicators have been kept simple and tied to records agencies should
     normally keep. Moreover, a majority of the indicators were also used in EGTAG I,a logical
     result of the linkage between the projects. The PCU's role will be in consolidating the
     information from the various agencies. Although the long term impacts of the endeavor are
      aimed at the Haitian public as beneficiary, the timeframe for the project will mean that within-
     institution changes are the most that can be measured realistically. The working hypothesis i s
     that if these within-institutional changes can be effected, the improved use of public resources
      will bring more benefits for all Haitians. However, within the life-of-project, it would be very
      difficult to measure that effect.

     Data collection: Data will be both qualitative and quantitative. Most will be derived directly
      from the office outputs (budgets, number of audits, trainees, investigations, rules and document
      designed) with some additional external evaluation of quality. While there i s always room for
      manipulating results, this type of primary data (with the exception of the qualitative assessments)
      i s less susceptible to this effect.

      Capacity: As all these offices tend to be short-staffed, collecting the data may be a challenge but
      the approach proposed i s rather basic. Efforts put into collecting and presenting data normally
      kept by the entities will be counted as counterpart inkind.

1. ProgramObjective andPhases
The proposed operation i s part of an assistance package described in IDA'STransitional Support
Strategy (TSS) for Haiti. As one of its four areas of concentration, the TSS defined a series of
operations to support Government efforts to strengthen economic governance and advance
institutional development. The first stage of this assistance included a $61 million Economic
Governance Reform Operation (EGRO) incorporating a number of economic-governance
triggers, and two technical assistance grants intended to help the GoH meet these objectives: the
LICUS TF Grant, approved in December, 2004 and well on its way to completion, and EGTAG
(I) begandisbursinginFebruary2006andisscheduledtocloseonDecember31,2007.
    which
The TSS also anticipated a scaled-up program upon confirmation of satisfactory progress by
mid-FY 06. As this progress has been made (as indicated by the full completion of the EGRO
benchmarks, advances in other ICF pillars2' and progress toward preparing a participatory
PRSP), the follow-on Grant proposed here i s intended to provide further assistance to
Government economic governance reforms.
The Government's program and IDA assistance are based on the economic governance chapter
of the Interim Cooperative Framework (ICF), described in Annex 1 and briefly summarized
below.     The current proposal also draws on subsequent discussions with the Government,
information provided b y other donors on their support in the area, and IDA'Sexperience with its
own assistance package. An effective, sustainable change program will require several years of
concerted, continuing effort. As all parties recognize, the ICF's objectives cannot be
accomplished quickly. For several of them, experience in other countries suggests that a five-
year time frame i s reasonable, assuming no setbacks. Thus, both sustained government
commitment and a substantial injection of donor resources will continue to be needed. The
proposed EGTAG I1 builds on institutional strengthening activities begun during the earlier
operations and would be coordinated with a second Economic Governance Reform Operation
already in preparation.
IDA'Smedium term assistance program in the area of economic governance i s also intended to
operate in close coordination with support provided by other donors. Movement during the next
stages of the Bank-supported program will, of course, be contingent on continuing Government
commitment to the reforms, the results of the first stages, and advances made with other donor
operations.
The progress achieved under EGTAGIand the proposed content of EGTAG I1are discussed in
detail below. The areas included in the two operations (and in the LICUS) are similar because
both are incrementally staged, short-term programs directed at advancing ICF objectives that will
necessarily require more than a year or two to achieve. Nonetheless, EGTAG I1incorporates a
 slight shift in emphasis to allow greater attention to ICF elements (e.g., human resources) which
could not be covered under EGTAG I.Otherwise, this follow-on TA operation continues

The following paragraphs summarize the overarching economic governance goals set out in the
ICF and providing the guidelines for all IDA and other donor support inthis area:
Financial Management. The ICF goal i s to develop a better capacity to produce and execute a
budget, track its implementation, and reduce irregular uses and project planning; and strengthen
the role of the Treasury in managing all expenditures. These actions are centered in the MEF
and in the MPCE, but also include strengthening of other relevant ministries and agencies.
EGTAG Ifocuses on helping the MEF and the MPCE to develop their capacities while other
donors work with the sector ministries and on the expansion (or replacement) of the financial
management information system (SYSDEP). IDA and the companion IDB assistance are
emphasizing the design of the basic budgetary procedures (established in the new Organic Law),
and ensuring that the MEF, the MPCE and key sector ministries have adequately prepared
personnel to implement them. B y the end o f EGTAG I,the goal i s that Haiti will have a
budgetary system that captures all proposed and effected operating and investment expenditures.

        management information system (SYSDEP); establish basic accounting processes
        compatible with international standards; set up a board of public accountants, strengthen
        budget preparation, strengthen the Ministry of Planning's ability in investment and
        project planning, and strengthen the Treasury's management of expenditures.

    0   Control - The ICF goal i s to ensure that the regulatory framework meets the provisions
        of the Organic Budget Law and in so-doing substantially improves the control
        environment. The principal focus i s on bringing the CSCCA's further implementing
        regulations, organization, internal processes, quality and quantity of staffing, and
        financial and material resources into line with its redefined responsibilities. Additional
         attention goes to upgrading the organization, legal framework, and human and other
        resources of other control agencies (DCB, DEC, DIF, DGIF) so that they can carry out
        their functions, individually and inbetter coordination with each other.

    0   Procurement - The ICF goal i s to establish a central control mechanism in the CNMP
         and ensure its ability to carry out its work, strengthen sector procurement offices,
         strengthen private sector capacity to tender bids, and consolidate the judicial and
        regulatory framework. The requirement for a new procurement law also foreseen in the
         ICF has been partially met with the enactment of the Procurement Decree in early 2005.
         However, additional legislation and possibly a new procurement law may also be
        required to complete the process.

Public Sector Service Delivery Monitoring and Encouraging Employee Performance
Although not included as a separate item in the ICF's economic governance section, this i s an

programs and results. This will also be a new area for Bank support although IDA and other
donor programs already work with service ministries. However, there is continuing concern as to

a. Project Development Objective: EGTAG11's specific objectives are to: support any additional
activities requiredto achieve the benchmarks set out under EGTAGIand to move beyond them
to (i)  increase the fluid and effective operation of the budgetary, control and procurement
systems in accordance with the new legal framework; (ii)         build a central human resources
management unit, provide it with the basic tools to oversee the human resources system, and
advance other elements of the plan to rationalize the distribution, upgrade the quality, and
improve the incentives for public sector employees; (iii)     create within the MEF's Office of

delivery; and (iv) augment the effectiveness and sustainability of the Government's anti-
corruption, transparency and civil society monitors' programs.
b. Indicators: IDA'Spackage of economic governance support i s a staged, incremental effort at
institutional building. Progress in each of the package's components will thus be measured
through the achievement of benchmarks in implementing the Government's longer-term
objective of improving system outputs by introducing and institutionalizing new processes and
structures. Altogether, the implementation of the EGTAG I1(in collaboration with activities of
other donors) will provide an improved institutional and operational framework for the
preparation and execution of the current and investment budget, tracking and control of
expenditures and outputs; the implementation of the new procurement decree, especially as
regards the effective oversight role of a fully functional CNMP and the adoption of more fully
competitive procurement processes; development and initial implementation of a human
resources management system and plan for upgrading staff quality and incentives; and the
broader dissemination of information to allow the more effective monitoring of government
operations by the anti-corruption unit, civil society and the Haitian citizenry in general.
Emphasis will be put on bringing existing structures and practices into conformity with the
improved legal framework as preparation for subsequent steps leading to further institutional
strengthening and public sector modernization. However, because of the short life of the project,
and the emphasis on institutional change, results indicators will focus on the latter, not on
downstream impacts. They will measure the extent of adoption o f new practices and structures
inthe following areas, as a necessary prelude to producingimprovements inoutput:

         Implementation of key processes, staff, and offices defined by the new budget law and
        procurement decree;
         Advances in the creation of a system to manage human resources, compliance with
         normative framework, and development of improved regulations;
         Advances in the creation of basic instruments for tracking service delivery in one or two
         key ministries as part of the MEF's development of a system for the strategic monitoring
         of sector policy;
         Advances in the development and activities of the anti-corruption office and civil society
         monitoring program; and
         Advances in donor coordination, communication and outreach in the area of economic
         governance.

Overallconclusionof the financialmanagementassessment
Because EGTAG I1 i s a follow-up operation to EGTAG Iand the LICUS TF Economic
Governance Grant, the project financial management arrangements will follow those of the
earlier operations. The same PCU overseeing EGTAG Iimplementation, which already has a
system and operations manual in place, will be used. For the LICUS TF, one MEF accountant
was given specific tasks to keep a separate set of book of accounts for the grant while the usual
approval and control mechanisms of the MEF were followed. Financial operations of the LICUS
grant are therefore recorded and reported separately. EGTAG I1will follow EGTAG 1's practice
of locating responsibility within the PCU and using an accountant hired by the PCU for this
purpose. Purchase of computer equipment for the PCU with EGTAGIand IDB funds will allow
the shift from the manual accounting system used under LICUS to the automated format needed
because of the larger financing and the more complex reporting requirement.
Although there are significant country risks because of major weaknesses in the public financial
management system and perceived level of corruption, the impact of these risks on the project i s
likely to be limited given the defined implementation arrangements, which do not rely on
government's system. Instead they build on a dedicated implementation unit with adequate
financial management system, procedures and staffing. The project itself does not present any
significant risk and its overall risk i s rated as moderate.

                          Annex 9: Economic and Financial Analysis
             HAITI: Second Economic Governance Technical AssistanceGrant

The proposed operation will focus on capacity building activities, and it i s therefore difficult to
quantify outputs and benefits, and to precisely calculate rates of return. Nevertheless, since
positive fiscal impacts are expected, project implementation i s projected to generate economic
gains. The most noteworthy economic and fiscal savings are expected to result from: (i)         the
implementation of a budgetaryprocess in accord with the new Organic Law and its incorporation
in an expanded automated expenditure tracking system (SYSDEP), the latter supported largely
by assistance provided by and coordinated with the IDB and USAID: (ii)the further
strengthening of the commission (CNMP) charged with overseeing procurement and ensuring
greater transparency and competitiveness in the process; (iii)developing the ex-post audit
function of the Cour Supe`rieure des Comptes et du Contentieux Administratif (CSCCA) and
coordinating this with a strengthened internal audit and control capacity; (iv) enhancing the
capabilities of the human resource unit to oversee new appointments, ensuring they follow legal
procedures and incorporate an emphasis on merit, and the development and coordination of
programs for training existing and new staff in necessary skills; (v) development of an
experimental program in a few agencies to track service delivery and its coordination with the
MEF's creation of a capacity for strategic monitoring of policy performance thereby ensuring
value for monies spent; (vi) strengthening the capacity of the new anti-corruption office (ULCC)
and publicizing its presence and activities; (vii) further developing the system of civil society
monitors and the MEF's communication program to inform all citizens of the content and
progress of the package of economic governance reforms; and             (viii) further developing
mechanisms to ensure greater coordination of all donor activities in economic governance
operations.
All of these measures aim at improving the government's ability to program and track the use of
public resources, including external assistance and human resources, strengthening the internal
and external controls used to promote transparency, fight corruption, and reduce waste, and
increasing all citizens' understanding of and support for the changes sought. Better management
of the government's resources should lead to improved performance and increased efficiency b y
strengthening the linkages between inputs and outputs and ensuring that funds are no longer
assigned to un-programmed catch-all categories (autres interventions publiques). The fiscal
impact of improved resource allocation, better planning and execution may be significant, as
efficiency gains translate into reduced investment and recurrent costs for a given level of output.
These in turn will contribute to a better investment climate, which in Haiti i s essential for long-
term economic growth, the reduction of unemployment and the improvement of critical social
conditions.
