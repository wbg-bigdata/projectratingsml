1. Project Context, Development Objectives and Design

1.1 Context at Appraisal

Continuous public service reform since 1987

1.      Public service reform in Tanzania goes back to 1987 when the government launched a
Civil Service Reform Program (CSRP). Implementation picked up in 1993 with the launch of the
Public and Parastatal Reform Project (PPRP) 1993-1999 supported by the Bank. The focus of this
project was to support the program of structural adjustment and enhance macro-economic stability
by restructuring of the public service and cost containment measures. There was strong support for
the CSRP from Development Partners (DP). With CSRP coming to an end in 1999 the government
followed this up with the launch of its Public Service Reform Program (PSRP) in 2000.

2.       The PSRP was designed to build on the CSRP 1 and sought to improve accountability,
transparency and resource management for service delivery. It was a long-term program to be
implemented in three phases: (i) installation of performance improvement systems (2000-2004);
(ii) performance management culture (2005-2008); and (iii) quality improvement cycles (2009-
2011). To finance the PSRP of the government, the Bank approved its Public Sector Reform
Project (henceforth PSR Project - to distinguish it from the program of the government with the
same acronym), Credit 3300-TA for US$41.2 million, in December 1999. This Project was
designed as a three phase Adaptable Program Loan (APL) corresponding to the three phases of the
PSRP. The PSRP also attracted support from CIDA (Canada), DANIDA (Denmark), DfID (UK)
and Ireland. Whilst the number of supporting DPs had reduced significantly, as compared to the
CSRP, the PSRP was still a priority for a significant number of DP.

3.       The PSR Project of the Bank was implemented more slowly than anticipated and ended in
2007 instead of 2004. Whilst the original PDO was to ensure that “the public service will deliver
efficiently and effectively…..economic and social programs on a continuous and sustainable basis?
these were revised in 2003 to focus on intermediate outcomes related more to capacity investments
and less on direct improvements in service delivery? 2 It achieved progress in several areas
including in developing public service management systems. However, even after taking eight
years instead of five, only a bare majority of the original targets was fully achieved, and another
quarter partially achieved. Since the PSR Project was an APL, triggers were prescribed for moving
from Phase I (PSR Project) to Phase II. Out of five triggers three were achieved while two were
only partially achieved. Nevertheless the project was assessed as Satisfactory when it closed in
December 2007. However, it was subsequently assessed as being MS in the IEG review and MU in
the IEG draft PPAR (June 13, 2013)

Rationale for the Performance Results and Accountability Project (PRAP)

4.      In 2005 the government launched a National Strategy for Growth and Reduction of Poverty
(in Swahili MKUKUTA). Its third pillar – good governance and accountability – was regarded by
the government as the bedrock of the other two pillars. It encompassed no fewer than six cross-
cutting public sector reform programs including the PSRP3. In response to the MKUKUTA and to
align with the government’s strategy, the Bank and DP supported the government in preparing the
Joint Assistance Strategy for Tanzania (JAST), endorsed by the Board in April 2007. MKUKUTA
II (2010/11-2014/15) maintains the same third pillar related to good governance and accountability.

5.      The PRAP responded directly to the MKUKUTA strategy of the government; the joint
commitments of the DPs (including the Bank) and the government under the JAST; and the
continuation of commitments under the APL structure of the PSR Project. It was assessed that the
government had demonstrated particularly strong political commitment to the reforms from the
highest level on down during the first phase of its program. Under the JAST, it was agreed by the
government and the DPs that it was critical for the government to further develop its capacity to
formulate and implement evidence based policies and programs and improve its capacity to deliver
high-quality, timely and effective public services. Continuing to implement the on-going public
sector reforms programs3 and mainstreaming them, where applicable, into the normal structures
and systems of Ministries, Departments and Agencies (MDA), Regions and Local Government
Authorities (LGA) was the agreed strategy. In the context of the PSRP, the government would
enhance the incentive structure to recruit and retain qualified personnel and further strengthen and
extend the Performance Management System, which had been introduced under the PSRP Phase I.
The Bank support provided through PRAP was aligned to the JAST strategy and the
recommendations of the 2005 World Bank Task Force on Capacity Development in Africa and the
objectives of the Africa Action Plan to implement the Strategic Framework for IDA in Africa.
Despite all five triggers for moving from Phase 1 to Phase II of the APL not being achieved, it was
decided that, in the spirit of long term commitment, the unmet triggers could be a work in progress,
which could continue under Phase II (PRAP).

6.      Higher-level objectives to which Bank support would contribute were good governance
and the accountability of leaders and public servants to the people of Tanzania. An effective public
service would provide a foundation for service delivery improvements and poverty reduction.
There would be improved coordination of the several reform programs and sector programs, as
well as enhanced MDAcapacity to implement their individual programs.

1.3 Revised PDO and Key Indicators, and reasons/justification

Background of restructuring

13.       The Mid Term Review (MTR) June 2010 (conducted jointly by the government and DP)
assessed achievement, against both DO and Implementation, as Moderately Unsatisfactory. This
was the first time that the degradation of the project was formally recognized. The MTR also
recommended immediate restructuring of PRAP to enhance its link to service delivery; narrow its
focus to key activities; enhance program monitoring and improve results. While agreeing that
something needed to be done, there was lack of consensus on the nature and scope of the
restructuring within the DPs and between government and the DPs. The Bank favored retaining the
link with service delivery and bringing the Local Government Authorities and their service
facilities into the program to strengthen this link. It was felt that only performance enhancement at
that decentralized level could impact service delivery outcomes. The government was unsure how
this would dovetail with the ongoing Local Government Reform Program. There were also
concerns about the manageability of the program if it was expanded to the LGAs. Some DPs shared
these concerns and felt that the existing program could improve given more time. Meanwhile, the
beginning of the holiday season (July, August) and the general elections held in October 2010
slowed down a resolution of these issues. By December 2010, a consensus had emerged (helped by
a Program Coordinator having finally joined in October 2010 after a lapse of one year) around the
Bank’s proposal. However, in January 2011 Regional Management decided , to close all “problem?
projects (of which PRAP was one). The focus now became to restructure and reduce activities to
what could be completed in the residual time available and to release the extra resources for other
projects. It was also decided to merge future support for PFMRP with PRAP by adding a new PFM
component. Earlier, PFMRP was financed under ATIP another “problem project? which closed on
December 1, 2011. This meant negotiating anew with the DPs and the government on the nature of

Meanwhile the government, based on the MTR recommendations, had decided to abandon the
“demand led component? of the PSRP with effect from July 2011 and to extend the program till
June 2014. DfID and CIDA followed suit by extending their support to August 15, 2014. However,
restructuring stalled because the new PFMRP program had not yet been finalized, which was a
precondition for including the PFM component in PRAP. In November 2011 with PFMRP still not
finalized, the Bank decided to go ahead with the restructuring without the proposed PFM
component. Negotiations were reopened for the second time with government. An amended
restructuring proposal was received from government on February 15, 2012, based on which a
Level I restructuring proposal was approved by the Board on April 9, 2012, on the basis of the
Restructuring Paper on a Proposed Restructuring for a Performance Results and Accountability
Project to the United Republic of Tanzania (Report No. 67177-TZ dated March 20, 2012). This
aligned the project to the restructured objectives and activities of the underlying PSRP of the
government.

14.      The Project Development Objective was revised to align it with the scope of activities in
the revised PSRP-II. The revised PDO was:

“To support the Recipient’s efforts to enhance performance management in the selected MDAs and

The revised PDO clarified the scope of the project sharply. Whilst the original PDO had
emphasized the link to improved service delivery as a result of improved performance management
across 67 MDAs, the revised PDO reduced the level of ambition and did not assume a direct link

between performance management systems and improved service delivery. This was based on the
experience of the APL in Phase I and the experience under Phase II till the time of restructuring.

was that the governments’ program (PSRP) was implemented at the level of the central government,

revised project also incorporated higher levels of realism by targeting only 14 MDAs as against 67
MDAs in the original project. The selected 14 MDAs were the major ones relating most closely to
service delivery 5 thereby continuing the overall objective of improving basic service delivery,
albeit indirectly, through the assumed impact of good performance management practices,
developed under the project.

2. Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes

2.1 Project Preparation, Design and Quality at Entry

Project Preparation

Background analysis

23.      In preparation for the second project of the APL (PRAP), a review of PSRPI (the first
project of the APL) implementation was commissioned jointly by President's Office - Public
Service Management PO-PSM) and DPs. It identified areas that would need further attention (i)
completing the installation and rolling-out of an integrated/computer-based Human Capital
Management Information System (HCMIS), including strengthening establishment controls and
payroll management; (ii) enforcing compliance of rules by public officials and accountability of the
public service managers, including by linking performance to pay and other incentives; (iii)
deepening the utilization of Performance Management System; especially ensuring quality of
policies, plans and performance reporting; (iv) extending the program for the creation of Executive
Agencies, (v) broadening private sector participation, including ensuring the cost efficiency and
effectiveness of out-sourcing non-core roles, functions and services by ministries, departments and
agencies (MDAs); (vi) improving human resources planning and development as reflected by



                                                 8
capacity and performance gaps in most MDAs; and (vii) coordinating better cross-cutting and
sector reforms to take advantage of the synergies among them.

Rationale for Bank Intervention

24.     The Bank and other DPs had supported the government’s public service reform program
since 1993. The Parastatal and Public Service Reform Project of the Bank (1993 to 2000) had
concluded satisfactorily. The follow-on Bank project (Public Service Reform Project) 1999 to 2007
had also ended satisfactorily. The Bank and other DPs had already committed under the JAST,
approved by the Board in April 2007, to continue support to the ongoing long term Public Service
Reform Program of the government. In addition the government’s continued commitment to the
program’s goals and to ongoing cross-cutting reforms had been confirmed by President Jakaya
Mrisho Kikwete when he took office in 2005 (he was reelected for a second five year term in
2010). There was a constituency of reformers within the executive and in the general public, which
had benefited from the liberalizing reforms since 1990s. Against a GDP growth of 3% per annum
in 1990-2000 growth had more than doubled to 7.1% in the period 2000-2009. Against a modest
export growth of 6.4% per annum by value of exports in the period 1990-2000 exports grew at 17%
per annum in value during 2000-09. Imports similarly increased sharply from a growth of just 1%
per annum in the earlier period (possibly constrained by import controls pre liberalization) to
20.5% per annum in the latter period. The construction industry was growing as was retail business
and trade. The creation of specialized Executive Agencies had improved central government
services and enhanced budget transparency. Privatization of State Owned Enterprises heralded a
new era of private sector led growth. There had also been significant rationalization of the public
sector workforce. The reform tempo seemed sustainable.

Participatory Processes

25.      The project’s preparation was widely inclusive, much more than its predecessor. The
government involved both internal stakeholders and non-state actors. As a result there was a
stronger focus on the demand side in the project. This applied to both MDAs and to their ultimate
clients. The MDAs were given the prime role in deciding what performance management tools and
systems they would adopt, and several incentive mechanisms encouraged their participation and
performance.

26.     For the ultimate clients of central government services, the government proposed to
develop a communication strategy and use information, education and communication
interventions designed to motivate the public to provide constructive feedback to the public service
and expect from them greater performance and accountability.

Safeguards

27.     The construction of the National Records Centre at Dodoma to improve records
management throughout the country triggered two safeguard policies: Environmental Assessment
(the project was rated category B) and Involuntary Resettlement. An environmental and social
management framework and a resettlement policy framework for the National Records Centre were
prepared and approved by the Bank.

Project Design

Lessons Taken Into Account



                                                 9
28.     The design of the PRAP, to align with the middle phase (“performance management
culture?) of the government’s program (now covering 2008-12), took into account the key lessons
from the earlier project as follows:

        1. Weaknesses in the governance of the PSRP, within government and with the
development partners: The project design provided for reinvigoration of the existing Public Sector
Reforms Steering Committee of all Permanent Secretaries chaired by the Chief Secretary, who is
also Secretary to the Cabinet and Head of the Public Service. It also broadened the PSRP
Management Committee, which provided inter-ministerial technical coordination of the reforms
including approval of plans and budgets and reporting, to include the development partners; this
demonstrated the close partnership and trust that had been established between the government and
the development partners.

        2. Weak sense of ownership of the PSRP reforms by MDAs, which viewed them as owned
by POPSM: The project design provided for a predominantly MDA-driven (“demand-led?)
implementation strategy. This was the key change from the first phase, which was POPSM-driven
(“supply-led?). MDAs would:

        - identify their needs and priorities
        - decide what (up to five) interventions to implement, from a broad menu provided by
        POPSM, and how to implement them. This might involve adaptation and customization of
        standard products or processes
        - implement the intervention, including its procurement
        - control the intervention’s finance through the Government’s accounting system,
        including the authorization to pay contractors and suppliers
        - monitor, evaluate, report progress and be accountable for the results
        - take a more central role in reviewing the program’s design and implementation
        arrangements.

        3. Areas needing further attention in the second phase: The project design provided for:

        - deepening the utilization of performance management systems
        - improving human resources planning and development, via completing the Human
        Capital Management Information System, and tackling MDA capacity and performance
        gaps
        - increased compliance by and accountability of MDA managers, via several incentive
        mechanisms beyond their increased sense of ownership of the reforms.

        4. Weaknesses and gaps in the coordination within government of the several cross-
cutting reform programs and with sector development programs: The project design provided for
the establishment of a Reform Coordination Unit in the office of the Chief Secretary.

28.      The PAD candidly listed eight critical risks, four to the project development objective and
four to component results. It was also candid in assessing that even with mitigation actions four of
these risks remained High and two Substantial. However, it did not draw the obvious conclusion
that overall this was a high-risk project.

The listed risks were as follows:

        - decline in political will amongst the leadership to continue with the cross-cutting reforms

The overall Risk Assessment for PRAP was “Modest? as against “Substantial? in APL I. This
indicates that the government and the Bank felt comfortable not only with the prevailing
environment for reforms but also the “doability? of the tasks envisaged under PRAP in the light of
past successes.

Overall Assessment of Project Design

30.     The project design followed on logically from the first phase of the program (“performance
management systems?) to the next phase of “performance management culture?. It assumed that
the high level of government commitment under the first phase would continue throughout the
second one; the risk of reduced commitment was cited but rated only as Modest. It is noteworthy
however that the assessment of this risk increased as compared to APL I when it was assessed as
Negligible. It is not clear if this was done in recognition of an internal political economy
assessment for the Bank of the government’s commitment to reforms (Mukundala 2005).The
project design did not reflect the necessary mitigating measures needed to manage this risk.



                                                   11
31.       The policy framework and project development objectives were clearly set out. The
results framework (causal chain between performance management enhancement and
improvements in service delivery) was complex and relied on assumptions rather than any hard
evidence. Most perplexingly it was unclear how the project would impact service delivery by
enhancing performance and accountability only in the central ministries since the bulk of the
service delivery was undertaken at a lower level of government (LGAs) and associated service
facilities. However it must be noted that PRAP simply continued the disjuncture between objectives
and project design which had begun in APL I (PSR Project). PSR Project (2000) had assumed that
performance enhancement could positively impact service delivery. It had to be restructured in
2003 by when it was possibly clear that an impact on service delivery was very remote. However
the same inconsistency was carried through to PRAP. It must be noted however, at the time the
drivers of service delivery were still being researched . It is only subsequent to the WDR 2004 that
the fundamentals of what drives service delivery became clearer. In fact more recent work (Sacks
2011) and (Fritz 2013) show that our ideas on what drives service delivery are still evolving. The
outcome objectives were both very ambitious encompassing “all MDAs? and were vaguely worded.
The manner in which enhanced performance was to be evidenced was highly qualitative. POPSM
developed indicators (PDO and intermediate indicators) and annual and end of project targets for
them. The PDO indicators had baseline values. However, the baseline values for most of the
intermediate indicators (9 out of 13) were unavailable when the project started, so the realism of
most of the targets for these indicators was unknown. Nevertheless, despite these short comings,
had the project been implemented as proposed, it could have enabled desirable improvements in the
functioning of the public service though the impact on service delivery would still be uncertain.

32.     The large number of participating MDAs (67), and wide range of activities (serving the 32
sub-components of the program and project), were causes of great complexity of the project design.
The project design failed to recognize that to deal with them all would place unreasonable strain on
the existing and likely capacity of the coordinating institution POPSM, which was simultaneously
reducing its reliance on outside experts. The government and its development partners, including
IDA, should have scaled back the proposed scope.

33.     Under the project the government made a major change of roles and approach to the
participating institutions. The tools and systems had been developed under the first project by
POPSM and introduced to MDAs in a top-down manner, which made the MDAs regard them as
owned by POPSM and which hampered adoption. The second project provided for MDAs to assess
their needs themselves and choose which tools to adopt from a broad menu offered by POPSM
(“demand-led? activities). There were also several incentives to MDAs to participate, including
some financial and technical assistance from POPSM. This was a generally positive change in
design.

34.       However, there was an over-estimate of the capacity of POPSM to assist each of many
MDAs through the cycle of adoption, and another over-estimate of the capacity of MDAs to design
and implement their own improvements. Also, the resources linked to the new tools were very
modest in relation to each MDA’s total activities, and the uptake would involve considerable time
and effort from MDA managers and senior staff. Insufficient consideration was given to the fact
that the first project had needed to be extended and even then, the triggers defined at the beginning
of the APL for moving from APL I (PSRP) to APL II (PRAP) were not wholly met. The core of
the first project had been the installation of performance management systems. Two broad agendas
remained incomplete: (i) the effective transfer of responsibility and accountability for results in
MDAs to public service managers (primarily the Permanent Secretaries) in the MDAs through
performance agreements; and (ii) enforcing application of new systems and tools, and compliance
with rules and procedures, by MDAs.


                                                 12
35.      POPSM’s Permanent Secretary (PS) was identified as the overall program coordinator.
There were several committees across government and within POPSM and with the development
partners which formed a logical structure. It had some improvements from the first phase; in
particular stronger coordination of the government’s several public sector reform programs and a
closer involvement of the development partners. Also, the use of regular government structures
and not a PIU was positive for acceptance and sustainability. But the PS (and his deputy) had other
duties, and was too senior for many implementation tasks. So there was provision for the full-time
program/project coordinator who was essential.

36.     Safeguard aspects of the design were handled appropriately. However the assessment of
the financial risk as Modest was optimistic. The significant increase in the implementing agencies
without a supporting enhancement in the project monitoring arrangements resulted directly in the
implementation problems of 2008-09 and 2009-10 when IFRs were not prepared by MDAs on a
timely basis and were inconsistent with accounting standards.

37.      Overall, the design failed to learn from the past with respect to the likely speed of
implementation; did not take into consideration the Banks’ internal assessment of PE risk; was too
complex in relation to the capacity available; did not provide specifically for the capacity needed to
monitor and coordinate the expanded scope of the project and did not acknowledge the lack of a
direct link between performance enhancement and service delivery. Consequently its virtues were
vastly overshadowed by its shortcomings.

2.2 Implementation

Context

38.     During the implementation period the criticality of capacity and performance enhancement
of the public service was diluted as the focus of government shifted to achieving higher rates of
economic growth. The Tanzanian economy grew rapidly during these years based on good
macroeconomic policies and expansion of the public sector particularly in service delivery. There
was a remarkable expansion in access to primary education services followed by a push to increase
access to secondary education. However the changed mindset of the government focused more on
economic growth rather than tackling the more troublesome area of public service reform. Private
investment in mining and agriculture was promoted and was expected to be an engine of growth,
which could compensate for the low efficiency levels of the bureaucracy. The Prime Minister’s
Office led in coordinating private investment and (through PMO-RALG) in managing reform at the
local government level. At the bureaucratic level reform continued to be a priority with budget
guidelines routinely requiring each Ministry to set aside funds for implementing the reforms.
However, these reform measures no longer enjoyed the close attention of the leadership of the past.

39.     In Phase I of the APL the POPSM had worked closely with former President Mkapa (1995-
2005) who tracked reforms personally. Under Phase II the relationship was more formal.
Nevertheless, President Kikwete inaugurated PSRP II and visited POPSM twice during the project
period to review the progress of the reforms. The focus on implementing the HCMIS in a
compressed time frame of nine months in 2011 was on account of his directive setting the target
date of completion. Undeniably the nature of reforms being implemented under PRAP was vastly
more difficult than under APL I. The PSR Project of APL I had mostly involved the framing of
guidelines and manuals and developing consensus around concepts. PRAP was about actually
implementing the concepts on the ground; changing the behavior of public officials and measuring



                                                 13
the resulting outcomes on service delivery. Implementing these concepts would have meant
perturbing the status quo with inevitable winners (professionals looking for merit based growth in
careers, marginalized groups and efficient private providers of services and goods) and losers
(entrenched interests like unions, officials surviving solely on their connections with the ruling
party and the patronage exercised by members of the ruling party) from change. Possibly, the
leadership assessed that the expected efficiency outcomes were insufficient to justify the political
capital which would need to be expended to implement the reform activities. It is widely agreed
outside the government and amongst development partners that these developments led to a decline
in the perceived relative priority of the public service reform program.

Implementation Before the Mid-Term Review and Restructuring

40.     The project was approved by the Board on September 27, 2007. The credit became
effective on January 16, 2008. Implementation proceeded with numerous problems and more
slowly than expected, and was more than a year behind schedule by the mid-term review which
took place from May 24 to June 7, 2010. Results to date were hard to measure and less than
commensurate with half the IDA credit having been disbursed already.

41.    The main features of the two and a half year implementation period before the mid-term
review were as follows:

        1. “Supply-led? activities (those for which POPSM was responsible) showed progress that
        was uneven but satisfactory overall in the development of performance management
        instruments, manuals, and guidelines, which were of high quality.            But their
        implementation and integration into the functioning of MDAs was weak.

        2. “Demand-led? activities (those for which MDAs took the lead, by selecting performance
        management tools/systems to adopt from a menu provided by POPSM) showed a slow
        uptake. This demand-led implementation strategy was the key difference from Phase I
        which had been completely POPSM-determined. In 2008-09 only 25 MDAs were targeted
        but in 2009-10 an ambitious work plan to increase the number beyond even the 67 MDAs
        targeted under the PRAP to 90 MDAs was approved though ultimately only 67 were
        benefited. A key driver for this decision was the difficulty in excluding MDAs from
        benefiting from this component. In 2009/10 67 MDAs undertook 128 activities,
        concentrated in six areas: strategic plans, open performance review and appraisals, e-
        government, client service charters, monitoring and evaluation, and records management.
        The large number of activities meant that the fund allocations to each MDA were
        insufficient for comprehensive reforms. Also, the expansion strained POPSM’s capacity; it
        was able to facilitate MDAs’ self-assessment and choice of activities (upstream), but not
        downstream where MDAs did not have the capacity to design and implement themselves.
        MDAs tended to stress training and workshops rather than changing the way they
        functioned. The weakness of POPSM supervision also contributed to some fiduciary
        problems with MDAs.

        3. Routine activities continued to be financed from the basket of pooled funds, instead of it
        being reserved for innovative activities with the routine ones transferred to the regular
        budget.

        4. Project implementation was not results oriented. There was a high “burn rate? of funds
        relative to results achieved.



                                                14
        5. Program management was less than adequate. There was no Program Coordinator for
        around a year and a Procurement Specialist was appointed only in 2011. Management
        committee meetings were not being held on time and not frequently enough. Reporting by
        the MDAs was inadequate.

        6. Baseline data for monitoring had still not been generated for 14 key indicators. Annual
        performance indicators (milestones) had been missed for two years. M&E for the demand-
        led activities focused on inputs rather than results and outcomes. To improve
        implementation, the Bank (and other development partners) played a very hands on role in
        reviewing costs of activities in detail; reducing generous allocations and deleting activities
        not leading to results and in some years, imposing ceilings on donor funding. As a result,
        some activities and even whole components of the Program were cut or scaled back.

42.      Despite the problems in the pre restructured project, a substantial number of activities were
initiated and partly completed during this period. To highlight a few: the NRC contract was
finalized and construction started; updating of the HCMIS system to version 9 was completed; the
Ungozi Leadership Institute was launched; a leadership competency assessment exercise was
completed for the first time in Tanzania as a training needs assessment tool; seven new Executive
Agencies were created; foundational reform started in Zanzibar, and work on improving records
management continued. In addition the Demand Side Approach (MDA-led) was implemented,
albeit with all its shortcomings. Details of implementation before restructuring are at Annex 2.

Restructuring

43.      The mid-term review in May/June 2010 led to proposals for major changes. They
constituted a serious disruption to the long-term program the government was pursuing and were
therefore difficult for it to accept. Discussions within the Bank and with the government and co-
financiers were stretched out and a consensus was reached only by June 2011. The government
restructured its program along the lines of the proposed PRAP restructuring. It also sent in a
formal request for restructuring of PRAP on August 15, 2011. A second round of negotiation was
started with the government and DPs. An amended request for restructuring PRAP (excluding the
PFM component) was received from the borrower on February 15, 2012. An agreed restructuring
of the project (sections 1.3, 1.6, and 1.7 above) was approved by the Board on April 9, 2012, 20
months after the mid-term review and less than a year before the closing date. Section 1.3 provides
more details.

44.     The major changes that had to be made (a “corrective restructuring?) reflected the
unsatisfactory performance during the first period. They were as follows:

        1. The PDO was aligned with the simplified Phase II program already adopted by the
        government. It better embodied the expected outcomes from the project.

        2. The number of MDAs involved was drastically reduced from 67, to 14 selected on the
        basis of their significant contribution to basic service delivery objectives. Ten of these
        were Ministries and the others were agencies. The reduction in the number of MDAs also
        reduced costs.

        3. Only 25 ongoing interventions which were directly relevant to improving performance
        management were retained, also reducing the overall cost. The components were
        recombined into four instead of seven.



                                                 15
         4. The demand-led approach was abandoned.             POPSM was henceforth the sole
         implementing agency.

         5.A small component for supporting records management, training and functional reviews
         of select Ministries in Zanzibar was added in recognition of the governments’ desire to
         include the Revolutionary Government of Zanzibar also, for the first time, in the process of
         public service reforms.

         6 New PDO indicators were defined that better reflected the expected outcomes. Related
         intermediate result indicators were added. In general the indicators had more modest
         expected achievements in recognition of the limited scope of the project. They also
         already had baseline data. The new targets had to be achieved within a relatively short
         time which also determined how aggressive they could be

         7. US$10 million of the credit was cancelled.

         8. The APL approach was abandoned

45.     A final important feature was that the closing date of the IDA credit was not extended.
Whilst till then the Bank had aligned its Project with the support of the other DPs and the
underlying program of the government this was a departure from that arrangement. The
government had extended its program to June 30, 2014, and the co-financing development partners;
CIDA and DfID had extended their financing periods accordingly. The decision was based on the
Bank’s internal considerations, although the case for extension was weak given inadequate
performance hitherto; but the decision did prejudge performance after the restructuring and
explains the reduced scope and scale of the restructured project.

Implementation of the Restructured Project

46.      Implementation performance on the greatly reduced program/project was much better after
the mid-term review. The government made changes in the generally agreed directions without
waiting for the formal restructuring of the IDA credit. In addition to its promotional role, POPSM
arranged for the uptake of some of the new tools to be made mandatory for MDAs. This
significantly improved project implementation in MDAs. Program/project management was
considerably enhanced, with the enhancement of POPSM’s capacity including the recruitment of a
new full-time coordinator from October 2010. Spending was tightly focused on activities leading
to results. Monitoring and reporting lags were quickly made up. The revised targets were achieved
by credit closing.

47.      Some highlights of project accomplishments mainly in the post-restructuring period were:
the wide rollout of the HCMIS; the records management systems; the policy initiatives including
particularly improvements in public sector pay relative to private which substantially exceeded the
target; the creation of additional Executive Agencies; the measures for advancement of women in
service; and the embedded system of performance management and accountability particularly in
the 14 MDAs retained in the project. Details of project implementation after restructuring, and on
the achievement of the revised targets, are at Annex 2.

2.3 Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Design, Implementation and Utilization

Design



                                                 16
48.      The government had developed a detailed results framework for the original project,
involving a clearer focus on results than for the prior project. POPSM and MDAs would each have
roles in M&E and would share responsibility and accountability for results. MDAs would derive
their own results frameworks (service delivery and outcomes), and monitor and evaluate project
interventions they implemented. They would meet government-wide cycles of quarterly progress,
annual performance and three-year outcome reports. POPSM would have responsibility for project
aspects it implemented, and for coordinating overall project M&E and reporting within government
and to the development partners.

49.       For the project, four PDO indicators and thirteen intermediate indicators were defined
initially. Several would be measured through annual exercises by POPSM, largely for the joint
annual reviews with development partners, and several more by taking data annually from the
Human Capital Management Information System. A few would require periodic surveys by
POPSM. While baseline values were available for all four PDO indicators, only four of thirteen
intermediate indicators had baseline values at the start of the project.

Implementation

50.     The MDAs which were implementing an important portion of the project in the period
2008-09 and 2009-10 did an inadequate job of both physical and financial reporting in the first half
of the project period. They submitted some reports late or not at all, and tended to concentrate on
inputs and financial flows. POPSM did not adequately monitor the decentralized project
implementation. As a result there were significant reporting lags and omissions. The intermediate
indicators mostly still had no baseline values at the mid-term review, because it had taken so long
to mount consultant studies to determine them. This made it difficult to evaluate progress.

51.      As a result of the mid-term review and restructuring, PDO indicators which were at the
output level rather than outcome indicators or were imprecise were dropped. Intermediate result
indicators were revised, and dropped where data generation had been difficult or the indicator did
not adequately address the expected intermediate results. Thereafter an almost entirely new set of
four PDO and eighteen intermediate indicators was monitored, all by POPSM. All four PDO
indicators and sixteen of the eighteen intermediate indicators had baseline values. Of the two
indicators which did not, one related to records management in Zanzibar, where activities were
being taken up for the first time under the government’s reform program. The second indicator
related to a new activity which was to track the proportion of cases related to breach of the code of
conduct which were resolved over the year.

52.     With the change to sole implementation by the POPSM and enhanced capacity for
program/project coordination, reporting and monitoring lags were made up quickly and prescribed
progress reports were submitted. Enough surveys were carried out and routine data processed to
provide information on the new PDO and intermediate indicators.

Utilization

53.      The data were used partly in the government’s cycles mentioned above and partly by the
project’s management structure and by POPSM itself to guide implementation. One important use
was for the semiannual program reviews of government with the development partners. Given the
lack of baseline data, the original intermediate results indicators were not useful. The post-
restructuring set however was used as intended.



                                                 17
2.4 Safeguard and Fiduciary Compliance

Financial Management

54.     Financial management arrangements took advantage of both overall improvements in the
government’s public financial management and POPSM’s experience with the prior project.
However, the new element in this project, of greatly increased MDA responsibility for financial
management and reporting, was not adequately addressed. Financial management risk was
assessed as modest whereas experience showed that the MDAs proved incapable of accounting
appropriately and consistently for expenditures and submitting IFRs on time.

55.     Financial management was assessed as satisfactory for the first two years of
implementation. However the participating MDAs – 25 through 2008/09, then 67 in 2009/10 –
proved unable to submit the number and quality of reports expected of them in timely fashion.
POPSM took mitigating steps starting in late 2009 by sending periodic advisory and supervision
missions to MDAs which included collecting the financial information required for reporting.
These actions succeeded in resolving gaps in reporting which led to qualified comments in the
audit reports for 2008/09 and 2009/10, since in each year the use of as much as US$9-10 million
could not be audited. Meanwhile POPSM supervision uncovered the case of five MDAs which
seemed to have used funds totaling about US$0.2 million on activities contrary to their
commitments to POPSM. These cases were pursued in detail. Further investigation revealed that
whilst the MDAs had not used funds for the specific activities for which they were allocated they
had been used for activities within program objectives and proper procedures were followed for
procurement and accounting of the funds. In addition these MDAs subsequently used their own
funds to achieve the outputs originally intended, and there were no cases of fraud or corruption
involved. On the other hand, the mid-term review found that two ministries had diverted program
funds totaling TZS 120 million to other activities; these funds were repaid to the project. One of the
Ministries was the Ministry of Home Affairs which was supported by the governments program but
not by the PRAP. The other Ministry was also dropped from the restructured project. There were
no concerns regarding financial management after the restructuring. Audit reports by the National
Audit Office were unqualified and financial reports were submitted on time.

Procurement

56.     Major procurements under the project were for the construction of the new National
Records Centre at Dodoma (estimated at US$4.9 million) and for about 25 consultant services
contracts for various aspects of the reforms (mostly $100,000-200,000 each). POPSM would be
responsible for all of these procurements. Its procurement unit had experience from implementing
the prior project. Its capacity, which ranged between four and six persons and included both
government employees (supplies officers) and procurement experts (consultants), was judged
adequate provided it was maintained. However, component leaders within POPSM needed training
in the production of consultant terms of reference and in contract management. Some 67 MDAs
would also procure under the project. They would be assessed, assisted and supervised by POPSM,
which would review three or if necessary more bid packages of MDAs as long as they had
inadequate capacity. The overall program risk for procurement, which was rated as Average, was
mitigated through providing training to staff, recruitment of a procurement expert, preparation of a
procurement manual, and improvement of working space including record keeping.

57.    Challenges encountered included delays in preparation of TORs for consultancy services
and technical specifications for goods/equipment. The mid-term review found that project
implementation was far behind schedule. There was less than adequate coordination between the


                                                 18
procurement unit and the user departments of POPSM. However, the main civil works package –
the National Records Centre (NRC) – and all planned goods/equipment and consultancy services
were delivered by the project closing date. The Program Coordinator of POPSM played a major
role in monitoring and coordinating the procurement process between the user departments and the
procurement unit of the POPSM which contributed significantly to the achievement of the
Procurement Plan. Procurement post-reviews by the Bank noted that generally procurement and
contracting arrangements were carried out in accordance with the legal agreement, and no red flags
were noted, while issues raised were addressed in agreed action plans.

Environmental Assessment

58.     An environmental and social management framework (ESMF) was prepared and approved
in 2007. The project was placed in category B – partial assessment. The main concern was with
the construction of the National Records Centre (NRC) in Dodoma. Office renovations for
POPSM and other agencies, mainly in Dar es Salaam, were within the existing building footprints
and raised only minor routine concerns.

59.     The mid-term review mission found that the required environmental and social impact
assessment (ESIA) at Dodoma had not been conducted. The terms of reference of the supervising
consultant did not include responsibilities with respect to assessing environmental safeguards.
Relevant staff had not been trained. These deficiencies were corrected and the assessment, from
which an Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP) for the NRC was developed, was
completed in 2011. Compliance with the SMP throughout the construction period was satisfactory.

Involuntary Resettlement

60.    A resettlement policy framework (RPF) was prepared and approved in 2007 with financing
under another project, covering the construction of the National Records Centre at Dodoma. The
mid-term review found that the process by which POPSM acquired the plot of land involved
adhered to Tanzanian land laws and the framework, and did not involve any sort of resettlement or
compensation of previous owners and users.

2.5 Post-completion Operation/Next Phase

61.     The project outcome has embedded the use of performance management and accountability
tools in key service delivery related ministries and agencies. These MDAs now need to be
rigorously monitored to assess the extent to which the regular use of these tools is sustained over
the next two years. In addition it would be useful if these tools are rolled out to all the service
delivery agencies and service facilities (hospitals, universities, parastatals) associated with these
ministries. It is only then that the impact on central government service delivery can be
comprehensively monitored. Similarly, the use of these tools should be made mandatory in all
sector based programs irrespective of whether they are funded by Development Partners or the
government. In this manner the MDAs which have not yet been covered would also benefit.

62.     The government’s PSRP Phase II program, which the project supported, will continue until
June 30, 2014. Government, CIDA, and DfID funding is available until then for a final work
program currently being agreed, as a result of the March 2013 annual review that took place
simultaneously with the ICR mission. Thus reforms made during the project period could be
sustained and furthered through the end of Phase II. As for physical works, to ensure efficient




                                                 19
operation of the NRC, POPSM should establish an effective repair and maintenance plan for these
facilities.

63.      The government continues to declare its commitment to public service reform. Beyond
Phase II it is expected to continue those reforms that have already been made in the area of
performance management tools and systems. Some of these – notably strategic plans and the
OPRAS (personnel evaluation system) – have already been made mandatory for the entire public
service, and M&E is also built into wider government programs. In addition the government is
expected to make selective efforts to further improve the public service, in line with its long term
commitment. Some of these efforts would be to sustain gains already made, some to consolidate,
some to spread the reforms to more MDAs, and some for the quality improvements always
envisaged for the third phase of the governments’ program.          Budget guidelines require each
Ministry to set aside funds for the implementation of the reforms. These are positive indications of
continuing government commitment. There is however no clear indication yet of the total
government financing that could be available after Phase II ends in 2014.

64.      IDA has moved away from across the board reforms and as the CAS 2012-15 states the
focus is now on sector programs and support for local governments which will embed the
performance and accountability tools, pay incentives and ICT applications. Other development
partners have yet to formalize their strategies but greater focus on “asymmetric? reform seems to
be a likely trend.

65.      The early success of the Sierra Leone Pay and Performance Project (Srivastava 2013) has
given credence to a new genre of results based PSR projects. The core of such projects is intense
engagement with the government to determine a set of results on the basis of which the agreed
finances are disbursed. Shifting away from the “input? based financing structure of Investment
lending to an “output? based financing mechanism has demonstrated the transfer of real ownership
over the process of project implementation to the government. This has been shown in Sierra
Leone to induce a higher level of commitment of the government to results and has sparked the
development of “innovative reform paths? by the government. Tanzania has for long been used to
“pooled fund projects? in which the input based financing is very much relaxed and funds are
disbursed based on certification of use of advances and the broad achievement of results. Informal
soundings with government counterparts indicate that they are very open to the flexibility in
implementation modality associated with results based projects and they would be eager to adopt
that structure. An urban project in Tanzania is already using a P4R format and this structure seems
appropriate for future public sector reform projects.

3.1 Relevance of Objectives, Design and Implementation

Relevance of Objectives

67.      Tanzania continues to implement programs with long-term perspectives, such as
MKUKUTA II (2010/112014/15) and the Long Term Perspective Plan (2011/12-2025/26). Annual
plans and the next multi-year exercise will draw on the strategic planning capabilities of the MDAs.
This is one area in which the PRAP objectives retain their relevance. However relevance must also
include “realism? in formulating the goal within the limited time span of a program/project. Whilst
the broad objectives of PRAP were aligned to the broad planning objectives of GOT, they were
significantly lacking on realism with respect to what could be achieved within the time span of the
program. This realization is reflected in the CAS (2012-15) of the Bank, which records that “one of
the lessons learnt from the earlier period is that broad-based governance reform programs, did not
progress as rapidly as desired. Greater attention should be given to strengthening service delivery
through local government and sector-specific support. In addition, the Bank’s program could more
systematically support the demand for good governance, including through use of ICT.? It is in
pursuit of this specificity, which was lacking in PRAP, that the CAS did not provide a follow-on
program for public service reform. The relevance of the objectives of the original project to current
country and global priorities, and to Bank assistance strategy, is rated “Modest?.

68.      Whilst it was not possible, due to various reasons, including the need to close the project
on the scheduled closing date, to establish a direct link with service delivery which happens in the
LGAs, the restructuring ensured that all the major Ministries associated with service delivery
(Education, Health, Water, Regional Administration, under which the LGAs work, Agriculture,
Lands and Housing, Industries and Trade and Labour and Employment) remained associated with
the project. It also made the DO more realistic and achievable in the time available; focused
activities; reduced components and reduced the number of MDAs within the overall goal of
enhancing performance and accountability. The relevance of the objectives of the restructured
project to those priorities and strategy is rated “Substantial?

Relevance of Design

69.      In the pre-restructuring period, the project design included a very large number of
activities. While most had some relevance to the original objectives, quite a number were less
closely related. The results framework involved many time-bound targets, many of them highly
ambitious, most without baseline values, and many qualitative; they were dropped wholesale at
restructuring. The causal chain between funding and outcomes was clear for expenditures related
to the capacity/performance objective if only somewhat convincing, but even less clear and
convincing as regards those related to the accountability objective.

70.     In the post-restructuring period, the project’s revised design was highly relevant to the
revised project objectives, after the elimination of all activities not closely focused on achieving the
objectives. The results framework was also clear as to objectives and targets for the short time
involved. The causal chain between funding and outcomes was clear for expenditures related to




                                                  21
the performance management objective, but again less clear as regards those related to the
responsiveness objective.

Ratings:

The relevance of the project’s original design is rated Modest.
The relevance of the restructured project’s design is rated High.

Relevance of Implementation

71.     A key element of the implementation strategy was decentralizing implementation to the
MDAs to enhance “ownership? and to integrate the performance enhancing and accountability
mechanisms into the functioning of the MDAs. There was little evidence to support this hypothesis
that merely by converting MDAs into implementing agencies “ownership? of the activities would
increase. The “demand led? component proved to be unsatisfactory and was abandoned. The
enhanced monitoring and reporting load was unbearable and resulted in less than adequate
monitoring and reporting. The relevance of the implementation strategy for the original project is
rated modest.

71. The restructuring altered the implementation strategy and reverted to the tried and tested
    implementation of the earlier projects by centralizing project implementation in POPSM. This
    strategy paid dividends with project implementation responding to the new arrangements. All
    budgeted activities were completed on schedule and the budgeted amounts were utilized prior
    to project closing. The relevance of the implementation strategy post restructuring is rated
    substantial.

Indicator 2: Level of accuracy of public service profile generated in all MDAs with Human Capital

84.      The HCMIS, which was an item of unfinished business under the prior project, was
completed under this project. It has now been installed in a phenomenal 347 public sector bodies

secretariats, 126 local government authorities, 26 higher learning institutions, and 4 referral
hospitals. 1034 HR officers have been trained on its 13 modules.

85.      It has already made a remarkable difference to a wide range of parties, with significant
efficiency gains. For individual public servants newly joining the service, and for those promoted
or earning other salary adjustments, delays and salary arrears which used to cover several months
(or even years in the worst cases) have been eliminated as salaries are now updated within a month.
For HR managers and financial staff, the accuracy and predictability of the payroll have increased
markedly. Ghost workers are identified easily and the incidence of such workers has reduced
significantly. Paperwork has reduced, saving time. HR planning has been facilitated.

86.      Given the importance of an individual’s record, its accuracy is critical. In this regard, no
individual is allowed to see his or her record and check it for accuracy, although if the salaries and
allowances they received were incorrect they would presumably protest. Furthermore, the
government’s definition of accurate (at least for purposes of this project) is a rather special one
which should more appropriately be called “complete? than “accurate?. It consists of measuring
(centrally) what percentage of the boxes on everyone’s forms contains an entry, as opposed to
being blank. The baseline value for this percentage was 60% in 2009/10 while the end of project
target was 95% in 2012/13.

87.      POPSM’s end of project report shows the figure increasing each year to 95% in 2012/13
(and 100% for new recruits). The achievement is exactly on target. Two issues however remain
unclear. First, was this a stretch target or is 95% accuracy a common feature of an integrated
payroll. Under the PEFA framework less than 3% retroactive adjustments in salary are considered
acceptable. On that basis and in view of the baseline of 60% the target of 95% was a reasonably
stretch target. Secondly, the definition of accuracy as proportion of input boxes which have data, is
more an indicator of data completeness, rather than accuracy. An accuracy indicator would show
the extent to which results/outputs match what they should be. However, it is appropriate to note
that the meetings with MDAs, undertaken during the ICR mission, all confirmed the significant



                                                 25
improvement in the accuracy and timeliness of the payroll. This outcome is aligned with the
measured improvements in the HCMIS.

The target was achieved.

3.5 Overarching Themes, Other Outcomes and Impacts

(a) Poverty Impacts, Gender Aspects, and Social Development

Gender Impact

115.   In Tanzania managerial positions in the public service require a masters degree or higher.
For advertised positions a masters’ degree confers a competitive advantage. The program
sponsored postgraduate degrees at Tanzanian universities for 237 women. A 2012 tracer study
showed that 52% of sponsored women had assumed decision making posts in 2010, increasing to
65% from 2011.

116.    A second area of affirmative action on gender was leadership training. Some 8,500
persons were trained in various aspects. Women made up 41% of the participants every year,
suggesting a praiseworthy effort to keep up the proportion over time.

Social Development

117.     The focus under the program was on gender, HIV and disability. POPSM produced
guidelines on diversity management in 2010 and on disability in 2008. Other achievements
included 832 staff declaring their HIV status and becoming eligible for special support; increased
accessing of voluntary testing and counseling services; some special services by MDAs for their
staff with disabilities; reduced discrimination against people living with HIV; and a new diversity
unit in the Ministry of Education and Vocational Training and a special education program
introduced.

(b) Institutional Change/ Strengthening

118.     Institutional strengthening and some changes were essential features of the project. As
described above, all the performance management and accountability tools were items of
institutional strengthening, which have been:

        - developed fully (although many under the prior project)
        - installed, in varying degrees in a large number of MDAs (67 under the project), and
        - are being used, in a smaller number of MDAs (measured only for the 15 MDAs retained
        in the restructured program and a few others).

119.    Although the penetration of these tools has been partial in relation to the original targets,
there has clearly been substantial institutional strengthening as a result of the project. Beyond the
individual MDAs concerned, the completion and widespread installation of the Human Capital
Management Information system (HCMIS) has been of considerable assistance to POPSM in
managing the payroll, and to human resources managers in addition to benefiting individual public
servants seeking quick and accurate salary payments and adjustments.

120.   The creation of another 13 Executive Agencies in a variety of sectors under the project
promises further service delivery and efficiency gains, also allowing their parent Ministries to
concentrate more on core government functions.

121.    The design and implementation of the project were overwhelmingly in-house by public

(c) Other Unintended Outcomes and Impacts (positive or negative)

122.    A positive outcome of the project not envisaged at the design stage is the enhanced
capacity to provide accurate information in real time on the entire public service profile and cost
through the integrated HCMIS, disaggregated for various levels, geographical areas and functions.

under the Open Government Partnership in which Tanzania is a member of the Global Steering

123.   A second unintended outcome of the project has been the kindling of interest and faith in

“establishment department? with the primary task of keeping personnel records and vetting

4. Assessment of Risk to Development Outcome
As discussed in section 2.5, the project’s objectives retain their relevance for the country over the
long-term. The bedrock of enhanced service delivery has to be a capable, efficient and responsive
civil service. The activities of the PRAP are closely aligned with this objective. Achieving growth
with shared prosperity and in particular optimum use of the revenue from extractives, which is
expected to increase sharply over the medium term, will require extensive restructuring of the
manner in which human resources are appointed and managed and the instruments for making
them directly accountable to citizens. This seems to indicate that the tools and instruments used by
PRAP to enhance performance and accountability in the selected 14 MDAs need to be rolled out to
all government entities for scaling up the positive impact and for making it visible in the quality of
services received by citizens. However, heeding the lessons from the past, it is important to
recognize that political economy factors often intrude into plans which seem to have a technical
rationale. With elections coming up in 2015 it is unlikely that foundational reforms will be
immediately feasible. Experience shows that short term results matter more at such times. Post
elections, much will depend on the outcome. There is higher political contestation now in Tanzania.
The medium term availability of enhanced domestic public resources; the proposed recourse to
external commercial markets and the availability of significant volumes of trade and investment
related soft credit are all likely to enhance the incentives for dominance in the political power
market. Simultaneously, growing incidents of random civil discord and the resort to violence,
reinforce the need for maintaining the status quo and may reduce the political space available for
perturbing the existing low levels of political and economic equilibrium, in a search for enhanced
effectiveness and efficiency.

6. Lessons Learned
150.    Design for the PE context
A rigorous assessment of political economy (PE) risks and the formulation of a risk mitigation
strategy are key to project success. In this case, the PE risks had been assessed in 2005 but the
negative findings on reform commitment were not seriously followed up at the time of project
appraisal in 2007, by which time early signals were available (amendment of the Public Service
Act reversing the reform intervention of open competition in public service for lateral entry
appointments being an example). The project design could have been suitably refocused towards
the entry points less likely to be open to PE risk.


151.    Don’t outpace the client
The APL I (PSR project) had needed to be restructured and extended from five to a period of eight
years, but this was not taken into account while scaling up the interventions in PRAP. The triggers
for APL I were not all achieved despite that phase being extended. Had sufficient weight been



                                                37
given to this red flag the design of PRAP could have been adjusted, to a more realistic pace of
implementation and a more manageable range of interventions. The complexity (“demandingness?)
of programs and projects has to be limited to the existing and likely capacity of
borrowers/implementers. This project failed to respect this principle despite the example provided
by its predecessor of continued low capacity and extended time lines for getting activities
completed.

152.    Project design must be limited to what can be measured and reported
Reform programs need to be aligned with the level of monitoring systems available or those which
can be reasonably embedded during the project period. The program suffered in the earlier phase
because activities were being undertaken without a suitable monitoring system to support them.
There is little point in undertaking activities which cannot be monitored or evaluated for impact
since establishing their value for money is difficult. The red flag that base lines for most of the
indicators were not available at the time the project became effective illustrated that the project was
over designed for the underlying monitoring systems. The low capacity to monitor and coordinate
across MDAs should have indicated that limiting the range and scope of the project would be
beneficial.

153.     APLs can produce complacency and inertia
Whilst Bank commitment to a long-term reform program is often valuable, the APL structure is
challenging in dynamic environments where the predictability of political commitment, reform
programs, processes and time lines is low. It becomes very difficult to specify ex ante what triggers
would present reasonably stretch targets for moving to the next phase of reforms. APLs also tend to
bind down the overall structure of the program which needs to be related to emerging priorities
over time. For example a BRN type of intervention may have been useful even in 2007 since the
success of the UK Prime Minister’s Service Delivery Unit was well known by then6 to reinvigorate
the reform momentum and link reforms to results on the ground. However, constrained by the
straightjacket of the APL as initially designed in 2000, it would be difficult to include it at that
stage.

154.     Program management needs support for large programs
Whilst projects must strive to mainstream activities into the government systems to ensure
sustainability (Accra Agenda 2008 and Paris Principles 2005), large multi donor pooled fund
financed reform programs pose very specific challenges in mainstreaming. The level of intensity of
effort, program management and monitoring skills required are likely to be well beyond the
capacity of the implementing Ministry. Ministries are equipped to handle their routine work.
Reform programs impose a highly variable peak load on their functioning. Staffing up the Ministry
to meet this load is wasteful since the pressure of work is unlikely to be sustained over time.
Reform programs try and compress into three to five years what would typically take ten to fifteen
years to do. A more efficient way of managing this temporary load of work is to staff the project
with core specialists, recruited for the project period, who would work with the designated officials
and support them. In this way whilst retaining ownership and control over program management
the implementing Ministry gets additional support to execute the additional tasks on time. The
program remained without a Program Coordinator and a Procurement Specialist for over a year till
October 2010 and until 2011 respectively, which negatively affected performance. Much of the




6
    Barber, Michael: 2008. Instruction to Deliver: Fighting to Reform Britain’s Public Services.

credit for the enhanced performance from July 2011 goes to the project management staff of the
PSRP, which was tasked to focus exclusively on the program.

155.     Specified outcomes must respond to felt needs of the client
Reforms without tangible benefits to clients, within a reasonable time, will not be sustained. Since
the program was not related to specific delivery outcomes its usefulness to the government became
less visible. Consequently, the attention paid by the leadership to the program activities reduced
significantly, negatively impacting the project. Had PSRP been vertically linked to the delivery of
actual results (as BRN is) it is conceivable it would have performed better due to the pressure of
multiple stakeholders: parliament, citizens and think tanks. Since the PSRP primarily focused on
improvements within the public service with no direct linkage to the delivery of services, it lacked
the “visibility’ so necessary for sustaining reform efforts.

156.     Develop core indicators to enhance standardization in assessments
The public service practice suffers from not having standardized core indicators. On the one hand,
this pressures projects to devise suitable outcome indicators. On the other, it reduces the incentives
for government to embed the measurement of these indicators in their monitoring framework since
they are project specific and possibly may not have lasting value. Identification of standardized
core indicators for public service reform could dilute this problem considerably. Monitoring of
indicators remained an issue till 2011 because these were not embedded in the routine monitoring
framework of the government. Many were changed and new ones substituted in the restructured
project either because the existing ones did not reflect the core reform areas sufficiently or because
they had proved difficult to measure. Standardization could ensure that across the government
performance and accountability is measured routinely in a commonly acceptable manner.

157.     Need to understand better how the “plumbing? links to results
The public service practice needs to define better the links between capacity building in specific
sectors and impact on service delivery. Similarly the link between incentives and public service
performance needs to be defined more credibly. These two sets of links are crucial for value for
money project design. There is little hard research based evidence of tradeoffs between alternative
routes to improving service delivery. The BRN approach (short term) versus the PSRP
(foundational approach) is one such trade off but the costs and benefits of either are difficult to
contextualize. Similarly, what motivates public servants to work more efficiently is not very well
understood. Do incentives work without accompanying sanctions? We know that often
performance based incentives can cause perverse results due to gaming but do not yet have a
science of using an “acceptable? set of incentives. We are also unclear about what causes citizens
to be satisfied with services. Does a given level of services result in a similar level of
“satisfaction?? Do citizens constantly look for incremental improvements in services (an escalator
which never stops)? Do citizens care more about the manner in which their needs are met rather
than the nominal level of services (Sacks 2011). Which interventions result in improved service
delivery? Is a good PFM system sufficient or does a combination of inputs and better availability
of - finance together result in better service delivery (Fritz, 2013)

158.    To pool or not to pool funds.
Participation in pooled funds by the Bank whilst being beneficial, is not without costs particularly
with respect to increasing the complexities for restructuring a project and binding the Bank to a
commonly agreed program and implementation strategy. For the government, the transaction cost
is minimized. It is also beneficial for other development partners, who feel secure that the Bank
team is safeguarding the fiduciary aspects of the utilization of their funds and who readily
acknowledge the sector knowledge and experience the Bank team brings to the table. For the Bank,
pooled funds are an instrument for leveraging its own financing to serve the client better. However,


                                                 39
participation in such funds should be selective and restricted to areas where there is significant
commonality of purpose and commonly agreed sector assessments exist; where sector plans are
well developed and where the government has demonstrated commitment to implementing them.
These conditions are generally absent in the case of public service reform and hence participation
in pooled funds for such projects should be highly selective. Opportunistic, targeted and flexible
interventions by the Bank are more likely to be successful in public service reform rather than large
across the board reform programs.

A. Before Restructuring

KRA 1 – Systems and structures to support service delivery

The original expected outcome related to this KRA was 'All MDA's are managing for service
delivery results, in line with standards disseminated to the public and the monitoring and evaluation
of outcomes and impact'. The assessment at midterm was that this objective was very ambitious
and was far from being achieved, although some foundations had been laid which might be built
upon in future years

This KRA was central to the project and was allocated about one third of the resources. It covered
five major activities:

       Institutionalize Performance Management Systems

       Restructure MDA's and re-engineer service delivery

       Outsource internal non-core services

       Build E-Government and knowledge management capacity

       Improve management of records that support service delivery.

On the supply-led side, POPSM developed only a few new tools beyond those prepared under the
previous project. They were however of good quality, e.g., a strategic planning manual and
guidelines for personnel evaluation and for client service charters. It facilitated MDA selection of
activities and provided upfront workshops and training to help build MDA capacity. But did not
have the capacity itself to “accompany? each MDA in each activity throughout its implementation
and relied upon annual monitoring.

On the demand-led side, by 2009/10 a large number of activities were underway in many MDAs as
follows:

                                # of MDA's 2009/10
Strategic planning              44
Client service charters         31
OPRA's                          47
M&E                             28
Restructuring                   2
E-Government                    46
Records management              30
Total # of initiatives          128

Most of these demand-led activities focused on training. There was little evidence of outputs and
outcomes related to management for service delivery results.

One area of results achieved was the creation of seven new executive agencies in the first half of
the project period.


                                                 48
Under outsourcing of non-core functions, just 2 MDAs outsourced cleaning and ground
maintenance.

In e-government POPSM was developing a government portal that would initially host three
services: a government directory, land registration, and company registration.

In records management, there were some successes in installation of improved manual records
management systems (reclassifying files by keywords instead of by numbers) and decongesting of
registries. However, approvals for the construction of the National Records Centre were delayed
and the budget was inadequate, and its environmental impact assessment had not been prepared.

KRA 2: Policy Development

The related objective was ‘fifteen MDAs are formulating their policies using participatory and
evidence-based processes and are monitoring whether their policies are being implemented and are
effective’. Only very partial achievement (four or five out of fifteen MDAs) appeared within reach.

Good revised guidelines were prepared.

Baseline studies on five MDAs arrived at relevant and operationally important conclusions and
recommendations, which constituted a good basis for further engagement on this important issue.
In particular, studies highlighted; i) the absence of a practice of stakeholder consultations in policy
design; ii) the design of policies without implementation plans or a reflection on the feasibility or
implementability of instruments; iii) an overreliance and narrow focus on laws and regulations to
achieve objectives, and iv) no review of the potential impact of policies.

Unfortunately however almost no MDAs selected policy development as a desired activity.

KRA 3: Pay, incentives and rewards

The target was ‘Wages of public servants are increasing in real terms, are in line with targets set in
the Medium Term Pay Policy, and are increasingly competitive with comparable private sector
positions. The use of allowances is more transparent and rational.’ The assessment of progress at
mid-term was that this target could be partially achieved,

Government decisions on pay policy had been delayed considerably.

MDAs had not advanced much in installing OPRAS; they had shown little interest in performance
appraisal or in better linking rewards to results.

Budgets introduced incremental changes in the use of allowances, which however remained
widespread, inequitably distributed and not transparent.

KRA 4: Accountability and Responsiveness

The target was ‘All MDAs are responding to their clients’ demands using a variety of tools,
systems and instruments.’

POPSM had developed a number of performance and accountability instruments but these were not
yet implemented in MDAs.


                                                  49
Guidelines had been drafted for complaints handling mechanisms.

Public servants were provided with a code of ethics and training on it, but without focus on
particular expected behavior change. A code of ethics compliance framework had been drafted.

Information education and communication (IEC) activities had included a baseline survey on
knowledge attitudes and practice about IEC in the public service, and helping to strengthen MDA
communication. However guidelines on promoting MDA collaboration with non-state actors
(NGOs, CSOs), to stimulate voice and demand for accountability, were not yet approved.

KRA 5: Systems for managing public servants

The target was ‘Human Resources (HR) processes and systems have been strengthened and
decentralized, especially in terms of recruitment, appraisal, HR planning and training.’

The new HR flagship process of Open Performance Review and Appraisal Scheme (OPRAS) had
been installed in 66% of MDAs. However, OPRAS was used at supervisor and management levels
predominantly, with interest depending on employers despite a government circular requiring
annual completion.

Payroll and staff inspections had been carried out in the Ministries of Health and Social Welfare
and of Education and Vocational Training (for secondary school teachers). This had led to greater
stability of the hitherto volatile payroll.

 The Human Capital Management Information System (HCMIS) was undergoing a software
upgrade and being rolled out to more MDAs.

Human resource planning and training were being undertaken more systematically. An HR
planning manual was completed. Nine of 15 MDAs had submitted their HR plans. 38 MDAs and
55 LGAs had reports on succession planning, important in view of expected gaps at management
levels in a few years. A training needs assessment manual had been drafted. 16 of 44 MDAs had
completed training plans after undertaking training needs assessments.

Guidelines on diversity, HIV/AIDS, gender and disability were at various stages.

Women and PLVD were being selected for master’s degrees to improve their career prospects.

KRA 6: Leadership Development

The target was: ‘leadership skills and competencies are identified, developed, nurtured and valued
in line with career development plans.’ All activities were significantly behind schedule - eight
activities of which one has made its first deliverable but is not near completion. The target which
is neither quantified nor qualified might be achieved on a very limited scale.

A leadership competency assessment framework of good quality had been produced.

Leadership development training programs had started on an outsourcing basis.

KRA 7: Change management and reform coordination

The target was “Under the leadership of the Chief Secretary’s Office, reforms are coordinated to
minimize overlap and to exploit opportunities for synergy. Reforms are integrated at the
MDA/LGA level to ensure ownership.? The target was broadly achievable except that not all
MDAs would be covered and the LGAs were not in the program/project design.

A Reforms Coordination Unit had been constituted and partially staffed. It had made progress in
establishing coordination processes like regular meetings of the interested parties to discuss
progress and exploit synergies.

POPSM was being strengthened in managing the PRSP through consultant support and training in

But POPSM had not yet recruited a program coordinator whose absence was being keenly felt.

B. After Restructuring

KRA 1: Systems and Policies to Support Service Delivery

1.1 Installation and Use of Performance Management Systems

The aim was to get five key tools installed and into use in MDAs: strategic plans (SP), monitoring
and evaluation (M&E), the open performance review and appraisal system (OPRAS), client
service charters (CSC) and complaints handling mechanisms (CHM).                   Surveys show
improvement on the first two, SP and M&E. However, performance remained relatively weak in
the utilization of CSC and OPRAS. The latter was implemented at higher levels of many
organizations, but not linked with rewards. It has been given a new impetus from a link with the
HCMIS – promotions are supposed to require OPRAS forms. Also the Chief Secretary has
declared the use of OPRAS to be mandatory. The indicator of quality in the implementation of
the five tools in 18 MDAs has improved from a baseline value of 62% in 2009/10 to 78% in
2012/13, exceeding the 70% end of project target.

1.2 Restructuring MDAs and Re-engineering of Service Delivery Systems

Executive Agencies increased from 23 to 36 by the end of the project, exceeding the target of
creating 2 per annum. Outsourcing of non-core activities was dropped after restructuring; just 2
MDAs have outsourced cleaning and ground maintenance.

Five business process improvement projects have led to improvements in such areas as company
name search; food and drug premises registration; payroll processing for new recruits, and
complaints handling. These are areas where ultimate clients of central government services can
see real improvements. From zero in 2009/10 the number reached the end of project target.

1.3 Records Management

About 100 institutions were facilitated and implemented keyword filing systems. Guidelines for
registry procedures and instructions for record users were developed and distributed. Four
Ministries with specialized records were assisted. Mobile shelves were installed in some MDA
registries. As a result the average time to retrieve subject records has fallen from 18 minutes in
2006/07 to 10 minutes which was the target for 2012/13. Zanzibar was also assisted (see below).

The government has constructed and equipped a new National Records Centre at Dodoma under
the project which has increased capacity from 200,000 to 700,000 linear meters. Moving non-
current records here will free up large amounts of space in MDA and lower-level registries, and
may permit giving up rental space used for three zonal registry offices.

1.4 E-government and Knowledge Management Systems

An e-government strategy was prepared. An Executive Agency for e-government was created.
MDAs were provided with modern office equipment and training for 254 staff which has
transformed government work. Seven MDAs in Dar es Salaam have been connected
electronically for both voice and data, and another project supported by IDA is financing work to
connect public institutions in Dar, Coast, and Dodoma regions.

The Government Portal has three services initially: government directory, land titles, and business
s name search. There is also a wealth of government information online. The Public Service


                                                58
Recruitment Secretariat web site has proved very useful for applicants, and saving money on
newspaper advertisements. Tanzania is a board member of the US Open Government
Partnership.

1.5 Policy Development

A Planning Commission has been re-established. All MDAs now have strategic plans. They
have policy and planning units which also deal with M&E. The Cabinet Secretariat established
an M&E Committee. Guidelines require policy papers to be submitted with implementation
strategies and action plans. Guidelines for enhancing the effective participation of non-state
actors in policy management processes were prepared.

A national policy team of 40 analysts was trained in participatory and evidence based policy
development, which serves as a reference group for Ministries. On these bases four such policies
have been developed, against a target of two.

KRA 2: Human Resources Management, Pay, Incentives and Leadership Development

2.1 Human Resource Processes and Systems

To facilitate MDAs’ HR planning, related and succession planning manuals and guidelines were
prepared. They were introduced for use in 46 institutions, of which 15 were facilitated to develop
their own succession plans. MDAs carried out training needs assessments and 47 MDAs were
facilitated to prepare training plans. The government also adopted a public service training policy
in 2012.

A PRAP indicator with the baseline value of zero in 2009/10 had the target that all 15 MDAs
would be implementing standardized HR tools (training needs assessment, human resources plan,
succession plan) by 2012/13. The target was achieved.

2.2 Diversity Management

The focus is on HIV, gender and disability. POPSM produced guidelines on diversity
management in 2010 and on disability in 2008. Sensitization after restructuring focused on 15
MDAs. From zero in 2009/10 16 MDAs were effectively implementing the former guideline.

PSRP sponsored postgraduate degrees for 237 women and 14 men with disabilities in the public
service. A 2012 tracer study showed that 52% of sponsored women had assumed decision
making posts in 2010, increasing to 65% from 2011.

Other achievements included 832 staff declaring their HIV status and becoming eligible for
special support; increased accessing of voluntary testing and counseling services; some special
services by MDAs for their staff with disabilities; reduced discrimination against people living
with HIV; and a new diversity unit in the Ministry of Education and Vocational Training and a
special education program introduced.

2.3 Management of HR Information

The Human capital Management Information System (HCMUIS) was completed with version 9
of the Lawson software. It was rolled out to 347 institutions in 2011/12 and 405 in 2012/13 to
cover all drawing salaries from the treasury, compared to only 27 in 2009/10 and a target of 54 by


                                                59
2012. It permitted removal of ghost workers, saving money; instant entry of new recruits; instant
salary adjustments; virtual elimination of salary arrears; reduced paperwork, and savings on
administrative travel to feed the system. Reliability of HR data has much improved. A figure of
95% accuracy, meaning completeness, has been achieved. HR and payroll management has been
greatly facilitated.

Two institutions continued to provide a lot of training to public officials. The Tanzania Public
Service College served about 35,000; several of its staff received some training, and facilities
were rehabilitated. The Tanzania Global Learning Agency, formerly a World Bank project and
now an Executive Agency, served 13,000 students and 22,000 visitors to its information center.

2.4 Pay and Incentives

This tackles public service pay in relation to private sector comparators, and incentives to attract
and retain staff for work in remote areas. A Presidential Pay Commission reported in 2008. A
new Pay and Incentives policy was approved in 2010 and its implementation strategy is still
under discussion. The Public Service Remuneration Board is now operational. Public pay has
improved greatly, from 49% of comparators in 2006/07 to 56% in 2010/11 and 78% in November
2012, far exceeding the target of 60%. A package of rationalized incentives for service in 33
difficult-to-staff districts has been developed but not yet approved.

There was also an attempt to rationalize staffing norms. In education the teacher-pupil ratio has
improved from 1:64 in 2008 to 1:47 in 2012, against an official ratio of 40. Health sector
employment as well as that of teachers has increased by about 15% from 2009/10 to 2011/12. In
combination with the recent swift rise in relative public pay, these increases could possibly lead
to macroeconomic pressures.

2.5 Leadership Development

Public servants become leaders around age 40 and 50%of senior officials will exit the service
within 10-15 years. POPSM developed a leadership competence framework used for training of
547 officials in various aspects. A proposal to establish a Senior Executive Service has been
drafted and another for a leadership ward scheme. More than 400 senior officials were exposed
to strategic contemporary issues. As many as 8,500 officials have been trained in aspects of
leadership, meeting the target, with female participation (suspiciously) constant at 41%
throughout.

KRA 3: Change Management and Reform Coordination

3.1 MDAs Lead and Drive the Reforms, using Government Processes and Procedures

POPSM’s capacity was increased to better manage a MDA-led program. 159 staff attended
relevant courses, 331 were trained in customer care, and HIV/AIDS awareness, and 185 in e-
office applications. POPSM was able to reduce its reliance on technical assistants from 15 in
2008 to 11 in 2010 and just 4 in 2012.

POPSM was also reorganized. A Planning Division, Human Capital Division, and ICT unit were
established; others were restructured or elevated. The revised structure was intended to
accommodate new mandates and responsibilities including HCMIS, e-government and M&E.




                                                60
MDAs were monitored for their accomplishment of PRAP activities within the targeted
timeframes. The baseline figure for this indicator was 60% in 2009/10. The target was 65% in
2012/13, but actual achievement was 93%.

3.2 Improve the Governance to Achieve Greater Efficiency and Accountability

Over the period there were 78 meetings of the Program Implementation Review committee, 7 out
of 10 Program Management Committee meetings, and 9 of 15 Program Management Sub-
Committee meetings. With development partners there were 4 supervision missions and the mid-
term review. After restructuring POPSM was able to keep the results matrix updated in timely
fashion.

However, planned biannual stakeholder meetings were never organized.

3.3 Enhance Coordination among Cross Cutting Reforms

After creation of the Reforms Coordination Unit in the Office of the Chief Secretary, core reform
common procedures were written up. Reform budgets were integrated into the regular
government budget system. Quarterly meetings were held at Permanent Secretary level and
another set at reform coordinators level. The intention was to exchange information and find
solutions to problems, avoid overlaps and gaps, and exploit synergies.

3.4 Strengthen the Awareness, Participation and Commitment to PSRP at all Levels

Awareness sessions were organized in the Office of the President and to present the program to
Members of Parliament. The President himself launched PSRP II and was instrumental in
making sure that the government embraced e-government and the HCMIS as modern mechanisms
for service delivery.

3.5 Manage and Administer PSRP in Compliance with Established Procedures

Monitoring included two annual reports on demand-led activities (before restructuring) and four
annual reports on supply-led activities, as well as 18 quarterly reports.

Special surveys were carried out to update the results matrix. These were: (i) Service Delivery
Survey (2009); (ii) Independent Management Study of (2009, 2011, 2012); (iii) Policy
Effectiveness study (2009); (iv) Provision of Information to the Public (2010, 2012); (v) A
Comparative Salary Survey (2012); (vi) Evaluation of Executive Agencies (2011); (viii) Social
Marketing Research (2012); Ethical Conduct in the Tanzania Public Service: A Case of the
Ministry of Lands, Housing and Human Settlements Development (2012).

Procurement and financial management were carried out in accordance with Tanzanian and
World Bank standards and procedures.

3.6 Strengthen the Support to Public Service Reform in Zanzibar

PRAP gave financial and technical support for the formulation of the Zanzibar PSRP starting in
2008.

Results were as follows: (i) approval of the Public Service Management Policy; (ii) Enactment of
the Public Service Act; (iii) draft Public Service Management Regulations; (iv) development of


                                               61
the President’s Office-Public Service and Good Governance (PO-PSGG’s) Capacity
Development Plan; and (v) draft framework for re-defining Role and Scope of the Zanzibar
government; and (vi) the establishment of a Centralized Human Resource Database.

Seminars were conducted for senior management in 5 pilot ministries and public servants of
different cadres and professions attended training in leadership, public administration, reforms
and records management. The functional analysis of all 16 ministries of the Zanzibar government
was conducted which suggested rationalization of structures.

Records management systems were installed and mobile shelves procured in five MDAs. Their
recovery time for subject records has fallen from 27 to 10 minutes.

KRA 4: Accountability and Responsiveness to the Public

4.1 Strengthen Demand Side of Accountability for Service Delivery

The idea was to strengthen feedback from the public. Guidelines for information education and
communication (IEC) were developed. TV and radio were used and printed materials distributed.
Eleven MDAs were supported including staff training to develop and use IEC strategies. There is
a Public Service Week. People at the various events not only requested and received information
but also gave feedback to the government on service standards. Also 330 grievance cases were
initiated at public service exhibitions and dealt with. There were also outreach events at 37 LGAs
between 20009 and 2011 which surfaced 537 questions.

An indicator of the public perception of public servants with integrity uses the Transparency
International Index for Tanzania. This bottomed at 2.6 in 2009/10 and rose to 3.5 in 2012/13.

Engagement with non-state actors has been undertaken cautiously, as a new area outside the
conventional way of government administration, but guidelines for engaging NSAs became
effective in 2011.




                                               62
4.2 Channels for Feedback from Citizens and Clients

Guidelines on complaints handling mechanism were adopted and disseminated to 78 public
service institutions. Complaints handling offices were established and officers appointed.
Typically there is a desk near the front door of each institution. From a baseline of zero in
2010/11 now a CHM is operational in 78 MDAs, far exceeding the project target of 15 for this
indicator for 2012/13.

There was a big improvement in citizens’ access to information from the government from 37%
of requests met in 2004/05 to 71% in 2009/10 and a further improvement to a plateau of 78%
since 2011/12.

4.3 Capacity of Ethics Oversight and Compliance Institutions

Tanzania’s four ethics-concerned institutions have some overlapping functions.             Some
rationalization of functions, capacity strengthening, training, and information exchange meetings
took place before restructuring. A curriculum for secondary schools was drafted. Higher
learning institutions have incorporated ethics in their curriculum.

4.4 Ethical Conduct in the Public Service

A compliance framework promoting discipline of public servants was put in place to monitor
compliance with a Code of Ethics and Conduct for Public Service. It was rolled out in 15 MDAs.
A social marketing strategy for enhancing ethical conduct was piloted in one ministry since 2012.

To promote ethics to citizens, fora were held with 24 NGOs, some of whom developed an Ethics
Manual with their own funds for raising citizen awareness.

POPSM assessed ethics compliance using the indicator of number of cases resolved out of those
started in select MDAs as a consequence of violations of the code of ethics. The baseline value
was 53% in 2009/10 and reached 90% in 2012/13.

Some 198 officials received training in performance contract management.

Annex 5. Beneficiary Survey Results

No formal survey of beneficiaries was undertaken for the reason that a number of the DO (citizen
satisfaction with services) and Intermediate Indicators (integrity of public servants) themselves are
survey based. However a focus group discussion was convened on March 20, 2013 from 2.00 to
5.00 PM to assess the impact of the scheme for granting scholarships to women in public service
for undertaking a Master’s Degree in which ten beneficiaries of the program participated. The
context of this program is that career progression in the public service is contingent on having a
minimum academic qualification at the Masters level which becomes a significant barrier for those
women who started working without the degree.

The procedure was an open ended discussion with limited facilitation around the issues for their
motivation in availing of this opportunity; their experiences and challenges; the outcomes and any
advice they had to offer.

The Participants shared that the program benefits those who have the thirst to succeed in their
professions. They had come to know about the program through multiple sources; friends, mentors,
colleagues, advertisement and government circular. All the women present had qualified from
Tanzanian Universities as per the terms of the scholarship.

All the recipients had been promoted within three years of earning their masters degrees. Some
had been given increased responsibilities and/or challenging assignments. Some had assumed
leadership positions, e.g. school principal. Most had received a great deal of support from their
families (one woman spoke of how her husband took over the household work entirely to free her
to pursue her studies), their work associates and their bosses. Inevitably some had had adverse
experiences where colleagues who were friends earlier became distant since they perceived the
student to have outgrown them intellectually. In one case the person was treated very adversely by
her immediate boss on her return to the office with the degree and she had to seek a transfer.

All agreed that the added qualification had added to the respect they got at home:
- husbands had more respect for them and shared decision-making
- husbands had been supportive while they were studying, although some recipients had a lot of
home duties too
- some had been able to send their children to good schools
- one had been able to buy a car.

The recipients saw several benefits in other areas and for other persons and groups:

- they were role models for children and for young women students
- some were acting as mentors to other women
- they encouraged other women to combine work and/or household duties with studying
- they brought benefits to their communities by participating in NGOs and CSOs, often in
leadership positions

They hoped that affirmative action programs such as this one would continue.

Annex 6. Stakeholder Workshop Report and Results

A focus group meeting with stakeholders consisting of academics, former POPSM staff,
consultants, members of important think tanks/foundations and Development Partners was held on
March 22, 2013 from 10.30 AM to 1.00 PM.

The list of participants is attached.

The structure of the interaction was free flowing with selective facilitation to elicit discussion on
the perceived objectives of the public service reform program; the relevance of the approach
adopted; the reasons why the success in the first phase could not be replicated in second phase; the
manner in which the constituency for such reforms can be enlarged particularly amongst
politicians; and thoughts for the future.

Context of PSR

The former Permanent Secretary, POPSM during the first phase of the program was requested to
kick off the discussion by providing the context against which the government considered it
prudent to launch what was admittedly an experiment with high political cost. There was consensus
that the difference between Tanzania today and what prevailed in 1990 prior to the start of the
program was huge. The positive changes over the last two decades were credited to the entire suite
of public sector programs introduced by the government during this period. The public service in
1990 was in a “shambolic state? and bureaucrats were derisively termed thieves and corrupt
managers. Efficiency levels have increased significantly over this period. The capacity of the
public service to deliver services is significantly higher than what it was two decades ago. Most
significantly there is a partnership with the private sector whilst previously the government was in
control not only of the economy but also of the actual production process and engaged extensively
in doing business which is not its comparative advantage. There is however a perceived slowdown
in public sector reform since 2006 and it is no longer given the priority it once had. This is puzzling
because the current government continues to remain focused on service delivery but oddly does not
prioritize making the delivery systems more efficient. The reasons could be “lack of knowledge of
the link between the two?, a desire to be populist or a reaction to the growing political opposition
which sometimes makes it difficult for a democratic government to take politically tough decisions.

Participants agreed that there had been an increase in “demand side pressure? from citizens for
reform but oddly this had made the government less transparent in its dealings rather than inspiring
it to be more open. Government data is less easily available now than it was previously.
Government is more defensive in its reactions to citizen demands for accountability. Many of the
malpractices which had been rooted out in the earlier reform period have reemerged; unreasonable
allowances for senior government officials mostly resident in Dar; the lack of external competition
in appointment of senior officials post 2007 (amendment of the Public Service Act barring external
competition). Some felt that the rules and regulations did not favor innovation and dynamism and
were now in fact hindering speedy implementation of government decisions.

Should the Bank have supported PSRP-II?

Participants were unequivocal that the Bank was right in supporting PSRP-II despite the early
signals of a less supportive environment. Firstly, it is very difficult to read the “tea leaves? and
predict the course of political alignments and strategies. Secondly, participants felt that public
service reform remains a vital need even today and hence the Bank has a role in supporting this

                                                  73
change. Some participants felt the Bank could have adapted faster to the emerging environment
than it did. None of the participants were happy at the Bank decision to not engage in across the
board public service reforms, at least till 2015. They felt that reform is a continuous, long term
process and the Bank must develop the resilience to weather short periods of low responsiveness
and keep its sights on the long haul.

Achievements of PSRP II

Participants were positive about the achievements of the PSRP-II though they were unable to
quantify where it had actually made a difference. This illustrates the low level of linkage between
the PSRP-II activities (which dealt mostly with the back room systems and processes of service
delivery) and improved outcomes in services. Principally participants were happy that the
government had continued to engage in reform and were confident that this would generate
positive externalities. Participants did feel that the government in general is more accountable
today than it was a decade ago and are happy at the trend. Similarly there is a sea change in access
to and the quality of public services today as compared to a decade ago. Participants were also
happy with the process of creating Executive Agencies, which they felt made service delivery
entities functionally independent, commercially oriented and more responsive to clients.
Participants attributed these positive outcomes to the comprehensive reform programs of the
government.

Success Factors for Reform

Participants was asked about their perceptions of how to enhance the pace and impact of reforms.
They responded that reform will fail unless it has the support of the “Big House? (the President).
Reform champions need to be able to be in touch directly with the President to constantly reinforce
their own legitimacy and the credibility of the reform process. They cited this as one key reason
why PSRP-I was so successful. In general reformers must “sell? the reform to politicians by
highlighting how they could benefit from the process. Unless this is done there will be no
constituency for reform. The quality and agenda of the Minister in charge also matters significantly.
Participants also felt that a reform program that does not visibly demonstrate results to the common
man will never have the impetus to counter vested interests that may lose from reforms.

Factors for Unsatisfactory Results/Failure of Reform

Participants cited a number of reasons why reforms could fail; lack of leadership; lack of support
from a dominant beneficiary group; a highly aggressive time frame for what may be a long drawn
out process; insufficient finances; unreasonably high expectations from reform; complicated reform
process without measurable indicators; poor supervision and follow up by the implementing
ministry; lack of buy in from the Minister in charge or the Permanent Secretary; too many
objectives; untargeted activities giving no results to citizens; and poor technical support for reform.

How does Tanzania Compare with Others on Reform Agenda

Participants felt that the reputation of the Tanzanian commitment to reform being among the best in
Africa was well deserved. The Tanzanian public service was assessed as highly qualified and
committed and relatively honest. The problem was felt to be lack of a spirit of leadership and over
emphasis on committee work and collective decisions. Participants contrasted this with Rwanda
where President Kagame uses reform as a mechanism to bring change and also boost his popularity.
Some participants felt that though Tanzania had high “capacity? its “capability? to deliver results
was low due to inadequate systems, processes and motivation. Kenya was cited as
a country with high “capability? but similar “capacity? as Tanzania.

A. EXTRACTS FROM THE BORROWER’S ICR

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This Report is intended to take stock of performance and recorded changes brought about by the
implementation of the Performance Results and Accountability Project (PRAP), which is a five-
year Project that became effective in January 2008. PRAP was approved by the World Bank vide
Project ID: P092898; Credit, Cr. 4363 –TA. Although, the International Development Association
(IDA) component closed on 31st December 2012, the grant from Department for International
Development (DfID) and Canadian International Development Agency (CIDA) will continue to
support PRSP II up to June 2014. The preparation of this report was coordinated by the President’s
Office - Public Service Management (PO-PSM) but involved active consultation with a range of
stakeholder’s.

PRAP was implemented using a basket funding arrangement to support Phase Two of the Public
Service Reform Program (PRSP II). The other partners to the basket are the Government of
Tanzania (GOT), DfID, CIDA and Danish Development Cooperation, (DANIDA). The partners
were to contribute a total of USD 103.8 Million, which was later reduced to USD 86.6 Million as a
result of restructuring by IDA following Mid Term Review (MTR) undertaken in May/June 2010,
and the exchange fluctuations.

MTR resulted in a substantial change of Project Development Objective (PDO) and indicators to
measure the program results as well as abandoning demand-led approach. In this regard, the
assessment of performance is mainly focusing on the restructured PRAP. PDO of restructured
PRAP was reformulated as: “To Enhance Performance Management in Selected Ministries
Departments and Agencies (MDAs) and to Increase their Responsiveness to Clients.? PDO was
to be achieved through the implementation of four Key Result Areas (KRAs): (i) Systems and
Policies to Support Service Delivery; (ii) Human Resource Management, Pay and Incentives,
Leadership and Capacity Development; (iii) Change Management and Reform Co-ordination; and
(iv) Accountability and Responsiveness to the Public.

PRAP implementation achieved significant results. All three PDO level indicators were fully met.
PDO level indicators were designed to measure the following: (i) Satisfaction with Central
Governments Services, (ii) Level of Accuracy of Public Service Profile generated in all MDAs
with Human Capital Management Information System (HCMIS) version 9, and (iii) Complaints
resolved out of received,

                                                76
The revised Results Framework contained 22 indicators for measuring the performance of PRAP at
two levels: (i) PDO level and (ii) Intermediate outcomes. The assessment of results shows that all
three PDO and 19 intermediate outcome indicators were met with some targets having been
surpassed. Financial performance as of November 2012 shows that USD 78.6 million (91 percent)
of the USD 86.6 was expended.

For the five years of implementation, the program received Unqualified Audit Reports from the
National Audit Office (NAO) which is a reflection of effective financial management and
accountability.

Changes recorded through the implementation of PRAP include: (i) institutionalization and
improvement in the use of PMS tool, systems and policies, (ii) reduced duplication of efforts and
enhanced accountability resulted from restructuring and clearly demarcation of functions amongst
various institutions, (iii) improved management of Government records leading to cost and time
serving, as well as decision making, (iv) creation of mechanisms and structures for effective
feedback e.g. Complaints Handling Mechanism (CHM) and improved service delivery through
Business Process Improvements (BPIs), (v) strengthened human resources management and
information systems, (vi) increased pay levels for technical and professional public service
personnel leading to recruitment and retention of personnel in the service as well as (vii) existing
capacity in local training institutions have reduced Government cost in training abroad.

The implementation of PRAP, however, brought up a number of lessons:

   Change of mindset among stakeholders needs to be sustained as they go beyond the installation
of systems;
  Reforms have tended to create huge expectations from both the public servants and general
public and therefore the need to continue to improve the systems for quality delivery of services;


Chapter 8       Lessons Learnt

(i) Political support is critical for effective implementation of a Reform Program. PSRP was
conceived in part, to respond to the political and economic environment that was changing in the
1980s through to the turn of the century. During this period, both the public and politicians were
demanding quality public services. On the economic front, GOT was under pressure to withdraw
from the production of goods and services, paving the way for the markets to operate. The call for
redefinition of the role of the Government was more dominant. The early phase of the PSRP clearly
reflected this political condition, and pressure to the public service to deliver is mainly responsible
for the achievements the program has registered.

(ii) The role of leadership to set and maintain the reform agenda cannot be overemphasized.
Throughout the course of implementation, PSRP continued to enjoy un-interrupted leadership. This
has helped to maintain consistence at the policy and technical levels. In the context of institutional
development an un-interrupted visionary leadership is more effective because it is devoid of
unnecessary ‘learning curves’ that tend to undermine performance of the institutions. PRSP was
able to sail through because of rich institutional strength emanating from the leadership.

(iii) Adequate financial resources have to be made available for smooth implementation of reforms.
Despite hitches here and there, generally resources for the program were made available in time.
Programs, no matter how better designed, are affected by insufficient budgets. PSRP was
exceptional because the Government and DPs committed themselves to the basket that was
relatively well resourced. This was possible because the Government was able to allocate resources

                                                  77
to the program. HCMIS and the Construction of the National Records Centre which form the
landmark of the program were completed because both the Government and DPs provided
sufficient funds for their construction.


iv) Mainstreaming of Reform Program into Government structures and processes, contribute to
effective implementation of reforms. PSRP was effectively implemented because the Government
mainstreamed it into Government operations. This approach, apart from building capacity of the
public servant to implement programs, it enhanced sustainability of the reform agenda. The part
institutionalization of PMS tools such as Strategic Planning, which is now mandatory for MDAs
budget approval, is in part explained on this approach. There are also other tools that were
developed through PSRP such as Client Service Charter, that are being promoted across the public
Service, including those that were excluded following restructuring of PRAP.

(v) These policy level lessons can be supported by the operational lessons that are discussed in the
sections that follow;

(vi) The understanding of the political environment enabled PO-PSM to design and implement the
Program which has gone well. PSRP was implemented in 2000 to-date the first part of which was
well documented in part, when the Government produced the first Annual State of the Public
Service Report in 2004. The Second Reports were produced in 2008 by the Government and World
Bank as the End of the Program Report and the Program Completion Report, respectively. These
documents support the conclusion that a good policy requires a corresponding good
implementation strategy. PRAP achievements (restructuring not withstanding) that were explained
in chapter three are in part explained on a design of the project. The design also involved
mobilization of stakeholders behind the program.


(vii) Reforms are implemented to bring change. Indeed, citizens receive changes with mixed
feelings. PSRP was designed after the Civil Service Reform Program that was mainly focusing on
cost containment. For the majority of the public, and the public servants in particular, reforms were
synonymous to retrenchment! A large part of program design involved stakeholders mobilization
while assuring the public servants were part of the program. It is from this context that the demand
led approach was adopted during the first stage of implementing PRAP. With the reduction of the
scope following restructuring of the program, the ‘appetite’ for reforms within the excluded
institutions was reduced, though they continued to be supported through the supply led activities.
Such a scenario, therefore, calls for flexibility, trust and sustained dialogue on both GOT and DPs
when it comes to program implementation. As PRAP comes to an end, this is a lesson that requires
further exploration in terms allowing the excluded institutions to ‘catch-up’ because they are also
part of the public service. Reforms are long a term endeavor, and both parties; the Development
Partners and the Government may wish to appreciate the longer term interests emanating from the
transformation of the public service, given the changing socio economic conditions.

(viii) At this point, few lessons ought to be brought forward as regards capacity of the public
service to deliver, given the emerging dynamics of the Tanzanian society, and coordination of
reforms. As pointed out earlier on, PSRP/PRAP was designed and implemented as a continuation
of the earlier efforts by the Government to reform the Public Service. During the earlier phases, the
Government relied, to a large extent on consultants, and experts outside the public service. This
was different during the design and implementation of PSRP II/PRAP. It is interesting to note that
the public servants played a leading role in the design and implementation of this program. This
shows that the strategy to mainstream the program into Government operations complimented
efforts for the Government’s capacity to design and implement its own programs. Experience


                                                 78
gained through PRAP should be continued in other programs since it has proved effective in terms
of sustainable capacity development of the public service.

(ix) Other lessons are related to programs coordination. Invariably the management of these reform
programs was through Program Implementation Units (PIUs), essentially detached from
mainstreaming operations of the respective organizational entity (i.e. Ministry). As the Government
embarked on crosscutting reform programs the issues related to reforms contradicting one another
in terms of policies, strategies, and implementation modalities started surfacing. To address those
issues, measures to coordinate the reforms were taken, that involved among others, the creation
Reform Coordination Unit (RCU) under the Chief Secretary in 2008. The location of the Unit in
the Office of the Chief Secretary was intended to provide it leverage and clout in coordinating the
various public sector reforms. This is an important lesson as it made it possible for the Government
to remain focused on reforms. Notwithstanding the progress made, the study findings suggest the
existence of a number of concerns due to the capacity constraints arising in part due to limited
mandate and scope of responsibilities, and the staff complement which is inadequate (in terms of
numbers and skill-mix) to effectively undertake its functions

(x) The lessons above generated some challenges that create opportunities for further
improvements. This is because, much as IDA component of PSRP is closing, with other DPs
window still open to June, 2014, it may not necessarily means that the transformation of the public
service is over. In the next chapter, a snapshot of the future needs is projected to show possible
areas of intervention if the public service is to steer the country through the challenges of the 21st
century.


Executive Summary (continued)

In future the Government will mobilize and invest resources in carefully selected reform
interventions with an objective of ensuring consolidation, gradual coverage of reforms
interventions to public service institutions as well as quality implementation and sustainability of
reform innovations such as HCMIS and BPIs; just to mention a few.

Proposed areas for investment will be those with high potential to develop capacity of public
institutions and personnel to deliver quality services to the public, expand use of Information
Communication Technology (ICT) in service delivery, expand involvement of different stakeholder
groups including people with special needs, creation of ownership of reform activities, and
strengthen capacity for change management, as well as ensuring enforcement of PO-PSM mandate.
The later can be achieved through harmonization of existing laws and legislations, e.g. Executive
Agencies Act Cap 245, laws that govern the management of government records to incorporate
records creation through electronic media.

Much as PRAP has resulted in enhancement of performance management and responsiveness of
service delivery to the public, the gains that have been achieved in terms of installation of systems
and tools as well as change of mindset need to be sustained. Efforts are required to ensure that
these interventions are brought to the highest quality as was anticipated when PSRP was originally
conceived; that is the need for quality improvement cycles’ phase.

B. COMMENTS ON THE IMPLEMENTATION COMPLETION AND RESULTS DRAFT
REPORT, May 20, 2013

1. Introduction

The Government acknowledges receipt of the draft Implementation Completion and Results Report
(ICR) report which has provided us with the Consultant’s perceptions and comments on the
Performance Results and Accountability Project (PRAP) implementation performance during the
period under review. The Government has gone through the report and wish to provide general and
specific comments in relation to the draft ICR.
Notwithstanding comments that will be given, the report has provided important information on
progress made during implementation of the Performance Results and Accountability Project
(PRAP) between 2008 and 2012.

2. General Comments of the Report

2.1. Context of the report.

The context in which the report was prepared as detailed from paragraph 1 to 41 does not influence
or support some of the conclusions that the Consultant has made. While the Consultant seems to
have understood the circumstances that led to adoption of PSRPII/PRAP the conclusions have not
taken these circumstances into account. The Consultant's analysis focused on two distinct reform
interventions, that is 'pre and post' restructuring periods to be independent of each other rather than
mutually reinforcing. Such an approach led the consultant to reach some conclusions that have no
clear evidence in terms of levels of performance, some of which will be raised under the specific
comments part of this presentation.

2.2. Consultant Doubt on Positive Performance in some areas

While we acknowledge consultant affirmative views on areas of poor performance, the
Government is concerned that in some areas where the project scored high the consultant was
doubtful. Such a stance might have influenced negatively the assessment of PRAP. (e.g. paragraph
89)

2.3. List of Government Officials interviewed.

There is no acknowledgement of the names of government officials that the Consultant met,
therefore it is not clear if their views are reflected in the report.

3. Specific Comments on the Report

Specific comments are:-

3.1. Language Use

In some assessment cases it was noted that the language used entrench perception that the borrower
is non achiever. Example in some part in the report it has been indicated the performance achieved
by the Government and results outcome were incidental. The language used can be interpreted as
discounting Government performance resulting from reform interventions.

3.2. Areas of Misinterpretation

3.2.1. Shift of Government focus

                                                  80
It is noted on page 11 paragraph 42 of the report that “the focus of reform shifted from President's
Office, Public Service Management (POPSM) to Prime Minister's Office, Regional
Administration and Local Government (PMORALG), which was in the lead in coordinating
private investment and managing reform at the local government level. POPSM was no longer in
the privileged position of working closely with the President’s Office (a privilege it enjoyed
earlier since the then Permanent Secretary had worked closely with the former President Mkapa
(1995-2005)?. The Government wishes to put in records that the focus of the Public Service
Reforms has never shifted from PO-PSM to PMORALG. This is because at any point in time the
Government has been implementing both cross cutting reforms and sectoral reforms whose aim is
to improve service delivery and contribute to the economic growth. In addition, each of these
programmes has its own goals, governance framework and funding mechanisms and is being
implemented simultaneously. While PO-PSM has been implementing PSRP, PMO-RALG has been
overseeing implementation of the Local Government Reform Programme. It should also be noted
that the mandate of coordinating “private sector investment? is vested in the Prime Minister’s
Office and not PMO-RALG.
The Consultant should understand that POPSM has continuously enjoyed working closely with the
President in realization of reform agenda. The President inaugurated PSRP II in February 2008,
established the Reforms Coordination Unit (RCU) under the Chief Secretary’s Office who is also
the Head of the Public Service and Secretary to the Cabinet. The President has been personally
monitoring implementation of the reforms including PSRP II of which visited PO-PSM twice
during the period. On regular basis he has been convening meeting with PO-PSM management
team to get status report on the implementation of the programme.
The commitment of the President to ensure the programme is successful is shown in his direct
intervention in respect to acquisition of the Human Capital Management hardware and software
when the Bank had refused to fund the project. The Government would like to reiterate its
commitment to reform agenda contrary to the opinion raised in Paragraph 129 page 30. This
commitment is demonstrated in the budget guidelines which require each Ministry to set aside
funds for the implementation of reforms.

3.2.2. Big Results Now Vs PSRP II

Paragraph 70 of the report concludes that “The BRN approach appears to leave aside the public
sector reform programs pursued for the last decade. POPSM is not in the driver’s seat nor
greatly represented in the exercise, which currently demands full-time participation of large
numbers of personnel. Nevertheless it does have members in the important committees
overseeing BRN?. With such a conclusion, the Consultant does not appreciate the impact of PSRP
II interventions will have in the realization of Big Results Now. Through PSRPII, the Government
installed management and accountability systems upon which BRN goals would be anchored. BRN
will use management systems and tools developed under PSRP. In terms of POPSM participation
in the formulation of BRN, it is evident that PO-PSM is represented in the Steering Committee,
Technical Committee and it participated in the Labs to provide technical inputs on human resource
management and performance management systems
.
3.2.3. Name of the President

Paragraph 24 the name of the President should read Jakaya Mrisho Kikwete not Kiewit
.
3.2.4. Scope of the Programme

It is noted on page 11, Paragraph 44 (2) the author states that the “Unfortunately, the government
at some point took the political decision to expand such activities under the program from the
initial 25 Ministries to all of the MDAs (potentially about a hundred)? The Government would

                                                81
like to put on record that the decision to expand the scope of the project to 90 MDAs, more than
original 67 MDAs, was based on agreement between the Development Partners (the Bank
inclusive) and the Government within the programme governance framework. At no point in time
political decision were used to influence the scope and expansion of the programme. Further the
records available show that the demand-led interventions were implemented in 67 MDAs and not
90 MDAs.

3.2.5. Training on HCMIS

Paragraph 86 the number of HROs trained on HCMIS was 1034 and not 700. Likewise the scope of
training covered 13 HR business modules and not 7 as stated in the report.




                                              82
3.2.6. Definition of baseline value

Paragraph 94, the last two sentences have gone beyond the agreed definition of the baseline value.
Therefore we propose the last two sentences from “However …. to as of 2011/2012? should be
dropped. Moreover the achieved of 82% was reached six month before the end of PRAP which is
96.47% of the original target of 85%. It is thus unfair to rate that the target was not achieved.

3.2.7. Pace and Disbursement of funds

Paragraph 102. While we acknowledge that there were challenges at the beginning of programme
implementation the government is of opinion that had a learning curve been provided for the
implementation of demand led approach, the programme would have recorded better results. The
government does not agree that the programme had gone through “rocky and somewhat wasteful
start? during pre-restructuring. Furthermore, the funds disbursed pre-restructuring was also used
for post-restructuring period. Records show that by the time of midterm review, around 47% of the
original budget had been expended and not two thirds (66.67%) as explained. The consultant
should not have used the post restructuring revised budget to make this judgment; instead this
period served as a learning curve and foundation for the post restructuring performance.

3.2.8. Rating Scheme

The rating scheme is not clear in that while the scale used to put the score point from one to six, it
would seem performance in some of the areas exceeded the weighting value given in the
assessment. For example table appearing below paragraph 117 where rating like 'substantial, and
high' are used. this raises the issues of consistence in terms of values allocated in each performance
indicator. The Consultant is advised to clarify the assessment criteria used or alternatively use the
criteria agreed during the design of the programme.


3.2.9. Rating Anomalies

Page 28 paragraph 117 rating for the two periods has implied ratings with different nomenclature
that are hard to correlate and integrate to arrive and make sense of the overall project outcome

Paragraph 123. It should be noted the responsibility of managing payroll is the responsibility of

Annex 8. Comments of Cofinanciers and Other Partners/Stakeholders

Comments of CIDA(Canada). Email dated May 21, 2013

The report captures some (but not all) of the PSRP story. A few general comments ...

1. Assessment of the project results and performance of key stakeholders - the
methodology used to rate/measure overall programme performance, based on the
disbursement of IDA funding, is problematic as it does not allow the assessment to capture
the final (which are the ultimate / key) results - but instead over-emphasizes the early
(lesser) results. We realize that this is standard Bank methodology - and we may not be
able to adjust it for the purposes of this ICR - but wanted to flag a concern about this
methodology as it does not allow the Report to recognize the final results.

2. Comprehensiveness of the report - the report has captured the overall design and
implementation context before and after restructuring, as well as many of the
issues/challenges that came with it. There could be more mention of some of the positive
results that are not directly and explicitly linked to the Results Framework and Monitoring
Indicators. The POPSM progress reports have quite a lot of information demonstrating
positive results which could feature more prominently in the report in order to tell the
PSRP story accurately.

3. Other comments - it might be worth mentioning that the Bank (and other DPs) played a
hands-on role in (not) approving certain elements of work plans and, in some years,
imposing ceilings on donor funding. As a result, some activities and even whole
components of the Program were cut or scaled back. Moreover, as more than a year was
lost during the restructuring phase, DFID and CIDA are continuing to support PSRP for a
further year. The Bank, however, is assessing project results at the original project
deadline (not taking the restructuring phase into consideration).

We hope the final draft will take into account these issues to make a comprehensive report.
