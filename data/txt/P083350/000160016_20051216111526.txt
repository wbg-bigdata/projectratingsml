A. STRATEGICCONTEXTAND RATIONALE

1. Country and sector issues
Niger is one o f the world's poorest countries. It has an annual gross national income (GNI) per
capita o f only US$ 200, and an average life expectancy o f 47 years. Niger has a population o f 11.06
million (2001), 80 percent o f whom live inrural areas. With a contraceptive prevalence rate (CPR)
o f 4 percent and a total fertility rate (TFR) o f 7.5 children per woman, Niger has one o f the highest
population growth rates in the world (3.1 percent). The results o f the last Demographic Health
Survey (DHS) carried out in 1998 showed a modern contraceptive prevalence rate o f only 2.1
percent in rural areas as opposed to 18.6 percent in urban areas. According to United Nations'
projection in 2002, under optimistic assumptions (3.5 children per woman in 2050), Niger's
population will reach 53 million and will be the second highest populated West African country
after Nigeria. As a Heavily IndebtedPoor Country (HIPC), Niger must rapidly fulfill several human
development targets set out in the 2002 Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP) to remain on
track for the debt initiative, and make meaningful progress toward achieving the Millennium
Development Goals (MDGs).

According to the country status report (CSR) on health prepared in 2002, some improvement has
been made over the last 10 years, but Niger's human development record remains poor. With the
exception o f HIV/AIDS affecting less than 1 percent o f the population*, Niger's health outcomes
are modest compared to other Sub-Saharan African (SSA) countries: child mortality i s 274 per
1,000, one o f the highest rates in the region; chronic child malnutrition rates approach 50 percent;
and maternal mortality averages 700 per 100,000 live births (see selected indicators for Niger and
other countries intable 1 and figure 1, Annex 1).

 (b)       Low quality and limited supply o f basic health services: Despite the pressing need to fight
 major causes o f disease, disability, and premature death, the most cost-effective interventions are

 physical and financial access barriers but also by the number o f qualified medical staff per
  inhabitant and in the health facilities. InNiger, this situation can be partly attributed to the unequal

  equipment, drugs and supplies is an important factor in both quality and low utilization o f basic
  services.

(e)     Insufficient capacity for effective overall sector management:       One o f the major and

Financial and Technical Partners (FTP) has beencarried out on a bilateral basis.

(0      Poor performance o f priority public health programs: Although there are numerous health
programs in Niger, the level o f performance i s often wanting. A major issue which needs to be
addressed i s the "verti'calization" o f programs which leads to serious inefficiencies and frequent
absence o f staff from the workplace because of disparate training sessions among other factors. The
leading causes o f mortality for children under five are measles, malaria, diarrhea, malnutrition, and
respiratory diseases. Malnutrition i s very prevalent as well as tropical communicable and parasitic
diseases. The severe food crisis o f 2005 has worsened the nutrition situation for children and
women. The very low prevalence o f modern contraceptives and the high maternal and neonatal
mortality requires that particular emphasis be placed on the reproductive health program in order to
increase the effectiveness o f essential health services. Malaria also remains a major public health
problem. According to baseline data collected in 2002, 30 percent o f morbidity and 52 percent o f
children's deaths inhealth centers are attributable to malaria. An average o f 850,000 cases per year
has beenrecordedby the health system.

(g)     Narrow focus o f community participation: Community participation in Niger, as in many
other African countries, emerged in the early 199Os, focusing on ways o f involving communities
more effectively in decision-making and the management o f their health, mainly at the primary
health care level. To date, community participation has translated largely into cost-recovery
programs and drug management, with little emphasis on empowerment o f community health
management committees for more decisive role in the decision-making process at the health center
level, Such narrow focus has had negative effects on the functionality o f the health committees,
their management, and their relationship with others actors and the overall involvement o f
communities on broader health concerns.

2. Rationale for Bank Involvement
The proposedproject objectives are in line with Niger's latest Country Assistance Strategy - (CAS,
January 21,2003). The Institutional Strengthening and Health Sector Support Project ("ISHSSP" or
the "Project") will respond to the CAS pillars for: (a) improvement in access o f the poor to quality
social services, and (b) strengthening institutional and individual capacity within and outside the
Government.

Inthe context ofthe Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS), andas mentioned inthe CAS, the Bank and
the Government share the view that, in order to maximize its development impact, a progressive
shift towards consolidated programmatic lending (Le. DPLs) will be the preferred lending



                                                   6

instrument.The Government sees the long history and credibility o f the Bank inNiger as essential
to continuedprogress on the health reform agenda.

The Niger' CAS directly targets human development deficiencies and vulnerabilities that result
from them. It seek to improve the delivery o f basic education (Cr.3812 and H060-NIR); to intensify
the fight against HIV/AIDS (H031-NIR), to support the Government in developing and
implementing an appropriate strategy on population in 2006; and to make water infrastructure
accessible to the poor (Cr.35050).

The International Development Association (IDA) will support the implementation o f the NHDP
(Plan de De`veloppement Sanitaire 2005-2009), with a focus on policy dialogue and areas o f
comparative advantage where IDA can best exercise its leverage, experience, and expertise. At the
same time, the IDA support will be flexible enough in order to be responsive to evolving
opportunities and constraints throughout the life o f the program.

The Project will be the thirdoperation inthe health sector inNiger. The first project (Health Project,
1986-1995; US$27.8 million, Credit 1668-NIR) supported improvement o f health sector
performance. The second project closed in December 2003 (Health Sector Development Project,
US$40 million, Credit 2915-NIR), contributed to implementation o f the NHDP (1994-2000). The
major focus o f these two projects centered on the initiation o f key sector reforms in the following
areas: (a) establishing mechanisms for investment planning and cost recovery; (b) increasing
attention to population, nutrition, maternal and child health care, and control o f communicable
diseases`including malaria and diarrhea; (c) training o f health personnel; (d) improving quality and
coverage o f basic health services, with particular focus on establishing a health district system; and
(e) enhancing availability and affordability o f essential generic drugs through restructuring and
strengtheningthe pharmaceutical sector. Despite the efforts, however, the final outcomes of these
projects were considered unsatisfactory because, primarily, they have failed to effectively address
key systemic issues such as institutional capacity andhumanresources.

This new Project will thus be different from the previous two because it will place significant
emphasis on strengtheningM O H capacity at all levels, which was never done previously and was
identified as a key factor for failure inthe sector. Inaddition, the new project is unique because, for
the first time in Niger, donors have agreed to work jointly, through a Sector-Wide Approach
(SWAP). This will include joint approach on project preparation, implementation and to carry out
joint annual reviews of work plans and sector performance (including expenditures and budget)
which will be monitored based on jointly derived Performance indicators. This approach will
rationalize investments (provide justification for interventions), harmonize donor inputs and
maintain the focus on outcomes and results ina context o f poverty reduction. When (i)     capacity has
improved and (ii)  the national systems are satisfactory to all the development partners working in
the health sector, focus will be placed on improved harmonization o f all financial procedures,
including those related to financial management and procurement, as a step towards sector budget
support or eventual full budget support.

IDA'Scomparative advantage: The Bank has been a strong supporter o f SWAP in this sector in
several African and Asian countries with generally good results. It would therefore be appropriate
for the bank to participate in this process in Niger by providing technical input and helping bridge
the noted financial  gaps in the sector. The Government and other development partners have
strongly supported the initiative and are relying on the Bank's experience to help buildthe program.

3. Higher level objectivesto whichthe Projectcontributes
Improving social sector perfonnance i s one o f the main goals o f the country's PRS. In the health
sector, this has been translated into a broad 1O-year strategic health sector Development Framework
(Cadre stratkgique pour le dkveloppement du secteur de la santk, 2002-2011). The NHDP and its
operational Five years work plan (Plan de travail Quinquennal- PTQ) reflect the main orientations
o f this framework. The NHDP integrates the institutional aspects, the marginal budgeting for
bottlenecks (MBB) approach (See Annex 4 for MBB definition), as well as comments from regional
and district staff following the national workshop in September 2004. This document was revised
and translated into a five years work plan (PTQ) with technical assistance hiredby the Government
and finalized in August 2005. The PTQ will, in turn, serve as the basis for the preparation o f the
Annual Actions Plan (AAP) at the central, regional, and district levels.

Poverty focus: The overall objective o f the NHDP, 2005-2009, i s to contribute to the reduction o f
maternal and child mortality through improvements in efficiency and quality o f care in the health
system. Maternal and child mortality disproportionately affects the poor inNiger. The NHDP lists
the following specific objectives: (i) increase access and the utilization by the population of quality
health services; (ii)  strengthen reproductive health activities; (iii) reinforce the decentralization
process in health; (iv) promote greater community participation in health; (v) improve management
and strengthen institutional capacities within the MOH; (vi) overcome qualitative and quantitative
deficits in health stafi (vii) improve effectiveness and efficiency of health infrastructure and health
inputs made available to health providers; and (viii) improve availability of essential drugs and
quality medical inputs in healthfacilities. The ISHSSP design provides an opportunity for efficient
and equitable utilization o f health resources which in turn reinforces provision o f services to the
poor and underserved. Inherent in the structure i s an explicit focus on equity and poverty reduction.

Improved governance and decentralization: The ISHSSP will be implemented in a context o f
relative overall political stability and improvement in such key areas as public financial
management, public procurement (Public ExpenditureManagement and Financial Accountability -
PEMFAR, PEAC, and PERC) as well as the decentralization process. Currently, a process o f
decentralizatioddeconcentration     i s  underway,   including a redefinition o f administrative
demarcations. Local elections were organized in July 2004, and gave way to the beginning o f
autonomous management bodies o f territorial communities. It i s expected that this process will
strengthengovernance andwill play a crucial role inthe development o fthe health sector.

1. Lendinginstrument
        The ISHSSP will be financed through a Sector Investment and Maintenance Loan (SIM) o f
an amount equivalent to US$35.0 million over a five-year period. The objective o f the IHSSP i s to
support the Government's Program to improve efficiency and quality of care in the health system,
and contribute to the reduction o f maternal and child mortality through:

        a. the strengthening o f human resources development and management;
        b. the expansion o f services and delivery o fMinimumHealthPackage (MHP); and
        c. the strengtheningo f governance and institutional capacity.

The proposed Project detailed in Annex 4 will support the strategies spelled out inthe NHDP, the
Government's main vehicle for health sector policy, and which will be implemented with the
support o f donors and non-governmental organizations (NGOs). IDA financing shall be pooled with
the contributions from the FrenchDevelopment Agency (AFD).

The objectives and outcomes for the project are exclusively drawn from the Niger's NHDP.
However, particular attention will be given to certain key aspects o f the NHDP, such as institutional
reforms (Annex 16), reproductive health (Annex 19), and malaria (Annex 20).

2. ProjectObjective and Sequence
The long-term objective o f the sector program (which is being supported by the World Bank and
FTP), i s to establish - through a SWAP - a health delivery system responsive to the needs o f the
people o f Niger and contributing to the reduction o f maternal and child mortality, by improving the
sector capacity, effectiveness and efficiency through the provision o f a minimum package o f
essential health services targeted at the poor, women and children.

The NHDP will support the reform o f the sector. The Government i s planning a series o f policy,
organizational and management changes to create capacity for implementing the NHDP. These
changes are consistent with the policy of: (i)   institutional changes (Structural organization, Human
resource management, Financial management); (ii)         Cost recovery scheme; (iii)  Essential generic
drugs availability and affordability; and iv) decentralization(performance based contract).

Sequerzce: Some key principles (detailed in Annex 4) will guide the overall implementation o f the
Project:
 (a) The MTEF will give priority to improvement o f existing health facilities before expanding the
     system. This means that personnel and operating costs will be allocated in priority to those
     facilities whose performance i s poor; consequently, any extension o f the system will occur
     using a phased approach, taking this principle into account. The MTEF will reflect the
     objectives and priorities agreed upon inthe NHDP ;
 (b) The Government will commit to allocating resources inpriority to poor and underserved areas,
     within the context ofthe PRS ;
 (c) Implementation of a new cost-recovery system will include exemption policies to facilitate
     access o f the poor to health services. It has been agreed that cost-recovery schemes should not
     mean the withdrawal o f the Government from the financing o f health facilities and services ;
     and
 (d) Resources will be transferred to implementingentities usingperformance-based contracts.

The project will support the Government's efforts to achieve the NHDP's objective (see section A.
3) and, more specifically, the M O H in implementing an AAP through financing a portion o f the
combined Government and external assistance budgets for policy development and operational
program activities. This financing will be assessed in September each year, before the adoption of
the budget by the Parliament. The IDA contribution will be approved by the Country Director based
on recommendations o f the Bank team resulting from joint assessment with the Government, FTP,
andother key stakeholders before the end o fDecember.

During the first two years o f NHDP implementation, priority will be given to making existing
health facilities operational to improve the level o f their utilization. The remaining period will be
usedto extendthe scope o f outreach activities, to deliver the minimumpackage o f essential health

   services, and to implement financial protection schemes. Additional emphasis will be given to the
   enhancement o f quality o f health services at every level.

  4. ProjectComponents
  The proposed project (see Annex 4 for a detailed description) will focus on addressing major
  institutional and organizational bottlenecks which could prevent achievement o f the development
  objectives by supporting the SWAP in the implementation o f the NHDP through the following
  strategic components:

       a) Strengthen human resource development and management strategy and implementation
            (Planning,         Production,        Deployment,          Performance          management,     Regulation and
            Administration);
       b) Support expansion and quality of health care through delivery of minimumhealth package
            by : i)improving essential drugs and medical supplies management; ii)strengthening
            delivery o f major public health programs (including reproductive health, malaria and
            nutrition) and improve quality o f services, and iii)improving demand, accessibility and
            utilization o f MHP;
       c) Strengthen governance and institutional capacity o f the sector by: i)strengthen the
            management o f the health system and institutional capacity; ii)ensuring strong management,
            adequate and equitable financing; iii)strengthening community participation, and;                           iv)
            putting in place an effective M&E mechanism to track program performance and ensure
            good donor coordination.

  Component 1 - Human resource development and management: This component will look
  comprehensively at the HR situation in Niger taking into account both the short and long term
  perspectives. Three things that stand in the HR crisis are : i)the current small number o f skilled
  staff and their inequitable distribution, ii)the constrained training capacity in the country and its
  inability to cope with the escalating demand and, iii)the public sector's inability to retain skilled
  staff. The private sector in Niger, unlike elsewhere in Africa, i s relatively small and lacks the
  absorptive capacity to take in dissatisfied public sector employees. Most disaffected health workers
  therefore either abandon medical practice or migrate to other countries, resultingin a net loss to the
  sector or country. Greater attraction and retention o f skilled staff has to be assured by reviewing
  working conditions and the overall remuneration package, within the context o f improved personnel
  policies. Matching staff posts with workload and developing performance based management
  systems will be critical to the delivery o f the MHP and reduction o f maternal and child mortality.
  Technical assistance will be needed to strengthen the HR management systems, including HR
  information systems and longer term strategies for the sector.
  Component 2 - Expansion o f services and delivery o f Minimum Health Package (MHP): The
  objective o f this component i s to increase access to quality essential health package to people in
  Niger, with special attention to effective delivery o f major public health programs that are critical to
  reaction o f maternal and child mortality to the poor and underserved populations. The component

                                                                     11

will also address demand side through community mobilization and address quality o f care issues
by looking into logistics and sustained availability o f essential drugs, medical equipment and
supplies needed for effective delivery of MHP. The content o f the MHP have already been defined.
The package will be introduced in an incremental manner and delivered at each level, building on
existing services and availability o f additional skilled personnel.

Component 3 - Strengthening governance and institutional capacity: The objective o f this
component i s to improve efficiency, effectiveness and accountability o f the health system by
ensuring strong management and fiduciary responsibility in the context o f the planned
decentralization and with effective community participation. An effective and responsive M&E
systemis critical to this component and will therefore be an area o f special focus.

In support of these components, the Project will finance small works, equipment, training,
workshops, pharmaceuticals, technical assistance, and operating costs, through the A A P s , which
will be submitted in draft form to the IDA (and other donors participating in their financing) for
approval.

The NHDP will serve as a policy framework that covers the entirety o f all health-related activities
inNiger. The details of the ISHSSP are expected to change from year to year as reflected inAAPs
and budgets developed by each level o f the health system, but will follow the policy framework of
the MOH and the overall guidance o f the NHDP and the financing plan. The AAP should be revised
ifneeded to achieve sustainability over the medium term particularly given the volatility of donor
funding inNiger. And it will ensure that (a) the high-yieldlow-cost interventions are implemented
as well as the proper treatment protocols, particularly for malaria, emergency obstetric care and
STI/HIV/AIDS; and (b) the financing requirement o f NHDP which indicates that an important
effort of resources mobilization needs to be carried out.

5. Lessonslearnedand reflectedinthe Projectdesign
ISHSSP design and preparation took into account the following lessons learned from the Bank's
performance in Niger, other SWAps in the Africa Region, and Economic Sector Work (ESW)
focusing on population, use o f health services inAfrica, and health financing, among others:

(a)     Expanding coverage needs to be carefully prioritized/designed and driven by both demand
and expected long-term resource availability. Ina resource-constrained environment like Niger, the
SWAP will give priority to improving the function o f existing health facilities as increased
geographic coverage does not necessarily presuppose increased use if proper resources (financial,
human and material) are not made available. Donors have agreed to be very cautious in increasing
recurrent costs by expanding existing facilities and constructing new ones. Outreach activities to
increase access to the minimum package o f essential health services will be encouraged were
appropriate.

(b)     Institutional capacity is a major issue which needs to be assessed and addressed seriously.
Strengthening the capacity at all levels o f the health system i s a prerequisite for the success o f
health sector reform in Niger. The proposed Project will place strong emphasis on identifying
critical institutional changes (adaptation o f MOH's structures, human resources management, and
management o f finance and expenditures) which will be required at each level and stage o f
implementation o f the five-year NHDP to ensure achievement o f its objectives (see Annex 16).


                                                    12

(c)     A SWAP as part of the country's PRSP and with donor-aligned support makes sense in
Niger. Donor participation is crucial to program success as is strong coordination among them,
particularly during the development o f a new approach. To date donors have worked in a rather
independentfashion inNiger.Inpursuingan approach based on SWAPprinciples, the Bank will: (i)
enter agreements and collaboration with donors according to the country's health sector priorities
with common objectives and indicators linked to the MTEF; and (ii)       strengthen M O H capacity to
become a fullpartner inthe PRSC process inthe mediumto long term.

 (d)    Health sector reform and supporting donor interventions need to be driven by results, a
clear set of objectives and performance benchmarks for service delivery. Through the SWAP, this
program intends to focus on content and monitoring o f outcomes, and not just on processes.
Although the two previous IDA-financed projects in the health sector inNiger managed to provide
some inputs for improving sector performance (see paragraph A.2), they failed to make a significant
difference in health outcomes because M O H did not have the capacity to translate the inputs into
outcomes and to rationalize choices in line with core priorities, available resources, and poverty-
reduction objectives. Removing the systemic bottlenecks i s critical for achieving fbndamental
changes. During Project preparation, elaboration o f the MTEF took into account the bottlenecks
which should be overcome to allow achievement o f MDGs. Donors and Government agreed in the
NHDP, including key indicators for joint monitoring and evaluation. In addition, budgetary
transfers to implementing entities, at the regional and district levels will be conducted according to
performance-based contracts which emphasize anticipated results. During the implementation o f
PEAC 11, the Bank financed a preparatory work with selected districts where the performance-based
contracts (PBC) were to be tested using a contract model (Annex 21) used in another sub-Saharan
country, Unfortunately, this work did not proceed due to lack o f government resources. The
modalities o f implementing PBCs, including how resources will flow to those districts have been
reviewed during appraisal and will be detailed inthe manual o f procedures to assist the districts.

(e)     Improving service quality is critical and committed health care staff essential. To create a
spirit of professionalism and motivation among health staff, developing an appropriate incentive
system to attract, maintain, and further develop staff is essential. The proposed Project focuses on
putting in place an integrated human resources reform strategy that will address the production,
training, evaluation, management, and regulation o f health personnel. Implementation o f an
incentive package for personnel to be redeployed to remote areas will be finalized and an agreement
will be reached with the MOEF the second half o f year 2006. The decentralization of budgetary
posts for contractual personnel inthe regions has been agreed by the Government pursuant to decree
dated April 22,2005 (No2005 108/PRN/MSP/LCE).

(f)     Cost-recovery in its current form is problematic as it disempowers and excludes the poor.
While the principle of contribution is very important, it should not constitute a financial barrier to
accessibility to services, nor be a substitute for Government financial responsibility. DuringProject
preparation, particular emphasis was placed on this point and a study has been conducted by M O H
to guide future strategy in this area. The results showed weak community participation and
payment, no protection system for the poorest, mismanagement o f funds, reduction o f the health
budget, availability o fresource inhealth districts account andweak availability o f essential generics
drugs in the country. An agreement on the core principles and the timetable for implementation o f
the results that will guide the review of the cost recovery policy has been reached during
negotiations. The conclusions (Le., adjustments to MOH budget to compensate for the loss o f
revenues generated by the exemption policy) will be reflected into the MTEF. This strategy will

                                                   13

include exemption measures and procedures to ensure that the poor have access to health services.
A final agreement has also been reached during negotiations regarding the provision of modern
contraceptive methods free o f charge, given the fact that Niger has the highest Total Fertility Rate
(TFR) inthe world (7.5 children), coupled with a very low modem contraceptive prevalence rate (4
percent).

(g)     Donors need to significantly co-finance the recurrent costs of Niger's health system so that
the country has at least a chance o f reaching the MDGs. This will require a long-term donor
commitment before the sector becomes sustainable. To create the environment for a reasonable
commitment to be made by donors and to encourage further external support, the health system
needs to be designedto function in a cost-effective manner.

(h)     The omnipresence of the central ministry in the decision-making process is not conducive to
swift action and results in the3eZd. The heavy involvement o f central levels o f the M O H in day-to-
day decision-making does not encourage initiatives, limits ownership for actions by health
personnel and the community, and generates unnecessary delays inthe implementation o f activities.
The proposed Project will assist M O H in redefining its role and responsibilities at the central and
peripheral level, and will strengthen the districts' and communities' capacity to engage actively in
the management and execution of plans. The performance-based contracts that will be promoted
also will create an enabling environment for this transition.

6. Indicationsof borrower andrecipientcommitmentand ownership
The Government o fNiger has beenundertaking major reforms inthe health sector, based on its first
NHDP (1994-2000) and the 1O-year strategic development framework. The current push o f reform
began with the preparation o f the Niger's PRS and the CSR for the health sector in2002. The M O H
set an agenda for reviewing policies and strategies so as i)to decentralize the management o f the
health system by enabling households and communities to identify and solve problems concerning
their health; ii)to decentralize responsibility and resources for service delivery to the districts,
which will be better placed to respond to local needs; iii)to improve access and utilization o f the
health services by reviewing the cost recovery scheme; iv) to scale up the institutional reforms o f
the M O H (including human resource, finance andorganization).

The reforms that Government is proposing are fully supported by the Bank's advocacy o f sector-
wide approaches and have ledto the following understandings: i)IDA would follow Government's
lead in the processes o f sector reform in the context to its NHDP; ii)as many donors as possible
need to collaborate on the AAP, and common implementation arrangements need to be sought over
time; iii)local capacity, rather than long-term foreign assistance, should be strengthened and relied
upon as much as possible; and iv) IDA would follow-up on sector wide development and systemic
issues, playing a role as financier o f last resort (for some activities) as far as i s practicable, so that
IDA funding is fully flexible for an agreed program.

The Government confirmed its commitment to continue the reform process inthe health sector, and
a participatory approach will be followed to ensure involvement o f stakeholders in all stages o f the
NHDP implementation. This will include use o f participatory involvement stakeholders through
information sharing, consultation and collaboration. Stakeholders include MOH, MOEF, PRSP
secretariat, private and public providers, FTP, parliamentary committees, district and regional
officials, civil society andNGOs.


                                                    14

Since the establishment of donor coordination mechanisms in 2004 by the designation o f Belgium
Cooperation as coordinator, M O H became more involved inthe policy dialogue and in discussions
with MOEF. To maintainthis ownership, joint reviewswill be organized twice a year (and open to a
variety o f stakeholders) to assess sector performance and reach agreement on AAPs for the coming
year.

7. Alternativesconsidered
The issue o f a classic investment project versus a broader sector investmentproject was thoroughly
discussed with the Government and donors duringProject identification and preparation. All parties
agreed that implementation i s slow when project activities are scattered and MOH staff are not fully
involved. Having a separate project implementation unit (PIU) does not necessarily facilitate
implementation. The SWAP will improve Government involvement and effectiveness as well as
strong partnership between FTP and MOH. A more comprehensive approach i s useful if (a)
national development policies provide the framework for strategic planning and partnedstakeholder
participation; (b) resource allocation i s linked to objectives and results; and (c) the natural tendency
to duplicate efforts i s reduced through interaction and dialogue. The development o f a SWAP has
demonstrated clearly that appropriate efforts and investments can rapidly build up national capacity
and significantly improve its systems.

Some beneficial aspects o f implementing a SWAP include: (a) allowing the sector to focus on
content and on monitoring outcomes rather than focusing on process alone; (b) orienting
investments to the achievement o f Project objectives rather than on inputs; and (c) forecasting and
designingAAPs to keeppace with improvement o f capacity and availability o fresources.


C. IMPLEMENTATION

1. Arrangementsfor coordinationamongdonors andbetweendonors andthe Government
The identification and preparation o f this Project is the result o fjoint missions o f the Association
and key bilateral and multilateral donors and agencies. This coordinated approach will be
maintained throughout the implementation o f the Project. All donors have welcomed this process
and intendto articulate their interventions around the NHDP (2005-2009) and an MTEF. Donors'
intent is confirmed in a M O U to be entered between the Government and each donor participating
in the financing of the health sector's program o f work, and a Partnership framework signed
betweenGovernment and its FTP involved inthe health sector.

During preparation, technical assistance on key areas was financed jointly by the EU, UNFPA,
UNICEF, WHO, the Belgium Cooperation, the French Cooperation, IDA, and the Government o f
Japan through a PHRD grant. It assisted the MOH in updating the NHDP, including deepening the
analysis on core bottlenecks, in carrying out a detailed institutional and organizational capacity
analysis at all levels of the health sector, elaborating the MTEF/MBB, and reviewing the
Management Information System (MIS).

2. Institutionaland implementationarrangements
To facilitate sustainable institutional development, there will not be a project unit. Instead, MOH's
structures will be developed and strengthened according to their mandates and functions.
Institutional and implementation arrangements for the ISHSSP will take into account the results and
recommendations emanating from the ongoing work on the institutional and organizational changes

                                                    15

inthe MOH, which aim at addressing critical capacity issues through: (a) adapting MOHstructures
at each level to its mandate and functions in the context o f the NHDP; (b) developing a human
resources management system; and (c) improving the management o f public finances and
expenditures o f the health sector.

Implementation will emphasize development o f national health systems and capacity. Common
procedures and implementation mechanisms, according to country-specific guidelines, will be
established progressively and adopted in a move towards program support/budget support as
capacity improves.

The successful implementation o f this SWAP will require a highlevel o f coordination and oversight
capacity within the MOH. As such, overall responsibility will rest with the Secretary General who
will ensure strategic coordination o f all actions under the NHDP. The Secretary General will
represent the MOH when liaising with cross-cutting ministries (Ministry o f Economy and Finance
and Ministry o f Civil Service) and will coordinate the overall functions and responsibilities o f the
technical directorates. The institutional and implementation arrangements will be organized at two
levels interms o f (a) technical implementation, and (b) fiduciary implementation arrangements.

Technical Implementation Arrangements: The Project will adopt an AAP approach that will give
the MOH responsibility for selecting interventions according to priorities set in the NHDP. This
approach will provide flexibility while ensuring accountability within the MOH. Performance-based
agreements will be reached between the region and the central level, and between the districts and
the regions. There will also be performance agreement between training institutions and hospitals
respectively and the central level. Signed agreements will allow for funds to flow to the
decentralized levels (regional and district) placing responsibilities on those levels to ensure efficient
resource management as they carry out their respective AAPs. As MOH capacity at all levels i s
developed over the Project implementation period, the AAP will be adjusted accordingly. During
the transitional period leading to the effective adaptation o f the Ministry's structure, each current
directorate will be responsible for carrying out its respective mandate according to the NHDP (See
description in Annex 6). Technical implementation o f the Project will be assigned to organizational
units within the MOH according to their comparative advantage. Strengthening measures to equip
these units to meet the challenges will be part o f the AAP. The first-year performance-based
agreements between the central and regional level and between the regional and district level, along
with a detailed first-year AAP, has been prepared during appraisal. Details on the mechanism o f
performance-based agreement discussed during appraisal will form a major part o f the Project
implementation manual.

Fiduciary Implementation Arrangements: In view o f MOH's inexperience and limited capacity in
managing fiduciary functions according to Bank procedures and guidelines, these functions will be
carried out with the support o f externally recruited technical assistance with the mandate to transfer
knowledge and capacity to Directorate o f Financial Affairs (DAF) personnel by mid-term. The DAF
will not implement Project activities, but will ensure that the implementing units' fiduciary needs
are adequately met in a timely manner in accordance with agreed-to AAPs. A fiduciary capacity
assessment (procurement and financial management and accounting) o f the DAF has been made and
the key findings and recommendations are attached in Annexes 7 and 8. It i s proposed that the DAF
capacity will be reinforced by the services o f a Financial Controller Specialist, a senior Accountant,
and a Procurement Specialist.

At this stage, however, there are no guarantees that by giving full responsibility to the various
directorates (whether at the central, regional, and district levels and further to institutional reform in
progress), the NHDP, or even the ISHSSP will be implemented smoothly. This will depend greatly
on M O H commitment to move steadily with the changes prior to and during Project
implementation. Hence, the process by which the M O H will adopt institutional changes ("mutations
institutionnelles") as envisaged under this Project will be determining in: (a) allocating Project

structures neededto move successfully toward the necessary institutional changes and establishing

3. Monitoring and evaluation (M&E) of outcomes/results
M&E o f the NHDP was identified as a major challenge during Project preparation. Failure to
address this challenge during the 1994-2000 NHDP resulted in serious inefficiencies and in the
incapacity o f M O H to plan adequately and to evaluate activities. Major issues facing the M&E
system in Niger can be summarized as follows: (a) an M&E system as such simply does not exist
and what exists i s not functional; (b) the structures responsible for M&E lack the capacity to
analyze data collected and inform policy decisions; (c) flow and use o f data collected i s poor; (d)
coordination among international partners i s insufficient; and (e) the participation o f various
regional and district committees inthe decision-making process i s lacking.

Strengthening the function within the M O H will be critical to ensuring that the strategies and
resources neededto implement them are inhand and have an impact on improving health outcomes.
Moreover, this function will serve as a planning and management tool. The M&E system that will
be implemented during the NHDP period will specifically assist in: (a) monitoring progress,
identifyingandresolving problems; and (b) timely assessment o f programs with a view to reorient
them as needed. Another important feature o f the M&E system will be to entrench the financial
monitoring necessary to ensure coherence between the NHDP operational plan and the MTEF.

Monitoring o f performance o f the ISHSSP will be done jointly by Government and development
partners through two mechanisms: (a) monitoring o f the overall health program against the
indicators selected (paragraph 3.2); the monitoring i s currently designed to identify benchmarks on
Project achievements at field level after one year and at mid-termdirectly linkedto the MDGs; and
(b) monitoring o f the coordination and harmonization o f procedures among donors, including
benchmark definitions o f appropriate operational and administrative coordination.

Duringthe first two years o f the ISHSSP, the overall M&E system will pay particular attention to
measuring the key processes intended to ensure Project success, such as access to services,
including availability o f human resources, and the health center functionality. The M&E system
also should provide indirect benefits that contribute to strengthening the technical role and
operational competency o f the National Health Information System (NHIS). Such benefits should
include improving MOH knowledge and capacity for effective coordination and supervision o f
interventions, reinforcing systematic use o f clinical guidelines/protocols, increasing knowledge o f
and responsiveness to community demands, and reinforcing adequate operational coordination
amongst partners promoting appropriate synergies, seeking economy o f scale, and preventing
overlapping or redundancy ingeographical areas.

The NHDP will rely on the creation o f four main committees to strengthenthe M&E system: (a) a
national technical committee; (b) regional health technical committees; (c) broader regional
committees that will include elected officials; and (d) the health district committees. The roles and
composition o f these various committees i s detailed in Annex 17. During Project preparation,

The Project's sustainability, both programmatic and financial is viewed and based on three criteria:

time to materialize.

(a) Government ownership and capacity. Several elements confirm MOH's commitment to and
    responsibility for this NHDP: (i) it took the leadinorganizing donor coordinationby appointing

D. APPRAISAL SUMMARY

1. Economic and financial analyses
Niger's economic performance during these past years indicates that the rural sector remains the
main engine o f growth. Capital expenditure dropped from 8.2 percent o f GDP in2002 to 7.4 percent
in 2003 while current budget expenditure, which stabilized around 11.O percent o f GDP between
2000 and 2002, represented 9.7 percent in 2003. Amounts allocated to the education and health
sectors (not only in the MOH budget) were respectively 14.5 percent and 9.9 percent, compared to
14.3 percent and 9.4 percent in 2002. Public funding o f the health sector i s largely ensured by
external sources, including nearly 95 percent for investmentexpenditure. This highshare o f external
funding inthe main budgeto fthe State, particularly the health sector, contributes to maintaining the
country's high financial dependence on external assistance. There i s a shortage o f funding
resources, especially at the operational level, as reflectedby the concentration o f major expenditures
at the central level to the detriment o f the peripheral or decentralized levels.

The different economic growth scenarios (high growth: average o f 5 percent per annum; reference
growth: 4 percent per annum; low growth: 2 percent per annum) result in varying levels o f
availability o f public resources, especially fiscal resources, which has a direct impact on amounts
allocated to each sector. Health expenditures covered by the Government budget, based on the
scenarios proposed by the macro-economic guidelines note of May 2003 (adjusted from the last
known data), amount to about XOF 53 billion per annum within the period 2005-2009 in the low-


                                                    22

growth scenario, XOF 55 billion in the so-called "reference" or average scenario, and XOF 61
billion inthe highgrowth scenario.

The operational expenditures o f the entire sector are expected to increase significantly in 2005 by
58 percent compared to the 2004 level, and by a fbrther 12.8 percent between 2005 and 2006. The
increase in expenditures resulted from the high increase in operational expenditures at the various
levels and additional expenditures committed in the framework o f the NHDP, as well as the
implementation or intensification o f a series o f sector development programs. The expenditure
profiles translated into costs show the necessity for mobilizing resources required to make the
public health systemoperational. Added to this are high increases in salaries and allowances paid to
health workers.

The economic appraisal undertaken through the NHDP largely considered the financial
sustainability o f the Project. The appraisal providedjustification for increasing resources to health,
shifting resources from investment to the new health centers to primary care and to wage
expenditures. This is intended to boost the functionality of the health services, make the services
more available to the poor, and ensure continued donor funding, particularly as the Project provides
more support at the district level. The appraisal also highlighted the need to better guide community
participation through the cost-recovery system and the private health provision and financing inthe
sector as highlighted in NHDP objectives. Institutional sustainability will be strengthened by
focusing on improved management practices (focusing on human resources and financial
management) and undertaking organizational restructuring to support mainstream organizations and
improve on in-house administrative processes.

The MTEF exercise that took place i s based on the Five years work plan (PTQ) which aims at
supporting eight priority programs. The MTEF i s meant to ensure their total financial coverage if
they are to be finalized and adopted by the Government. These programs are: (i)      increasing access
to, and utilization by, the population of quality health services; (ii)    strengthening reproductive
health activities; (iii)reinforcement of the decentralization process in health; (iv) promotion of
greater community participation in health local policies ;(v) improvement of management and
strengthening institutional capacities within the MOH; (vi) overcome qualitative and quantitative
deficits in health stafi (vii) improvement of effectiveness and eflciency of health infrastructure and
health inputs made available to health providers; and (viii) improvement of the availability of
essential drugs and quality medical supplies in healthfacilities.

A process of harmonization is therefore essential, using the work already completed by the MOH,
so that the content o f the "Budget program 2005-2007" can be homogeneous with the PTQ and not
the other way around. The PTQ must be the reference framework. It is therefore essential, in order
to ensure coherence with the health policy, to ensure that the budget program i s in line with the
PTQ. The PTQ i s at the heart o f the "expenditures area" o f the MTEF, and as such will be costed
according to a new budgetpresented and implementedby program.

Two options are proposed for the implementation o f the health policy as defined and structured.
One option refers to a greater number o f activities, while the other option offers a smaller scenario
more focused on the priorities. The total cost o f the larger scenario i s XOF 372.6 billion for 2005-
2009, with an annual average o f XOF 74.5 billion, while the smaller scenario i s estimated at 339.5
billion o f CFAF, with XOF 67.9 billion in average per year. The financing requirement, o f the order


                                                   23

o f 4 percent o f GNP, indicates an important ceiling effect starting in 2005 for total public
expendituresas for those relatedto the Ministryo fHealth.

In sum, the following three main conclusions can be drawn: (a) Financing of the PTQ under its
large scenario i s uncertain, but not impossible. It requires a conjunction o f variables that are highly
favorable, such as a growth in GNP during the period, donor contribution, and negotiations o f the
Government toward the health sector; (b) On the contrary, financing o f the PTQ under the smaller
scenario seems more likely in the context o f a less-ambitious program as long as the Government's
commitment to the sector remains; (c) Support from the donor community will continue to play a
crucial role, directly and indirectly, in the financing o f health policy, regardless o f the scenario
selected.

In summary it is worth repeating that the calculation related to the resources that are potentially
available does not take into account the issue o f the low rate o f budget execution that must be
improved.

A. Country Background
Niger is a large landlocked country covering an area o f 1.27 million km2,and is 600 km away from the
closest seashore. The population was estimated at 11.06 million in 2001, about one-half o f which i s less than
15 years old. The population i s growing at about 3.1 percent per year which i s above the average for SSA
(2.38). The human resource base is weak with an adult literacy rate o f only 14 percent (9 percent among
women) and a primary school enrollment ratio o f less than 50 percent. The slim natural resource base o f
degrading arable land i s fragile due to low and irregular rainfall and low and declining soil fertility.

Access to basic services i s inadequate inNiger and human development indicators are very poor. Improving
social sector performance i s one o f the Government's main goals. Overall, the Government has made
significant progress in the critical area o f poverty reduction. Putting the country back onto a development
path will require pursuit o f the reform agenda, diversion o f real resources toward private sector development,
and actions to restructure public expenditures towards specific objectives to improve primary school
enrollment and to improve the basic primary health coverage. Pursuit o f the reform agenda resides inNiger's
ability to continue fiscal discipline and aid flows, political stabilization, and the increase o f investments to
rekindlegrowth, especially insatisfying the large needs inthe social sectors.

To improve its Human Resources environment, the Government o f Niger has decided to organize its health

(i). Planning: Theprocess ofplanningmustbeableto answer the followingquestions: Howmanyof

(e)      Insufficient capacity for effective overall sector management. One o f the major and generalized
problems at all levels o f the public health administration is MOH's overall weak institutional management
capacity. As a result o f this, planning and prioritization o f activities i s often undermined. Health care

disease prevention or outreach activities (with the exception o f immunization campaigns). In addition, the
implementation o f vertical health programs for specific issues being managed outside the MOH, such as
HIV/AIDS, has been accompanied by poor o f coordination between the different actors. The coordination
between the MOH and the different financial and technical partners (FTP) has been carried out on a bilateral
basis. Generally, the FTP develop programs      -  together with the Government  - that follow historical relations
with certain regions, or are embedded into their particular mandates. There has been a growing sense,
however, that this approach has intrinsic weaknesses because it does not allow for development o f a global
health sector plan based on a common vision with mutual commitments.

One of the major and generalized problems at all levels o f the public health administration i s MOH's overall
weak institutional management capacity. High turnover rates for Minister and senior staff, lack o f an
information and planning system, and an overly centralized pattern o f decision-making (despite the
decentralization efforts) are some o f the reasons underlyingthe weak institutional capacity. Onthe side o f the
financial management system, effectiveness i s restricted by a number o f weaknesses in areas crucial for a
well-performing and decentralized/deconcentrated health system, namely: (1) budgeting allocation i s based
on past patterns without due consideration o f real needs and equity concerns; (2) insufficiencies exist in the
systemfor allocating funds from the central to lower levels; (3) delays occur inthe effective transfer from the
central to lower administrative levels; (4) weaknesses in the accounting system include a limitedcapacity to
effectively absorb funds, inparticular with regard to the vertical programs.

The inexistence o f a policy for routine maintenance o f buildings and materials generates not only negative
effects on the availability and functionality o f these items      - nonfunctional district hospitals, damage o f

the already scarce resources. Last butnot least, the failure to develop intersectoral strategies has beena major

(f)       Poor Performance of priority public health programs. Although there are numerous health

A planning process at the regional and district level will be launched and will be finalized in the framework

this level for the implementation o f health policies. Various handbooks and procedures o fthe program have
been developed and will be used within the framework o f the NHDP.

The main strategies outlined in the current NHDP and the commitment o f all stakeholders have provided
sufficient basis for IDA to opt for a SWAP for its proposed third investment inthe health sector.

     The MBB approach will help to link resources to effective interventions and to outcomes. "The MBB
     identifies implementation bottlenecks in the health system that should be removed to optimize expected
     health outcomes and then estimates the marginal cost of overcoming those constraints. MBB helps to
     formulate medium-term national or provincial expenditureplans that explicitly link expenditure to health
     Millennium Development Goals, such as the reduction in child and maternal mortality, and optimally
     allocate newly available resources to achieve such targets. It facilitates a process of budgeting for




                                                                       47

government health expenditures that starts by improving allocative eflciency of the newly available
resources, andprovides a basisfor policy dialogue and planning".4

Niger began the exercise o f MBB with the support o f UNICEF. A team o f representatives from M O H and
MOEF was initiated into the process in collaborationwith key partners WHO, WB, UNFPA, and Belgian
Cooperation. This first exercise made it possible to develop a description o f indicators and the source of
data necessary to identify program bottlenecks relating to child survival. The exercise, which i s at a
beginning stage, will make it possible for all actors to prepare their own work plan based on the resolution
o f the bottlenecks. The Project will help in the implementation o f this process, in its integration in the
MTEF, and it is hoped that the MBB will help to ensure greater mobilization o f financial resources to
achieve outcomes as well as an effective intervention o f activities to achieve NHDP goals.

B. Main Strategies
The Project will support Niger's national health development plan for the upcoming five years, and is
built around the following specific objectives: (i)increase access by the population to quality health
services; (ii)strengthen reproductive health activities; (iii)accelerate the decentralization process in
health; (iv) promote greater community participation in health; (v) improve management and strengthen
institutional capabilities within the MOH; (vi) overcome qualitative and quantitative deficits in health
staff; (vii) improve the effectiveness and efficiency o f use o f health infrastructure and health inputs made
available to health providers; and (viii) improve the availability of essential drugs and quality medical
inputsinhealth facilities.

To achieve its goal, the NHDP will seek to make a basic package o f health services universally accessible

Component 1: Human resources development and management

Key actions envisaged under this component are to: (a) revise human resources norms; (b) implement

E.GuidingPrinciplesfor Projectimplementation
Implementationo f the Project will be based on the following guidingprinciples:

1. Make the existing health facilities operational: this implies that the recurrent costs, in particular for
    staff, are guaranteed in the long run to optimally use the existing infrastructure. N o new health


                                                     52

    facilities and specialized equipment will be introduced unless their corresponding long-termrecurrent
    costs are ensured.

2.   Two-phase implementation:
         0   Phase I,covering the first two years o f Project implementation, will involve tackling the most
             urgent issues, starting by overcoming the major bottlenecks for a well-performing health
             system. This means that emphasis will be given first and foremost to providing understaffed
             health facilities with the necessary skilled personnel. In addition, the application of
             prioritization criteria will contribute to reducing the inter - and intraregional differences.
             Finally, improving the operation o f the fixed and advanced strategy by optimizing the use o f
             human and other resources will allow extending health coverage to populations within the 0-
             15 kmradius.
         0   Phase 11, covering the remaining three years, will concentrate on consolidation and expansion
             o f prior achievements. During this phase prior actions will be continued and, if needed,
             intensified, and the mobile strategy will be reinforced to bolster the scope o f outreach
             activities. Basic benefit packages will be defined in order to target scarce resources on
             essential health care, and financial protection schemes will be scaled up. Furthermore,
             emphasis will be given to enhance the quality o f health care services at every level.

3.  Implementation o f the marginal bottleneck budgeting approach to identify optimal financing to
    overcome bottlenecks in the health system performance: The exercise initiated by M O H and
    supported by UNICEF will be considered while programming optimal financing o f the Project. To
    overcome the bottlenecks, selection among the different possible alternatives will be based on cost-
    effectiveness criteria. Inthis effort, the need for solidarity will not be neglected to avoid the exclusion
    o fthe poorest.

4. This phased approach should be implemented in a flexible way to enable a smooth implementation o f
    donor-finded projects already planned by the technical and financial partners; however, the donor
    community will watch over the Government's compliance with its commitments regarding the
    guarantee o f recurrent costs to make the existing infrastructure operational.

5.  Development o f partnerships (publidprivate; MOH, regions, districts, and health facilities; donor
    community and NGO / associations, etc.). These partnerships will be favored where possible.

6. Geographic prioritization o f the Project: Implementation o f the Project will give priority to
    geographic zones according to their poverty level, health status, and health services coverage to
    achieve quick results and to increase equity.

7. Long-term financial sustainability o f actions will be constantly kept inmind.

F.Institutionaland OrganizationalChangeProcess
The objective o f this process is to provide the MOH with the necessary tools and capacity to correct its
dysfinctions which prevent adequate response to sectoral priorities as presented in the NHDP. Three
main areas o f dysfunctions have been identified: (a) organizational structure o f the MOH; (b) human
resources management; and (c) management o f public finances and expenditures. Through a progressive
approach o f "learning while doing", the change process aims at changing the role o f the central MOH
from "doing everything itself' to deconcentrating key hnctions that are more inherent to the
deconcentrated levels (region and district). This will enable the central level to concentrate on its mission
of leading sector policy and strategies. It i s expected that these changes will contribute to making all
levels increasingly responsible and accountable for outcomes.

A. SECTOR ISSUES

Structure of administrative,financial and accounting management: The implementing entity for the
project i s MOH, which i s responsible for the country's health policy. MOH's organization chart shows
the official bodies that will be tasked with the administrative, financial and accountingmanagement ofthe
National HealthDevelopment Plan for 2005-2009 (NHDP) in a sector-wide implementation approach and
with the cooperation o fthe technical and financial partners.




                                                     59

These bodies are: (i) Directorate o f Financial Affairs (DAF) and the Directorate o f Studies and
                        the
Planning (DEP) at the central level; (ii)the RegionalDirectorates o f Public Health (DRSP) and the Health
Districts (DS), which are the deconcentratedbodies at the regional and departmental levels, respectively.

The chief purpose of the DRSP and D S i s to apply the country's health policy. It shouldbe notedthat they
have already managed the financial resources o f a certain number o fprojects in support o f health financed
by partners such as the World Bank and the AfDB, and have benefited from the administrative, financial
and accounting arrangements set for that purpose. However, the expertise thus gained has disappeared
with the departure o f the accountants contracted on a temporary basis, as a result o f which the software
and the manuals have not been used, the financial performance statements have not been produced and
the bank accounts relating to the financing received from these partners have been closed, while visits by
public and private auditors have become few and far between.

Slowness o f disbursements resulting in very low budget performance rates coupled with delays in
justification o f advances are among the management problems encountered in the health system. The
regional directorates are required to report to the central level for their planning, annual programming,
resource management, coordination, supervision and monitoring and evaluation activities, at both the DS
echelon and their own level. They do not always receive feedback from the central level on their various
reports.

Staffing: Each DS has a manager, and the majority o f these managers (78%) have received appropriate
training in LomC (Togo) or at the Public Health Institute (ISP) in Niamey. Essentially, these managers
remain nurses holding a state diploma or higher-level technicians in nursing care. They follow by means
o f a register the appropriations allocated from the State budget and keep stock accounts. No physical
inventories at all are made in some DS and such inventorying i s not systematic in others. Ingeneral, these
managers work alone, and if they have any help their assistants are not professional managers. Most
often, they have only received training limited to the practices and procedures o f certain technical and
financial partners, and this training has only been mastered very sketchily. In all, the managers have no
practical experience interms o f the partners' financial accounting methods.

Each DSRP has an administrative and Financial Department (SAF), the chief o f which has the same
profile and training needs as his DS colleague. In general he supervises three staff that i s responsible for
equipment maintenance, automobile repair and maintenance o f operating equipment. The personnel are
managed by the SAF itself. There i s neither an accountant nor a staffer who keeps the accounts
concerning the DRSP's and partners' resources.

At the central level, the M O H has (i)General Inspector in charge o f the Financial Services controller;
                                        a
(ii)eight senior civil servants within the DAF and (iii)the new chart of accounts (budgetary
nomenclatures o f the Niger State).

Programming, Budgetingand Budget Monitoring:In general the annual action plans o f the M O H set
the physical and financial objectives which are then spelled out by terms o f reference by activity. They
include both the funds allocated by the State (delegated appropriations) and the financings provided by
Niger's partners. As a rule, the entire staff is involved in the budgetary programming and preparation
work. The budget forecasts are not always realistic, because they are based on at times highly optimistic
resource mobilization assumptions. This situation i s the cause o f certain divergences between budgeted
and actual figures at year's end. The actual expenditures are sufficiently close to the budget estimates but
explanations are not always requiredfor major differences. The procedures that were inplace for planning
the partner-financed activities, gathering the data from the departments responsible for the different
components and for preparing the budgets have disappeared with the ending o f the technical and financial
partners' financing.

Flow of Funds:At the DRSP and DS level, the only movements o f funds are in general those resulting
from sums received and disbursed in cash to finance activities under the partners' supervision. The
amounts thus managed in cash and deposited in strongboxes on occasions total XOF 50 million for
funding the national immunization campaigns (Journbe Nationale de Vaccination - JNV). In certain
DRSP and D S with bank accounts, suppliers are not paid by check but in cash. Financial transactions
around a bank account with all the procedures and accounting that entails simply do not exist. The
fiduciary risk remains substantial.

Internal Control:Generally speaking, separation o f tasks is observed by the DRSP personnel involved in
administrative, financial and accounting management. Operations are authorized by the Regional
Director; the accounts are kept by the Manager and the management or maintenance o f the goods i s
entrusted to staff members who are generally under the Manager's supervision. At the DS level, the Chief
Physician authorizes the ordering o f goods and services, but the accounting and payment functions remain
inthe Manager's hands.

The DS and the DRSP have a Financial Control Manual for the JNV funds and a Manual o f Procedures
for managing the funds allocated by the WHO Representative's office in Niger. These can be considered
internal control documents and contain procedures for budget monitoring, cash management,
management o f purchases, invoice processing, management o f the vehicle fleet, missions and travel,
training, management o f investments and management o f fuel stocks. They also include budget codes and
outlines for monitoring and reconciliation statements. However, for the needs o f the DS and DRSP, the
organization o f the DS and DRSP could usefully be added, together with the different job descriptions
plus the accounting procedures for the keeping o f certain books, publication o f certain reports and the
filing of accounting documents. These manuals are not used as working tools in the DS and DRSP; for
example, the Managers do not produce reports as recommended, although they have been trained inusing
the manuals.

A system exists for management of State property at DS and DRSP level inorder to protect such property
from fraud, waste and abuse, but the procedures need to be formalized; reliable coding must be devised
and exhaustive physical inventories must be systematically conducted each year. However, while there
are registers or lists o f stocks that are kept up to date, these are not reconciled with the accounts
concerned.

Inthe administrative phase, a prior control o f the appropriations delegated by the State to the DRSP and
DS i s performed by the Regional Payments Authorization Sub department and the concomitant controls
during the accounting phase are made by the Regional Disbursements Office and the Collector's Office,
respectively. The public expenditure channels at the deconcentrated level are slow moving and liquid
funds are not always available whenneeded.

Renderingof FinancialAccounts and Computerized Systems: The periodic renderingo f financial data
on the use o f the financings granted by the partners i s not done systematically or in accordance with the
partners' requirements.On the other hand, the DRSP and the DS account for the use o f funds inparticular
instances, the best example being the accounting for the funding provided for the immunization
campaigns. The administrative, financial and accounting system i s semi computerized; the keeping o f
accounts for stocks and funds is essentially done manually while spreadsheets and word-processing are
only used from time to time to meet specific requirements (statements, financial analyses, etc.). There are
still certain DS and DRSP staffs involved in this type o f work that have not been trained in these
applications.

Internal Audits: The internal audit o f the DS and DRSP i s performed by the Inspectorate General o f
Health Services (IGS), which began to conduct ex post audits in all regions o f the country in the first


                                                    61

quarter o f the current year (2005). The reports are still awaited by the deconcentrated services. Internal
audits are also carried out by each technical and financial partner, which assign the task to qualified
staffers. The procedures and practices followed vary from on a partner to another (audit trail, recovery o f
original expenditure vouchers, official feedback, etc.), which does not help to reduce costs or to effect any
real strengtheningo fthe management capacity o f the DS and DRSP.

OutsideAudit: Audits by the "Cour des Comptes" or the State Inspectorate General are extremely rare if
they happen at all. On the other hand certain partners have set up arrangements for ensuringthe efficient
use o f the financing they have granted the Government o f Niger through the DS and DRSP.

A. GENERAL

Procurement for the proposed Project will be carried out in accordance with the World Bank's
"Guidelines: Procurement under IBRD Loans and IDA Credits" dated M a y 2004; and "Guidelines:
Selection and Employment o f Consultants by World Bank Borrowers" dated M a y 2004, and the
provisions stipulated in the Development Credit Agreement. The various items under different
expenditure categories are described in general below. For each contract to be financed by the Credit, the
different procurement methods or consultant selection methods, the need for pre-qualification, estimated
costs, prior review requirements, and time frame are agreed between the Borrower and the Association in
the Procurement Plan. The Procurement Plan will be updated at least annually or as required to reflect the
actual Project implementation needs and improvements in institutional capacity.

Procurement of Works: The Project will support small rehabilitation o f dilapidated health facilities at
regional, district, and primary levels across the country. A strategic plan for the rehabilitation o f the
primary health care facilities will be developed before civil works packages are completed. Civil works
estimated to cost less than $200,000 equivalent per contract may be procured under contracts awarded on
the basis of National Competitive Bidding (NCB). Civil works contracts costing less than US$30,000
equivalent per contract will be procured on the basis o f simplifiedbidding documents (SBD) by soliciting
quotations from not less than three (3) qualified domestic contractors, preferably more in order to obtain
at least three comparable offers. The invitation shall include a detailed description o f the work, including
basic specifications, required completion date, a basic form o f agreement acceptable to the Association,
and relevant drawings, where applicable. Inall cases the award shall be made to the contractor who offers
the lowest price quotation for the required work, and who has the experience and resources to complete
the contract successfully. Domestic Preference will not be applicable. The procurementwill be done using
Bidding Documents agreed with or satisfactory to the Association. Probably, new health centers will be
financed by the Project after the mid term review, and after prior approval by IDA. Community
procurement o f goods and works required for the Project shall, pursuant to paragraph 3.17 o f the
Guidelines, be carried out in accordance with procedures acceptable to the Association as set forth in the
Project Implementation Manual (PIM).

Procurement of Goods: Goods procured under this Project will include: pharmaceuticals, vaccines,
medical equipment and supplies, motor vehicles, motorcycles, office equipment, and furniture. Goods
estimated to cost US$200,000 equivalent and above per contract will be procured through International
Competitive Bidding (ICB). For exceptional cases (namely, vaccines), and with the Association's prior
approval, Limited International Bidding (LIB) may be employed. Individual contracts costing less than
US$200,000 equivalent will be procured through National Competitive Bidding (NCB) procedures.
Pharmaceuticals, medical equipment, and supplies with an estimated value o f less than US$30,000
equivalent may be procured from UNICEF, WHO, UNFPA, and other specialized agencies o f the United
Nations, Direct Contracting (DC) may be employed with prior approval o f the Association for certain
cases, such as procurement o f medical equipment or spare parts which must be compatible with existing
equipment.

Other goods with an estimated value o f less than US$30,000 equivalent may be procured through
shopping or the UNDP Inter-Agency Procurement Services Office (IAPSO), based on comparing price
quotations from at least three eligible suppliers in accordance with IDA Procurement Guidelines
(paragraph 3.5) and June 9, 2000 Memorandum "Guidance on Shopping" issued by the Bank. Requests
for such quotations will be in writing, and will include time and place for delivery o f the quotations; a



                                                    68

clear descriptiodspecification and quantity o f the goods; as well as requirements for delivery time, place
for delivery o f goods, and installation requirements as appropriate. The request for quotations should be
sent to at least three reputable suppliers; however, it may be preferable to approach more suppliers
because not all three suppliers may respond so that at least three competitive quotations are received.
Quotations will be opened and evaluated at the same time. Whenever possible, goods o f similar nature,
and if needed during the same period, should be grouped into packages o f US$200,000 equivalent or

NIGER: INSTITUTIONAL STRENGTHENING AND HEALTH SECTOR SUPPORT PROJECT

A.       MACROECONOMIC CONTEXT

The Government o f Niger is in the third year o f implementation o f its PRS, which is the strategic
reference framework used for its interventions in the economic, financial, and social sectors since 2002.
The political and social stability in the country has helped to strengthen and solidify stronger relations
with development partners. Niger's economic performance duringthese past years indicates that the rural
sector remains the main engine o f growth. The good agricultural production associated with the drive o f
the private sector and the boost in investments enabled the country to achieve a 6.3 percent growth in
2003 compared to 5.3 percent in 2002; these rates are higher than the average 4 percent estimated for the
period. These results have enabled Niger to attain the completionpoint o f the HIPC initiative (April 2004)
and, thereby, benefit from debt relief.

At the budgetary level, the basic overall balance was XOF 30.8 billion, or 2 percent of GDP in 2003,
while the total accumulated budget revenue in 2003 amounted to XOF 156.7 billion compared to 160.9
billion in 2001, showing a decline o f 2.6 percent. Furthermore, tax revenues improved by 5.2 percent
compared to 2002, following the 11.4 percent increase in internaltax revenues alleviated by the decline in
import and export duties (3.6 percent). However, the rate o f fiscal pressure remained low (9.8 percent o f
GDP in 2003 compared to 10.1 percent in 2002) in relation to the West African Economic and Monetary
Union (WAEMU) norm (17 percent).

There was a slight decline o f 1.3 percent in total expenditure and net loans between 2002 and 2003.
Capital expenditure dropped from 8.2 percent o f GDP in 2002 to 7.4 percent in 2003, while current
budget expenditure, which stabilized around 11.O percent o f GDP between 2000 and 2002, represented
9.7 percent in 2003. In particular, the wage bill was reduced from 3.9 percent o f GDP in 2002 to 3.7
percent in 2003. The percentage of revenues absorbed therefore dropped from 38.3 percent in 2002 to
37.5 percent in2003 as a result o f a restrictive salary policy targeted at a ratio o f 28.74 percent in2005.

Despite a 34 percent rise in budgetary aid, the overall level o f funding declined by 13.7 percent, mainly
due to the reduction in budgetary loans and non-refundable grants for projects. However, because o f
budgetary regulation and tight control o f expenditures, the Government was able to improve the different
budgetary balances and maintain a more significant allocation o f resources to the priority sectors under
the PRS. In2003, the share o f resources allocated to these sectors represented 48.9 percent o f the budget
compared to 47.2 percent in 2002. Amounts allocated to the education and health sectors were
respectively 14.5 percent and 9.9 percent, compared to 14.3 percent and 9.4 percent in 2002.

B. GROWTHAND DEVELOPMENTOF THE HEALTH SECTOR

        a) Fundingof the public healthsector

Funding modalities o f the sector: Public funding o f the health sector is largely ensured by external
sources, including nearly 95 percent for investment expenditure. Operational expenses, though partly
funded with budgetary aid from international cooperation agencies, amounted to XOF 10.14 billion in
2000 and XOF 16.32 billion in 2004 (Finance Act). These expenditures (investment and recurrent),
representing 10.60 percent of the country's overall expenditures, declined in 2004, representing only 7.20
percent, whereas they should have stabilized at 10 percent o f state expenditures. The introduction o f cost-
recovery schemes in the 1990s initially resulted in a decline in the use o f health services, but helped to
mobilize new resources amounting to nearly XOF 2 billion in 2002. The amount recovered was mostly

         b) Analytical markers of effects of growth on health

Over the period 2001-2003, Niger's annual average growth at market and constant prices was slightly
above 5 percent, resulting in an increase in real growth o f 2 percent per capita. For 2004, the prospects6
appear less satisfactory than estimated, mainly as a result o f less favorable rainfall levels than in 2003.
The estimated growth rate was 3.6 percent, compared to an initial estimate o f 4.1 percent. There would
be, therefore, a highly modest increase in per capita income given the population growth rate o f 3.1
percent.

This overall favorable growth had an impact on the health status o f the population. The study o f
determinants o f the mortality trend implies the conduct o f econometric analysis. Briefly, the literature
uses models where the determination o f the health status i s based on variable vectors specific to
individuals, households, and health zones, to which are added national variables and the value o f the
health indicator at the beginning o f the observation period'. Controlling for potential health determinants
other than growth reveals that the intuitive hypothesis whereby infant, notably infant-child, mortality
would be reduced with growth i s not systematically confirmed by the crosscutting or country analyses.

The period o f observation o f growth in Niger i s too brief and there are no adequate data to allow for an
econometric analysis here. But at least we can attempt to indicate the trend o f possible effects o f
economic growth inNiger during these past years on infant mortality rate, infant-child mortality rate, and
the nutritional situation by describing the nature o f this growth and analyzing the different mechanisms
where it has favorably had an impact on mortality and morbidity indicators.

by the fact that growth, as we saw, concerned more the rural world, including the agricultural and cattle
breeding sectors. This process may have led to an improvement inthe nutritional situation with a synergic
impact on the improvement o f the health status, with malnutrition increasing the risk o f mortality".

The literature on determinants o f mortality also invites us to think that the growth o f these past years
could have a positive effect on infant mortality rates through an effect on reduction o f fertility and birth
spacing. But in the case o fNiger, one must be very cautious for several reasons: (i) observation period  the
is very short; (ii) are some powerfbl pro-birth sociocultural factors and low availability o f family
                        there
planning;"      and (iii)there i s high instability (recurrent) in economic growth (See Figure 10). This
instability may have reduced the expected positive impact o f growth on health status, as it induced an
increase in saving^'^ at varying degrees depending on the socioeconomic categories. In this case, the
extra income i s not "transformed" into healthpromoting inputs.

Increase in savings results either from anticipation o f the about turn in the economic situation due to the
contraction o f the cycle o f drought (precautionary savings), or from repayment o f debts contracted during
the years o fnegative growth. More generally, instability o fper capita income impedes behavioral changes
(recourse to care, etc.). A survey conducted in the framework o f the Total Food Security Program helped
to identify seven groups at risk o f food insecurity and nine departments with "very high rates o f
vulnerability", for which one may think that growth instability mostly impeded the potential health gains
from growth2'.

It is likely that the growth inper capita income these past years contributedto improve the health o f the
population, including that o f many o f the poor. W e have seen that there was a "range o f presumptions",
leading us to think that this had been the case. But there are still some major uncertainties and it i s
obvious that health benefits from growth would have been variable depending on income and poverty
levels, professional categories, cultural contexts, and regions. It also should be remembered that the
literature on determinants o f health status shows that the different factors that could have an impact
(positive or negative) on healthmay, depending on the context, be enhanced or opposed.

C.MEDIUM-TERMEXPENDITURESFRAMEWORK(MTEF)

The MTEF is based on the PTQ proposal which aims at supporting sevenpriority programs. The MTEF i s
meant to ensure their total financial coverage, if they are to be finalized and adopted by the Government.
A process o f harmonization is therefore essential, using the work already done by the MOH, so that the
content o f the "Budget program 2005-2007" can be perfectly homogeneous with the PTQ and not the
other way around. The PTQ must be the reference framework. It i s therefore essential, in order to ensure
coherence with the health policy, to ensure that the budget-program i s in line with the PTQ. The PTQ i s
at the heart o f the "expenditures area" o f the MTEF, and as such will be costed according to a new budget
presentedand implementedby the project.

 Two options are proposed for the implementation o f the health policy as defined and structured. One
 option refers to a greater number o f activities, while the other option offers a smaller scenario more
 focused on the priorities in order to address financial constraints. The total cost o f the larger scenario i s
 372.6 billion o f CFAF for 2005-2009, with an annual average o f 74.5 billion o f CFAF, while the smaller
 scenario i s estimated at 339.5 billion o f CFAF, with 67.9 billion o f CFAF in average per year.

 The financing requirement, o f the order o f 4 percent o f GNP, indicates an important ceiling effect starting
 in 2005, for total public expenditures as for those related to the Ministryo f Health. The question is: Can
 this requirement be covered? To respond, one would needto assess the macroeconomic framework within
 which the health sector subscribes.

 Insum, the followingthree mainconclusions canbe drawn:
     -    The financing o f the PTQ under its large scenario i s unsure, but not impossible. It requires a
          conjunction o f variables that are highly favorable, such as a growth in GNP during the period,
          donor contributions, and negotiations o f the Government toward the health sector;
     -    On the contrary, the financing o f the PTQ under its smaller scenario seems more likely in the
          context o f a less ambitious program as long as the Government's commitment to the sector

     -    remains;
          Support from the donor community will continue to play a crucial role, directly and indirectly, in
          the financing o fthe healthpolicy, regardless ofthe scenario that is selected.

INSTITUTIONAL BASEAND OUTLOOK

Inview o f the dearth o f resources, successful implementationo fthe 2005-2009 NHDPis contingent on a
reorganization o f the administration o f the health sector within a management system that i s oriented
towards attainable objectives, taking into account the acute shortage o f medium- and long-term funding.
Specific expected outcomes are the geographic redistribution o f health services (in rural and remote
versus urban areas), enhanced access o f the poor to basic health services and essential generic drugs, and
improved health care quality.

To increase the availability and accessibility o f quality health services to the population, including the
poorest groups, the financing procedures and organization o f the health system must be reformed. Early
results o f performance-based financing strategies recently tried out in countries whose economic level i s
comparable to Niger's are very promising. That approach requires separating the following functions:
-   Regulation (assigned to the M O H ).
-   Financing (to be assigned to one or more distinct M O H units whose establishment should be
    supported, since no such structures currently exist inNiger).
-   Provision o f health care services (by public or private bodies, health facilities run by community-
    based or religious organizations, and decentralized units o f MOH or related ministries).

Gradual reform o f financing procedures and the organization o f the health system within a SWAP should
-leadMOH,
     to institutional changes involving three groups:
            which should refocus exclusively on policy formulation and regulation.
-
-   Financingbodies, which should fulfill their role efficiently purchasinghealth services.
    Health facilities, which should be able to provide service in accordance with the contracts concluded
    with one or more financingbodies.

These changes should be driven by public expenditure procedures restructured with a view to pooling the
available resources and ensuring their outcome- and impact-based allocation. That approach should,
however, be enhanced through concurrent experimental initiatives, such as pilot projects consisting o f
contracting out services "internally" (to other public bodies or units) or "externally" (to non-state
operators), undertaken in specific, targeted geographic areas or in connection with priority health
expenditures.

A. Obiectives

The institutional and human resources changes envisaged aim at:
    -    Refocusing the MOH on its regulatory responsibilities at the national, regional, and sub regional
         levels;
    -    Decentralizing preventive and curative operational tasks by assigning them to public and non-
         state partners;
    -    Allocating human, material, and financial resources according to the priority objectives o f the

    -    national healthpolicy; and
         Broadeningthe communities' participation informulating and following up local healthpolicies.

Insum, restructuring the MOH and modernizingthe management o f its human and financial resources are
justified in view o f a far-reaching health system reform, clearly demarcating the functions o f regulation,

financing, and provision o f services. The process o f achieving the institutional and human resources
changes i s part o f a broader policy.

B. Comr>onents

B.1. The structural component

The establishment, subject to approval by the authorities, o f an institutional framework adapted to the
challenges posed and the functions envisaged will necessitate capacity-building in the following areas: (i)
Comprehensive reorganization o f the MOH, including its internal and external relations; (ii)
Establishment o f consistent programs o f activity for all M O H units and definition o f a corresponding set
o f sector performance indicators; (iii)Streamlining o f the legal and regulatory framework; (iv)
Implementation o f new information and communication technologies.

The restructuring o f the MOH is intended to build an organization with the focus needed to implement
effectively the Ministry'spolicy.

The following key steps are envisaged:
-   Preparing a new organizational chart: breaking down the M O H missions into tasks assigned to the
    central units o f the Ministry and establishing an appropriate hierarchical structure and functional
    relationships, to extend to its relationships with deconcentrated, decentralized, and non-state units o f
    the health sector.
-   Definingthe functions and organizational charts o f all central and deconcentrated units at all levels.
-   Drafting job descriptions for all positions provided for in the organizational charts, including the
    required qualifications and skills. This information, entered in a positions database, i s also necessary
    for human resources management (HRM) staffing decisions, outlining o f career paths in the light o f
                                                -
    structural needs, and forward-looking assessment o f required staff, positions, and qualifications.

A health map will be drawn up and used to plan health sector funding and investment and to allocate
material and human resources according to Government guidelines and priorities regarding the health
sector (NHDP).

A set o f sector performance indicators is necessary at all levels. Input, activity, and output indicators are
non-existent and must be developed in order to regulate the activities o f the sector. Reliable data obtained
through the National Health Information System - NHIS (S'stdme National d 'Information Sanitaire -
SNIS)should be supplementedwith additional information on each unit for effective, feedback-basedunit
management and for the administration o f M O H as a whole. The role o f MOH in internal or external
contracting will consist in defining the service packages to be funded, issuing provider qualification
specifications, and signing contracts, verifying that services are actually provided, and overseeing their
quality. Defining follow-up indicators, however, presupposes the adoption o f a strategy for financing and
organizing the health system.

The updating o f regulations should focus on restructuring the MOH and redefining its role in the health
sector. Consideration should be given to various ongoing or planned reforms          - first and foremost the
establishment o f communes, whose role in the area o f health will modify the relationship between the
basic facilities and the districts and regions. Furthermore, recourse to non-state operators requires an
appropriate redistribution o f authority among the various levels, as well as the deconcentrated units
responsible for monitoring the new partnerships.

Lastly, all health management agreements to be eventually concluded should conform to the legal
framework o f Niger. The feasibility studies to be carried out will address inter alia: (i) quality assurance;

and (ii) cost of the activities included in the health service packages (including staff costs based on
          the
remuneration levels that would be attractive enough to draw and retain the required staff, especially in
remote or difficult areas).

HRMwill berationalizedwith aview to:
-     Attracting staff for the vacant positions;
-     Retaining the staff hired (by providing standard and additional incentives, as well as adequate living
-     and working conditions);
      Buildingthe capacities o fthe core staff (through continuing training, and career development).

HRMwill enable the MOH to meet its needs in terms of: (i)          number and location o f positions; and (ii)
profile  of competencies required for each position. Possible changes in the nature and structure o f the
activities o f the Ministrywill serve as the basis for the projected management ofjobs and individuals.

Support for HRM changes in the M O H will be provided in the following areas: (i)Eliminating

C. Methodologyfor achieving the changes sought

Building the capacities o f a ministry entails changing its structure into a form that is currently
undetermined. They will have to be built gradually, through phases o f transition. In-depth change should
be spearheaded by the M O H and its partners themselves and should be driven by economic considerations
(efficiency) and technological, social, and institutional factors. By its nature, any transformation that
experts might impose would meet existing needs less effectively than measures taken by the actors
themselves.

Instead o f imposing a normative model that imposes tools, objectives, and timeframes, the flexible and
participatory approach to be adopted consists o f a sustainable partnership with the M O H and its partners.
Decisions regarding priorities and the stages o f change should be taken along the way by the officials
concerned, whose contribution in terms o f ideas, work, focus on results, and allocation o f funding will be
crucial. This approach i s a long-term one, and assistance i s therefore provided at a pace compatible with
the degree o fchange that the beneficiaries can control and manage.


                                                      89

 Social and cultural attitudes in MOH, that is, all o f the elements enabling its staff to coexist,
communicate, work, and succeed as a group, should be allowed to evolve in order to meet eventually the
requirements o f decentralization, deconcentration, and partnership with non-state actors. To that end, the
MOH should progressively promote ''values'' and implement change in such a way that it discourages
arbitrary exercise o f power, precludes deviant practices, and shows a firm political resolve conducive to
change.

At present, the majority o f M O H staff has a narrow view o f the Ministry'srole, which they perceive as
being confined to the public sub sector. That vision should be widened to allow for effective
implementation o f the national health policy through a broad organization and financing o f the health
system. The involvement o f new operators calls for recasting the operation and building the capacities o f
the MOH, whose organization and action have so far been geared to the direct provision o fpreventive and
curative services. The top M O H echelons should implement an internal information policy paving the
way for the changes and should steer the process.

InMay 2004, an internalworking group, consisting o feight senior M O H officials, was set upinthe belief
that the active participation o f the actors o f the health system i s crucial to the success o f a dynamic
process o f sustainable change, adaptable to geographic constraints and to new circumstances encountered
inthe field. The group has prepareda comprehensive assessmento fthe current operationo fthe M O Hand
summarized the recommendations made in a number o f studies on the MOH, conducted in recent years.
In September 2004, two "panels for the promotion o f change" held a discussion seminar for the main
actors o f the health sector nationwide. The panels comprised 12 officials, including the members o f the
initial group, and there were 800 seminar participants. When the new procedures or tools are introduced
and the institutional and human resources changes begin, that group o f M O H officials will be responsible
for coordinating and promoting the changes.

Insuch aprocess, consultants should act as facilitators rather than prescribers. They should be the catalyst
that spurs management, in the current stage and throughout the transformation process. Theirs i s a
"systemic" role, which they can play because they are: (i)   Proficient in various techniques, none o f which
suffices   to improve the system; (ii)  Experiencedin organizational development and institutional capacity
building; and (iii) to convey the above knowledge to a heterogeneous working group.

2. Status of maternaland childhealthinNiger

2.1.    Demographic indicators: The demographic issues in Niger have been well summarized in a
recent study by the Bank. Niger has a young population with a broad based pyramid typical o f countries
that have not yet entered the demographic transition. Currently, Niger has the world's fastest growing
population and one o f the highest fertility (TFR o f 7.5 children per woman). The very low contraceptive
prevalence rate (4%), high fertility and youthhl population have led to an alarming projection o f Niger's
population, expected to reach 55 million by 2050. While the population momentum i s already set, there i s
urgentneed to address the high fertility inthe country dare to the known linkage with maternal and child
health. In response to this the Government and the Bank are designing a separate population project that
will addressthe issues from a multisectoral perspective.

2.2.    Maternal and child health indicators: In the last decade, Niger registered very modest
improvements in child outcome, while the situation did not improve at all on maternal indicators. The
underlyingreasons are many and include       - lack o f knowledge o f risks and illness, poor access to M C H
services, poor quality o f services, poor nutrition and gender disparities.

    Poor access and utilization of MCH services: Poor access to services has both geographic and
    socioeconomic aspects. Distance i s factor but so are s the cost o f care and the quality o f care as
    perceived by consumers. For maternal and child health some socio cultural factors also come into
    play. In 2003, 50% o f the population lived more than 5km away from a health facility; 15% lived
    more than 15 km away. The effect o f this is seen in the data on both antenatal deliveries in health
    facilities inNiger, the regional and poor rich differentials.

    Family planning: Although overall contraceptive prevalence in Niger is very low, there are
    significant regional differences which give hope that intensification o f programmatic efforts will bear
    fruits. There i s significant unmet need for FP in Niger and the Kollo project has shown that, this can
    be addressed even inrural areas.

    Maternal, newbornand child health
    -    Safe motherhood: Highparity, early and late age at pregnancy and abortion were identified as
         key maternal risk factors inNiger. As elsewhere in the region, the causes on maternal death are
         largely preventable through effective antenatal care, skilled attendance at delivery and access to

    -    emergency obstetric care. Allot needs to be done to make this possible.
         Antenatal care: Antenatal care coverage is estimated to be 42% with wide variation by region
         and wealth quintile. Most o f the Antenatal care attendance i s however only single visit with
         variable but mostly poor quality o f care. This calls for not just expansion but ensuring that
         services are established to offer focused antenatal care consisting o f four visits, appropriate
         counseling and examination plus interventions to preventboth anemia and malaria inpregnancy.

    -    Integration with both malaria and HIV/AIDS programs i s therefore essential.
         Skilled attendance at birth: Currently, only 16% o f deliveries receive skilled attendance
         nationally. The majority o f deliveries occur at home by Traditional Birth Attendant (TBAs) and
         other community members. The recommendations o f the Reproductive Health Regional Task
         force on the role o f TBAs would appear relevant to the situation inNiger.
    -    Emergency obstetric care: Less than 30% o f rural health facilities can offer basic obstetric care,
         which may still be underestimating the size o f the challenge. Urban centers are much better off,
         with over 60% o f facilities capable o f the service. For comprehensive obstetric care nearly half o f
         the 113 facilities are unable to offer it. Caesarian section rate is less than 1%.
    -    Post natalcare: Post natalcare is rare inNiger with less than 2%.
    -    Newborn outcomes: Neonatal mortality rate in Niger is estimated to be in excess o f 162/1000

         most deliveries occur at home. L o w birth weight, prematurity and birth asphyxia account for most
         o f the deaths.
    -    Infant mortality: Infant and under five mortality remains high in Niger despite some recent
         gains. There is also a wide variation between rural and urban areas.
    -    Under five nutrition: Malnutrition is rife in Niger, not made any easier by recurrent droughts
         and famine.

    Harmful traditionalpractices:Most o f these are gender based and continue to undermine the health

NATIONAL PLANAGAINST THE MALARIA
The general objective o f the strategic plan to fight against malaria is the reduction from at least 30% o f
morbidity and mortality due to malaria. The expected results at the end o f 2005 will relate to : i)at least
60% of the people with a presumption o f malaria will have access to a rapid and adequate treatment
within 24 hours ;ii)at least 60% o f the people at the risk, especially pregnant women and children under
five years old, will benefit o f from protection measures such as the insecticide treated mosquito nets and
other interventions; iii)at least 60% o f the pregnant women, especially those pregnant for the first time,
will have access to the chemoprophylaxis or apreventiveintermittenttreatment.

To achieve these goals, the national program will stress i)the improvement o f the first line o f treatment
for the cases o f malaria ; ii)the prevention o f malaria including physical and biological fight against the
vectors, the promotion o f insecticide treated mosquito nets, the preventive intermittent treatment for
pregnant women; iii)the promotion o f research aiming at the improvement o f the services in order to roll
back malaria ; iv) strengthening o f the national program and the development o f an effective partnership
with other actors.

CONSTRAINTS
Despite the strategic and vertical position o f the program at the central level, all activities related to fight
against malaria are integrated in the programs implemented by regional and districts which are the
operational level o f the Niger health system. But that i s held with major constraints which can be

to access to drugs related to the new treatment (Artemisin, Sulfadoxine Pyrimethamine); iii)difficulties to
implement a social marketingprogram ;and iv) capacity weakness to fight against the vector.

111.     PROCEDURESFOR PAYMENT INTO THE ACCOUNT OF THE DS

The account o f the DS will be replenished with funds deriving solely from the common support fund for
implementation o f the HDP.

The initial payment into the account o f the DS will be based on the activities planned for a period o f four
months and an annual DS budget adopted by the Steering Committee. A copy o f the Agreement approved
by the DS and the DRSP will be submitted to the Directorate o f Financial Affairs (hereinafter "the DAF")
inorder to obtainthe first payment.

The subsequent payments will be made upon submission to the DAF o f the following documents:

         a)      the request for funds signedby the ChiefPhysician o fthe District (MCD);
         b)      the certified statement o f expenditures;
         c)      the statement o freconcilement o fthe advance account;

financed, to verify the equipment purchased or the results o f activities carried out and to communicate all
necessary information.
