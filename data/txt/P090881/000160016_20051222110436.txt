                                         I. INTRODUCTION

1. This program document presents a proposed grantkredit to the Republic o f Uganda for
SDR93.2 million or US$135 million equivalent (SDR77.7 million or US$112.5 million
equivalent as grant, and SDR15.5 million or US$22.5 million equivalent as credit), to finance the
fifth Poverty Reduction Support Operation(PRSCS). Buildingon four preceding PRSCs, PRSCS
i s the first to support Uganda's third Poverty Eradication Action Plan (PEAP') approved by
Cabinet inNovember 2004. The third PEAP takes stock o f more than a decade o f achievements
in economic reforms and poverty reduction, and brings renewed focus to accelerating growth
through productivity growth and a more prominent role for the private sector. Each o f the four
previous PRSCs established a rolling three-year reform program with performance benchmarks,
policy measures, and outcome targets. The third PEAP establishes a comprehensive results and
policy framework and the PRSC operation i s aligned with this. The reformprogram presented in
previous PRSCs was based upon the second PEAP. The new framework i s more comprehensive,
but remains broadly consistent with the priorities of the previous national plans. The PRSC
operations will continue to be the core o f the Bank's support for Uganda as spelt out in the
Uganda Joint Assistance Strategy (UJAS).'
2. The proposed PRSCS supports the third PEAP (2005-8), and extends the earlier focus on
health, education and water sector programs (since PRSCl), public sector management and anti-
corruption, rural growth agenda (PRSC2), and gender, infant and maternal mortality, and results-
orientation (PRSC4). While continuing to support reforms in these areas, PRSCS deepens the
emphasis on growth, both through further elaboration o f the rural development agenda, and
through policies that support non-farm employment and income growth. The program document
describes reforms to be supported by PRSCS and planned areas o f focus for the next two years.
3. The proposed reform program, including prior actions, has been developed jointly with the
Government of Uganda (GoU) in close cooperation with other Development Partners, including
the African Development Bank, Germany, Ireland, the Netherlands, Norway, Sweden, and the
United Kingdom. Budget support from these Development Partners i s partially contingent upon
satisfactory PRSC progress.

9. Uganda's economy grew by 5.8 percent in 2004/05. Strong growth continued in
telecommunication and education services, construction, and manufacturing. Gross domestic
investments were higher at 23.8 percent in 2004/05 as private sector investment rose to 17.9
percent o f GDP. With improved weather conditions and further growth in non-agricultural
sectors, the economy i s expected to grow by 6.6 percent in2005/06.
10. Macroeconomic Environment: Inflation has been somewhat volatile during the year due to
food crop prices affected by weather. Headline inflation rose moderately from 5.1 percent in
2003/04 to 8.2 percent in 2004/05, while underlying inflation (excluding food crops) fell from
5.1 percent to 4.5 percent in the same period. Uganda's external position worsened slightly as
terms o f trade reversed the improvements that had been recorded over the previous 2 years. With
slower export volume growth, the current account deficit before grants' as a share o f GDP rose to
12.7 percent in2004/05 from 12 percent in2003/04. The deficit target o f 12.8 percent in 2005/06
remains tentative due mainly to the continued high price o f                    The nominal and real effective

11. On theJiscal side, efforts are continuing to reduce the fiscal deficit through stronger revenue
performance and further consolidation o f government expenditures. The fiscal deficit, excluding
grants, i s expected to decline from 11.7 percent to 9.7 percent o f GDP between 2003/04 and
2004/05, after being above 10.0 percent over the last 4 years. Government revenues have grown
by an annual average o f 12.1 percent over the last 5 years, butrevenues as a share o f GDP stands
only at around 12 percent, well below the 18 percent average for all Sub-Saharan African
countries.  7   Government expenditures grew steadily since 1997/98, supported by external
resources directed at poverty reduction, and stood at 22.3 percent o f GDP in 2004/05 with 38
percent o f the spending channeled through the Poverty Action Fund (PAF). However, there are
increasing concerns over the ad hoc interventions in the budget process, accumulation o f arrears,
and additional budget pressures outside o f priority areas, especially inPublic Administration.
12. Regional Integration. In January 2005, the newly inaugurated East African Community
(EAC) Customs Union instituted a 3-band common external tariff for Kenya, Tanzania and
Uganda.     The resulting common tariff rates are higher than the pre-Customs Union tariff
structure in Uganda, but the removal o f the discriminatory excise taxes and import surcharges i s
expected to moderate the adverse impact on competitiveness. Inthe short term, the revenue loss
to Uganda on account o f EAC Customs Union i s projected to amount to 0.2 percent o f GDP in
2004/05, increasing further to 0.6 percent o f GDP in 2005/06. Inthe longer term, the formation
of the regional market, together with measures to enhance competitiveness o f local industries, i s
expected to contribute to a healthier external position for the country.
13. Sustainability of external debt: Uganda's public external financing requirements for the next
3 years are estimated at US$2,054 million, in the form o f grants and concessional loans. A debt
sustainability analysis currently beingprepared by Bank staff shows that the ratio o f Uganda's net
present value (NPV) o f debt to 3 year average exports stood at 229 percent at end 2004/05,
compared to 198 percent projected in the debt sustainability analysis o f 2002. The deterioration
inthe ratio reflects changes inexogenous factors         - notably lower international interest rates, and
a weaker U S dollar. Uganda's exports were higher, and new borrowing was broadly consistent
with projections made in 2002. Using one-year exports, in line with the new IDNIMF Debt
Sustainability Framework for Low-Income Countries (LIC-DSA), the NF'V o f Uganda's debt in
2004/05 amounted to 179 percent o f exports, slightly below the sustainability threshold o f 200
percent for strong policy performers. Also at 24 percent and 10 percent respectively, the ratios of
the net present value o f debt to GDP and debt service to exports remain well below the indicative
thresholds in the LIC-DSA framework. Uganda currently qualifies for receiving 45 percent o f
resources under IDA14 in the form o f grants. The Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative (MDRI) i s
expected to substantially reduce Uganda's debt burden.
14. Governance. Concrete progress in improving governance i s critical for Uganda to meet its
development objectives.         The government has established the institutions required to fight

15. Uganda's first PEAP in 1997 provided the model for PRSPs worldwide. It was revised in

The Third Poverty Eradication Action Plan(PEAP)
16. The third PEAP restates Uganda's ambitions o f becoming a middle income country within
twenty years, sets out targets for key "strategic results" in income poverty and inequality,
improved human development, and increased GDP growth. It identifies four core challenges:

     0   Restoring security, dealing with consequences o f conflict, and improving regional equity;
     0   Restoring sustainable growth inthe incomes o f the poor;
     0   Improving education quality, cutting mortality and increasing people's control over
         family size;
     0   Usingpublic resources transparently and efficiently to eradicate poverty.
These are to be addressed through a series o f policies and measures grouped under five "pillars".
17. Pillar one: Economic Management GoU's objectives are to maintain macroeconomic
stability, promote private sector driven, export-led growth, restrict inflation, raise domestic
revenue, and reduce the fiscal deficit. It also sets out measures to promote financial deepening
and micro finance, to reform the pension sector, and to promote private investment, trade
liberalization and export diversification
18. Pillar mo: Enhancing Production, Competitiveness and Incomes.                  GOU's objectives
include the successful implementation o f the Plan for Modernization o f Agriculture (PMA) and
the Medium Term Competitiveness Strategy (MTCS) to (i)increase smallholder agricultural
production and productivity and quality o f output; (ii)increase investment in transport and
power; (iii)  increase financial service outreach, particularly inthe agricultural sector; (iv) remove
bureaucratic obstacles and explore cost-effective ways o f delivering business development
services to micro, small and medium-scale enterprises (MSMEs); and (v) introduce sustainable
management o fnatural resources.
19. Pillar three: Security, Conflict Resolution and Disaster Management brings together a
range o f proposed interventions including defense and security sector reform, conflict resolution,
the reduction o f cattle rustling, small arms control, disaster preparedness and management
(including refugees), and rehabilitation o f conflict-affected areas. The overall objective i s to
focus on human security as a prerequisite for the successful implementation o f the PEAP.
20. Pillar four: Good Governance adopts a comprehensive approach to governance issues
ranging fkom democratization and anti-conuption to public service reform. The overall objective
o f the pillar i s the strengthening o f political governance, human rights, legal andjustice systems,
and public sector management and accountability.

   Poverty and social impacts
   83. Poverty and Social Impact Assessments (PSIAs) have allowed Government to analyze the
   distributional impact o f policy reforms on the well-being of different stakeholder groups, with
   particular focus on the poor and vulnerable. Uganda has a long tradition o f analysis o f poverty
   and social impacts o f policies, and most core diagnostic or sector work address these issues even
   though the final product i s not presented in as a PSIA. A large number o f such studies have been
   undertaken by Government, the Bank and other partners to analyze poverty and social impacts o f
   policies and these are described in the section on analytical underpinnings above. A
   comprehensive poverty assessment i s being undertaken. PSIAs on lands issues and on local
   government taxation are planned.          The assessment o f the poverty and social impact o f the
   measures proposed by the PEAP and supported by the PRSC i s carried out in the context o f
   Uganda's PEAP monitoring system and in particular the biannual household surveys that
   analyze, among other things, poverty trends and dynamics. The surveys which cover the entire
   country provide a rich source o f information on household socio-economic characteristics
   including, consumption expenditures, employment, and access to social services, etc.                       The
   surveys are complemented by the more qualitative participatory poverty assessments. Emphasis
   i s increasingly being placed on outcome inthe PEAP monitoring and on indicators which provide

Risks and efforts to mitigatethem
93. The risks facing the program can be broadly classified into two categories: risks that reflect
the broader context in Uganda, and risks that pertain in particular to the PRSC reforms. The
overall risks are presented in the UJAS and in Annex 5. The risks are discussed briefly below
with a particular emphasis on those that are more relevant for the PRSC program, and the PRSC
efforts to mitigate the risks. In addition, specific risks to the implementation o f core areas the
program are also identified.
Risksthat reflect the broader context inUganda
94. Uncertainty arising from political transition. Uganda currently faces a political transition
and rising political uncertainty with potential for turmoil. In July 2005, Ugandans voted to
restore multiparty politics and Parliament voted to lift the two-term limit. On November 18,
2005 the Central Executive Committee o f the National Resistance Movement (NRM) endorsed
President Museveni as the party's chairperson and presidential candidate in the general elections
scheduled for March 2006. The current political environment and upcoming presidential and
parliamentary elections may distract Uganda's attention from timely implementation o f the
growth and poverty reduction and economic governance agendas. The risk o f increased
patronage, policy reversal, and ad-hoc policy interventions may result in undesired outcomes.
There i s also the risk o f limited efforts aimed at supporting a level playing field for the private
sector and combating graft. These risks are mitigated through policy dialogue at the highest
level, and also through the PRSC, by the continued strengthening and buttressing of institutional
and administrative processes, including a robust and transparent budget development and
management process.        Strategies for response if the environment changes significantly are
discussed in the UJAS. A strong and stable Government apparatus, with transparent and
consistent decision-making and priority setting processes will help mitigate these risks.
95. Continued conflict in northern Uganda. If the internal security situation, especially in the
North, fails to improve or the regional security deteriorates, it would hinder achievement o f
national and local development objectives. The socio-economic challenges posed by the conflict
inthe Northandthe cattle-rustlingrelatedinsecurity inthe East have affecteda large segment of
Uganda's poor, heightening regional disparities. The destruction o f the livelihoods o f the
communities living in these areas has resulted in very high or rising (as in the East) poverty
levels and displaced some 1.2 million persons. The World Bank, working with other partners,
will continue to support the provision o f better social services in the areas ravaged by war and
insecurity.
96. Poor governance undermines confidence. Slow progress in reducing corruption could harm
Uganda's external credibility towards potential private investors and development partners.
Corruption also threatens public confidence, and could reduce the impact o f Government
programs. The Bank i s working with Government and Development Partners to support a more
vigorous implementation o f Uganda's national anti-corruption action plan in conjunction with
public service reformmeasures. These are primarily institutional and legislative reforms aimed at
developing a robust anti-corruption system. The PRSCs also assist government to enforce
particularly important legislation such as the Leadership Code.
97. Weak institutional and public sector capacity, particularly at sub-national levels. Uganda
needs to address the pervasive public sector weaknesses particularly at the district levels. Strong
ownership o f the PEAP agenda by the entire public sector and civil society, and ongoing public
sector reforms including pay reform, should contribute to sustained improvements in public



                                                  31

capabilities. Inaddition to public service reform, the PRSC6 i s also anticipated to include a prior
action on decentralization.
98. Exogenous shocks, arising from adverse weather patterns or terms of trade. Uganda i s
reliant on agriculture for the bulk o f its GDP and exports, thus weather- or terns o f trade-related
shocks can have serious negative impacts on the country's GDP and the balance o f payments.
Government has been pursuing sound macroeconomic policies, structural and institutional
reforms aimed at improving access to markets and enhancing competitiveness. Through the
PMA, Government seeks to enhance production, competitiveness and incomes o f the rural poor
who are the most vulnerable to these shocks. The PRSC supports these efforts.
99. Poor mortality and reproductive health outcomes. Notwithstanding Uganda's gains in the
fight against HIV/AIDS; high infant mortality and maternal mortality, and a high fertility rate,
remain serious challenges. A multisectoral taskforce has been analyzing the determinants o f
infant and maternal mortality and has proposed strategies to tackle them. The population
secretariat i s looking at measures to address population questions, and actions are proposed in
the third PEAP. Development Partners have been involved in the health, water, and education
sectors in the context o f PRSCs and other stand-alone operations, and are supporting infant and
maternal mortality-reducing initiatives.
Risksspecific to the PRSC:
100. Risk of slow progress in the implementation of public service reform. Slow progress in
implementation o f the public service reforms, could adversely affect several o f the outcomes that
are supported by the PRSC. The many interacting dimensions o f public service reforms,
including: management o f the public sector, staffing and pay reforn, capacity building, establish
effective institutional practices, and to avoid overlap and duplication o f efforts inboth core and
more peripheral agencies- are all issues that are intrinsically linked, are strongly influenced by
politics and organized interests, and cuts across most other sectors. The PRSC will continue to
assist government inthe implementationo f the public service reformprogram.
101. Risk of poor Government coordination of the implementation of the PEAP and PRSC, The
reform program contains multiple elements that require coordination and follow-up across
sectors and agencies. Weak institutions, duplication o f effort, and poor intersectoral linkages
have made it difficult to ensure well-coordinated implementation o f previous PRSCs. The new
Government-wide PEAP coordination arrangements are promising and should go a long way
towards mitigating these risks.

(iv) pressure from select groups for accelerated pay awards which cannot be
         accommodated within the pay reform resources.
(v) high pay differentials between commissions/autonomous agencies and the public
         service.

other improvements in human resource management to achieve improved performance
levels. Therefore the Public Service Reform Programme (2005 - 9) i s being updated and

                                            Prior Action 6
                   The Independenceofthe Officeof Auditor General(OAG)

A.     Main Issue:
The establishment o f the Auditor General's Office as an independent, autonomous and self-
accounting body. Interference or possibility o f interference in the Auditor General's
independence can have a direct influence on the objectivity, quality and credibility o f hisher
reports.
Independence Criteria
    Some o f the principles that demonstrate independence are:

1. Appointment and Dismissal:The power to appoint and dismiss the Auditor General should
   not be vested in an auditee o f the AG. Presently, the AG i s appointed by the President with
    approval o f Parliament. Removal/dismissal i s by the President.
2. Control and remuneration of staff The Auditor General's ability to conduct qualitative
    audits depends on the rightnumber and calibre o f staff. To be properly in charge, the Auditor
    General needs greater flexibility to decide the grades o f staff and their remuneration in order
   to attract and retain better qualified staff. Presently, all staff in the Auditor General's office
    are civil servants managed under the Uganda Public Service rules and regulations
    administered by the Public Service Commission and the Ministry o f Public Service for
    appointments, promotions, grading, discipline, pay and terms o f service.
3.  The Cost of running AG's office should be a direct charge to the ConsolidatedFund.
    This will assure certainty o f funds availability for planned audits. Presently OAG's budget is
    controlled by ministry o f Finance Planning and Economic Development. Additionally,
    releases by Finance are not guaranteed as they can under-release depending on priorities in
    government's other programs.
4. Budgetary independence:To provide sufficient operational independence so that OAG can
   plan and organise duties o f staff and publish timely, well-researched audit reports without
    inhibitions based on shortage o f resources, OAG should not be eroded by external financial
    control. Presently OAG's budget i s appropriated by Parliament like any other department and
    i s subject to scrutinies o f the Ministry o fFinance with possible reductions.
5. Right of access to all relevantpapers and information.OAG should have rights o f access
    to all papers and information to obtain whatever explanations and information i s considered
    necessary for the proper execution o f hisher duties. Classified expenditure has been until
    recently outside OAG's access.

B.     OBJECTIVES
The thrust now i s on the enactment o f appropriate legislation to enhance the independence of
OAG through:
                  0   Amendment to the Constitution, and
                  0   Enactment o f an Audit Act.

C.     ROADMAP
 a.    As part of the constitution amendment process, proposals to address OAG's independence
         were submitted to the Constitutional Review Commission (CRC) and Cabinet. Part o f the
         outcome o f this activity, has been the issuing by government o f a White Paper. The



                                                    103

         White Paper contains comments on recommendations by the CRC as well as new
         proposals.
         "Government accepts the above recommendations aimed at preserving the independence
         and integrity of the Auditor General, and agrees with the age of retirement for the
         Auditor General is 65 years for voluntary retirement and 70 years for compulsory
         retirement as recommended. Government is also of the view that the Auditor General
         should be given autonomy to employ and discipline his or her own staff and should be
         expressly empowered to hire private firms of auditors to assist the Auditor General in
         theperformance of his or her constitutionalfunctions.
         Government is of the view that the Powers of the Auditor Generalfor the appointment of
         staff should be exercised in consultation with the Public Service Commission. Further,
         Government is of the view that the remuneration of staff of the Auditor General should
         be determined by the Salaries and Remuneration Board being established under the
         Constitution ''(page 70).
 b.      Itis noted that a Salaries andRemuneration Boardhas beenproposedby Government for
         purposes o f determining the salaries o f all public officers and all persons whose salaries
         are paid from the Consolidated Fund (page 122). Whereas this does not fully address the
         principles o f the Audit Bill as approved by the Cabinet, it i s a good starting point for
         lobbying Parliament to address the rest o f the independence criteria.

 c.      After the amendment to the Constitution, the final part will be the enactment o f an Audit
         Act). The Audit Bill 2002 has since been drafted and comments on it were submitted (in
         January 2003) to the Solicitor General by the Auditor General, and the Minister for
         Finance, Planning and Economic Development. Consultations continue to further
         enhance the Bill.


D.    ANTICIPATED MILESTONES
Inthe pursuit o f effective independence for OAG, certain issues will need special attention and
follow up.
Appointment and Dismissal. The president should nominate candidates and submit to
Parliament for appointment. Removal/dismissal should be in line with the removal o f judges o f
the highcourt.
Control and remuneration of staff. The Auditor General should have complete autonomy to
determine (a) the number o f staff he/she considers necessary to discharge the functions o f hisher
office; (b) appointment and removal of staff; staff grades, their pay and other conditions o f
service; etc.
Entire Cost OAG should be a direct charge to the Consolidated Fund. OAG should be accorded
self-accounting status with the budget o f his/her office charged to the CF. This would remove the
fear o f under-releases by MOFPEDwhich mayjeopardise OAG's planned audit programs.
Budgetary independence. The budget o f OAG should be considered by an independent Board
for submission direct to Parliament for approval o f the house. The MOFPED should no longer
have a controlling interest inOAGbudget and release processes.
Right of access. All expenditure with recourse to public funds should be open to OAG's
scrutiny. Independence in this area has been strengthened the PFAA (Classified Expenditure)




                                                 104

Regulations, July 2003, which allowed OAG access to classified expenditure. Application will to
classified expenditure incurred inyear ended June 2004.

Joint Teamto managethe Process
Inorder to ensure a harmonized and consistent effort to progress the draft Bill, ajoint team of
seven people with membership from the Office o f the Auditor General, and the Office o f the
Accountant General was formed in May 2004 to manage the process and ensure the Bill i s
progressed as appropriate.

E.        RECENT DEVELOPMENTS
InJuly 2005, Parliament passed the ConstitutionalAmendment Bill that contained provisions to
strengthen the operational independence o f the Auditor General. The Auditor General will be
empowered to hire and fire his staff in consultation with the Public Service Commission when
the constitutional provisions come into force. In addition, the provisions for the removal o f the
Auditor General have also been strengthened. The Auditor General will no longer be fired
directly by the President but through a tribunal appointed by the president who can only
recommend such action if the Auditor General failed to perform his functions, misbehaved or
there was misconduct.

A.      The Procurement Prior Action under PRSC 5 has been agreed with the Ministry o f

ANNEX 4: PRINCIPLES FORDETERMINING PRIOR ACTIONS
Principles for Prior Actions inthe Uganda PovertyReductionStrategySupport Credit (PRSC)
Programs. The following principles have beendeveloped during PRSC4 to guide the development ofprior
actions for the PRSC programs:
 1. About the Principles
     These principles concern the establishment o f Prior Actions (conditions) for disbursemento f the World
  Bank-supported PRSCs in Uganda. The principles are intended to be complementary and subordinate to
  the agreed "Partnership Principles between Government of Uganda and its Development Partners",
  Kampala September 2003.
2.   The Poverty ReductionSupport Credit (PRSC)
  a. The PRSC i s a core operation to implement the objectives of Uganda's Poverty Eradication Action
      Plafloverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PEAPPRSP), and the Bank Group's Country Assistance
      Strategy (CAS).
  b. The PRSCs are sequential annual credits, and each PRSC is seen as an annual step in a three-year
      medium-term reformprogram.
3.   Prior Actions
  a. Each PRSC is based on a set o f conditions ("prior actions") that the government fulfills before the
      gradcredit is presented to the World Bank Board. These prior actions are based on shared
      expectations between Government o f Uganda (GoU), the World Bank (WB), and other development
      partners.
  b. Prior actions should be based uponpolicy dialogue, and aligned with Uganda's Poverty Eradication
      Action Plan and country assistance priorities. Prior actions should normally correspond to all the
      major reform areas (pillars) o f the Poverty Eradication Action Program. The starting point for
      discussion is the set o f prior actions o f the preceding PRSC.
  C.  The flexibility inherent in the PRSC comes not from defining vague or easily-met prior actions, but
      from agreeing on specific and monitorable milestones and thenmeasuring progress against them, with
      reasonedjudgments allowing for disciplined adaptation.
  d. Agreement on prior actions is reached between GoU, the Bank, and other Development Partners
       shortly after pre-appraisal and before appraisal. Prior actions are at this stage considered binding, but
       are in exceptional circumstances adaptable in the face o f uncertainties inside and outside o f the
      program.
  e.   Completion o f the prior actions i s a condition for proceeding to the World Bank Board for approval o f
      the grandcredit.
   f. When prior actions are not met by negotiations, there are three alternatives: (i)     reduce support; (ii)
       delay program; and (iii) Credit intranches.
                                 release

 4. Anticipated Prior Actions
 a.  Each PRSC also includes a notional set o f tentative prior actions that are presented in the program
     documentation. The tentative prior actions are not binding for the next PRSC.
b. As one PRSC becomes effective, and the preparation of the next commences, the tentative actions
     identified under the first help shape and form the basis for preparation and agreement o f prior actions
     underthe next. I t is important for the reformprogramto have a predictable and sustained approach.
 c.  The anticipated prior actions should normally be discussed, and agreement onbroadareas to be covered
     should be reached at the pre-appraisal o f the preceding PRSC.
 d. Exact area and precise wording of the anticipated prior actions should be agreed during appraisal and
     negotiations of,thepreceding PRSC.
 e.  Where tentative prior actions may have to be revised, the fault may lie in a poor choice o f tentative
     actions, unexpectedly weak execution o f elements o f the reform program, faster than expected
     implementation o f elements o f the reform program, or changing circumstances outside the reform
     program.

ANNEX 6: GENDERISSUESINTHE OPERATION
Backgroundand Context. Inthe revision of the Poverty Eradication Action Plan(PEAP), the
Ugandanauthorities gave muchmore substantive attention to gender issues as a cross-cutting
dimension o f development. The 2004 PEAP is considerably stronger inarticulating the
relevance of gender concerns for growth andpoverty reduction. Specifically, the PEAP states
that "it is n o w clear that removing constraints caused byHIV/AIDS, environment, and above
all gender inequalities is key to achieving Uganda's poverty eradication goals." The PEAP
recognizes that economic incentives differ for m e n and for women, and that this has important
implications for GDP growth and output. To this end, the PEAP identifies "actions to enhance
gender equality" as one of the critical measures required to boost GDP growth to the 7 percent
per annum PEAP target.
102. GOU has followed through on the expanded focus given to gender in the PEAP by addressing
some key gender issues in the PRSC process. In PRSC4, this focused in particular on: (i)      preparing
gender and equity budget guidelines for the 2005/06 budget process, which were attached to the
Budget Circular issued in November 2004; (ii)launching a work program to address gender and
economic growth linkages in policymaking; (iii)     reinforcing the gender-focused actions o f the JLOS
undertakings, including support for tabling the Domestic Relations Bill (DRB) in Parliament, and for
preparation o f a Sexual Offences Bill (SOB); (iv) strengthening the efforts to address women's land
rights as part o f the implementationof the Land Sector Strategic Plan (LSSP); and (v) supporting the
establishment o f a coherent policy framework for gender mainstreaming through the adoption and
implementation o f a revisedNational Gender Policy.
Principalgender focusedactions ineachPEAPpillar
103. These PRSC4 actions provide a platform for consolidating and expanding the gender-focused
agenda o f PRSC5, which focuses on: (i)    supportingthe mainstreamingo f gender and equity objectives
inplanning andbudgetingthrough further work to implementthe gender and equity budget guidelines
issued in 2004; (ii)  deepening the work program on gender and growth linkages for policymaking in
Uganda, with increasing focus on trade issues and supporting the wider growth-enhancing policy
framework of the PEAP; (iii)    continuing the implementation o f the women's land component o f the
LSSP, with particular emphasis on completing the baseline assessment, developing mechanisms for
tracking the implementation o f the provisions o f the Land Act concerning family security of
occupancy, and carrying out better targeted information campaigns among stakeholders on women's
land rights; (iv) continuing to support the implementation o f the gender-focused elements o f the JLOS
undertakings, including strategies to support passage o f the DRB, and to launch the preparation o f the
SOB; and (v) continuing support to the formulation and implementation o f the country's National
Gender Policy. To this end, the Government has prepared a set o f gender-focused actions, which are
reflected inthe annualized PEAP results and policy matrix, as summarized below.
PEAPPillarI:Economicmanagement
104. Progress. Mainstreaming gender and equity objectives in planning and budgeting. The
November 2004 Budget Call Circular for the 2005/06 budget preparation cycle committed the
Government to mainstreaming gender and equity objectives in the planning and budgeting process.
Guidelines to support this effort were attached to the Circular.     Implementation has been difficult,
however, because the guidelines were widely perceived as overly complex and insufficiently user-
friendly. Moreover, efforts to provide technical support and capacity were mobilized, but too late to
affect significantly the preparation o f the 2005/06 budget.

address emerging challenges for Uganda's economic and social development. As the PRSC process

integration o f gender issues into planning and budgeting, through strengthening the integration o f
gender into Sectoral Budget Framework papers, and to use the growth-focused analytical work to

ANNEX 7: UGANDA AND THE MDGs

Uganda has made progress towards achieving the MDGs, but there i s uncertainty as to whether some
o f these targets can be met, especially in health. According to the UN Millennium Project Report
(2005), Uganda i s one o fthe countries that requires additional resources to reach all the MDGs.
Despite the recent slow down in the downward trend in poverty, Uganda i s still currently on track to
meet the incomepovertyMDG. Government commitment and leadership, and timely and coordinated
external support have also ensured Uganda i s making strong progress to meet the MDG for
education, although primary completion rates remain disappointing. 86% o f school age children are
currently enrolled in primary schools and in primary education (although not secondary), wealth and
gender biases have largely been addressed. Children in the lowest wealth quintile are as likely to go
to primary school as the highest wealth quintile.

Maternal mortality and under-5 mortality MDGs will be difficult to achieve. The last survey o f

Malaria incidence appears to have increased with resistance to existing treatment. However, the

The MDG for reducing HIV/AIDS was met in 1996 due to early intervention, political leadership,

ordinatingrole.
Uganda i s likely to achieve the access to the safe drinkingwater MDGtarget, but i s currently o f f track
to meet other targets under MDG 7 on environmental sustainability. The revised PEAP recognises
environment and natural resources (ENR) as key issues affecting the livelihoods, health and
vulnerability o f the poor. Environmental degradation i s likely to increase the costs incurred by
government in achieving wider PEAP objectives inaddition to beinga constraint on sustained growth.

been registered in the context o f reducing inequalities inprimary education. Inequalities in secondary

inUganda has drawn attention to inequality in access and control of productive resources; women's
overall work burden; and the problem of domestic violence. A gender team has been established
under the leadership o f the Ministry of Labour, Gender and Social Development, to actively pursue
gender mainstreaming through PEAP implementation. This will require, importantly, a clearer focus
on gender and equity issues inthe setting of the government's budget. Policy progress has beenmade,
but this needs to be translated into action.

performance criterion relating to the accumulation of new domestic budgetary arrears under

reduction strategy paper, the Poverty Eradication Action Plan, and the associatedjoint World
