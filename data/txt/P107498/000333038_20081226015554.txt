                                  1. INTRODUCTION

1.       This program document proposes the second o f a second series o f three Poverty
Reduction Support CreditdGrants for Benin(PRSC-5 ofPRSCs 4-6), designed to support
implementation of the Government's poverty reduction strategy. This second series aims
to improve the regulatory framework and policy environment for private investment,
including support for completion of cotton sector and infrastructure reforms, as well as to
support progress toward the MDGs by improving access to basic services and ensuring
greater efficiency o f public expenditures onhuman capital, andtoward better governance,
notably throughpublic financial management reforms.

2.       The reform policies supported by this new series are embedded in and aligned
with Benin's Strategy for Growth and Poverty Reduction (SCRP). The SCRP covering
the period 2007-2009     and the accompanying Joint Staff Advisory Note (JSAN) were
considered by the Executive Board on June 28, 2007. An Implementation Completion
Report (ICR) for the PRSC 1-3 series was conducted during 2008, and the lessons
learned from the implementation o f that PRSC series are taken into account inthe design
o fthis follow-up series.

3.       The design o f the series benefited from large consultations with various
stakeholders under the Government's leadership, and included close coordination with a
group o f donors who provide budget support to Benin on the SCRP implementation.
These consultations ensure government ownership of the supported policies and
harmonize donors around a common set o fkeyreforms.


                                2. COUNTRYCONTEXT

4.      With a per capita income o f US$570 (Atlas method, 2007), Benin ranks in the
lower income group o f countries. Following the 1989 fimdamental political change, the
country has enjoyed relative political stability and democracy has been greatly
strengthened with presidential elections regularly taking place with a peaceful transfer of
power. Important structural reforms have also beenundertaken and growth rates averaged
around 4.7 percent annually during the last decade, resulting in modest increases in per
capita incomes as well as improvements in human development. Nonetheless, poverty
remains widespread and the economy remains poorly diversified and vulnerable to
external shocks.

5.      The economy heavily relies on the agricultural sector, in particular cotton, and
transit trade with Nigeria. The agricultural sector accounts for about 32 percent o f GDP
and provides nearly 70 percent o f the country's workforce while cotton is the major
primary export commodity with about 40 percent o f total exports over 2006-07. Transit
trade with Nigeria is estimated to represent between 6.5 and 7.5 percent o f GDP and
explains the vulnerability of the economy to trade policy changes in its neighboring and
maintrade partner.


                                             3

6.       Benin held presidential elections in March 2006, and a new government was
installed in April o f that year.              Parliamentary elections were held in March 2007,
followed by several cabinet changes and municipal elections held in April 2008. During
2008, there were difficulties in maintaining a working majority coalition in the National
Assembly.       Tensions with the Assembly arose on several occasions, challenging the
ability o f the government to implement its economic program. However, the government
has made some important recent advances in establishing consensus on structural
reforms, and ambitions reforms in cotton, telecom and energy have finally been
approved. Political economy constraints are not considered to pose an undue risk to the
program supported bythe PRSC-5.

                            2.1       RECENT       ECONOMICDEVELOPMENTS

7.       Despite a difficult international environment, economic performance has
remained broadly satisfactory and macroeconomic stability maintained. Real GDP
growth continued to recover from 3.3 percent in 2003-05 to 3.8 percent in 2006 and 4.6
percent in2007 helled by increased activity inthe port and services sectors and a pickup
in agricultural production thanks to a favorable food harvest. Economic growth is
projected to hrther strengthen to 5.1 percent in 2008 underpinned by higher cotton
production, picked up activity in the trade and transportation sectors as well as in
construction and public works.. While inflation was broadly contained under the
WAEMU convergence criterion o f 3 percent over the past years, inflationary pressures
increased in 2008. Indeed, despite measures introduced by the Government' in late 2007
to cope with the world food andoil price increases and limit their impact on the poor and
despite adequate domestic food availability, overall inflation reached 5.1 percent in
August 2008 and is expected to average 8.8 percent in 2008 compared to 1.3 percent in
2007.

8.       Regarding external developments, the external current account deteriorated with
the deficit (excluding current official grants) widening from 6.4 percent o f GDP in2006
to 7.4 percent o f GDP in 2007 due to severe exogenous shocks and weakened
competitiveness o f the economy. However, thanks to substantial capital inflows, the
overall balance o f payments continued to record a surplus (3.2 percent o f GDP). As a
result, Benin contributed positively to the net foreign assets o f the regional central bank
with gross reserves averaging 8.5 months o f imports. The current account is expected to
further deteriorate in 2008 to 10.4 percent o f GDP. Increased balance of payments
financing needs would be met by an augmentation o f access to IMF resources and
expected additional resources from donors.

              2.2     MACROECONOMIC ANDDEBT   OUTLOOK             SUSTAINABILITY

12.      The Government's macroeconomic objectives set out in the SCRP for 2007-09
are to accelerate growth, reduce poverty and lift Benin to emerging-economy status.
Underthe assumption that the pace o f reforms would be accelerated, economic growth is
expected to progressively recover from 3.3 percent in2004-2006 to 5.6 percent in 2008-
2010 despite significant risks o f a difficult external environment (table 1). Inflation i s
expected to decelerate to 6.5 percent in 2009 from 8.8 percent in 2008 as inflationary
pressures associated with the food and fuel crisis abate and to fall gradually to below 3
percent in2010 andthereafter as medium-term supply responses take effect.

13.      Economic growth is expected to be drivenby: (i)a more dynamic services sector;
(ii)apickupinagriculturalproduction; and(iii) increaseinpublicinvestments in
                                                              an
infrastructure. However, significant risks of a continued difficult international
environment, in the form o f weakened demand for exports and financial flows and
volatility incommodity prices and exchange rates, could dampen economic prospects. In
the short term, Benin's ongoing recovery i s expected to be resilient due to the following
factors. Recent structural reforms -- improving the fimctioning o f the cotton sector,
increasing the availability o f electricity, and creating important opportunities in the
telecom sector - should support the economic recovery. Inaddition, some cotton exports
for 2009 have been placed under contract at favorable prices while the recent
strengthening o f the dollar will raise the CFA price o f cotton. Furthermore, current
projections indicate that Nigeria's transit trade with Benin will remain fairly robust and
FDI is expected to hold up given ongoing projects and Benin's long-term growth
prospects. However, a protracted global economic downturn could lead to slower growth
in Benin over the medium term. Despite these risks, following a joint review in
September 2008, both the Bank and IMF teams concluded that the macroeconomic
framework was adequate.

14.      The investment ratio is projected to increase from 21.4 percent in 2007 to 24.3
percent in2010 fuelled by an ambitious public investment program and to a lesser extent
by private investment. The external current account deficit is expected to slightly
improve to 7.5 percent of GDP in2010 after a sharp deteriorationin2008-09 (9.7 percent

payment order basis and excluding grants) would widen from 1.4 percent of GDP in2007
to about 4.2 percent o f GDP in 2008-10 reflecting increased social and development
expenditures requiredfor the implementation of the strategy's Priority Action Plan. Total
expenditures would increase from 22 percent of GDP in2007 to 24.2 percent o f GDP by
2010, o f which 48 percent would be allocated to priority sectors. External conditions are
likely to remain challenging and pose significant risks to the above macroeconomic
outlook.    A deeper-than-expected global downturn caused by the financial crisis could
dampenexport demand and financial flows (notably FDIand aid). Other possible shocks
include terms of trade volatility andor deterioration, euro appreciation, bad weather
affecting agriculture, and loss of confidence that could cause capital outflows.

15.      Preliminary results from the 2008 Debt Sustainability Analysis (DSA) jointly
conducted by IDA and IMF teams confirm that Benin's risk o f debt distress is moderate
and that the country's external debt would remain sustainable over the medium-term.
Debt cancellation under H P C and MDRI initiatives and a prudent borrowing policy
helped to reduce the country's total public external debt from 58.3 percent in 2000 to
around 12.5 percent o f GDP at end-2007. However, although the risks to medium-term
debt sustainability are estimated to be moderate, the D S A also stressedthat it i s important
that the government continue to conduct a prudent borrowing policy and rely only on
concessional loans and grants.

16.      The external financing requirements are estimated at US$304 million in 2008,
resulting inan estimated total financing gap o f US$125 million after projected debt relief
(includingMDRIresources). This gap is expected to be fully coveredby expected budget
support from the European Union, other development partners and IDA including the
EFAcatalytic h dgrant. For 2009, the estimated total financing gap after projected debt
relief i s about US$109 million, which will be partly filled by funds from the proposed
PRSC-5. The remaining gap for 2009 is expected to be filled by budget support from
other multi-lateral andbi-lateral donors.

17.      Benin's first Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper was discussed by the IDA and
IMF Boards in March 2003. Since the new administration took office in 2006,
development policy has been governed by the five-year Document d 'Orientations
Stratkgiques (DOS) while the revised PRS -- the Strategy for Growth and Poverty
Reduction (SCRP) -- was designed as the medium-term implementation plan for the

                                3.5    LESSONS   LEARNED

40.     The design o f the proposed operation has benefited from a number o f lessons
drawn from guidance from Operations Policy and Country Services (OPCS), and the

implementation o f the first PRSC series (PRSC 1-3) and PRSC-4.6 Key lessons are
highlighted below:

    Selectivity and simplicity o f the design are essential to the success o f the operation.
    Obstacles due to administrative resistance to change and lack o f capacity cannot be
    underestimated. There i s also a need to be realistic about the scope and pace o f
    reform. Thus, this second PRSC series deepens support inthe areas o f reforms where
    progress has been slow and focuses on critical bottlenecks. For instance, while the
    first PRSC series laid the foundations for successful change in key service delivery
    sectors, the approach was not targeted enough to the poor, and thus this PRSC series
    increased its equity focus.

    Lending for development policy should not rely on conditionality alone to catalyze
    and implement reforms. To be hlly effective, the policy dialogue must be supported
    by other instruments that identify institutional weaknesses and help build country
    capacity step by step (OPCS, 2001). The proposed operation is complemented by
    support and technical assistance providedby other ongoing Bank projects (see section
    3.3).

    The design o f Bank support for policy reforms needs to fully integrate the efforts o f
    other active donors. The proposed operation continues this approach through close
    coordination with key ongoing donor operations, notably those o f donors providing
    budget support (see section 3.4).

    It is important to properly align the PRSC with budget management, so that the
    priority components o f the PRSC and the performance budgets o f those ministries are
    supportive o f each other. It i s also important that budget support be phased with the
    budget cycle, in order to properly impact on budget composition, and to provide
    budget support ina timelymanner. UnderPRSC-5, joint donor support for the budget
    system reform action planwill reinforce this approach.

    The effectiveness o f the PRSCs in supporting sector reforms can be highly increased
    by using a sector wide approach, as was successfully experienced in rural water
    supply. The approach federated donors around the government's objectives and
    allowed a mix o f central government programs, decentralized programs, budget
    support and projects to combine efficiently. Under the proposed operation, this
    approach will continue in the water sector and expand to the health and transport
    sectors.

    Attention should be paid to the quality o f outcome indicators at the outset, making
    sure that these indicators are relevant to the objectives, and that they can be measured
    at every step o f implementation. It is crucial to align closely performance indicators
    for the PRSCs with those for the PRSP, the CAS, and the program budgets o f key
   ministries. It is also important that the various donors involved inbudget support use
   the same set o f indicators. The exercise o fharmonizing these indicators is inprogress.

4.3.1 Performan ce-based Public ExpenditureManagement

86.     Over the last years, the Government has demonstrated its commitment to
strengthen the public financial management system and to move to a performance-based
public expenditure management. This results-oriented budget system i s at the core o f the
Government's coordinated efforts to implement the SCRP. The programs identified as
best promoting private sector growth and access to quality basic services were included
inthe program budgets ofthe line ministries, andthe budget system reforms support the
implementation of these program budgets. The reforms also supported the institution of
performance monitoring reports which promoted a vital focus on results.

87.     The SCRP steering committee, based in the Ministry of Economy and Finance,
works with all priority sectors to support the design, execution and monitoring o f their
programbudgets and to make these the foundation of the use o f national resources. The
most recent and important reform introduced by the authorities was the adoption o f a
policy framework for Results-Oriented Budget Management (Gestion Budgktaire Axke
sur Zes R&suZtats,GBAR) in December 2005. The GBAR policy was based on the 2005
CFAA update and other public financial management reviews, includingrecent PERs. In
April 2006, the Government adopted an operational action plan for implementing the
policy and measuring progress. The operational action plan contains a series o f measures
aimed at improving the macroeconomic framework setting and tax administration;
strengthening public financial and results-oriented management; enhancing internal and
external financial control; and accelerating administrative reforms. However, progress in
implementing the ambitious GBAR reform agenda has been mixed to date as noted by
recent assessments, in particular the 2007 Public Expenditure and Financial
Accountability (PEFA) assessment.

88.     The PEFA assessment confirmed that Benin's PFM has improved since 2000
through progress made in institutionalizing program budgets, including procedures for
their preparation, implementation and control; advancing the computerization o f budget
execution, accounting and reporting; strengthening the design and implementation o f
procurement reforms; and implementing the reforms in internal and external budget
control. The PEFA also identified weaknesses, notably: poor management o f wage bill
budgeting and salary        disbursements;   weak   treasury   management;   insufficient
management and supervision o f financial risks associated with public firms and
decentralized spending authorities; and difficulties in obtaining information on budget
execution.

89.     On the basis o f the PEFA assessment, the Government has prepared a detailed
action plan to strengthen the country's PFM and improve the efficiency o f public
resources use. This four-year plan, which refines and integrates GBAR priorities, has
been discussed with development partners during a workshop inJune 2008. The proposed
program o f reforms i s comprehensive and covers most o f the weaknesses identified inthe
PEFA assessment.The next steps for the authorities are to improve the sequencing o fthe
short andmedium term actions for PFMreform, taking into account capacity constraints.
Key donors including the EU, the Netherlands and DANIDA are planning to set-up a
fundto support the implementationo fthe actionplan.

90.      Budget execution in 2007 benefited from the more systematic application o f
budget procedures, as spending by exceptional procedures was reduced to less than 6
percent of the total, reference prices were used in procurement, and internal debts were
cleared. Yet this greater adherence to official procedures exposed a number o f gaps in
the capacity to make the system work as designed, and budget execution inthe first half

4.3.2 Budget Accounting and Controls

93.     The ambitious public finance reform program has increased accountability in
sector ministries and transparency in accounting and financial information. Under
PRSC1-3, the Integrated System o f Public Financial Management (SIGFIP) became
operational and ensures greater transparency inthe management o fpublic resources. The
new nomenclature facilitates budget presentation and identification o f expenditures for
poverty reduction. The use o f exceptional procedures was audited in 2004 and 2006 and
followed upwith actions which reduced their use.

94.     The reliability o f financial reports has improved since 2006 with the interface
between SIGFIP and ASTER. However, the credibility o f the accounting system is
underminedby long delays inthe production o f financial reports which can affect data
accuracy. This situation is mainly due to the lack o f accountants. The Ministry o f

95.     Despite these improvements in the reliability o f the overall budgetary system,

 framework. The decree has given a crucial role o f coordinating internal controls to the

4.3.4 Strengthening the Legal and Judicial Sector

102.     The Government has recognized the importance o f an effective judicial and legal
system as a key factor o f sustainable economic growth. With the Bank's support, in2001
the Government prepared andadopted a ten-year IntegratedProgramfor the development
and modernization o f Benin's legal and judiciary systems. Key planned reforms o f the
Program include: (i)    improving the capacity o f the Ministry o f Justice (MJLDH) by
encouraging a better organization o f the Ministry, ensuring the recruitment and training
o f staff and the provision of the necessary equipment; (ii)   improving service delivery of
the courts by rehabilitating existing court buildings and constructing new courts,
reforming the status o f the court clerk's office, better training for judges and clerks, and
providing equipment for the courts; (iii)     modernization of the penitentiary system by
improving the infrastructure and living conditions o f the prison system, providing
training facilities in prisons and support for social reintegration o f inmates; and (iv)
ensuring protection o f human rights by modernizing the legal framework, providing
support to NGOs andbuildingawareness among the population o f their rights.

103.     Overall, implementation o f the MJLDH's ten-year reform program continues to
progress slowly as mentioned by the recent mid-term review (carried out in M a y 2008),
with deferral o f planned activities from year to year, especially with respect to reforms
related to human rights or the penitentiary system. The weak internal capacity and
coordination, and chronic insufficiency o f funds within the MJLDH, continue to be the
keybottlenecks inthis area.

104.     One o f the primary issues o fthe justice systeminBeninis the excessive slowness
o f the judicial process due principallyto insufficient number o f courts, judges and other
members o f the legal profession, and their inadequate geographic distribution which
contribute to centralization and congestion o f the activities o f the courts. Infact out o f the
28 tribunaux depremiire instance (TPIs) created by Law n.2001-37 o f August 27, 2002,
only 8 are in use. Inresponse to these concerns, the Ministry's program o f construction
o f additional courts has been reinforced through the financial support o f the EU and the
MCA, which are together financing the rehabilitation o f 5 o f the TPIs in 2007 and the
remaining 3 in2008, and the construction o f a total o f 10 additional courts over the next
couple o f years. In parallel, the ten-year reform program o f the MJLDH continues to
provide for the yearly recruitment o f a minimumnumber ofjudges and clerks, as well as
their initial and continuing professionaltraining. The Government's recent adoption o f a
new law clarifying the status o f law clerks and requiring their professional training
should increase the effectiveness o f the judicial process. The programs financed by the
EUand the M C A further provide for the computerization o f the judicial process for the
newly constructed or rehabilitated courts, which should permit to accelerate the judicial
procedures.

4.3.6 PublicAdministration

107.     The civil service statute today inforce dates to 1986, i.e. a text enacted before the
political and economic liberalization o f the 1990s, which inparticular features automatic
career advancement regardless o f performance.       The wage bill (about 40 percent o f
recurrent expenditure) i s under control, but salaries do not succeed in retaining skilled
professionals. Also, years o f recruitment freezes following the fiscal crisis o f 1989 have
resulted ina skewed age distribution o f the civil servants. The forthcoming retirement o f
many civil servants - many o f which belong to the categories A and B - needs to be
planned for. It i s also an opportunity to adopt strict and transparent recruitment rules in
order to strengthen the civil service. Strategic planning and enforcement o f transparent
recruitment rules are thus top priorities, which must also take into account the need to
redress the geographical imbalance incivil servants' distribution across the country.

108.     The civil service reform agenda is moving forward, albeit slowly and unevenly.
The dialogue on a new civil service statute (and also on a possible local government's
civil service statute) remains stalled: the ESW on Civil Service Reform described the
deep constraints impeding progress o f this activity. However, many concrete steps are
being taken which can, within the existing legal framework, improve human resources
management. The Government has launched the following activities. Deconcentrationo f
some key human resource management processes to the education ministries i s effective
and is being rolled over to the Ministries o f Health and Agriculture (with a parallel


                                             33

program o f training and capacity building). The directory o f job descriptions and
recruitment conditions for high level technical positions was finalized and validated,
providing the administration with an important instrument to promote merit-based
recruitment, promoting a depoliticized administration.

109.     Financing i s being secured to overhaul the FUR, the Humanresources database,
and install an integrated payroll and human resources database. The system will be a
deconcentrated instrument integrating all HR management data, and including a forward-
planning capability. With respect to forward staff planning, a decision was taken to
appoint a humanresources manager in each ministry, and a consultant i s being hired to
elaborate a recruitment policy and a procedures manual. The creation of a Ministryfor
Administrative and Institutional reform aims to strengthen coordination o f reforms being
designed or implemented inthe country (including decentralization andpublic finances).

4.3.7    Transparency and Anti-Corruption

110.     Promoting good governance and reducing corruption remains a priority for the
Government o f Benin. A number o f measures that relate to the governance environment
are being addressed with a particular accent on the justice sector, civil service reform,
decentralization, public finance, the promotion o f transparency and the fight against all
forms o f corruption.

111. In 2007 the Government of Benin, with technical support from the World Bank
Institute and financial support from the African Development Bank and the World Bank,
completed the diagnostic survey on the state o f governance and corruption. The survey,
which covered 3,260 respondents from households, private sector firms and public sector
institutions, was conducted following a participatory methodology that promoted civil
society engagement and an open dialogue on governance challenges. The authorities
disseminated the results o f the survey at the national level through workshops with broad
participationby government, civil society andthe press. The results o f the survey showed
that public services playing a central role inthe development ofthe country, like customs,
taxes, the judiciary, the police, electricity and water, public works and to a minor extent
health and education, are seriously affected by poor governance and corruption. The
survey results also showed that users o f public services spend substantial sums on non-
official payments to access public services.

112.     The IGE in coordination with the Observatoire de Lutte contre la corruption
(OLC) will lead the revision o f the National Anticorruption Strategy (originally prepared
in2001) as well as preparation of an action plan for the implementationof the strategy.
The revised strategy will focus on actions that will reduce corruption inkey public sector
institutions but will also place a strong accent on strengthening the demand side o f
governance. Reinforcing civil society, the role of the National Assembly, improving the
media environment and information flow are areas that play an important role in the
promotion o ftransparency, accountability and inreducing corruption.

                               6.2      POVERTY AND SOCIAL IMPACTS

 124.     The proposed PRSC-5 is expected to have a significant positive direct impact on
 poverty reduction. InPillar I,components are designed to improve competitiveness and
 agricultural diversification, thus helping to promote investment, growth and the rural
 economy. The Government's 2006 annual report on the social situation (Rapport sur le
 Profil Social National) put emphasis on the strong link between unemployment,
 underemployment and poverty. Thus, support for accelerating economic growth and
 private investment, including through an improved business environment and
 infrastructure, would support employment creation and poverty reduction.                            Land
 ownership insecurity i s also seen as one of the major causes ofpoverty; thus providing an
 adequate legal framework to protect small farmers and micro entrepreneurs, encouraging
 savings and providing more micro-credits will contribute to poverty reduction. The PRSC
 program also supports increased access to electricity, especially in rural areas. As has

                                                     40

been demonstrated inWorld Bankresearch, most of the poor do not currently have access
to electricity, thus providing access to the grid i s the single most important subsidy
offered to the poor, as the alternative sources of energyusedby those without grid access
are more expen~ive.~The focus on expanding access o f the rural population to electricity
through the rural electrification fund is particularly noteworthy.

125.     In addition, the program promotes better functioning o f the cotton sector, and
results from the 2002 household survey show that households deriving income from
cotton are overwhelmingly rural and poor.. A 2004 PSIA analysis o f planned reforms in
the cotton sector in Benin (Report No. 29951-BJ) confirms the social value o f pursuing
the agenda foreseen, including the privatization of SONAPRA. This analysis was usedto
support the design o f actions inthe cotton sector, including activities to stabilize incomes
for cotton farmers when world prices or exchange rates are unfavorable. Household
survey results will be updated in the planned poverty assessment (see CAS Report No.
46485-BJ) and PSIA-related issues in the agriculture sector will be revisited in an
upcoming study on agricultural growth and diversification. Also, in2006 the Government
o f Benin (Obsewatoire du Changement Social; Ministere du Developpement de
Z'Economie et des Finances) undertook a PSIA on land reform with technical support
from UNDP. This analysis fed into the land tenure reform component o f the Private
Sector Letter o f Development Policy, whose implementation i s supported by the PRSC
series. The study noted that converting living permits into ownership rights was o f
critical importance for the poor, and especially for ensuring rights to the benefits o f
investments tied to the land.

126.     Components o f Pillar I1 are clearly aimed at improving access and quality o f
services delivered to the poor. The plannednew poverty assessment and upcoming PER
will allow for more refined analysis o f the impact o f social service spending (see CAS
Report No. 46485-BJ).               Finally in Pillar 111, the strengthening o f public sector
management is expected to enhance the efficiency and effectiveness o f public resources
inthe delivery of development services andinimproving the legal framework governing
the private sector. It should be noted that equitable development is an explicit objective
of the SCRP, and the PRSC-5 supports it through components emphasizing access to
basic services for vulnerable groups.

                                  Box 1: Good Practice Principles o n Conditionality
Principle 1:Reinforce Ownership
The new PRSC4-6 series builds on the foundation o f the progress achieved during the first PRSP as well as o n the
second PRSP (SCRP) adopted by Government in April 2007 after broad-based consultations with sector ministries,
civil society, the private sector, unions and financial and technical partners. The new PRSC series supports policies
and programs laid out inthe SCRP and for which there i s a clear evidence o f strong ownership and commitment o f the
Government. A one-day consultation review held in September 2007 on progress in implementing the
recommendations o f the 2005 Review of World Bank Conditionality in Benin concluded that the PRSC-supported
program was well-aligned with the SCRP's priorities and that Government's ownership o f the program has
significantly improved.    Participants also noted that analytic work plays an important role in informing policy
discussions and Government's choices but pointed out that fkrther progress should be made in disseminating their
results.

Principle 2: Agree upfront with thegovernment and otherfinancialpartnerson a coordinated accountability
framework
The PRSC-5 support is summarized in a policy matrix. The PRSP process and the move towards a programmatic
approach have fostered coordination between the Bank and development partners that are providing budget support
(AfDB, EU, Denmark, the Netherlands, and Switzerland) and are helping to improve alignment o f their assistance to
Benin's PRSPhlTEF. In October 2006, an M o U was discussed between the Government and the development
partners providing budget support to consolidate this cooperation. This M o U includes a proposed joint matrix of
proposed reforms with related indicators. It translates the desire o f the Government and technical and financial
development partners to ensure that budget support becomes the key instrument o f their cooperation in the
implementation o f the SCRP and related sector strategies and policies, and to ensure a good coordination o f the
various donor interventions. A one-day workshop was held among all donors inFebruary 2007 to improve this MOU
and adherence to the Paris Declaration. Further discussions took place duringthe joint mission in September 2007 and
the revised MOUwas signed on December 4, 2007. The government and development partners have also established a
Monitoring and Evaluationframework o f the Paris Declaration.

Principle 3: Customize the accountability framework and modalities of Bank support to country circumstances
 In October 2006, the Government announced its strategic orientation for its 2006-2011 Development Plan. These
strategic choices formed the framework for the SCRP o n which this operation i s based. On the issue o f politically
sensitive reforms, participants o f the conditionality review noted that in some cases the political environment and
social aspects o f the reforms have not been fully taken into account and thus led to important delays. Therefore, the
Bank support for sensitive policy reforms under the new PRSC series is based on a clear understanding o f Benin's
specific circumstances and the pace set by the new Government to ensure stronger ownership.

Principle 4: Chooseonly actions criticalfor achieving results as conditionsfor disbursement
The proposed PRSC-5 includes a set o f prior actions which are embedded in a results framework with specific targets,
incoherence with the SCRP objectives. The prior actions are focused on critical policies to support ongoing reforms
in the areas o f sustainable economic growth, improved service delivery, and better governance. This approach helps
overcome institutional bottlenecks for priority sectors and strengthen institutional capacities, with support of other
donors and IDA'Songoing operations. Although significant progress has been made in streamlining the policy matrix
supported by the donors providing budget support, the Benin consultations concluded that further progress can be
made inreducing the number o fbenchmarks.

Principle 5: Conduct transparent progress reviews conducive to predictable and performance-based financial
support
As agreed with Government, joint SCRP reviews andjoint budget and appraisal missions take place yearly. To ensure
timely feedback into the policymaking and budget processes, efforts have been made by the Government and the
donors to coordinate various review processes around the SCRP implementation, leading to a successful alignment o f
review cycles with the budget preparation timetable and financing needs. However, further progress i s needed in
providing clear baselines for results. The M o U proposes to use annually available performance indicators from the
SCRP to assess progress against agreed targets.

                           6.6     ENVIRONMENTAL      ASPECTS

136.    The activities supported by the proposed operation are not likely to have
significant negative environmental impacts. The potential environmental and social
effects associated with the proposed PRSC-5 are mainly related to the rural development
(including cotton), energy, health and education sectors. As noted in the project
document for PRSC-4, the new series o f PRSCs continues to draw on extensive
Environment Assessments undertaken during the preparation o f PRSC 1-3 which took
into account the environmental and social analysis conducted for other Bank operations
as well as the capacity o f national and local public sector agencies to manage the
environmental and social issues facing Benin. Furthermore, support for Benin's
environmental compliance i s provided by the environmental management activities in
several IDA investment projects inthe sectors o f energy, forestry, cotton, andmalaria.

137.    The Ministryo fEnvironment adopted a Medium-Term Strategic Planin2002 that
highlightedthe mainenvironmental objectives, andthe new Ministryo fEnvironmentand
Natural Protection (MEW)i s now updatingthat strategy. The MEPN, recently separated
from the Agriculture Ministry, and the Benin Environmental Agency (DE) share the
responsibility and objectives in the areas o f environmental management and impact
assessments. This mandate focuses on the country's major environmental threats. It
evolves around the following activities: a) strengthening and deepening the regulatory
framework, b) developing environmental compliance and enforcement tools for
environmental impact assessment, c) protecting coastal resources and controlling coastal
erosion, d) controlling urban transport pollution, and e) increasing access to urban
sanitation and drainage services. To this end, small environmental units within key line
ministries have been established and trained in identifying and implementing measures


                                           45

for mitigating any adverse environmental or social impacts of public investment
programs. The degree o f activity o f these units varies across sectors. The Agriculture
Ministrystill retains important environment functions, and the unitswithin the Transport
Ministry and Water Directorate have been reinforced. Units in certain other ministries
are hinderedby lack o f training and resources.

138.    The Benin Environmental Agency has overall responsibility for ensuring that
Environmental Assessments are carried out for all projects in accordance with national
standards. Environmental compliance certificates and environmental management plans
for environmental impact assessments are designed by ABE, ratified by the MENP and
then sent to every sector focal point, and ABE puts them on the online environmental
database. Sector and regional focal points send monthly reports to ABE. ABE analyzes
these monthly reports and sets up a quarterly meeting with the sector and regional focal
points in order to organize the national report on environmental monitoring. ABE
elaborates an annual report, which i s sent to both sector and regional focal points and
shared on line.

 139.   The 2006/7 Annual Progress Report on the SCRP cites the recent increase in
training on environmental issues for various stakeholders and the completion o f new
guidelines on management o f shared ecosystems and strategic environmental
assessments. It also cites the completion o f 43 environmental impact reports and 5
industrial audits. Furthermore, the APR provides statistical results on the programs to
fight deforestation and soil degradation. Sector experts believe that investments in soil
enrichment and crop rotation will become more effective as land rights, and therefore
control o fthe benefits o f these investments, become more secure.

140.    The reforms in the rural water sector aim at increasing water supply access and
equity and improving sustainability o f the existing facilities (including the piloting o f
PPP arrangement in small towns). The Ministry o f Energy and Water Resources had
adopted appropriate measures to deal with the potential environment and social issues
associated with the planned investments. It was agreed that these measures, including
carrying out o f mandatory full EIA for small dams, control o f wastewater and mud
around wells, and compliance with the requirements pertaining to the minimum distance
between wells and latrines, will continue to be enforced by the existing functional EIA
unito fthe ministry.

                            6.7     R I S K S AND RISKS MITIGATION

141.    Several risks could jeopardize the expected outcomes and benefits of this
operation. These include: (i)     weakening commitment to and consensus for structural
reforms to sustain economic growth; (ii)      weak capacity to implement sector programs and
to promote reforms; and (iii)  vulnerability to commodity and exchange rate volatility.

142.    Weakening commitment to and consensus for structural reforms. Special
interests may try to weaken the momentum for structural reforms. Also, the tensions with
the National Assembly may challenge the ability o f the Government to implement its
reform program. To help mitigate this risk, IDA i s supporting engagement with
stakeholders, including the Parliament, to build a consensus on key reforms as well as
leverage the dissemination o f AAA, such as the C E M and the telecoms study, as a means


                                                46

to increase knowledge on the broad advantages to be gained through planned structural
reforms.

143.    Weak capacity to implement sector programs. The expenditure execution rate i s
low in many sectors, particularly for investment spending. The sector ministries have
difficulties in implementingthe program budgets since they are not filly aligned with the
classic form o f budgets that are voted by the National Assembly. The Government is
aware o f the weaknesses and the MEPDEAP has identified the main causes o f low
execution: (i) o f specific sector strategies; (ii) o f sector procurement plans and
                lack                                  lack

ministries' lack o f capacities inthe key functions ofprogramming, financial management
andprocurement. Section 4.3.1 details specific measures identified by the Government to

144.    Vulnerability to commodity price and exchange rate fluctuations.            Benin's
economic growth remains vulnerable to external shocks. In particular, Benin i s
vulnerable to fluctuations o fworld prices for cotton and oil, andto the appreciation of the
Euro to which the CFAF i s pegged at a fixed rate against the U S dollar. Since cotton is
the main export product o f the economy, low international prices hurt the country's
performance and negatively affect the rural population. The potential fiscal impact o f
continuous low cotton prices i s also significant. In addition, high oil prices would affect
the country's economic performance.        Furthermore, external conditions are likely to
remain challenging and pose significant risks to the country's macroeconomic outlook.
A deeper-than-expected global downturn caused by the financial crisis could dampen
export demand and financial flows (notably FDI and aid). Other possible shocks include
terms o f trade volatility, bad weather affecting agriculture, and loss o f confidence that
could cause capital outflows. These risks are mitigated by specific measures supported
bythe proposed operation andplannedoperations inthe energy sector to open the market
to new producers, in agriculture to diversify production, in cotton to improve the
marketing o f cotton production, and to strengthen the regulatory framework and the
policy environment to support economic diversification.

IMF Executive Board Completes Fifth Review Under PRGF
Arrangement with Benin and Approves US$1.3 Million
Disbursement

Press Release No. 081323
December 15, 2008

The Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) has completed the fifth
review of Benin's economic performance under the SDR 15.48 million (about US823.1
million) Povertv Reduction and Growth Facilitv (PRGF] arrangement. The arrangement
was approved on August 5, 2005 (see Press Release No. 05/190), and subsequently
extended to August 4, 2009. I n June 2008 it was augmented by 150 percent in order to
help the country deal with rising food and oil prices.
I n completing the review, the Board approved Benin's request for waivers of the non-
observance of two performance criteria pertaining to net domestic financing and to the
contracting of new non-concessional external debt. The completion of the review enables
Benin to draw an amount equivalent to SDR 0.88 million (about US$1.3 million).
Following the Executive Board's discussion, Mr. Murilo Portugal, Deputy Managing
Director and Acting Chair, stated:
"Supported by prudent fiscal policies, economic growth in Benin continues to strengthen,
while the inflationary impact of higher international food and fuel prices is subsiding. The
authorities are cognizant of the need to keep the momentum on fiscal consolidation and
structural reforms, in order to preserve macroeconomic stability and to raise growth
further to help reduce poverty. These efforts have become more crucial with the
weakening of the global economy.
"The authorities have allowed full pass-through of international prices, accompanied by
well-targeted safety net measures. This will help the economy adjust and foster a
positive agricultural supply response. The authorities are persevering in their efforts to
improve governance in the revenue agencies and strengthen tax and customs
administration. It will also be critical to improve public expenditure management and
bolster civil service reform. These reforms will create increased fiscal space for poverty
reduction and growth-supporting expenditures.
"The preservation of fiscal and public debt sustainability will require continued prudent
public debt management, reliance on highly concessional external financing, and limited
recourse to nonconcessional financing in the regional financial market.
"Further structural reforms are needed to free Benin's growth potential. The recent
privatization of SONAPRA's ginning activities and Continental Bank-Benin will help
strengthen Benin's competitiveness. The authorities are encouraged to complete the
comprehensive reform strategy for the cotton sector, restructure the state-owned
telecommunications and electricity companies, and enhance the competitiveness of the
port of Cotonou. These reforms are critical to improving the delivery of public services,
lowering production costs, and alleviating capacity constraints," Mr. Portugal said.

                                   INTRODUCTION

 1.This development policy letter describes the reform program supported by the
fifth Poverty ReductionSupport Grant (PRSC-5), aiming at providing support for the
implementation of Benin's strategy for growth and poverty reduction which was
adopted by the Government in February 2007. It highlightsthe major economic and
financial orientations for 2008-2010.

2. This letter which is a follow up to that of 21st May 2007, describes the actions
that the Government intends to carry out in the mid-term for the effective
implementationof its strategy for growth and poverty reduction (SCRP) focusing on
measures that will enable increased efficiency and impact of public expenses and
thus contribute to improve public services especially for the poorest. Attached as
annex to this letter is the matrix of the updated policies and measures supported by
the Poverty Reduction Support Grant (PRSC), prepared in collaboration with IDA'S
team and the other development partners including the European Union, Denmark,
Switzerland, the Netherlands, France and Germany. This matrix summarizes and
specifies the content of this letter.

3. The matrix of policies and measures reflect the effort in defining priorities on the
basis of the budget Constraints identified within the framework of 2008-2010 mid-
term expenditures and the institutional constraints affecting the administration's
absorption capacity. The implementation of actions and the achievement of the
results highlighted in bold in the matrix are doomed crucial for the success of the
reform program. Therefore, these actions and results are prerequisitesfor PRSC-5.

1. Contextandrecentdevelopment

4. At the end of the implementation of the Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (DSRP)
for 2003-2005, the Government developed and adopted in February 2007 the
Strategy for Growth and Poverty Reduction (SCRP) for 2007-2009. This SCRP makes
operational the strategic development orientations (OSD) which highlight the major
points of the mid-term development vision of the Government for 2006-2011. The
major actions of SCRP are incorporated into the PriorityAction Program (PAP) which
ensures connection between the Strategy and the program-budgets.

5. The 2007 progress report for the first year of implementationof SCRP 2007-2009
was drafted in August 2008. This report was jointly reviewed by the Government
and Technical and FinancialPartners.

6. This year, the implementation of the SCRP was marked by two major crises which
undoubtedly had significant impact on the implementation of the actions contained
in the Priority Action Program. These crises are the namely the energy crisis and the
food crisis which occurred during the fourth quarter of 2007. Short, medium and
long term measures taken by the Government include: (i) capacity building for
ONASA and reduction of taxation on largely consumed products; (ii) Emergency


                                          59

Food Security Support Program; (iii) diversification of the economy through the
development of other agricultural product lines; (iv) provision of agricultural inputs
for food crops; and (v) development of the Ouem6 and Niger valleys. These
measures have enabled to relatively have the situation under control and facilitated
the implementation of actions aiming at improving the living conditions of the
populations.

7. The Government intends to pursue its efforts to create a conducive environment
for vigorous economic growth that could significantly reduce poverty. That is why it
is requesting the support of IDA through the poverty reduction support grant
(PRSC), to provide support in the implementation of its reform program and take
part in the financing of its Strategy for Growth and Poverty Reduction.


2.       Reform programfor 2008-2010

8. The Strategy for Growth and Poverty Reduction (SCRP) is organized around five
(05) major strategic Pillars including:
Pillar 1:Acceleration of growth
Pillar 2: Developmentof infrastructure
Pillar 3: Human capital building
Pillar 4: Promotion of good governance
Pillar 5: Balancedand sustainable developmentof the national space.

9. The 2008-2010 reform program is organized around these same strategic axes. In
addition to these measures aiming at improvingthe macroeconomicframework, the
government wants to strengthen the progress in the achievement of poverty
reduction objectives, and especially these related to the social indicators. Moreover,
in December 2007, the National Assembly has passed the 2008 finance bill in
accordance with the medium-term expenditure framework for 2008-2010 and the
prioritiesof the SCRP.

10. During the first four years of implementation of the PRSC, a tool used by IDA to
support the program of Benin (PRSC-1 to PRSC-4), efforts focused on improving: (i)
access to drinking water in rural areas; (ii) the performance of the national health
system; (iii) the quality of primary education; and (iv) environmental and forestry
management; (v) the strengthening of the legal and judiciary system; and (vi) the
improvement of business environment. Cross-cutting issues were also underscored,
including (i) reforms of public expenditures and strengthening of the fiduciary
framework; (ii) the promotion of anti corruption activities; (iii) the strengthening of
the monitoring and evaluation of the 2007-2009 Strategy for Growth and Poverty
Reduction; (iv) civil service and administrative reforms; and (v) decentralization.

11.This program will help to strengthen the reforms engaged and endeavor to: (i)
facilitate growth with the impulse of the private sector; (ii) increasethe contribution
of the agricultural sector to growth; (iii) ensure access to drinking water in rural and



                                            60

semi-urban areas; (iv) improve the performance of the national health sector; (v)
increase the contribution of the education sector to the country's development
needs; (vi) improve the judiciary framework; (vii) improve internal and external
audit; (viii) strengthen the legal and judiciary sector for better performance; (ix)
improve the implementation of decentralization; (x) improve human resources
management in order to achieve sectoral policy objectives; (xi) improve
transparence, strengthen communication and reduce the level of corruption and
related costs; and (xii) improve the management of public finance and civil services.

                 2.1. Pillar Iof the SCRP: acceleration of growth

12, The strategy adopted by the Government to ensure acceleration of growth is
based on four major areas of intervention: (i) stabilization of the macroeconomic
framework; (ii) boost the dynamism of the private sector; (iii) diversification of the
economy ;and (iv) promotion of regional integration.

13. The reforms implemented in 2007 enabled to strengthen the growth started
since 2006. The growth rate was 4.6% against 3.8% in 2006 and 2.9% in 2005.
Despite the persistent food and energy crises, prospect for growth is still favorable
with a growth rate forecast of 5.1% in 2008.

14. The objective of the Government is to reach a growth rate of 5.7% in 2009. In
order to do this, it is planning to increase public investment rate from 8.2% of GDP
to 8.8% in 2009. Private investment would be 15.0/0 in 2008 against 15.1% in
2009. As for the total investment, it would increase from 23.2% of GDP in 2008 to
23.9% in 2009.

15. With the high cost of life throughout the world and more specifically in Benin,
inflation should decrease from 8.8% in 2008 to 6.5% in 2009. The arrangements
made by the Government to develop domestic production in order to efficiently face
the demand and the prospects for reducing the pressure of inflation associated with
international prices would enable to bring inflation down to below 3% in 2010 as set
by UEMOA's convergence pad.

16, The Government's revenues should amount to 19.3% of the GDP in 2008 against
20.6% in 2007. They would reach 19.8% of GDP in 2009 and 20.0% of GDP in
2010. As for expenditures, they are expected to increase to 23.3% of GDP in 2008
against 22.0% in 2007. They would be 24% of GDP in 2009 and 24.2% of GDP in
2010. This will lead to a deficit (on a payment order basis and excluding grant)
which would widen from 1.4% of GDP in 2007 to 4% of GDP in 2008, 4.2% of GDP
in 2009 and 4.2% of PIB in 2010.

17. The achievement of these income and expenditures objectives will be effective
with the implementation of the measures taken to broaden the domestic tax base
and build the capacity of the players in the public expenditures chain. Within this



                                          61

framework, the Government will progressively extend the use of the Single Fiscal
Identifier, strengthen tax and customs control bodies, build the control capacity of
the Directoratefor Big Companiesfor the monitoring of VAT-return, extension of the
ITtool SYDONIA++ to twelve other customs posts and capacity buildingfor the staff
of public procurement units. Finalizing the audit of public expenditures chain will
enable to identify the urgent measures to be taken for streamlining implementation
of expenditures, more particularly those related to investments.

18. The Government pursued the implementation of the development strategy for
the private sector through land reforms, reforms on the industrial free zone and tax
system. Therefore, the Government has (i) had the land law passed; (ii) streamlined
the procedures for obtaining identification numbers for taxpayers; (iii) created the
conditions for turning residence permits into land titles; (iv) strengthened dialog for
public-private partnership; (vi) continued sanitation work, pavement, power supply
for the free industrial zone; (vii) regulatedthe microfinance sector. In order to make
the private sector play a major role for a vigorous and sustainable economic growth,
the Government will pursue its efforts to: (i) improve indicators related to the
business environment; and (ii) adopt a fiscal reform strategy by reducing business
taxes.                                                                              .

19. With the conviction that the structural reforms engaged will significantly
contribute to boosting economic growth, the Government continued to implement
actions plannedfor its withdrawal from the productivesector.

20. As a matter of fact, the industrial unit of SONAPRA was sold to a joint venture.
The private partner was selected on the basis of an international bidding process.
The share scheme includes the State (33.5% of the shareholding), the successful
bidder (33.5%), `public Bkninois et etranger' (17.5%), local governments (8.5%),
cotton producers' organizations (6%), and workers' union (1%). Apart from the
reference private investor, the Government will hold the share of the other
shareholders over a transitional period at the end of which the government will
progressively withdraw until its participationcorrespondsto a blocking minority.

21. As regards the national power supply utility (Socjetg B6njnoiSe d`Energie
/ecLrjque),its financial situation was significantly improved with the implementation
of the management recovery plan. The reforms engaged with the support of the
World Bank are related not only to the recovery of this utility, but also to the
improvement of the quality of service. As for the telecommunications sector,
improving telephone services is a priority for the Government. Significant progress
has been achieved in this sector, and the telecommunications network, especially
GSM communication, has markedly improved. As for Bknin TELECOMS SA, the
implementation of the timeframe for its privatization is still on-going. After
implementing a financial recovery plan for the utility, the Government launched a
call for expression of interest for the recruitment of a pool of consultants to assist
the Government in developing an action plan and a general schedule for the
opening of its capital, conduct a technical, accounting and financial audit, assist the



                                           62

Government in formulating a strategy for opening the capital of the utility, prepare
all the international procurement documents and assist the Government in
conducting the whole process. Concerning the Port of Cotonou, the Government,
