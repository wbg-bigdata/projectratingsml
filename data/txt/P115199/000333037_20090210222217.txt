Project        The proposed Public Expenditure Support Facility (PESF) of USD 2 billion is a DPL with Deferred
Development    Drawdown Option (DDO). Its main objective is to assist the Government of Indonesia (GoI) in
Objective(s)   addressingthe potential adverse impacts of the ongoing global financial crisis on public expenditures
and           during 2009/10. Learning the lessons from the crisis of 1997/98, the Go1 would like to ensure that
Contribution   essential public expenditures are maintained even during this period of stress. Therefore, the Go1
to CPS         would like to put in place arrangements that would give international and domestic markets
              confidence to enable it to raise needed resources from them. These arrangements include a set of
               confidence-boosting policy measures and back-up financing arrangements- a part of which is the
               proposed loan. This support would be available, if the current stress in the market continues and is
               aimed at reducing the possibility of a financing shortfall. The loan will support the GoI's efforts inthe
               following policy areas: (1) reassuringfinancial markets and maintaining financial system stability (2)
               sustaining critical public expenditures while maintaining budget discipline and (3) facilitating private
               investmentand supporting exports.

               Given that the proposedoperation is a responseto the impacts on Indonesia ofthe financial crisis that
               essentially broke in last trimester of 2008, the 2009-2012 CPS did not explicitly envisage such
               support. However, the policy reform agenda supportedby the proposedPESF, which aims at enabling
              an improved institutional response by Indonesiato the global financial crisis, is fully in line with the
               emphasis of the CPS on strengtheningIndonesia's institutions.

Risks and Risk The operationand the Government's reform programare subjectto disruption based on several risks:
Mitigation         The modal scenario the Bank foresees is one where liquidity conditions ease sufficiently, both
                   globally and in Indonesia, such that the signaling effect of the support package is all that is
                   needed. If this is the case, the Go1 would meet its financing target from market sources and the
                   supportwould remain an unexercisedoption.
                   A second scenario is one where market conditions remain tight enough that the draw-down
                   triggers articulated in the Financing Plan are met but there is no outright crisis. Under this
                   scenariothe Go1would draw down a portion or all of the loans provided by the World Bank and
                   other developmentpartnersto support critical public expenditures.
                   A third scenario, and one that constitutes a key risk inthis proposedoperation, is that of a sudden
                   capital outflow crisis. Although indicators of Indonesia's current account and balance of
                   payments are still strong, Indonesia is more exposed than other EAP countries to "externally
                   mobile capital" because of its relatively open capital account and the degree of foreign presence
                   in its stock and government bond markets.The legacy of the 1998 crisis has also left Indonesian
                   investors sensitive to exchange rate movementsand proneto capital flight. The main risk is from
                   large scale capital outflows, both in the form of foreign investors liquidating their positions in
                   Indonesian stocks and bonds, and domestic investors fleeing the rupiah. Were a sudden capital
                   outflow crisis to occur, the typical disbursement circumstances for development policy loan
                   support, in particular the specific requirement for an appropriate overall macroeconomic
                   framework, are unlikely to be met. Should the above scenario take place in advance of the loan
                   disbursement, the PESF funds would not disburse. However, there also remainsthe risk that such
                   a crisis erupts, after disbursementshavebeen effectedby developmentpartners, with implications
                   for further Bank support. Such a situation would most likely require a macroeconomic package
                   with other institutions potentially playing a more prominent role and any support package would
                   then conceivably become part of such a larger macroeconomic package. The proposed operation
                   is intended to mitigate such a risk by increasing market confidence in the strength of economy
                   through improvementsin the government's overall financial position and sustainedreform efforts
                   aimed at financial sector stability, domestic demand expansion, strengthening of infrastructure
                   and social safety net programs and continued improvements to the environment for private
                   investmentsand exports.

                                  I. INTRODUCTION
1.      The proposedIndonesia Public Expenditure Support Facility (PESF),in the form of
a USD 2 billion single tranche DPLwith a DDO, comes at a critical time for Indonesia, and
i s designed to support Indonesia's key development efforts at this time of need. Like many
other developing economies, Indonesia has had to cope with the immediate ripple effects of the
turmoil and crisis of confidence that has disrupted the global financial system in the last few
months, while at the same time preparing for an anticipated growth slowdown and continued
tight liquidity in 2009. The government has responded proactively to the rapidly evolving
situation andthe Bank and other partnershave assistedinand continue to support these efforts.

2.      Indonesia came into the growing crisis (in the final quarter of 2008) in a strong
position. Real GDP growth had accelerated to a ten-year high of 6.3 percent in 2007 and
Indonesiawas the only major economy in East Asia that did not experience a growth slowdown
inthe first halfof2008, despite unstableglobal financial markets and a slowing world economy.
Though year-on-year GDP growth did slow inthe third quarter to 6.1 percent, the slowdown was
much milder than expected. Indonesia has had a primary budget surplus (excluding interest
payments) in each of the last three years and has maintained its overall budget deficit at levels
that are low by international standards-1.3       percent of GDP in 2007, and according to
preliminary realization data, 0.1 percent in 2008. Inflation, the main concern of economic
policymakers around mid-year, appears to have peaked in the third quarter of 2008, following
the shift of monetary policy to a more contractionary footing and waning upstream price
pressures.Indonesia's debt-to-GDP ratio has also declined significantly from 55 per cent in2004
to 34.9 percent in 2007 and is estimated to have declined to 30 percent by the end o f 2008.
Indonesiahas achieved the most dramatic decline in debt-to-GDP ratio of any major economy in
the region.

3.      The ripple effects of the global turmoil since mid-September did, however,
adversely and significantly affect Indonesia's financial markets, especially in the two
months till mid-November. Because of its relatively open capital account, the significant
foreign presence in its stock and bond markets, and the legacy of the 1998 crisis (which left
Indonesianinvestors sensitiveto exchange rate movements and prone to capital flight) Indonesia
is more exposedto "externally mobile capital" than other countries in East Asia. This manifested
itself during the month of October in a 32 percent decline in the stock market, a nearly 450 basis
points increase in yields on medium-term domestic bonds, a 700 basis points increase in
Indonesia's international bond spreads and a 17 percent depreciation of the Indonesian rupiah
against the U S dollar. Indonesia's international reserves, which had reached a high of USD 60
billion, fell by nearly USD 10 billion to USD 50 billion in the month and a half to the end of
October.

4.      Developments since late November provide some indications that Indonesia's
financial markets may be stabilizing, though it is still too early to be certain and the risk of
further disruption remains, while the adverse impacts of a likely growth slowdown are
beginning to be felt. The Indonesianrupiah did fall by another 10 percent against the US dollar
during the month of November, but Indonesia's international reserves remained stable at around
USD 50 billion inNovember before recovering by almost USD 1.5 billion inDecember, andthe
stock market also recovered some of its earlier losses through December and January. With the


                                                1

rupiah appearing to stabilize and the external growth environment rapidly weakening, Bank
Indonesia lowered its policy rate by 25 bps to 9.25 percent in its December meeting, and by a
further 50 bps to 8.75 percent at its January meeting.Needlessto say, significant challenges still
remain on both the domestic and external fronts. Indonesian corporations are believed to have
reasonably well diversified financing and not to be significantly leveraged, but, further
depreciation and demand destruction in the event of a pronounced slowdown will affect
corporations and result in rising non performing loans (NPLs) and erosion of bank capital. And
given Indonesia's exposureto externally mobile capital, continued volatility in financial markets
and spikes inglobal risk aversioncould cause further disruptive capital outflows.

5.      The government is well-aware of the challenges that lie ahead and has taken a
number of precautionary and proactive measures in response. To reduce the likelihood of
further disruption and maintain the stability of the financial system, the President has signed a
number of regulations, most notably a financial safety net regulation that clarifies the roles,
responsibilities and procedures that govern the actions and responses of Bank Indonesia, the
Minister of Finance, and the Deposit Insurance Corporation in the event of the failure of a
financial institution, and a regulation that increases the ceiling on deposit insurance from 100
million to 2 billion rupiah, while giving the governmentthe authority to further raise the deposit
insurance ceiling or issue a blanket universal guarantee quickly if circumstances demand. These
measures have, up till now, helpedto reinforce the stability of the Indonesianbanking system. In
the single instance where a small non-systemic bank did fail, the measures empowered the
regulatory authorities to quickly take control ofthe situation.

6.      The 2009 budget passed in November provided the government with the flexibility
to respond to evolving economic conditions and to implement a fiscal stimulus program
and to accelerate budget execution, a prerequisite for enhancingthe impact and timeliness
of any fiscal stimulus package. Thus, in the second half of January, the government took to
Parliament a revised budget responding to the global downturn, and expands the 2009 deficit
from a budgeted 1% of GDP to 2.5% of GDP. Spending on infrastructure, social protection,
including for the flagship community-based poverty alleviation program, and education will
expand, while revenues are budgeted to fall with cuts in tax rates, slower nominal GDP growth
and lower global commodity prices. In so doing Indonesia hopes to avoid repeating the
experience of the 1998 Asian crisis, when infrastructure spending fell drastically. And while
much remains to be done to improve energy efficiency and competition in local energy markets
the government has announcedthat it plans to take advantage of lower global oil prices and is
now adjusting administered fuel prices in line with movements in market prices, up to a pre-
specified ceiling. From 2009, the central government is also sharing the cost of subsidies with
sub-national governments. These are critical steps contributing to the longer term budget
improvement efforts being undertaken by government.

7.      To alleviate financing constraints if global liquidity conditions do not ease, and as an
important element of its precautionary stance looking forward, the government has
requested budget support from the World Bank in the form of a USD 2 billion DPL with
DDO-the      PESF described in this program documentwhich the Government has stated
it intends to exercise only when certain conditions are met. The government has also
requested the World Bank's assistance in facilitating additional financing of around USD 3-4
billion from other development partners, which the government has stated it intends to exercise
only under the same conditions as those of the PESF. The request for financing stems from
concerns about Indonesia's ability to meet its gross financing needs-which    are high in the next

                                                 2

few years because of the maturity structure and servicing costs of its existing debt-if the current
global liquidity crisis does not ease over the next six to twelve months.

8.      The desirable outcome for the Government is to be able to mobilize resources from
the markets capitalizing on the confidence created by the PESF-led support package, but
not actually drawing down PESF resources. The Government believes that the announcement
of a support package which the Government has stated would only be exercised when the
conditions set out in its own financing plan are met, backed up by a program o f confidence-
boosting policy measures, would send a strong positive signal to the markets, making it more
likely that Indonesia would meet its financing needs for CY2009 from market sources. A
support package would naturally also offer a fall-back option inthe event that market conditions
continue to be unfavorable or even deteriorate. This commitment of the Government to raise
resources first and foremost from the international and domestic markets is also indicated by the
fact that it requested a DPL with a DDO as opposed to a traditional DPL. The DDO gives the
Governmentthe necessary flexibility to draw down the resourceson an as-needed basis.

9.      Indonesia's request to lead this support effort was directed at the World Bank since
the need is for fiscal resources rather than Central Bank reserves. Indonesiaat this time does
not seek nor require support for macroeconomic adjustment. This support package is clearly
preventive innature. It cannot and would not substitute for a macroeconomicpackagethat could
be required ifthe situation significantly deteriorated.

10.     The PESF is intended to complement the regular DPL and IDPL program, the latest
installments of which, DPL-5 for USD750 million and IDPL-2 for USDZOO million, were
approved by the Board on December 9, 2008. The PESF focuses on a subset of the policy
areas covered under the regular DPL program, areas inwhich further policy action is particularly
important as part of a proactive response to the adverse current situation and likely future
stresses (see Annex 2 for the PESF ResultsFramework). While the PESF complements the DPL
series, clearly Bank support to Indonesia at this stage is also a function of the solid track record
o f reforms that has been established by the Government over the last four years - both within
and outside the context of the DPL series. There is also a continued and clear commitment to
additional reforms - within and outside the context of the PESF. Annex 3 outlines the policy
actions supported by DPLs 1-4. Annex 4 outlines the policy actions supportedby DPL5 and the
indicative actions likely to be supported by DPLs 6 and 7 (expected in the 41-42 o f the Bank's
FY 10 and 11 respectively). The ongoing DPL series will also provide the Bank with a
mechanism to continue to be engaged in the policy reform agenda in the areas being supported
by the PESF.

11.     While the regular DPL series aims to advance Indonesia's longer-term institutional
reform agenda and focuses on the many important but often incremental steps that need to
be taken to realize this agenda, the PESF being proposed would support critical stroke-of-
the-pen measures that are being implemented to deal with the current situation, but also,
where relevant, help accelerate and intensify the process that is already being supported by

         B. Reasons for request

such support would send a strong positive signal to the markets and that would make it more
likely that it would meet its financing target for CY2009. The support package would also offer
a fall-back option in the event that market conditions continue to be unfavorable or even
deteriorate.

20.     The Government's current borrowing plans for 2009 do not include any draw

contingent, and therefore, first and foremost a means of sending a strong positive signal to the
markets and secondly, as a fall-back option ifa financing shortfall does emerge.

21.     An innovative aspect of the design of the PESF is its contingent nature with respect

                   111. COUNTRYCONTEXTAND RECENTECONOMIC
                                               DEVELOPMENTS

24.     Indonesia came into the current financial crisis (in the final quarter of 2008) in a
strong position.Real GDP growth had acceleratedto a ten-year high of 6.3 percent in 2007 and
Indonesiawas the only major economy in East Asia that did not experience a growth slowdown
inthe first half of2008, despite unstable global financial markets and a slowing world economy.
Though year-on-year GDP growth did slow inthe third quarter to 6.1 percent, the slowdown was
much milder than expected. Indonesia has had a primary budget surplus (excluding interest
payments) in each of the last three years and has maintained its overall budget deficit at levels
that are low by international standards-1.3        percent of GDP in 2007, and according to
preliminary realization data, 0.1 percent in 2008. Inflation, the main concern of economic
policymakers around mid-year, appears to have peaked in the third quarter of 2008, following
the shift of monetary policy to a more contractionary footing and waning upstream price
pressures. Indonesia's debt-to-GDP ratio has also declined significantly from 55 percent in 2004
to 34.9 percent in 2007 and is estimated to have declined towards 30 percent by end of 2008.
Indonesiahas achieved the most dramatic decline in debt-to-GDP ratio of any major economy in
the region.

25.     The globalturmoil since mid-September had, however, significant adverse effects on
Indonesia's financial markets, especially in the two months till mid-November. Because of
its relatively open capital account, the large presence of foreign investors in its stock and bond
markets, and the legacy of the 1998 crisis (which left Indonesian investors sensitive to exchange
rate movements and prone to capital flight) Indonesia is more exposed to "externally mobile
capital" than other countries in East Asia. This manifested itself during the month of October in
a 32 percent decline inthe stock market, a nearly 450 basis points increase in yields on medium-
term domestic bonds, a 700 basis points increase inIndonesia's international bond spreads and a
17 percent depreciation of the Indonesianrupiah against the U S dollar. Indonesia's international
reserves, which had reached a high o f USD 60 billion, fell by nearly USD 10 billion to USD 50
billion inthe month and a halfto the end of October.

26.     Developments since late November provide some indications that Indonesia's
financial marketsmay be stabilizing, though it is still too early to be certain and the risk of
further disruptionremains,while the adverse impactsof a likely growth slowdown are only
beginning to be felt. While the Indonesian rupiah fell further during the three weeks of
November against the US dollar, it then appreciated by 12 percent to 10,863 by early January.
Indonesia's international reserves remained stable at a little above USD 50 billion in November
and December, as did the stock market, which rose by 16 percent from the start of December to
early January. With the rupiah appearing to stabilize and the external growth environment
rapidly weakening, Bank Indonesia lowered its policy rate by 25 bps to 9.25 percent in its
December meeting and by a further 50 bps at its January meeting. Needless to say, significant
challenges still remain on both the domestic and external fronts. Indonesian corporations are
believed to have reasonably well diversified finance and not be significantly leveraged, but,
further depreciation and demand destruction in the event of a pronounced slowdown will affect
corporations and result in rising NPLs and erosion of bank capital. And given Indonesia's
exposure to externally mobile capital, continued volatility in financial markets and spikes in
global risk aversion could cause further disruptive capital outflows.

         B. The globalfinancialturmoilbuffetedIndonesia'smarkets in
             October and November of 2008

36.     Indonesia's financial markets have been adversely affected by the global turmoil
since the onset of the global financial crisis. As of January 2009, the stock market index had
declined by around 50 per cent on a year earlier -with one quarter ofthe decline occurring since
October 1. Mining and agricultural stocks fell most from the start of 2008 reflecting declining
expectations for commodity prices. Financial sector stocks fell, on average, by much less than
the overall market, reflecting the limited direct exposure of Indonesia's banks to developments
especially in US financial markets, plus a strong capital base and low levels of non-performing
loans.

37.     The rupiah came under pressure from early October to late November, after
tracking broadly sideways against the US dollar since mid-2006. In October 2008 the IDR
depreciated against most major currencies, losing one-third of its value against the USD by late
November. Bank Indonesia intervened in the spot market in September and October especially.
The early intervention was driven in part by concerns that letting the IDR cross various
thresholds (e.g., IDR 10,000 per USD) risked triggering domestic capital flight. Significant flight
appears not to have occurred however, and official statistics suggest that BI's net intervention
was minimal in November and December, with the central bank's foreign reserves standing at
USD 50.182 billion by late November before rising to USD 51.64 billion by the end of
December.

41.      Waning inflation and the deteriorating economic outlook is allowing BI to ease the
monetary tightening. By the third quarter of 2008 inflation expectations had returnedto late-
2007 levels, by which point commodity prices, upstream producer prices and growth in the
money supply all suggest inflationary pressures had slackened. Year-on-year CPI inflation fell
from a peak of 12.14 percent in August to 11.06 percent by December. Waning inflationary
pressures, an apparent reduction in the downwards pressures on the rupiah and the rapidly
weakening external growth environment (discussed below) led BI to lower its policy rate by
25 bps to 9.25 percent at its December meeting, and then by 50 bps to 8.75 in January. These
cuts followed liquidity injections into the inter-bank market in October and November, and a
narrowing ofthe interbankdeposit and lending rate wedge in early December.

42.      Direct impacts of the US financial crisis on the domestic banking system, which
holds nearly 80 percent of financial assets, have thus far been limited. With a relatively high
average capital adequacy ratio, the sector continues to be relatively well capitalized. And the
NPL ratio, rising very slightly to 4.0 percent in November 2008, is at a relatively low level.
However, Indonesian banks do face challenges. Over the past year, loan growth, at over
36 percent, has been extremely rapid. With deposits growing at less than halfthis pace, the loan-
deposit ratio increased rapidly and reduced liquidity coming into the crisis. A more significant
depreciation could affect some corporations and result in rising NPLs and the erosion of bank
capital.

43.      The exposure of the corporate non-financial sector to the global turmoil has been
limited thus far except for a few specific cases and increasing reports of difficulties
accessing trade finance. The balance sheet vulnerabilities of the Indonesian corporate sector

                                                         15

have been reduced over the last decade. Indonesian firms largely finance their investment
through retained earnings, and leverage ratios in the corporate sector have fallen. But when
corporates have borrowed, they have often chosen to do so from foreign sources. In 2007
Indonesian firms obtained almost half of their external financing from foreign sources and this
exposure to foreign sources makes firms vulnerable to global financial market conditions
especially as loans turn over. There are also cases of Indonesian corporations, especially
commodity intensive ones, having taken on significant exposure to foreign debt markets after
having pledged shares as collateral for expansions. However, overall short-term external debt
exposure of both the private and public sector appears manageable-USD           22.3 billion due
within a year from November 2008, down USD 6 billion below July's level-given       international
reservesof USD 5 1.6 billion (as of December 30). There are also increasing reports by exporters
and importers that Indonesian banks are not honoring overseas bank's Letters of Credit, and vice
versa, due to dollar liquidity problems and perceptions of increasedcounterparty risk.

44.     The risk of capital flight has become more pronouncedwith the crisis. Becauseof its
relatively open capital account, the significant foreign presence in its stock and bond markets,
and the legacy of the 1998 crisis, which has left Indonesian investors sensitive to exchange rate
movements and prone to capital flight, Indonesia is more exposed to "externally mobile capital"
than other countries in East Asia. In the aftermath of the turmoil in US markets there was a
"mixed" pull back of foreign investors from Indonesianstock and bond markets (where up to 20
percent of domestic governmentbondswere held by foreigners). This withdrawal of liquidity put
pressure on stock prices and especially on bond yields and then on the exchange rate. Through
September rapid credit growth (reflecting economic momentum) had already been putting
pressure on bond yields, in part as BIwas contracting the money supply. By October liquidity in
rupiah but especially in USD was scarce and subsequently BI reversed course to make more
liquidity in both currencies available through various means. But the liquidity squeeze and price
effects had other impacts, as some, so far limited, firms that had borrowed against the values of
their shares faced margin calls. In addition a subsidiary of Bank Indonesia, Indover, failed to
make a payment on its debt and Bank Indonesia did not honor what was perceived to be a
comfort letter precipitating additional credit worthiness concerns and feeding into higher credit
default swaps.

45.     While significant amounts of external capital have already been withdrawn from the
country's financial markets, Indonesia still remainsvulnerable to further capital flight. As
noted above, Indonesia is a financially open economy with significant foreign holdings,
especially in the stock and government debt markets. Indonesia is vulnerable, as are many
emerging economies in this period of global financial turmoil and heightened risk aversion, to
sudden capital outflows in the form of foreigners liquidating their positions and Indonesians
fleeing the rupiah. Foreigners are estimated to hold around 16 percent, or IDR 86 trillion or
slightly less than USD 8 billion, of the stock of government bonds, down from 20 percent in the
third quarter. This ratio fell during the peak of the financial turbulence in October and early
November 2008, before stabilizing. As funds were repatriated due to liquidity demands and
rising risk, pressures on interest and exchange rates rose, and this cycle could repeat.
Nonetheless, Indonesia's reserves provide adequate cover for current levels o f short-term debt.
The latest available data for 2008 report that the stock of obligations due within one year was
USD22.3 billion, or 43 percent ofIndonesia's foreign reserves. Meanwhile, stock exchangedata
suggest that, through much of the period of the more extreme financial turmoil from late
September 2008, foreigners were net purchasers.

49.       Assuming a prolonged global recession roughly comparable to the one in 1982 (the
worst for a composite of Indonesian trading partners from 1970s to present), Indonesia's
growth is projected to fall from 6 percent in 2008 to approximately 4.0 to 4.5 percent in
2009 before recoveringto near 5.8 percent in 2010. Investment and exports are expectedto be
most affected by the current economic turmoil. Investment is expectedto be flat in 2009, before
growth recovers in 2010 to around 7 percent. Export volume growth is projected to slow from
2008's 14 percent pace to around 1-2 percent in 2009, before recovering towards 8 percent in
2010. The one positive impact of global events is the marked slowing in inflation, which will
provide some support to households' real incomes. After 2008's annual average inflation rate
near 10 percent, inflation i s expected to slow to near 7 percent in 2009 and 6 percent in 2010.
Poorer households, which consume relatively more food, should see an even more marked
slowdown inthe increase in living costs.

50.       The uncertainty surrounding the global outlook make these projections subject to
far more variability than usual, which increases the uncertainty surrounding the
government's financing needs. Third quarter 2008 outturns across Indonesia's major trading
partners have generally been below expectations, and an array of indicators suggests some major
economies entered a deep recession in the fourth quarter. In addition, commodity prices have
fallen dramatically. These developments suggest significant downside risks. A recession deeper
than 1982 across Indonesia's trading partners, plus commodity prices remaining near their early-
January levels, would see Indonesia's growth in 2009 near 3.5 percent before recovering to


                                                             18

around 5.5 percent in 2010. In turn, slower aggregate GDP growth implies weaker government
revenues, leading to larger primary deficits and greater gross financing needs - this scenario
adds about 0.2 percentagepoints of GDP to the budget deficit and gross financing needs, and the
government's proposedrevised 2009 budget and financing plan reflect this weakening.

51.      Global economic outturns significantly below the modal projection are likely to be
associatedwith increased balanceof payments vulnerability. The current account balance is
unlikely to move into a significant deficit under the lower growth scenario, as both imports and
exports are likely to be weaker, while lower commodity prices reduce profit remittances, most
likely more than offsetting lower workers' remittances. However the capital account is likely to
be weaker, as risk averseness in global financial markets reduces net portfolio investment, and as
lower projected profitability on investment projects and tighter credit conditions for the private
sector lead to lower net foreign direct investment. With the associated likely weakening in the
currency and/or drawdown in foreign reserves, the risk of an extreme, negative balance of
paymentsevent becomes more elevated inthis scenario.

52.      Governments, including Indonesia's, are introducing large fiscal and monetary
stimulus packages, generatingsome upside risk to the modal projections. In early January
2009, the Indonesiangovernment announceda 1percentof GDP stimulus package, providing tax
cuts, social safety net support, expanded infrastructure investment, and support to industries
most affected by the crisis. This package, plus beneficial effects of stimulus packages being
implemented elsewhere, could see Indonesia's growth approaching 5 percent in 2009 and
increasing to at least 6 percent in 2010. While the relatively stronger growth will support
government revenues, the greater expenditure under the stimulus package expands the
government's budget deficit, hence gross financing needs. This scenario, however, may also
bring relatively stronger import growth, dragging net exports and adding to current account
pressures. The impact on capital flows is ambiguous, with the greater financing requirements
likely to be supportedthrough larger net official and private inflows, while Indonesia's relatively
stronger growth outlook and improved investment climate (e.g., through increasedinfrastructure
investment) may attract relatively more foreign direct investment.

         E. The Political, Social and Governance Context
53.     Political stability and the consolidation of democracy have continued to deepen.
While Indonesia's democracy at the national level i s vibrant and the policy debate often
vigorous, political stability and the institutions that support it appear to be increasingly robust.
Furthermore, lively public debate against a backdropof political stability is proving conducive to
improving levels of governance and sustainable economic growth. Sub-national elections over
the past year have been largely well-run and peaceful, which bodes well for the election year in
2009. Regional elections have often resulted in losses for incumbents, demonstrating the
electorate's political savvy and readiness to punish poor performance. In legislation that
strengthens democracy and curtails the control of large political parties, the parliament (DPR)
amended the Law on Regional Governance to enable independent candidates to take part in
regional-head elections. Overall, Indonesia's governance indicators have improved markedly in
recentyears, although there is still considerable scope for further progress.

54.     Separatist movements and regional conflicts have remained benign. The deepening
transformation towards a decentralized model of government administration, together with
improving levels of local governance and direct local elections, has contributed to a lesseningof
tensions in previously conflict-prone regions, such as Aceh, Maluku, Sulawesi, Flores and
Papua. Religious tensions remain, however, as was witnessed in recent violent protests by
radical Islamic groups against the Ahmadiyya sect of Islam that is consideredheretical by Sunni
Muslimsas well as against liberal groups supporting religious tolerance and women's rights. In
comparisonwith recent years the police appear to be taking a firmer stand against law-breakers,
who are now more likely to be held accountablefor legal infringements.

55.     Terrorism appears to be under control, with no significant terrorist incidentsin the
past year. Indonesia continues to display an impressivetrack record in dealing with the threat
of terrorism, with the government's strategy of dissuasion rather than armed confrontation
becoming an international model for combating terrorism. Leadersof extremist groups have been
arrested and several plots intercepted. In response to the improved security situation, the United
States has lowered its threat warning for Indonesia, and Australia has removed specific travel
warnings against visiting some of Indonesia's more volatile regions.

56.     Political tensions can be expected to increase with the start of campaigning for the
2009 elections. Having won 61 percent o f the vote in a second-round run-off in 2004, President
Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono remains generally popular, although this popularity suffered
somewhat from continuing high levels of poverty and unemployment and the steep price rises of
staple foods and fuel in 2008. However, declining inflation, as a result of falling global
commodity prices, as well as the Government's reduction in fuel prices to partially adjust to the
steep fall in the global oil markets has also contributed to the recent improvements in poll
numbers. The President has continued to push for key structural reforms including cutting fuel
subsidies for the third time in mid-2008. These structural reforms, together with strengthening
economic fundamentals, have supported Indonesian growth, with the economy achieving its
fastest post-crisis growth rate in2007.

57.     Targeted poverty alleviation programs have helped to avoid widespread social
unrest. Repeatinga scheme used successfully in2005-06, the government complementedthe cut
in the fuel subsidy in May 2008 with the implementation shortly thereafter of a direct cash
transfer program to compensate the poor with quarterly payments in excess of the impact of

                                                 20

inflation, including both the fuel price rises and the unrelated food price inflation. This program,
which runs for one year and covers some 19 million households, was a major factor in winning
acceptance of the subsidy cuts among the general populace and ensuring that the initial protests
subsidedwithin a matter ofweeks.

58.     The government's anti-corruption credentials remain strong and indicators of
effective control of corruption in Indonesia are improving steadily, albeit still at a low level.
Sufficient and dedicated resources have been made available to most of the agencies critical to
anti-corruption efforts. Powerful independent oversight and prosecutorial institutions such as the
Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK) and the Anti-Corruption Court have begunto deliver
tangible results. In addition, institutions like the Supreme Audit Agency (BPK), the Financial
Transaction and Analysis Centre (PPATK), and the Attorney General's Office have all critically
improved their anti-corruption track record. The leadership of the KPK was replaced at the end
of 2007 and, despite initial concerns the new leadership team has shown itself as vigorous as its
predecessor inmaintaining the momentumofthe KPK's investigations.

59.     High profile corruption investigations have been launched, leading to a number of
successful prosecutions.For example, as of September 2008, seven parliamentary members are
under investigation or prosecution as suspects in several corruption cases, most of them
involving bribery. Some of the parliamentarymembers were arrested when caught red-handed
receiving bribes.A senior prosecutor has also been sentenced to 20 years inprison for receiving
bribes. This case has in turn led to the removal of three very senior prosecutors, including two
Deputies of the Attorney General. The focus of one case involved the transfer of funds from the
central bank (BI) to members of the parliamentary finance commission in order to facilitate the
passage (and amendment) of several bills, one ofwhich could have transferred responsibility for
supervising the financial sector from BI to a new regulatory agency under the Ministry of
Finance. Elsewhere, customs offices have been raided and customs officials arrested for
receiving suspect payments, while successful prosecutions have been made against regional
government officials for corruption.

60.     The government is also introducing systemic measures to advance on civil service
reform. Senior civil servants inthe Ministry ofFinance may no longer also hold senior positions
instate-ownedenterprises, avoiding conflicts of interest that hadpreviously been commonplace.
More generally throughout the civil service, pay levels are gradually being brought into line with
the skill and responsibility requirements of each position and improving the nexus with their
private sector counterparts. Civil service rules remain overly rigid, however, and the procedures
for recruitment and promotion continue to stymie more far-reaching enhancement of public
sector performance.

61.     While more needs to be done in developing and implementing a coherent, well-
focused strategy for civil service reforms within the state administration, there are some
promising developments. Previously, the KPK was in charge of pushing and monitoring civil
service reform as a corruption prevention measure within several institutions such as the
Ministry of Finance, the Supreme Court and the BPK, but when this task was handed over to
MenPAN in 2007 progress became very slow. Despite the limited progress by MenPAN,
individual government agencies including the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Ministry of Trade and
most recently the Attorney General's Office have initiated important internal bureaucratic
reforms which are well underway.


                                                  21

62.     Reforms in one important area        -  the judiciary   -are making limited headway.
Despite its importance for legal certainty and investor confidence, reform of the judiciary has
made little progress over the past year. One modest positive development has been PP No.
53/2008, that categorizes"fees" collected by the courts as non-tax state income, thereby obliging
the courtsto passthe finds to the state treasury. Previously, the funds hadbeenheld by the court
system and court officials, including the head of the Supreme Court, had refusedto allow access
to BPK auditors to audit the funds.

63.     There have been improvements in the public procurement environment, but the
system still has significant deficiencies in its regulatory and, more importantly,
implementation aspects.         Keppres No. 80/2003 provided a national public procurement
regulation that meets most of what is generally regarded as accepted international practice,
including basic principles: transparency, open and fair competition, economy and efficiency.
This decree also requiresthat a national public procurementoffice be establishedas a regulatory
body for public procurement. With the introduction o f Keppres No. 80/2003, a previous
certification ("pre-qualification") process for suppliers/ providers of goods and services was, in
most instances, abandonedby the government. Keppres No. 80/2003 also established the basis
for sanctions, complaint-handling and requirements for certification of users. However, there
have been delays in establishing a strong regulatory body; slow progress in the development of
procurement regulations anchored by a law; slow progress in the development of standard tools
in terms of bidding documents and users manuals; weakness in procurement capacity in
implementing agencies, especially at the provincial and district levels; and collusion and
corruption practices in the bidding process as well as concerns over the efficiency of anti-
corruption and sanctionmeasures.

64.      Significant progress is being made on Indonesia's public financial management
systems. The strengths includea sound regulatory framework for most parts of the budget cycle,
an orderly, transparentand predictable budget formulation process, fund predictability for budget
execution, sound budget classification, comprehensive and transparent budget documentation,
Government Accounting Standards that are aligned with comparable international standards, a
chart of accountswhich is broadly consistent with GFSM 2001 and a strengthenedexternal audit
institution.

65.      Overall, Government has undertaken in a sustained manner, a number of reforms
that directly influence the environment in which public expenditure is planned, budgeted,
executed and accounted for across all levels of government. A more detailed commentary on
progressinimplementing these reforms is given inAnnex 7.

66.      The ongoing DPL program has supported the Government's implementation of

         F. LessonsLearned

87.     The PESF incorporates the lessons learned over the course of the DPL program.
These lessons stem from the nature and evolution of the relationship between the Bank and the
government, and also from the characteristics ofthe DPL program as a means of offering policy
support, together with the changingpolicy environment on the ground. Some of the lessons may
be particular to the Indonesiancase because ofthe unique nature ofthe Bank's engagementwith
the Government of Indonesia and the range of resources that the World Bank program in
Indonesiahas available through trust funds.

88.    Lesson 1: Strong government ownership and committed counterparts are vital, but
just as important is ownership over the pace of the reform process. As a growing middle-
income country, Indonesiacan access domestic and international capital markets, thus offering it
a range of financing choices. While the Bank's financing is attractive, the driving reason for the
government's engagement with the Bank through the DPL program is the utility of the
instrumentinbolstering, locking inand acceleratingcritical reforms. To succeed, DPLs not only
need the commitment of government at the highest level but also that of mid-level officials
responsiblefor implementing the reforms and following progress on a daily basis. Inareas where
broad consensus is still evolving, such as streamlining budget execution, the pace of
implementation may be slower than desired but their very inclusion in the DPL-supported
agenda maintains a focus on the policy issue, prompts on-going deliberation o f policy options,
strengthens the resolve ofreform champions, and may eventually leadto reform breakthroughs.

89.     Lesson 2: Progress on institutional reforms need not be linear and unidirectional
and there should be sufficient flexibility for modificationsand increased complexity as the
reform program evolves. Unlike stroke-of-the-pen reforms, institutional reforms that involve
changing the way the government works, especially in the face of entrenched interests and
organizational cultures, are complicated, often unpredictable undertakings for which there are no
simple recipes. Trial and error is part and parcel of the processand there may be the occasional
dead-ends that will require backtracking and finding an alternate route. In a lively democracy, in
particular, with multiple centers of power and competing interests, the momentum for specific
reforms may ebb and flow. What is needed in this context is patience and a continued sense of
urgency and preparedness so that when the right opportunity presents itself, reforms that had
otherwise been apparently slowed, may be advanced rapidly. The effort to strengthen public
procurement in Indonesiaprovides an example ofthis. DPL 1recognizedthe issuance in 2003 of
a Presidential Decree on Government Procurement. Procurement remained part of the dialogue
insubsequent years but the openingto buildonthat initial step did not becomeavailable till DPL
4, at which point the initial trigger to set up a procurement unit was exceeded in that a fully
independentnational procurementoffice was establishedby PresidentialDecree.

90.     Lesson 3: DPLs are only one instrument among many that are available in a multi-
faceted engagement, and the choice of instrument should be contingent on the issue, the
politicalcontext and the institutionalcircumstances.This is relatedto Principle 3 of the Good
Practice Principles on Conditionality but is particularly applicable in the Indonesian case
because of the size and scope of the World Bank program here. The World Bank program in
Indonesia is one of the largest of any country in terms of number of staff and overall resources.
Large teams are working closely on a daily basis with government counterparts at often very
senior levels on a wide range of issues from public financial management and trade and
investment, to finance, public expenditures and poverty. In this context the DPL is not the only

                                                30

instrument for supporting reform. In fact, at times, and on certain politically-sensitive and
contentious policy issues, such as subsidy reductions, it may be the wrong instrument. An
example would be the case of the government's rice policy during much of 2006 and 2007.
Changing the policy would clearly have been a reform with positive impacts on the welfare of
the poor and yet it would have been counterproductivefor the Bank to engage on that through a
high-profile instrument such as the DPL under which any actions might have been interpretedas
conditionality.

91.     Lesson 4: Mapping out a multi-year reform program at the outset, when possible,
helps provide meaningful direction, substantive results, and makes the reforms more
sustainable. With the preparation of DPL5 coinciding with the start of a new CPS in which a
multi-year DPL program is clearly envisioned, a more systematic attempt was made to adopt a
multi-year strategic frame for the program, and to lay out a basic mapping ofthe reform program
that more specifically describes the end posts, though still allowing for flexibility on how to
arrive there. The PESF provides an opportunity, within the context of a global financial crisis, to
further speed up the reforms in some of these areas, such as in the investment climate and
budgetary processes. In other areas, such as the financial sector, the continued engagement of
the Bank over the last several years in the context of the DPL series, provides a sound basis for
undertakingpolicy actions neededto mitigate the effects ofthe crisis.

i. Background

96.      In the financial sector, many of the actions being taken currently have been
informed by lessons learned from the 1997/1998 crisis. During that crisis, the Government

restore confidence in the banking system. These actions included amendments of laws o f banks

coordination, division of responsibilities and procedures for provision of Government's financial
(lending) support in times of financial crisis due to failure of systematically important financial
institutions or other systemic factors. In the Indonesian context, the FSN includes consolidated
supervision and regulation, lender of last resort (for normal and emergency situations), deposit
protection, crisis managementand resolution procedures.

law and submitted a draft to parliament in June 2005. The process had since stalled partly
due to the lack of sense of urgency. However, in the interim, as a mitigating measure, the
Ministry of Finance and Bank Indonesia signed a Memorandum of Understanding on rules and
procedures of provision of emergency lending assistance to systemically important financial
institutions.

i.      Background

110.    Indonesia has made progress in its overall economic development and poverty has

annually since 2002, and this economic growth over the past six years has benefited the poor,
especially recently. The poverty rate declined from 17.4 in2003 to 15.4 percent in2008.

111.    While Indonesia's macro-economic progress has been solid, there are concerns
regardingthe slow pace of poverty reductionand extensivevulnerability. Poverty remains a
serious problem in Indonesia as 15.4 percent of the population or 35 million persons live below
the national poverty line while another 42 percent are vulnerable to falling into poverty iftheir
circumstances deteriorate. Public services especially for the poor are inadequate for a middle-

income country, and non-income poverty is a major problem. Malnutrition and maternal

112.   Concerns regarding the number of vulnerable Indonesians are becoming more

The Government is concerned that the current crisis will have adverse effects not only on its
financing needs and financial markets but also on its efforts to reduce poverty as growth falls,
retrenchments rise, employment opportunities shrink, and a general deterioration in living

Area 3 A. The investmentregulatory framework

iv.    Background

128.   Indonesia's Parliament approved a landmark new investment law in March 2007,
Law Number 25 of 2007. The new law replaced separate laws on foreign and domestic
investmentdating from 1967 and 1968 and provides a single legislative framework for domestic
and foreign investment.Article 12 ofthe new Law states that all business activities not explicitly
closed or restricted to investment are open, and that restrictions must be stated in a Presidential
Regulation (Perpres). This marks the first time that an Indonesian law has required a single
comprehensive investment negative list, issued by the President, and the first clear statement in
law that activities not included inthe list are fully opento investment.

129.   In fulfilling the Law, the Go1 issued Presidential Regulation 77 of 2007 to
incorporate size and foreign equity restrictions on investment into a single authoritative


                                                  46

document issued by the President, which would prevent line ministries and government
agencies from creating their own restrictions. This regulation is named The List of Business
Activities Closed to Investment and Activities Open to Investment with Restrictions, and it
represents the first revision of the investment negative list since 2001. The regulation is also
intended to function as a simple check list that could be understood by anyone, eliminating the
need for official discretion. This required a much more detailed, hence longer, investment
negative list. The changes enhanced the clarity and transparency of investment policy, thereby
helping to reduce investor uncertainty.         However, the new investment negative list
simultaneously introduced significant new restrictions on foreign investment in key economic
sectors, such as telecommunications, ports, hospitals, retail trade, logistics, insurance and
pharmaceuticals.

130.     The reaction of investorsto the new investmentnegative list was mixed. On the one
hand investors welcomed the greater clarity and legal certainty in the new list. But on the other
hand, they were surprised by the Government's decision to reduce foreign equity limits inmajor
sectors of the economy, as this marked the first significant reversion toward a more restrictive
investmentregime following several decades of liberalization.

131.    A grandfathering clause was written for existing companies which exceed foreign
equity limits but uncertainties and differing interpretations created new forms of investor
uncertainty.     As a general principle it was decided that existing companies should be
grandfathered. However, the details of grandfathering are technically complex. For example, it
was unclear whether grandfathering would be based on the regulations that existed immediately
prior to the issuance of the new negative list, on the regulations that existed at the time the
company was first approved, or on the equity ceiling stated in the investor's approval letter. It
was also unclear whether grandfathering would apply without limit to expansions, changes of
location, license renewal, changes of share ownership, and other changes in the company's
activities that might require government approval. Several disputes have subsequently emerged
over the issue of grandfathering, with government agencies making up their own rules in the
absence of precise guidelines.

132.     More recently the government has announced in the press that it will change a long
standing policy of not applying the investment negativelist to publicly listed companies, but
no regulations have been issued to implement this announcement, creating widespread
uncertainty among listed companies at a time when the domestic capital market is under severe
pressure.

v.      Relevance to the PESF and proposed actions to improve the investmentclimate

133.     One of the priority areas in the PESF is to improve the investmentclimate in order
to promote strong private sector investment. The new restrictions in the investment negative
list are a major obstacle to attracting investment into telecommunications, ports, health care, and
other sectors where higher investment levels are urgently needed and possible even during the
current slowdown. Uncertainty created by unclear rules on grandfathering and publicly listed
companies act to deter investment and expansion. Reducing investor uncertainty by clarifying
the rules and relaxing restrictions in key sectors could help Indonesia offset negative investor
sentiment caused by the global financial crisis.

Area 3 B. Transparency in extractive industries' revenues

138. Indonesiahas experienced a steady decline in oil productionfor more than a decade,

which will lead EITI implementation, the hiring of the Independent Reconciler (akutan

Area 3C. Trade finance

ix.     Background

146.    The global financial crisis is fast spilling over into Indonesia and other developing
countries and affecting the capacity to finance international trade transactions. Growing
liquidity pressure and greater perceived risk in matured markets is causing havoc in many
emerging countries, as foreign banks abruptlyreduced or stopped lending and stepped back even
from trade credits and guarantees which have traditionally been considered one of the most
secure methods of finance in the banking industry. Domestic and foreign banks are exceedingly
cautious about taking the counterparty bank risk under letters of credit. In addition, large foreign
buyers are trying to extend the maturity of accounts payable (received from suppliers) while
reducing the maturity of accounts receivable (offered to customers). This implies Indonesian
exporters are squeezed for liquidity in both directions.

147.    The underlyingcause of the recent contraction in trade finance businessis the fall in
the threshold of confidence in financial markets, stemming mainly from a) heightening risk
profile of parties to foreign trade with respect to counterparty risks, agency risks, market risks,
operational risks and performance risks; b) drying up of available liquidity allocated to trade
finance business; and, c) from the perceived uncertainties overshadowing future availability of
foreign currency and the ability of the market players to access foreign currency sources in the
future. The roots of the contraction of trade financing in Indonesia are thus not any different to
those in other countries. As reported in the WTO meeting on trade financing the 12th of
November 2008, the tightness in liquidity and rising risk premia have resulted from companies
and banks reducing highly leveragedpositions, scrambling to recapitalize, and allocating the use
of capital against risk-adjusted returnon capital and Base1I1requirements that force the market
to re-price risk.

148.    With around 85 percent of Indonesia's trade financed by open accounts and 15
percent by way of documentarycredits, such as lettersof credit, trade finance is critical to
sustaining its trade (these percentages are similar to the world average which are 80 and 20
percent re~pectively).~Compounding the decline in global demand, a key concern is that a lack
of confidence among creditors may actually stall global trade. Shipping and commodity markets
are rife with talk that banks are refusing to honor letters of credits from other banks and holding
back guarantees of commodity buyers and sellers needto ship their commodities.

149.    Although trade financing is clearly collateralized and tied directly to cross-border
trade transactions and usually straightforward to organize, it is nevertheless highly
vulnerable in times of crisis. Trade finance to developing countries collapsedduring the 1997-
98 East Asian financial crisis-bank        financed trade credits declined by about 50 percent in
Korea and 80 percent in Indonesia. The recent collapse in a number of commodity prices has
also undermined perceptions of exporter creditworthiness and the coverage provided by goods
shipped as collateral. Moreover, many firms that lend to exporters are not keen on being stuck
with the collateral for a loan ina remoteport and having to try to sell the goods.

150.    There is growing evidence that the current credit crunch is impacting the volume
and cost of trade financing. Although comprehensive data on the supply of trade finance are
not readily available, excess demand is already reflected in higher banking cost to confirm a
letter of credit. Bank customers are now being required to put SO-100% cash margin to get L/C
opened by banks, whereas that margin was 20-30% before. At a meeting with the largest 11
banks providers of trade financing, one large bank openly admitted to a 20% fall in L/C credits.
Banks also reportedthat they do not want to provide trade financing to new clients, so as not to
increase risk in their balance sheets. This has led to an upwardly shift of the supply curve and
has resultedin lower volumes and greater cost.

x.      Relevance to the PESFand proposed actions to ease access to trade finance

151.    One of the channels through which a global financial crisis can be transmitted to the
real economy is through the tightening of trade finance. The tightening of trade financing
prevents Indonesia exporters from fully responding to the favorable environment of a
depreciated rupiah and commodity prices which are still high relative to 2003 levels. Easing
trade financing would help diminish the slowdown in export growth at a time of a slowing down
economy.

152.    Trade financing is not included in DPL5 as it was not a significant constraint on
trade at that time.

153.    Given the rapidly deteriorating financial landscape, the Indonesian authorities, like
those of other emerging economies, have begun to intervene. Indonesia and other major
emerging markets (e.g., India, Korea, and Russia) with short term debt predominantly in the
form of trade credits and guarantees are reported to be looking at ways to extend official credit
lines to banksto pay their trade debts.

154.    Bank Indonesia, the Central Bank of Indonesia, has taken a more direct role in the
market by activating its "re-discount window" for trade receivables.            The re-discount
window is not envisaged as a credit coverage mechanism but, for the moment, as a liquidity
providing mechanism.

155.    In December, Parliament passed a law establishing the Indonesian Eximbank to
further assist in the process of easing the provision of trade credit. Under the law, the current
Bank Ekspor Indonesia (which is a commercial bank) will be converted into an agency of the

assistance in the area of trade finance as part of which the hture role of the Eximbank is also
being examinedand recommendations basedon international experience are being provided.

156.    The World Bank Group is also taking measures to ease access to trade finance by

In addition, IFC is studying the possibility of creating a complementary, temporary Trade Pool
of up to US$2 billion to support trade finance for banks or countries that are currently not a
priority for the GTFP, such as Indonesia and other MICs. IFC is already in negotiations with

Indonesiato extend trade finance facilities to help them maintain a high volume oftrade at a time

          B. Environmental,Poverty,and SocialAspects

163.    OP 8.60 (Development Policy Lending) mandates the Bank to determine whether
policies supported by the operation are likely to cause significant effects on the country's
environment, forests and other natural resources as well as assess the poverty and social
impact. The PESF supports key areas of the Government's crisis-response agenda, including
financial sector reforms, investment climate improvement, and sustainingpublic expenditures to
address the impacts of the crisis. Since the PESF supports direct actions aimed at sustaining
expenditures to help in the areas of poverty alleviation and social protection, it is expected to
have a positive impact on these aspects. The environmental assessment concludes that there are
few areas where environmental issues may arise, and several general improvements in
transparency and regulatory consistency could lead to positive improvements in environmental
and natural resourcemanagement inthe longer term.

          B1.     Poverty and SocialImpacts

164.    I t is expected that this operation will have a large positive impact on poverty
reduction. Policy Area 2: Sustaining Critical Public Expenditures While Maintaining Budget
Discipline was designed specifically to mitigate the effects of the crisis on the poor and
vulnerable. In particular, actions would involve establishing a poverty monitoring system in
anticipation of possible growth slowdown or crisis; ensuring that the monitoring information is
respondedto in a concerted and effective manner; sustaining and ifnecessary increasing critical
public expenditures in the areas of poverty reduction, social protection and infrastructure
development; and expediting public spending inthese critical sectors.

165.    The Government's draft blueprint recognizes the need for real-time information as
the crisis unfolds and the likely heterogeneous effects across the archipelago. It is expected
that data for monitoring will be disaggregated by region as well as gender. Monitoring gender
impactswill be particularly important with regardto changes in employment status and access to
health and education services. The Government's proposed monitoring plan is in fact an action
plan to conduct poverty and social impact analyses as the crisis rolls out, using both quantitative
and qualitative techniques including monthly surveys, health and education facility information
collection, key informant interviews and rapid field appraisals. The Government has already
begun collecting the most recent data by district on formal and nonformal worker employment
trends, returning overseas workers, and the welfare of vulnerable and poor groups. The planned
response mechanismincludesworking through and adapting current poverty reduction and social
protection programs such as CCTs which specifically address these segments, especially

vulnerable women. The Government has already begun consultations with national research

         B2.       Environmental aspects

166.    A detailed review of the environmental aspects of the proposed policy triggers for
PESF is attachedas Annex 8. A briefsummary is presentedbelow.

167.    The Government recognizes that resource degradation poses a risk for continued
growth and development (Country Natural Resource and Environmental Analysis.
BAPPENAS, 2007). Resource depletion affects citizens' well-being and weakens prospects for
sustainable development, for example in forestry, sanitation, and water and air pollution.
Inadequate environmental management and deteriorating environmental conditions impose
significant economic costs, which are disproportionately borne by the poor. Though there have
been improvements in policies and capacity, implementation of environmental management
programs has been weak and gaps between policy and practice have increased under
decentralization.

168.    In recent years, Indonesia has taken important steps to revise environmental
legislation, rationalize energy pricing, reduce deforestation, and increase environmental
spending. In the last year, climate change has galvanizedefforts to see environmental concerns
as sustainable development issues.     Indonesia has reduced fuel subsidies, thus increasing
incentives to conserve fuel and reduce emissions; published its blueprint for integrating climate
change into the national planning and budgeting process; established the National Council on
Climate Change as a center for coordination; and included climate/environment actions in the
annualwork plan, state budget, and medium term development plan.

169.    There is increasing recognition that environmental issues are indeed interlinked
with issues of governance, rule of law, incentives, investment, transparency, and access to
information (as well as attitude, awareness, education, constituency, etc.). Environmental
and natural resource discourse and management approaches are only now turning toward the
upstreampolicy issues that affect downstream outcomes and practices. Gradually, the DPL (and
IDPL) process is taking on policy challenges more directly related to environment and natural
resource issues (e.g., EITI, environmental legislation) and could be used in the future to address
other fundamental policy issues that affect environmental performance and outcomes, although
the Government has chosen to engage with other donors (such as the Agence Franqaise de
Dkveloppement and Government of Japan) for more direct support for the environmental and
climate change agenda.

170.    Actions under Policy Area 1are intended to mitigate some of the potential negative
impacts of global financial sector instability and recession, thus protecting the economy,
job creation potential, and the government's spending power. These actions will have no
likely significant environmental effects. Actions under Policy Area 2 are intended to improve
poverty monitoring and coordination efforts and to expedite or increase critical public
expendituresinthe event of a global slowdown. On the positive side, the monitoring systemwill

help to anticipate and target interventions to protect the poor and disadvantaged groups,
including women. These actions should protect planned spending on approved projects, provide
additional stimulus in key areas, and allow smoother implementation during the budget year,
thus would have no likely significant or negativeenvironmental effects, and potentially positive
effects by aiming mainly at poverty alleviation and social protection.

171.    Actions under Policy Area 3 are intended to clarify rules regarding the Investment
Negative List, improve the investment climate by reducing uncertainties and bureaucratic
discretion, introduce revenue transparency in the extractive industries sector, and support
trade financing. These actions will not relax or undermineany environmental requirements, but
rather reduce uncertainty, and improve transparency and governance. Provisions for stabilizing
trade financing under Policy Area 3 aim to prevent declines in trade in the event of a global
downturn and thus would not introduce new, likely, or significant effects. Implementing EITI
will increase local government and civil society access to information about natural resource
revenues, a first step toward improving governance inthe extractive naturalresource sectors. In
the short term, there are no likely, significant effects. In the medium term, these reforms help
develop the will and confidence to take on additional improvements in rule of law, regulatory
certainty and accountability more generally - including, potentially, in environmental regulation
and enforcement.

          D. Scenarios andRisks

176.    The modal scenario the Bank foresees is one where liquidity conditions ease
sufficiently, both globally and in Indonesia, such that the signaling effect of the support
package is all that is needed. Ifthis is the case, the Go1would meet its financing target from
market sources and the support would remain an unexercisedoption.

177.    A second scenario is one where market conditions remain tight enough that the
draw-down trigger is met but there is no outright crisis. Under this scenario the Go1would
draw down a portion of the loans provided by the World Bank and other development partners
under the Financing Planto support critical public expenditures.

178.    A third scenario, and one that constitutes a key risk in this proposed operation, is
that of a sudden capital outflow crisis. Indonesia's current account and balance of payments
are still in surplus (though likely to shrink in the coming quarters). Indonesia had a current
account surplus of US$ 10 billion in 2007 and US$ 900 million in the first half of 2008 (despite
the rising costs of oil imports). We expect that going forward the current account balance will
deteriorate but stay in neutral territory. As such, although indicators of Indonesia's current
account and balance of payments are still strong, Indonesia is more exposed than other EAP
countries to "externally mobile capital" because of its relatively open capital account and the
degree of foreign presence in its stock and government bond markets. The legacy of the 1998
crisis has also left Indonesian investors sensitive to exchange rate movements and prone to
capital flight. The main risk is from large scale capital outflows, both in the form of foreign
investors liquidating their positions in Indonesian stocks and bonds, and domestic investors
fleeing the rupiah. Were a sudden capital outflow crisis to occur, the typical disbursement
circumstances for development policy loan support, in particular the specific requirement for an
appropriate overall macroeconomic framework, are unlikely to be met. Should the above
scenario take place in advance of the loan disbursement, the PESF funds would not disburse.
However, there also remains the risk that such a crisis erupts after disbursements have been
effected by development partners, with implications for further Bank support. Such a situation
would most likely require a macroeconomicpackage with other institutions potentially playing a
more prominent role and any support package would then conceivably become part of such a
larger macroeconomicpackage.

                              Annex 6. Debt SustainabilityAnalysis

180.    Indonesia has achieved dramatic success in reducing its debt burden over this
decade. By late 2008, even after a one-fifth depreciation against the USD, Indonesia's debt
burden is projected to be a little over 30 percent of GDP, or almost 5 percentage points lower
than a year earlier. While the government has demonstrated an ongoing commitment to a
sustainable fiscal position, it remains important to assess the impact of historically plausible
growth, fiscal, exchange rate and interest rate shocks on the burden of Indonesia's debt levels
and servicing requirements.

181,     The debt sustainability analysis presented in the following figures begins with a
baseline projection of economic growth, of a constant real exchange rate, servicing
requirements of Indonesia's existing stock of debt in line with the contractual obligations,
and plausible disbursement projections consistent with the government's budget and
financing plans and historical trends. It then shocks key variables, using their historical
volatility. In the case of Indonesia, the period over which these variables' volatility is calculated
includes the economic crisis of the late 1990s. It also shocks the variables by holding, for
example, the budget deficit at current levels. If 2008 is taken as the base year, when the
government achieved a very small surplus, this leads to a rapid fall in Indonesia's debt burden.
If,however, 2009 is taken as the year, when the governmentplansto apply the financing surplus
from 2008 to fund a stimulus packageto offset the impact o fthe global economic downturn, then
the outcomewould be a turn-around inthe recenttrends with Indonesia's debt burdenexpanding
again.

182.    Assessing the sustainability of Indonesia's debt burden requirescomparison against
a generally accepted benchmark. These benchmarks account for an economy's policy
performance, hence the likely ability of its policy makersto manageexogenous shocks and their
impact on the health of its public finances. This policy performance can be expressed, for
example, in its Country Policy and Institutional Assessment rating. Indonesia's CPIA rating was
assessed to be 3.86 in 2007, placing it in the `strong' category. With this rating, the thresholds
above which an economy's debt level and servicing requirements are consideredto be of concern
are relatively high (these thresholds are illustrated with the impact of various shocks in Figure
8).

            Annex 7. TechnicalAnnex on Public Financial Management (PFM)

                         1. Backgroundand Analytical Underpinnings

183.   This operation provides general budget support to the Indonesian government and
will be executed through the government's public financial management systems. This
annex summarizes the current state of these systems and the ongoing reforms with a view to
assessing the attendant fiduciary risks with respect to the proceeds of this development policy
loan.

184.   The public financial management systems in Indonesia have strengths and
weaknesses. The strengths include a sound regulatory framework for most parts of the budget
cycle, an orderly, transparent and predictable budget formulation process, fund predictability for
budget execution, sound budget classification, comprehensive and transparent budget
documentation, Government Accounting Standards that are aligned with comparable
international standards, a chart of accounts which is broadly consistent with GFSM 2001 and a
strengthenedexternal audit institution.

185.   These strong points are the result of a concerted reform effort among key
stakeholders to the budget process since the political transition in 1998. These efforts were
given momentum and direction inthe 2001 White Paper on public financial managementreform.
Key stakeholders to the budget processremain committed to further reform through an evolving
and pragmatic reform agenda. The focus of government reform is on the implementation of an
integrated financial management information system, further unification of the budget, further
improvement of the chart of accounts, restructuring of budget programs, the introduction of a
medium term expenditure framework and performance based budgeting, a full roll-out of the
TSA, improvement ofthe institutional and regulatory framework for procurement, improvements
to cash and asset management, internal controls and internal and external audit.

186.   DPLs 1 4 have had public financial management as an area of focus and have
contributed to some of the significant improvements made in the last few years. The details
of the results achieved in DPLs 1-4 on PFM are discussed in Annex 3 under Pillar 3. PFM
improvement is a key focus area for DPL 5 and the priority policy actions related to the area are
discussedindetail inChapter V (Section C) ofthe program document.

187.   The assessment draws on recent economic and sector work undertaken by the
World Bank and other development partners. A PEFA assessment was concluded in June
2008. In addition we have taken into considerationthe findings o f ajoint Bank-Fund mission on
Strengthening Budget Management undertaken in June 2008, the results of a previous joint
Bank-Fund mission on Budget Reform Priorities reported to government in June 2007 and the
September 2008 update of the ROSC on Fiscal Transparency in Indonesia from 2006 (IMF
Country Report No. 08/298, Selected issues; Indonesia). The assessment also draws on the
ongoing policy dialogue and advisory services provided through the GFMRAP and the
associatedPFMMulti-Donor Trust Fund.

                 2. Key PFMIssues-Recent Developmentsand the way ahead

188.     Significant advances have been made in budget preparation - instituting a state
budget which combines the previous separate recurrent and development budgets and
improvements in budget transparency. However, problems in budget execution persist with
50 percent of the capital expenditure still being spent in the last quarter. The Treasury has
conducted a survey of the impediments to the disbursement of budgetary funds through
questionnaires. The results of the survey are being reviewed and will form the basis for an action
plan to smooth out disbursements during the fiscal year.

189.     Implementation of a TSA as foreseen by State Treasury Law No. 1/2004 is ongoing.
The Treasury in cooperationwith BI is presently piloting the daily sweep of government revenue
deposits from collecting branchesand post offices into the TSA. Depending on the success o fthe
pilot, the daily sweep will be extended to all commercial bank branches starting January 2009.
The public procurement environment has witnessed improvements in the last few years.
However, there are still weaknesses in its regulatory and implementation aspects. Perpres No.
106/2007 was signed in December 2007 establishing an independentagency - LKPP - which is
responsible for planning and development of strategies, policies and regulations associatedwith
the procurement using public funds. Making the newly established LKPP operational by
recruiting staffand allocating budget is a priority.

190.     The Government Accounting Standards are well aligned with comparable
international standards (IPSAs). The chart of accounts is broadly consistent with GFSM
2001. The aggregate Government Annual Financial Statements have been prepared in a timely
manner for four years now. However, there are questions on reliability. The Supreme Audit
Agency (BPK) has for the last four years issued a "disclaimer" opinion on these financial
statements. Among the reasons cited for this were internal control weaknesses inthe preparation
ofthe financial statements and instancesof non compliance with applicable regulations.

191.     The internal audit framework of the Government is extensive but the institutional
structure is complex. Government Regulation 60/2008 on Government's Internal Controls
issued in August 2008 defines more clearly the roles and responsibilities of the different players
in internal audit. The overall internal control framework continues to be weak with paper
intensive processes and inefficient use of human resources.           GO1 is preparing for the
implementation of an IntegratedFinancial Management System known by the acronym SPAN to
replace the multitude of financial data processing applications currently used by government
institutions.

192.     The external audit institution, BPK, has received significant increase in its
operating budget to fund an increase in the number of auditors as well as regional offices.
The audit report on the financial statements is submitted to legislature within 5 months after the
end of the year. A peer review of the BPK was completed in 2004 and revealed serious
weaknesses incapacity. Since then, improvementsare gradually being made to the organization,
with new hires andtraining. BPK has a follow up peer review plannedfor FY 2009.

193.     Powerful independent oversight and prosecutorial institutions such as the
Corruption Eradication Commission(KPK) and the Anti-Corruption Court have begun to
deliver tangible results. Highprofile corruption investigations have beenlaunched, leadingto a
number of successful prosecutions.

194.   Parliamentary oversight of budgets processes has been strengthened. Parliamentary
commissions have become particularly active in scrutinizing and amending the
government's draft annual budget. The government published IMF fiscal transparency report
and undertook its socialization throughout Indonesia. The public availability of fiscal
information is already improving and the government has published a fiscal risk statement
together with the 2008 budget.

195.   These reforms directly influence the environment in which public expenditure is
planned, budgeted, executed and accounted for across all levels of government. A more
detailed commentary on progress in implementing these reforms is given below to help identify
the attendant risks arising from the borrower's capacity to manage this loan and determine the
fiduciary arrangementsfor this operation.


                                3. Reforming BudgetFormulation

196.   Fiscal relationships between the executive and legislature are defined in the State
FinanceLaw No. 1712003. The HouseofRepresentatives(DPR) is the principal legislative body
and it plays a significant role in shaping the budget and fiscal policy. The budget is passed by
agreementbetweenthe presidentand the DPR. The process involves an examination by the DPR
of the macroeconomic framework, macro-fiscal policies and the detailed budget allocations. The
budget has been the centerpiece o f strengthening the legislature vis-a-vis the executive in the
political transition following 1998and the relative roles of the executive and legislative branches
inthis areaare evolving.

197.   The budget documentation is comprehensive with the main outstanding area of
improvement being explanation of the expenditure implications of new policy initiatives.
The laws governing budget management clearly specify the responsibilities of the Minister of
Finance and comprehensiveregulations have been developed. While the president, as headof the
government, has overall authority to exercise national fiscal management, Law No. 17/2003
clearly delegates the responsibility of overall fiscal management of central government finances
to the Minister of Finance as chief financial officer. The law also assigns responsibilities to
individual ministers, governors and other local authorities for financial management and
accountability in theirjurisdictions.

198.   Provisions of the new regulations are being implemented. Budget classification and
execution is based on administrative, economic and functional classification consistent with
COFOG/GFSM2001. The Ministry of Finance is developing plans for a medium-term
expenditure framework (MTEF) to guide budget policy. Forward estimates at the aggregate level
were introducedwith the 2008 budget and are also being included in the 2009 budget. Efforts in
Bappenas and the Ministry of Finance are ongoing to redefine the budget program structure and
to develop templates and manuals for a medium-term expenditure framework and performance
basedbudgeting.The efforts are plannedto be piloted inthe coming years.

199.   The level of extra-budgetary expenditures is not known and the issue is not on the
reform agenda.

                                      4. ReformingBudget Execution

200.    Indonesiastill spends 50 percentof its total capital expenditurein the final quarter
of the year.6 For the past six years, spending has always started slowly and then accelerated
towards the end of each year. This spending pattern is of concern because project
implementation is disrupted by an adverse cycle. Moreover, under-spending on capital
expenditure constrains increases in infrastructure investments. Slow and back-loaded
disbursements are symptoms of more severe challenges that are encountered at each stage of the
public expenditure managementcycle.

201.     The government maintainsa comparatively rigid budget execution process.Detailed
input controls aim to ensure that the composition of the budget complies with political priorities
and that the budget is not altered during execution. Spending warrants (DIPAs), while now
issued at the start of the fiscal year, contain excessive amounts of detail leaving little flexibility
for adjustments in the composition of inputs needed to carry out a given activity. Re-allocations
across DIPAs from delayed programs to better performing ones that could enhance satisfactory
implementation of the overall expenditure program require lengthy revision, sometimes
involvingparliament. Such inflexibility creates practical difficulties and inefficiencies.

202.     With expanding fiscal space, public investment is increasing, putting additional
pressure on PFM systems. Planning and executing public investment is inherently more
demandingthan current or mandatory expenditure.


                        5. ReformingTreasury, Cash and Asset Management

203.     Transparency is still weakened by a multitude of bank accounts. The DG Treasury
itself maintains multiplebank accounts, both in rupiah and foreign currency, at Bank Indonesia.
External audit reports from 2004 to 2006 noted that there are around 4,600 bank accounts that
have not been reported to the DG Treasury. By the end of December 2007, the Ministry of
Finance completed a census of all government accounts and identified 32,570 accounts.
Subsequently 2,086 accounts have been closed. Efforts are on-going to determine which of the
remaining 30,484 accounts can be included in the TSA regime either directly or through a
mechanismofdaily sweeps into the TSA.

Treasury oversight, with a view to closing all those that do not serve the public interest. Pilot

Indonesia and commercial banks on interest payments for government balances held at Bank
Indonesia and remuneration o f banking services by the government. The Treasury is presently
piloting the daily sweep of government revenue deposits from collecting branches and post

                                    6. Procurement Reforms
209.    The past few years have witnessed improvements in the public procurement
environment. Keppres No. 80/2003 provided a national public procurement regulation that
meets most of what i s generally regarded as accepted international practice, including basic
principles: transparency, open and fair competition, economy and efficiency. This decree also
requires that a national public procurement office be established as a regulatory body for public
procurement. With the introduction of Keppres No. 80/2003, a previous certification ("pre-
qualification") process for suppliers/ providers of goods and services was, in most instances,
abandonedby the government. This previously led to segmentation of the market, especially at
the provincial level. Keppres No. 80/2003 also established the basis for sanctions, complaint-
handling and requirementsfor certification of users.


                                                84

210.    However, the public procurement system still has significant deficiencies in its
regulatory and, more importantly, implementation aspects. There have been delays in
establishing a strong regulatory body; slow progress in the development of procurement
regulations anchored by a law; slow progress in the development of standard tools in terms of
bidding documents and users manuals; weakness in procurement capacity in implementing
agencies, especially at the provincial and district levels; and collusion and corruption practices in
the bidding process as well as concerns over the efficiency of anti-corruption and sanction
measures.

211.    This is supported by the findings of some analytical tools and reports such as the
self-diagnostic assessment conducted by the NPPO in 2007 using the Base-Line Indicators
(BLIs) tool developed under the World Bank and OECD Development Assistance
Committee Procurement Round Table initiative. The assessment o f the BLIs presents a
"snapshot" comparison of the actual system against the international standards or the "model
system" that the BLIs represent. Baseline bench marking of Indonesia's procurement system in
June 2007 showed it scoring 62.5 percent for the Legislative and Regulatory Framework
(PILLAR I), 55 percent for Institutional Framework and Management Capacity (PILLAR II),
59.3 percent for Procurement Operations and Market Practices (PILLAR IIO and 69 percent for
Integrity and Transparency of the Public Procurement System (PILLAR Iv) in comparison with
recognized international standards. Other studies indicate that there is significant room for
improvement.

212.    Perpres No. 106/2007 was signed in December 2007 establishing an independent
agency, the Lembaga Kebijakan Pengadaan Barang/Jasa Pemerintah (LKPP) or National
Public Procurement Office, that will be responsible for sustainable, integrated, focused and
coordinated planning and development of strategies/policies/regulations associated with the
procurement of goods/works/services using public funds. This institution will report directly
to the President of the Republic of Indonesia. The organization structure for the new LKPP is
being developed. In addition to the Chairman, who heads LKPP, and an Executive Secretary,
there will be four departments, each headedby a Deputy with responsibilities for (i)   Strategy and
Policy Development, (ii)Monitoring-Evaluation and Information Systems, (iii)Human
Resources Development, and (iv) Legal Affairs and Settlement of Objections respectively. The
LKPP is currently staffing Echelon I1positions and expected to complete all the staffing needs
by the end of 2008.

213.    A number of laws have been enacted since 2000 that impact, or make reference to,
public procurement. These include civil works, state finance, treasury, audit and small-scale
business. The pace of Indonesia's decentralization reforms has impacted on public sector
procurement at all levels o f government, with ministers, governors, and even mayors able to
issue decrees, regulations and instructions. The plethora of regulations i s often inconsistent and
many regulations do not meet accepted international practice. Consequently, national
procurement reform and the need for national procurementpolicies and standards are critical.

214.    Indonesia's   legal framework for public sector procurement can best be
strengthened by anchoring it with an overarching consolidated and comprehensive
national public sector procurement law at the highest level that (i)establishes the
fundamental principles and proceduresapplicable to all public sector procurement, (ii)    allows for
the imposition of sanctions where the principles and procedures, particularly those relating to
good governance and ethics, are not met, (iii)amends other laws that refer to public sector

                                                 85

procurement, and (iv) ensures that such a law has the necessary authority in a decentralized
environment. The LKPP is currently drafting a procurement law with an expectedtwo years time
horizon for enacting this law as it needs to go through a review and consultation process
followed by submission to the parliament.

215.     In the meantime, LKPP has consolidated the different amendments of Keppres No.
80/2003 into one document. It is also planning to revise this with the objective of improving it
with possible simplification for users.

216.     Standard bidding documents (SBD): Currently, there are no national standard bidding
documents that are used regularly by all government agencies in Indonesia. Some implementing
agencies (such as the Ministry of Public Works) have developed standard bidding documents for
their own use. The NPPO, through the support of ADB, has drafted eight National Model
(Standard) Bidding Documents being (1) An Explanatory Guide, (2) Goods with Pre-
qualification, (3) Goods with Post-qualification, (4) Works with Pre-qualification, (5) Works
with Post-qualification, (6) Other Services with Pre-qualification, (7) Other Services with Post-
qualification, and (8) Consulting Services, and that are now available in a draft form. The use of
these documents is still not mandatory and they are being used on a pilot basis. It is important for
LKPP to collect comments from users on these documents, improve them and mandate their use
on a national basis. One ofthe improvementswould be to harmonize such documents with those
of International Development Partnerssuch as the World Bank, ADB, JBIC and AusAID.

217.     Public Procurement Function: There has been growing concern, both by the
government and the Bank, over the pace of project implementation. Significant delays and slow
disbursementseem to occur across the entire PFM cycle. Projectsreported delays and difficulties
in budget preparation and approval, budget execution, and implementation/procurement.
Specifically, there are concerns with the implementation/procurementcapacity of government
staff and the absence of incentives for project managers/procurementcommittees. Added to this,
fear of prosecution by auditors and anti-corruption agencies has also affected projects due to the
reluctance of officials to take action amid growing worries of being prosecuted under anti-
corruption legislation, even over minor mistakes. LKPP is considering assessing the introduction
of central procurementunitsat ministriesand at local government level.

218.     Capacity-building: Keppres No. 80/2003 required the government to adopt an
examination system and certification for procurement practitioners within government
contracting entities in order to ensure that their level of competence is consistent with their level
of responsibilities. So far, the rate of staff passingthe basic procurementcertification is very low
(less than 20 percent) which amplifies the need for a strategic approach to capacity-building.
LKPP is inthe process of developing a strategy for humanresourcesand training/certification on
public procurement. This will entail development of training courses and criteria for tertiary
institutions.

219.     Corruption,sanctions and independentappeals mechanism: Despite improvements in
the public procurement system, concerns remain over corrupt and collusive practices. While
addressing this requires an overall governance strategy, there are several important elements in
the procurement system that can support such a strategy, for example an independent complaints
mechanism, an effective sanctions mechanism, and improving transparency in the procurement
processthrough the use of e-procurement.



                                                   86

220.    Keppres No. 80/2003 requires the establishment of a complaints handling
mechanism. However, this is not set up independently but within each implementing agency.
LKPP is now in the process of developing a strategy to approach complaints handling. While it
is imperative to develop procedures for an independent complaints handling mechanism, LKPP
should avoid becoming the agency to which these complaints are addressed but rather develop a
system and monitor its efficiency and reliability.

221.    E-procurement: The Ministry of Public Works, Ministry of Communications and
Information and the City of Surabaya have all been at the forefront in the development of e-
Government Procurement (e-GP) technology. The NPPO was also taking the lead on behalf of
the government for implementation of e-GP in Indonesia at a national and sub-national level.
The critical issue now is to develop a roadmap for the development of e-GP in the country,
setting the roles of the different stakeholders, and the responsibility for ensuring minimum inter-
operability and core data standards for the various e-GP providers to comply with.

228.     The Audit Board (BPK) was established under the Constitution and its roles and
responsibilities are set out in Law No.15/2004. The organizationalstructure o f BPK is outlined

                               9. InternalControls and Internal Audit

233.     The internal audit framework of the government is extensive but the institutional
structure is complex and the mandates and division of labor between the various internal
audit institutions have not been clear. PP No. 60/2008 on government internal controls issued
inAugust 2008 defines more clearly the roles and responsibilities ofthe different players.

234.     The BPKP is now entrusted with the task of designing the guidelines on internal
control implementation, providing training and improving the competence of the
government's internal auditors on government's internal controls - essentially an advisory
role. BPKP is also mandated to audit cross-sectoral activities, respond to audit requests by the
presidentand audit the government's treasury activities ifassigned by MoF.

235.     Inspectors-General (IG) are still the main internal audit apparatus of the line
ministries. In principle, IGs have a mandate to audit all MDA activities and determine their
annual audit plan independently and all units within an MDA are requiredto provide data needed
by IGs. In practice, most audits by IGs check compliance with technical and operational aspects
o f activities, including compliance with internal control procedures. Evaluations to determine the
adequacy o f internal control systems are rarely carried out. A systematic risk-based approach to
internal audits is still in its infancy. Starting from 2006, IGs also review their MDA's annual
financial statements, to ensure their accuracy, reliability and integrity, prior to their submission
to DG Treasury inMoF.

236.     There was no formal commitment control system at Satker and MDA levels that
records, reports, monitors or controls spending commitments during the year against
approved budgets and cash availability. However, Finance Minister Regulation No
171PMK.05/2007 on Accounting System for Line Ministries now passes on the responsibility
for commitment control to line ministries. The DIPA documents, which are detailed down to the
spending unit or Satker level, prepared by the MoF establish annual budgeted ceilings on
expenditure commitments. These DIPAs generally prevent MDAs from placing orders above the
approved budgets (DIPA limits) for particular spending items.

expenditures, which supplement other written procedures on accounting and recording
transactions. The processes are, however, paper intensive and involve inefficient use o f staff
resources. For example, the supporting documentation required for payment o f goods and
services covers multiple documents which duplicate information, with weak audit trails and
accounting evidence.     8

                     10. ReformingRegionalPublic FinancialManagement

241.    Decentralization has provided local governments with significant resources and
authority. More than one-third o f general government expenditures are now executed by sub-
national government. This requires an adequate regulatory fiamework and PFM capacity at the
local level. After decentralization, the central government passed comprehensive legislation for
PFMreforms at the regional level to mirror the changes being made at the center. The regulatory
framework for regional PFM reforms is now largely in place including mechanisms for fiscal
transfers to local governments. However, most regions lack adequate technical and human
resources to implement the reforms. Sub-national governments are obliged by law to report
certain fiscal and financial information to the central government. Most local governments now
do report the information required but do so with some delay. Accordingly it is now possible to
produce consolidatedgeneral government reports although with some delay.


242.    Allocationsto individual sub-nationalgovernmentunitsare formally issuedwith the
passingof the nationalbudget in October beforethe financialyear commencesbut notional
allocations are also included in the budget presented to the DPR in August. Inpractice, this
leaves sufficient time for sub-national government unitsto finalize their budgets but submissions
are often late due to local government constraints. However, budget allocations o f shared
revenues (non-tax revenue) are not issued before MarcWApril in the financial year. The notional
allocations are significantly underestimated by the central government and disbursements from
the center to the regions are back loaded towards the end o f the fiscal year. Both practices are
deliberately chosen by central government to ensure fiscal discipline but they constrain
successful financial management at local level.

243.    Because of over-budgetingof expenditure by sub-nationalgovernments and under-
budgeting of transfers by central government many sub-national governments have built
up reservesin recent years.


                                   11. State-OwnedEnterprises

244.    Indonesia has a sound legal framework for corporate governance and monitoring
SOE operations.Firstly, the SOE Law No. 19/2003 sets a clear ownership policy as the state is
not allowed to be involved in the day-to-day management o f SOEs and must allow them full
                                                 90

operational autonomy. Secondly, a 2002 ministerial decree clearly details corporate governance
responsibilities, including the need to: (i)submit each SOE's financial statements to the State
Ministry for State-Owned Enterprises and to the appropriate line ministry each quarter; (ii)
prepare annual financial statements that must be audited internally and by an independent
auditor; and (iii)publish SOE annual reports ina timely manner.

245.    Existing laws and regulations governing SOEs are not effectively implemented.
Long delays still occur in auditing and publishing SOE financial statements. SOE annual reports
are usually only regularly and systematically made public in cases where companies have
accessed the local capital markets with listed instruments. The government has included fiscal
risk statements in the budgets for FY08 and FY09 and is working on further improving the
quality ofthe budget process.

246.    The planned use of PPPs to accelerate investment will create fiscal risks that merit
disclosure and the government has taken steps to mitigate those risks. PP No. 6712005
emphasizes careful project preparation and the selection of bidders in an open, competitive
tender and gives the Minister of Finance the right to approve or reject requests for government
support.


                                     12. Debt Management

247.    During the previous DPL series, two major initiatives in debt management have
been implemented; a clear debt management strategy which was publicly announced, and a
newly created DG for Public Debt Management (DGDM), which has a risk management
oversight for both domestic and external debts.

248.    By 2007 the government had also successfully reduced its debt-to-GDP ratio to
approximately 35 percent from the peak of over 90 percent in 1999. Despite the positive
development in terms of debt sustainability and institutional reforms, the government's
institutional capacity to manage public debts remains weak. Audit reports confirm that data and
reports on domestic debt are reliable but indicate that data and reports on foreign debt differ from
donor records and may not be accurate.


                              13. Governance and Anti-corruption

249.     Indicators of corruption in Indonesia are improving, albeit slowly. Sufficient and
dedicated resources have been made available to most of the agencies critical to anti-
corruption efforts. Powerful independent oversight and prosecutorial institutions such as the
KPK and the Anti-Corruption Court have begun to deliver tangible results. In addition,
institutions like the BPK, the Financial Transaction and Analysis Centre (PPATK), and the
Attorney General's Office have all critically improved their anti-corruption track record.

250.    High profile corruption investigations have been launched, leading to a number of
successful prosecutions. For example, as of September 2008, seven parliamentary members are
under investigation or prosecution as suspects in several corruption cases, most of them
involving bribery. Some of the parliamentary members were arrested when caught red-handed
receiving bribes. A senior prosecutor has also been sentenced to 20 years inprison for receiving


                                                91

bribes. This case has in turn led to the removal of three very senior prosecutors, includingtwo
Deputies ofthe Attorney General.

251.    While there have clearly been successes and there is strong momentum in
investigating and prosecutingindividual corruption cases, there has been less progress in
developing and implementing a coherent, well-focused strategy for corruption prevention
within the state administration. MenPAN, the ministry tasked with coordinating, monitoring
and evaluating implementationof such a strategy, has limited capability in this regard. As a
result, implementationof the existing anti-corruption strategy for the state administration has
beenweak.

252.    While progress in developing and implementing a coherent, well-focused strategy
for corruption prevention in the state administration is limited, recent developments have
been promising. Previously, the KPK was in charge of pushing and monitoring bureaucratic
reform as a corruption prevention measure within several institutions such as the Ministry o f
Finance, the Supreme Court and the BPK, but when this task was handed over to MenPAN in
2007 progress became very slow. Despite the limited progress by MenPAN, individual
government agencies including the Ministry o f Foreign Affairs, Ministry of Trade and most
recently the Attorney General's Office have initiated important internal bureaucratic reforms
which are well underway.

                             15. Management of Foreign Exchange

256.   The foreign exchange control environment is assessed to be generally satisfactory.
The country is no longer subject to the Extended Arrangement from the IMF. Bank Indonesia
(BI) was last subject to the transitional procedures under the Fund's safeguards assessment
policy in 2002.     That assessment recommended remedial action to address a number o f
vulnerabilities in the audit arrangements o f BI. The main recommendations have been
implemented, including the establishment o f an independent audit committee at BI and the
publication o f BI's audited financial statements. Audited financial statements o f BI for 2007
have been reviewed and the audit report issued by the BPK contained an unqualified audit
opinion.


                                            16. Conclusion

257.   Indonesia has established a sound legal and administrative framework for modern
PFM. Changes in the legal and regulatory architecture are now largely complete and the
momentum has shifted towards implementation o f new PFMpractices.

258.   Known weaknesses in financial management and accountability continue to be
gradually addressed through the PFM reform program discussed above. The development
partners are actively engaged in this process. Key elements o f the reforms are supported by the
DPL triggers, as well as the GFMRAP project and initiatives supported by development partners.
However, much remains to be done, and it will take time to realize the full impact o f these
reforms.

259.   In the meantime, some fiduciary risks could arise for this operation. Although the
pace o f implementing the planned reforms has been somewhat slow over the past year, the
trajectory o f reform is inthe right direction and the current government continues to demonstrate
a commitment to continue the task o f completing the planned reforms inPFM. Taking these into
consideration, the Bank assessment team does not propose putting in place any additional
fiduciary arrangements for this operation.

               Annex 8. EnvironmentalReview of PESF ProposedPolicy Program

GeneralOverview of EnvironmentalIssues

260.    The Governmentrecognizesthat resourcedegradationposes a risk for continued growth and
development. The Country Natural Resource and Environmental Analysis (Bappenas, 2007)
addresses concerns related to energy, water, forestry, mining and climate change. "Negative
externalities from inappropriate or inadequate environmental management and over-utilization of
natural resources" can undermine hture growth prospects by degrading water quality, increasing
competition for water quantity, declining quality of air and soils, and contributions to GHG
emissions.     The Government acknowledges many examples of resource depletion that affect
citizens' well-being and weaken prospects for sustainable development, for example in forestry,
sanitation, and water and air pollution. "Over-reliance on natural resources negatively impacts the
environment, which in turn disrupts quality o f life and livelihoods of both poor and well-off
Indonesians."

261.    Indonesia understands the strong economic sustainability rationale to address
governance and policy concerns in the energy sector. "Relatively low energy consumption per
capita and high energy intensity indicate both Indonesia's relatively low welfare and inefficient use
of energy." The Government is also working to improve policy consistency, reducedistortions, and
improve management to become more efficient and competitive, encourage energy security,
improve environmental servicedquality of life, and free up budgetary resources for priority needs
for investment and job creation. Excess and inefficient use of energy resources causes emissions
and pollution that damage the environment and impose health costs. Indonesia is also striving to
increase the use of its abundant renewable energy sources, including geothermal, hydro, and
biomass.

262.    Inadequate environmental management is a challenge for Indonesia that hurts the
poor and the economy. Deteriorating environmental conditions impose significant economic costs
(BAPPENAS, 2007), which are disproportionately borne by the poor because they are more likely
to be exposed to pollution and less likely to afford mitigation measures. Though there have been
some improvements in policies and investments in capacity development, implementation of
environmental management programs has been weak and gaps between policy and practice have
increasedunder decentralization.

263.    In recent years, global attention on climate change has galvanized efforts to address
environmental concerns in the context of sustainable development. Indonesia has taken a
number o f important steps to rationalize energy pricing and reduce deforestation. The Government
has doubled spending on environmental programs in recent years and improved environmental laws
and regulations. For more than a decade, Indonesia has used a tax break system to encourage
import of pollution control technology and is now looking at ways to provide incentives for import
of climate friendly technologies. Indonesia offers soft loans (with donor assistance) for installation
of pollution control equipment. Inthe last year, Indonesia has: reduced fuel subsidies inthe face of
rapidly rising global fuel prices, thus increasing incentives to conserve fuel and reduce emissions;
published its blueprint for integrating climate change into the national planning and budgeting
process; established a National Council on Climate Change as a center for coordination across the
many branches of government; and included climate change/environmentactivities into the annual


                                                 94

work plan, the financial notes for the state budget, and the mediumterm development plan for the
next five years.

264.    The Government is making progress but has chosen with engage with development
partnersother than the Bank to advance the environmentalagenda. The Government is deeply
engaged with the international community in studying and funding environmental and climate
change actions and programs.        Although perceptions are changing and the dialogue about
sustainability as an economic issue and climate change as a development issue is advancing, the
DPL process is seen to be focused on macro economic and investment climate issues. Many
environmental issues are indeed interlinked with issues of governance, rule of law, incentives,
investment, transparency, and access to information (as well as attitude, awareness, education,
constituency, etc.) However, the environmental and natural resource discourse and management
approaches are only recently beginning to turn toward the underlying and upstream policy issues
that affect downstreamoutcomes and practices.

265.    The DPL process is buildingthe capacity and appetite for taking on fundamental and
structural issues. Success with the kinds of reforms in the policy matrix is gradually building the
will and confidence to improve rule of law, regulatory certainty and accountability more generally.
Increasing transparency in resource revenues, regulatory certainty in the investment climate, and
civil society access to information will begin to buildthe capacity and institutions neededultimately
to improve environmental and natural resource outcomes, at least indirectly. Gradually, the DPL
(and IDPL) process is taking on policy issues and challenges more directly related to environment
and natural resource sector issues (e.g., EITI, environmental legislation).         It is hoped that,
eventually, the Government will recognize some key areas where the DPL approach can be used
more explicitly to address fundamental policy issues that affect environmental performance and
outcomes.

EnvironmentalReview of Operation

266.    Under Operational Policy 8.60 (Development Policy Lending) the Bank determines whether
policies supported by the operation are likely to cause significant effects on the country's
environment, forests and other natural resources. The preliminary environmental review for PESF
DPL DDO is contained in a matrix and discussed briefly here. Because the operation is focused on
financial, investmentand trade support actions, the environmental review indicates few areas where
environmental issues may arise, and several general improvements in transparency and regulatory
consistency could leadto positive effects inthe longer term.

267.    Policy Area 1: Reassuring Financial Markets and Maintaining Financial System
Stability. Triggers under this policy area all focus on banking sector roles, criteria, regulations
most to increase confidence and stability of the financial system, in the face of impending global
economic conditions. These actions are intendedto mitigate some of the potential negative impacts
of global financial sector instability and recession, thus protecting the economy, job creation
potential, and the government's spending power. These actions will have no likely significant
environmental effects.

268.    Policy Area 2: Sustaining Critical Public Expenditures While Maintaining Budget
Discipline. Establishing a poverty monitoring system and a decree on coordination efforts should
have no likely significant negative environmental or social effects. Rather the opposite: this action
will be useful for anticipating and targeting interventions to protect the poor. Data collection and
analysis shouldpay attention to gender and region to improve future targeting.

                                                  95

269.     Committing to actions that sustain or increase critical public expenditures and
expedite budget disbursements(especially in the areas of poverty alleviation,social protection,
and infrastructuredevelopment) in the event of a pronounced growth slowdown should have no
likely significant effects, since these actions could, at best, return conditions to the status quo ante
as planned and budgeted. Increased or accelerated disbursement would be aimed at previously
planned and approved projects, not used to introduce new or poorly designed short term
 intervention^.^

270.     In fact, there is potential for social and environmentally positive effects, since
increased expenditures would be aimed at poverty alleviation and social protection. There is
also potential for positive benefitsfrom smoother government implementation of plannedprograms,
less crisis managementor corner cutting at the end of the year.

271.     Policy Area 3: Crowding In Private Investment and Supporting Exports. Efforts to
establish a Presidential Regulation on the Investment Negative List aim to improve the investment
climate by clarifying rules of the game and reducing uncertainties and bureaucratic discretion.
These actions will not relax or undermine any environmental rules or licensing requirements", but
rather reduce uncertainty, improve transparency, and improve governance for potential investors.
To the extent that these actions improve enabling conditions, more investments could be proposed
in future (especially in target sectors including telecoms, ports, hospitals, retail trade, logistics,
insurance, and pharmaceuticals). Environmental impact assessment and compliance rules would
remain in place - and in fact there could be stronger rationale for enforcing these, if transparency,
accountability and rule of law are strengthenedwithin the bureaucracy generally. Inthe short term,
there are no likely, significant effects. Inthe longer term, success with these kinds of reforms can
step-by-step build the will and confidence to improve rule of law, regulatory certainty and
accountability more generally - including, one day, inenvironmental regulation and enforcement.

272.     Taking steps to increase transparency regarding revenues from extractive industries
hasthe potentialto improveenvironmentaland natural resource outcomes, at least indirectly.
EITI is an internationally recognized global standard that promotes revenue transparency. This set
of triggers will increase local government and civil society access to information about natural
resource revenues, and thus has the potential to improve governance in the extractive industries.
This will contribute generally to the Government's anti-corruption efforts, potentially saving state
revenuesfor priority public expenditures on social and other programs. These actions will enhance
the investment climate by improving rule of law and consistency. Improved rule of law and
regulatory consistency, as well as access and participation by civil society, have the potential to
(indirectly) produce significant positive effects for environmental and natural resource management
for the affected sectors.

273.     Opening a re-discount window for trade finance aims to improve liquidity, with the
expectation to keep trade from declining too far from prior levels in the event of global
downturn. This will not increase trade or target particular sectors beyond levels that have been
seen inrecent past. Thus, there are no likely significant effects.
