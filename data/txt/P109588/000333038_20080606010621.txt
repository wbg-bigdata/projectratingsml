I. STRATEGICCONTEXTANDRATIONALE

A. Countryand sector issues
1. Accelerated development o foil, gas andmineralresources ando fassociated industries and
infrastructure i s a key element o f the strategy o fthe Government o f Cameroon (GoC) for growth
andpoverty reduction. The sustainable development o fthe country's natural resources canbe
beneficial to the citizens of Cameroon iftransparently managed, because it can generate revenues
that can beusedto deliver social services andeducation, find transport infrastructure, and
generate employment opportunities.
2.   A keyprerequisite o fthis strategy is the availability o f sufficient andreliable electricity,
both for household and industrial purposes. Demand for mediumand low voltage i s expected to
grow at an average rate o f 6 percent per year duringthe period 2006-2020. GoC has developed
the Least Cost Development Plan (LCDP) to increase electricity supply for urbanand industrial
usage. Implementationo f the planwould include a succession o f investments inlarge
infrastructureprojects, such as the Kribi gas power plant, the Dibamba power plant, the Lom
Pangar and Nachtigal dams, andpossibly the Song Mbengue, Song Ndeng, Kikot, and Noun
Wouri dams. GoC has also created the Electricity Development Corporation (EDC) to
implement this policy.
3.   Increased electricity supply would make it possible to develop several large mines, most
particularly:
         Sundance Resources i s developing the Mbalam Iron Ore Project inSouth Eastern
         Cameroon, next to the NkiNational Park. It i s located inclose proximity to the Belinga
         Iron Ore Project inGabon, and together with Belinga forms part o f an emergingiron ore
         province extending through the Republic o f Cameroon, the Republic o f Congo, and
         Gabon. Productioni s scheduled to start in2011. Ironproductionwould require a
         500km longrailway to a deep sea mineralport inKribi, including approximately 200km
         through some o f the most intact primarygrowth forest inCameroon.
         Geovic Cameroon (GeoCam) holds exclusive rights to develop the Nkamouna cobalt-
         nickel laterite deposit next to Lomie and to the Dja Wildlife Reserve inSouth Eastern
         Cameroon. The GeoCam miningpermit covers the entire cobalt mineral province in
         southeastern Cameroon, perhaps the largest primary cobalt resource inthe world. The
         company plans to start cobalt andnickel production in2009.
         Cameroon also has extensive bauxite reserves at Minim-Martap andNgaouanda, located
         inthe remotenorthernparts o fthe country. Development o fthese deposits will require
         substantial infrastructure development, including upgrading the railway line to the
         aluminum smelter sites inSouthern Cameroon.
4.   Another keypiece o f Cameroon's accelerated development strategy i s the transformation o f
the Kribi area into an industrial hub, including Cameroon's maindeep seaport, with rail links to
miningsites.
5.   The investmentsunder considerationinthe energy andminingsectors could bringhuge
economic and social benefits, but also bring considerable environmental and social risks that
needproper mitigation measures and transparent planningand oversight processes. For example,

futuremininginvestments andthe industrial development o fKribiwill have largescale impacts
beyond their direct footprint, including impacts on watersheds and on regional development that
could cancel parts o f the expected benefits.

shown that there i s insufficient capacity within Cameroonto satisfactorily address environmental
andsocial issuesrelatedto a largeinfrastructurepro ect. Moreover, LomPangar has shownthat
lack o f compliance with international best practices regardingthe management o f environmental

participating ina project and delay its implementation. The scale of the issuesthat will arise
from investmentscurrentlyunder considerationinthe energy andminingsectors is at least
an order of magnitudegreater than the risks associatedwith the LomPangarHydropower
Project,or even the ChadCameroonPipeline.

The Chad-Cameroon Pipeline Project
7.   The Chad-Cameroon Pipeline Project (CCPP) was the first significant test o f Cameroon's
capacity to identify the potential environmental and social impacts o f a large infrastructure
project, define mitigation measures, and monitor their implementation. As a lender to Chad and
Cameroon, to fund their participation inthe pipeline, the Bank assessedthat the existing
regulatory and institutional framework for environmental issues, based on the 1996
Environmental Management law, was too weak, and that relying on this framework was too
considerable a risk. As a consequence, it was agreed with the Government o f Cameroon that the
CCPP's environmental and social risks would be managed outside o f the Ministryincharge o f
the environment. A PresidentialDecree from 1997 had established a Comiti dePilotage et de
Suivi des Pipeline (CPSP), hosted by the Socikti Nationale des Hydrocarbures (SNH), to
monitor the implementationo f the Chad CameroonPipeline Environmental Management Plan
(EMP) and o f any future oil or gas pipelines. Government entrusted the monitoring o fthe CCPP
Environmental Management Plan (EMP) to CPSP, and requested Bank support through the
CameroonPetroleumEnvironmentCapacityEnhancement(CAPECE) Project,which
started in2001.
8.   The CAPECE was successful insecuring an overall satisfactory level o f compliance by
COTCO (Cameroon Oil Transport Company) with the obligations spelled out inthe EMP for the
CCPP. It also contributed indeveloping the capacity o f staff inseveral ministries, by involving
them ina subsidiary manner inmonitoring activities. The key achievements o fthe CAPECE
include: (i)   a subset o f the draft implementationdecrees for the 1996 Environmental
Management Law, (ii)        a draft National Emergency Oil Spill Response Plan, (iii)study for     a
monitoring the protection o f the coast and the marine environment inthe context o f the
CAPECE, (iv) a Long-term Vision Strategy for Environmental and Social Management inthe

Current situation
12. The last 10years have beencharacterized by minimal collaboration between the different
national institutions involved inenvironmental management. Environmental debate was
concerned with delimitating spheres o f influence rather than the substance o f environmental
management. More specifically, the institutional framework established for the Chad-Cameroon
pipeline cannot be replicated to sectors other than the management of pipelines. The CAPECE
developed the skills o f selected individuals, but did not strengthen environmental institutions and


                                                    3

staff from technical ministries mobilizedbythe CAPECE tendedto report on their work directly
to the CPSP rather than to their own administrations, thus undermining institutional capacity
building. The Government o fCameroonhas indicated its intentionto correct this situationby
fully implementing the provisions o fthe 1996EnvironmentalManagement Law. It established a
Ministry o f Environment andNature Protection (MinEP) in2004 with a mandate to design,
coordinate and monitor Cameroon's environment policy, andthe core feature o f Cameroon's
environmental policy is the decision to enable this Ministryto fulfill its statutory mandate as the
country's environmental regulator.
13. MinEP's mandate i s very broad and extends beyond environmental regulation to include:
         Coordinating andmonitoring regional or international cooperation institutions involved
         inenvironmentalissues;

         ministries;
         Negotiating international agreements and conventions related to environmental
         protection and ensure their implementation.

Committee for the Environment (ICE), which devotes over 90 percent o f its time to
environmental regulatory issues, such as EAs, environmental audits, and EMPs. The Ministry
includes a Secre'tariatGe'ne'ral,anInspection Ge'ne'rale,and 5 departments:

D.Lessonslearnedandreflectedinthe projectdesign
42. The project draws key lessons from past experience inother countries endeavoring to
establish national level regulatory frameworks for environmental management:
         Nearly all African countries have an apex institutiontasked with ensuring the
         compliance o f large investmentswith national EA requirements. However,
         environmental and social issues associated with large investments are by nature
         crosscutting, and are thus o f direct concern to many other stakeholders. This
         multiplicity o f stakeholders is neither exceptional nor deplorable, but rather i s inherent
         to environmental management. Therefore, exclusive focus on the institution incharge
         o f EAs i s not conducive to successful management o f environmental and social issues.
         A fundamental design feature o fthe PReCESSE is to promote collaborationbetweenthe
         Ministryincharge ofEnvironment and other major stakeholders inenvironmental
         management, each according to its statutory mandate, byproviding these stakeholders
         with the support requiredto fulfill their respective mandates.
         Regulators should be institutionally independent from the bodies they regulate, since
         otherwise they are confronted with difficult to manage conflicting interests. The
         greatest number o f exceptions to this principle is inthe oil and gas sector, which has led
         to distinct and less open regulatory frameworks in several countries. Project design
         seeks to address this issue for the EnergySector, by drawing a clear distinction between
         the roles o fregulatory bodies and that o f investors.
      0  The implementationo f environmental regulations i s often subjected to highpolitical
         pressure, particularly for large investments with many externalities. Project design
         would address this issue bypromoting transparency inthe management o f
         environmental and social impacts through disclosure requirements and the constructive
         involvement o f CSOs, andby conducting studies that show the economic benefits o f
         compliance with international standards and procedures derived from: (i)     limitingcostly
         externalities, (ii)facilitating investments from reputable investors, and (ii)promoting
         good governance through greater accountability.



                                                  10

         Lack o f sustained funding for overseeing the EA process i s a very frequent major
         constraint, particularly ifno cost recovery mechanism i s put inplace, such as the
         collection o f fees for EA review and for monitoring EMP implementation, o fpollution
         permits, and o f fines for environmental infractions. The problem is most pronounced
         when the EA regulator is a Government Department, and usually less acute when it is
         established as a distinct agency or authority. There was a previous attempt to establish
         such an agency at the time o f CAPECE preparation, but the option was rejected. The
         establishment o f such an environmental agency inCameroon i s also one o f the options
         raised ina study made by Royal Haskoning under the CAPECE. It was agreed with
         GoC that changes inthe institutional framework would not be further discussed during
         project preparation and that the immediate focus would be on making the EA
         framework fully effective. Nonetheless, the task team will recommend to Government
         duringappraisal that the project funds a study on the sustainable financing o f
         environmental regulations that would present GoC with clear options at the time o f the
         mid-term review.
         The experience from the portfolio o fBank-funded Environmental Support Projects
         (ESP) inthe 1990sshows that linkingsupport to specific investments or sectors
         accelerate capacitybuildingthrough problem solving. This lesson has been
         incorporatedinto the project's design where the overarching indicator i s a measure o f
         environmental and social compliance inthe energy sector.

V. APPRAISAL SUMMARY

A. Economic and financial analyses
73. The project i s built around the implementationo fthe EAprocess inCameroon. There is a
dearth o f academic literature on the economic benefits o f implementing EA processes, and thus it
is not possible to quantify the benefits o f implementing an EA process, except on a case by case
basis.
74. The EAprocess i s an effort to anticipate measure andweigh the socioeconomic and
biophysical changes that may result from a project. By identifying, estimating and mitigating
project externalities and their costs, the EAprocess assists decision-makers inconsidering a
project's overall costs andbenefits, and ensuring that project benefits exceed overall project
costs. Furthermore, having a solid EA system inplace i s also good for Cameroon's reputation
and will make it more attractive to reputable investors.
75. The cost o fmanagingthe EAprocess itselfby Government is small relativeto the size o f
investments, particularly once enough capacity has beenestablished inthe institution incharge
o fmanaging the process, andcan be recoveredthrough reasonable review and monitoring fees.
However, the main costs associated with an EA process are borne by the investor, including the
cost o fpreparingthe EA, andthe cost o f implementing the associated EMP. There can also be
significant costs interms o f lost economic growth. A good EA process would take into account
all o f these elements and leadto decisions that result intangible economic benefits.

TheEconomy
1. Promotingrapidgrowth, creatingjobs, and acceleratingimprovementsingovernance.
Poverty inCameroonremains widespread with about 40 percent o f the country's more than 16
million people livingunder the poverty threshold o f about US$1 per day. The growth o f the
economy forecasted inthe PRSP did not materialize andthe country i s off track for meeting most
o f the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) and will have difficulty inreachingthem2.
Cameroon, however, is performing below potential, given its richnatural resource endowment,
relatively well trained labor force, bilingual population, and access to the sea. Stronger andmore
diversified growth including the development o f agriculture, services and manufacturing sectors
will require tackling the constraints to private investment. These constraints include corruption,
inefficient tax administration, limited access to and highcosts o f financing, and unreliable power
supply. The challenge for the country is therefore to promote rapid economic growth as an
engine for job creation and poverty alleviation.
2.    Improvement ingovernance including anticonuption measures i s necessary ifthe country
hopes to improve the business climate and enhance growth prospects. Although Cameroon has
made progress inthis area inthe past few years much remains to be done and corruption
represents perhaps the country's greatest development challenge3.
3.    Managingthe fiscal space. Two years ago, Cameroonreceived debt reliefunder two major
international initiatives4,clearing the way for a write-down o f its external debt from about 40
percent o f GDP in2005 to 5 percent o f GDP in2006. Cameroon i s now poisedto make faster
progress toward improving living conditions and reducing poverty. Cameroon should in
particular make use o f this breathing space created by debt reliefto get onto a higher growth
trajectory that would edge it closer to achieving the MDGs. Debt reliefhas opened up new
opportunities. Cameroon i s usingthe freed-up resources to increase priority spending, including
on health, education, agriculture, infrastructure development, and institutionbuilding. Higher
current spending, however, could undermine fiscal sustainability ifthe debt reliefi s not managed
properly. The quality o f public expenditures should also be improved andthe reduction o f
subsidies to public enterprises should be a priority. The resources freed-up could be redirected
towards education, health, and infrastructuredevelopment.

                          Annex 4: DetailedProjectDescription
 CAMEROON: Environmentaland Social CapacityBuildingfor the EnergySector



1. The purpose o fthe project is to support Government efforts to manage the
environmental and social externalities arising from large infrastructure investments in
Cameroon. This would be done byrequiring compliance o f these investments with
international bestpractices for the management o f environmental and social issues.
Beyond these compliance requirements, the explicit management o f externalities in a
transparent manner would also help ensure that large investments take into account the
broader development context.
2.   The instrument around which the project i s organizedi s the 2005 Environmental
Assessment Decree. This Decree andthe upstream 1996 Environment Law definekey
steps inthe EA process: (i) o f the Project, (ii)
                              scoping                    preparationo f the ToRs for the
EA, (iii)preparationo fthe EA, (iv) submissionof the EA, (v) review o f the EA, (vi)
approval o fthe EA, (vii) delivery o f associated permitsand licenses, (viii)
implementation andmonitoring o f compliance with the EMP, and (ix) certification o f
compliance.
3.   The EA Decree and the 1996 Environment Law designate MinEP as the national
environmental regulator, incharge o f overseeing environmental compliance in
Cameroon. They also implicitly assign subsidiary roles inimplementing the EA
regulations to other institutions with mandates that intersect MinEP's, e.g. forestry, land,
health, or water. Each o f these subsidiary regulators i s the custodian o f a specific set o f
rules, regulations andprocedures intheir own sector that affect environmental
management. Effective management o f environmental and social issues requires that
relevant subsidiary regulators be associated whenever a project raises issues that concern
them. For example, forestry mustbe involved ifa dam inundates a forest, or landsmust
be involved ifan infrastructure causes resettlement. The Inter-Ministerial Committee for
the Environment (ICE) is the structure where the MinEP and subsidiaryregulators can
coordinate their interventions. ICE membership i s limited, but it can invite nonmembers
as needed to its technical discussions.
4.   The EA decree also implicitly defines roles and responsibilities for investment
promoters and operators that are also complementary to the role o f MinEP and subsidiary
environmental regulators. Thus, the promoter i s responsible for: (i)  preparing ToRs for
the EA o fhis investment and gettingthem reviewed and approved by MinEP, (ii)          for
preparing the EA, audit or EMP and getting it reviewed and approved by MinEP, and (iii)
for implementing the EMP and reporting on its implementationto MinEP. Several
ministries have created environmental cells to follow up on the investments they oversee,
most particularly the MinEE and the Ministryo f Public Works, and it i s important that
these cells also be involved inICE.
5.   The 1996 Law defines a set o f hndamentalprinciples for environmental
management, including the Pollute and Pay Principle (PPP), and the Principle o f
Participation. According to PPP, "charges resultingfrom measures aimed at preventing,
reducing and fighting against pollution and the rehabilitation o fpolluted areas shall be


                                                                                              33

borne by the polluter." Thus PPP shifts the responsibility o f dealing with pollution from
governments to the entities producing it. Its reference i s critical because PPPpromotes
the integrationo fthe environment into the economic sphere through the use o fprice
signals and the use o f economic instrumentssuch as pollution charges and permits.
6.    The project generalizes the PPPprinciple to all types o fimpacts, Le. promoters,
investors and operators should be held accountable for all social and environmental
impacts inexcess o f the baseline at the time o fproject inception, and the cost o f
mitigation measures requiredto address these impacts should be borne by the promoter,
investor or operator. The Government i s responsible for liabilities below the baselines;
thus, ensuring that appropriate baselines are availableinEMPs is critical to the effective
implementation o fPPP. For example, a promoter might be responsible for the increase in
infectious diseases arising from an infrastructureproject, but not for the initial level o f
disease. However, promoters should not be heldresponsible for activities normally done
byGovernment. Thus, apromoter mightberesponsible for the implementationo fa
resettlement plan, but not for the development o f the cadastral framework requiredto
implement this plan. The provisions o f the EMP for a specific investment must clearly
define: (i) the respective responsibilities o fthe promoter and o f the environmental
regulators, and (ii) the liabilities and obligations o fthe promoter, andthe residual
liabilities and obligations o f the Government o f Cameroon.

                              Annex 9: Economicand FinancialAnalysis
     CAMEROON: EnvironmentalandSocialCapacityBuildingfor the EnergySector

1. Cameroonhas started on the pathtowards environmental management, buthas yet to make
some o f the critical choices regarding environmental standards andprocedures. The PReCESSE
will support the Government of Cameroon inmaking these choices, while underlining the
benefits o f environmental regulation incontrolling externalities and increasingthe broadbenefits
derived from investments, without discouraging beneficial investments through redtape and
economically unsound regulations.
2.   Support by the PReCESSE includes the full implementation o f its nascent EA system, and
the development standards andprocedures for environmental management.
3.   The EA system i s the entrypoint o f investments into the country's regulatory system. It i s
essentially a governance process for identifying externalities, and allocatingresponsibility for
their management. The EA system does not initself set environmental standards, but rather
relies on whatever standards are inforce. Infact, a primary concern o f the project is to ensure
good management o f the EAprocess accordingto service standardsinterms o f responsiveness
andpublic consultation, thus improving the enabling environment for investors.
4.   There i s a dearth o f literature regarding the economic benefits o f EA systems. For example,
does the existence o f an EA system benefit or inhibit growth? A summary economic analysis
specific to Cameroon's EA system could start by considering its costs relative to its benefits.
5.   Costs are shared by between the regulator and the promoter/investor/operator.

      0  Regulator costs are borne by the MinEP and subsidiary regulatory institutions that
         participate inthe management o fthe EA process and include staff costs and operating
         costs. Regulator costs are currently less thanUS$1million. The bulk o f the project will
         cover the costs associatedwith upscaling the existing EA systemto all large
         investments, and ensuringmonitoring o f compliance bypromoters/investors/operators
         with their EMP commitments.
      0  Investor costs are mainly the cost o fpreparing EAs and EMPs andreview costs.
         ENEMPpreparationcosts for largeprojects usually represent no more than 1-2percent
         o f overall project costs. Review and monitoring costs per project are capped at
         approximately US$lO,OOO per project and are paid directly to the Environment Fund,
         which i s inconsequential. These fees serve to fund the cost o freviewsby the Inter-
         Ministerial Committee on the Environment.
6.   The existence o f a responsive, fair and stable EA process is a major contribution to good
governance, which i s itselfa key element o f economic growth. A major benefits o f the EA
system i s greater transparency andpublic accountability inthe management o fproject
externalities. The EA process i s most often the only mechanism that allows for inputs from
concerned stakeholders, and the additional information brought out duringthe EAprocess can
lead to improvements inthe project's design and defuse potential social tensions, while not
negatively affecting the project's rate o freturn.

7.   The EA itself can serve as a conduit for economic analysis as mentioned by John Dixon and
Stefan0 Pagiola (April 1998)inthe context o fthe Bank's safeguard policies. Dixonand Pagiola
outline several approaches that can be followed for valuing environmental impacts.
8.   Incontrast, there arehugeeconomic issueslinkedto the choice ofstandards andprocedures
for regulating the environment, and an abundant literature has been written on about the
economic benefits andcosts o f environmental management. The choice o f standards and
procedures is a major political decision that Cameroon i s now confronting. Ina review o f the
economics o f environmental regulation, William A. Brock (2000) recommends avoiding one size
fits all regulation instruments infavor o f tiered and flexible solutions, that combine elements
from measures o f willingness-to-pay (cost-benefit) and measures o f willingness-to-vote. In
addition, Brock suggests that "since environmental regulationproblemsface enormous

Purpose of thisPaper
1. The World Bank has learned from worldwide experience that technically well-designed
operations intendedto buildenvironmental and social management capacity can fall short o f
their intended outcomes when working arrangements are not well suited to the needs and
circumstances o fthe client, the consultants, and conditions inthe country. Cameroon i s facing
development decisions inthe energy sector that will by themselves have far-reaching
consequences for the natural and social environments, and the development that will be enabled
inother sectors bythe availability o fnew energy at new locationswill have additional impacts.
PReCESSE was designed to enhance the probability that as many o fthose consequences as
possible will be positive, while strengthening the number and capacity o f stakeholders in
environmental and social assessments, mainly inthe energy sector, as well as ensuring
ownership o f the project's management system. This paper is intended, first o f all, as a
framework for decisions on the optimal working arrangements for PReCESSE. Itbeginsby
identifying several common pitfalls and then describes alternative arrangements to avoid them.
These alternative arrangements must be taken into account by the client and the Bank, while
allowing reframingissues ina consensual manner,to optimize results duringproject
implementation. Its other objective is to examine ways inwhich the accomplishments o f
PReCESSE can be applied indevelopment planningand decision makingas they become
available, without waiting for the end o f the project. As choices are made, the paper can be
amended to become a document for all stakeholders that describe the working arrangements.

ThePitfalls
2.   Deliverablesdo not meetthe client'sneeds. Eventhe most carefully written terms o f
reference may not prevent disappointment inthe usefulness o f the materials and services the
consultant delivers. One source o f difficulty is terms o freference based on assumptions about
the client's needs that turn out to be inappropriate; there may have beeninsufficient consultation
with prospective users, or perhaps the users themselves didnot or couldnot fully understand
their needs untilsome months into the project. The consultant may not have the right
experience, or its personnelmay not have the right style for assisting the client. At the end o f a
similarproject inIndonesia, the client told the Bank "We had a large team working in their
offices andproducing numerous guidelines and manuals, but what we really needed was afew
senior people sitting in our offices and helping us on a daily basis to improve the way we were
carrying out our work." Furthermore, the results o f specific cases o f capacity buildingtargeting
MinEP and other Government departments have not met expectations, because the consultants
didnot understandMinEP's statutory mandate andprojects. As a result, MinEPsenior staffs
have had to compensate for the deficiencies o fthese consultants and train their colleagues.
3.   Consultantsandclient's expectationsandneedsare divergent. The arrival o fa new
team o f consultants ina government office imposes demands ranging from basic questions -how
does the telephone system work, for example - to quick decisions necessary to get work started.
Inanagency that is under-staffedandover-worked, what was advertised as the answer to their
problems becomes itself a new set o fproblems. From the consultant's perspective, not being

                                                                                                  68

able to obtain timely decisions on work plansand schedules or comments on draft documents i s a
source o f fixstration. Inaddition, consultants like to be paid ina timely manner when they reach
payment milestones. Client agencies do not necessarily accord the same priority to processing
consultant payments. Inaddition, when there i s a large number o f consulting contracts, as there
i s likely to be inPReCESSE, the workload o f administering those contracts can overwhelm the
procurement capacity o f the agencies. The client-consultant relationship and the work both
suffer. Lastly, client agencies have on occasions been dissatisfied with the results provided by
consultants that had less technical expertise than itself.
4.    The process for review,feedback,revisionand approvalof consultantproductswas
notdefinedin advance. Client stafffinds itself overwhelmed with the number and size o f
outputs, and consultants struggle with delays and uncertainties, including not knowing when a
product is finished and accepted.
5.    Transparencyandconsultationare neglected. Nearly everyone working in
environmental management realizes the importance o f a participatory approach. However, inthe
press o fbusiness, it is easy to forget or to fail to keep stakeholders informed o fprogress and to
allow sufficient time for disclosure o f draft documents and consultations. Inthis context,
stakeholders include: technical staff inthe agency concerned that will have to work with the
product; representatives o f the affectedindustry inthe case o f sectoral guidelines or standards;
environmental organizations; academic institutions research centers, technical implementing
agencies inthe relevant fields, as well as professional associations or other representatives o f the
consulting professions that will help private andpublic clients implement new procedures,
guidelines and standards.
6.    Senior managementis notadequatelyengaged in the capacity-buildingeffort. Most o f
the consultant-client interactioninevitably occurs at the technical or middle-management levels.
Senior management needs to be engaged, to provide direction and make decisions at critical
junctures, to accept and implement (or direct the implementation of) the outputs o f capacity-
building, andto "champion" the entireprocess. Without this, there is substantial risk that the
newcapacity will not make a difference inhow decisions are made anddevelopment projects are
managed - that the time and expenditure will be wasted.
7.    Decision-makingis "business as usual" as capacity-buildingproceeds. This is not a
problem when, for example, standards and guidelines exist and are only being updated, and the
development proposals have localized and straightforward impacts. However, when major
decisions that will affect the overall direction and nature o f development on a regional scale and
for years to come are on the agenda, as is the case inCameroon's energy sector, and when
political considerations compete with environmental and social science inthe decision process,
capacity-building efforts can be undermined. Conversely, there i s an opportunity for mutual
reinforcement: decisions can be improved ifthe decision-makers apply interimoutputs from the
capacity-building and conform to the principles it i s based on, and enthusiasm and support for
the capacity-building work will be augmented.
