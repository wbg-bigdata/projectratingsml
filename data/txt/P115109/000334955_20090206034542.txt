I. STRATEGICCONTEXTANDRATIONALE

A. Countryandsector issues

CountryBackground
1. Armenia has strong economic fundamentals going into the global financial crisis. Armenia
has hadthe highest policy reform (CPIA) ranking of all IDA countries for the past several years.
As outlined in their recent PRSP (discussed at the Board inNovember 2008), GDP growth has
averaged above 10 percent per year and poverty has fallen from 55 to 26 percent over the past
decade. Fiscal deficits and public debt remain quite modest in relation to GDP - among the
lowest in the CIS. Further, all projects in the current portfolio are rated satisfactory and the
FY08 disbursement rate was 34 percent. Notwithstanding these strong fundamentals, Armenia
faces several critical vulnerabilities stemming from the global economic and financial crisis,
which are detailedbelow.

2. Strong economic fundamentals have shielded the economy from the initial effects of the
global output slowdown and financial crisis, but economic vulnerabilities have been accentuated
by the crisis and the situation is deteriorating. Though Armenia entered the crisis from a
relatively robust position, the crisis may affect the country through three main channels: labor
markets (through a decline in remittances), product markets (through a decline in trade), and
capital markets (through a decrease in FDI and bank borrowing). Consequently, Armenia will
require significant support from development partners to help withstand the economic shocks in
a manner that does not undermine its development agenda and considerable gains in poverty
reduction.

3. GDP growth is likely, at best, to be negligible in 2009.           Given recent national and
international data, GDP growth is currently projected at zero in 2009, and further contraction
cannotbe ruledout, depending on the pace of global recovery. The real estate sector has already
begun to experience a severe contraction, as has metallurgy, and signals are emerging that other
sectors are also under stress. Unemployment rose sharply in the last quarter of 2008, as
numerous gold and molybdenum mines closed. Agricultural growth also slowed substantially
toward the endof2008. Inflationis estimatedto have declined to 5.2 percent.

4. Exports will be affected by sharply declining prices of non-ferrous metals coupled with
plummeting external demand. Exports are estimated to have declined by about 6.3 percent in
2008 (in USD terms) and are projected to decline further in 2009. The current account deficit
widened substantially from 6.4 percent of GDP in2007 to 13 percent in2008, and it is projected
to worsen further in 2009. Due to deterioration in both the underlying capital and current
accounts, the overall external financing gap has increasedsignificantly.

5. The global financial crisis poses serious challenges for maintaining the robust economic
growth and poverty reduction that Armenia enjoyed for much of the past decade.            Since
economic growth i s generally pro-poor, the poor and vulnerable may be disproportionately
affected by an economic downturn. Poverty simulations show that for every percentage point
reduction inthe GDP growth rate, national poverty incidence increasesby 0.6 percentagepoints.

6. The crisis, which is coming at the heels o f the food and fuel price increases in 2007 and the
early part of 2008, could have a wide ranging impact on the poor and vulnerable through its
effects on employment and remittances. The loss o f income and employment opportunities
would lead to declining current consumption and ability to smooth consumption and further
depletion of productive assets. The extreme poor are likely to be hit the hardest. For an income
decline equivalent to 10 percent o f the food poverty line, the proportion of those whose
consumption would bebelow the food poverty line would double from 3.8 percent to 7.7 percent
of the population. The increase in the depth and severity of poverty could be even larger,
pushing many poor households to the edge of survival.

7. A disproportionately large share of Armenian migrants are engaged in the Russian
construction sector. Remittances from abroad, a significant contributor to consumption and
investment for Armenia, have been slowing in the forth quarter of 2008 and will likely contract
further in2009 inlight ofthe close economic and investment linkages with Russia. Remittances,
at US$ 1.6 billion estimatedin2008, accountedfor nearly20 percent of GDP and are expectedto
decline sharply. FDIi s also expected to drop significantly in 2009 to 4.9 percent of GDP, down
from 7.6 percent in2007.

Background of theFinancialSector

8. Following the breakup of the Soviet Unionand the subsequent liberalization o f the economic
environment, the number ofbanks increased from 6 in 1991to 74 banksby the end of 1993. The
authorities initiated a program of bank reform and consolidation in 1994. Between 1994 and
1996, more than thirty banking licenses were withdrawn. Today the banking system comprises
22 relatively well capitalized banks. At end-2008, the total capitalization of the banking system
was about 27 percent and the share of total assets and total loans was 28.1 percent and 16.9
percent of GDP, respectively. However, totalbanking sector assets to GDP is substantially below
the CIS' average ofover 50 percentin2007.

9. Overall, the financial system i s small and bank dominated and, as such, i s not yet a major
contributor to economic growth. Banks' funding sources are mostly from deposits, external
financing and own capital. Deposit costs range from 6 - 11 percent, whilst lending rates vary
from 16 - 24 percent, depending upon the type of credit. The non-bank financial accounts for
only some three percent o f total financial system assets; stock market capitalization is equivalent
to less than one percent o f GDP. The high cost of loans averaging about 20 percent reflects

financial intermediation by domestic institutions.

progress putting into practice the recommendations of the 2000 FSAP. Furthermore, successful
design and implementation of economic policies and structural reforms have resulted inpositive

11. To ensure further development o f the financial system while maintaining financial stability,

into 57 percent growth in credit in 2008, mostly in consumer, corporate and mortgage lending.
However, bank lending slowed inthe forth quarter, as didthe growth indeposits.

13. To meet the recent boom in the credit demand, Armenian banks have sharply increased
borrowings from external sources beginning in 2007 (figure 1) though much i s from foreign
parent banks. The loan to deposit ratio i s about 135%, above the median level of comparable
countries, increasing vulnerabilities to re-financing risk. The proposed AFSME facility i s
designed to mitigate such re-financing risks with a stable source of longer term funding.

14. The financial crisis and the resulting global liquidity squeeze have effectively blocked
external borrowing for banks in many emerging markets, including Armenia. Foreign banks
operating in Armenia (which total about 66 percent o f bank capitalization at end-2007) may not
continue funding their subsidiaries at prior levels. Many internationally active banks are in the
process of re-buildingcapital and liquidity in their home markets. Going forward, foreign banks
may cut their emerging market exposure, call upon subsidiaries for dividend payments, reduce
credit lines, or at worst, prematurely call in existing credit lines. This loss o f access to funds
would reduce the ability o f Armenia to sustain growth. As a result, domestic banks may become
even more reluctant to lend to SMEs.

TheSME Sector in Armenia

15. The total number of registered SMEs2was more than 80,000 at end-2007, which represents
about 98 percent of total registered companies. About half of all registered SMEs are inYerevan.
The contribution of SMEs to creation of new jobs was significant, as nearly 16,000 new SMEs
were registered in 2007, supporting the creation of about 23,000 newjobs, the majority of which
were outside Yerevan.

16. Over the past several years, the GOAhas implemented an SME support program aimed at
stimulating development of the SME sector through the Ministry of Economy's SME
Development National Center3 (SMEDNC) network. The program provides a variety of both
technical and financial assistance to SMEs in all 10 regions of Armenia. More than AMD 2
billion has been allocated for the implementation of SME programs from 2002 - 2008. Nearly
20,000 start-up and operating SMEs have benefited from the state support through the above
schemes.

17. The share o f the SME sector in GDP is estimated at nearly 42% in 2008 (figure 2) compared
to less than 35% in 2002 The SME share in the country's total exports has also increased over
the past several years and in the beginning of 2008 amounted to about 17 percent compared to
14.3 percent in2003. According to the SMEDNC, in2007 SMEshave beenmost active intrade,
agriculture and food processingandthe construction sectors.

B. Rationale for Bankinvolvement

19. The rationale for advancing this proposed wholesale credit line operation to SMEs centers on
strengtheningaccess to finance and improving the resiliency of Armenia's private and financial
sectors inthe face o f the impacts of the global financial crisis on banks and remittances, the two
main sources of financing for Armenian SMEs.

20. As banks' rollover or re-financing risks have been substantially magnifiedinthe past months,
banks inboth developed and emerging markets have begun to conserve liquidity, inter alia, to
enable them to set aside larger loan loss provisions and to better prepare for the repayment o f
external obligations. Inaddition, reduced remittance flows to SMEs are likely to leave them with
less informal sources o f funds to invest. Availability of credit will therefore be key in providing
the neededcushion for investmentandjob creation inthe comingyear.

21. In this context, credit in 2009 to Armenian business, and in particular to SMEs, will most
likely be limited by the following factors. First, although Armenian banks are now relatively
liquid, with high capital adequacy ratios, going forward they face some importantuncertainties
ingrowing their lendingportfolio, particularlyinlocalcurrency. As a consequence ofthe global
situation and related developments inArmenia, inrecent months the growth of deposits has been
slower and there has been a trend towards dollarization.                    Secund, banks in Armenia, as
elsewhere, may tighten credit conditions, especially for SME borrowers, who traditionally can
offer the least collateral to secure a loan. Constrained bank resources inlocal currency will likely
exacerbate this tightening of credit conditions for SMEs, most of which are not export-oriented
and, therefore, lack the capacity to hedge borrowing in foreign currency.                  Third, migrant
remittances, a significant contributor to consumption and investment for Armenia, are likely to
slow down as early as in the final quarter of 2009. As a large portion o f Armenian migrants are
engaged in the Russian construction sector, a significant share of such remittances are expected
to be vulnerable to the financial crisis.

C. Higher level objectives to which the project contributes




                                                  12

22. The Sustainable Development Program (SDP), approvedby the government in October 2008,
i s Armenia's second Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP-2), which lays out a second
generation economic growth and diversification strategy aimed at deepening competition and
fostering private sector development. The SDP has three key goals covering the period 2009-
2021: (i)  reducing poverty, including the elimination of extreme poverty; (ii)  ensuring human
development; and (iii)    broadening economic growth and accelerating development o f lagging
regions.

23. The proposed AFSME credit line facility i s designed to address the third goal by supporting
SME development through a difficult period when these businesses are otherwise likely to face
increasing difficulties of raising finance through the banking system and/or via remittances. By
making mediumterm finance available through local banks, the project will help to prevent job
losses in the SME sector, as well as encourage some job expansion and growth. Continuing and
enhancing financial support to SMEs i s essential for them to be able to invest intechnology and
capacity, thereby raising output and productivity. Evidence4 suggests that a well-functioning
financial system is beneficial to the poor, andthe proposedproject is designedwith this inmind.

24. The most recent Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) for Armenia covered FY05 to FY08 and
was completed successfully, and anew Country Partnership Strategy (CPS) is under preparation.
While the CAS did aim to foster pro-poor growth, it didnot specifically includesupport for SME
credit. Inview of recent global developments, however, assisting Armenia mitigate the impacts
of the economic crisis on its growth and development agenda will be an important objective of
the forthcoming CPS, andthe proposedproject i s an important component of that effort.


11.     PROJECT DESCRIPTION

A. LendingInstrument

25. The proposed lending instrument is a Financial Intermediary Loan (FIL), providing IBRD
funds to the CentralBank ofArmenia, with a guarantee from the RepublicofArmenia. The CBA
will in turn enter into subsidiary loan agreements with eligible Participating Financial
Intermediaries (PFIs). The FILwill be a Variable Spread Loan (VSL) inU S Dollars.

26. The AFSME project will be implementedthrough the CBA's existing PIU, whose principal
tasks will include: (i)screening and qualifying PFIs; (ii)disbursing project funds to PFIs for on-
lending to SMEs in accordance with policies and procedures set out in an agreed Operational
Manual; and (iii)   monitoring the PFIs' performance under the scheme. The CBA, through the
PIU, will coordinate closely with those donors already active in SME finance and/or capacity
building, namely the World Bank's RESCAD project, the IFC, KfW,EBRDandUSAID.

27. The eligibility criteria for PFIs' creditworthiness and operating systems have been
determined by the Bank in close collaboration with the CBA as Armenia's bank supervisory
authority (see Appendix 4.2). Credit extended by the CBA to the PFIs will be on market terms,
as will sub-loans to the final SME sub-borrowers. The size of the project loan is based on

estimates of the financing needs of the SME sector during 2009-10 and on the analysis of likely
decreases inremittancesand availability ofnormalbank credit for SMEs during this period.

B. Project development objective and key indicators

28. The project's development objective is to maintain or increase Armenian small and medium
enterprises' access to medium-term finance. The project will positively impact SMEs and
participating banks both during and beyond the implementation period (2009-2011). As a
revolving facility with an initial five year maturity, PFIs may recycle loans to additional SMEs
before repayment to the CBA. This will enable PFIs to finance a large number of sub-loans
through the life of the AFSME facility.

29. Intermediate outcome indicators (see Annex 3 for more details) will measure the amount of
medium term financing made available to SMEs and the number of SMEs receiving medium
term financing, tracking those which are already clients of the banking system and those which
are first time borrowers, as well as the geographic and sectoral distribution of lending. Credit
access, turnover growth and employment outcomes generated by supported SMEs would be
measuredthroughout the project to offer lessons which may be helpfulin designing future crisis
response schemes for SMEs, inArmenia and elsewhere.

C. Project Description

30. This is a single component operation, which will provide wholesale funding to qualified
banks5 for on-lending to SMEs. The CBA will enter into Subsidiary Loan Agreements with
participating banks (PFIs). There are no geographic limitations, and PFIs will on-lend to SMEs
throughout Armenia. See Appendix 4.3 for a description of on-lending arrangements.

3 1. PFIs will include both local and foreign owned banks that meet specific eligibility criteria as
laid out in Appendix 4.2. The initial ceiling on sub-loans is set at US$150,000 in the project
Operational Manual; this will be reviewed periodically and any changes agreed with the Bank.
The Operational Manualalso provides a `negative list' of activities which will not be eligible for
financing under the facility.

32. Credit and foreign exchange risks. All credit risks will be borne by the originating PFI. As
most SMEs' income and expenses are denominated inArmenian Dram (AMD), the CBA expects
that most sub-loans will be in local currency. However, PFIs may choose to lend to sub-
borrowers in foreign exchange, in the event SMEs request this and have a verifiable source o f
foreign exchange earnings.

33. The foreign exchange risks derived from the CBA's borrowing in USD and lending in AMD
will be substantially mitigated through a variable rate feature of the PFI loans, and funded
through a spread above World Bank loan rates and related commitment fees to be derived from

D. Lessonslearnedandreflectedinthe projectdesign

34. The project reflects lessons learned from recent regional analytic work on SME development.
Specific support to SMEs i s justified by the fact that SMEs face market failures that need to be
addressed to avoid increasing the gap between them and larger firms. SMEs: (a) are relatively
more affected by business environment constraints (in particular, regulation and transaction
burdens) than are larger firms; (b) have less access to credit (finance constraints); and (c) have
less access to information, advisory services, technology and innovation (knowledge
 constraint^).^ Given the current global crisis, is reasonable to expect that finance constraints in
                                                      it
particular will be heightened. Without proactive support during such a period, real sector
economic activity by SMEs would likely contract

35. Taking these lessons into account, the project proposes to help mitigate access to finance
constraints by providing a credit line specifically targeting SMEs. The Government i s
undertakingcomplementary initiatives through its SMEDNC to reduce business environment and
knowledge constraints (partly in collaboration with the World Bank-see                   Section 3 on World
Bank complementary operations), ensuringthat key constraints facing SMEs will be addressed.

36. The project has also benefited from lessons learnedfrom credit line financing throughout the
world (e.g., India, China, Ukraine, Bosnia and Turkey) regarding attention to sub-borrower
accountability and management capacity, the importance of clearly defined and transparent
indicators for monitoring both performance o f financial intermediaries, overall project impact,
and the need for flexibility in the design o f operating arrangements to facilitate adjustment to
reflect changing circumstances on the ground. Specifically, (i) PIU has been assigned clear
                                                                          the
responsibilities for project implementation; (ii)        the capacities of the qualified banks (or PFIs) has
been independently evaluated and the banks' determined to be able to implement the operation
(see Annex 6); (iii)   the credit line facilities have been available for both investment as well as
working capital financing, thus increasing flexibility; (iv) the team has defined clear project
indicators (see section B.2); (v) the pricing of SME loans has been left to the PFIs, based on their
assessment o f on-lending risks; and (vi) a majority o f banks in country have expressed strong
demandfor medium term credit*. Inaddition, the success o f the credit lines have been achieved
through comprehensive project supervision and monitoring throughout the life ofthe project.

37. Finally, after in-depth discussions with those responsible for implementing other past or
ongoing credit line schemes in Armenia itself (including an IDA renewable energy project, IDA
rural development project, and the CBA PIU administering KfW credit lines, the task team
incorporated several additional features concerning environmental and other safeguards
screening, a clear negative list, and the requirement that all loans be initially funded from the
PFI's own resources, which would then seek financing through the PIU (to avoid PFIs borrowing
from the CBA without on-lendingfully to SMEs).

 E. Alternativesconsideredandreasonsfor rejection

 38. Alternatives considered included: (i)    the possibility of designing a broader SME operation to
 include initiatives to remove business environment and knowledge constraints that SMEs
 currently face, as well as technical assistance to improve more Armenia's financial information
 infrastructure; and (ii)the inclusion o f a larger number ofparticipating banks.

 39. The first alternative was discarded, not because the objectives are unimportant, but because
 other activities are underway to address capacity issues of SMEs and financial information
 infrastructure. Specifically, the Ministryo f Economy's SMEDNC is focusing on capacity issues.
 Regarding participating banks, it was decided that clear and transparent due diligence criteria be
 applied rigorously, with the result that 8 o f the 22 banksinArmenia will be eligibleto participate
 from the start of the project. At the same time, the qualification of banks will be an ongoing
 process and it is conceivable that some additional banks may meet eligibility criteria to

 confirmation from the CBA and a bilateral donor that it would provide SME underwriting
 training to banks.

framework, including big economic growth, market oriented policies, and a strong financial
regulatory regime.              Nevertheless, given the global financial crisis, the external economic
environment for developing countries has deteriorated markedly in 2008.                        Going forward,
foreign banks may cut their emerging market exposure, call upon subsidiaries for dividend
payments, reduce credit lines, and/or call in existing credit lines. This loss o f access to funds

structural changes that have been undertaken by the government, if supported by external
assistance, will contribute to andmay even ensure continued highrates o f economic growth.

 participate upon the successful conclusion o f a time-bound corrective action plan, acceptable to
 the C B A and the World Bank.

E. Social

71. The proposedproject will be socially beneficial. By increasing access to finance and growth

72. No social safeguards are triggered by the project. No activities are planned indisputedareas.
The project will not finance any investments implying either the involuntary acquisition of land

73. Furthermore, SMEs will be eligible to participate inthe project underthe condition they have

project Operating Manual for use by the PFIs and PIU.

responsibilities in carrying out the environmental work in the course of the project

Environmental Assessment and the Armenian environmental legislation.

                      Annex 1: Country and Sector or ProgramBackground
               ARMENIA: Access to Financefor SmallandMediumEnterprises

1. The financial system o f Armenia remains small and dominated through 22 licensed banks,
with total banking assets about 28 percent o f estimated GDP at end-2008. The growth of the
banking sector accelerated since early 2007, fueled by growing deposit base and foreign
borrowing of the banks that increased the ability o f the banks to provide credits. It is expected
that credit to GDP ratio will reach around 17 percent by end 2008. This expansion is largely
driven by strong growth o f consumer, corporate andmortgage credits.

2. The country heavily relies on private transfers and remittances as a source o f capital for
development. Total private transfers recorded inthe current account were equal to 17 percent o f
GDP in2007. More than 75 percent oftotal remittances to Armenia originate inRussia. In2004-
2008 there was very strong relationship between USD denominated GDP in Russia and the flow
of remittances to Armenia. As the growth rate inRussia will likely slow down in 2009, Armenia
may experience major slowdown in remittances compared to 2004-08 when the average annual
growth rate o fremittances were above 30 percent.

3. However, remittances to Armenia may decline in absolute terms in 2009 (figure 4) because
relatively small-scale private investments in Armenia's real estate often classified as remittances
may disappear if housing market continues to deteriorate. According to various estimates, in
recent years such flows comprised between 25-40 percent o f private transfers (from 4 to 7
percent o f GDP). Investments in real estate by Diaspora Armenians are perhaps the main factor
behind very large investments in housing sector (around 14 percent of GDP) and boom in
construction inrecent years. Declining real estate prices are likely to reverse this trend and affect
the growth rate.

1. The overall objective of the project i s to support the development of Armenia's small and

corruption, the environment for conducting public procurement inArmenia was one of high risk

2. The 2005 Country Financial Accountability Assessment (CFAA) report concluded that the
overall fiduciary risk22in Armenia is significant. The key reasons are: (i)               inadequate capacity of
core control and supervisory agencies performing the audits within the public sector; (ii)
although most of the basic laws are in place with respect to various entities' (private sector and
public enterprises, including state non-commercial organizations) financial reporting, the
compliance remains a problem and authorities need to improve the quality o f auditing,
monitoring and supervision. The PEFA assessment report published in October 2008 also
demonstrates that several critical Public Financial Management (PFM) elements including
internal controls, internal and external audit, and financial reporting are still weak.

3. The project will be implemented by the unit within the CBA with reliance on the CBA
systems regarding operating costs (not financed under the project) and Designated account
maintenance. No other country system FM elements are planned to be used under the project.

opinions have been always issued. As the banking arrangements with the CBA under the
ongoing KfW on-lending schemes havebeen found satisfactory, they will remain mainly inplace
during AFSME project implementation. Thus, the fiduciary risk of the stand-alone financial
management arrangements for this Bank-financed investment project in Armenia is considered
moderate.

                                Annex 10: SafeguardPolicyIssues
              ARMENIA: Access to Financefor SmallandMediumEnterprises


1. The project is designed to develop small and medium enterprises through the provision o f
finance. The likelihood of encountering involuntary resettlement cases was carefully explored
and discussed during the project design. It was agreed that the project will support only private
sector SMEs.

2. Furthermore, SMEs will be eligible to participate inthe project under the condition they have
not acquired and/or would not acquire land for activities which involve officially supported
expropriation or who would use proceeds to invest in an enterprise which would require the
involuntary displacement o f existing occupants or economic users of any plot o f land, regardless
of its current ownership. With these restrictions inplace, the project does not trigger OP/BP 4.12
Involuntary Resettlement.

3. The project has been classified as Category FI (Financial Intermediary). This classification is
based on the nature o f this operation, which i s that specific investments to be financed during its
implementation are not known by the time o f the project appraisal. Sub-loan applications to be
financed under the project will be received on a regular basis once the project is under
implementation.

4. OP/BP 4.01 Environmental Assessment i s triggered by the project. As the project will
support small and medium enterprises, it i s not expected that any proposed activities will cause
multiple, significant, and irreversible adverse environmental andor health impacts. Despitea low
likelihood o f occurrence, sub-projects which would qualify for Category A for environmental
assessment purposes are excludedfrom the activities eligible for the project support.

properly categorized and respective environmental review is carried out for them.

o f all agencies involved in it, is described in the environmental framework document and
included inthe Operations Manual o f the project.

applicants, approving them, and carrying out environmental monitoring in the course o f sub-
project implementation. Approval of high risk Category B sub-projects (B+) will require prior
