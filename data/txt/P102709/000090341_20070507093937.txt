                                      I. INTRODUCTION

1.     This document constitutes the Program Document for a proposed Third Programmatic
Support for Institution Building (PSIB 111) Grant to the Islamic Republic o f Afghanistan o f SDR
52.9 million (US$SO million equivalent).             The PSIB series i s aimed at supporting the
implementation o f the Government o f Afghanistan's (GOA)medium-term development strategy
withinthe context o f a satisfactory macroeconomic framework. GOA'Sstrategy is laid out inthe
Interim Afghanistan National Development Strategy (I-ANDs), which was presented to the
international community at the London Conference in January 2006. The PSIBs are one-tranche
operations based on prior actions and provide rolling support to the Government's development
strategy. The series was initiated in 2004 (PSIB I,     US$80 million credit in July 2004, and PSIB
11, US$80 million grant in December 2005). Each operation specifies a set o f triggers which,
when met, provide the basis for proceeding with the next operation. Progress against the triggers
outlined in the Program Document for PSIB 11has been satisfactory and the proposed operation
buildson these achievements. The country assistance strategy inwhich this operation is anchored
i s outlined inthe Interim Strategy Note (ISN) o f the World Bank Group for Afghanistan (IDA/R-
2006-0073) discussed at the Board inM a y 2006.

2.     The overall objective o f this operation i s to deepen and sustain the reforms underway inthe
areas o f public administration and fiscal management. The success o f these reforms i s essential
for building an effective and accountable state and i s critical for successful poverty reduction.
The PSIBs are components o f an overall package o f operations that aim at buildingthe financial
management o f the state, raising fiduciary standards, and supporting Public Administration
Reform (PAR). A series o f investment and technical support operations constitute the other
critical components o f support to these reforms.               By strengthening institutions, the
implementation o f these reforms will inturn facilitate more efficient allocation of fiscal resources
to priority activities inthe areas o f human development and private sector development.

3.     The proposed operation is the last in a series o f three. This series focused on buildingthe
core institutions o f budget management and public administration. As the Government finalizes
its Afghanistan National Development Strategy (ANDs, expected by mid-2008), its priorities are
shiftingtoward delivering services throughout the Government. The PSIBprogram i s expected to
transition in support o f this shift. Buildingon the achevements supportedby the first series, the
new series would support the ANDs, in particular to extend reforms from the center to line
ministriesand provinces, and strengthen accountability. Inorder to providepredictability to GOA
and ensure continuity in the dialogue on reforms, indicative triggers for the first operation o f the
new series have been agreed (Section V.C).          These triggers will be reviewed jointly with the
Government inlight o f the priorities that will emerge from the ANDs.

4.     The Program Document i s structured as follows.           Section I1 describes Afghanistan's
achievements since 2001, challenges, and future prospects, with a focus on the role o f the PSIBs.
Section I11presents GOA'Sdevelopment strategy outlining recent achievements, key challenges,
and the reform strategy in each reform area, particularly those supported under the PSIBs.
Section IV describes the Bank's assistance to Afghanistan and coordination with other donors.
Section V describes the proposed operation, including the reform areas to be supported by the
Grants, the prior actions before Board presentation o f PSIB 111, and the indicative triggers toward
a new series.      Section VI outlines key implementation parameters for the proposed Grant,
includingbenefits andrisks.

                   11.     AFGHANISTAN'S DEVELOPMENT SINCE 2001

5.    Afghanistan has achieved significant results over the last five years in a challenging
context. Nevertheless, the situation remains very fragile, especially in view o f the tense, and in
some areas deteriorating, security situation. Notwithstanding the economic recovery and the
implementation o f a sound development strategy, the country has not emerged fully from its state
o f conflict: the security and political situation has not fully normalized, during the past year the
security situation especially in the South has deteriorated, development achievements are frail,
and challenges remain enormous.            This Section reviews the country context, describes
institutional change supported by the PSIB operations, assesses the development impact, and
outlines the medium-term framework for the program.

A.     Country Context

6.     TheBonn Political Process and Beyond. InDecember 2001, Afghan factions met inBonn
under UN auspices to agree on a transitional process leading to elections for a "broad-based,
gender-sensitive, multi-ethnic, and fully representative Government". Much has been achieved
since then. A new Constitutionwas adopted inJanuary 2004. Hamid Karzai (previously head o f
the transitional administration) became Afghanistan's first democratically elected President in
December 2004. Elections for the WolesiJirgu (lower house o f the National Assembly) and for
Provincial Councils took place in September 2005. Female turnout inboth elections was around
40 percent o f the total electorate and more than a quarter o f Wolesi Jirgu members are female.
The inauguration o f the National Assembly in December 2005 marked the formal conclusion o f
the Bonn process, a major achievement. In spite o f concerns about Parliament's fragmentation
and lack o f experience, the first year o f operation o f the Parliament i s generally viewed as
positive, with ministers (and several other high officials) confirmed and the first annual budget
approved. However, the normalization o f political culture still has a long way to go before the
Parliament can achieve its objectives o frepresenting Afghan citizens and holding the executive to
account.    The next presidential elections, scheduled for 2009, will also bear upon political
developments inthe coming years.

7.     Stabilization and recovery beyond the Bonn process i s anchored in the Afghanistan
Compact presentedand agreed at a high-level international conference inLondon on January 3 1-
February 1 2006. Covering 2006-2010, this new framework i s based on mutual commitments
between GOAand the international community, with detailed benchmarks and timelines (see
Annex 1). The Compact emphasizes increased Afghan ownership and leadership. Enhancing the

8.     Security and Rule of Law. Since 2001, security has been a major challenge. The situation
deteriorated in 2006, stabilized somewhat during the winter, but remains volatile. Sources o f
insecurity are complex, including increased insurgency (particularly in the Southern and Eastern
regions), criminality, drug-related activity, and terrorism (including explosions and suicide
bombings inurban areas). The objective o f the post-Taliban reformo f the Afghan security forces
was to build an 70,000-strong Afghan National Army (ANA) and 62,000 strong Afghan National
Police (ANP). Although a 36,000-strong ANA has been built and most o f the ANP has been
trained, the capacity o f these forces remains insufficient to guarantee security across the country
and external support remains essential.          Coordination between Afghan forces, and with
international troops, i s challenging.         Coordination between security and development
interventions has become a priority.         The Disarmament, Demobilization, and Reintegration
(DDR) initiative has been completed (63,000 former combatants have been disarmed and
demobilized and 55,000 assisted with reintegration) and a follow-up program initiated (the
Disbandment o f Illegal Armed Groups program). Beyond their impact on security and the rule o f
law, these challenges affect the Government's credibility, which i s compounded by perceptions o f
patronage, corruption, and criminality, including injudicial and law enforcement institutions.

9.    Opium Economy. The opium economy remains Afghanistan's leading economic activity.
Its share in the national economy went down from 38 percent in 2002/03 to 26 percent in
2004/05. But, in 2006, production increased to an unprecedented 6,100 tons o f opium, further
enhancing Afghanistan's position as the dominant supplier o f illicit opiates to the world. Twenty
eight out o f the country's 34 provinces and about 13 percent (3 million) o f the total population are
directly involved in opium cultivation, while the livelihoods o f more people rely indirectly on it.
Despite initial progress in the containment o f illicit drug production, the 21 percent decrease in
area planted with poppy recorded in 2005 was followed by a 59 percent increase in 2006, with
possibility o f a further increase in2007. There are complex linkages between the opium industry
and insecurity. Opiumrevenues are providing resources to elements opposed to the emergence o f
an effective, accountable state. Drug-related corruption and crime undermine the institution-
buildingprocess, notably in the security sector. Dependence on opium production also distorts
the economy and discourages the development o flicit economic activities.

10. Development Strategy. In January 2006, the Government completed its I-ANDs and
presented it, together with the Afghanistan Compact, to the international community.               This
strategy serves as the country's interim Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP). It builds on
earlier achievements, includingthe NationaI Development Framework (April 2002) and the report
Securing Afghanistan's Future (March 2004).        It is anchored in a vision for Afghanistan: "to
consolidate peace and stability through just, democratic processes and institutions, and to reduce
poverty and achieve prosperity through broad based and equitable economic growth".               These
objectives are consistent with the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) national targets
adopted by the Cabinet in 2005 (Table 4). The LANDS is structured around three pillars:              (1)
security, (ii)governance, rule o f law and human rights, and (iii)economic and social
development. The Government has now embarked on a process o f analytical work, sector
strategy formulation, and consultations to develop the I-ANDsinto a full ANDs by mid-2008.


B.     Institution-Building
11.    Institution-building is at the core o f the objectives o f the PSIBs, and significant
achievements have been made under PSIB Iand 11, on which the proposedGrant builds (Table 2).
These achievements, in the areas o f macroeconomic management, Public Finance Management
(PFM), and PAR, include:         (i)improvements in the legal and regulatory framework; (ii)
improvements in budget management processes, which, together with PAR, strengthen
Government stewardship o f the development process; and (iii)          improvements in civil service
efficiency. The PSIB series also contributes to building a monitoring framework and provides
technical assistance in the form o f policy dialogue and advice, analytical work, training, and
system investments, as part o fthe IDAprogramthat complements it (Section IV).

12. Macroeconomic Management.                The GOA i s maintaining a stable macroeconomic
framework as evidenced by the successful completion of all quarterly reviews under the IMF
Staff-Monitored Program (SMP, from March 2004 to March 2006) and the first review of the
Poverty Reductionand Growth Facility (PRGF) program (initiated inJune 2006, cf. Annex 2). A
currency reform was completed inearly 2003. Monetary policy has been restrained, supported by
adherence to fiscal discipline and a no-overdraft rule that prohibits Central Bank financing o f the
deficit. Inflation i s decelerating and year-on-year inflation fell to 7 percent at the end o f 2006107
(compared to 14.9 percent in 2004/05).        The nominal exchange rate i s stable, while the real
exchange rate i s slowly appreciating owing to the significant inflows o f foreign exchange.

13.   Public Finance Management. Significant achievements inPFMhave built the credibility
o f the budget as a policy instrument and as a vehicle for good governance (Annex 3). First,
starting in2002 from a budget focused on recurrent costs only and simply seeking to list donors'
projects, the quality o f the budget has improved steadily. Major milestones were the adoption
and strengthening of the Medium Term Fiscal Framework (MTFF), the integration o f the

                                                  3

operating and development budgets, and a closer alignment between budgets and the
development strategy. As noted above, these processes have contributed to a record o f fiscal
discipline (evidenced by the absence o f overdrafts and the stability of the non-teaching civil
service size). Second, from a dysfimctional payment system and a weak control framework in
2002, budget execution and fiduciary controls have been enhanced with the implementationo fthe
centralized computerized Afghanistan Financial Management Information System (AFMIS) and
the system o f Treasury Single Account. A modem regulatory fiamework has been adopted, with
the Public Finance and Expenditure Management (PFEM) Law, the associated regulations, and an
Accounting Manual. These developments have contributed to an increase inthe implementation
ratio o f the budget and tighter fiduciary standards. Third, procurement reform i s now moving
forward, with the establishment o f a modem regulatory framework (including the Procurement
Law), a capacity development program throughout Government, and a system to monitor and
disclose procurement activities. In November 2006, the centralized procurement unit received
I S 0 9001 certification. Fourth, for the first time, the 2005/06 budget was audited and the audit
report was sent to Parliament in a timely manner. A number o f these achievements were made
possible by sustained technical assistance: developing in-house capacity remains a critical
challenge for the Government.

14.       Public Administration. A quarter o f a century of wars and civil strife had left Afghanistan
in2001 with a collapsed administrative apparatus, a lack ofbasic skills, inadequatemanagement,
confused organizations, and a pay scale unable to attract the needed qualified personnel. At the
same time, it was recognized early on that it would be impractical to imagme a total

C.     DevelopmentAchievementsand Challenges

19.    Progress inbuildinginstitutions has translated into significant development achievements.
The challenges however remain enormous, as evidenced by MDGindicators (Table 4 and Annex
5). Poverty i s widespread: even duringthe most affluent season ina year (i.e. after the harvest), a
third o f the population cannot meet basic needs o f living. Vulnerability is high: a small income
shock could significantly increase the share o f the poor population. Disparities, across regions
and gender, are considerable.




  Source: Staff estimates based onNRVA 2005.

20.    Economic Growth. Afghanistan's economy achieved a major recovery from a state o f
near-collapse at the end o f the Taliban rule. The real value o fnon-opium Gross Domestic Product
(GDP) increased by 16 percent on average over the last four years (Table 1). GDP per capita i s
estimated at about US$300 in 2005/06 (up from less than US$200 in 2001). Economic growth
has been particularly strong in services and construction, driven by the reconstruction effort.
Lower growth in 2004/05 and 2006/07 was mostly due to a significant drought.               The legal
economy i s dominated by small-farm agriculture and other primary sector industry with
traditional methods o f production. Secondary and service sectors are still limited to a few
industries. Existingsources o f growth need to be broadened to a more diverse and sustainable set
o f growth dnvers.

21.    Human Development.               Achievements in the area o f human development are very
significant, albeit starting from a dire situation. Health services are gaining outreach, with the
Basic Package o f Health Services (BPHS) covering about 90 percent o f the population.
According to a third-party evaluation, quality o f care and the use o f services are improving (for
instance, the coverage o f prenatal care has increased from 5 percent in 2003 to 71 percent in
2006). Nevertheless, health indicators for Afghanistan (Table 4) remain among the worst in the
world. There has been tremendous demand for education and some increase in supply (teacher
recruitment and school rehabilitation). Although enrollment increased from 1 to 5 million in 5
years, the net enrollment rate inprimary education i s only 25 percent, with serious concerns about
the quality o f education. The adult literacy rate is still low at 31 percent o f the 15-24 years old


                                                    7

population.   In higher education, for the first time in the history o f Afghanistan, a private
university opened in2007 inadditionto the 18 existingpublic universities.

22.    Successful nationwide social programs include the National Emergency Employment
Program (NEEP), which has provided 5 million labor days and rehabilitated 3,000 km o f roads,
and the National Solidarity Program(NSP), a system o f grants to communities that already covers
over half of Afghanistan's rural communities and has provided 17 million labor days. The NSP
represents a significant institutional achievement with the 17,000 elected Community
Development Councils, now formally recognized by a law and becomingpotentially village-level
institutions for planning, coordination, and monitoring local development activities. Finally,
despite dramatic gaps, the situation o f Afghan women has improved through: (i)a clear legal
basis under the new Constitution for women to participate in political and economic affairs (a
quarter o f parliamentarians are women); (ii)   a significant increase in school enrollment o f grls;
and (iii)progress inmaternal care and expanded access to reproductive health.

23.   Infrastructure. Achievements ininfrastructure are significant, even though infrastructure,
which was devastated by the conflict or inexistent before the conflict, remains one o f the key
constraints to private-sector development and service delivery. Inparticular, ina country where
around 80 percent o f the population lives in rural, often remote, and mountainous areas,
expanding access to infrastructure for the rural population remains an enormous challenge. Inthe
roads sector, the rehabilitationo f the core national highway network i s now fully funded and well
under implementation, while a quarter o f the estimated 34,000 km rural road network has been
returned to maintainable condition. However, infrastructure and transport services are far from
functioning, limiting market integration in many areas. Investments in the power sector have
focused on rehabilitation o f damaged generation plants and associated transmission lines for
Kabul and Kandahar, the distribution network in Kabul, and construction o f transmission lines
from the North to bringlower-cost importedpower to Kabul. Power supply remains very limited
and o f poor quality, with only about 16 percent o f the population having access to grid-supplied
power. Most o f Afghanistan's energy needs are met by traditional fuels, such as wood, which
have serious adverse health and environmental effects.             Telecommunication has rapidly
expanded, driven by a competitive market and large private investments. The country currently
has a teledensity o f almost 10 percent (2.1 million cellular mobile users and 75,000 telephone
main lines). About 32 percent o f all inhabitants now have access to improved water sources, and
65 percent have access to improved sanitation facilities.

24.    Financial Sector. In2001, almost all credit and other financial transactions were carried
out in the informal sector. DAB and the state-owned banks were plagued by problems o f weak
corporate governance and management, unshlled staff, and outdated technological resources,
preventing them from undertaking conventional banking functions and raising grave liquidity and
solvency issues.    Since then, progress has been made in rebuilding DAB'Scapacity. New
legislation (Central Bank Law and Banking Law) was adopted to grant the Central Bank
independence and establishthe basis for a modern legal framework for a two-tier banking system.
Key prudential regulations and anti-money laundering legislationhave been adopted. Banks and
other financial and non-financial institutions are required to report suspicious transactions to a
Fiscal Intelligence Unit, established in 2005 as semi-autonomous within DAB. Fifteen
commercial banks have now been licensed, some with branches in large urban areas outside o f
Kabul. A vibrant network o f micro-finance institutions, under the umbrella o f the Microfinance
Investment Support Facility for Afghanistan (MISFA), created in 2003, has been developed. It
now serves more than 280,000 clients, spread out over much o f the country, and has disbursed
more than US$170 million in loans. The large informal financial sector, the hawala system,
continues to operate inparallelto the conventional banlung systemand offers similar services.

25.   Private Sector Development. Some progress has been made in improving the business
environment, even though security, infrastructure, and corruption remain key constraints.


                                                  8

According to the 2007 Doing Business report, Afghanistan ranks 17'h out o f 175 countries inease
o f registering and establishing a business (although it ranks poorly on most of the other
dimensions): it only takes 3 procedures, usually completed within 8 days, to fulfill all start-up
requirements. This remarkable achievement was made possible through the establishment in
2003 o f the Afghanistan Investment Support Agency (AISA) as a one-stop shop for .business
regstration. AISA has registered over US$1.5 billion innew investments since its creation, one
third from foreign sources andmore than halfinconstruction and construction materials. The tax
code was restructured and clarified, customs tariffs were rationalized, existing trade agreements
have been renewed, and new agreements entered. As a result, private investment reached 8
percent o fGDP in2005/06 and both imports and exports are growing at double-digit rates.


D.     Prospects

26.    The remainder o f this section outlines a medium-tern framework fully consistent with the
IMF'sPRGF, which covers the periodMarch2006 -March2009.

27.    Growth. Economic growth i s a cornerstone o fthe I-ANDs,which targets 9 percent growth
per annum (non-opium GDP). The strategy identifies four sources o f growth: (i)           high-value
agriculture, pastoralism, agro-processing, and rural industries; (ii) productive use o f state assets;
(iii) andextractiveindustries;and(iv)regionalcooperationandtransittradeinenergyand
     mining
goods. It also identifies a number o f challenges to sustaining growth: ensuring security and the
rule o f law; maintaining macroeconomic stability; reforming the legal and regulatory framework
to create a climate conducive to private sector investment (e.g. property registration, contract
enforcement, and investor protection); and, more generally, buildingan effective and accountable
state. This strategy is in line with the findings o f the 2005 Investment Climate Assessment,
which identified basic infrastructure, notably electricity and transport, access to land, corruption,
and accessto finance as the key constraints to investment. Sustained agriculturalgrowth requires
productivity gains from adoption o f available technologies, rehabilitation o f irrigation schemes,
and investments inrural roads for better accessto inputand output markets (Para. 85).

28.    Revenue Mobilization. The Government committed in the Afghanistan                 Compact to
increase its domestic revenues to 8 percent o f GDP by 2010/11. Revenues increased in 2006/07
to more than 6 percent o f GDP (Table 5). GoA's strategy includes policy measures to diversify
its sources o f revenues, from import duties to a broader tax base, and to increase compliance
(Para. 40). Factoring inexpected significant economic growth, projections indicate a doubling o f
domestic revenues by 2009/10 inline withthe Compact target.

29.    Expenditures. The fiscal framework for the core budget incorporates several expenditure
pressures, drivingtotal expenditures from U S $ l . l billion in2005/06 to US$1.6 billion in2006/07
and US$2.7 billion by 2009/10. On the operating budget side, the framework i s consistent with
GoA's objective to finance operating expenditures with its own revenues by 2011/12 (the ratio
will increase from 64 percent in2005/06 to 82 percent in2009/10). Key increases include: (i)      the
implementation o f the P&G policy; (ii)     the recruitment o f 10,000 teachers per year; (iii)    an
increase in non-wage expenditures (to make up for the initial insufficient level, operate and
maintain new investments, and absorb some costs being transferred from the external budget - for
instance, donors at first funded the cost o f procuring diesel fuel to produce electricity mainly for
Kabul but this funding stopped in 2006, with the need for GOAto assume this cost); and (iv)
significant increases in the security sector (ANA troop levels increasing toward targeted levels,
implementation o f the Pay and Rank reform in the ANP, ANP force levels increasing beyond
initial estimates, and related increases in non-salary expenditures).    Development expenditures
are expected to increase over the next years to meet the development challenges o f the country.
This assumes a gradual shift o f donor assistance from the external to the core budget, a key
objective o f the I-ANDs, and a substantial increase in implementation capacity to scale up
spending (in2005/06, only 37 percent o fthe original development budget was spent, Table 3).

30.     Financing. The Government is committed to fiscal discipline and will maintain its no-
overdraft policy. In2005/06, the core budget had a surplus (inclusive o f donor grants) o f US$171
million, which led to an increase in deposits (Table 3).                          Some increase in cash deposits i s a
positive outcome because significant risks (uneven spending patterns, risks related to revenue
collection and external assistance volatility) cannot be mitigated, given the absence o f domestic
financial markets. However, the level o f these deposits also came to reflect in part weak budget
implementation. The fiscal need for the proposed operation i s premised on a reduction in these
cash deposits during the latter part o f 2006/07 and a plan to further reduce them in 2007108.
Hence cash deposits are not expected to increase in the future - except in the event o f one-off
revenues such as liquidation proceeds. Donors' assistance will remain crucial for financing
Afghanistan's budget in coming years. Building on the US$10.5 billion pledged at the London
Conference and a gradual shift from the external to the core budget, external assistance to the
budget is expected to increase from US$l.O billion in 2006/07 to US$1.7 billion in 2009/10
(including through the ARTF, which i s expected to remain a key vehicle for this assistance).
Finally, Afghanistan has adopted a prudent external debt strategy and expects to borrow only on
concessional terms. Its eligibility for the HIPC Initiative, combined with the Multilateral Debt
Relief Initiative (MDRI) and bilaterals' commitment to debt cancellation, should lead to
significant debt reliefinthe comingyears (Para. 18).

32.    The Government's strategy for economic growth and poverty reduction i s formulated inthe
LANDS. Its key elements can be articulated around five areas o f reform, an important subset o f
which i s supported by this series o f PSIBs (Section V)': (i)         promoting security and rule of law;
(ii)maintainingmacroeconomic stability; (iii)strengthening public administration and fiscal
management. (iv) enhancinghuman development; and (v) promotingprivate sector development.


A.      Promoting Security and Rule of Law

33.    Recent Achievements and Challenges. As noted in Para. 8, training o f the new ANP and
the ANA i s ongoing. The National Drug Control Strategy (NDCS) has been promulgated. The
activities o f the Office o f the Attorney General accelerated duringthe year. But challenges on the
reform front remain significant. The police reform process has been obstructed by issues o f
coordination, alleged corruption (notably drug-related), lack o f capacity, a weak appointments
process for senior positions, and shortfalls in funding. Managmg the fiscal costs o f increasing
security expenditures i s also challenging. Implementing the NDCS remains a very serious
challenge, exacerbated by the increase in opium poppy cultivation in2006. Finally, the reform o f
the justice system suffers from lack o f independence o f judges, concentration o f power and
functions in the Supreme Court, insufficient correctional facilities, inadequate legal framework,
deficiency o f the internal security forces, perceptions o f corruption, and an inadequatenumber o f
criminaljustice professionals.

34.    Reform Strategy. Recognizing the critical role o f security, the GOAhas adopted a special
MDGon enhancing security and has made security one o f the three pillars o fthe I-ANDs. The
reform strategy includes plans to train the new ANP, establish an auxiliary police force, and
accelerate the recruitment o f the ANA, which has important fiscal implications (Para. 29). To
improve the judicial system and ensure its independence, the strategy includes efforts to rebuild
the administration o f juvenile justice, prison, and law enforcement systems. The Government
also seeks to build human rights institutions and capacity, raise awareness, and develop societal
consensus around humanrights issues.

B.3      RevenuePolicy and Administration

39.    Recent Achievements and Challenges. Progress in increasing revenues i s an important

higher tariff bands on certain goods, partly in lieu o f excises. The Large Taxpayer Office was
further strengthened and the computerized system for customs (Automated System for Customs

broaden the tax base while eliminating a number o f small "nuisance" taxes.           Second, the

strengthening provincial Mustoufiats to carry out their revenue functions.       Third, GOAwill
continue to implement its five-year strategic customs administration plan, with the refurbishment
o f key border posts, the roll-out o f ASYCUDA (Para. 84), and a clarification o f the roles of the

administration to assess vulnerabilities to corruptionand implement mitigating measures.

support, the Central Statistics Office (CSO) prepared a poverty diagnostic. Training to use the
NRVA data has accelerated in2006. A work programhas been adoptedto develop baseline data
for the I-ANDs. Progress was made in preparing the census, including (i)testing the

capacity will be a trigger toward the successor program.

C.l      Government Structure

43.   Recent Achievements and Challenges. After the approval by Cabinet in November 2005

PSIB 111). Significant progress has beenmade inthe Ministryo f Education (MoE), which i s fully

supervision capacity and the development o f a strategy to expand access to financial services will
be a trigger toward the successor program.

E.4      State-Owned EnterpriseRestructuring

81.    Recent Achievements and Challenges.             The Government made progress on the
restructuring o f public enterprises in 2005/06, a trigger for PSIB 111. Progress includes: (i)
Cabinet approval o f a privatization policy for enterprises under the SOE Law; (ii)    gazetting o f
amendments to the SOE Law to allow liquidation, privatization, and joint ventures; (iii)
classification o f the 65 SOEs to separate those that remain under state ownership for the time
being (9 enterprises) from those to be divested through liquidation or privatization (56
enterprises); (iv) adoption by Cabinet o f a Policy on Social Safety Nets; and (v) issuance o f
liquidation and corporatization procedures. However, this agenda needs further progress. Public
corporations (GOA'Sparticipation in enterprises not operating under the SOE Law) are virtually
not monitored by the Government and there is no clear strategy for restructuring them. Although
SOEs are not a major fiscal burden, the Parliament has expressed concerns about restructuring
processes and vulnerabilities to corruption. In addition, financial oversight by the GOAover
SOEs remains weak.        While SOEs provide the M o F with regular reports on their financial
situation and performance, the quality o fthis data is very weak.

82.    Reform Strategy. The Government is committed to divest 56 SOEs by the end o f 2009
(Compact benchmark, 8.3). A clear policy will be developed to govern new investments and
reporting requirements o f the SOEs that will remain under Government ownership. Dialogue
with stakeholders will be undertaken to develop the understanding o fthe reforms' objectives and
address Parliament's concerns about these reforms. Progress in the oversight o f publicly-owned
enterprises with Government participation,         a critical condition for transparency and
accountability, will be a trigger toward the successor program.

E.5      Trade Reform

83.    Recent Achievements and Challenges. Afghanistan has made significant progress in
opening its trade regime and recently joined several regional trade organizations (Central Asian
Regonal Economic Council and South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation), which will
increase trade facilitation. Despite some changes in 2006, the trade regime remains one of the
most open in the region (Para. 39). Challenges include: (i)    a weak customs administration with
vulnerability to corruption; (ii)  numerous constraints to transit, in addition to security issues
(multiple fees and taxes, delays and uncertainties at multiple checkpoints, etc.); and (iii)poorly
defined institutionalresponsibilities for behind the border issues.

procedures and curtail smuggling, notably with the establishment o f a computerized transit
management system; (ii)     improve regional trade and transit issues - including development o f
efficient, safe, and reliable, transport corridors; and (iii)

E.6      AgriculturalDevelopment

85.    Recent Achievements and Challenges. With 80 percent o f the population living in rural
areas and the dominance o f opium production, agnculture is at the core o f the Government's
development strategy.     Since 2001, agnculture production has revived - the result o f better
climate conditions and some rural infrastructure investments (including roads, Para. 23, and
irrigation). In2006, the Government prepared an Agriculture Sector Development Strategy and a
Master Plan to implement it. Administrative reforms inthe Ministryo f Agriculture and h g a t i o n
(MAI) were initiated. The MAI also initiated a pilot to use community mediation for land
conflict resolution. However, the four key constraints to higher productivity in the agricultural
sector remain: (i)poor utilization o f water resources and unsustainable water resource
management practices; (ii)   dysfunctional research and extension services; (iii) underdevelopment
o f agnculture markets and support services; and (iv) lack of rural credit. Some o f these issues
stem from weaknesses in the legal framework for land rights, a combination o f overlap,
inconsistency, and uncertainty between several legal basis (customary, religious, and state law).
Cutting across these issues is the need to align piecemeal activities with the new sector strategy
and reformingthe ministriesinthe sector.

86.    Reform Strategy. The Government i s committed to create a framework conducive to
competitiveness and efficient factor markets in agnculture. The Government's strategy focuses
on perennial horticulture, livestock production, and food security and calls for the establishment
o f the necessary legal, institutional, regulatory, and incentive framework to increase agnculture
productionand productivity and enable agnculture-based industries. A ministerial committee has
been formed to develop programs that exploit the area-based linkages between policies and
investments in several sectors. In the critical area o f water management, the recently formed
Supreme Council for Water Affairs Management will finalize a water sector strategy.               The
Government i s also committed to reform the system o f land registration and land dispute
settlement (Compact Benchmark2.8). Finally, the MAIwill continue developingits capacity and
focus on its core role to create an enabling environment for agriculture development.

E.7      Oil,Gas, andMiningDevelopment

87.    Recent Achievements and Challenges. Afghanistan has substantial reserves o f solid
minerals, with known endowment o f base metals, coal, quarry materials, marble and dimension
stone, industrial minerals, semiprecious stones, and modest reserves o f natural gas.              As
emphasized in the LANDS, the extractive industry sector can be a source o f economic growth
and Government's revenues. Modem Minerals and Hydrocarbons Laws were adopted in 2005,
although the latter has shortcomings that need to be addressed. The 2005 amendments to the
Income Tax Law (Para. 39) have created an attractive fiscal regime for the sector. The tendering
o f the Aynak mine i s under way. The Government has committed to good governance in the
sector by: (i)adopting regulations and model contracts to implement the laws; (ii)   establishing an
International Advisory Council to review transactions; (iii)   usingthe services o f an international
firm to build the cadastre and grant licenses; and (iv) implementing the principles o f the
Extractive Industry Transparency Initiative. However, implementing these commitments and
attractingprivate investment inthe sector remaina major challenge.

88.    Reform Strategy. The Government i s committed to focusing its role on regulation and
developing a policy environment conducive to private sector participation and good governance,
ensuring underground assets are utilized to their potential and proceeds for Government are used

effectively (Box 1). The reform strategy will consist of: (i)  amending the Hydrocarbons Law; (ii)
implementingthe new laws; and (iii)    reforming the Ministry o f Mines from a vertically integrated
extractive industryconglomerate to a regulatory ministry.

E.     LessonsLearned

100. Through the successful implementation o f PSIB Iand II, investment operations, and
                                                                    other
analybcal work as described above, a number o f lessons have been learned. These lessons are in
line with, and build on, lessons learned in other fragile states (e.g. "Good Practice Note on
DevelopmentPolicy Lending inFragile States").

101. First, the ISN, summarizing lessons from the implementation o f the TSS, noted that
reconstruction i s not only about infrastructure, but nothing less than rebuilding the state itself. As
such, the process o f reconstruction takes time, reinforcing the need for programmatic support to
buildinginstitutions.

102. Second, the PSIBs series, together with the ARTF and IMF programs, has greatly
reinforced the importance and credibility o f the budget and, more recently, o f the MTFF.
Expenditure systems have been strengthened and some capacity has been built to implement the
budget and development programs inan accountable manner. There remainmany gaps, however,
as demonstrated by the large shortfall inbudget implementation (Table 3). Analytical work and
policy dialogue suggest that these gaps require further clarification o f the regulatory framework
for PFM and administrative refom, while extending the r e f o m effort from the center (MoF and
IARCSC inparticular) to line ministries and provinces.

103. Third, in the context o f Afghanistan, principles o f selectivity, ownership, and flexibility,
which are applicable across countries, mustbe specifically emphasized (Box 3).

104. Fourth, PSIB's experience emphasized the need to maximize synergies with other
instruments. As described in other sections, the design builds on synergies with the Bank's
analytical work and investment operations, as well as synergies with the IMF's PRGF, the ADB's
program loans, and the ARTF. All o f these activities, in turn, are closely coordinated with the
Government's reform program.

105. Finally, PSIB has demonstrated the importance o f a solid monitoring and evaluation system
for two reasons. First, such a system didnot exist inAfghanistan. Second, because o f its scope,
evaluating the success o f the reform program supported by the PSIB (notably PAR) i s difficult
given the complexity o f defining indicators that are neither solely input-driven, nor excessively
distant from the actions under Government's control. While PSIB has already supported some
effort toward monitoring and evaluation (for the fiscal and public administration areas, the
education andhealth sectors, and the development strategy), these efforts mustbe intensified.

                            Box 3: GoodPracticePrincipleson Conditionality
Principle 1: Reinforceownership
There is strong ownership o f the PSIB-supported program, itself based on the GoA's development strategy
(Section VI.A). The track-record built over the first two operations, with most triggers met, is a fiuther
indication o f ownership and commitment. Robust progress to tighten fiduciary standards (with the initial
exception o f procurement reform until the M o F became, in 2005, a champion for this agenda) i s a
testimony to the strong leadership exerted by the MoF. The PAR agenda has been more contentious,
leading the reform program supported by PSIB to first focus on the development of a clear plan (to be
rolled out in the coming years). Ownership for restructuring state-owned banks has been weaker, with
delays inimplementing agreed reforms, but the GOAremains committed to the reformagenda (Para. 79).
Principle 2: Agree upfront with the Government and other financial partners on a coordinated
accountabilityframework
The policy matrix (cf. Annex 1) was agreed with the Government and serves to monitor progressunder the
reformprogram. It i s tightly linked to the policy matrix put forward by the Government inthe I-ANDs, as
well as other donor-supported agreements (such as the ARTF's PAM). However, as noted in the text,
while more effective aid is a central I-ANDs objective and aid coordination is improving, there are still
many gaps, which reflect both the weak capacity o f the Government to manage aid and the inability of
some donors to hlly use Government systems in these circumstances. The PSIB program supports the
Government's objective o f expanding the use o f country systems.
Principle 3: Customize the accountability framework and modalities of Bank support to country
circumstances
The PSIB program has been adjusted to country circumstances and reflects the need for selectivity and
flexibility.  As noted above, strong ownership has systematically been a criterion for selecting policy
reform areas.    For instance, with respect to SOEs, instead o f outright privatization, the program has
supported the preparation o f an adequate regulatory framework for restructuring as well as stronger
monitoring o f those SOEs that are most likely to stay under Government ownership. Similarly, the increase
inelectricitytariffsinKabulwas incorporatedintheprogramonlywhenitclearlybecamethe only solution
to ensure the financial viability o f power supply inKabul; inaddition, the potential distributional impact o f
the reformwas reviewed (Box 2).
Principle4: Chooseactions critical for achievingresultsas conditionsfor disbursement
Selectivity is critical when (i)capacity i s limited and (ii) needs appear to be urgent and top-priority. The
                                                             all
focus o f the PSIB series on a limited set o f reforms was essential to establish a track record o f reform
implementation. For instance, the bottlenecks inthe legislative agenda show the imperative of prioritizing
new laws. Flexibility also has been important given the fluid situation on the ground. Triggers have been
viewed as a portfolio o f critical actions and were assessed flexibly with due consideration to changing
circumstances.     For instance, the trigger for PSIB IIon improvements in fiduciary standards initially
included the drafting o f a new audit law. As the GoA's priorities shified and another law (the PFEM Law)
provided a sufficient basis for an independent external audit, this part o f the trigger was not retained as
prior action for PSIB 11. In some areas, it was felt necessary to include as triggers the development o f
strategies and action plans, reflecting the early stages o f the development process inAfghanistan. The need
for flexibility has validated the design of this program as a series of one-tranche operations based on prior
actions. Flexibility becomes even more critical as the program shifts to supporting reforms outside Kabul.
Principle 5: Conduct transparent progress reviews conducive to predictable and performance-based
financial support
Progress reviews o f the PSIB program have been conducted about every 4-6 months. They are based on
inter-ministerial discussions chaired by the Deputy Minister for Finance. They are thoroughly documented
to ensure transparency and predictability. A large degree o f flexibility was however kept in reaching
agreement on the timing o f each operation, to take into account the volatile context and the constraints on
budget implementation.

                           VI.     OPERATIONIMPLEMENTATION

A.     Country Ownership

113. The policy and strategy framework for PSIB i s driven by national priorities for state
building, economic growth, and poverty reduction as reflected in the I-ANDs and the budget.
The GOAheld a series o f consultations during the I-ANDs preparation (including with NSP
representatives, the private sector, and the donor community) and has adopted an ambitious plan
o f consultations to prepare the full ANDs. Priorities identified through this process will be
reflected in the successor program. The implementation o f PSIB Iand 11, as well as other
programs (including ADB's and IMF's programs), also has shown that ownership o fthe program
by counterpart ministries and the Central Bank is strong. The definition o f the reform areas
supported by the PSIBs, prior actions, and triggers results from a highly collaborative process
between the Bank and the Government under the leadership o f MoF.

114. Several aspects o f the PSIBs are expected to foster and support broader country ownership.
First, the PSIBs provide incremental fundingto the Government that can be used strategically to
implement development programs country-wide. Second, the PSIBs allow the Government to
continue building a record o f accomplishments while adapting its reform program as it goes
forward. For example, the number o f prior actions and triggers included inthis operation reflects
not only the solid achievements to date, but also the level o f challenges that the Government i s
willing to confront. As such, the PSIBs can be used strategically as they providebothleverageto
implement core reforms and the flexibility required to adapt to unforeseen developments, which
can only increase overall country ownership.


B.      MonitoringandEvaluation

115. Progress has been made in 2005/06 toward developing capacity to monitor and evaluate
outcomes o f and progress under the reform program. Main achievements o f the previous years
have been deepened. Monetary and fiscal data continue to be releasedon a timely basis, which i s
supported by continued adherence to IMF programs. Performance in the P F M area continues to
be monitored with a set o fperformance indicators (Annex 3). The M o E and the MoPH continued
the development o f their management information system to monitor education and health
indicators. A major breakthrough was the completion o f the 2005 NRVA, creating for the first
time since 2001 a solid database to set a baseline for a number o f socio-economic indicators. On
this basis, inMarch 2007, the CSO, with support from IDA, prepared a baseline for its poverty
diagnostic (Para. 41). Also in 2007, the IARCSC adopted a framework to monitor civil service
reforms and, eventually, their impact on service delivery. The monitoring o f the Compact and the
preparation o f the ANDs have created a renewed demand for robust monitoring mechanisms.

116. There remain however many gaps and the statistics sector needs institutional reform. The
Government i s committed to continue developing its statistical capacity. This requires enhancing
capacity at all levels o f planning, design, data collection, and analysis. Progress int h s area will
be a trigger toward the successor program (Para. 42) and i s also supported by the IMF's PRGF.

117. IDA, in collaboration with other agencies, i s supporting the Government's strategy in the
area o f monitoring and evaluation. It i s providing extensive technical assistance to fully use the
NRVA data and improve its design for the next round. This in turn will permit IDA and the
Government to intensify their dialogue on the equity impact o f public spending and contribute to
preparingthe ANDs. This technical assistance will also enable further institutional reform at the
CSO. Finally, the PSIB Grants, together with other IDA lending and analytical instruments,
support reforms o f the monitoring framework, including in the fiscal and procurement areas,
PAR, health, education, and power, paving the way for a gradual transition toward an outcome
focus (Section V.C).


                                                  33

118. In this context, monitoring and evaluation o f the PSIB program focuses largely on
processes and inputs, as opposed to outcomes.        Table 2 summarizes key indicators that are
monitored, including two impact indicators that are beyond the Government's control (see Annex
4). This monitoring framework can be seen as focusing on intermediate outcomes, while the
support to statistical capacity i s expected to build up the capacity to monitor final outcomes,
including in line with the IDA 14 Results Measurement System. The successor program will
buildonprogress inboth areas.


C.     FiduciaryAspects

119. As outlined in Section 111, significant progress has been made over the last few years in
reducing fiduciary risks. This i s evidenced by an overall positive trend o f the ARTF eligibility
ratio (Table 2), with negative changes (such as the higher ineligibility o f payroll in 2005/06, the
result o f recruitment o f teachers beyond the budgeted ceilings) met by proactive corrective
actions (establishment o f a certified payroll in M o E in that case).   These risks remain high,
however and a number o f measures are taken to mitigate them (Section G). First, several o f the
triggers for the proposed operation (notably the regulations to implement the PFEM and
Procurement Laws and the submission o f the audited annual accounts to Parliament) have laidthe
ground for further improvements in fiduciary standards. The indicative triggers toward the
successor program include further progress in fiduciary areas (Section 1II.C).          Second, this
program i s complemented by a series o f IDA investment projects to strengthen the treasury,
procurement, internal and external audit functions (Section IV). Third, IDA has conducted a
Public Finance Management Review, which provides the analytical underpinnings for the
fiduciary aspects o f the PSIBs. As part o f this review, the performance o f the PFM systems has
been rated using the framework developed by IDA and other development partners under the
Public Expenditure and Financial Accountability program (Annex 3).             This framework is
designed to track progress toward an increase in fiduciary standards. The PFM Review also
highlighted the need to expand reforms from the MoF to spending units, a shift that would be
supported by the successor program. Finally, fiduciary risks are mitigated by the coordination
between the PSIBs series, the ARTF (and its PAM), andthe IMF PRGF.

120. DAB, Afghanistan's central bank, has recently completed and submitted to Parliament its
audited 2004/05 and 2005/06 financial statements based on international standards. InJune 2006,
the IMF conducted a Safeguard Assessment of the DAB. The Assessment noted serious
vulnerabilities in several areas. Since then, one o f the key recommendations o f the Assessment,
the completion and auditing o f the last two annual financial statements, has been implemented.
Another, the implementation o f a new integrated accounting system, is well under way. The
Government i s committed to improving DAB'Soperations consistent with the principles of good
governance embedded inthe Assessment. In addition, to monitor the flow o f funds at DAB, for
the purpose o f this operation, a dedicated Deposit Account will be established at DAB (see
below). This account will form part o f the country's official foreign exchange reserves.


D.     Grant Administration,Disbursement,andAuditing

 121. Grant Amount, Beneficiary, Terms, and Tranching. The Recipient is the Islamic
Republic o f Afghanistan, representedby MoF. The Grant i s for an amount equivalent to US$80
million, on standard IDA terms to be released in one tranche following approval o f the Grant and
notification by the Association o f Grant effectiveness. The completion o f the prior actions and
the maintenance o f a satisfactory macroeconomic framework are sufficient to release the Grant.

 122. Implementation, Monitoring, and Supervision. Duringthe preparation o f the Grant, IDA
has assisted the Government to help ensure timely implementation of the agreed measures.
Ministries responsible for monitoring progress have been identified and a plan to monitor

indicators developed. To assess the impact o fthe proposed operation, IDA will monitor progress
inachievingthe triggersdescribedinthe LDP.

123. Grant Management. A dedicated Deposit Account in U S Dollars will be established at
DAB inthe name o f the MoF. The Account will be managed by MoF, on terms and conditions
satisfactory to IDA. The only funds paidto this account would be the proceeds o fthe Grant.

124. Disbursements. Upon notificationby IDA o f Grant effectiveness, and with the submission
bythe Recipient o f a withdrawal application, the proceeds o f the Grant will be depositedby IDA
into the DepositAccount o f the Recipient. An amount equivalent to the Grant proceeds will then
be credited to an account o f the Government to finance budgeted expenditures. Disbursements
will not be linked to specific purchases and no procurement requirements will be necessary.
However, the proceeds o f the Grant cannot be used for ineligible expenditures (i.e. to finance
goods and services from the IDA'S standard negative list as reflected in the Financing
Agreement). If, after deposit into the Deposit Account, the proceeds o f the Grant are used for
ineligible expenditures, IDA will require the Recipient to either (i)      return the amount to the
account for use for eligible purposes, or (ii)      refund the amount directly to IDA. Eligible
expenditures would include payments by the GOAto meet its external service obligations,
provided that there are legal agreements that establish such external debt service obligations.
Transactions and balances o f the Government account will be fully incorporated into the
Government's accounting records and financial statements, via the AFMIS.

 125. Reporting, Accounting, and Auditing. The Financing Agreement gives IDA the right to
require the Recipient to audit the Deposit Account through agreed terms o f references. IDA may
also request audits o f the Deposit Account prior to its closure in accordance with the Financing
Agreement. Given IDA'Sexperience with the use o f DAB for the special accounts o f its projects,
IDA doesnot intendto requestthe Borrowerto have the DepositAccount audited.

 126. ClosingDate. The expected closing date o fthe Grant will be March21,2008.


E.     EnvironmentalAspects

 127. The Government i s committed to develop the regulatory framework and the institutions to
protect the environment (Compact benchmark, 3.7). The policies supported under this operation
focus on a subset o f the Government's overall program and are not likely to significantly affect
the environment. The specific actions supported by the operation focus on establishing legal,
administrative, and regulatory functions o f the &ate and do not include physical investments or
policy changes that would significantly affect the environment. As a result, they are not likely to
have any significant impact on the country's environment, forests, and other natural resources.
The increase inelectricity tariff i s not expected to lead to any significant shift towards fuel wood
or polluting fuels, since electricity supply i s unreliable and very little electricity i s used for
heating and coolungby low income families. Nevertheless, this impact will be monitored.


F.     PovertyandSocialImplications

 128. The poverty and social implications o f the reform program supported by the Grants were
reviewed. Most o fthe reforms supportedby the program are expectedto have significant positive
impacts on poverty reduction, notably by increasing and allocating fiscal resources in line with
the I-ANDs. Inaddition, the Grants support the development o f a stronger statistical base, which
will enable analysis and dialogue on the distributional effects o f the reformprogram, notably the
impact on equity (reducing regional and rural-urban inequities in service provision) and
vulnerable groups (poor, women, single-parent households, migratingcommunities).




                                                  35

129. Two areas were identified as having a potential adverse impact. First, SOE reforms (Para.
128) could have an impact on employment. Jointly with its restructuring policy, the Government
has adopted a Social Safety Net policy. The compensation, which covers severance payments
and training, i s comparable to packages in other countries (as discussed in the policy note
Afghanistan: a Privatization Strategy). Inaddition, employment in SOEs (around 40,000) i s low
by international standards. Hence, the impact is expected to be limited, but IDA will monitor the
implementation o f the Social Safety Net policy. Second, the electricity tariff increase, although
limited to Kabul, could have a negative impact on the poorest. Despite a weak statistical base,
some preliminary analysis (Box 2) suggests that the impact i s limited inthe short-term, and could
be positive in the longer term as higher tariffs contribute to overall sector reforms and better
access. IDA will monitor the impact o f this tariff increase on access to electricity for the poorest
usingthe NRVA2007 (the completiono fwhich is a trigger for the successor program).

130. IDA will also monitor developments in PAR (Para. 13). Public employment i s low by
international standards and retrenchment i s not a priority option to restructure the civil service
(the demographic profile o f the civil service makes retirement an attractive option to deal with
any need to create space for recruiting new slulls).     However, IDA will review proposals for
managing employees that do not qualify for the new pay scale. Building on the 2005 Gender
Assessment (National Reconstruction and Poverty Reduction - the Role of Women in
Afghanistan's Future), IDA will also continue the dialogue on women in the civil service.
Indeed, although civil service reforms such as the introduction o f meritocracy are an opportunity
to enhance the role o f women in the Government, realizing this opportunity requires targeted
programs: omitting those would be a missed opportunity, with adverse consequences.


G.     Benefits,Risks,andRiskMitigation

131. Benefits. The PSIB Grants provide potential benefits that contribute to economic growth
and poverty reduction through further building core state institutions. By strengthening public
administration and fiscal management, together with IDA investment projects as well as
analytical and advisory services (Section IV), they enhance the role o f the budget as a core policy
instrument. By strengthening its institutional capacity and providing budgetary resources, this
allows the GOAto: (i) up the implementation o f its development strategy, including service
                        speed
delivery beyond Kabul; (ii)  enhance its credibility; and (iii) an increasing share o f donors'
                                                               attract
support to the national budget. This will benefit the whole population, including the poor,
contribute to mitigate the risk o f deterioration in the political and security situation, and enable
the transition to the new phase of the GOA'Sdevelopment strategy. This will also reduce the
implementation risks o f all projects including this operation.       The Grants also support the
implementationo freforms leadingto a more efficient allocationo f fiscal resources inthe areas o f
human development and private sector development, which will help reduce poverty. Finally, the
programmatic approach allows continuity, predictability, and flexibility in supporting the
development strategy and addressing fiscal needs.

132. Risks.      The Grants face significant country (political, security, macroeconomic, and
capacity) and project-specific (fiduciary and implementation) risks (Table 8).                Further
deterioration o f the security situation constitutes the largest source o f risk.    As reviewed in
Section 11, such deterioration would slow down budget implementation, .have an adverse impact
on revenue mobilization, and take strategic priority over the implementation o f the reform
program. Its impact on the political normalization process, combined with the still fragile nature
o f political processes, could also stall the reform program and lead to political uncertainty and
associated unrest. The drug industry continues to pose complex risks that are interrelated with
security and governance issues. The macroeconomic scenario i s uncertain since both donor
assistance and the response o fthe private sector are highlydependent on the political and security
situation (Section 1I). Capacity is generally weak and the deteriorating security situation could
weaken the link between reforms supported by the Grants (such as budget management) and

                                                 36

actual service delivery in some areas. Although mitigated by commitments made at the London
Conference, wavering donor support would also delay the implementationo f the reformprogram.
Finally, even though the risk o f sudden reversal in the reform agenda may not be too high, the
commitment to focus on the development agenda could be weakened by the deteriorating security
situation and increasing political focus on o fthe 2009 Presidential elections.

133. In summary, country risks, assessed with indicators o f institutional capacity and rule o f
law, are high. The mitigation o f these risks, which affect the whole IDA program, is largely
outside the scope o f this project. However, as long as the framework o f the national budget i s in
place, the Grants can mitigate some risks, by contributing to macroeconomic stability (through
fiscal sustainability), allowing GOAto deepen the implementation o f its programs beyond Kabul,
contributingto maintaining a focus on key reforms, and buildingthe core institutions o fthe State.

134. The Grants also have project-specific fiduciary and implementation risks (including
weakened ownership, weak capacity to implement the budget and the reform program, uncertain
impact o f PAR on service delivery, and vulnerabilities to corruption). Some o f these risks have
materialized during the implementation o f PSIB Iand I1(for instance the capacity to gazette laws
or reform state-owned banks, see Section IV.E). However, a satisfactory track record has been
built,with improvements infiduciary standards andbudget implementationandarecordo fpolicy
reforms (implementation of PSIB Iand I1and completion o f all reviews under the IMF-supported
SMP and PRGF). Inaddition, the arrangements in place for the ARTF and IDA projects (EPAP
and PACBP) provide direct support to the Government's fiduciary systems and contribute to
higher fiduciary standards for all Government transactions.                               Despite these mitigating factors,
fiduciary risks remain significant and will be closely monitored inconsultation with other donors.

135. To mitigate these risks, prior actions and triggers focus on areas for which the Government,
under the leadership o f the MoF, has clearly expressed ownership and has already built a good
track record of reforms. IDA and other donors are also supporting the Government's efforts to
strengthen its implementation capacity through investments and technical assistance. Continued
IDA assistance -together with the triggerstoward the proposed successor program-inthe areas
o f public finance management and public administration, will also contribute to raising fiduciary
standards and implementation capacity. Finally, the program design as a series o f operations
basedon prior actions provides IDA with the scope to adjust subsequent operations to the security
and political situation and the pace o freform implementation.

                                         I.INTRODUCTION
Background

1.       Afghanistan has achieved significant results in the last five years despite a challenging
context. Following the Presidential elections in late 2004, the new administration has been
emphasizing the importance o fmoving from the concept o f post-conflict emergency management
to a more medium-term, sustainable development framework and increasing Government
ownership o f policies. Two decades o f conflict had reduced physical and social infrastructure to
skeletal proportions. Coherent Government and administration were non-existent. Social
indicators were among the world's lowest, and simply maintaining security was an immense
challenge. This transition necessitates reorienting efforts from day-to-day management in
ministries and Government agencies to emphasizing forward looking strategies.

2.       Establishing the institutions and processes necessary for a peaceful political transition has
been at the forefront o f the Government's agenda, and in line with commitments made at the
Bonn Conference. Both the presidential and parliamentary elections (September 2005) were
significant achievements for Afghanistan. The security situation, however, deteriorated in 2006,
stabilized somewhat duringthe winter, and remains volatile.

3.       The Government o fAfghanistan (GOA)remained committed to rebuilding economic and
financial institutions and implementing sound macroeconomic policies. Development priorities
have been identified in the Interim Afghanistan National Development Strategy (I-ANDs). This
strategy serves as the country's interim Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper. It builds on earlier
achievements, including the National Development Framework (April 2002) and the report
Securing Afghanistan's Future (March 2004). The I-ANDs i s complemented by the Afghanistan

                                                      ANNEX 3
                                              PERFORMANCEOF
              AFGHANISTAN'S PUBLICFINANCEMANAGEMENT SYSTEM


1.      This annex is the executive summary o f the Public Financial Management (PFM)
Performance Assessment for Afghanistan prepared as part o f the Public Finance Management
Review undertaken by the World Bank in close cooperation with the Government o f Afghanistan
and various extemal partners in 2005. It i s based on a performance measurement framework
developed by the Public Expenditure and Financial Accountability (PEFA) partnership program3
which identifies a set o f critical objectives o f a PFM system and a standard set o f high-level PFM
indicators to assess performance against those objectives. The methodologyprovides a consistent
framework for analysis and a monitoring tool for the Government to manage reform and
demonstrate progress to domestic and international stakeholders.

2.      The situation inAfghanistan has some special features which needto be kept inmindwhen
interpretingthe ratings. First, some ratings reflect temporary factors (for instance, untilrecently,
the absence o f a Parliament). Second, some ratings are based on changes made recently, and it i s
still uncertain whether these changes will be sustained. Third, the ratings assess the current
situation in which significant external support (both advisory and operational) i s being provided
to the Government; in several areas, this raises sustainability issues as the external support will
have to be reduced over time, especially inrelation to financial management operations.

3.      Public financial management in Afghanistan as depicted through the PFM Performance
Indicators as o f June 2005 i s summarized below, under six critical attributes o f a public financial
management systemand i s outlined inthe attached table.

Credibility of the budget (Performance Indicators 1-4)
4.      Maintaining fiscal discipline has been a key Government priority. While the budget, before
foreign grants, i s always in deficit given the low level o f domestic revenues, over the past three
years the out-turn o f the deficit has been below planned levels. Three mechanisms have
contributed to this outcome: (i)         strong progress in revenue mobilization, although as a share o f
GDP it remains at well below half o f the level found inother low-income countries; (ii)formal               a
mid-year review process that monitors execution and may adjust the appropriations; and (iii)                        a
strictly appliedno-overdraft policy that prohibits central bank financing o fthe budget.

5.      Despite continuing improvements in budget processes, the credibility o f the budget is
hampered by optimistic budget projections, particularly for development expenditures, mainly
attributable to a lack o frealism at the budget formulation stage and limitedcapacity to implement
the budget. Moreover, the External Budget (accounting for roughly three quarters o f total public
spending in the country) remains effectively outside the Government's control, further reducing
the credibility o f the budget. There are two specific issues: (i)             the external budget i s to a large
extent outside the Government's control (and so to that extend cannot be used as an instrument o f
policy); and (ii)the gap between donor commitments reported in the budget and actual
expenditures i s large. A detailed analysis shows large deviations between budgeted amounts and
actual out-turns.

framework, indicator set, and detailed instructions for ratings are inPEFA (2005), available at www.oefa.Org.

Comprehensivenessand transparencyof budget information (Performance Indicators 5-10)
6.    The Government i s committed to share budgetary information transparently. The annual
budget for the national government is impressive, with coverage o f most public spending o f the
general Government sector, whether implemented by the Government or directly by donors. The
budget i s a useful public document relating the expenditures on specific projects to National
Programs. Revenue and expenditure records for the core budget are maintained through the
budget implementation system known as AFMIS (the Afghanistan Financial Management
Information System). Comprehensive reports on budgetary aggregates are producedmonthly and
are now available on the Ministry o f Finance's (MoF) website. This information i s generally
reliable but i s affected by lags inrecording provincial operations and weak reconciliation. There
are also uncertainties about the quality o f reported information on the external budget.           In
addition, fiscal risks which could arise from activities in state-owned enterprises and
municipalities are not effectively monitored because the financial infomation is unverified and
too limited for fiscal risk management inthese areas.

Policy-basedbudgeting (Performance Indicators 11-12)
7.    Some progress has been made toward the Government's key objective o f making the
national budget the central instrument o f policy and reform, but there are still constraints. The
full implications o fpolicy decisions, including for fiscal sustainability, are not taken into account
due to lack o f capacity in Government to develop policy, agree on trade-offs among investments
and recurrent costs, and matchresources with policies. Multi-year planningi s also constrained by
the complexity o f aid coordination.      Budgeting for recurrent and investment expenditures i s
carried out separately, raising the fiscal sustainability risks from recurrent cost implications o f
investments. The budget process is orderly and well understood within the Government, but it
needs to be linked more closely with the Government's strategy; more widely owned politically,
includingby Cabinet and the Parliament (when formed); and more reflective o f sector issues with
more engagement by sector agencies, provinces, and civil society.

Predictability and control in budgetexecution (Performance Indicators 13-21)
8.    Muchprogress has been made inimproving the implementationo f the budget (inparticular
with respect to the allotment process and cash management), but there is a need to enhance
predictability o f fimding for service delivery units. Line departments at the provincial level often
cannot predict with accuracy the funds that will be available to them because o f delays in
communicating with the provincial offices o f the M o F and the complexity o f the allotment
process; this has led to cash rationing at the discretion o f Treasury and the Moustufiats. Many
adjustments are made during the year, in particular through allotment transfers and use o f
contingencies. No infomation is available at the local level (e.g. primary schools) on actual
resources available.

9.    A particular weakness in the budget execution cycle is related to revenue collection. The
overall system i s not very effective as demonstrated by the large gap between revenues that could
be collected and actual collections. Inaddition, despite some progress with taxpayer registration,
the control framework on the revenue side remains weak.           Nevertheless, clarification o f the
legislation and strengthening o f financial management (banking arrangements to ensure a steady
flow o f collected revenues to the Treasury Single Account, TSA) have generated some
improvements inrevenueperformance.

10.    Much progress has been in implementing the control framework, as reflected by a
significant increase in the eligibility rate for reimbursements o f expenditures by the Afghanistan
Reconstruction Trust Fund (ARTF). There i s a division o f duties between the line ministries,
which approve expenditure, and the MoF, which keeps records and makes payments. These
arrangements include external operational support in Treasury and the review by the ARTF
Monitoring Agent. The controls also include effective cash management, allotment control on
budget uses, and reconciliations o f records o f the Treasury and the line ministries. Significant
weaknesses remain however, compounded by the lack o f internal audit to provide feedback on
performance and systems. Further improvements in the internal control framework are needed
both for payroll and non-payroll expenditures and for revenues. The recent approval o f a new
PFEM Law and planned financial regulations will further help strengthening the control
framework.

11. Most procurement at present is carried out under standards agreed with donors.
Procurement carried out under national rules, however, i s subject to an outdated regulatory
framework which lacks an institutional support adequate for effective enforcement. There i s
insufficient competition as the basis for contract awards and no guarantee of value for money.
The new Procurement Law will address these issues.

Accounting, recording, and reporting (Performance Indicators 22-25)
12.    Considerable improvements have been made in this area. The recording of expenditures
and revenues has been strengthened by partial computerization ofthe AFMIS, notably inthe M o F
(also piloted in two line ministries). Accountability o f line ministries and the M o F to Cabinet i s
supported by frequent reporting on budget implementation by the Government, but improving
accounting and reporting capacity in line ministries i s required. Significant progress has been
made toward consolidation o f bank accounts under a system o f TSA. However, much remains to
be done to improve standards of accounting, frequency o f bank reconciliation, and usefulness o f
reporting for line ministries' management.

External scrutiny and audit (Performance Indicators 26-28)
13.    Government accountability generally i s sought through the publication o f audited financial
statements on the state budget and specific donor funds. The Auditor General i s workmg to
international auditing standards with operational support from an audit advisor. However, the
final audit o f the 2003/04 annual statements has not yet beenreleased. Inaddition, the absence o f
a Parliament (before the elections held in September 2005) has limited the extent o f external
scrutiny o fthe budget and its performance.

Donorpractices (Donor Performance Indicators 1-3)
14.    Donors have mobilized a considerable amount o f support to Afghanistan's budget. In
particular, support to the Government's recurrent budget through the ARTF has been large and
predictable. However, direct budget support still accounts for only a very small percentage o f
total external assistance to Afghanistan, and the proportion of aid that i s managed under national
procedures remains low, with most aid executed by donors through their contractors. Financial
information provided by donors on donor-executed assistance activities, while reasonably
comprehensive, is subject to delays, usually relates to disbursements rather than actual
expenditures, and may be partial and suffer from inaccuracies and inconsistencies.

Impact on development outcomes
15.    The strengths and weaknesses o f Afghanistan's PFM system as summarized above have an
important impact on the country's development. First, the strong achievements in terms o f fiscal
discipline, cash control, and aggregate transparency have contributed to macroeconomic stability,
as well as to sustained external assistance. There i s need for more progress, however, in
broadening the comprehensiveness o f the Core Budget, in addition to efforts to improve the
monitoring of other fiscal activities, such as State-Owned Enterprises, municipalities, and
external assistance outside the core budget.         Second, weak sector strategies, inadequate
prioritization, and lack o f informationon results have made it difficult to allocate resources across
and within sectors appropriately. Third, weaknesses in procurement, insufficient financial data
given to line managers, and initial delays in making payments outside Kabul have been a
constraint on the efficiency o f public expenditures. These problems, along with difficulties in
getting specific data from donors on expenditures o f externally-implemented projects and a lack
o f realism inpublicly available budget numbers, have exacerbated the disconnect between public
expectations and actual delivery o f services.
