The project's overall development objective would be to enhance Chile's policy and
institutional innovation framework for competitiveness and improve the impact o f priority
innovation programs. More specifically, the project's objective is: (i)to strengthen the
Ministry of Economy's capacity on innovation and ensure its coherence with other policies
for competitiveness; (ii)strengthen CONICYT and improve the coherence, quality and
relevance o f research funding policy inChile; and (iii) stimulate technology transfer and the

A.      STRATEGIC CONTEXT AND RATIONALE

1. Countryand sector issues

Chile has been the fastest growing economy in Latin America since 1990 (Table 1). The
impressive performance o f the economy owes much to a strong institutional framework,
exemplary monetary policies and deep integration into the global economy. Due to strong growth
Chile has made notable progress towards the goal o f convergence with OECD economies. The
income gap, however, remains sizeable. Per capita income in Chile (adjusted for purchasing
power parity) i s currently less than 40 percent o f the OECD average leaving ample room for
further catch-up inrelative living standards.

The Asian financial crises o f the late 1990s revealed vulnerabilities o f the Chilean economy to
external shocks, in part due to shortcomings in productivity and economic diversification.
Despite progress inrecent decades, Chile remains highly dependent on the export o f unprocessed
natural resources, notably copper (Figure 1). Growth deceleration in 2006 has also raised
concerns that Chile may have started to exhaust previous sources o f growth. While there have
been concerns in the private sector o f a real effective exchange rate appreciation (16 percent
from 2003 to 2007), there i s little evidence that this has affected (non-copper) export growth
(IMF 2007) thus far. That said, this calls for further productivity increases in the (non-copper)
sector.

Chile will need fast and sustained growth to further reduce poverty, continue improving living
standards, and close the income gap with the lower tier of OECD countries by 2020. The
perception of sound macroeconomic management is now well entrenched. Chile is one of the
few sovereign borrowers in Latin America, along with Mexico and more recently Brazil and
Peru, to enjoy an investment-gradecredit rating. Hence, Chile i s in a favorable position to focus
on microeconomic reforms aimed at boosting the capacity of firms to sustain productivity growth
and develop new business opportunities. Evidence from economies that have successfully
diversified and added value to their natural resource base such as Australia and Finland suggests
that boosting Chile's capacity to innovate should be at the center of such reforms. Cross-country
analysis indicates that half of all differences in income and growth is attributable to differences
in total factor productivity (TFP), generally associated with technological development,
innovation, and the investment climate (see Chile, Development Policy Review, 2006). Chile's
total factor productivity has historically been relatively high, however during the 1990s, this
trendhas reversedand Chile's TFP has sharply decreased.

A potential explanation for the low levels of TFP is related to the microeconomic constraints to
institutional adjustment costs (Bergoeing and Repetto, 2003). However, as mentioned by the
Development Policy Review for Chile (2006), if the gap in TFP were due solely to
microeconomic obstacles, the inefficiencies would appear to be surprisingly large relative to the
US given the extensive microreforms that have taken place in Chile. The latter suggests that
innovation deserves to be a central issue due to its impact on raising firms' capacity to introduce
productivity-improving measures. Cohen and Levinthal (1989) argue that R&D is not only
crucial to generate new knowledge, but to enhance "absorptive capacity" (Le., the firm's ability
to use and benefit from existing information). Investments ininnovations that do not necessarily
involve R&D may also yield high dividends inthe mediumterm for Chile. Marimon (2008), for
example, indicates that innovations in process and organizational forms that are not accounted
for inofficial statistics have facilitated the growth of outward looking industriessuchas banking,
tourism and textiles inSpain.

2. Sector issues

In the area o f science, technology and innovation (STI), Chile has made gains over the last
decade but continues to lag substantially advanced economies with respect to most STI
indicators (Table 2). Chile's R&D spending per capita, full-time equivalent researchers as a
percent o f population, and patent activity significantly lag the efforts o f OECD countries, even
those with a natural resource base, in many cases by more than an order o f magnitude. The
differences are not due solely to income disparities; on some indicators Chile also falls behind
dynamic economies with lower GDP per capita such as China and India. Several factors explain
Chile's low performance relative to more advanced economies:

Chile 's policies and institutional JFameworkfor innovation is underdeveloped preventing an
eflcient use ofpublic support for innovation. Until recently, the country did not have a fully
articulated innovation policy with coherent and achievable objectives.                                                                                     The institutional
framework takes current shape more from historical precedent, rather than from explicit
decisions regarding the roles and functions of actors; in many cases, it i s fragmented, shows
overlaps and lacks clear accountability mechanisms. Diverse studies and assessments have
warned about the lack of policy coordination in Chile's national innovation system. Several
ministries -including the Ministries of Economy, Education, Planning, Agriculture, Transport
and Telecommunications, Public Works, and Foreign Affairs- have historically beeninvolved in
the formulation of STI policies and the design and management of programs (Box l),                                                                                                       but
cooperationbetweenactors has beenminimal.

As a result, Chile has had a wide array of uncoordinated policies and programs for R&D and for
technology diffusion with significant overlap between content and objectives. For instance,
coordination between Chile's two key implementing agencies--the Chilean Science and
Technology Council (CONICYT) and the Innovation promotion group (InnovaChile) of the
Chilean Development Agency (CORF0)--has traditionally been modest. Support for team-based
research, which could potentially have a high impact, is also fragmented among various
institutions that follow different principles and strategic priorities for allocating funding.
Fragmentation reduces the effectiveness of public expenditures, results in duplication and leads
to excessive diffusion of programs including to some areas with relatively low social rates of
return.The lack of explicit policy-making evaluation mechanisms has impededlearning, created
inertia and prevented change despite duplication and fragmentation. This i s an area for
significant improvement.

Need to build capacity in the Ministry of Economy (MoE) to design, manage and evaluate
innovation and microeconomic policies. Shortly after the presentationof the National Innovation
Strategy (volume 1) prepared by the National Innovation Council in February 2007, the


                                                                                                     8

Government formed an Inter-Ministerial Committee on Innovation tasked with the responsibility
o f formulating innovation policies, giving orientation to Chile's support system for innovation
and addressing problems that hinder the systems effectiveness, including fragmentation and
overlap. The Committee i s led by the MoE and comprises the Minister o f Finance, the Minister
o f Education, and other line ministries. The Committee has a Technical Secretariat attached to
the MoE. The Ministry is also tasked with the formulation, implementation, and evaluation o f
policies concerning markets regulation and small and medium size enterprises (SMEs). It will
need a strong capacity to set the policy agenda, monitor the impact o f innovation and
microeconomic policies and hold implementing agencies accountable for results, a capacity that
it currently does not possess. A major institutional strengthening effort is thus required to
enhance its capacity.

Chile's stock of human capital for innovation and technology management is low and
geographically concentrated. A major barrier to innovation in Chile is the insufficient level of
advanced human capital and disarticulation between skill production and the needs o f the
productive sector. This i s partially demonstrated by the low number of young researchers
working in private companies, but pertains also to the nature and quality o f the skills being
produced. Despite a strong increase in the number o f researchers in the last decade, the stock i s
still comparatively low (Figure 3). Furthermore, Chilean universities have a limited tradition o f
stimulating entrepreneurship or linkages with industry, and young researchers tend to stay in the
public sector, working either for government research institutes or universities. An additional
barrier to innovation in Chile is the lack o f mobility o f advanced human capital within the
country and modest international linkages. The existent stock o f advanced human capital i s
geographically concentrated inthe Santiago area, which reflects the lack o f a bridge between the
regions and the metropolitanarea.

Business Enterprise investment i s 45.8 percent of all R&D spending-a     reasonable percentage
by regional standards. Nonetheless, the existing evidence suggests that the private and public
R&D efforts are mostly isolated and parallel rather than complementary, impeding the
transformation of knowledge into productive innovation. There is a growing consensus that Chile
i s not close to deriving full commercial benefit from either the knowledge or the skills that its
publicly-financed research efforts are producing. Inrecent years, successful pilot initiatives for
public-private research partnerships have emerged in Chile. These initiatives are modest and
fragmented among CORFO, CONICYT, and FIA.

Inadequate mechanismsfor transferring technology to SMEs. Small and medium size enterprises
inChile account for roughly 95 percent ofthe total employment. Hence, SMEdevelopmentis at
the heart of enhancing Chile's innovative potential and TFP growth. Besides increasing R&D,
there i s a large potential for non-R&D driven TFP growth by enhancing the capacity of SMEs to
adopt existing technologies and improving organization and management. There is little
experience of collaborative technology development in Chile that is led by the private sector and
substantially funded by them. Through InnovaChile's programs, CORFO has supported the
formation of business networks for technology transfer but this initiative is not well articulated
with other programs nor is it tailored to the specific needs of firms in priority sectors of the
economy. Evidence from countries such as Spain and Ireland suggests that Chile would benefit
from a broad vision of innovation which includes the development of a coherent framework for
technology services targeting SMEs.

Weak incentives and insuficient support for technology based start-up companies. There has
been a wide range of initiatives implementedto foster the growth of new firms in sectors with

by start-up companies.

Governmentstrategy

Innovation is a top priority in Chile's competitiveness agenda. In November 2005 the Lagos
Government set up a high-level Commission to prepare the ground for the formulation o f a
national strategy for innovation and competitiveness. In its final report from March 2006 the
Commission recommended the establishment of a permanent National Innovation Council
responsible for strategy formulation and providing policy advice to the executive. InMay 2006,
President Michelle Bachelet formed such a Council with participation o f high-level experts on
STI and government representatives. The Council presented the first volume o f a National
Innovation Strategy in January 2007, which was endorsed by President Bachelet shortly
thereafter. The strategy underscores that becoming a knowledge economy means adding value to
sectors where Chile has demonstrated comparative advantages while supporting key emerging
sectors. The Council presented the second volume o f the National Innovation Strategy with more
detailed recommendations in February 2008. The two volumes were prepared on the basis of
consultations with relevant stakeholders and advice from international independent experts to
incorporate best international practices.

The first volume of the Innovation strategy defines strategic innovation goals for a 12-year
horizon and general principles to guide innovation policy.' Its key elements are to (i)build a
strong platform for human capital that will substantially expand tertiary education, especially in
technical areas, and will support the formation o f highly specialized human capital in STI; (ii)
enhance research capacity, with an emphasis on applied research; (iii) innovation within

new models to promote technology transfer; (iv) generate a pro-active culture o f innovation
within Chile's society; (v) set in place a robust governance structure for the innovation system;
and (vi) build regional STI capacity in areas linked to their economic specialization. A second
complementary volume o f the National Innovation Strategy will be issued shortly providing
more detailed guidance on innovation policies and the supporting institutional framework. The
Inter-ministerial Committee on Innovation will be responsible for the strategy's implementation
under the leadership o f the MoE. A second volume with more detailed recommendations for
implementingthe proposed National Innovation strategy was presented to President Bachelet in
February 2008.

3. Rationalefor Bankinvolvement

Over the last few years, the World Bank has assistedthe Government of Chile inthe innovation
area through policy advice (e.g., the preparation of a Strategy to Promote Innovative SMEs
(2004), an Investment Climate Assessment (2006), and the Development Policy Review (2006))
and support for two investment projects - the Millennium Science Initiative followed by the
Science for the Knowledge Economy Project. The latter, implemented by CONICYT, was
conceived as a two-stage APL since testing new instruments inthe innovation system involves a
learning and incubation process before they can be consolidated. The APL1 was satisfactorily
implementedand closed at the end of March 2007 as originally envisaged (see Annex 2.4 for a
summary of key results).

The Government of Chile has requested a new investment project to foster Innovation. The
project would build on the aforementioned APLl but would have a wider set of objectives to
accommodate Chile's more ambitious new policy vision. It would support key elements of the
national innovation strategy with a strong emphasis on building robust institutional foundations.
The Government regards the World Bank as a strategic partner due to its expertise in the
innovation area, knowledge of a wide range of international experiences such as OECD and
Asia, and the capacity to help adapt international practices and instrumentsto Chile's reality and
needs.

The proposedproject is fully aligned with the goals of the Country Partnership Strategy (CPS).
Inparticular, it will support the objective of converging with OECD income levels and living
standards by accelerating sustainable growth through increases in total factor productivity. The
proposedproject would have strong synergies with other Bank interventions aimed at increasing
Chile's innovation capacity, inparticular the Tertiary Education Financefor Results APLl.

4. Higher level objectivesto which the projectcontributes

The proposedproject would contribute to the high level objective of increasingthe international
competitiveness of Chile's economy and stimulating sustainedeconomic growth andjob creation
by adding value to productionand diversifying exports. It would do this by strengthening Chile's
capacity to compete as a knowledge economy (see Annex 3 for a causality tree linking the high
level objective to project activities). The project is also fully aligned with the innovation pillar
of the government's national development strategy.

4. Lessonslearnedand reflectedin the project design

The World Bank has supported several projects related to institutional strengthening and STI
development in Latin America and other regions (Annex 2). The proposed project builds on the
lessons learned from those operations as well as from analytical work and experiences o f OECD
countries. Lessons from the implementation and achievements o f the APL I(Science for the
Knowledge Economy) were also taken into account (Annex 2.4).

Component 1:Institutionalstrengthening: Strong political commitment and the creation of
consensus among the various stakeholders are critical to the successful implementation o f
institutional reforms. The first component o f the project takes these lessons into account usingas
a main input a consultation process within the Ministry regarding its new vision, mission and
structure, which involves all the key stakeholders (Ministry's units and departments) in such a
way that there is ownership o f the reform process. The consultation process also involves
institutions under the tutelage or oversight o f the MoE, senior authorities within the government,
and the National Innovation Council. Broad-based consultations will ensure the sustainability
and coherence o f the institutional strengtheningprocess. In addition, the Minister of Economy i s
providing leadership, clear strategic direction, and commitment to the process o f institutional
strengthening, which are critical for success.

Component 2: StrengtheningChile's science base:             A lesson learned in the implementation
o f other STI related projects in Chile (the Science for the Knowledge Economy and the
Millennium Science Initiative) and throughout the region is that competition-based research
funding has several positive effects: it encourages quality research, it rewards the most
productive researchers and therefore makes the maximum use o f their talent, and it increases the
efficiency of research funding. At the same time, it favors quality institutions and channels
resources towards groups with a potential. This lesson will be reflected in the preparation o f
CONICYT's strategic plan (subcomponent 2.1) and inthe implementation o f subcomponent 2.2,
which will ensure that all research grants will be awarded on transparent and competitive
selection processes with strong participation o f internationalpeer-reviewers.

Component 3: Strengtheningtechnology transfer institutionsand practices: International
experience indicates that schemes to support productive innovation often take on multiple goals



                                                  20

and fragment into a plethora o f highly specific subsidiary schemes in response to small but
effective lobbying by special interest groups. The strategic goals are lost and firms looking for
assistance are confused by a fragmented and complex offer. Also, specific subsidiary schemes
lendthemselves to easier capture. The proposed project takes these lessons into account and will
help bring strategic coherence to public support for "start up o f knowledge companies" and
technology transfer initiatives.

Sector-specific technology centers can play an important role intransferring technology to SMEs
firms as illustrated by the experiences o f Spain and Italy. Such centers have accelerated the pace
at which modern technologies are adopted by small and medium enterprises. Inmost cases, the
public sector has played a key role in facilitating their initial development and in bringing
together complementary private sector support. Private sector participation i s crucial to ensure
that these centers met the needs o f the productive sector. That said, some public assistance for
such technology centers i s justified because they provide some quasi-public goods, for example,
by helpingovercome potential coordination failures or conducting pre-competitive research that
can be shared with all SMEs within a productive cluster.

Finally, a lesson learned from the OECD countries and the Asian tiger economies i s the need to
ensure a balance between neutral and selective research support and innovation policies. Chile
has a tradition of sector neutrality when providing support for advanced human capital and
innovation. As i s evidenced by countries like Australia, Finland, Taiwan, and South Korea,
selective innovation and cluster policies have played a role in stimulating growth by tailoring
government support to sector specific market failures and areas o f revealed competitive
advantage. It is difficult for a small open economy to develop research capacity o f excellence
across all areas, which calls for greater focus and some selectivity connected to the productive
sectors. This new approach i s reflected in Chile's National Innovation Strategy (volume 1 and
volume 2) and incorporated in the project's emphasis on providing support for innovation in
areas o f strategic importance to Chile's economy (Component 2 and Component 3).

At the same time, experience shows that selective policies come with the risk of capture and
government failure in the design process. The monitoring and evaluation strategies supported
under subcomponents 1.3, 2.3, and 3.3, including the establishment o f a Monitoring and
Evaluation Unit at the MoE, are designed to minimize the risk o f disarticulation by
systematically evaluating the impact and effectiveness o f public funded instruments to stimulate
innovation.

5. Alternatives consideredand reasons for rejection

The project was originally conceived as the second phase o f a two-stage operation of the project
Science for Knowledge Economy (APL). See Annex 2.4 for a summary o f the results o f the
phase one o f the project. The design was justified considering that piloting new instruments
involves a learning process. The second phase would provide the opportunity to scale up and
adjust project components. Important changes have taken place since the time the APL Iwas
designed that have paved the way for an acceleration o f policy, institutional, and program
reforms. As noted earlier, a National Innovation Council was set up inMay 2006 and a National
Innovation Strategy (volume 1) has been produced taking a comprehensive look at the national



                                                21

innovation system. As a consequence, the Chilean authorities have widened the scope of Bank
support from its original focus on CONICYT to a system-wide approach including C O W 0 and
the MoE, which will have primary responsibility for coordinating innovation policy.

Continuing with a second phase of the APL project would have constituted a limited approach
and would not have respondedto the emerging needs of the Government of Chile. As currently
envisioned, the project will address some of the key challenges identified by the National
Innovation Council, starting with a strengthening of the MoE that will lead and oversee the
innovation strategy's implementation. Such institutional strengthening constitutes the first
priority to enhance Chile's national innovation system and would not be achieved through a
second phase of the APL project. In this context, a specific investment loan will provide the
necessary framework for a comprehensive approach involving the modernization of different
institutions and the reform andexpansion of awider range of innovation programs.

D. APPRAISAL SUMMARY

1. Economicandfinancialanalyses

Total factor productivity is significantly increased by productive innovation, which is a key
source of economic and employment growth. Empiricalliterature, as shown inAnnex 9, provides
estimates of social returns to R&Dthat range from around 30 percent to 90 percent. Inparticular,
Ledennan and Maloney (2003) suggest that social rates of return for investment in R&D for
mediumincome countries such as Chile are around 55 percent.

However, there exist failures in the market for science, technology and R&D. These market
failures stem from certain characteristics of innovation such as: (i)inappropiability of
knowledge; (ii)partial intangibility of the assets involved in innovation; (iii)information
failures; and (iii)
                  network failures among researchers, institutions and the private sector, meaning
that individuals and institutions see a higher risk than benefit in collaborating, given the
fungibility of knowledge.

Market failures lead to a mismatch of social and private returns. In this context, market failures
and misalignment of profits suggest that there is a significant scope for public interventions. The
proposed project components are designed to capacitate and enable strategic key actors in the
Chilean national innovation systemto target market failures more efficiently.

                     Annex 1: Country and Sector or ProgramBackground


Chile's economic growth has been impressive. Based on a strong institutional framework,
structural reforms, supportive fiscal and monetary policies and trade liberalization, Chile has
almost doubled its per capita income since 1990. Chile's growth was export-driven, and Chile's
main export goods are commodities. In the last 10 years, primary commodities were about 84
percent o f the total Chilean exports. Inthe same time-frame, Chilean exports more than doubled
invalue. Besides this enormous, resource-based growth, main challenges remain on the way to
convergence with the OECD.

First, income inequality is still very high. Inequality is persistent, as highas inthe most unequal
countries in Latin America and higher than in OECD countries. Chilean inequality is almost
twice that o f advanced economies. Second, the slowdown in growth during the last half decade
has raised concerns that Chile may have exhausted its source o f growth. Total factor productivity
(TFP) declined rather than increase in Chile. Given that cross-country analysis shows that
differences in TFP account for half o f all differences in income and growth, this tendency i s
worrisome2. A potential explanation for the low levels o f TFP i s related to the microeconomic
impediments to institutional adjustment costs (Bergoeing and Repetto, 2003). However, as
mentioned by the Development Policy Review for Chile (2006), if the gap in TFP were due
solely to adjustment costs, the inefficiencies would appear to be surprisingly large relative to the
US given the extensive micro reforms that have taken place in Chile. The latter suggests that
innovation deserves to be a central issue due to its impact on raising firms' capacity to introduce
productivity-improving measures. In this context, Chile has focused on enhancing its
performance inthe innovation area.

President Lagos appointed a first innovation council in 2005, which then, under the current
President o f the Republic, Michelle Bachelet, was transformed into the National Council of
Innovation for Competitiveness. This Council publisheda white paper on innovation that present
first steps towards a national strategy for innovation in January 2007. The strategy has a long-
term horizon and i s comprehensive in its approach. Shortly after the government had approved
the strategy in February 2007, it formed an inter-ministerial cabinet on innovation tasked with
the responsibility of formulating Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) policies             and
coordinating Chile's support system for innovation. The cabinet i s led by the Ministry o f
Economy and comprises the Ministries o f Finance and Education and other line ministries. The
Ministry o f Economy functions as a secretariat to the committee and is responsible for
implementing its decisions in coordination with other line ministries. Furthermore, a bill to
regulate the permanent operation o f the institutional framework for innovation was presented to
Congress in2007.

Even though Chile has shown the political will to increase productive innovation, there are
institutional and microeconomic inefficiencies in the policy field o f innovation related both to a
fragmented institutional framework for innovation and to a more neutral research and
development policy.

The current innovation system is characterized by a plentitude of actors and institutions. More
than a decade o f experimentationwith new modes of public financing for research has brought
concrete benefits to the Chilean National Innovation System.            However, this has led to
fragmentation in the delivery o f government support for innovation, which in turn renders
policy-coordination and long-term planning difficult. There are significant agency coordination
failures, especially between C O W 0 and CONICYT. Many funds and programs are not
sufficiently differentiated in their objectives, rationale and types o f outcome they want to
achieve. Monitoring and evaluation o f project and program outputs has not been sufficiently
thorough. Consequently, it has often preventedimprovementsto funding practice.

There has been a tendency for each major agency to develop its own response to all problems,
resulting in a fragmentation o f the public support system. Different institutions often cater to a
similar client base and seek to overcome comparable market failures, but follow different
principles and strategic priorities for allocating funding. The fragmentation o f public funds can
impede overall effectiveness, preclude potential economies o f scale and scope and creates
unnecessary overlap in the implementation o f programs. Fragmentation also discourages the
achievement o f critical mass inkey research areas and may diminish incentives for the formation
o f multi-disciplinary research teams. This i s clearly detrimental to the efficient allocation o f
scarce budgetary resources. The existing division o f labor among the institutions forming the
N I S is almost entirely based on the type o f beneficiary and does not yet reflect policy objectives
and rationale. Support, such as for team-based research, i s thus very limited in his impact. An
example o fthese inter-agency coordination failures exists with regardto pre-competitive projects
promoted in InnovaChile and FONDEF and, with regard to centers o f excellence in scientific
research promoted by the Millennium Science Initiative and FONDAP. As part o f its mandate,
the newly formed Inter-ministerial Committee on Innovation will foster collaboration across
agencies o f the NIS to resolve the inefficiencies stemming from problems such as gaps,
fragmentation, and overlap.

A slow move towards development and implementation of intelligent policies for selectivity in
research funding. Chile has traditionally followed a neutral research and development policy.
Experiences from successful innovators such as Finlandand Australia show that a certain degree
of selectivity is crucial in order to create a critical mass and to prevent "a too thin spreading o f
thejam". The white paper published by the National Council for Innovation recognizes the need
for selective funding given the low level o f overall investment in innovation. However,
resistance in certain stakeholder groups against a more selective approach i s only dwindling
slowly. The new National Innovation Strategy seeks to optimize the contributions o f different
agencies and actors, through, inter alia, intelligent policies for balancing selectivity and
neutrality on innovation support activities.

The existing institutional and microeconomic inefficiencies inthe policy field of innovation have
contributed to the low output in innovation, particularly a low and concentrated rate o f private
innovation inthe country, which could partially explain the low TFP level for Chile.

This annex outlines the monitoring and evaluating (M&E) module for the Chile: Promoting
Innovation for Competitiveness Project. This objective is to: (i)          monitor the project's progress
towards its Development Objective; (ii)      provide informationon needed corrective action during
implementation; and (iii)collect lessons learned on the effectiveness of the proposed project
components.

The M&Emodule will build on activities under sub-components 1.3,2.3 and 3.3, which supports
the establishment or strengthening of monitoring and evaluation capacities including the design
of a comprehensive "Monitoring and Evaluation Unit" for innovation and PSD development
(sub-component 1.3). The unit will provide technically sound information on the status and
trends in key indicators o f STI and competitiveness; perform international benchmarking of the



                                                    41

aforementioned indicators; monitor and analyze innovation performance and competitiveness
within key economic clusters; and evaluate the impact and effectiveness of public policies and
publicly funded instruments. This effort will be closely coordinated with the monitoring and
evaluation strategies that will be performed by CONICYT, C O W 0 and the Secretariat of the
CNIC.

The unit would maintain a small professional staff responsible for coordinating the collection of
statistics and updating project indicators incollaboration with CONICYT and C O W 0 and other
actors of the NIS. Hence, the project-by design-provides a strong basis for accessing updated,
reliable information on the sector.

Duringpreparation a causality tree approachwas introduced inorder to:


    0    Establish a coherent theory of change for the project by articulating the intermediate
        mechanismsand results through which inputsand activities are expected to contribute to
         desired outcomes
    0    Makerelationships betweensub-componentsandhigher order objectives explicit so they
         can be monitored, validated and managed
    0   Provide a framework for selecting indicators at the sub-component, component and sector
         level

Quantitative and qualitative indicators were selected based on the framework provided by the
causality tree. Emphasis was given to creating clear, balanced, relevant and measurable
indicators. To the extent possible, indicators were designed to build on existing reporting
structures inorder to ensure a flow of information from the early phases of the project.

R&Dinvestment,patents, firms engagedininnovationactivities, andthe impact ofthese changes
on total factor productivity can be observed only after prolonged interventions. The selected
indicators reflect intermediate outcomes on the way toward these longer term goals.

                              Annex 4: Detailed Project Description


The project's main development objective would be to enhance Chile's policy and institutional
innovation framework for competitiveness and improve the impact o f priority innovation
programs. It will achieve the above through three components: (i)strengthening the policy and
institutional capacity o f the Ministry of Economy (MoE) on innovation and ensuring its
coherence with other competitiveness policies; (ii)  strengtheningCONICYT and improving the
coherence, quality and relevance of research funding in Chile; and (iii)stimulating technology
transfer and the creation of new technology based enterprises through InnovaChile's programs.

The MoE would coordinate and oversee overall project implementation in coordination with the
Inter-ministerial Cabinet on Innovation and would be responsible for implementing Component
I.CONICYTandCOW0wouldimplementcomponentsI1and111,respectivelyinaccordance
with performance agreements negotiated withthe MoE.

Component 1: Strengthening the MoE capacity on innovation policy and its coherence with
other competitiveness policies.

This component would enhance the MoE's institutional capacity to formulate, monitor, and
evaluate innovation policies in accordance with the guidelines o f the National Innovation
Strategy. In addition, it would support selected elements o f the broader Strategic Plan to
strengthenthe MoE, which i s under advanced preparation, inorder to foster consistency between
innovation policies and other policies seeking to enhance Chile's competitiveness. This would
also ensure that the innovation related units o f the MoE operate within a solid and adequate
institutional framework promoting sustainability o f reforms. The design o f this component
benefited from an extensive consultation process with internal and external stakeholders,
including institutions under the tutelage o f the MoE. This process would continue during project
implementation. The component would comprise two subcomponents.

Subcomponent 1.1. Strengthening the MOE capacity toformulate and implementpolicies

To support the MoE's capacity to formulate and implement innovation policies, this sub-
component would strengthen the MoE's Innovation Unit and its linkages with others units within
the MoE to both ensure the coherence of innovation policies and exploit the synergies between
innovation activities and other competitiveness policies o f the MoE. It will also assist with
improvementsto selected administrative/managementsupport services that are necessary for the
innovation unit and other innovation related units to function efficiently.

In particular, the sub-component would comprise the following activities: (i)review of the
organizational structure o f the MoE with a view to ensure the coherence and sustainability o f the
innovation activities with other competitiveness policies of the MoE; (ii)development o f a
performance-based management system that would link budgetary allocations to performance
outcomes and would help the MoE management monitor performance outcomes (starting with
the innovation unit); (iii)development of a human resources management system that would
allow the MoE management to simplify and automate HR related procedures and monitor more
systematically the quality o f its human resources (starting with the Innovation Unit); (iv)


                                                  49

development of management information systems and acquisition of related equipment to
support the above activities; (v) training of staff of the MoE on innovation policies and related
competitiveness areas, and on areas related to performance management systems and human
resource management system; (vi) consultations with stakeholders to prepare and facilitate the
successful implementation of the above activities; and (vii) other technical studies as might be
necessary to accomplish the above objectives. The subcomponent would finance consultant
services, managementinformationsystems, ICT equipment, surveys, workshops, andtraining.

The revised performance based-management system and human resource management system
would be tested and applied at the Innovation Unit and the Monitoring and Evaluation Unit (see
below). It i s expected that these would be gradually extended to other units of the MoE. The
performance based-management would follow broad policies and guidelines issued by DIPRES.
Similarly, the new human resourcemanagement system would be fully coherent with regulations
and policy guidelines on public employment.

Subcomponent 1.2:Establishment of a Monitoring and Evaluation Unit

This sub-component would support the establishment of a Monitoring and Evaluation Unit
within the MoE directly reporting to the Minister. The unit will be responsible for (i)providing
both technically sound information on the status and trends in key indicators of innovation, in
particular, and related competitiveness areas; (ii) performing international benchmarking of the
aforementioned information or indicators; and (iii)evaluating the impact and effectiveness of
policies and publicly funded instrumentsto stimulate innovation and competitiveness.The unit's
findings will support the learning process and provide feedback into policy-making. It will be
developed in coordination with other public agencies, inter alia CORFO, CONICYT and the
Secretariat of the National Innovation Council.

Inparticular, the subcomponent would support the following activities: (i)design of the unit's
structure; (ii)design of methodologies to construct innovation indicators according to best
international standards; (iii)
                             evaluations and surveys; (iv) development of necessary management
information systems and acquisition of related equipment; (v) training of staff to conduct the
above activities; and (vi) seminars to disseminate the unit's analytical work. The subcomponent
would finance consultant services, management information systems, ICT equipment, surveys,
workshops, and training.

Subcomponent 1.3:Project Coordination Unit

This subcomponent would support the Project Coordination Unit within the Ministry of
Economy, which is responsible for implementing component I,supervising overall project
finances and technical outputs, coordinating activities with C O W 0 and CONICYT, preparing
reports to the Government and the Bank, and contracting auditing of the project. The
subcomponent would finance consultant services, operating costs, training, dissemination
activities, and minor office equipment.

Component2: StrengtheningChile's sciencebase

The White Paper ofthe National Innovation Council identifies CONICYT as a key implementing
agency inChile's public support system for innovation. Fostering basic and appliedresearchand
the formation of human capital for innovation are its core responsibilities. In addition,
CONICYT would promote initiatives to enhance university-industry linkages in coordination
with other institutions such as CORFO. To achieve these goals, CONICYT needs to sharpen its
vision and improve its performance and accountability in key areas. This component would
strengthen CONICYT by helping the institution formulate a strategic plan with clear medium-
term goals aligned with the national innovation strategy, develop and implement a coherent
researchsupport policy, and increase its capacity to design and evaluate programs. At the end of
the project, CONICYT would be organized in a way that responds closely to its core mandate
and would have increased capacity to design, overseef and learn from the implementation of its
programs. The Inter-ministerial Committee on Innovation and the National Innovation Council
would guide CONICYT inthe implementation of this component.

Subcomponent 2.1 -Sharpening CONICYT's role within the National Innovation System

This subcomponent would lay the foundations for CONICYT to become a more effective
institutionwithin the national innovation system. It would support consultancies that would help
CONICYT empirically evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of its recent performance, sharpen
its vision, define clear program goals in line with the guidelines of the national innovation
strategy, and prepare a medium-term plan to achieve those goals. The medium-term strategic
plan would cover several dimensions, including changes to programs as well as to its
organizational structure, processes, and human resource capacity. In addition, it would support
the dissemination of the strategic plan and other activities (training, advisory services and
management information systems) to help CONICYT initiate its implementation. The strategic
plan will be prepared and implemented in consultation with the Inter-ministerial Committee on
Innovation andthe Ministry of Education.

The subcomponent would finance consultant services, training, management information
systems, and workshops.

Subcomponent 2.2 - Support for consolidation and reformulation of funding for research and
advancedhuman capital

In recent years, CONICYT has made important advances in broadening its support for
knowledge creation, advanced human capital formation, and innovation. At the same time, the
period of experimentation with new instruments has been done with insufficient coordination
with other agencies (especially CORFO), with suboptimal monitoring and evaluation data.
CONICYT currently maintains eight major funding programs with at least thirty-nine
subprograms. Some of these pursue similar or even identical objectives, and few have sufficient
measure of program efficiency, effectiveness, and impact.

The second subcomponent would support the consolidation of programs and the reform of
funding modalities in ways consistent with CONICYT's medium-termstrategic goals. The top



                                               51

priority for the consolidation will be: (i)to expand funding to programs that demonstrate
progress in fulfilling CONICYT's specific goals within the framework of the National
Innovation Strategy; and (ii)to reduce unnecessary overlap and administrative inefficiency
across the multiplicity of programs. Once the strategic plan is finalized, CONICYT will adjust its
funding to better achieve these goals. Within the strategic plan, this component would support
the improvement of group research funding (including supporting infrastructure) and the
consolidation and/or reform of other priority programs that might have emerged from the
strategic plan. The specific programs to be financed would be agreed between CONICYT (in
consultation with the Ministry of Education and the Ministry of Economy) and the Bank within
four months of the completion of the Strategic Plan or at an earlier date, and the technical
guidelines of the programs to be supported would be agreed between CONICYT and the Bank
and incorporated inthe Operational Manual.

To this end, the subcomponent could finance fellowships and competitive grants (subprojects) to
research teams that could include training of researchers, scholarships for students conducting
researchin supportedteams, researchmaterials, researchequipment of excellence, dissemination
activities, participation in national and international research activities, and travel to participate
in conferences and other training activities related to the research. Progress in the adjustment
and consolidation would bejudged against an existing baseline of programs and purposes, some
of which are duplicative.

Subcomponent2.3 -Strengthening of CONICYT's monitoringandevaluation capacity

The third subcomponent would improve CONICYT's monitoring and evaluation capacity. The
subcomponent would assist CONICYT reach international best practice in evaluating its
programs and in monitoring indicators related to national research capacity and the formation of
advanced human capital. Regional and international data would be gathered to monitor
distribution of research capacities within the country and in relation to other economies.
Monitoring and evaluation indicators would be usedas inputs for decision-making by CONICYT
and other policy-makers within the national innovation system. The above would be achieved by
expanding the capacities of CONICYT's Department of Studies. Staff would be trained,
methodologies for collecting monitoring and evaluation indicators would be developed or
adapted, the indicators themselves would be collected and disseminated, evaluations would be
implemented, and supporting management information systems would be developed. The
Department of Studies will work very closely and exchange information with other monitoring
unitsofthe national innovation systemto ensure complementarity.

The subcomponent would finance analytical work, surveys, staff training, training materials,
disseminationactivities, and managementinformation systems to accomplish the set objectives.

Component 3: Fosteringtechnology transfer and new ventures

The component would strengthen InnovaChile (CORF0)-led programs that seek to stimulate the
start-up of knowledge intensive businesses and the adoption of higher value technologies by
enterprises, with particular attention to SMEs. It would achieve the above through three
subcomponents: (i)upgrading new technology based enterprise development, (ii)strengthening



                                                 52

technology extension and transfer services for enterprises, in particular SMEs, and (iii)
strengtheningInnovaChile's monitoring and evaluation capacity.

Subcomponent 3.1 - Fostering New Ventures

A wide range of initiatives have been implementedto foster the growth of new technology based
firms including the development o f university based incubators and early stage funds. Their
impact, however, has not been too successful because o f a range o f design and implementation
gaps.

This subcomponent would seek to enhance the articulation o f existing programs and fill gaps in
regard to the incubation o f innovative businesses by developing and implementing a
benchmarking scheme for incubators. This would involve the certification o f incubators and
training o f their staff and CORFO's staff responsible for managing and monitoring the
incubation network. It i s expected that the benchmarking model would distinguish among
different categories o f incubators (e.g., incubators that support export oriented businesses or
international incubators, incubators with a regional or sectoral focus, and others with a more
national focus). It would also assist CORFO in the development and upgrading o f business
accelerators that will "accelerate" the entry o f domestic firms inthe international market.Finally,
it will provide technical assistance to review other support instruments for start ups and design
new instruments to support early stage finding of knowledge-based firms, ensuring close
articulation with other programs to promote new ventures.

To achieve the above, the subcomponent would: (i)design a benchmarking and certification
scheme for incubators; (ii)provide training to help implement the new system; (iii)provide
matching grants (subprojects) for incubators and business accelerators to upgrade their capacity
and become certified; and (iv) carry out analytical work to design or redesign complementary
instrumentsto foster technology start ups (for example, early stage funds).

Itwould finance consultant services and training, and competitive matching grants for incubators
subject to the presentation o f satisfactory business plans. Eligible grant expenditures would
include consultant services, training, and minor equipment.

Subcomponent 3.2 -Strengthening technology transfer and extension

There i s very little experience o f collaborative technology development in Chile led and
substantially funded by the private sector. This subcomponent would seek to enhance
enterprises' technological capabilities through two complementary instruments in order to take
into account the heterogeneity o f these enterprises. The first would develop at least two industrial
technology centers oriented to the priority clusters with a critical mass o f enterprises, in
particular SMEs; and the second would develop a national scheme for technology extension that
seeks to reach a more diverse group o f enterprises, inparticular SMEs.

On the one hand, this subcomponent would foster the development and upgrading o f
collaborative industrial technology centers linked to priority clusters, as defined in the national
innovation strategy. This could build on the work done in consortia financed under the previous
project (APL I)and aim to encourage centers that are led and funded mainly by the firms


                                                  53

benefiting. Activities would include: (i)promotion o f a collaborative approach across the cluster;
(ii)seedthedevelopment ofatleast2 centersthroughjointfundingofinvestment inspecialist
equipment, skills transfer and operating practices in international benchmarks of best practice;
(iii)establish the governance structure within the framework of a clear business plan; (iv)
develop priority technology programs relevant to firms needs; and (v) monitor and evaluate
progress and identify other priority clusters for the establishment o f similar centers.

In parallel, this sub-component would accelerate the development of a national technology
extension scheme. The present situation i s o f a plethora o f small initiatives that are inadequately
targeted and confusing to both administrators and beneficiaries. A review has been completed
and a strategic direction has been selected with priority being given to working with those
medium sized firms which are willing to upgrade their productivity but need assistance to do it
efficiently. Activities would include: (i)a call for competitive bids to select management and
delivery partners o f the technology extension networks/centers; (iii)    develop detailed programs
with selected partners; (iv) market the program and attract firms on a shared cost basis for
relevant projects; (v) promote outcomes o f interventions to alert other firms to the benefits o f
innovation strategies; and (vi) review experience and identify potential to further expand the
networks inthe mediumterm.

Insum, the following activities would be supported: (i)carry out analytical studies to designthe
guidelines to select the technology centers with a cluster focus and those with a multisectoral
focus ("extension scheme"); (ii)dissemination o f the program prior to its launch and during
implementation to show results; (iii)   training; and (iv) provision o f competitive matching grants
(subprojects) to legal entities that seek to establish or upgrade technology centers and technology
extension networks according to the guidelines mentioned initem (i).

Eligible grant expenditures would include consultant services, domestic and overseas training,
equipment, small works to adapt existing buildings, and compensation of the manager and key
personnel on a declining basis. Based on successful international experiences, the Board o f the
technology centers would be private-sector led and comprise substantial private sector
participation and the sponsors o f the centers would make substantial cash and in-kind

                          Annex 9: Economicand Financial Analysis


This annex focuses on the economic rationale on which the World Bank support to promote
innovation in Chile i s based. Firstly, the value o f productive innovation for economic growth i s
explained. Secondly, there i s an explanation o f the market failures that require public
interventions. Thirdly, an analysis o f empirical information pertaining to returns to investments
in science, technology and innovation at the macro-level follows. Finally, it addresses the
specific market failure that each intervention seeks to remedy.

1.Value of research,development,science and innovationfor economic growth

Economic literature has extensively documented the role o f technological innovation as a key
source o f sustainable income and employment growth (De Ferranti et al, 2003, De Ferranti, et al.,
2002). The society's ability to produce and commercialize scientific and technological
knowledge i s critical for gains in productivity, global competitiveness, and economic growth.
Countries that had invested in innovation early now have economies with a strong capacity to
innovate and turn knowledge into business conditional to having a favorable business, regulatory
and institutional environment and macroeconomic stability. Hence, this project aims to
strengthen the national framework for domestic R&D and knowledge absorptive capabilities so
that Chile can generate cutting edge innovations and be in a better position to take advantage o f
the existing global stock o fknowledge.

2. Rationale for public policy intervention

Market failures inthe market for science and technology and a misalignment of profits prevent
innovation from occurring at a socially efficient level. Public investments related to innovation
are warranted inorder to decrease these market failures and to align profits better. For Chile, the
following market failures are particularly relevant.


    0  Insuficient appropiability of knowledge. Knowledge i s a public good that quickly
       escapes from the hands o f its creator. Firms that invest in knowledge generation cannot
        absorb all the benefits resulting from their activities. This reduces the incentive to invest
       both inthe development o f new products and processes.


    0  Failures of information. Information i s not available due to the disadvantageous
       combination o f high costs to produce it and a low possibility o f retaining its economic
       benefit. However, information i s essential for many agents o f the system, has high social
       benefits and i s necessary for innovation activities.


    0  Intangibility of assets. Often, assets generated by innovation are intangible such as
       education or training in the use o f new technologies. Therefore, many investments in
       innovation cannot or only insufficiently provide collateral. In financial markets such as
       Chile, this mainly affects technology-based SMEs with regard to the gap between angel
       investors' capital and the larger tranches o f investmentfrom venture capital funds.




                                                 67

    0  Networkfailures. Many technology services follow rules o f economies o f scale. Effective
       resources needto be concentrated ina manner beyond the capacity o f the individual firm,
       thus creating the need for coordination. There is also a need for collaboration among
       institutions and firms for innovation diffusion and application. The cost o f bringing
       together and coordinating the various agents and institutions involved in the production
       o f knowledge i s relatively highand no single agent has the incentives to bear those costs,
       since the benefits o f such coordination are not appropriable and companies fear "free-
       riding" from their competitors.


    0  Tacit Knowledge. Most knowledge i s not easily codified and hence can only be
       transmitted through interactions between individuals. This characteristic o f knowledge
       provides the rationale for the facilitation o f interactions among researchers and users.


    0  There exists misalignment of profits. As suggested by Griliches (1992) and others, the
       social rate o f return on R&D expenditures i s often three times as large as the private rate
       o f return. Ledermanand Maloney estimate the average social rate o f return for innovation
       to be at 55 percent. Misalignment between social and private returns to investment in
       innovation lead to a socially sub-optimal outcome, or, inother words to a situation where,
       demand and supply meet at a non-pareto-optimal equilibrium. This suggests that there is
       significant scope for public interventions to align social and private returns."
       Furthermore, there exists an inter-temporal delay inprofits. Benavente, De Gregorio, and
       Nuiiez (2005) discovered that private R&D investment leads to a more than 54 percent
       return, thus being more than three times larger than the returnobtained for capital. But,
       due to learning effects, R&D expenditure causes contemporaneous negative impacts on
       firms.

3. Empiricalevidence on the importanceof innovationinvestment

The evidence for positive returns to R&D is abundant inmore mature economies. An analysis of
59 studies from OECD countries found average private rates o f return to research between 10
and 20 percent at both the firm and industry level. Significant positive spillovers were also
found, leading to social rates o f return ranging between 20 and 50 percent (Cameron 1998 in
OECD 2000).

Jones and Williams (1998) review estimates of the economic gains from firm expenditures on
R&D for new products and production techniques. These calculations greatly simplify the
innovation process-for instance disregarding the company's existing absorptive capacity prior to
the measured R&D expenditure-and necessitate assumptions of the time lag of benefits.
However, they help quantify the potential of R&D. First, Jones and Williams note that in
regressionswith firm-level data, private returns cluster around 10 to 15 percent, but can range to
more than 30 percent (see also Hall 1995). Reviewing social returns, they find that estimates
average about 28 percent when only the effects of R&D from one's own industry are included.
Social returns average over 90 percent according to the broadest measure, which includes the
effects of product R&D in one industry on productivity in other industries. This compares to
averagereal stock marketreturns of roughly 7 percent.

4. Economicrationalefor the project'sinterventionsat the microlevel

Individual investors and researchers do not reap the benefits o f knowledge that spills over to the
rest o f society. The project components attempt to deal with these market failures by reducing
the costs and risks associated with innovation, thereby increasing private return. The components
enhance the policy and institutional framework for innovation and thus improve the impact o f
priority innovation programs and support key interfaces in the innovation system. The project
assists the Chilean government in creating the necessary framework to combat market failures
allowing researchers and business individuals to carry out knowledge-generating activities that
would not have been undertaken otherwise. Given Chile's ongoing involvement in innovation
policy, it addresses microeconomic inefficiencies o f the N I S directly by creating the capacity to
carry out a successful, relevant and dynamic innovation policy which i s based on Chile's
particular chances and needs. Given the dynamic and changing nature o f innovation, the ability
to adapt any innovation policy to new challenges i s crucial and requires special attention.

Component1: Strengtheningthe MinistryofEconomy

To tackle the market failures discussed inthe section 2 o f this annex, sound government policies
and institutions are critical. The Chilean government is developing an institutional structure that
allows policy formulation, implementation, supervision and evaluation by creating the Inter-
ministerial Committee o f Innovation for Competitiveness. The Ministry o f Economy i s
responsible for providing the technical support to the Committee to ensure its effective
functioning, for which reason the Ministry has created an Innovation Unit. Component 1 would
foster activities which strengthenthe institutional capacity o f the Ministry to tackle this new task.
This would include a review of the Ministry's institutional structure, the modernization of the
planning and results-management systems, and a strengthening o f its human resources. In
addition, the establishment o f the Monitoring and Evaluation Unit will inform policy-making
helping to improve the impact o fpublic interventions on innovation. Inaddition, the Government
has established an independent National Innovation Council.

Component2: StrengtheningCONICYT

This component will restructure the support for team-research by integrating and establishing
common guidelines for programs. It will also tackle imperfect information by emphasizing
support for projects in priority areas o f the Chilean economy. By strengthening the mobility o f
advanced human capital regionally and internationally, the component will further address the
problem o f knowledge concentration as well as decrease failures o f network and information.
Strengthening o f CONICYT's monitoring and evaluation capacity will furthermore decrease
information failures and microeconomic inefficiencies.

Component3: EnhancingInnovaChile's knowledgeeconomy offering

Component 3 will foster CORFO's capacity to: (i)strengthen the deal flow, (ii)enhance
assistance to international market penetration, and (iii)improve the effectiveness o f technology
transfer instruments reducing information failures. .In addition, strengthening o f CORFO's
monitoring and evaluation capacity will further decrease information failures.
