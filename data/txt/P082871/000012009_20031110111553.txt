                               I.BACKGROUNDANDRATIONALE

1.      Peru has been going through rapidpolitical reforms duringthe past two years. The return
to full democracy after the Fujimori administration has ledto a drive for greater transparency and
reduction of corruption. The Congress, courts, political parties, media and civil society have
been taking advantage of the new political environment to participate actively in policy making
and keep a vigilant eye on public management. The Congress has often been the leading force in
movements towards deepening democracy and strengthening the rule of law.                        It has taken
initiatives on major reforms and quickly responded to the Executive Branch's reform initiatives,
albeit often modifying them significantly.
2.      Within this setting, one of the most deeply felt demands of the Peruvian populationis to
reduce the concentration of state power by decentralizing public functions and resources to
lower levels of government and communities. In response, the Toledo govemment in 2002
proposed a number of reforms, several of which the Congress has enacted. Major laws were
passedregardingregional governments, basic decentralization, fiscal responsibility, participatory
budgeting, and public sector reform. Other reforms along with their supporting regulations are
being prepared as well. In total, these reforms are changing the way in which Peru's public
sector operates.
3.      The Government has effectively followed a more gradual approach to decentralization
than a single comprehensive campaign. Its calendar calls for the reform effort already underway
to continue until 2006. In its approach, Peru i s capitalizing on the lessons it learned from other
Latin American countries that adopted and began implementing decentralization policies two or
more decades ago. It i s also drawing on its own-largely            failed-prior   experience in this field.
From all these, the Government has set three guidingprinciples:
                 Decentralization should be fiscally neutral
                 Decentralization should be gradual
                 Decentralization should enhance economic competitiveness
4.       "Fiscal neutrality'' means that decentralization should create no additional fiscal burden
on the public sector, either through transfers to or borrowing by regional or local governments.
"Graduality" means that, since the managerial capacity of Peru's regional and local governments
i s uneven, the transfers of resources and responsibilities should take place slowly and be
restricted to those entities which have demonstrated effective capabilities.                      "Enhanced
competitiveness" means that local and in particular regional governments should be geared up to
facilitate andundertake neededinvestments and mobilize private sector resources.

5.       For Peru, gradual decentralization also involves the recognition that its main benefits will
only come after a few years. Accordingly, the initial emphasis of decentralization policies must
ensure continuation of a hardbudgetconstraint for the entire public sector. Inaddition, the risks
of "moral hazard" in connection with regional and municipal borrowing are necessarily high.
Therefore, the first Loan of the proposed programmatic series i s strongly focused on preserving
fiscal stabilization during the take-off period of decentralization when pressures to accelerate
spending will be substantial.
6.       Simultaneously, the Government is moving to put in place the framework needed to
harvest the anticipated benefits of decentralization in the form of efficiency gains. For this
reason, the proposed Loan would support the further advancement of the legal framework and
the initiation of implementing actions involving the civil service, community participation,
performance management contracts, reporting and accounting standards, accreditation of
regional and local governments, redistribution of government assets, strengthening budget
coordination and revenue mobilization. Moreover, the Government's decentralization program
i s linked to its agenda for enhancing competitiveness and growth. Since the construction of this
comprehensive structure i s a formidable task, this Loan seeks primarily to support policy design
and the initial period of decentralization's implementation, and selected new key measures to
promote competitiveness and growth.


         11. RECENTPOLITICALAND MACROECONOMICDEVELOPMENTS

A. CurrentPoliticalContext
7.      The arrival of President Toledo has strengtheneddemocracy in Peru. After a decade of
the Fujimori regime and a short transitional government, President Toledo was elected to office
amidst an economic recession. Beginning its period inJuly 2001, the new Government received
support from the international community at the Madrid Consultative Group Meeting in October
2001, and signed a two-year Stand-By Arrangement with the IMF in February 2002. In July
2002, the Government also signed a National Agreement ("Acuerdo National") to catalyze
consensus among the major political parties, unions and civil society on an ambitious poverty
reduction agenda supportedby fiscal discipline and decentralization.
8.       Although the economy i s stable and growing, the Government's agenda continues to face
difficult political challenges. The popularity of the President, as measured by opinion polls, is
low, albeit slightly improving (15 percent end-August), due to the accumulation of unfulfilled
expectations fueled by election-time promises, lack of a stable consensus in Congress, and the
public's perceptionthat, inspite of rapideconomic growth, real wages, employment and incomes
of the poor have showed only small improvements. In November 2002, regional governments
were elected for the first time. Whereas the Alianza Popular Revolucionara Americana (APRA)
opposition party won half of the regional governments, the government's coalition won only two
regions (Cusco and Callao), and independent political parties won the rest.
9.      Political tensions have risen but the administration has so far maintained legislative
support for keepingthe reform program on track. InMay 2003, the country entered into a state
of emergency to deal with public sector workers and agricultural producers. The strike was
resolved by a pay increase for teachers and police. A Shining Path hostage taking of 70 gas
pipeline workers, albeit resolved in 24 hours, heightened the perception that narco-terrorism may


                                                  2

be on the rise again. Recently, elected regional governments are pressing for greater transfers.
Some are opposed to the elimination of the regional tax benefits that foster tax evasion, while
others are taking a more realistic approach, looking for ways to balance their finances with their
own initiatives. Civil society has been active in monitoring progress in the decentralization
process, including tracking transfers to regional and local governments and the performance of
these governments. Nevertheless, the Government has maintained the course of reforms. In
May, Congress passeda revised Law of FiscalPrudence and Transparency; and inJune-July, it
approved tax and spending measures to finance the aforementioned wage increases, rather than
to permit a higher deficit.
10.      A new Cabinet was appointed inJuly. The new PrimeMinister, Beatriz Merino, and her
team have brought renewed strength to the Government's reform program.                            Her plan i s
comprehensive and ambitious and includes a new system of concessions that would be attractive
to the regional governments, a tax reform and fiscal measures to ensure the fiscal sustainability
of the reform process, and the merging and decentralization of the social programs, includingthe
introduction of conditioned transfers, benchmarks and performance management at the regional
and local     level.     She also declared a state of emergency in the education sector.                       A
comprehensive judicial reform has also been launched under her leadership. The polls have
reacted very favorably to the new Cabinet, giving it approval ratings of over 50 percent. Civil
society support for decentralization i s high, as has been reflected in the participatory budgeting
iniatiatives that took place this year throughout the country.

B. RecentMacroeconomic Developments
11.      DESPITEDIFFICULT EXTERNALENVIRONMENTAND UNCERTAINDOMESTICPOLITICS, THE
                   A
PERUVIAN    ECONOMY HAS PROVEN RESILIENT.                   In 2002, a year of deep recession for some
countries in Latin America and average GDP growth of -1.2 percent for the region as a whole,
Peru's growth was highest in the region (see Annex III).              Led by mining, manufacturing and
construction, real GDP grew 5.3 percent, nearly two points above the original forecasts of 3.5
percent. Similarly, the annual inflation rate of 1.5 percent (end-of period) was the second lowest
in the region in 2002. Consumption also boomed, supported by record low interest rates and
stability in prices and the exchange rate. Investment gradually recovered, but only during the
last quarter of 2002. The current account deficit remained at manageable levels and was
comfortably financed by public and private capital inflows. In 2002, largely boostedby exports
from Antamina,2 Peru recorded its first trade surplus since 1990. Gross reserves at the end of
2002 stood at US$9.7 billion or 103 percent of dollar deposits inthe banking sector.
12.      Early indicators of economic activity suggest that Peru's recovery i s broad basedand the
projected 4 percent GDP growth for 2003 i s likely to be achieved (Figure 1). In July 2003,
following a four month consecutive decline, inflation was at 2 percent year over year, near the
bottom of the Central Bank's target range of 1.5-3.5 percent for 2003. International reserves are
more than twice the level of short-term external debt liabilities and increasing due to a dynamic
export performance and strong private capital-namely              foreign direct investment-inflows          into
sizable mininginvestment projects, which put moderate pressure on the Nuevo Sol to appreciate.

17.     MACROECONOMIC          IMPLICATIONS OF GOVERNMENT               REFORM PROGRAM.The Government
recognizes that decentralization could result in high macroeconomic costs for Peru unless the
necessary fiscal measures are taken. First, the central government deficit can widen if the
government i s unable to reduce costs or increase revenue to adapt to the changing functions at
different levels of government. Second, efforts to eliminate fiscal deficits of regional and local
governments can take considerable time. Third, decentralization could reduce the ability of the
central government to manage economic shocks as a result of lesser flexibility and reduced
resources at the national level. The Government i s also aware that decentralization generally is
best pursued in a climate of sustained growth to generate the additional resources necessary.
With 5.2 percent of growth last year and a relatively sound macroeconomic situation, Peruis ina
stable position to pursue fiscal decentralization. However, it i s clear that fiscal volatility has
increased over the last few years. The Government therefore i s committed to reducing the pre-
decentralization fiscal deficit andto fiscal stability overall.

21.     While the likelihood of a sharp deceleration in growth i s low at this stage, it i s important
to highlight that the Peruvian economy faces significant vulnerabilities. If not addressed, these
could leadto a low growth situation such as the period before 2002. The major ones are the high

political instability that could make it difficult to implement the current reform program.

vulnerabilities.   The sound regulatory environment for dollar lending and the high level of

mitigate its high debt risk, the Government has designeda debt management strategy under the
revised Marco Macroecondmico Multiannual (MMM)that is basedon reducing the cost of new

450 basis points below market rates. Additional domestic debt is not a cheaper substitute for
multilateral financing and would worsen Peru's repayment profile. To mitigate the effects of
political uncertainty, the Government has obtained from Congress special powers that would
support the implementation of the much needed tax reforms, without the need for possibly

36.     In 1992, the Fujimori government suspendedthe regional governments. Deconcentrated
central government agencies responsible to central command, the so-called CTARs were created,

provincial delegations of those agencies, further compounding the very high level of

resources. Against this backdrop, the Toledo government came to power in July 2001 and
launched an ambitious decentralization effort based on the state reform agenda set out in the
"AcuerdoNacional."
38.     There i s a broad consensus to decentralize, demonstrated by an impressive commitment
of all parts of government and the support of the population, and by the rapid speed of the
legislative process.      Decentralization i s seen as an integral part of modernization and

B. ObjectivesandGuidingPrincipalsof Decentralization

39.     The Government sees decentralization as a key part of the democratization,
modernization and decentralization "triangle" on which the Toledo government was elected, and
which continues to be high in its policy agenda. Its decentralization goals include making Peru
more inclusive especially by reaching out to neglected territories andpopulations. The excessive
concentration o f power by the Fujimori government kindled pressures for a greater vertical
balance of powers by increasing participation and resources o f regional and local governments.
Linked to this is the realization that public administration in Peru is highly ineffective and
inefficient. By the end o f the Toledo administration, the Government hopes to implement
significant modernization o f the state as well as improved government transparency.                     To
accomplish this, Peru has opted for a highly participatory model of decentralization
unprecedented in Latin America. The program's institutional framework includes consultative
bodies with civil society participation at the regional and local level. Inaddition, a participatory
process has been installed in public decision-making, notably through "participatory budgeting."
Participation and transparency would also be enhanced through wide-ranging laws on these
subjects, as well as systematic consultations of civil society and regional and local governments
on important legal norms By the end of the Toledo government, regional and local governments
are expected to regularly prepare participatory plans and budgets, and have installed effective
mechanisms for public engagement and oversight.                Central government will consult more
broadly with civil society, for example on major policy initiatives and legal norms.

decentralization, namely (i)increasing efficiency in the provision o f public services and (ii)

C. Legal andInstitutional Frameworkfor Decentralization

41.     The legal basisfor decentralization was set bythe 2001 constitutional amendment andthe
passage of the Decentralization Framework Law in July 2002. This framework i s complemented
by other laws recently passed or about to be passed in Congress (see Annex I). Law forThe
Regional Governments defines the functions for regional institutions.          This, together with
complementary laws, provided the basis for the first package of social programs to be
transferred. The law for municipal governments provides municipal government 6 percent of
total public sector budget by 2004, rising to 12 percent by 2008. Congress has passeda number
of other laws with important implicationsfor the decentralization process, such as the framework
law on participatory budgeting, the law of decentralized investment promotion and important
sectoral laws like the General Education Law. Furthermore, the LRTF sets conditions governing
regional and localborrowing.
42.     The legal framework for decentralization is thus reasonably well advanced. However it
lacks two supporting measures: a fiscal decentralization law and a law of programmatic transfers.
Both bills have been submitted to the Congress. This i s important because some functional
assignments and transfers to regional and local governments were made without a clear
definition of the fiscal framework.     In addition, the decentralization process confronts legal
complexities. There i s a multitude of general and sectoral laws, complemented by numerous
regulations and guidelines. The speed at which they have been passed sometimes tended to
weaken their quality.     This has at times led to contradictions between different laws or
implementing regulations. In short, impressive progress has been made to shape a suitable legal
framework, but, in important respects, it i s too fragmented and inconsistent. The Government
therefore i s planning to take corrective actions, including the adoption of more comprehensive
laws to avoid further fragmentation and greater intersectoral coordination in their design and
implementation.

D. InstitutionalCoordination

43.     A National Decentralization Council (CND)              was created to coordinate the
decentralization process. It is responsible for (i)  overseeing and monitoring the program; (ii)
providing support for institution-strengthening of regional and local governments; (iii)
coordinating and channeling international assistance; and (iv) designing plans for the transfer of
resources and functions to regional and local governments, and from 2004 onwards, their annual
decentralization plans.      The CND has prepared a plan for monitoring and evaluating
decentralization progress and is designing the relevant system However, despite efforts in
capacity-building, the CND so far has not been able to demonstrate the necessary strength and
vision to effectively lead the process. It also appears quite isolated from other actors at the
central as well as at the regional and local levels. Therefore, the MEFand the Presidency of the
Council of Ministers (PCM) have filled the vacuum, assuming a greater role in policy
formulation. A stronger alignment between the PCM, in charge of modernization of the state
and the CND decentralization agenda, may help to provide greater dynamism to the process and
better coordination with other actors.

             IV. PROGRESSWITH THE DECENTRALIZATIONPROGRAM

44.     Peru's decentralization program i s already producing results. The strides to date indicate
that it has created a valuable opportunity to raise the efficiency of public expenditure, and to
increase coverage and improve the quality of public services. It also has shown a potential for
expanding civil society participation and mobilizing additional privateinputs for the provision of
public goods. But, at the same time, the Government's actions have raised several problems that
it needs to control. One in particular is the possibility of over-extending the role of the public
sector and stimulating sub-optimal investment. Also, experience in L A C has demonstrated that
poorly designed fiscal decentralization can produce significant imbalances, and might lead to
excessive debt at the regional andor local level. There i s also the possibility of the deterioration
of public (particularly social) services, due to confused responsibilities and accountability.
Overall, the institutional and technical capacities of regional and local governments to assume
the functions andresourcestransferred to them will be the great question mark of the process.
45.     The Government program is structured around three inter-connected pillars: a) fiscal
sustainability, b) institutionalcapacity strengthening, and c) growth and competitiveness. Each of
thesepillarsi s discussedbelow.

47.     FISCALNEUTRALITY.        Keeping decentralization fiscally neutral is one of the dominant
principles of the Government's reform program.         The setting for fiscal decentralization is
dominated by four elements of the Government's fiscal policy. The relation of public debt to
GNP reached a relatively high level (46 percent of GNP) before decentralization started.
Second, fiscal consolidation needs to be gradual if the country i s to maintain the economic
recovery that started in 2002. Third, tax authorities have already decided to preserve the main
characteristics of the tax system, thereby limitingscope for reform. Fourth, Government policy is
to expand the tax baserather than increasetax rates.

49.     The fiscal neutrality standard proclaimed in the Government's decentralization program

way or another, and has put additional pressure on fiscal deficits. By contrast, the Government

expenditures at the central level. The Government intends to keep close track of the costs of
decentralization to ensure that this policy process does not derail the entire public sector from the
fiscal sustainability path.

51.     TAXATION. At the beginning of the 199Os, Peru reformed its tax system. It remained
basically made up of five taxes: a general sales tax (GST) organized as a value-added tax; an
income tax on legal entities; an income tax on individuals; tariffs on imports; and a selective
excise tax. At the end of the reform, these taxes had moderate rates and relatively broad tax
bases and the system's assignment of private resources was reasonably neutral. The result was
that the tax burden, 8 percent of the GDP at the beginning of the 199Os, reached almost 17
percent in 1997."
52.     The system has been complicated by the awards of tax benefits aimed at promoting
particular economic sectors and geographic areas. These have diminished potential collections,
distorted the assignment of resources, and increased administrative costs."            Inaddition, the tax
stability agreementsestablished inthe 1990shave hurt tax administration, makingthe tax system
lessneutral andefficient than it was after the reforms.
53.     At present, the revenues of the regions and municipalities (other than the transfers related
to the decentralization measures) include two percent of the GST, potential increases in tax
collections after the elimination of numerous exemptions, the real estate tax, fees for
governmental services ("rates"),        and property tax ("contributions").       In this area, the role of
regional and local entities i s confined only to the collection and control of the real estate tax and
service fees. They can enter into agreements to assume those tasks related to control of national
taxes on behalf of SUNAT, the national tax agency.
54.     For decentralization to work, it i s important to strengthen the tax bases of regional and
local governments. The traditional dilemma of ensuring that the jurisdiction for spending i s the
same as the one that raises the money has to be solved within the intergovernmental transfer
system. However, the Peruvian Constitution does not authorize regional level taxation. It i s a
unitary state in which the Congress exclusively exercises tax authority. Even the prices of
services and property tax rates are set by national law. As well, central government authorities
consider that the regional and local entities have not effectively used all the means already at
their disposal for collection and control. Besides, keepingtax collection concentrated inSUNAT
would theoretically retain the "advantages of scale" administration.
55.     In view of this constraint, tax managementat the regional level can be accomplished if a
portion of a tax (for example, income tax) i s a substantial portion of the overall transfer. This i s
the government approach with the proposed Fiscal Decentralization Law. All regions but Lima
and Callao will receive 30 percent (60 percent if macro-regions) of national taxes (VAT and
excise taxes) collected in the region. It would also be desirable to devolve some tax authority to
regional levels rather than creating new tax bases.

71.      ACCREDITATIONAND MANAGEMENT             CAPACITY.    A distinctive characteristic of Peru's
decentralization model i s its accreditation process. This aims to condition transferring functions
and resources to regional and local governments on the certification of their adequate
managementcapacities.
72.      Latin American experience suggests that successful decentralization requires an integral
strategy to link transfers to the competence of regional and local governments and changes in
the parties' roles and responsibilities. Therefore, the Government decided that its transfer of
functions and resources should be parallel with accreditations of capacity standards of the
projected recipients. To this end, the Ley de Bases de Descentralizacidn (LBD) requires the
passage of an accreditation law (Ley de Acreditacidn). This law, which has been in elaboration
now for half a year, would establish a contracted-out system of accreditation under which an
independent third party, supervised by the CND, would conduct and supervise the process. The
aim i s to ensure that regional institutions possess at least minimumadministrative competence.
73.      The draft law has not yet been passed because of differing ideas about the technical
certification needs of regional and local governments, while there are strong pressuresto speed
decentralization without such "bureaucratic" requirements. Inaddition, there are different views
about the participation of the line ministries and the kind of criteria to be used for the
accreditation process. In the interim, social program responsibilities have been transferred to
regional governments subject to a simplified accreditation system.               For the municipal
governments, no system has yet been developed. The CND has established a list of verification
criteria comparable to those for the regions, andthe Ministry of Women and Social Development
(MIMDES)has created a system of minimum criteria for the transfer of social programs. But
these approachesare not sufficiently linked, indicatinga needfor one comprehensive system.
74.      The accreditation law should ensure that transfers are made when regional and local
governments have demonstrated effective capacity to carry them out. This demonstration should
encompass successful fulfillment of at least minimum requirements regarding public
management competence and ability to meet fiscal discipline and sectoral standards.
International experience also indicates the importance of transparency in the process and
safeguards against political interference. It i s also important that the law clearly establish who i s
to bear the cost of accreditation.

   0   Limited efficiency and effectiveness: Peru does not have an excessive central government
       bureaucracy by Latin American standards, but a large part of it is clearly inefficient, if
       not dysfunctional. Few incentives for efficiency andeffectiveness exist.
   0   Limited transparency and widespread patronage: Patronage practices increased under the
       past regime. Since then, the central government has taken some limited measures to
       improve the quality of civil service and provision of public services. This was restricted
       for fear that stronger actions would harmthe government's popularity and its possibilities
       for reelection.     As a result, the civil service is still considered ineffective, non-
       transparent, andthe captive of special interest groups.
   0   Fragmented civil service regimes: The civil service regime i s split up into a variety of
       sub-regimes that lack coordination. The dominant system, based on Decreto Legislative
       (D.L.)276 from 1984, is nolonger comprehensive. Two others account for more than 20
       percent of personnel expenses, and provide substantially higher wages than those under
       D.L. 276. A number of other regimes are also in operation. Since the 1990s, the
       government has sought to implement informal solutions to resolve their disparities.
   0   Personal management: There are few merit-based incentives and the ones that exist are
       generally circumvented. Tenure i s almost automatic once a person i s appointed. The
       present rules create the worst of all worlds: guaranteed and increasingly well-paid posts
       for a large number of people, many of whom demonstrate limited motivation and
       competence.
   0   Wages: Overall, the wage system i s largely ineffective. More than 60 percent of pay i s
       not linked to it, its composition i s fragmented and it i s not inline with the requirements of
        a modem public administration. This i s exacerbatedby the fact that the current wage bill
       i s not fiscally sustainable, representing about 40 percent of total government expenditure
        and 6.6 percent of GDP, the second highest in Latin America.                Furthermore, the
       constitutionally-protected pension system for some public employees guarantees a
       pension that i s 100percent of their final wages.
   0   Decentralization and civil service: The existing civil service rules are designed for a
        unitary, centralized state. They do not take into account the particular needs at the
       regional and local level. Also, there has been little concern about the implications of the
        decentralization process on civil service in terms of institutional reform and the
        mechanisms of public administration needed for the transfer of functions and resources.
        Special civil service reforms for the regional and local level complementing the more
        comprehensive civil service reform are needed.
91.    The above problems are especially pressing as the fiscal cost of decentralization will be a
key factor in the future. In the regional governments that will assume functions, 2004
expenditure for senior civil servants officials are estimated to amount to US$25 million.
Municipalities are likely to face substantial contingencies (mostly for pensions). Also, the
transfer of functions might well lead to new appointments.
92.    The Government is currently reformingthe legal framework for the civil service, which i s
expected to lead to a substantial overhaul.       Its goal i s to install an institutional and legal
framework that could permit the rationalization and professionalization of the civil service,
bringingabout more competent, merit-based management. The executive drafted a Ley Marco
de Empleo Publico which i s expected to be approved shortly. It would promote a unified civil

performance andprovisions to reducepersonnel when needed.

C. GrowthandCompetitiveness

95.     The success of the decentralization process, to a large extent, will be measured by the
increased level of development and economic activity of the regions, along with increased
employment and reduced poverty. To secure those benefits, improvements in countrywide
competitiveness and productivity are necessary to accompany the decentralization process. B y
devolving powers to the regions and addressing key competitiveness bottlenecks, the
Government expects to increase levels of regional development. It therefore seeks to improve
competitiveness and attract the private investment needed to promote sustain high growth. The
administration has committed itself to (i)improving Peru's international standing on
competitiveness from its current rank (68thof 75 countries), to 48th or better by 2006; (ii)   and
doubling the value of exports to reach US$14.2 billion by 2006, with growth focusing on
nontraditional exports, in conjunction with measures to increase the regions' productive
capabilities. The decentralization program i s viewed as an important tool for this purpose, by
enabling local governments-particularly    the regions-to    become more engaged in their own
development, availing themselves of local conditions and assets for suitable economic activity to
their areas.
96.     Peru has made important advances since the early 1990s to foster increased
competitiveness and exports. External tariffs have been more than halved and non-tariff barriers
are among the lowest in the region. An adequate foreign investment law and a reasonable
business environment exist, which helped         attracted significant FDI flows.      Numerous
privatizations and concessions, involving telecommunications, electricity, natural gas, railways,
toll roads, the Matarani port and the Lima airport, have created significant private sector
participation ininfrastructure. A new law has set the stage for a possible concessioning of Peru's
main ports. These have been complemented by setting an adequate regulatory framework and
regulatory agencies, including an integrated transport regulatory agency.        In addition, the

                                               28

Government organized a broad-based dialogue with the private sector in 2002 to develop
proposals to stimulate exports, which the Government has movedquickly to implement.
97.     However, to increase competitiveness and to accelerate exports, a better business and
investment climate i s also needed. The last decade saw a surge of FDI to Peru, boosting the
average annual inflows to about US$2.5 billion, up from US$40 million in the late 1980s. But
the vast majority of these capital flows was resource-seeking-primarily            in mining-and     asset-
seeking. At the height of the privatization program from 1994 to 1997, nearly 70 percent of FDI
resulted from the transfer of state-owned assets. Very little "greenfield" FDIwent to productive
sectors that are generally perceived to be important growth poles based on export expansion,
such as agriculture, fisheries, manufacturing andforestry.
98.     Recent World Bank Group studies on constraints to private enterprise found the
following key impediment^:'^
           High levels of uncertainty among firms regarding laws, regulations, and economic
           policies.     Uncertainty results from both general policies (such as labor and tax
           policies) andfrom the level of businessinteractions with the Government. The higher
            the number of interactions with the Government (such as tax or labor inspections,
            biddingon a government contract or court cases) the higher the level of uncertainty.
            Higher levels of uncertainty are associated with significant changes in firm behavior.
           For each one point rise inthe level of uncertainty, on a five point scale, investment in
            new machinery and equipment dropped by an equivalent of 16 percent; investment in
            outside training was drastically reduced, and the profitability of firms fell by an
            average of nearly two percent. Legal uncertainty i s a further problem, as Peruvian
            firms report significant delays in court cases regarding contract disputes (200-300
            days on average), and additional delays in enforcing judicial decision (100-150 days
            i s not uncommon).         Complementing the previous point, there i s also a need to
            improve the business environment and the investment climate by reducing red tape,
            improving registries, lowering entry costs and time to register, eliminating
            unnecessary bureaucracy, and harmonizing and consolidating national, regional and
            local procedures for doing business. As well, the issue of aligning regional and local
            licensingpractices with national norms will have to be monitoredclosely.

        e   Quality standards are lacking, and innovation is limited. Peru i s far behind other
            L A C countries and countries with similar per capita income levels in instituting
            quality standards and certification processes.           This is particularly important in
            agriculture and fisheries, where Peru's successful competitors from Brazil and Chile
            to South Africa and Australia have adopted sector-wide standards that meet global
            benchmarks. Peru has fallen short, with only 201 companies certified to I S 0 9000
            standards by the end of 2002, compared to about 600 in Colombia, 800 in Chile, and
            7,000 in Brazil. It has similarly trailed in levels of investment in research and
            development (R&D) and the production of patents.

99.    The proposed Loan would therefore support the Government's plans to address the
aforementioned obstacles to increased competitiveness. The scheduled transfer of assets from
the central government to regional and local governments presents opportunities with great
potential to spur gains in competitiveness and economic growth, especially regarding improving
transportation infrastructure and attracting private investment. Lower levels of government are
more in tune with the needs of businesses in their regions.            By receiving control over
transportation infrastructure, they should be better able to define priorities and oversee
improvements, either with their own funds or those from privatizations and concessions. As
well, legislation about assets transfers could play an important role in providing incentives for
regional integration if the asset classification is framed in a wider geographical territory than the
current departments (regions) as the country moves towards the formation of macroregions.
100. With this in mind, the Government intends to have pending regulations on asset
decentralization define principles for assigning assets to different levels of government and the
processes for these transfers.      The transfer of non-corporate assets to regional and local
governments would be linked with accreditation criteria and, once a function i s transferred, its
assets would follow as well.       New asset management rules would be compatible with the
decisions adopted in other areas of decentralization, for example, transfer of sectoral
responsibilities, resources and competences, entrepreneurial activity, and rules for the formation
of macroregions.
101. An important element of this activity centers on the definition of the property of public
corporations. The definition could have a great impact on the possibilities of sale, concession or
private administration of corporations. The Government accordingly plans to issue legislation to
adjudicate ownership of public assets, and assist regional and local governments to rationalize
and register their assets and property titles. This i s important because to date, most assets
inheritedfrom former department councils have no titles. Other actions to be taken in the short
term include setting rules for the treatment of state corporations' liabilities and debt secured by
assets.  In the case of government-owned companies, an important part of their debt was
assumedby the central government, which uses the resources transferred from the companies to
service it.

102.   Within the decentralization framework there are risks that financially-strapped local
governments could increase uncertainty and negatively affect investment and growth. This
might result from municipal authorities creating new local fees and charges on business (for
example, for cleaning, parks, and inspection services) to increase revenues. In addition, the
decentralization could complicate administrative and regulatory uncertainty over what level of
government or even agency has responsibility and authority over the various permits needed for
investment. Rent-seeking behavior in public goods procurement i s a further driver of the firms'
perception of policy uncertainty. To avoid these problems, this operation ensures that (i)the
funding level for local governments is commensurate with their responsibilities, (ii)the
government delineates clear lines of responsibility for investment and business regulations, (iii)
there is a coherent regulation simplification program at regional and municipal level with
necessary funding and technical assistance, and (iv) a transparent process for procurement i s in
place at the local level.

would be supported by the proposedLoan include:

E. Environment

119.    As a structural adjustment program, the proposed operation does not trigger OP 4.01,
and, as a consequence, does not require an environmental rating. OD 8.60 applies in order to
ensure due diligence on the application of the "do not harm'' principle.            In case that
decentralization entails lower capacity at the regional and local level to identify and mitigate
environmental impacts of reforms, especially regarding resource management and oversight of
investment projects, their capacity will be closely evaluated. Capacity-strengthening would then
take place and mitigating measureswill also be taken, whenever needed. This could be the case
for infrastructure investments as part of the competitiveness component, such as roads andports.

G. FinancialManagement

124.   The Government i s committed to improving transparency and access to information to
promote effective governance and administration of public finances. A Country Financial
Accountability Assessment (CFAA) was prepared in February 2001. Although the scope of the
CFAA regarding financial management was significantly broader than the scope of this
operation's budget component, the CFAA recommendations are applicable to, and consistent
with, the DECSAL. The Country Financial Accountability Assessment concluded that the
financial tools at the disposal of the Congress and Executive Branch comprised (a) a robust
financial administration legislation, (b) highly dedicated staff, and (c) an effective integrated
financial management system (SIAF). The major recommendations of the CFAA related to (a)
more effective oversight by Congress of the budget process and review of the allocations of
available funding, (b) more detailed and frequent reporting on expenditures to Congress, and (c)
unfetteredsurveillance andreporting by the CGR of the acts andreports of the executive.
125.    StrengthenedLegal Framework The National Control Law No 27785 was approved on
July 22,2002 and ensures functional autonomy of the CGR, inaccordancewith the provisions of
the Constitution. The CGR is now exercising ex-post control according to its legal mandateover
the finances of the Ministry of Defence and other security services, and a special audit has been
carried out. In 2003, two important regulations were issued to support the 2002 law. The first
regulation institutionalises the role and functions of the internal auditor at all levels of
government (national, regional, local) and the second regulates the designation of eligibility of
private audit firms to perform financial and operations audits of public entities, including the
procedures for registration, evaluation, designation, public competition, and ex-post quality
controlby the CGR.

investment projects and data on the timing and amount of transfers to regional and local
governments. These reports are very comprehensive and easily accessible. Monthly reports of
budget execution data have been published for all entities in the central government and
decentralized entities. The extension of the SIAF 2 to the Ministryof Defence andother civilian
ministries has strengthenedthe control environment. SIAF i s implemented in 42 operating units

expenditure.

I.Benefits

137.    The Government's decentralization process will have the twin benefits of promoting
sustained, broad-basedeconomic growth and improving the quality of and access to government
services by all citizens. These benefits, in turn, will contribute to the long-term reduction of
poverty inPeru.


                                                42

138.    The proposed Loan will contribute to these goals by helping the Government develop a
coherent framework for the implementation of decentralization, with a focus on the most
appropriate sequencing of actions, clearly defined responsibilities and the fiscal neutrality of the
entire process.
139.    More specifically, the proposed Loan will help to (i)    protect fiscal sustainability during
Peru's transition to a more decentralized state; (ii)strengthen management and fiscal capacity at
regional and local levels for their progressive assumption of service responsibilities; and (iii)
support the creation of a better regulatory and investment environment for upgrading Peru's
competitiveness.
140.    More specifically, the reform program to be supported by the DECSAL would promote
fiscal consolidation and protect fiscal responsibility.     Fiscal reforms would also strengthen
countercyclical fiscal institutions and rules; expand the tax base and improve the efficiency of
tax collection at national and local levels; contribute to reducing country risk; and free resources
for key programs. A stricter framework for debt by regional and/or local governments should
lessenthe risks of excessive borrowingandhelp sustainthe hardbudget constraint.
141. The proposed program would also help provide the fiscal and institutional framework
neededto support a gradual decentralization process that seeks to achieve better service delivery
at regional and local levels.     It would improve the design and effectiveness of functional
assignments at different levels of government. The proposed accreditation system, enhanced
monitoring and evaluation capacity, selected civil service reforms, and expanded participationof
civil society and social vigilance, especially through participatory budgeting, should be
especially rewarding.
142.    Lastly, the program would help accelerate Peru's economic growth by enhancing
competitiveness and structural change for increased productivity and resource mobilization. In
the process, the Loan should stimulate increased private sector participation and investment in
the wake of measures to open the economy, implement sounder regulatory policies, and make
markets more flexible.
143.    Overall, through these factors, the program would promote more growth, free additional
resources for social sector investment, and enhance Government's capacity to effectively design
and deliver public services in a manner that should particularlybenefit the poor andvulnerable.

J. Risks

144.    All prior actions under this operation have been achieved, but there are risks
accompanying the proposed Loan that might pose a threat to fiscal stability and growth
performance. Current risks are high on political, economic, financial and technical grounds.
145.    Political risks. The Executive Branch i s committed to the program's goals and reforms.
However, it has thus far only obtained limited agreement with other key actors on strategic
choices and implementation methods. Some of these represent strong entrenched interests whose
opposition could conceivably derail the fulfillment of fiscal sustainability requirements. Further,
some of the remaining reforms require Congressional endorsement, possibly entailing delays or
substantial changes. And the decentralization process has so far been manifested mainly in
promises, leading to unrealistic popular expectations and frustrations over the perceived lack of
concrete visible products. These couldcombine with the President's reducedpopularity, frequent
cabinet changes and other developments to increase social unrest andpolitical instability.

                                                 43

146.   The election cycle might increase political risks. National elections (for President and
Congress) are scheduled for 2006. Not only could the national government fall into the control
of another party but there might also be a new national-regional political balance. Candidates
and elected politicians are likely to press for additional transfers to regional and local
governments without clearly defined responsibilities or corresponding budget cuts at the national
level.
147.   Economic and financial risks.        There are important economic and financial risks
associated with the Government's program of accelerated reforms, from both external and
internal sources. All risks mentionedbelow could leadPeruto have a less stable macroeconomic
environment, significantly slower economic growth, and to depart from the program of reforms
agreedunder this operation.
       Extemal risks. Peru faces several external risks.          Economic activity i s mostly
       concentrated inthe primary sector, which makes the country highly vulnerable to natural
       disasters.     Moreover, the relativey narrow export base (mostly dependent on
       commodities) increases the economy's exposure to terms of trade shocks. A severe
       deterioration of terms of trade or lower-than-expected foreign investment inflows might
       put at riskthe growthmomentumandmacroeconomic stability.
       Zntemal risks. The proposed tax reform package could have a recessionary impact; a
       major concern i s the proposed financial transactions tax.       While this tax would be
       relatively easy to administer and will increase government revenues in the short term, by
       raising the cost of capital and increasing the cost of financial transactions, it could
       increase interest rates, which will likely lead to lower investment and economic activity
       as well as negative allocative distortions. A regressive redistribution of credit following a
       financial transaction tax on the competitiveness of most regions may also result. An
       additional risk may come from fiscal slippage due to lower-than-expected revenues or
       increased spending. This, inturn, would leadto increasedmacroeconomic instability and
       higher country risk, negatively affecting interest rates. Such a situation would adversely
       affect fiscal sustainability, as both domestic and foreign borrowing will become more
       expensive. Furthermore, the high degree of dollarization of the banking system and the
       risks of external shocks heightens the system's exposure to exchange rate risks. There i s
       no significant asset-liability mismatch in the banking sector itself. But, an unexpected
       adjustment in the exchange rate could affect the non-tradable corporate sector's capacity
       to repay their loans in foreign currency, thereby weaken the banking system. The current
       level of public debt andits servicing requirements constrains the Government's ability to
       adjust to external and internal shocks.
148.   Govemment actions to mitigate political, economic andfinancial risk. The Government
i s undertaking several actions to mitigate political, economic and financial risks. To mitigate
political risks, the Toledo administration has made significant efforts to reach out to opposition
political parties in Congress in order to ensure their support for the Government's reform
program.     The Government is also relaunching the Acuerdo Nacional to increase greater
consensus on its medium- andlong-term program, and is considering including short-term policy
measures as well. It will also strengthencoordination on critical areas, such as decentralization
by giving the PCM a greater role and at the same time greater coordination mandate inthis area.
A National PlanningOffice is to be createdeither as partof the Presidential Office or the PCMto
coordinate strategic planning. At the same time, broader consultations are being held with the


                                                44

private sector, for example on the financial transaction tax and with civil society, on the draft
regulation of the participatory budgeting regulation.       A promising sign of its success was
Congress' recently approved executive powers for President Toledo to enact tax measures by
decree, which the Congress will then vote on within 90 days. As well, led by the MEF, the
Toledo administration has already successfully held off interest groups that were pressuring for
additional expenditures.     To mitigate external risks, the Government i s promoting export
diversification and competitiveness enhancement by reducing transaction costs for exports, a
program supported by the proposed Loan and other World Bank operations. In addition, the
current flexible exchange rate policy permits the country to better adapt to terms of trade and
other external shocks. The Government i s aware of the drawbacks of the financial transactions
tax, and is proposing to approve the tax only for a temporary period, and also offeringtax credits
against the GST or the VAT for those paying the new tax. The Law of Fiscal Responsibility and
Transparency acts as a safeguard against any potential deviation of the primary deficit from the
Government's targets. The risks in the banking sector are mitigatedby continued strengthening
of bank supervision through issuingnorms such as requiring banks to identify the exchange risk
of dollar-lending to clients with income streams mainly inlocal currency. High capital adequacy
ratios and the strong external liquidity position further reduce risks in the banking system.
Finally, gradual fiscal consolidation and a prudent borrowing strategy are ensuring that Peru's
public debt remains in a sustainable path. The Government has designated a debt management
strategy under the revised MMM that i s based on reducing the cost of new borrowing and
extending maturities through multilateralfinancing.
149.    Technical risks. The implementation capacity of the public sector may be overstretched
by the need for significant program policy and reform actions, exacerbating the weak
governmental machinery at regional and local levels. Local governments traditionally have had
very limited functions and resources and were often overridden by central government
operations. Since monitoring and evaluation have traditionally been rare, ensuring effective
quality and transparency on public spending will be especially challenging.
150.   The following actions will entail specific risks:
           (a) Risk of paralyzing social and infrastructure investment programs-particularly
               those targeted to the poor-during the transfer of responsibilities from specialized
               central government units to transition to regional and local governments. The
               government is committed to reducing this risk by (i)     providing in the budget bill
               for 2004 that in case a municipality does not receive accreditation, the transfer of
               resources for social investment programs will go to MIMDES; (ii)            seeking to
               extend simplified procurement requirements to regional and local governments
               managing these programs; and (iii)proceeding quickly with approval of new
               fiscal responsibility and transfers laws so that new inter-government supervision
               arrangements can be included in the new budget, including greater monitoring of
               the decentralization of service-delivery, especially inthe social sectors.
           (b) Accreditation is a technically difficult, politically sensitive tool. It entails the risk
               of (i)rapid accreditation without satisfaction of the criteria as a result of
               overriding political pressures (what has happenedelse where), or (ii)     political use
               of accreditation to restrain some regional and local governments or promote
               others. The government intends to prevent this risk by (i)introducing objective
               and verifiable accreditation requirements, (ii)delegating assessment of the


                                                45

               process to external (civil society) agencies, and (iii)     requiring publication of
               accreditation assessments.                            ~




          (c) Civil sewice reform is bound to be a slow, risky process at the regional level. The
               creation of new regional governments entails the risks of (i)   rapid expansions of
               payrolls and/or wage bill; and (ii) rapid rotation of current employees to appoint
               political sympathizers, weakening government capacity due to inexperienced
               newcomers. The latter i s not a great risk, however. The stability of regular
               employees i s protected by law and a large proportion of the employees of the
               former CTARs (predecessors to current regional governments) was temporary in
               any case. Besides, the government is committed to minimizing the risk of a
               growing regional payroll by its strict limits on the growth and size of current
               expenditures. A study tour for civil service reform for key policy makers was
               undertaken to discuss lessons learned to strengthen capacity for sustainable civil
               service reform.
151.   These and other risks are well recognized, and some are already being addressed. The
MEF and the Council of Ministers have so far successfully articulated and defended the goals
and means of the decentralization program, and should continue to provide leadership. The
Congress has been collaborative rather than antagonistic. Positive "ownership" for reform has
been demonstrated by line ministries and autonomous government institutions. Civil society i s
also very supportive, particularly about transparency and participatory budgeting. It has been
active in monitoring decentralization through its social oversight system, designed to track
decentralization progress and obstacles, such as the "Purticipa Ped' inititiative.
152.   The Bank as well has actedto help mitigate these risks. It collaborated successfully with
the authorities in designing support for the decentralization program through a series of phased
steps, with adequate provision for mid-course reviews and possibly needed modifications. This
should lessen exposure, unduly optimistic programming and unrealistic expectations. Other
Bank actions taken include (i)structuring reform support in selected sectors around programs
and processes rather than projects and actions, as has already occurred in the social sectors; (ii)
maintaining an active technical partnership with the Government for the preparation of this
operation in strengthening sensitive aspects, e.g., tax decentralization, inter-government
transfers, regional and local      debt and competitiveness strategies; (iii)stressing fiscal
sustainability in the conditions for maintaining a "high case" lending program; and (iv)
simultaneously ensuring under PSRLIII that there would be sufficient budget resources for
protection of social services. In order to reinforce these activities, the Bank intends to continue
working closely together with the with the IADB and other donors on public sector management
improvement efforts
153.   Inaddition, the Bankhas opened, andwill maintain, a close dialogue with different parts
of government, including the new regional presidents and local governments. The Bank has
similarly been active in engaging Congress and political parties on the legal framework on
decentralization. and its implementation. Furthermore, the Bank will continue to seek to be
actively engagedwith civil society.
154.   To help mitigate technical risks, the Bank will provide technical assistance on the
monitoring and evaluation of the planned transfers of social programs and support to civil
society. The Government will establish a Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) system to track
progress with the outcome monitoring indicators of this programmatic loan (see Table 7). The

M&E system will be based at the Ministry of Finance, and will pay particular attention to (i)

                          LEGALFRAMEWORKDECENTRALIZATION
                                                 FOR

       The legal framework for Peru's decentralization i s formed by numbers of laws and
regulations that are beinggradually enacted. Congress, different levels of government, political
parties and civil society participate in this complex policy-making process. The 2002 Ley de
Bases, which establishes the basic legal framework for decentralization, addresses (i)the
assignment of services and responsibilities of different government levels; (ii)     fiscal rules and
transfer mechanisms; (iii)   the creation of the National Decentralization Council; and (iv) the
sequencingof the decentralization process.
       The Law envisions a decentralization processwhich is:
       Permanent:legally bindingon all government institutions
       Irreversible: aiming to meet long-term goals
       Democratic: promotingequality of opportunity andbroad participation
       Integral: encompassingthe whole country, includingboththe public and private sector
        Gradual: takingplace inprogressive steps
       Italso establishes specific principles of the decentralization process:
       clear definition of responsibilities;
       transparency andpredictability;
       fiscal neutrality, by avoiding any transfers of resources that are not accompanied by a
       transfer of responsibilities; and
       fiscal sustainability, through fiscal rules governing the regional and local entities'
       indebtedness and spending.
       Key objectives of decentralization are to promote:
       economic development and competitiveness;
       modernization and simplification of administrative systems and processes;
       assigning responsibility for public services to levels closest to the users; and
       citizenparticipation ingovernance.


       The following section summarizes progress on the complex web of decentralization

or expects to take from 2001 through the first quarter of 2004. It i s based on the legislative

Tax on Financial Transactions
        The Government i s considering the implementation of a tax on financial transactions to
temporarily increase collections and obtain essential information for controlling income tax
evasion. It would tax banking debits and credits, collection operations and cash transfers for
organized payment systems.
        Part of the idea is to emulate results obtained in Brazil's successful implementation of a
provisional financial activity tax. The information this tax provided enabled the Brazilian
government to cross information from income tax declarations with that on taxpayers' financial
activity. As a result, tax collection in Brazil increasedby 26 percent, and 126 percent in some
municipalities.
        A low aliquot of approximately 2 per 1,000 should be established for this temporary tax.
It has been suggested that after a temporary period, the same aliquot be maintained, but as a
payment on account of the GST and the income tax. This could prevent a skewed tax burden on
formal economy taxpayers and a tax on informal taxpayers that could leadto their formalization.
Furthermore, valuable information might be obtained to combat illegal money flows, and to
detect financial activity allowing adjustments inthe GST and income tax.

        The objective of this annex i s to assist the Government of Peru in improving country
competitiveness/productivityand increasing growth and employment, with special emphasis in
facilitating export development as an engine of growth and regional development. As a result of
Peru's recent political decentralization and the creation of regional governments, and because a
large part of Peru's export potential rest in products produced in the regions away from Lima,
this assistance places a special emphasis in facilitating and strengthening the capacity of the
new regional governments and coordinating with the private sector in improving regional
productive output and exports.
        The success of the decentralization process, to a large extent, will be measured by the
increased level of development and economic activity of the regions, along with increased
employment and reduction on poverty. To secure those benefits, improvements in countrywide
competitiveness and productivity are necessary, and efforts to address key competitiveness and
productivity bottlenecks must accompany the decentralization process.
        Peru's Competitiveness Position: Regardless of the measure or indicator one uses to
evaluate country competitiveness, Peru's ranking i s very low. Inone of the most commonly used
competitiveness studies16Peru ranks, on aggregate, between 60th and 68th of 75-80 countries
surveyed. Peru's sector or thematic competitiveness rankings are equally disappointing.
        Export Performance:       Given Peru's low competitive standings, Peru's poor export
performance over the last decade should not be surprising, given the correlation between
competitiveness and exports.      Peru's poor export performance (Table 1 and Figure 1) is
particularly strikingwhen compared to other countries inLatin America, and even more so when
noting that Peru is a rich country with abundant mines, large fish-stocks and rich lands and
forests. International experience clearly shows that exports can be a powerful engine of
economic recovery and growth, but this potential has been left unrealized in Peru due most
importantly to a lack of overall competitiveness.

        Government Priorities: Aware of this unrealized potential, the Government has made

        Peru's main advancements to date in competitiveness: Peru has made important
advances since the early 1990s to put in place an appropriate policy environment for
competitiveness and exports: (i)       external tariffs have been more than halved; (ii)non-tariff

flows; (v) there has been significant private sector participation in infrastructure, such as the

regulatory agencies, including an integrated transport regulatory agency. As well, in 2002 the

         Quality: In today's world markets, while price remains as a key factor, standards dictate
entry. This i s particularly important in agriculture and fisheries where every one of Peru's
successful competitors from Brazil and Chile to South Africa and Australia have adopted sector-
wide standards that meet the global benchmarks of most professional buyers. Peru has failed to
do that. Standards are becomingthe yardstick not only to measurethe quality of the products, but
especially how well were they manufactured. Hazard Critical Control Point (HACCP) and I S 0
9000 are today essential requisites for international trade. As an indicator, by the end of 2002,
only 201 companies in Peru were certified to I S 0 9000 standards, while Colombia has about
600, Chile about 800, andBrazil around 7,000.

rarely comply with standards and basic norms required to access world markets, thus not
surprisingly their poor showing in exports. In addition, in the agro industry sector the relevant
institutions sectors lack capacity and speed of response to the ever increasing demands of
producers for phytosanitary certificates required by intemational buyers. Moreover, the essential
and effective reciprocity agreements and programs between countries, so as to facilitate access
are limited. Supply i s very fragmented, supply chains are rarely integrated, and investment on
innovation is quite low. Knowledge i s limited, and its diffusion is low as a result of limited
forward and backward linkages. Thus technological development in Peruvian products i s
reduced.

region, amounting to a bit over US$300 in 2002, which is far below the WTO's published world

sectors, i s reflectiveof a low level of competitiveness. While Peru has succeededas low-income
factor-driven economy-characterized by a reliance on primary products and commodities-it
has yet to transition to a higher value-added investment-driven economy, in which efficiency in
producing standard products and services becomes a dominant source of competitiveness.21
Such a transition i s not an easy one to make, however it will be critical if Peru is to move beyond
its traditional primary commodity base of exports, in pursuit of higher, more sustainable
economic growth.

       Entry and exit of firms remains costly and problematic in Peru. In The Other Path,
Hernando de Soto shows that the prohibitively high cost of establishing a business inPeru denies
economic opportunity to the poor. In 1983 de Soto's research team followed all necessary
bureaucratic procedures in setting up a one-employee garment factory in the outskirts of Lima.

entrepreneur has. Today it takes one hundred and fourteen days and US$463.5 starting a
business. However it i s highcomparing with other countries, as can be seen inTable 5.
       The last decade brought a surge of FDIto Peru, boosting the average annual inflows to
about $2.5 billion, up from $40 million in the late 1980s. By 1996, Peru appeared on the top 10
list of developing country recipients of FDI. The vast majority of these capital flows was
resource-seeking-primarily   in mining-and     asset-seeking, thanks to a successful privatization
program. At the height of the privatizationprogram from 1994 to 1997, nearly 70 percent of FDI
resulted from the transfer of state-owned assets. Sectors that benefited most from these flows

                                            Annex IX
                             PUBLIC DEBTSUSTAINABILITY:      ANALYSIS
        Strong economic growth and significant progress in the fiscal front during the period
2002-03 have contributed to a declining trend in Peru's public sector level of indebtedness. In
addition, recent successful debt management operations such as the placement of a 10-year
global bond and the retirement of Brady bonds have moved Peru further towards the
achievement of a more manageable debt situation.              However, despite these positive
developments, a key concern is whether the current level of public debt is sustainable in the
mediumterm, .i.e. if the country will be able to meet its future debt service obligations without
compromising growth. This annex examines Peru's projected debt dynamics for the period
2003-2010 under alternative scenarios. It concludes that, under the assumption of continuation
of current economic policies and a favorable external environment, the downward trend in the
public debt-to-GDP ratio should continue over the mediumterm.

MacroeconomicBackground: Economic Rebound and Structural Reforms

        Despite complex domestic politics; economic activity has proven resilient, and the
medium-term outlook remains favorable. In 2002, a year of deep recession for Latin America
(average GDP growth was -1.2 percent), Peru's economy outperformed the region. Led by
mining, manufacturing and construction, real GDP grew 5.3 percent, nearly two points abovethe
original forecasts of 3.5 percent. On the demand side, domestic consumption growth has been
strong and the main growth driver. Investment has been gradually rising, albeit from a low base.
The annual inflation rate at 1.5 percent (end-of period) was the second lowest in the region in
2002.
        Early indicators of economic activity suggest that Peru's recovery i s broad based and the
prospects for 2003 are equally good. Overall, domestic factors remain strong and real GDP
growth is projected at 4 percent for 2003.        The primary sector has benefited from high
international prices (notably copper and gold prices), and non-traditional exports should benefit
from the Andean Trade Promotion andDrugEradication Act (ATPDEA), which will likely boost
textile exports. Inflation has been kept at relatively low levels. The official target for 2003
inflation is 2.5 percent and international reserves are adequate-more     than twice the level of
short term external debt liabilities-and  increasing due to the dynamic export performance and
strong private capital inflows. Supported by declining international interest rates, spreads on
local and dollar interest rates remain stable. The unemployment rate shows a moderate declining
trendearly 2003, although unemployment inLimahas not fallen andmost employment increases
have beenconcentratedin large firms.
        Fiscal imbalances have been gradually reduced. In 2002, the fiscal deficit target of 2.1
percent of GDP was successfully met by the non-financial public sector. The strong fiscal
performance i s likely to continue as the Government has made the necessary tax, expenditure
and debt management reforms to guide fiscal and debt management in coming years. The
projected small primary surplus for 2003 would be the first since 1998, and the 2 percent of GDP
fiscal deficit target of the Non-Financial Public Sector (NFPS) i s estimated to be met this year.
These achievements are partly the outcome of a gradual tax reform that aims to increase
revenues, widen the tax base and give the tax collection agency strengthened powers to reduce
tax evasion. Tax measures have led to a marked improvement in the tax ratio, projected to be



                                                97

 12.7 percent of GDP for 2003. Improving fiscal balances have also benefited from austere
expenditure policies.          ~                                           ~~~~~         ~~                        ~




        The economic rebound of the last few years and gradual fiscal consolidation have
contributed to improved debt burden indicators. Total public debt-to-GDP ratio has decreased
from 48 percent of GDP in 1999 to an estimated 44 percent in 2003. External public debt
represents 78 percent of total public debt and i s virtually all long term.29 Yet, the sustainability
of Peru's public debt i s exposedto some moderaterisks.                  While domestic debt burden is modest
at 9 percent of GDP, Peru's high degree of dollarization makes it vulnerable to exchange rate
risks and limits the degrees of freedom for fiscal and monetary policy.

                                           Debt Sustainability Analysis

Assumptions

        General assumptions for both the baseline scenario and alternative scenarios are spelled
out in Box 1. The baseline scenario assumes a continuation of the current prudent
macroeconomic policies and reforms. This scenario i s predicated on the assumption that real
GDP andexport growth are robust throughout the projection period. Macroeconomic conditions
remain stable, with average inflation hovering around 2 percent per year. Average GDP growth
over the period 2003-10 inthis scenario i s roughly 4.2 percent. Gross reservesremain high at an
average of 9.2 months of import cover over the same period. Underpinningthis scenario i s the
 assumption of the authorities' continued commitment to adhere to fiscal soundness. The overall
NFPS deficit narrows to less than 1percent by the end of the projection period.

percent of GDP by the end of the projection period. Although the projected share of debt service
ingovernment revenuesfor the next few yearsremainsrelatively highat 33 percent, the prospect
of a declining debt to GDP ratio and implementation of prudent fiscal policies should boost
private sector confidence and facilitate debt management in light of the bunching of debt
servicing inthe next few years.

Low Growth Scenario

         This scenarioassumes a less favorable external environment and/or slower output growth
due to backtracking on fiscal and other key reforms. In particular, GDP growth i s assumed to
slow from 4 percent in 2003 to an average of 2 percent during the 2004-2010 period (average
growth rate of the period 1998-02). The overall NFPS deficit returns to the levels of 1998-01,
i.e. 2.4 percent of GDP.
         A deceleration of growth to 2 percent over the period 2004-2010 could threaten Peru's
medium-term debt sustainability. Debt and debt service indicators increase steadily throughout
the projection period. he debt-to-GDP ratio reaches 49 percent by 2010, about 14 percentage
points higher that in the baseline scenario. Under this scenario, the debt-service ratio instead of
declining from 2004, would rise again to over 25 percent of exports of goods and services, and
stay above that level for the remainder of the period.       Reflecting the higher debt service
associated with larger financing gaps and higher borrowing costs, the fiscal situation could
worsen significantly.    Debt service as a ratio of GDP reachesover 5 percent of GDP, compared
to 3.5 percent in the baseline scenario. Debt service as a share of tax revenues increases steadily
to over 45 percent.

High Growth Scenario

        This is the best case scenario,which presents a sensitivity analysis of the debt-GDP ratio
to higher growth supported by fiscal discipline and higher investment to GDP ratio than in the
baseline case. GDP growth rates are assumed to be 5.5 percent during 2004-05 followed by 5
percent over the period 2006-10. Interest rates on new debt decline from 10 percent to 7.5
percent from 2004 onward. The NFPS fiscal deficits decline from 1.9 percent in 2003, to 1.3
percentin2004, and 0.7 percent from 2005 onward.

       The debt-GDP ratio falls to 31 percent-in 2010 under this scenariofrom 44.7 percent in
2003. The primary balance required to cover interest liabilities remains stable at roughly 1.1
percent over the projection period. The debt servicing ratio falls from 22 percent in 2003 to 17
percent as a proportion of exports of goods and services, decreases slightly from 4.5 percent to
3.8 percent as a proportion of GDP, and falls from 35.6 percent to 31.5 percent as a proportion of
central government tax revenue, over the period.

                                           Conclusion

       The results of the analysis suggest that Peru's public debt dynamics are unlikely to be a
cause of concern in light o f strong growth prospects and the authorities' commitment to a
prudent borrowing strategy.    While the medium-term outlook is favorable, sensitivity tests
suggest some external vulnerability. If the economy does not perform as well as in the baseline
scenario or if the external environment deteriorates, Peru's faces a potential sustainability
problem over the medium term. The importance thus of strong growth and fiscal consolidation in
ensuring debt sustainability over the medium term cannot be overemphasized.

Dear Mr.Wolfensohn,

This letter is to inform you that President Alejandro Toledo's Administration has begun to
implement a process for the decentralization of powers, responsibilities and resources from the
Central Government to the municipal and regional levels, and to promote a favorable environment
for increasing competitiveness within the country.           The objectives of this process of
decentralization are to promote economic development and competitiveness, encourage the
modernizationand simplification of administrative processes and systems, assign responsibility for
public services to the levels of government closest to the users, and encourage citizen participation
in the design, development, application and supervision of public policies. The objectives under
the heading of increasing competitiveness include raising productivity levels within business
enterprises in order to generate enhanced value added in production; increase the quantity, variety
and quality of products, more particularly those intended for the external market; expand markets,
and promote a favorable climate for investment.
Under the "Decentralization and Competitiveness Policies Program" agreed with the World Bank
(WB), specific reforms are to be implementedinfour areas:

1. Decentralizationand Fiscal Sustainability.

2. InstitutionalDevelopment for Decentralization.

3. Intergovernmental Management Agreements and Evaluation of Decentralization.

4. Competitiveness and Economic Growth.

Inorder to provide a better understanding of the actions to be undertaken, the present document
will describe:

a.      The Peruvian Government's Economic Program, contained in the 2004-2006 Multi-Year
        Macroeconomic Framework, which has received the backing of the International Monetary
        Fund and which encompasses fiscal, external, exchangeandmonetary policies.

b.      The institutional and management framework for the decentralization process, which will
        be presentedinthe same order as the four points outlined above.

I. Background

1.1 This operation represents a collaborative effort on the part of the World Bank and the
     Government of Peru designedprimarily to:

     0  Implement the decentralization process without adversely affecting the quality and
        timeliness of the services and functions being transferred, or undermining prudent
        management of the economy; and

     0  Speed up the introduction of competitiveness within the country, both through the
        implementation of reforms in the institutional area, and through the realization of
        specific policies inthis area.

1.2 Development of the decentralization process i s a matter of crucial importance for the
    Government of Peru, which i s why it i s designing a normative-institutional framework to
    facilitate the process, including suchnormativeactions as:

     0  Amendment of Chapter XIV of Title IV of the Political Constitution of Peru,
        establishing decentralization as a permanent process aimed at ensuring the overall
        development of the country and defining areas of competence for regional and
        municipal governments.


     0  Promulgation of the Law on the Bases for Decentralization (Law No. 27783),
        establishing the objective, principles, goals and general criteria underlying the process
        of decentralization; regulating the structure of the regions and municipalities; defining
        the powers of the three levels of government and determining the assets and resources
        of the regional and local governments; and regulating relations among the three
        separate levels of government.


     0  Promulgation of the Organic Law on Regional Governments (Law No. 27867),
        establishing the purpose and organization of this level of government, its exclusive and
        sharedpowers, its general and specific functions, and the principlesgoverningregional
        policy and administration.


     0  Promulgation of the Organic Law on Municipalities (Law No. 27972), establishing
        the organization, powers and specific functions of municipalities, together with the
        municipal financial system.
1.3 Inaccordance with the Law on the Bases for Decentralization, the generalprinciples of the
    process are intended to support a decentralization that will be (i)permanent, (ii)
    irreversible, (iii)democratic, (iv) comprehensive, and (v) gradual. Thus the Law states
    that the specific principles of the process are a clear definition of responsibilities,
    transparency and predictability, neutrality in the transfer of resources, and fiscal
     sustainability.

11.    Macroeconomic Policy and External FinancialFramework

1.5    The Government's economic program has as its central objectives a frontal attack on
       poverty and unemployment, implying the maintenance of high and sustained rates of
       economic growth over the medium and long term, low rates of inflation, and a level of
       international reserves sufficient to guarantee the sustainability of the country's external
       accounts.

1.6    Despite the crisis that affected a number of countries in the region, the Peruvian economy
       recorded a solid performance in 2002. Exceeding all expectations, the country's Gross
       Domestic Product (GDP) rose 5.2 percent in real terms, cumulative inflation through
       December was 1.5 percent2,the consolidated public sector deficit amounted to 2.2 percent
       of GDP, the deficit on the current account of the balance of payments was 2 percent, and it
       has remained at that level, strengthening the country's external position. Interest rates fell
       to their lowest levels inthe modem history of Peru, and the exchange rate remained stable,
        with a monthly depreciation of only 2.3 percent in 2002.             The banking system was
       strengthenedby an increase in deposits both in national currency and in foreign exchange,

      forward of measuresand policies to improve productivity and competitiveness within Peru.

1.26  The Board (Consejo Directivo) i s the CNC's top deliberative and consultative body. It i s
      responsible for drawing up the general lines of the National Competitiveness Plan, defining
      the Strategy, approving action plans, adopting the decisions required for their application,
      and supervising their execution.

1.27  In addition - as indicated in Article 6 of the Law on the Bases for Decentralization (Law
      No. 27783) - one of the objectives of this process i s to promote self-sustaining economic

     development and competitiveness within the regions and localities making up Peru, as well
     as provision of the economic and social infrastructure neededto promote inversion in the
     various parts of the country.      In this context, it should be noted that the Central
     Government has signed three contracts with subnational governments for the provision of
     infrastructure.

1.28 Moreover, in order to promote a more dynamic entrepreneurial sector and support
     increased competitiveness among businesses, the Law on the National Port System (Ley
     del Sistema Portuario Nacional) (Law No. 27943) has been approved and was published
     on March 1, 2003. The object of this law i s to regulate activities and services within the
     terminals, infrastructure and other installations located in maritime, river and lake ports; to
     promote the development and competitiveness of ports; and facilitate multimodal
     transportation operations, the modernization of port infrastructures, and the development of
     logistics chains inwhich the ports participate.

.29  Proposed policy reformsfor the sectoral program: The objective of these reforms i s to
     strengthen the institutional underpinning for the competitiveness program and to develop
     and implement the National Competitiveness Strategy, by aligning the policies and actions
     (both public and private sector) needed to ensure successful implementation of the
     National Competitiveness Plan.          Likewise, to coordinate development of the
     competitiveness and decentralization processes, promote private sector participation, and
     ensure a favorable environment for investments.

1.30 Inparticular, the actions proposedas part of this Programarecenteredonthe issuanceby
     the Central Government of guidelines for drawing up the National Competitiveness Plan,
     together with the promotionof regionalcompetitiveness plans on a pilot basis.

1.31 In addition, in order to promote an institutional environment conducive to private
     initiatives, an Administrative Procedures Information and Monitoring System (Sistema de
     Informacidn y Monitoreo de Trbmites Administrativos - SIMTA) will be developed within
     government agencies, starting on a pilot basis with four institutions. This system will
     make it possible to monitor the procedures businesses are required to follow, the status of
     the application and the name of the official responsible for handling it, and ultimately to
     develop a unified register of functions and procedures and thereby reduce the time and
     costs involved in establishing a business. Lastly, this system will form an institutional
     basis for the development of government electronic tools serving both citizens and
     businesses.

1.32 The system will be developed within central government agencies dealing with such
     business procedures as permits, authorizations and processing of payments, including a
     measuring and efficiency mechanismthat will show the status of the procedure inquestion,
     the time taken and the official responsible ("tramitdmetro" or "processometer"), and a
     unifiedregister of functions and procedures which can be used to develop an institutional
     basis for the creation of government electronic tools serving bothcitizens andbusinesses

1.33 Given the importance for the development of business enterprises of adopting minimum
     quality standards and continuing innovation, the Central Government, acting through the
     National Institute for the Defense of Competition and Protection of Intellectual Property

        (Fondo de Ciencia y Tecnologia) - FONCYT, to promote research and development
        designed to enhance business competitiveness. The National Council for Science and

1.34    As regards the awarding of concessions, the Central Government intends to promote

IV.     Conclusion

As is evident from the above, the Government of Peru i s undertaking major actions to implement a
process of decentralization to help bring about greater efficiency in the allocation of public funds
and strengthen citizen participation, within the framework of a responsibility and transparency
based system of fiscal management.           Similarly, as regards competitiveness, the Central
Government, incoordinationwith the private sector, i s moving forward on buildingan institutional
framework that will favor business development, together with the design and implementation of
specific policies gearedto that objective.

The Government undertakes to continue working in these areas, for which it will need continued
World Bank support inthe areas inquestion.

Inlight ofthe foregoing, the Government of Peruhereby requestsapproval of the "Decentralization
and Competitiveness Policies Program."

Background
After several years of strong performance, Peru's economy weakenedinthe late 1990s, mainly as
a result of adverse external shocks, a weakening structural reform effort, and domestic political
turmoil. Real GDP growth averaged7 percentduring 1993-97, but only 1percent over 1998-
2001. Inflation declined progressively since the early 1990s and was slightly negative by end-
2001.

In2002, economic activityis recovering andreal GDPis expectedto grow by3.7 percent, and
annual inflation i s projected at 2 percent. Employment duringthe first ninemonths of the year
rose 2.3 percent with respectto its level inthe same perioda year earlier. After a sizeable fiscal
stimulusinthe secondhalf of last year, fiscal policy was tightened inearly 2002, andthe
combinedpublic sector deficit i s projected at 2.3 percent of GDP for the entire year. The external
positionis robust, with an expectedaccumulation of official international reservesof over US$1
billion. During2002, progresswas madeinintroducing structural reforms inthe fiscal area,
including on tax andpension system reform, fiscal transparency, andfiscal rules. The authorities
also introduced an inflation targeting framework for monetary policy, with the medium-term
inflation-target centeredat a midpoint of 2.5 percent plushinus 1percentagepoint. Moreover,
continued efforts havebeen madeto strengthenthe banking system, along the lines of
suggestionsfrom the 2000 Financial Sector Assessment Program. Bank portfolios and
profitability improved, loan provisioningwas high, andrisk-adjusted capital-assetratios
increasedfor the systemas a whole. Credit to the private sector has beenbasically flat, but there
are signs that it i s pickingup.

The program for 2003 i s basedon continued economic recovery, with GDP growth projected at
3.5-4.0 percent.Economic activity i s expectedto pick up owing to an improvedinvestment
climate, reflecting a more stableexternal and domestic political environment. Inflationis
targeted at 2.5 percent, and international reserveswould increasemoderately. The external
current account deficit is projected to fall below 2 percent of GDP, with continued strong growth
of traditional exports and a pick up innontraditional exports under the expandedU.S. trade
preference arrangementfor Andean countries.

Executive Board Assessment

Directors commendedthe authorities for their skillful economic management that had facilitated
a positive macroeconomic performance in 2002, despite the weakening external environment and
difficult political situation. Indeed, the Peruvian economy has shown considerable resilience to
adverse developments, especially compared with the region. Directors stressedthat continued
implementationo f sound macroeconomic policies and structural reforms, including a strong
effort on institution building, i s essential for Peruto sustain growth, resist external shocks, and
continue to successfully address the risks relatedto the highly dollarized banking system and the
relatively large public debt.

Directors supported the authorities' overall policy framework, which aims at maintaining
macroeconomic stability and reinforcing an already solid financial system. They notedthat the
policy framework centers appropriately on medium-term fiscal consolidation, inflation targeting
with a floating exchange rate, and supporting structural reforms. Directors stressed that the
authorities should garner the necessary political consensus for this policy agenda, as lack of
adequate ownership could pose a serious risk to successful policy implementation. Equally,
economic performance would benefit from a strengthening of governance inpublic sector
operations.

Directors endorsed the authorities' strategy o f gradual fiscal consolidationwhile supporting the
economic recovery. They emphasized that completing the comprehensive tax reformbegun in
2002 by phasing out regional and sectoral tax exemptions was key to puttingthe public finances
on a sustainable basis. Most Directors expressed concern that the recent tax amnesty could
undermine compliance and suggestedthat it be clearly indicated to taxpayers that the
government will not rely on tax amnesties inthe future. With regard to expenditures, Directors
welcomed the recent restraint on increases inwages and pensions. They also welcomed the on-
going work to improve the quality of public expenditure to ensure that the most vulnerable of the
population are well protected. Inaddition, it would be important to increase the level o f social
spending inorder to alleviate poverty-this could also help mobilize political acceptance for the
reform agenda.

Directors stressedthe importance o f carrying out fiscal decentralization in a cautious and fiscally
neutral manner. They encouraged the authorities to work closely with the World Bank and Inter-
American Development Bank inimplementing decentralization and considered that pilot
programs for the transfer of expenditure responsibilities could be useful vehicles to test the fiscal
impact of decentralization. Directors also stressed the importance of early congressional approval
o f the revisedfiscal responsibility law as it contains important regulations on regional and local
government finances.




                                                   126

Directors welcomed the authorities' intention to reactivatethe domestic bondmarket, pointing
out that the development of this market would havebroadpositive effects for the economy and
provide protection against adverse developmentsininternational capital markets.

While notingthe change inthe privatization strategy inview of strong public opposition to
outright privatization, Directors encouragedthe authorities to continue to seek ways for private
sector participation instate enterprise operations.They welcomed the authorities' plans for
continued reform of the public pension system. Directors warned, however, that the proposal to
lower the retirement age inthe public pension systemwas likely to have significant negative
consequencesfor the public finances.

Directors endorsedthe authorities' inflation-targeting framework for monetary policy and
commended the central bank for maintaining inflation at low levels. They recommended
continued work to refine the inflation forecasting process and to strengthenits integration with
the monetary policy decision-making framework.

Directors considered that the current flexible exchangerate policy has servedPeruwell in
adjusting to external shocks, andthey supportedthe authorities' intentionto continue with the
present regime.

Directors welcomed the sound financial position of the banking system, which has improved in
recent years inthe wake of the significant consolidation that the systemhas experienced.They
consideredit important that the supervisory agency continue to enhanceprudential oversight,
especially inevaluating credit risk derived from foreign-currency lending operations and in
ensuring that all bankshave adequatecapital.

Directors considered reducing the high degree of financial dollarization as an important goal that,
while requiringtime, will dependmainly on continued pursuit of prudent macroeconomic
policies, aided by a strong prudential framework and development of the domestic capital
market.They notedthat the highlevel of official international reserveshas beenakey factor in
Peru's ability to avoid the kindof contagion that has affected other highly dollarized economies
inthe region, andthey welcomed the authorities' commitment to keep reservesat highlevels.

Directors welcomed the authorities' progresswith anti-money laundering andcombating the
financing of terrorism and encouragedthem to continue their efforts inthis area.

Directors supportedthe authorities' intentionto continue to maintain both an open trade and
paymentsregime and the current relativelyflexible system of labor market norms. They
encouragedthe authorities to implement as soon as possible a mechanism for protecting the
unemployed.

Directors noted that Peruwas one of the early subscribers to the Special DataDissemination
Standard. They welcomed the improvedtransparencyinmonetary policy following the adoption
of inflation-targeting and strengthenedbankingsystemdata. Directors viewedthe Reports on the
Observanceof Standards and Codes modules on fiscal transparency and data as important steps
to improving information dissemination.
