                                  I.      INTRODUCTION

1.         The Colombian economy i s facing the impacts of a sharp aggregate demand
contraction, but sound macroeconomic policies implemented in recent years have
substantially improved the country's economic resilience against crisis. In the near term,
economic activity i s likely to reverse i t s decline and the Bank's base scenario for Colombia
includes GDP growth o f 0.4 percent in 2009 and a modest rebound o f economic activity in
2010. Despite the impact o f global conditions, Colombia i s in a better position to confront the
global crisis than other L A C countries, as a result o f i t s countercyclical monetary policy and
fiscal stimulus over 2009. While there remains uncertainty as regards to the depth and length
o f the global recession, neither external nor fiscal sustainability appear at risk.

2.         Over the past few years, the Government's strategy in social protection has
focused mostly on expansion of coverage for core social assistance, social insurance
(health and old-age), and training programs. In addition, the Government has focused on
issues o f efficiency, as illustrated, among others, by the effort to develop a national targeting
system which can be used by all programs to reach the poorest and most vulnerable and by the
efforts to design and begin the implementation o f core information systems. Despite important
gains, challenges remain, in particular in expanding the coverage o f social insurance and
strengthening programs to foster employability through training, labor intermediation, and
unemployment services. Efforts are also required to reach the poorest and most vulnerable
families and to promote greater coordination o f programs.

3.       The crisis has highlighted both the strengths and the weaknesses of the existing
social protection system. The crisis and the response o f the Government have shown that
core social protection programs were able to protect the poorest and that the system could
function within a crisis context. This i s the legacy o f a decade o f investment in programs and
information systems. However, the crisis also highlighted remaining needs for greater
coverage o f social insurance programs and active labor market policies, and better integration.

4.      The operation proposes to consolidate past gains and support the reform agenda
in areas that need strengthening and consolidation. The development objective o f the
proposed operation i s to strengthen an inclusive, equitable and eflcient social protection
system by: (1) promoting greater integration, efficiency and equity o f social insurance, (2)
improving the targeting, efficiency, and articulation o f social assistance programs, and (3)
promoting higher quality training and employment services. To achieve this objective, the
operation supports policies in a broad range o f areas, in particular:

   efforts to increase the coverage o f inclusive systems serving the entire population such as
   the national training and labor intermediation system or health insurance,
   efforts to build an equitable system that serves the poor and vulnerable through improved
   targeting and coordination o f social assistance and greater efforts to reach the poorest, and

     systems, rationalization, simplification o f processes, and improvements in program

                                1.      COUNTRY CONTEXT

6.         Colombia i s entering a period of political change, with presidential elections
planned for M a y 2010. The current President has announced his interest in seeking a third
term, and his popularity remains high, largely because o f important security gains during his
administrations. However, the legality o f a third term i s s t i l l uncertain (the Constitution was
modified in 2005 to allow for a second term o f the President). Although a change in
leadership could lead to changes in policy orientation, Colombia has a tradition o f continuity
in its policy frameworks, partly as a result o f its democratic processes, emphasis on principles
o f results-based management, focus on evaluations o f core programs, and efforts to strengthen
institutions and systems. Hence, significant reversals in the sectors supported by this
operation are unlikely to be made on the basis o f political affiliation.

7.      The macroeconomic and fiscal policy framework i s adequate for the purpose o f
this operation. As presented below in more detail, the Government has maintained sound
macroeconomic policies, and external and fiscal sustainability are not a major concern. Even a
low growth scenario does not pose a serious problem to the public debt sustainability.

Recent Economic Developments in Colombia
8.       The Colombian economy i s starting to show signs of recovery. During 2008,
economic growth decelerated in the first three quarters and turned negative in the last quarter,
thereby bringing GDP growth for the year at a modest 2.5 percent. Since then, GDP has
increased modestly in the first two quarters o f 2009 (on a seasonally adjusted quarterly basis).
O n the basis o f recent developments in economic activity, the Bank's base scenario for
Colombia includes GDP growth o f 0.4 percent in 2009.

9.       Prior to the onset of the global economic downturn, Colombia exhibited high
economic growth within a framework o f enhanced macroeconomic stability. The
Colombian economy experienced strong and sustained growth over the 2003-2007 period
supported by the favorable external environment and sound economic policies. Real economic
growth accelerated from 2.5 percent in 2002 to 7.5 percent in 2007, inflation moderated
during the period, and the current account deficit remained modest despite strong domestic
demand, particularly in investment. Strong growth supported unemployment reduction from
over 17 percent in 2002 to approximately 12 percent in 2007. High external demand,
improved terms o f trade and lower cost o f international credit created a favorable environment
for growth, supported by the improved security situation in Colombia and stable
macroeconomic policies.

10.     I n 2008 economic growth decelerated sharply as exports declined harshly and
internal demand grew moderately. In the fourth quarter o f 2008, year-on-year economic
growth stood at -1.1 percent, partly reflecting past monetary tightening that resulted in a
significant deceleration in credit growth (from a positive growth o f 33.6 percent year-on-year
at end o f 2006 to a decrease o f -44 percent year-on-year in December 2008) and partly
because Banks around the world were hoarding liquidity due to heightened risk aversion
during the last four months o f 2008. In the third quarter o f 2008, export growth which had
remained relatively resilient, collapsed due to the global slowdown. The fourth quarter
followed a similar negative path, and the current account for 2008 ended up in a deficit o f 2.8
percent o f GDP, a similar figure to the one in 2007.

 11.    Prices have been following a declining trend after the fuel and food price hike.
Shocks to fuel and food prices pushed inflation to 7.7 percent at end-2008 well in excess o f
the Central Bank's target o f 5 percent for 2008. In spite o f this, inflationary pressures have
been decelerating already since end-2008 as the external price shock was corrected. As a
result, Banco de la Repziblica authorities have lowered the policy interest rate aiming at a
reactivation o f the economy while prices are expected to continue on a downward path.

Macroeconomic Outlook and Debt Sustainability
12.     Sound macroeconomic policies implemented in recent years have substantially
improved the country's economic resilience against crisis. Several factors ensure external
current account financing in this difficult global conditions: the flexible exchange rate, the
decline in foreign external debt (which has almost halved since 2003 due to the sustained FDI
flows), and adequate international reserves (over US$25.2 billion, or 11 percent o f GDP at
end-October 2009) that more than doubles the annual external debt amortizations. Fiscal
consolidation and the Government public debt management strategy put public debt in a
declining path and reduce the share o f foreign denominated debt. The authorities' successful
placement o f US$2.0 billion o f 10 year bonds in international markets in addition to securing
funds from multilaterals in order to meet public sector external financing requirements further
reduces financial vulnerabilities. The exchange rate appreciated in recent months and i s back
now to 2008 levels.

14.    Despite the impact of global conditions, the impact in Colombia has not been as
severe as feared, as the country i s in a better position to confront the global crisis than
other LAC countries. Factors that typically magnify external shocks resulting in severe
economic disruptions, were not present in Colombia. In some Latin American countries,
external shocks have been typically amplified by severely appreciated real exchange rates,
weak domestic financial sectors and real and financial dollarization. In the Colombian case,
the factors that typically magnify crisis acted as a muffler. Colombia entered the turbulence
period with an exchange rate value close to i t s equilibrium level. The flexible exchange rate
acted as a shock absorber helping to counter the deceleration in external demand. The strength

                                                               4
o f the financial sector, with healthy financial indicators, has prevented a credit crunch, and
credit to the private sector has recovered significantly in the last months. Financial
dollarization i s not a destabilizing factor in Colombia.

15.     I n spite of limited space for pursuing an aggressive counter-cyclical policy, the
measures implemented by the authorities contributed to mitigate the impact of a global
slowdown on growth and employment generation of the domestic economy. Monetary
policy was actively used to counter the effects o f the cycle as weaken demand and lower food
and oil prices kept inflation on a downward trend. The sharp reduction in public sector debt to
GDP ratio during the past years has also provided room for some fiscal loosening. As
reflected in Table 2, Government expenditure increased by 2.5 percentage points between
2008 and 2009, from 26.9 percent to 29.4 percent o f GDP. In addition, the GoC obtained an
IMF precautionary flexible credit line (FCL) for access to U S $ l l . 1 billion.

16.     While the Colombian economy i s starting to show signs o f recovery, uncertainty
remains as regards to the velocity of the upturn. While risks to the macroeconomic
projections for 2010 are on the downside, external and fiscal sustainability are not a major
concern. Given the current environment, a fall in o i l prices would negatively affect the fiscal
outlook and the current account balance. The political tensions between Colombia and
Venezuela could further deteriorate the country's current account. T o gauge these risks a l o w
growth scenario i s evaluated. The scenario shows the impact o f a drop o f the o i l price to a l o w
o f US$32 per barrel and a deterioration o f the external environment during 2010. The
simulation indicates that Colombia has enough reserves to cushion a potential external

in social protection are likely to have a fiscal impact. In particular:

   Pension system. Reforms in 2005 reduced liabilities by 19 percentage points o f GDP and
   the overall net present value o f pension contingent liabilities was reduced from 260
   percent o f GDP in 1993 to 160 percent in 2007. Government estimates project that

     pension transfers will peak around 5 percent o f GDP in 2010 and decline significantly

     and a l o w retirement age (reformed in 1993), l o w efficiency (no integration o f

     strong economic growth during the period. This operation supports efforts to improve

Poverty Reduction and Inequality
 18.      By 2008, the nationwide poverty rate stood at 46 percent of the population, and
the extreme poverty rate at 17.8 percent2. These represent a decline in poverty o f 8
percentage points and a decline in extreme poverty o f 2 percentage points since 2002. With
these trends, Colombia i s moving slowly towards achieving the Millennium Development
Goals by 2015, though this w i l l require sustaining the increase in social spending and
improvements in targeting. Indeed, the decrease in extreme poverty has actually stalled since
2005 when it reached a bottom o f 15.7 percent, before rebounding to 17.8 percent.

19.      Improvements in national averages mask important ethnic, gender, and
regional differences and substantial disparities remain. Poverty in rural areas remains high
at 65 percent in 2008 compared with 40 percent in urban areas, as presented in Figure 2.
Extreme poverty has actually increased in rural areas by 5 percentage points between 2005
and 2008. Also, Afro-Colombians (who represent 10.5 percent o f the population) and
indigenous peoples (3.4 percent) experience poverty disproportionately. With the poorest 20
percent o f the population receiving only 3 percent o f national income, Colombia has a highly
unequal income distribution. The Gini index remains stubbornly around .59, the third-worst in
Latin America, behind Paraguay and Brazil. Promoting the inclusion o f vulnerable groups
remains one o f Colombia's greatest challenges.

The Global Crisis and the Social Protection System's Response

20.      The global crisis can impact households through a series of channels, including
labor markets, remittances and public services. In terms o f labor markets, shocks can happen
through loss o f employment, reduction in the number o f hours employed, and/or in hourly
wage. This seems to have affected urban areas mostly, as illustrated by an increased urban
unemployment rate and the decrease in the number o f formal sector workers registered in the
national registry. In terms o f remittances, Colombia has experienced a decline since the last
quarter o f 2008, expected to amount to 15 percent in 2009. Finally, in terms o f the provision
o f public services, the Government has had to reduce i t s planned investment expenditure by
3.6 percent in the Spring o f 200g3.In the area o f social protection, core investments have been
protected and even expanded in some cases. T w o o f the core agencies providing social
protection services have experienced limited reductions in the planned investment
expenditure, by 2 percent for Accidn Social (which manages Familias en Accidn, the core
conditional cash transfer program and Juntos, the Government's strategy to help the extreme
poor and displaced out o f poverty) and by 1.5 percent for the Ministry o f Social Protection
(which covers subsidized health insurance, public health, and social protection). The
institutions most affected, with a decrease o f over 5 percent over the planned level, were the
Ministries o f Transport, Justice, Planning, as well as Agriculture, Defense and Education.
2 1.    Colombia's earlier investments in developing a comprehensive social protection
system have limited losses for the poorest. While challenges remain in terms o f coverage o f
the system and i t s efficiency, the programs in place were able to maintain a minimum welfare
level for their beneficiaries during the crisis, preventing households from engaging into
depletive coping strategies which result in often-irreversible losses in human, physical and
financial assets. Some o f the Social Protection programs were also able to scale up to respond
to the crisis. This, combined with the increased investments in infrastructure, seem to have
somewhat contained impacts on households. The crisis, however, also revealed the rather
limited reach o f active labor market policies and unemployment programs in particular, an
area where the Government will need to invest in the coming years.

and launched a stimulus package, focusing both on investments in infrastructure and on

28.       T h e crisis provides a catalyst for renewed efforts to consolidate the social

including conversations around labor market rigidities in terms o f payroll taxes and a high
minimum wage, which limit the ability o f the social insurance system to expand its coverage;
conversations around the need for further coordination and integration o f programs especially
in their focus on the poorest; conversations on the need to reform programs that are not
efficient or effective; and conversations on the need for an integrated system for human
capital and skills formation, which brings together all types o f education and learning. The
crisis hence provides an opportunity to consolidate gains, seek cost-cutting measures and
increases in efficiency, and develop reforms in the area o f active labor market policies.

Lessons Learned
40.       Lessons learned from earlier operations were taken into account. In particular5:
      Reforms for the development of an integrated social protection system require strong
      commitment. Most second-generation reforms, which aim at integrating and harmonizing
      programs to provide a consistent and comprehensive system o f social protection, are very
      complex and require medium term commitment and support.

      For institutional reforms, the difficulty mostly resides in the reforms'
      implementation, rather than in their design or adoption. When reforms aim at bringing
      institutions to coordinate and articulate the provision o f services, the greatest hurdles
      typically l i e in the actual implementation o f the reforms. Indeed, getting institutions to
      agree to a model might be within easy reach, but ensuring they effectively do implement it
      i s much more complex, as incentives typically need to be re-aligned.

      The implementation of core reforms to the national training system takes many
      years, reflecting the complexity o f the area. The development o f a national training
      system, with numerous operators in the private sector, requires the definition o f a common
      framework, but also the adoption o f this framework as the regulatory norms for actors in
      the sector. Reforms in this area have been slow, but very significant progress has been
      made. A slower pace might have been key to ensuring full ownership.

      A strong program of analytical work i s crucial to underpin the operation. The
      program required both country and international knowledge to operationalize the
      strategies. In particular, Bank knowledge was a key input t o innovative strategies.

  V.   THE PROPOSED DEVELOPMENT POLICY LOAN ON PROMOTING AN
   INCLUSIVE, EQUITABLE AND EFFICIENT SOCIAL PROTECTION SYSTEM

4 1.     The proposed operation supports selected policy actions that continue and deepen a
broad reform program in three key social protection policy areas. The vision i s that o f a social
protection system that responds adequately to the needs o f all Colombians in terms o f
consumption smoothing (helping households to save for old age and insure themselves against
risks such as unemployment or sickness), poverty prevention (helping the poorest reach a
minimum welfare and access basic services), and human capital accumulation (contributing to
better skills and the accumulation o f human capital to reduce dependency on social security
and promote self-insurance). In particular, the reform program aim at equipping the country
with the frameworks and systems that form the basis o f a social protection system that i s more
inclusive, equitable and eflcient.

42.      Within this framework, the development objective o f the proposed operation i s to
strengthen an inclusive, equitable and eflcient social protection system by: (1) promoting
greater integration, efficiency and equity o f social insurance, (2) improving the targeting,
efficiency, and articulation o f social assistance programs, and (3) promoting higher quality
training and employment services. To achieve this objective, the operation supports policies
in a broad range o f areas - from targeting systems to pension institutions and active labor
market policies. In particular:

   To build an inclusive system, the program aims at developing or strengthening strong
   national systems for all segments o f Colombia's population - health insurance for all
   citizens, training system and labor intermediation for all workers, etc.
   To build an equitable system, the program aims at strengthening i t s targeting tools,
   orienting some programs towards the poorest, and improving the coordination o f its social
   assistance programs to reach the poorest in an integral fashion. Within the inclusive
   system which serves all Colombians, this ensures that the system focuses on the poorest,
   both by targeting better i t s resources and by developing adapting i t s interventions.
   Finally, to build an eflcient system, the program aims at developing information systems
   that can help coordinate, rationalize and cut transaction costs; establishing efficient
   institutions that can manage the various elements o f the national social protection system;
   and evaluating i t s programs to seek improvements and efficiency gains.

Area 1.1. Improving the processes o collection o contributions for a more eficient
                                     f          f
management o the social insurance system

48.      The Colombian Social Protection system i s a very complex system involving
numerous entities. Some o f these entities receive contributions from workers and employers
on a monthly basis. These include contributions for health insurance, pension, workers
compensation, the Worker's Family Assistance Funds (cajas de compensacidn, which manage
the child allowance and other benefits), the Colombian Institute for Family Welfare (ICBF,
which provides child care services), and the national training institute SENA. Health and
pensions are in turn subdivided in sub-systems, where multiple public and private entities
provide services. Until recently, each entity was responsible for the collection o f i t s
contributions, which impeded a more efficient coordinated effort and represented a very
heavy bureaucratic load for workers and employers.

49.     To seek efficiency gains in the collection o f social protection contributions, the
Ministry o f Social Protection has been very active in developing a fully computerized
information system. The objective o f the unified integrated contribution system (Planilla
Integrada de Liquidacidn de Aportes, PILA) is to facilitate the process o f payment o f
contributions by operating as a single entry point for all contributions made by employers and
self-employed individuals, resulting in lower transaction costs and increased efficiency
through the integration o f the legally required contributions in the electronic format.

50.       The P I L A was initiated in 2005 and has increased i t s coverage over various periods.
Initially, the P I L A only included employees o f large firms. I t then gradually included smaller
firms, helping those that needed it to learn to use the system. In July 2008, a dramatic step
was taken, when the system's mandatory use was extended to self-employed workers. Finally,
the last exceptions were removed in the spring o f 2009. In August 2009, the system included
about 8,660,000 individuals, which represents a significant increase from the level o f
6,s 10,000 in July 2008, considering most new additions are single-person enterprises, rather
than large enterprises with numerous employees. While the system was designed a few years
ago, the design and the initial phase o f implementation were rather straightforward, as they
represented an improvement in the management o f contributions, but applied mostly to those

                                                18
that were already in the system. The broadening beyond the formal employees and the
removal o f the last exception, both more recent reforms, have been really critical steps in the
reform o f social protection. Indeed, they represent a significant change in policy, with the
enrolment o f self-employed and very small enterprises in the overall system, resulting in a
broader coverage o f the formal system.

51.       In addition, to increase the efficiency in the management o f contributions, and
thereby increase the system's coverage and sustainability, the Government has established the
basis for the creation o f a special unit responsible for overseeing the computation and
collection o f contributions (Law 115 1 o f 2007). The UGPP (Unidad Administrativa Especial
de Gestidn Pensional y Contribuciones Parafiscales de la Proteccidn Social) i s created in the
ambit o f the Ministry o f Finance and will have administrative autonomy and independent
patrimony. Decree 169 o f January 2008 established the UGPP's functions. The Government
has also finalized the technical study proposing a structure for the UGPP in October 2009 and
i s currently preparing the decree that will define its internal structure and staffing.

Area 1.3: Designing a sustainable and equitable health insurance system

60.     In 1993, the Government initiated a shift toward a system that would provide
subsidized health insurance to the poor by entitling them to a specified l i s t o f services, while
workers in the formal sector - independent o f their poverty status - would enroll in a
contributory health insurance regime financed via payroll taxes, under the hypothesis that
insurance-based entitlements are an effective means to ensure access to health services,
especially in highly resource-constrained systems. This structure also promoted efficiency as
providers receive payment on the basis o f the services rendered. This shift has led to dramatic
increases in health insurance coverage, from 57 percent in 1997 up to 88 percent in 2007, with
most increase taking place amongst the poorest population.

61.       Despite these gains, important challenges remain in terms o f coverage, quality and
equity o f health care, and the financing o f the insurance systems. In July 2008, a Supreme
Court issued ruling defining patient rights regarding health coverage further increased these
challenges. The ruling was a response to the sharp increase in the number o f judicial
complaints (tutelas) demanding payment for health procedures not covered by the health
insurance schemes. Most o f these complaints have been successful for the plaintiff, and, as a
result, insurance providers have been forced to pay corresponding bills, creating financial
pressure o n insurance companies and the national budget. The Court required the Government
to establish a strategy to eliminate coverage and financing disparities and to increase
portability among insurance companies.

programs, the Government has adopted the harmonization o f the packages o f services for
children under the age o f 12, for the subsidized and the contributory regimes, as reflected in

help the Government identify the key issues in implementing the Court order before applying

Area 2.1: Addressing extreme poverty through an integral strategy o tailored support and
                                                                   f

68.      Extreme chronic poverty persists in Colombia, despite investments in social
assistance programs. Extreme poverty actually increased recently, with around 18 percent o f
the entire population below the extreme poverty line in 2008. This phenomenon i s particularly
severe in rural areas, where 33 percent o f the population i s in extreme poverty. The extreme
poor include many uneducated youth and unskilled self-employed adults. Over a third o f them
work in agriculture and almost half are self-employed. Amongst the extreme poor, while more
than nine children in ten between the age 6 and 11 go to school, this ratio falls to seven in ten
for 12 to 17 years old, and to one in six or seven for 18 to 24 years old.

69.      The situation o f this particular group requires a focused strategy to bring the extreme
poor and vulnerable within the reach o f public services and provide tailored support and
access to basic services. The Government defined a strategy to attend the poorest with direct
family support and preferential access from relevant social programs, called the Juntos
strategy. The strategy established 45 targets for each family, covering the areas of
identification, income and work, education, health, nutrition, housing, family dynamics,
banking and savings and access to justice. The strategy focuses on helping families build the
skills, physical and financial assets they need to grow out o f poverty and develop sustainable
income generation mechanisms. Both at the national and at the local level, one o f the key
challenges o f this strategy i s to coordinate across several sectors and government levels, and
help programs adapt their services to this population.

70.       After the pilot phase, the Government has launched the full implementation o f the
Juntos Strategy at the end o f 2008 and the beginning o f 2009. As o f November 2009, at least
900,000 families were covered by the family accompaniment component (1 50,000 displaced
and 750,000 extreme poor families). The Government also developed instruments for the
articulation o f agencies at the national level and engaged into partnerships with at least 80
percent o f all municipalities (890 municipalities, out o f a total o f 1103) resulting in co-
financing and coordination at the local level. The weakest municipalities, in fiscal and
institutional terms, benefit from greater contributions from the central Government as well as
capacity building.

71,      To promote an effective strategy, the Government has undertaken an evaluation o f
the operational processes o f the pilots in 2009 and fine-tuned i t s operational manual prior to
the launch o f the strategy. The Government has also initiated a rigorous impact evaluation to
assess the effectiveness o f the program in reaching the poorest families, providing the support

                                               22
they need to connect to services, and providing preferential entry into core social protection
programs. The base line has been collected, and the final evaluation is expected in April 2010.

72.       The Juntos strategy is housed in the Office o f the President for Social Action, rather
than in a core sectoral institution. However, the Juntos strategy has been strongly anchored
and institutionalized within the broader Government apparatus through a series o f
mechanisms: First, it uses core information systems to identify i t s beneficiaries (SISBEN, see
below under Area 2.2) and to identify needs and gaps in coverage (RUAF, see below under
Area 2.3). Second, the strategy i s different from typical programs, as it i s based on the actual
coordination o f existing programs and services to provide preferential access to beneficiaries.
This coordination i s led by the Ministry o f Social Protection and the National Planning
Department. Third, the strategy i s also organized in partnership with local authorities at the
departmental and municipal level, through formal agreements. Finally, the series o f
evaluations undertaken will provide the basis for informed decision-making. The World Bank
i s focusing i t s technical assistance on mechanisms to ensure the strategy's institutionalization.

Area 2.2: Increasing programs ' impact through a more agile targeting system

75.      Most targeted public programs identify their beneficiaries using similar groups

Policy Area 3: Promoting higher quality training and employment services
87.      As described in the section on analytical underpinning, Colombia experiences
relatively high levels o f informality, and the welfare o f informal workers i s on average lower
than that o f formal workers. L o w skills and productivity, complex registration procedures,
rigid social insurance mechanisms, as well as the segmentation o f the labor market resulting
from a high minimum wage and payroll taxes are important factors behind this pattern. This
operation focuses on policy actions that strengthen workers' human capital and facilitate their
access to jobs through better training programs and labor market intermediation. In addition,
Policy Area 1 described earlier supports efforts to broaden the coverage o f social insurance
beyond formal sector workers, and the team continues i t s dialogue on options to address
broader issues o f labor costs and rigidity in the provision o f services they finance through i t s
Non-Lending Technical Assistance.

88.      In particular, this operation proposes to support: (1) reforms to strengthen the national
training to offer relevant and high quality training, (2) reforms to establish labor market
intermediation and unemployment services that promote effective entry into the labor force,

most vulnerable. Some o f these policies are less advanced than those supported under areas 1
and 2. They represent the next large reform agenda that the Government needs to develop to
deepen i t s fight against extreme poverty and promote employability for greater economic
growth and equity. While some reforms may appear less mature, they are the initial stages o f
a much broader nascent critical reform program.

Risks and Risks Mitigation
120.     There are four types o f risks to this operation. The Government has implemented
several strategies to address them, focusing on strengthening institutions and institutional
arrangements, designing strong sets o f norms and regulations to institutionalize and
consolidate systems, monitoring and evaluating programs, increasing efficiency,
disseminating information about programs and activities, and engaging in dialogue with the
public and specific interest groups.

12 1.    Macroeconomic risks: A risk to the operation arises from the potential deterioration
o f macroeconomic conditions, in particular l o w growth and international o i l prices, which
could limit resources available for Colombia's budget. As presented in the macroeconomic
assessment and evidenced by recent trends and the IMF's endorsement o f Government
policies in this area, the Government i s mitigating these risks through sound macroeconomic
policies, a flexible exchange rate, a decline in its external debt and adequate international
reserves. It has demonstrated i t s ability to handle the first year o f crisis, and even in a l o w
growth scenario, neither external nor fiscal sustainability appear at risk. Similarly, the
sustainability analysis undertaken suggests that debt levels are manageable in the near term.

 122.     Transition and governance-related risk: Presidential elections are planned for M a y
2010. While the current President has expressed his desire to seek a third mandate, the legality
o f this option remains undecided. A change in leadership could cause instability, through
changes in key Government positions and in policy orientation. This risk i s somewhat reduced
in Colombia, which has a tradition o f continuity in i t s policy frameworks, as highlighted in
Bank evaluations. In social sectors, the Government developed i t s reform program in
consultation with key stakeholders, resulting in buy-in and support. The risk i s also mitigated
by continued emphasis on principles o f results-based management, focus on evaluation o f
programs, and efforts to strengthen institutions and systems, some o f which are supported by
this operation. These promote good governance in programs' management, through increased
transparency and accountability. Overall, significant reversals in the sectors supported by this
operation are unlikely to be made o n the basis o f political affiliation. During the electoral
campaign, the team will take advantage o f the World Bank group's engagement with key


                                                34
candidates and their parties to present its vision and recommendations for the key policies
supported by this operation.

123.    Social sectors-specific risks:
    Complexity o f the reform processes: some reforms supported by this operation are
    complex and require considerable coordination across agencies. For instance, developing
    a unified information system for all social services requires all agencies to comply with
    norms, formats, technological choices, and processes. In the area o f j o b training, reforms
    call for the coordination of agencies that develop norms and standards; evaluate
    institutions, programs and individuals; or provide training services. T o address this
    concern, the Government has focused on ensuring i t s technical staff gains expertise in the
    management of systems to ensure institutional memory; designing legal and regulatory
    frameworks with the involvement o f all actors to ensure support; on ensuring D N P
    provides technical assistance; and on adopting reasonable implementation schedules.
    Pressure by interest groups: Some reforms could trigger opposition by specific groups,
    who perceive they will be negatively affected and their private interests challenged. For
    instance, actions related to the j o b training system could be perceived as a threat by those
    with a privileged, unchallenged, role. Changes to health financing mechanisms could
    threaten insurance companies or service providers. To mitigate these, the Government has
    engaged in active dialogues where stakeholders can voice their concerns. In the area o f
    training, the proposed minimum requirements, quality criteria and system were designed
    with the core stakeholders, both within Government and outside, resulting in a consensus
    on the direction and details o f the reform. The Government has also designed gradual
    reforms, often including measures for the periods during which a particular system i s
    phased out while a new one is phased in. Over the past years, opposition to reforms in our
    sector has been limited and has not slowed down their implementation. The Government
    i s also increasing its efforts to inform and engage with civil society through SINERGIA.
0   Fiscal pressure f r o m the financing o f health and old-age income insurance: Transfers
    to the public pension system continue to create strong fiscal pressures, despite the 2005
    reforms that reduced liabilities by 19 percentage points o f GDP. The annual transfer has
    stabilized around 3.5 percent o f GDP. Similarly, expansion o f coverage o f the health
    insurance subsidized program (from 7 to almost 23 million persons between 1997 and
    2008) and the efforts to respond to Colombia's Supreme Court request for greater
    coverage and an equalization o f benefits across regimes are likely to create fiscal
    pressures. The cost o f expansion has so far been contained, partly because o f design and
    strong economic growth during the period. Efforts to improve efficiency through stronger
    institutions and information systems supported by this operation, together with broader
    cost-cutting efforts in hospital management, should help mitigate these risks.


124.    Risks related to violence; The risk related to internal violence i s an increase in
displacement o f population in conflict-affected areas, and difficulties for the Government
programs to effectively serve their target population in these areas. While the risks remain, the
Government i s credited with a significant improvement in internal violence, through a policy
o f demobilization and reintegration o f ex-combatants, and systematic prosecution o f illegal
combatants.

During his second term in office, President Alvaro Uribe VBlez remains committed to the
implementation of the structural reforms set forth in the National Development Plan (PND) 2006-
20 10 "A Community State: Development for All," which seek to preserve macroeconomic stability
and integration into the global economy. These reforms are also aimed at providing a suitable
environment for sustainable growth, creating the necessary conditions for a broad concept of
development that complements growth, and ensuring that the Government adopts a truly vigilant
approach to social equity. In recent years, implementation o f these reforms, as well as those included
in the structural reform program o f 2002-2006, has led to satisfactory macroeconomic performance in
terms o f economic growth, a reduction in unemployment and poverty, lower inflation, effective public
debt management, fiscal deficit reduction, and the strengthening o f institutional frameworks. Many of
these measures have been endorsed by the World Bank.

However, the global financial crisis has primarily affected Colombia in such areas as reduced capital
inflows, a decline in exports, higher levels o f informal employment, and lower fiscal revenues. In
order to ensure the sustainability o f the reform package, the Government o f Colombia i s requesting a
Social Sector Policy Reform Loan from the World Bank.

This Program will support reforms in three social policy areas: (i)
                                                                  further improvements in the social
protection system; (ii)the attainment of equity, efficiency, and coordination among the social
development programs; and (iii) development of high quality training programs and employment
                                the
services.

In developing and improving the social protection system, the Colombian Government has managed to
expand coverage and improve the administration of the pension system. In addition, through the
General Social Security Health System (SGSSS), steps are being taken to provide citizens with better
tools not only to prevent illness and improve and restore health, but also to prevent a reduction in
families' disposable incomes, which would create the risk of falling into poverty in the event of an
illness or economic shock. The specific actions focus on universalizingthe subsidized health insurance
system and moving toward equity in a l l benefit plans (in Colombia there are two benefit plans: one for
citizens with the ability to pay, and another for those without), especially among the population under
12 years of age. This i s important since various assessments have shown that insurance provides
greater access to health services, reduces the cost to families, and improves their financial protection.
Progress in terms o f coverage has been bolstered by government efforts geared toward improving the
efficiency and equity of the system, so as to better focus on and monitor the status o f beneficiaries,
depending on their ability to pay and the provision o f subsidies.

                                                  40
In terms o f the administration of the pension system, we have established the Special Administrative
Unit for Pension Management and Parafiscal Contributions for Social Protection (UGPP) by means of
Law 115 1 of 2007 within the Ministry of Finance and Public Credit, with legal status, administrative
autonomy, and i t s own capital.

The UGPP will, on the one hand, assume responsibility for granting accrued pension rights currently
handled by the administrators of the Rkgimen de Media Prima [public pension plan] and the national
government entities that were tasked with this responsibility in instances where pension settlements
have been or will be ordered; and on the other hand, undertake the monitoring and oversight o f the
appropriate, complete, and timely settlement and payment o f parafiscal social security contributions. I t
will also assume responsibility for follow-up, collaboration, and the determination of the appropriate,
complete, and timely settlement and payment o f parafiscal contributions for Social Protection. The
UGPP i s currently in the midst o f an operational restructuringprocess.

The National Development Plan, adopted by Law 115 1 of 2007, established the national State
Industrial and Commercial Enterprise (EICE), with legal status, administrative autonomy, and its own
capital, within the Ministry of Social Protection, known as the Colombian Pensions Administrator
(COLPENSIONES). Its mandate i s to administer, at the State level, the Rkgimen de Media Prima with
defined benefit, which entails administration o f the periodic economic benefits covered in Legislative
Act 01 of 2005, in accordance with the provisions of the relevant law. It will also assume
responsibility for the settlement obligations and commitments of CAJANAL, EICE, CAPRECOM,
and the Social Security Institute, with respect to the administration of pensions. COLPENSIONES i s
currently being established.

With regard to the achievement of equity and coordination among the social welfare programs, the
Colombian Government i s committed to reducing poverty and implementing the Social Protection
Network for Overcoming Extreme Poverty - Red Juntos. This strategy i s designed to ensure the
effective integrationof the poorest and displaced families into the Government's social service system
and to promote the granting o f basic benefits through joint responsibility o f families, in order to
overcome extreme poverty. To facilitate this process, the Government of Colombia has implemented
tools that are designed to address the needs o f the population living in extreme poverty, some o f which
are: (i) methodology focusing on persons living in extreme poverty, through the Red Juntos, which
        a
seeks to provide comprehensive assistance for the target population; (ii)a tool for planning
expenditure and addressing the needs of the population living in extreme poverty and the displaced
population; and (iii) joint financing for assistance to the extremely poor and displaced families found
in 85 percent of the country's municipalities.

In addition to the increase in social welfare for the poorest, comprehensive social security has become
a fundamental pillar of social protection, and i s designed to ensure that the country's entire population
has access to it, since there must be compensation or assistance mechanisms to finance access for the
poorest. The Colombian Government has therefore been working on three proposals to address the
challenges in the area of comprehensive social security: (i)    combating the evasion and avoidance o f
insurance through the consolidation o f taxpayer information systems and records; (ii)    creating suitable
incentives for segments of the population not yet covered; and (iii)        creating insurance or income
protection mechanisms for the most vulnerable groups.

There i s also a component that covers additional support that the Government must provide to the
poorest and most vulnerable families so that they can chart the course for their own development,
known as the Social Development System. To this end, in terms of the achievement of equity,
efficiency, and coordination among social development programs, the Colombian Government has
been implementingthe Red Juntos, whose fundamental objective i s to help overcome extreme poverty,
as set forth in the National Council for Social and Economic Policy (CONPES) document 102 of

                                                   41
2006. The Red seeks to focus on and provide preferential access to the State's social programs for the
population living in extreme poverty and the displaced population across the country, thus enabling
them to realize their potential and acquire the skills needed to overcome difficult conditions. JUNTOS
also treats the family as a unit of intervention and does follow up and direct monitoring o f families'
achievements, through a system of joint responsibility, and uses a multidimensional concept of
poverty that transcends poverty and indigence, which are income-based indicators.

In terms of the development o f high quality training programs and employment services, the Income
Generation Policy (CONPES 3616) seeks to improve the individual situation of persons living in
extreme poverty, through: (i)   educational interventions- an increase in human capital, improvement in
skills and abilities and vocational training - which enable them to enter the labor market and be
productive; (ii)  productive interventions - welfare and comprehensive support in the planning, start
up, and l i f e cycle o f businesses and productive projects, so that jobs are created through self-
employment; (iii)    institutional interventions, on two levels: at the territorialhational level, so that
policy outcomes are reflected in the implementation at the departmental and municipal levels; and at
the government level, so that public sector entities streamline efforts to provide comprehensive
assistance for this target population; and finally, (iv) the strengthening o f the social development
system, which ensures that the gains achieved are not wiped out by the effects o f temporary shocks on
economic performance.

In terms o f relevance, the third chapter of the National Development Plan "Poverty Reduction and the
Promotion o f Employment and Equity," proposes a reduction in the average time spent seeking
employment by using an information system that allows for efficient and timely employment
intermediation services. To this end, efforts are being made to strengthen and implement new and
modern network systems, in association with the public and private intermediation services, which
will enable workers, companies, institutions, and the general public to access reliable and complete
information to improve employment intermediation and adapt the training of human resources to the
demands of the labor market, all within the context o f the National Training for Work System's
(SNFT) consolidation strategy, and better education and training o f the labor force. Finally, through
this strategy, schemes will hopefully be developed so that ongoing training will create a greater focus
on economic areas and sectors that stimulate competitiveness, innovation, and technological
advancement.

In terms of greater mobility between the various educational levels and modalities, and greater
relevance and coordination between the Human Capital Training System (SFCH) and the productive
sector, the Government i s strengthening the Quality Training for Work System - SCAFT, in order to
consolidate the SFCH's quality assurance system, and further improve the quality of the courses
offered as a crucial component for the sound development o f education and training for work policies.

In this regard, in Visidn Colombia 2019, in the chapter on "Bridging the Social and Regional Gaps"
the Government i s proposing inclusion of the goal of `Consolidating a National Training for Work
System,' which will require identification of profiles and relevant skills, determination of the
mechanisms and requirements for the accreditation of institutions and programs, and a description o f
program equivalences, with emphasis on skills-basedtraining, so as to facilitate educational mobility
for the users of the training.

Therefore, by virtue o f Decree 2566 of 2003 and Law 1188 o f 2088, the basic quality requirements
were established for offering academic programs in higher education. The requirements are organized
into academic and institutional categories and seek to encourage self-evaluation as the primary tool in
the quest for a better education: Law 1188 of 2008 specifically regulates the accreditation o f higher
education programs and also seeks greater relevance o f the SFCH's programs and institutions to the
needs o f the productive sector.

      L                                                    Summary
                 The Colombian economy i s starting to show signs o f recovery. After registering negative
                 growth in the second h a l f o f 2008, GDP has increased modestly in the f i r s t two quarters o f
                 2009 (on a seasonally adjusted quarterly basis). On the basis o f recent developments in
                 economic activity, the Bank's base scenario for Colombia includes GDP growth o f 0.4
                 percent in 2009 and a modest rebound o f economic activity in 2010.
                 Sound macroeconomic policies implemented in recent years have substantially improved
                 the country's economic resilience against crisis. Several factors ensure external current
                 account financing in difficult global conditions: the flexible exchange rate, the decline in
                 foreign external debt (which has almost halved since 2003 due to the sustained FDI
                 flows), and adequate international reserves (over USD25.2 billion, or 11 percent o f G D P
                 at end-October 2009) that more than doubles the annual external debt amortizations.
                 Fiscal consolidation and the Government public debt management strategy put public debt
                 in a declining path and reduce the share o f foreign denominated debt. The authorities'
                 successful placement o f bonds in international markets in addition to securing funds from
                 multilaterals in order to meet public sector external financing requirements further reduces
                 financial vulnerabilities. The exchange rate appreciated in recent months and i s back n o w
                 to 2008 levels.

                                 A. RECENT      DEVELOPMENTS

1.      T h e Colombian economy i s starting to show signs o f recovery. After registering
negative growth in the second half o f 2008, GDP has increased modestly in the first two
quarters o f 2009 (on a seasonally adjusted quarterly basis). Economic activity decelerated in
the first three quarters o f 2008 and actually turned negative during the last quarter, thereby
bringing GDP growth for the year at a modest 2.5 percent. But on the basis o f recent
developments in economic activity, the Bank's base scenario for Colombia includes GDP
growth o f 0.4 percent in 2009 and a modest rebound o f economic activity in 2010.

2.     P r i o r to the onset o f the global economic downturn, Colombia exhibited high
economic growth within a framework o f enhanced macroeconomic stability. The
Colombian economy experienced strong and sustained growth over the 2003-2007 period
supported by the favorable external environment and sound economic policies. Real economic
growth accelerated from 2.5 percent in 2002 to 7.5 percent in 2007, inflation moderated
during the period, and the current account deficit remained modest despite strong domestic
demand, particularly in investment. Strong growth supported unemployment reduction from
over 17 percent in 2002 to approximately 12 percent in 2007. High external demand,
improved terms o f trade and lower cost o f international credit created a favorable environment
for growth, supported by the improved security situation in Colombia and stable
macroeconomic policies.

internal demand grew moderately. In the fourth quarter o f 2008, y-o-y economic growth

December 2008) and partly because Banks around the world were hoarding liquidity due to
heightened risk aversion during the last four months o f 2008. In the third quarter o f 2008,
export growth which had remained relatively resilient, collapsed due to the global slowdown.

4.     Prices have been following a declining trend after the fuel and food price hike.
Shocks to fuel and food prices pushed inflation to 7.7 percent at end-2008 well in excess o f
the Central Bank's target o f 5 percent for 2008. Nevertheless, inflationary pressures have been
decelerating already since end-2008 as the external price shock was corrected. As a result,
Banco de la Repziblica authorities have lowered the policy interest rate aiming at a
reactivation o f the economy while prices are expected to continue on a downward path.
Authorities have cut the central bank's intervention rate eight times since end-2008 and
consequently, the rate has been lowered from 10 percent in December 2008 to 4 percent in
September 2009.

         5.      Continued low fiscal deficits propitiated a reduction in public sector debt,
         although concerns remain regarding expenditure rigidities. In recent years, fiscal policy
         has aimed to reduce public sector and external vulnerabilities by ensuring debt sustainability.
         The combination o f domestic economic growth, improved international conditions, peso
         appreciation (which reduces the cost o f servicing foreign currency-denominated debt), and
         revenue-enhancing policy reforms improved the fiscal accounts.39 As a result, the combined
         public sector deficit decreased from 3.6 percent o f GDP in 2002 to a balance in 2005.
         However, budget and legal rigidities that resist spending cuts have slowed the progress of
         policy reforms on the expenditure side. Despite the strong revenue performance, expenditure
         dynamics -especially pension expenditures and transfers to sub national governments- have
         resulted in modest fiscal deficits during 2006-2007 (see Table 2). In 2008, the non financial
         public sector balance reached a deficit o f 0.3 percent o f GDP, better than the Government's
         target o f -0.9 percent o f GDP. Transfers to the main state-run pension system will continue to
         expand over the next decade -as payouts greatly exceed new contributions-, despite the 2005
         constitutional reform that reduced the net present value o f pension liabilities by 19 percentage
         points o f GDP by eliminating special regimes for state employees and imposing ceilings on
         benefits in the public-sector pension system. Social expenditures have also increased over the
         last years, climbing from 12.4 percent o f GDP in 2002 to 12.8 percent in 2006 and they are
         expected to further increase to 13.9 percent o f GDP in 2009.

6.       As the global financial turbulence intensified in the second half o f 2008,
Colombian financial markets were negatively affected in line with developments in other
Latin American countries although conditions have improved during 2009. The
withdrawal o f liquidity in financial markets amid increased risk aversion resulted in larger
sovereign spreads and stock market losses across the region. In Colombia, the EMBI spread
increased to 740 bps at end October, 2008 but since then it has declined to 237 at the end o f
October 2009. The stock market index declined b y a third in line with developments
elsewhere in 2008 but has since totally recovered by the end o f September 2009. The
exchange rate appreciation, prompted in part by tightening monetary policy, reversed and the
exchange rate depreciated by about 16 percent from August 2008 to December 2008, limiting
the loss o f reserves during 2008 to US$125 million dollar (about 0.5 percent o f total reserves).
In this context the Central Bank removed controls to capital inflows, a temporary measure to
prevent excessive exchange rate volatility. To some extent, the exchange rate depreciation
was a welcome development given i t s impact on competitiveness. However since end-2008 to
end o f October 2009 the exchange rate has appreciated by about 11 percent. An increase in
credit risk premiums and funding costs pushed the lending rate up b y around 81 basis points
in the last quarter o f 2008 with respect to the first quarter o f the same year, but declines in
policy rates since December 2008 have brought lending rates as o f September 2009 down by
613 b.p., reaching a level even lower than in early 2007.

7.    The downturn in global economic activity has negatively affected t h e Colombian
economy through several channels. The main channels o f transmission o f the global
economic and financial shocks to the Colombian economy are the following:

                                                                                                  61
 (a)    Weaker export revenues. Colombia has a relatively open economy (exports o f goods
       and services are equivalent to over 17 percent o f GDP while total trade amounts to
       about 36 percent o f GDP). In particular, o i l exports represent on average about 29
       percent o f total exports. While exports have become more geographically diversified
       towards other countries/regions beyond the U.S ., export volumes have decelerated in
       the fourth quarter o f 2008 growing by only 2.4 percent, y/y, in real terms versus a
       strong expansion during the same period in 2007 (19 percent). This has translated into a
       deceleration in GDP, which contracted by 1.1 percent y-0-y during the fourth quarter o f
       2008 (compared to 8.2 percent in the same period in 2007). Furthermore, exports
       volumes fell by 0.5 percent during the first quarter o f 2009 and the contraction
       intensified during the second quarter with a 5.7 percent drop. It is estimated that a 1
       percent decline in U S growth, the main trade partner o f Colombia, lowers real export
       growth by 0.3 percent.

 (b)   Lower o i l prices. Colombia received a record-high level o f revenue from o i l exports at
       US$12.2 billion in 2008 as a result o f a historically high average o i l price for the
       Colombian mix o f crude o i l at US$134.34 per barrel. The dramatic drop o f international
       o i l prices to a minimum o f US$39 per barrel for the Colombian m i x early this year has
       a significant impact on the country's public finances as o i l accounts for about 20
       percent o f total public sector revenues. Although Colombian mix prices show a mild
       recovery in the recent months, o i l prices are expected to remain well below the records
       levels o f 2008. This moderation in o i l prices -ceteris paribus- will slightly increase the
       current account deficit and the public sector deficit given that the Government didn't
       lower the price o f gasoline this year. The funds stemming from the difference between
       international and local o i l prices are instead be gathered in a fuel price-stabilization
       fund.

9.      Factors that typically magnify external shocks resulting in severe economic
disruptions, are not present in Colombia. In some Latin American countries, external
shocks have been typically amplified by severely appreciated real exchange rates, weak
domestic financial sectors and real and financial dollarization. Fortunately, in the Colombian
case, the factors that typically magnify crisis acted as a muffler. Colombia entered the
turbulence period with an exchange rate value close to i t s equilibrium level4'. The flexible
exchange rate acted as a shock absorber helping to counter the deceleration in external
demand. The strength o f the financial sector, with healthy financial indicators, has prevented a
credit crunch, and credit to the private sector- although it decelerated considerably at the
beginning o f the year, reflecting lower demand and increased risk aversion- has recovered
significantly in the last months, growing 36 percent in real terms as o f August 2009. Financial
dollarization i s not a destabilizing factor in Colombia.

10.       I n spite o f limited space for pursuing an aggressive counter-cyclical policy, the
measures implemented by the authorities contributed to mitigate the impact o f a global
slowdown on growth and employment generation o f the domestic economy. Monetary
policy was actively used to counter the effects o f the cycle as weaken demand and lower food
and o i l prices kept inflation on a downward trend. The sharp reduction in public sector debt to
GDP ratio during the past years has also provided room for some fiscal loosening. As a result,
fiscal expenditures will increase b y 2.5 percent o f GDP in 2009, with respect to 2008. The
main measures adopted by the authorities include:

            Expansionary monetary policy. Monetary policy was tightened substantially
            between mid 2006 and mid-2008, the policy rate was increased by 400 b.p. amid
            concerns o f possible overheating o f the economy. In the last quarter o f 2008 a

ANNEX 8: KEY POLICY AREAS SUPPORTED BY THE OPERATION


Policy Area 1: Promoting greater integration and efficiency of social insurance

Area 1.1. Improving the processes o collection o contributions for a more efficient
                                    f           f
             f
management o the social insurance system.

1.      The Social Insurance system in Colombia was until recently very complex, with
multiple agencies operating parallel schemes, and with a system to collect contributions that
was cumbersome and extremely complex for most employers to apply. The strengthening o f
the information systems that support the social insurance system i s a priority for the current
government, as i s the development o f an entity that has oversight and responsibility for the
process.

2.      In this context, the Ministry o f Social Protection has been very active in recent years
in developing new, fully computerized information systems that are inter-operable. Among
these, the Integrated Contributions Payment System (Planilla Integrada de Liquidacidn de
Aportes - PILA), allows employers or the self-employed to submit all contributions at once,
through a web-based system. The P I L A was launched in mid-2005 in an effort to modernize
and automatize the system o f payment o f contributions. I t s main objective i s to facilitate the
payment process o f contributions by operating as a single entry point for all contributions
made by employers and self employed individuals (for contributions to the pension system,
health insurance, workers' compensation, the SENA, the I C B F and the Cajas de
Compensacidn Familiar). As such, it also improves the compliance o f contributions by
integrating in an electronic format the legally required contributions.

3.      The P I L A has increased i t s coverage over various periods. Initially, the P I L A only
included employees o f large firms. I t then gradually included smaller f i r m s , helping those that
needed it to learn to use the system. In July 2008, a dramatic step was taking, when the
system's mandatory use was extended to self-employed workers. Finally, the last exceptions
were removed in the Sprint o f 2009. In August 2009, the system included about 8,660,000
individuals, which represents a significant increase from the level o f 6,8 10,000 in July 2008,
considering most new additions are single-person enterprises, rather than large enterprises
with numerous employees. The number o f self-employed in the system increased from around
70,000 in June 2007 to over 1,500,000 in August 2009.

4.      In terms o f results, the installation o f the system and its progressive r o l l out have
reduced employer's transaction costs and increased the efficiency o f the process o f collection
o f contributions. Part o f the reduction in cost i s experienced through the reduction in
paperwork that needs to be filled in, the reduction in the number o f transactions that need to
be made, and through the reduced need for employers to understand and calculate the
different elements o f the payroll contributions, which are automatically calculated. These
include contributions to the pension system, health insurance, Worker's Family Assistance
Funds (Cajas de Compensacidn Familiar, CCFs), National Service for Job Training (Servicio
Nacional de Aprendizaje, SENA), Colombian Institute for Family Welfare (Instituto
Colombiano de Bienestar Familias, ICBF) and professional risk management agency. In
addition, the creation o f a unified data base o f workers guarantees the uniformity o f the basis



                                                 72
for contributions for each worker, standardizes the information, makes controls easier and i s
expected to diminish processing errors.

5.      In addition, to increase the efficiency in the management o f contributions, and thereby
increase the system's coverage and sustainability, the Government has established the basis
for the creation o f a special unit in charge o f monitoring and integrating the information to
ensure the compliance with the payment o f contributions (Law 115 1 o f 2007). The UGPP
(Unidad Administrativa Especial de Gestidn Pensional y Contribuciones Parafiscales de la
Proteccidn Social) i s created in the ambit o f the Ministry o f Finance and will have
administrative autonomy and independent patrimony. Decree 169 o f January 2008 established
the UGPP's functions and the Government finalized the technical study proposing a structure
for the UGPP in October 2009. The consolidated information that will be available at the
UGPP, plus i t s legal capacity o f engaging in judiciary collection o f contributions i s also an
improvement in the level o f compliance with social security contributions in the system.

Area 1.2: Consolidating the public pension systemfor greater efficiency

6.      The public administration o f the defined benefit pension schemes faced important
challenges arising from i t s internal administrative structure as well as from external
circumstances such as the increasing amount o f litigation involved in the determination o f
pension rights. The functions o f determining pension rights and collecting contributions were
disseminated in the different entities that administered the regimes, the most important ones
being Cajanal, Caprecom and the Instituto de Seguros Sociales, ISS. This dissemination
resulted in the lack o f integration o f administrative records and the diversity in the
interpretation o f pension rules. Furthermore, important cases o f fraud and corruption have
been detected in the determination o f pension rights.

7.       The Government has initiated a series o f reforms to harmonize the institutional
arrangement o f the social insurance system in the short term, in order to achieve cost-savings
and build a more efficient provision o f services in this area. The objective o f the
reorganization i s to increase its efficiency by insuring that benefits are more accurately paid.
The reorganization involves the creation and implementation o f two agencies. First, the UGPP
i s being created, as described above, which will be responsible for the recognition o f pension
rights o f the schemes that are being closed (in addition to i t s functions already discussed
under Policy area 1.1.). In the future, the P I L A described above will provide a strong basis
for work history records, which are critical for the definition o f pension rights. Second, a new
public entity will be created, Colpensiones,with the objective o f managing the defined benefit
system for active contributors. Colpensiones will integrate the management o f the defined
benefit pension system under one public entity, which should contribute to i t s efficiency,
homogenization o f criteria and integration o f data and information systems. Clear obligations
and improved governance structure should contribute to i t s transparency and due process in
the payment o f pensions. As opposed to ISS, Colpensiones will be in charge only o f pension-
related administration, which should also help in improving its efficiency.

8.      The creation o f Colpensiones i s clearly an important step forward in the integration o f
the public administration o f the pension system aiming at improving i t s efficiency. The only
question mark o f the reorganization may be the division o f labor between the new entities, in
particular the fact that UGPP will be in charge o f pension recognition o f beneficiaries coming
from closed schemes, while Colpensiones eventually will have to do the same process for its

                                               73
own affiliates. The separation o f responsibilities may be justified as a transition arrangement
given the backload o f cases, ongoing litigations and cases o f fraud that has occurred within
the schemes being closed.

9.      The Government i s currently developing the structure and internal organization o f this
entity, which will be formed and staffed partly from the National Social Security Institute
(Instituto de Seguros Sociales, ISS). As part o f the reform, the Government decided to close
key public entities that operated the public "pay as you go" pension system and already
proceeded with the closing o f one o f them (Cajunal).

10.     Besides the issues addressed by the described policies, more generally, Colombia has
one o f the lowest coverage rates for social security programs in Latin America, well below
what would be expected given i t s income per capita. While some elements o f the design o f the
system i t s e l f and specific institutional weaknesses can partly explain l o w coverage, the main
drivers appear to be outside the realm o f the pension system itself. Indeed, l o w coverage
seems to be linked to the fact that the minimum pension is explicitly tied to the minimum
wage, to the level o f the minimum wage i t s e l f in Colombia, and more generally to the high
level o f payroll taxes. These elements imply that the system must collect high contributions,
over a long vesting period, and from people who earn relatively high wages in order to be
financially sustainable. With these constraints, there seem to be little that a change in the
design can achieve in terms o f improving coverage.

 11.    The general features o f Colombia's labor market would need to be adjusted to promote
greater coverage. Changing these i s a very complex undertaking, as legal constraints are
difficult to challenge, and will likely require a long t e r m strategy. In the meantime, the
Government i s making efforts to increase the coverage o f i t s pension system. For instance, it
i s exploring options to bring individuals who work part-time, or those who might have l o w
income but some capacity to save, into the pension system and to promote their long-term
savings for old-age income security. At this stage, the full details o f these proposals are not
completely laid out, and it i s difficult to judge if these efforts will be effective and efficient in
achieving their purpose without fragmenting the pension system.

21.     The Government decided to launch a new strategy for the poorest families in
Colombia, reflecting the priority placed o n reaching the poorest o f the poor and the
recognition that these groups are better served with an integral approach which aims at
improving their situation on all poverty fronts (rather than focusing o n a specific aspect as do
most programs). Extreme poverty actually increased recently, with around 18 percent o f the
entire population below the extreme poverty l i n e in 2008. This phenomenon i s particularly
severe in rural areas, where 33 percent o f the population i s in extreme poverty (urban rate i s
13 percent). The extreme poor count with many uneducated youth and unskilled self-
employed adults. Over a third o f them work in agriculture and almost half are self-employed.
While more than nine children in ten between the age 6 and 11 go to school, this ratio falls to
seven in t e n for 12 to 17 years old, and to one in six or seven for 18 to 24 years old.

22.     Building on experience in the region and within Colombia, the Government set up the
Red Juntos strategy, which puts together a new management model for social policy t o
improve the living conditions o f the poor and contribute to social and economic stability o f
families living in displacement. This model articulates the public agencies and the
beneficiaries so that the families have preferential access to social services and, therefore,
they are more likely to be able to meet the program's goal.

23.      The goals o f the Juntos strategy are to improve the quality o f life for families living in
extreme poverty, to adapt the supply o f social services to the specific needs o f these families,
and to strengthen the institutional capabilities o f local government. The operational strategy
i s to incorporate the extremely poor households (the program i s targeting the poorest 1,5
million households according to the SISBEN, the national targeting instrument described
under Area 2.2 below) into social services and help them to improve their situation by: (1)
integrating the timely delivery to them o f relevant social services; (2) providing these families
counseling and preferential access to social services to enable them to meet a set o f minimum
standards; and (3) establishing a framework o f co-responsibility with the family. In order to
guide the work with the family and the inter-institutional coordination there are a range o f
minimum standards (goals) grouped into 9 dimensions as presented in Table 1. These could
be thought o f as the minimum conditions that ideally a family living in extreme poverty
should accomplish in order to improve their situation and escape poverty traps. About 15
public agencies already participate in the Juntos network and this number i s expected t o
increase over time.

32.     As part o f the tools designed to equip the Social Protection System with the means to
effectively develop an integrated approach, coordinate programs, and seek cost-savings and

platforms. One o f the fundamental characteristics o f these systems i s their compatibility and

basis for efficient and precise program design and implementation, for articulation, as well as
for informed funding decisions and allocation o f resources more generally.

provide guidance to workers in the selection o f courses.

51.     Part o f the long-run vision o f the Government for labor markets i s to develop a series

regional monitoring o f labor markets and intermediation services (inclusiveness and
efficiency), development o f a policy framework to promote the income o f the poorest
(equity), and improvements in the programs aimed at protecting workers from unemployment
shocks (equity and efficiency).

 Area 3.3: Adapting training programs and active labor market policies to address the situation of
 flte poorest and most vulnerable

 56.      As presented above, the situation o f the extreme poor and the displaced population i s rather
 specific, and calls for interventions which are more focused and tailored, and combine different
 services. In the area o f labor markets, in particular, the level o f skills o f this population i s on average
 very low, as well as their productivity. So, while the Government is strengthening the overall
 training system and promoting active labor market policies for all, it is also important to
 address the specific needs o f the poorest and most vulnerable.

 57.     In the context o f the implementation o f the Juntos strategy, the Government reviewed
 in details the situation o f the poorest and the services adapted to their specific needs. The
 CONPES 3616 presents some characteristics o f the extreme poor that are barriers for their
 participation in labor markets: l o w human capital, lack o f access to capital, need for
 psychological support (especially the displaced) and need for assistance in launching their
 own businesses. The diagnostic highlighted that many existing programs do not reach the
 poorest or displaced, and that coordination between entities remains problematic.

 58.     To respond to the issue, the Government identified the need for an integral approach
 that would provide a personalized accompaniment for each adult, combining a series o f
 interventions including education, training, psycho-social support, labor intermediation
 services, access to assets, technical assistance and apprenticeship, among others. I t developed
 the Income Generation Policy for the Extreme Poor and the Displaced (Politica de
 Generacidn de Ingresos para l a Poblacidn en Situacidn de Pohreza Extrema y/o
 Desplazarniento) approved in the CONPES Document number 3616. The main purpose o f the
 policy i s to develop and increase the productive potential o f the extreme poor and displaced,
 developing their skills and creating opportunities for asset access and accumulation, and in the
 medium and long term, achieving social and economic stability. The policy builds on the
 framework o f the Juntos strategy and targets young adults aged 18 and older belonging to
 households in the poorest 1.5 m i l l i o n households, as identified by Juntos on the basis o f the
 SISBEN information system.


                                                      91
59.      The policy seeks to provide the mechanisms needed to overcome barriers for
generating sufficient and sustainable income to promote the economic independence o f the
families. The actions will consist o f improving, integrating and targeting existing instruments
properly. The current framework for income generation needs to be improved: efforts are
generally isolated and uncoordinated, often without recognizing the particular characteristics
o f the target population. Overall, the policy focuses on increasing the productive potential o f
the population, i.e. improving their supply o f labor, rather than o n enhancing demand for their
existing skills (although a few elements o f the strategy are designed to encourage the
recruitment o f the target groups).
