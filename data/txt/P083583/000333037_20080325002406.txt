                                             1. INTRODUCTION

1.1      This program document proposes an Economic Governance and Recovery Grant
(EGRG) to the Republic of C8te d'Ivoire in the amount of SDR[194] million (US$[308]
million equivalent). The EGRG supports the full normalization o f relations between CBte
d'Ivoire and the World Bank, and is also the first Development Policy Operation (DPO)' aimed
at supporting the development strategy of the Government o f C8te d'Ivoire in the context o f a
multi-donor re-engagement process based on the Ouagadougou Peace Accord o f March 4, 2007.
The proceeds o f the grant will be used for (i)         direct reimbursement o f a bridge loan extended to
clear CBte d'Ivoire's arrears to the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development and
the International Development Association, and (ii)budgeted fiscal needs in the period
immediately after arrears clearance.              In terms of IDA resources, C8te d'Ivoire meets the
eligibility criteria that have been discussed with the Executive Directors for exceptional arrears
clearance support from IDA.* Since CBte d'Ivoire is at highrisk o f debt distress, IDA resources
are provided on grants terms, per the stipulations of the grant allocation system agreed with IDA
donors for the IDA 14period (FY06-FY08). C8te d'Ivoire is also eligible for annual exceptional
post-conflict allocations from IDA.

1.2      C8te d'Ivoire is emerging from a political crisis that began in 1999 and erupted in
civil conflict in September 2002. The proposedgrant would assist the country in its post-
conflict economicandsocial recovery. For the first time since the civil war, CGte d'Ivoire has a
government o f national reconciliation bringing together the key parties to the conflict under a
unified government.           Given the complex post-conflict environment and governance and
transparency issues facing the government, the proposed grant supports govemment-owned
reforms to improve governance, transparency and efficiency in public expenditure management
as well as advance structural reforms aimed at strengthening governance and transparency inthe
key sectors o f the economy (energy, cocoa and financial sectors). These reforms would lead over
time to strengthened fiscal sustainability, improved economic governance and enhanced
transparency o f State institutions and processes4ritical elements for the country's economic

projections o f debt ratios for end-2007, C8te d'Ivoire would qualify for relief under the Enhanced
HIPC Initiative. The operation would then pave the way for HIPC debt relief under the HIPC

        2. C ~ T D'IVOIRE'S DEVELOPMENT CHALLENGES AND PROSPECTS
                      E

2.1       C6ted'Ivoire, a medium-sizecountrywith a populationof 18.4 million,is the largest
economy in the West African Economic and Monetary Union (UEMOA) and is criticalto
the overallrecovery of the sub-region. Given its size (accounting for 40 percent o fGDPinthe
UEMOA), its relatively high income per capita (US$870 in 2006 as per the Bank's Atlas
methodology), central location and infrastructure, including the port o f Abidjan which i s an
important transport hub for the landlocked neighboring countries, CBte d'Ivoire has an important
economic influence over the rest o f the region. With its large immigrant population, CBte
d'Ivoire has also been an important source o f immigrant worker remittances for neighboring
countries.      The country is the largest supplier o f cocoa in the world and has a relatively
diversified agricultural economy as well as a basic agro-processing industry. Despite its natural
resources, however, the civil war in 2002-03 and subsequent years o f stalemate in the peace
process have taken a heavy toll on the economy and social outcomes. As a result, CBte d'Ivoire
now ranks 166thout o f 177 countries interms o f human development, according to the UNDP's
2007 Human Development Report, considerably lower than what would be expected given its
income level.

                                   RECENT     POLITICAL DEVELOPMENTS

2.2       C8te d'Ivoire has been undergoinga prolongedperiodof politicalinstabilitysince a
military coup broke out in December 1999. After efforts to promote national reconciliation
gained momentum, the country was again thrown into turmoil in September 2002. The crisis
began as an armed uprising by mutinous soldiers faced with likely demobilization. It then
escalated into a full-blown rebellion with political overtones. The country was split intwo parts,
with the rebel forces (Forces Nouvelles, FN) holding the north and the west andthe Government
in control of the broad southern and central regions, with French (Operation Licorne) and UN
forces acting as a buffer between the two. Several attempts were made to resolve the situation,
beginning with the Marcoussis Accord in January 2003. However, progress in restoring peace
and political stability, after faltering under successive transition governments, stalled for over
five years.

2.3       On March 7, 2007 a power sharing agreement (the Ouagadougou Accord) was
reachedbetween President Gbagbo and the ex-rebelForces Nouvellesleader, Soro, under
the auspices of BurkinaFaso's PresidentCompaorC. The Accord, which receivedthe support
o f the international community, sets out a roadmap for disarmament, demobilization and
reintegration (DDR), the dismantling of militias, reunification o f the country, and preparations
for elections for 2008. A new transition Government, with FN leader Soro as Prime Minister,
was formed in early April; an integrated command center for the army and rebel forces was set

                                     IMPACT OF THE CONFLICT

2.5     The conflictand politicalinstabilityhavetaken a heavy toll on growth and poverty.
In2000-06, average real GDP growth turned negative (-0.4 percent) and was well below the
rates in the rest o f UEMOA (4.1 percent) and sub-Saharan Africa (SSA, 4.9 percent).             As
concerns about the political and security situation grew and external financing dwindled, public
and private investment dropped below earlier (from 13.7 percent o f GDP in 1994-99 to 10.3
percent during 2000-06) and comparator levels (20.3 percent inthe UEMOA and 18.5 percent in
SSA). Private sector activity was scaled back, especially inindustry, and by 2006 real GDP was
some 35 percent below its pre-crisis trend level. The partition o f the country disrupted trade
within the country and diminishedCGte d'Ivoire's role as a regionalhub. Export volume growth
fell between 1994-99 (8.7 percent) and2000-06 (2.3 percent). The external current account held
up (1.5 percent o f GDP during 2000-06) thanks to an increase in oil exports since 2002 and
overall favorable terms o f trade (notably for oil). The financial sector was adversely affected by
the crisis, as all 19 bank branches were closed inthe Center-North-West region since 2002, the
quality o f bank loans portfolios declined (in part because o f government arrears to the private
sector), andthe already weakjudicial system deteriorated further. Developments inCGte d'Ivoire
also hurtthe UEMOAregional trade and output.

2.6     Fiscal performance and transparency have suffered, owing to falling revenues and
crisis-related expenditure pressures and weakened accountability. As growth slowed and
the tax base eroded, total revenue fell. Also, pressures for defense and discretionary spending
rose while external assistance was suspended. While overall fiscal deficits narrowed (from 2.7
percent during 1994-99 to 1.3 percent during 2000-06) due to relatively disciplined cash
management, the country could not comply with most fiscal UEMOA criteria and domestic and
external arrears increased significantly. The composition o f expenditure also deteriorated as
health and education spending fell as a share o f GDP, from 5.3 percent o f GDP in 1999 to 5.1
percent in2006; the level o fhealth spending fell to a precariouslylow 0.9 percent o f GDP (Table
2.2).

2.7     With frequent changes in government, political and security concerns dominating
daily life andthe absence of an approvedbudgetoftenuntilmid-year,the standardbudget

(over 50 percent estimated in 2006) was executed outside regular budget procedures using

6.2       StrengtheningGovernment systems. The fiduciary standards in CGte d'Ivoire are as

country budgetary and financial management systems will have to be substantially upgraded.
However, the largest component o f the proposed grant would be for the reimbursement o f a
bridge loan used to clear World Bank arrears, with payment to the outside party providing that

support component, the improvement infiduciary standardsi s a central objective o fthe proposed

operation and the Government has made encouraging progress in implementing measures to

6.3       As detailed above in the section on the Government's program and the EGRG
design, these measures include the full reinstatement and application o f normal budget
preparation and execution procedures; a significant reduction in the use o f treasury advances;
regular publication o f budget execution reports; clearance o f the backlog o f outstanding draft

6.14   Inthe current environment of a country emerging from conflict, the proposed operation

6.15   Security risk. Despite progress in building consensus, political tensions could lead to

consequences for the security situation, defense spending, and the timing o f elections. Such
delays could jeopardize the attainment o f program objectives, dampen donor support, and
provoke a resurgence o f political instability. Specifically, resurgence o f new rebel activity not
controlled by the current, unified government could undermine the peace and reengagement
process. Also, slow progress in addressing the most pressing social and economic problems
could lead to instability in volatile areas and adversely affect security conditions, the business
climate and macroeconomic management.             The Government is pursuing efforts to improve
security. Notably, it has recently launched a disarmament, demobilization and reintegration
(DDR) program and "audienceforaine" that would lead to the resolution o f the identification
problem underlying the conflict. While mitigation of the security risk falls outside of the direct
scope o f this operation, it i s expected that continued international support for the peace process,

should help mitigate this risk.

6.17    Macroeconomic risk. The country's macroeconomic performance represents another

             C6te d'Ivoire -Assessment Letterby FundStafffor the WorldBank

TheIMF supported Coted 'Ivoire in 2007 under its Emergency Post-Conflict Assistance (EPCA),
approved by its Board on August 3, 2007. Thefiscal outcomefor that year was close toprogram
targets, but the composition of spending deviated from the program as overruns on current
spending were ofset by lower spending on crisis-exit programs and investment. There was
progress on structural reforms, especially on improving transparency in the energy and
cocoa/coflee sectors, although some measures were done with delays. The main objectives of the
2008 program, expected to be supported by further EPCA, are to preserve macroeconomic
stability and lay the basis for a rapid return to pre-crisis growth rates. Fiscal policy aims at
continued consolidation while addressing urgent post-conflict spending needs. Structural
measures seek tofurther enhancetransparency inpublic resource management.

In August 2007, C6te d'Ivoire began implementing a program supported by EPCA that
covered the remainder of 2007. The EPCA-supported program was approved by the IMF
Executive Board in an amount o f SDR 40.65 million at the time o f the Article rV consultation,
on August 3, 2007. The program aimed at laying the basis for addressing fiscal imbalances, an
unsustainable debt burden, and governance issues. In particular, it focused on removing
constraints to post-conflict recovery, creating fiscal space for restoring social services and basic
infrastructure in the whole country, and enhancing transparency inpublic resource management,
notably inthe budget and the energy and cocodcoffee sectors. Furthermore, the program sought
to begin normalizing relations with external creditors, starting with regularizing arrears to
multilaterals.

In November 2007 and February 2008, IMF staff reviewedmacroeconomic developments
and performance under the 2007 program and agreed ad referendum on an economic
programfor 2008. IMF staff will shortly submit the authorities' request for a second EPCA to
support their program in an amount o f SDR 40.65 million to IMF Management. The request and
staff report are expected to be considered by the IMF Board around end-March 2008. A
satisfactory track record under EPCA could pave the way for an arrangement under the IMF's
Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) and the decision point under the Heavily
Indebted Poor Counters (HIPC) Initiative later in2008.

Helpedby the reunification,the economic recovery is taking hold. Growth, estimated at 1%
percent in 2007, was driven by upturns in transportation, construction, and trade, while the
decline inmany other sectors halted. For the first time since 1999, real private sector investment
grew again and private capital outflows began declining. A temporary fall inoil production due
to technical problems prevented growth to be higher. Inflation remained moderate despite a rise
inprices ofcertain foodstuffs inthe thirdquarter. The real andnominal effective exchange rates
appreciated by 2percent in2007 as the US. dollar depreciated against the euro. Despite
favorable terms o f trade, the external current account surplus fell from an estimated 3 percent of
GDP in2006 to 1.4 percent in2007, because oil and cocoa export volumes fell and import
volumes rose faster than real GDP.


                                                 57

The fiscal outcome in 2007 was broadly in line with program targets. The primary basic
surplus was 0.6 percent o f GDP, below the target o f 1 percent o f GDP. However, the
composition of expenditure did not meet expectations as operating spending, especially those o f
national institutions (including sovereignty outlays and military premia) exceeded budget
allocations, while crisis-resolution and investment outlays were lower. Revenue was broadly on
target, but only because o f large recourse to advance tax payments, especially on cocoa export
duties. Thanks to efforts to mobilize revenue and resources on the regional financial market, the
government was able to settle a significant amount o f domestic arrears (including on subsidies to
private schools) and stayed up-to-date on current debt obligations to almost all multilateral
creditors. By end-February 2008 it had also finished paying its share inthe clearance o f arrears to
the World Bank.

There was progress on structural reforms, especially on improving transparency in the
energy and cocoakoffee sectors, although some measures incurred delays or were
postponedto 2008. Budget execution statements began to be regularly issued to the Council of
Ministers. Reporting on the collection and use o f quasi-fiscal levies on cocoa exports (% percent
o f GDP) improved and a large part o f levies accumulated in 2006-07 was allocated to
infiastructural and social projects in rural areas to be awarded through public tender and
supervised by an interministerial committee in close consultation with World Bank staff. Two
Bank-financed audits o f the energy sector were completed and the third, for the electricity
subsector, will be ready shortly. Compliance with EITI procedures is advancing and regular
reporting o f physical and financial flows in the energy sector to the Council o f Ministers has
increased awareness o f transparency issues. The increase inelectricity tariffs to bringthem closer
to prices inthe region andreduce the budgetary subsidies was delayed by several months because
of social tensions and food price increases, but was implementedinearly 2008.

The main objectivesof the programfor 2008 are to preserve macroeconomic stability and
lay the basis for a rapid return to pre-crisis growth rates. Real GDP growth should reach
3 percent in2008. It should be helped by a recovery in oiVgas output toward the 2006 level,
favorable prices for agricultural exports, and more generally, the reunification of the country and
the return o f private sector confidence. The current account surplus i s expected to hrther narrow
as imports, driven bythe recovery, outpace exports.

Fiscal policy in 2008 will focus on continued fiscal consolidationwhile addressingurgent
post-conflict needs. The target for the primary balance is 0.8 percent o f GDP, close to the
outturn for 2007. Non-priority spending i s to be reduced to create space for meeting social, basic
infrastructure, and other post-conflict needs. In addition to having raised electricity tariffs, the
authorities are committed to increase petroleum pumpprices (unchanged since 2005) by at least 8
percent on average by mid-year; these measures should help contain subsidies to the ailing
electricity sector and safeguard tax revenue from petroleum products. The authorities intend to
step up redeployment o f tax administrations inthe Center-North-West zones, as well as efforts to
reduce tax fraud. To meet exceptional needs during this crisis-exit year, the program foresees
large contributions to the budget from the national oil company, PETROCI. The residual
financing gap-after     taking into account clearance o f arrears to the AfDB, identified budget

support from multilaterals, and other donor support-is  expected to be met through the issuance
o fbonds on the WAEMU financial market.

Structural measures remain aimed at fostering transparency and safeguarding budget
resources, while deeper reform to enhance efficiency are initiated with Bank support.
Public expenditure management should improve through strict adherence to budget allocations,
and publication of budget execution statements every quarter. Measures in the energy sector
include (i)more complete reporting on financial flows in the oiVgas and electricity sectors; (ii)
preparingreports on oiVgas extraction andrevenue inconformity with EITIprocedures; and(iii)
applying fully the automatic petroleum pricing mechanism starting in mid-2008. Measures to
enhance transparency in the cocodcoffee and cotton sectors include: (i)       publishing quarterly
reports on the collection and use for rural investment o f quasi-fiscal levies; and (ii)
                                                                                       publishing
the budgets and certified accounts o f the sector agencies. In addition, levies will be further
reduced from CFAF 46.5 per kilo to 41.0 in2008/09 inorder to enhance producer incentives and
boost farmers' incomes.

Fund staff is of the view that the2008 program, if vigorously implemented with strong
ownershipby the united government, will support C8te d'Ivoire's exit from crisis towards
economic and social recovery. Among the risks are possible renewed political tensions, delays
inimplementingthe peace accord, spending overruns before the elections, and erodingconsensus
to implement structural reforms However, these risks are balanced by satisfactory program
implementation so far, the broad-based commitment o f key political actors to program
objectives, growing private sector confidence, and the increasing support from the international
community  .
