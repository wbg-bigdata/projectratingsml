Component A. Ankang-Maoba Expressway(AME). This component includesthe
construction o f an 87 kmexpressway connecting Ankang andMaoba, close to the border with
the Sichuan Province. The AME would consist o f four lanes designed for a speed o f 80 km/hr,
takinginto account the mountainous terrain o fthe corridor. This component includes the
construction o f the relevant ancillary buildings andthe acquisition o fthe relevant electrical and
mechanical (E&M) facilities. Itwill also require the acquisition o f landand the resettlement of
people. The design o fthe AME has paid adequate consideration to road safety features and has
minimized the social and environmental impacts, based on the analysis undertakenat the
feasibility stage and further adjustments requiredupon completion o fthe preliminary
engineering designs. A joint local-foreign consulting firm will supervise the construction o fthe
AME, ina manner similar to the one implementedunderthe AME component o fthe Second
ShaanxiProvincial Highway Project. The AME will connect witha 23 kilometers, four lanes
expressway between Maoba andthe border with Sichuan near Bashan Township (the Maoba-
Bashan Expressway) which i s beingconstructed contemporaneously with the construction of the
AME, fundedentirely by counterpart funds, anddirectly relatedto the AME.

A. STRATEGICCONTEXT AND RATIONALE
1. Countryandsector issues
1.      China has experienced significant improvements ineconomic growth, poverty reduction
and increasedaccess ofthe poorestpopulations to public servicesinthe last tenyears. Fromthe
early 1990sto 2001, the poverty rate decreasedalmost by halfto 16.6%, while the GDP growth
has remained around 8% per annum since the mid-1990s. By the beginning ofthe new
millenniumlife expectancy at 70 was higher thanincome-comparablecountries. However,
despitethe economic growth there havebeenincreasing disparities among regions, with higher
incidence of poverty and slower economic growth inthe Westernprovinces.

2.      ShaanxiProvince, with per capita GDP at around 70% of the national average, ranks
amongthe bottom fifthofthe 31provinces and regions of China, interms of economic
development. Fifty of its 107counties have beenofficially designatedpoor by the national
standard. A significant portion ofthe poor population inShaanxilives inthe two northern and
three southerncounties, the latter located inthe Ankang region. Lack oftransport infrastructure
has contributed to the depressed economic conditions inthese counties.

3.      Ankang CityPrefecture, with 11.5% (23,500 km2)ofthe total area of ShaanxiProvince,
has 8% (almost 3 million) of its population. Located inthe southernmountainous territory of the
province, Ankang has a strong potential for expansion of agricultural (e.g., tea), mineral (e.g.,
flagstone), andhydropower industries, and development oftourism. Lack of adequatetransport
accessibility and aroad network ofpoor quality are major constraints on the social and economic
development of the Ankang region. As of 2004,8 out of 10counties inAnkang have been
officially designatedpoor by the national standard.

4.      As part ofthe 11th Five-Year Plan, Chinese government is committed to promote a
"harmonized society", and to "balance andcoordinate" development betweenthe advanced
regions along the coast andthe economically lagging middle, west and northeastregions. To
achieve this goal, China has laid out the strategy to "develop the west, revitalize the northeast,
renewthe middle, andkeepthe leadingpositionof the east" through interaction,
supplementation, and integration among regions. Supporting this strategy, recent research shows
that investmentsthat canfacilitate the growth ofthe non-coastalregional hubs, such as Sichuan,
Hubeiand Shaanxi, would promote forward andbackwardlinkages withthe inlandwestern
regions andreduce the regional development gaps without sacrificing the aggregate growth.'

5.      The Government of China-at      boththe central and provincial levels-seeks      to address
the transport needs of Shaanxithrough two main investmentinterventions:

6.      These interventions, carried out inthe transport sector, are ofunprecedentedlevel in
China during the last 15 years. Roads, railways, andnavigable inlandwaterways have seen their
networks increaseby about 76%, 26% and 14%respectively between1990 and2003. Inthe
road sector, those investments are starting to mature and strategies needto rebalancethe
emphasis given across road classes andbetweennew construction andmaintenance. The proper
management of road networks requires adjustments to the managementframework, putting in
place adequate decision support systems with up-to-date data on road condition anduse and
developing alternative mechanismsfor the maintenance of road networks. Attentionto road
safety, tolling strategies, andvehicle operations (e.g., truck overloading) must continue to be
emphasized. Monitoring and evaluation can help decision making process, towards the
optimization of the allocation and use of resources. A comprehensivetreatment ofthese issues
will allow the road institutions to make their gradualtransformation from effectively building
highways to efficiently managing transport assets. The proposedproject seeks to contribute in
this respect.

7.      These interventions and strategies are inline with the Bank's FY03-05 Country
Assistance Strategy (CAS) and the FY06-10 Country Partnership Strategy (CPS), and those set
underthe Eleventh Five-Year Planfor the period 2006-2010, approvedby the State Council at
the endof2005, inparticular intheir aim at improvingthe investment climate and addressingthe
needs of disadvantaged groups and underdevelopedregions. A recent World Bank working
paper further supportsthis type of initiativea2Ultimately, these interventions would leadto
improvedproductivityinrural areas, enhancedcompetitiveness, and inter- and intra-provincial
trade, fostering the development of lagging Central and Western regions.

2. Rationalefor Bank involvement
8.      To address the strategic priorities set inthe Eleventh Five-Year-Plan and as reflected in
the Country Partnership Strategy (CPS) for 2006-2010, as part ofthe efforts to promote more
balanceddevelopment among the Chinese regions and expandeconomic opportunities for the
rural poor, the Government of China has requested the World Bankto support the development
ofthe so-called "7918" China NationalExpresswayNetwork (CNEN) Plan. This planis to build
7 radial links to Beijing, 9 North-South corridors, and 18 East-West corridors totaling 85,000 km
ofhigh-grade highways. The 7918 CNEN has beendesignedto reachmore than 1billionpeople
by connecting all provincial capitals and large urbancenters of more thanhalf-a-million
inhabitants with cities of more than 200,000 people (See Map 1inAnnex 15).

9.      The project, inparticular, will contribute to develop the North-South highway corridor
withinthe ShaanxiProvince andattaining the fullbenefits ofthe CNENnetwork connectivity in
a landlocked province, fostering its integration with its neighboring provinces and its

3. Higherlevelobjectivesto whichthe projectcontributes

11.     The project fits squarely withinthe Government o fChina's strategy to reduce disparities
among provinces and betweenrural andurbanareas. This goal i s part o fthe CPS for 2006-2010,
recently approvedby the World Bank Board o f Directors. Specifically, the CPS sets five pillars
andwithinthem three areas of support more directly relatedto the proposed project: (a) reducing
barriers to trade and investment; (b) enhancing public service delivery, and (c) expanding the
economic opportunities for the rural poor. The project will contribute particularly to the latter.

12.     The completion o f the project will enhance the accessibility o f one o fthe poorest regions
o f Shaanxi with potential for increasing production activities inagriculture, tourism and
minerals. The territorial perspective o fthe project that attempts to contribute to economic
growth with a pro-poor perspective-through      the combination ofinvestments inthe expressway
network and the lower classes or roads inthe expressway corridor-will       contribute to the higher
level objective o f reducing poverty inthe Ankang region and facilitating its trade-related
activities and the expansion o f its economic potential.

3. Projectdevelopment objective and key indicators

15.        The objective o fthe Project i s to assist the Borrower inimproving passenger and freight
traffic flows inan efficient and safe manner along the corridor from Ankangto Maoba in
Shaanxi Province by: (i)       enhancing road infrastructure capacity andnetwork integration along

16.        These objectives respond to a territorial perspective o fthe interventions that attempt to
address the transport needs o f the Ankang region inthe context o fthe construction o fthe Xian-
Ankang-Sichuan Boundary expressway. This expressway will lead to one o f China's most
industrial and populated cities, Chongqing. Shaanxi i s a strategic landtransport hub--surrounded
by seven other provinces--where North-South corridors intersect East-West corridors that link
coastal areas with the Western provinces. The project would meet boththe provincial
government objective to improve the transport network to support the social and economic
development o fthe province (especially inthe lagging counties, such as Ankang), andthe central
government objective to facilitate trade betweenthe Western Region andthe coastal areas.

17.        The assessment o f the achievement o fthe project development objective will be carried
out through the measurement o f outcome indicators, including: reduction inaverage freight rates,
travel times, and accident rates along the corridor to be constructed, as well as increases in
transport flows andreduction inpassenger and freight services along the rehabilitated local
roads, and higher percentage o fthe national/provincial roadnetwork with updated information
on its condition, and enhanced knowledge and applicationo fupdated road management
approaches.

5. Lessons learned and reflected inthe project design
22.     Most highway projects inChina have had a combination of an expressway component
witharelatively smaller localroads component. Inmost cases, the attentionand efforthave
been concentratedon the expressway component which i s typically the largest component, and
the one that requiresmost ofthe financial resources. Underthe proposedproject, SPCDhas
agreedto provide amore balancedattention andresources among the mainproject components,
seekingto contribute, simultaneously, ina more proactive mannerto the growth andpoverty
agendas of Shaanxi. Specific design issues, which incorporate lessons learnt from past
operationsinclude:

   Added efforts to review thefeasibility of theproposed investmentsand the quality of
    engineering designs. The project preparation processhas stressed the need to ensure the
    good quality of designs. This was startedby the review of the feasibility study and then
    followed with several independentreviews ofthe expressway designs. SPCD has entrusted
    the reviewofthe preliminary designto the FirstHighway Survey DesignInstitute of MOC;
    inaddition, a foreign expertteam (SpanishConsultants)hasbeencommissioned bythe Bank
    to conduct a thorough design review, includinga road safety audit. Furthermore, given that
   major variations often occur inprojects whose design is not basedon sufficient soil testing,
    supplementalgeological investigation will be carried out during the detailed design stage,
    especially on road sections where large bridges, tunnels, deep cuts, andhigh fills are located.
   Refocusing the sector agenda. Duringproject identification andpreparation a dialogue on a
   broader set of issues related to the managementof the road infrastructure inthe Shaanxi
   Province was undertaken.The ideawas to aim at initiatingthe capacity buildingprocessby
    refocusingthe provincial strategies from one that stresses the construction of highways, to
    one that emphasizesthe managementofthe road network and its traffic.

    Complementing high-class roads with a complementary component of lower-class roads.
    Often the selection o f investment o f lower-class roads respond to a dispersed distribution o f
    road segments without a coherent planthat can help achieve greater benefits to all
    components o f the project. Inthis case, upon the selection o f the main investment

    by the expressway corridor. SPCD agreed to increase the percentage o fthe loan allocated to

    The definition and design of the institutional strengthening subcomponents builduponthe

23.     The selected expressway section constitutes the third expressway project proposedby the

becausethe provincial government was able to secure alternative financing sources and preferred
the Bankto focus on a section for which it had a higher value added interms o f strategies and
technological innovations. Once the overall project was identified for World Bank financing,
several alternative alignments were considered and, through the feasibility study, the SPCD
selected the alternative that maximized economic returns, and minimized the negative
environmental and social impacts.

24.     To enhance the overall impact o f the AME component several alternatives were
considered, includinga province-wide lower-class rehabilitation program on one end, to one that
concentrated the location o f all local roads around the AME corridor. The rejection o f the
province-wide approach was based on the need to undertake a territorial perspective o fthe
project with an aim at maximizing the benefits o fthe AME investment to the surrounding rural
counties andtownships.

25.     The design o fthe AME expressway was subject to several alternative analyses over the

impacts on environmental sensitive sites such as the Ying Lake WetlandNature Reserve, scenic
spots, water source reservationareas, and cultural relics sites; (2) the minimization o f
resettlement and landoccupation; (3) the minimization o f impacts on community activities and
infrastructure; and (4) optimization o f engineering and financial aspects. (Further details on the
analysis and rejection o f alternatives are provided inthe environment section and related annex.)

3. Sustainability

38.     Based on the Bank's experience with the transport sector inChinathe physical assets are
expected to be constructed to highquality standards and completed on schedule. Moreover,
detailed designs and construction implementation will be closely supervised. Long-term
sustainability, particularly o f the road assets, will depend on effective and timely maintenance.
The support provided under the project to better undertake the collection o f data on the condition
o f road assets and the mechanisms to incorporate the data obtained into the management systems
o f the SPCD seeks to enhance the knowledge base to improve the maintenance planning
decisions. This effort shall lead to maximize the use o fthe maintenance resources and increase
the sustainability o fthe road investments.

39.     Roadmaintenance budgets inShaanxiare comparable to those o f other provinces with
similar networks. These budgets have increased by 53% overall and 42% on a per-km basis in
the last three years, inresponse to the increase inthe size and inthe maintenance needs ofthe
network, respectively. The Bank's assessment i s that while overall budgetfigures appear
reasonable albeit limitedinthe context o fthe likely upcoming needs o f a maturing network, a
fundamental issue remains inthat budgetestimates are based on coarse standardized estimates o f
cost and not based on any real estimate o fthe state and needs o f the system. The SPCD has
startedto adopt a maintenance management system which, ifproperly used, would allow the
maintenance agency to build more credible transparent budget estimates. Such estimates could
be a basis for discussions among various SPCD field units to ensure that resources are used ina
more optimal manner for the actual maintenance needs o f the different segments o f the road
network. A Loan covenant requiring an annual review o f SPCD's roadmaintenance budget and
planhas been agreedupon. The goal o fthe reviewwill be to identify ways inwhichroad
maintenance needs can be better forecast andprovided for.

1.       Sincethe early 1 9 9 0the
                               ~ ~ economy of China has moved from a centrally planned

2.       Inthe late 1990~~governments at all levels embarkedon major highway investment

the West and facilitate the economic growth of Central and Westernprovinces, including

With constrainedfunds, and the inadequatedecision makingmanagementtools it is difficult

Enhance maintenance managementpractices. Current force-account mechanismsappear not

InstitutionalFramework

1.      The overall developmentandimplementation ofthe laws, policies andregulations related
to transport inthe ShaanxiProvince rests within the ShaanxiProvincial Communications
Department (SPCD), and is responsibledirectly or by providingfinancial support and technical
guidance for the construction of highway infrastructure (as well as waterway infrastructure). To
undertake these responsibilities, SPCD has about 100core technical staff located at its
headquartersinXi'an, divided among 10 divisions that dependfrom four Deputy Directors who
directly report to the GeneralDirector. Attachment 1includesa schematic representationof the
organization structureof SPCD.

2.      SPCD is directlyresponsiblefor investments on Shaanxi's national and provincial
highways (witha current lengthof about 11,000 km) and on the expressway network (with a
current lengthof about 1,300 km). Inaddition, SPCD, through the cities and counties inthe
province, is responsiblefor supporting the development of the county andtownship roads and,
through villages, the rest of the lower-class rural roads. SPCD conforms to the general sector
policies andtechnical guidelines issues by the Ministryof Communications (MOC). This
division of responsibilities for investmentstranslates into a similar division of responsibilities for
the administration and maintenance of the road network inShaanxi. The administration and
maintenanceof the network takes place at three levels: (a) STPD's ShaanxiProvincial Highway
Bureau (SPHB); (b) the eleventraffic bureaus of the citiedprefectures; and (c) local-level
highway units ofthe country highway bureaus. SPHB provides resourcetransfers andtechnical
guidance on the maintenance of county and township roads. The direct maintenanceof village
roads i s managedby the communication departmentsat the municipal and county level and
operatedlocally.

3.      The managementand operation of the expresswaynetwork rests ontwo mainunits of
SPCD: the Provincial Transport Construction Corporation and the ShaanxiProvincial
Expressway Company. These two units overseethe activities of other subunits responsible for
specific sections of that network (suchas the Xi'an Bypass ManagementBureauor the
Provincial Transport InvestmentCorporation). Currently these units manage about 1,300 kmof
expressways(to become 1,600 by the end of 2006). There are about 140kmthat are operated
andmanagedby mixedstock-listed companies.

4.      Inthe Shaanxiprovince, eachexpresswayuses differenttoll chargesbasedonthe
construction cost and affordability of the road users. To determine the toll charges, SPCD
makes a proposal to the Shaanxi Provincial Government (SPG). The SPG will review the
proposal and consult with the Provincial Price Bureau (SPPB) before the final decisions are
made. Usually, the SPCD seeks higher toll charges while the SPPB tends to advocate lower toll
charges. The expresswaytoll chargeswill be the result of the adequatelyjustified agreement
betweenthe SPCD and the SPPB.

3.      The AME will passthrough Ankang andother two smaller cities (Ziyang andMaoba).
The AME will bethe only highgrade highway inthe Ankang region, which is one ofthe poorest

SOCIAL

I.SocialAssessmentSummary

BackgroundandObjective

1.      The proposedproject includesthe AME component of 87 kmandthe LRIP component of
atotal of 1,480 km. The social assessment was to: (a) facilitate a better understandingof social
economic development inthe local areas, includingits bottlenecks, constraints and developing
trends; (b) assess direct andindirectproject impact; (c) bring out the needs and feedback of local
population regardingthe project; (d) assess the poverty status, causes, alleviation efforts inthe
project areas andthe project impact on poverty reduction; (e) to identify ethnic minority
communities inthe project areas and assess the project impact on them; and (f) disseminate
project informationand facilitate consultation and participation of the localpopulation inthe
project design.

OrganizationandMethodology

2.      The social assessment team consistedofanadvisory group ofexperts from Houhai
University and field team from ChanganUniversity inthe Xi'an andAnkang areas. The social
assessment was conductedthrough desk review of historical data, government statistics,
academic research, and field work using participatory rural appraisal, anthropological
methodologies andquestionnaire surveys. Sampling was carefully done to be representative
taking into consideration of various factors such as social economic status, geographical
variations, different administrative areas, economic entities and ethnicity, covering bothproject
components. The assessment was conductedinJuly 2005.

Key Findings

3.      Social economicstatus. Ankang City lies withinQinba mountainous areas. The area has
aweak industrial base and inefficient agriculture structure, factors that are not helpedby lagging
behindininfrastructure development. Itsrichnatural resourcesremainto betappedina
sustainablemanner. This is one of the key poverty pockets inChina. O f its ten counties and
districts, sevenhave state level poverty status. In2002, its average GDP per capita i s only
38.5% ofthat nation-wide and 55.8% ofthat in ShaanxiProvince. The average farmer income
per capita is only 1417yuan, about 57% ofthat inChina and 88% ofthat inShaanxi. Ankang is
one of the focus areas in ShaanxiProvince for poverty reduction.

4.      These areas are rich innatural resources, with good potential for development. Key
livelihood activities inrural areas includeplanting (paddy, corn and wheat, mostly for self-
consumption), with an average landholdingof 0.6-3 muper capita, garden vegetable growing,
teaplanting andtobacco growing (key cashcrop, mainly inZiyang county), livestock (pig,



                                               - 70 -

sheep), cocoons raising and non-farmjobs inother areas. Non-farm jobs are found to contribute
to over 50% o f family income. Several generic features were identified inthe rural areas:

    The share o f farming income is on a declining trend.
    Two conflicting phenomenon seem to co-exist. Onthe one hand, there i s a general shortage
    o f landand average per capita landholding is low. Onthe other hand, the returns from
    farmingare low and there is a general reluctance to cultivate more land.
    The number o f migrantworkers ina family, andwhen they startedgoing out first, are telling
    benchmarks for wealth ranking. Those families who have more members working on non-
    farmjobs andstarted early are the most well-to-do inthe villages.
0   Inthe plantingandlivestockindustry,there seemsto bea willingness to go for less grainbut
    more cash crops plantingto increase income. However, the poor communications are a big
    constraint and many have chosen instead the alternative o f migrating out for non-farmjobs.

5.       Gender. The traditional division o flabor inthe family seems to continue. Women take
up almost all householdwork while menassume more labor-intensive work andoften go out for
migrantjobs. However, some changingtrends are also observed:

0   Women's status i s improving, with their improved education, exposures and experiences they
    get from outside and their economic status inthe family. However, their interest inpublic
    affairs i s still low.
0   Those women who did not migrate for non-farmjobs, undertake all the house chores and
    farming. Inthose families whose husbands are migrantworkers, women are under
    tremendous pressure with all the household work and farming. This intensity varies with the
    family financial situation.
0   Women are still facing big challenges inhygiene and public health.
0   They rely on traditional approaches for farming, labor intensive and yet with low economic
    returns.
a   They know about the project and support its construction inthe hope that it will bringalong a
    better life, particularly with transport conditions.

6.       Ethnic minority communities. Hanpeople account for 99% o fthe local population, with
a small population o f Huiethnicity, about 0.7% inthe project areas. The majority o f the Hui
population are urbanresidents inChenguan Town away from the AME. There are some Hui
households scattered among Han villages. The assessment reveals that the Huipopulation i s not
indigenous inthe local areas and they moved into these areas over the past few centuries. Most
o fthem are currently engaged inbusiness and trade with some infarming. Apart from religion,
there i s not much difference with the Hanpeople interms o f language and livelihood activities.
The assessment concludedthat they do not fall under the definition of indigenouspeople as
defined inWorld Bank OP 4.10. The assessment also found that the project would not adversely
affect them. Instead they would greatly benefit from the improved roads conditions under the
project.

7.       Communication conditions and bottlenecks. Ankangi s a relatively isolated area, with
poor road access. Waterways used to be an important communication channel, but have dropped
insignificance. Theynowmainly serve internaltransport inthe localareas. Though villages


                                                -71   -

withroadaccesshave reached about 90%, the accessroads are very poor andurgently need
rehabilitation. These have constrained local development interms o ftravel time, safety and
tapping o f local resources.

8.      Project impact analysis. The project will have an impact on various stakeholders inthe
local areas. These include enterprises, factories, urbanresidents, farmers along the highways and
roads, andpeople engaged inthe transport business. Ingeneral, the project will benefit greatly
local enterprises, processing factories, urbanpopulation and people engaged inbusiness
associated with transport as well as the farmingpopulation.

9.      The farmingpopulation is amongthe primary stakeholders. Farmers losing landand
houses will be adversely affected andthey will be compensated through implementation o f
livelihood rehabilitationprogram. Given the linear type o f impact and the relatively low level o f
dependency on farming, the project's adverse impacts are expected to be small to most villagers
andthe livelihood programput inplace is considered adequate to address these impacts. At the
same time, they along with other rural population will benefit greatly from the employment
generated duringproject construction, the newhighway and improved roads, as well as the
expected benefits inlocal development.

10.     The AME and the LRIP will speedup local road network construction and facilitate
faster and more efficient communications among provinces, cities, counties and villages. This
will facilitate faster transport andmarketingof localproduce, improve conditions for a faster
transfer o f rural surplus labor, and development o f rich natural resources. With these it i s also
expected that the local population will have easier and faster access to information and more
exposure to new ideas. They will inturnhelp with the economic development ingeneral. Local
population are also expected to benefit from the reduction o f their cost and time o f transport.

11.     Project impact onpoverty reduction. Ankang is one o fthe pockets o fpoverty inChina.
Some population i s still inabsolute poverty as defined by the state. Over 230,000 people, about
10% o f its population, are receiving assistance for minimum subsistence. Key factors relate to
natural conditions, low levels o f educationandpoor skills, lack o f access to information, lack o f
investment inagricultural production, and infrastructure development.

12.     The government has started an active poverty reductionprogram. Itsmainmeasures
include voluntary resettlement from difficult and harshliving environments, investment increase
inrural infrastructure, improvingrural industrystructure, strengthening training andextension
services. The project i s expected to contribute to the poverty reduction efforts through
improving the transport conditions. This will reduce the cost andtime o f travel and increase
travel safety. Local farmers would also be provided with a quicker and cheaper access to
markets for their agricultural products. Conditions would also be improved for the tapping o f
local resources, including the potential for tourism. The project will also generate employment
opportunities during and after construction, particularly the service industries.

13,     Information dissemination andpublic consultation. The social assessmentteam
disseminated project information through meetings and interviews for feedback from the local
population. The main feedback i s summarized as follows:


                                                - 72 -

   Itis generally desired that the projectshouldbe completed as early aspossible to improve
   transport conditions;
   most people agree with the proposed alignment, but some hope the rural program can be
   expanded;
   most people are receptive to the resettlement program andmany want cash compensation ina
   fair and transparent fashion; and
   people are concerned about noise, air and water pollution as well as water conservation and
   vegetation loss, but believe proposed safeguards measures are sufficient.

ConclusionsandRecommendations

14.    The social assessment concludedthat:

   The current poor transport conditions are considered the biggest bottleneck inthe social
   economic development inAnkang area. The proposed project i s seen as an important
   measure to improve the transport conditions and i s desired greatly by local people.
0  The project will not only promote the regional development inAnkang area, but also impact
   positively the daily livelihoods o fthe local population. Therefore the project will boost the
   poverty reduction efforts and impacts.
0  The project i s expected to help enhance women's economic and social status.
0  The Huiethnic minority population i s not indigenouspeople as defined inthe World Bank
   OP 4.10. Nevertheless, as they are mostly engaged inbusiness and trade, they will benefit
   greatly from the project.

15.    The social assessment further summarized the following recommendations from local
population and governments:

   The project alignment should be optimized to minimize farmland acquisition.
   Sufficient consideration should be givento crossings for local population.
   Cash compensation as requested by many should be considered together with other
   livelihood development measures to ensure restorationo f living standard. Particular
   attention should be givento the poor vulnerable families inresettlement to avoid
   impoverishment.
   Compensation rates, amount and mitigation measures should be disclosed fully to the local
   population. Compensation payments should be a fair, just andtransparent process to be
   monitored by the public.
   Environmental impacts should be mitigated ina timely manner.
   Access road construction should be considered together with village road improvements.
   Priority should be given to local population for employment opportunities during
   construction and construction material procurement.

BaselineEnvironmentalConditions

37.     Theproject area has a hilly andmountainous landscape,with elevation range of 330 mto
2,650 m. About 200 kmof the HanRiver which is aprimary branch ofthe Yangtze River, and
its two sub-branches, Yue River and RenRiver, lie inthe project area. Although there are rich
biodiversity resources inthe region, extensivedata collection, field survey, remote sensingimage
analysis and consultation with local authorities andpublic showed no indicationof the existence
of valuable nature habitats as definedinOP4.04. The detailed field survey was commissioned to
ecological experts from Ankang MunicipalEnvironmental Monitoring Station and Forestry
Bureau.

38.     ShaanxiProvincial Government establishedtheYing LakeWetlandNature Reserve
(YLWNR) in2002 for anartificial lake formed along the HanRiver claimingthe importance of
protecting birds such as black andwhite storks. YLWNR has atotal area o f 19,800 ha including
2,276 ha of Core Zone, 3,864 ha of Buffer Zone and 13,660 ha of Experimental Zone, and
managedby Ying Lake Nature Reserve ManagementBureau. Underthe Chinese regulations no
developmentprojects are allowed inCore and Buffer Zones, while inthe Experimental Zones
certain environmentally benign projects may be allowed under the approval of relevant
Environmental Protection Bureau. Many humanactivities exist inthe Experimental Zone of
YLWNR. The alignment avoids the core reservoir part of YLWNR, butthere are two sections
withinthe Experimental Zone, Le. one section (9.8 km) along the Yuehe Riverandthe other
section (5.2 km) adjacent to the reservoir. Five-time scientific survey and consultation with
public/experts, which were executedby Chang'an University, Ankang Environmental
Monitoring Station, Forestry Bureau and Station of Water and Soil Conservation, concluded that
the major function of YLWNR was rather for drinking water resourcesprotection, while its
wetlandnatural habitat value was muchlower thanoriginally claimed. Itwas concluded were
that the 9.8 kmsection lay along the well developedmountain slope of the Yuehe River valley



                                               - 78 -

surrounded with farmlands and village buildings so that its ecological function was insignificant.
The area adjacent to the 5.2 kmsection was not a habitat o fthe protectedbirds. There are
existing railway and roads (S207 and S310) inthe same corridor within the ExperimentalZone.
Inthis regard, the Shaanxi Provincial EPBissueda confirmation letter inMarch2005 stating that
the alignment was fully inline with the zoning requirement o f YLWNR.

39.     Baseline monitoring was conducted inMay andJune 2005, andthe results showed that
bothnoise and air quality conditions are generally good around the alignment andwill be in
compliance with the relevant standards. The current water quality o fthe Hanand RenRiver
along the alignment is fairly good, meeting the Class I11water quality standard.

40.      Shaanxi Archeological Institutewas contracted to conduct a cultural relics survey in
September 2005. The survey identifiednine cultural relics sites, including one provincial-level
andeight county-level sites. They were one building,two tomb stones andsix underground
tombshelics.

41.     The task team reviewed EIA o f a linked expressway inthe north for due diligence. Itwas

PotentialEnvironmentalImpactsandMitigationMeasures

DesignPhase

42.     Ying Lake WetlandNatureReserve. As the result o fan extensive alternative analysis,
the alignment has beensubstantially altered from its original scheme. This turnedthe alignment
away from the core reservoir part, thus significantly reducing potential environmental impacts to
YLWNR. Although the Experimental Zone o fYLWNR is by definition deemedas "critical
natural habitats" under OP.4.04, it was concludedthat the project would not involve its
significant conversion or degradation because: (a) the area subject to conversion was relatively
small (0.42% o fthe total area) and the best possible effort had been made to minimize the
conversion; (b) the area subject to conversionhad already been well developed andhad less
significant ecological value according to the detailed scientific survey and consultation with
public/experts; (c) the confirmation letter issued by the Provincial Environmental Protection
Bureauensured that the project was fully incompliance with the management framework o f
YLWNR; and(d) extensive preventiodmitigation measures were to be provided including
stricter water pollution, solid waste management and soil erosion control measures, as well as the
strengthening o f the management capacity to address increased tourists inaccordance with the
Plano f YLWNR.

43.     The alignment has beenfine tunedto avoidthree sites (above ground) out o fthe
identifiednine sites. For remaining six sites which may be affected by the project, SPCD has
developed mitigation measures and their implementation arrangements, including cost estimates

andtimetable. According to the arrangements, these six sites will be further excavated by a

subject to off-site preservation.

44.     Among the 39 noise sensitive sites (3 1villages and eight schools), noise levels were
predicted to exceed the standards at one location duringday time and at 11 locations duringnight
time in2011. Appropriate insulationmeasures were designedto be implementedat those

ConstructionPhase

45.     Heavy machinery operation and other construction activities may cause adverse impacts
on the acoustic environment inthe populated project areas. Strict regulations on the schedule o f
constructionworks will be implemented to minimize such adverse impacts. Heavy machinery
operationwill be suspended between 1O:OO p.m. and 6:OO a.m. near residential areas (150 m).
When continuous construction i s necessary, a prior public notice will be providedto nearby
communities and approval from local environmental protection authority is needed. Temporary
noise-barriers will be installed for sensitive sites.

46.     Dustduringthe constructionwork was identifiedas amajor environmental impact.
Asphalt and cement mixingplants will be located at least 200 mfrom residential areas on the
leeward. Material storage sites will be properly covered, and construction sitedmixing sites will
be regularly sprayed with water to suppress dust. Personal protection equipment (e.g. masks)
will be provided for workers wherever necessary.

47.     Wastewater from construction sites and camps will not be discharged into water bodies.
They will be appropriately treated andappliedto land. Domestic solid wastes from construction
sites and camps will be regularly collected and disposed o f inan appropriate manner. Incase o f
bridge construction, cofferdams and sedimentation ponds will be made to prevent water bodies
from increased turbidity.

48.     Tree cutting will be minimized inthe project area. Soil borrowing and spoil disposal
sites will be carefully selected and timely re-vegetationor reclamation will be conducted to
minimize soil erosion. Extensive soil erosion prevention planningwas developed and included
inEMP. Educationfor workers onwild life protectionwill beprovided. Re-vegetationprogram
will use local plant species, rather thanforeign species. Withinthe area o f YLWNR,
establishment o fborrow pits and spoil disposal sites and discharge o f domestic wastewater are

pollution, solid waste management, and soil erosion control measuresto minimize the impacts.

50.      Wastewater from service areas or toll stations will be processed by integrated biological

AlternativeAnalysis

51.      Duringthe preparationo fthe project, the final alignment was carefully selectedthrough
the comprehensive analysis o f five major alternatives schemes including "without project case".
Major considerations included inthe design are: (1) to minimize impacts on environmental
sensitive sites such as YLWNR, scenic spots, water source reservationareas and cultural relics
sites; (2) to minimize resettlement and land occupation; (3) to minimize impacts on community
activities and infrastructure; and (4) engineering and financial aspects.

52.      Significant efforts were givento minimize the environmental impacts on YLWNR and
associated sensitive sites. Since the proposed alignment at feasibility study phase (Hanjiang
Scheme) passedthrough the core reservoir part o f YLWNR as well as Ying Lake Provincial
Scenic Spot andProvincial First Grade Water Source ReservationArea, extensive discussion
was made among project designers, engineers and environmental protection personnel. As the
result, the HanjiangScheme was rejected at the final stage o f feasibility study phase, and an
alternative scheme (Haoping Scheme) was selected despite increased demolition and land
occupation as well as the need o f a longer tunnel. The Haoping Scheme could mostly avoid the
impacts on Ying Lake Provincial Scenic Spot and Provincial First Grade Water Source
Reservation Area, and significantly reduce the impacts on YLWNR by turningaway the
alignment from its core reservoir part although part o fthe road section still runs through the edge
o f the ExperimentalZone.
