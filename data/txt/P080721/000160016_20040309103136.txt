A.       ProjectDevelopmentObjective

1. Project developmentobjectives (see Annex 1)

More than 500,000 persons inthe Caribbean region are estimated to be infected with HIVlAIDS
at the end o f 2003. None o f the Caribbean countries and territories has been spared from
HIV/AIDS. The political leadership, the private sector, and civil society of these countries are
keenly aware of the disastrous consequences o f the pandemic in Sub-Saharan Africa countries
and are committed to protect their economies and to safeguard the health and well beingo f their
people through an aggressive regionalprogram o f support to prevention and treatment actions.

The World BanWIDA have been working intensively already with six o f the fourteen eligible
countries that include the six IDA and IDA-blendcountries (Guyana, Haiti, St. Lucia, St. Vincent
and the Grenadines, Dominica and Grenada). The Multi-Country HIV/AIDS Prevention and
Control Adaptable ProgramLending (APL)for the Caribbean Regionwas approved by the World
Bank Board of Directors in June 2001 with country loans, credits, and grants that amount to
US$l55 million. As of February 2004, US$ 86 million has been committed under the first two
APL phases (the program treats specific country projects developed within the regional
framework as "phases" o f a horizontal APL; countries are grouped under each "phase" according
to their readiness to undertake the initial set o f activities aimed at establishing sustainable
domestic structures for HIVlAIDSprevention and control). A regional approach was discussed
and agreed on duringAPL preparation, and now the intention is to include the proposedproject as
part of the thirdphaseo f the APL to help implement region-wide efforts.

The proposedproject, inparallelwith other internationalassistance, would help to establish,
consolidate and effectively coordinate regional support to the Caribbean Community's
(CARICOM) member countries intheir efforts to reduce the impact o f HIVlAIDS on the human
and economic development o fthe Caribbean Region, especially inthe context o f the accelerated
movement toward a Caribbean Single Market and Economy, and create a venue for knowledge
sharing and exchangeo f country experiences to understand and respond to the epidemic.


The development objectives o fthe regional programare threefold:

    0    an effectiveness objective by contributing to halt and began to reverse the spread of the
         HIV/AIDS epidemic by 2015;
    0    an efficiency objective by providingthe beneficiary countrieslpopulations inthe region
         with the knowledge and the tools       for informing and mobilizing decision makers,
         promoting the rights o fpersons livingwith HIV/AIDS (PLWA), to combat stigmatisation
         and discrimination, to manage and control opportunistic infections, to share clinical
         laboratory capacity and diagnostic tools, to strengthen surveillance and reporting, and to
         support establishing andmaintainingreliable information systems;

2. ati ion alefor theIDA Grant

The Indivisibility Argument. Until today support for controlling HIV/AIDS has focused on
national country-specific projects and it has become obvious that national programs are hindered
inreachingtheir objectives becauseof: (i) idiosyncraticmake upofthe region(multicultural,
                                              the
multilingual, multiracial, interdependent economies, common histories, unequal economic
development, porous borders; see Box 2a.1 and Tables 2a,1-2a..4 inAnnex 2); (ii)       tourism as the
leading economic base with strong seasonal fluctuations and large fluid inter-country movement
o f workers and visitors; (iii)limitedefforts by some countries to combat the spread o f the virus;
and (iv) the absence o f a regional tracking and surveillance system documenting and reporting on
how and where the virus is spreading and by whom. The proposed regional program therefore is
based on evidence and beliefs that national actions could be strengthened by a coordinated
synergistic regional response, by sharingbest practices, and by solidarity among the countries in
arresting the epidemic, and that the regional impact in halting and beginning to reverse
HIVlAIDS incidence and prevalence would be larger than the sum o f the country-by-country
outcomes. A coordinated regional response to the AIDS epidemic addresses the limited human
resource capacity available in many of the smaller countries o f the Caribbean and would help to
bridge the gap between countries that have a strong response to HIV/AIDS and those that are
strugglingto respond.

Successful previous experience with regional approaches, as well as clear economic analyses,
point to regional integration as the best mechanism for achieving efficiency among small
economies. A recent report from the Commonwealth Heads o f Government also reinforces the
economic justification for a regional approach-particularly          among small island economies.
With a borderless epidemic such as HIVIAIDS and a highlymobile and fluidpopulation such as
that found in the Caribbean, political boundaries are obscured by the facto regionalization. The
establishment o f a Caribbean Single Market and Economy, expected to come into full force by
2005, would only increase population mobility and resource flows across the nations o f the
region. Previous experiences and successes o fworking cooperatively across the Regionwould be
essential inaddressing the HIV/AIDS epidemic inthe Caribbean.

The proposed regional program represents an initiative o f the Pan Caribbean Partnership
(PANCAP), a regional arrangement coordinated by the CARICOM Secretariat, the organization
responsible for regional policy and cooperation among the Caribbean countries, to put in place
and implement a collective response to address the HIV/AIDS epidemic. The regional program
wouldprimarilvbenefit the followingmember countries that are part o f CARICOM:

The proposed project builds on the strengths and history of Caribbean regional cooperation.
Sharing knowledge and expertise across countries have been key features o f Caribbean
cooperation. So too has functional cooperation through institutions that work supra-nationally
across the region. Recently, for example, countries inthe Regionhave acted inconcert on actions
to adjust economies and regulations to accommodate climate change (e.g,, OECS Climate Change
Project and the Global Environmental Facility (GEF) Climate Change Project also managed
through CARICOM). They have also acted collectively indealingwith the cruise line industryon
solid waste disposal. Economic issues such as the access o f Caribbean-produced bananas to
European markets have also been addressed regionally, In the area o f health, the Caribbean
Cooperation in Health (CCH), in existence since 1982 and coordinated by CARICOM and Pan
American Health Organization (PAHOIWHO),               facilitates information sharing and the
implementation o f health systems reform, including a special emphasis on HIVIAIDS. In
addition, the Caribbean Epidemiology Center (CAREC), an international regional organization
and a specialized center o f PAHOIWHO, locatedinTrinidad and Tobago, provides services to all
countries inthe Region. Inthe area o f drug supply and quality, the Pharmaceutical Procurement
Service (PPS) located in St. Lucia has ensured the supply and availability o f quality essential
medicines to Organization o f Eastern Caribbean States (OECS) member countries for the last 16
years using a regional pooled procurement mechanism. In addition, the Caribbean Drug Testing
Laboratory (CRDTL) inJamaicaprovides essential services to Caribbean countries in the quality
evaluation o f medicines used in priority health programs. Most recently countries have entered
regional negotiations with the pharmaceutical industryto reduce the price o f antiretroviral drugs
for the region.

For these reasons, while HIV/AIDS by nature is a public health issue with substantial
externalities cutting across geographic borders, it is even more so in the fluid Caribbean
environment, Thus, HIV/AIDSprevention and control efforts in the Caribbean require not only
individual national but also cross-border actions that would reduce the risks o f gaps in
knowledge, strategy, the prevention and control o f the pandemic that might exist if countries did
not move ina coordinated manner.

Regional institutions such as CARICOM, CAREC, the Caribbean Regional Network o f People
Living with AIDS (CRN+), the University o f the West Indies (UWI), and the United Nations
AIDS Program (UNAIDS), who are part of the Pan Caribbean Partnership Against HIVIAIDS
(PANCAP) and to be supported under the proposed program, play a pivotalrole inmonitoring the
social conditions o f the Caribbean people and in developing institutional responsesto emerging
challenges. As a whole, these institutions are now promoting and coordinating a range o f health

promotion and disease prevention and controlprograms to address the HIVlAIDS epidemic inthe
Caribbean Region. These organizations therefore truly serve the Caribbean region as a whole and
not a particular country (see Table 2.1 in Annex 2 for a list o f these organizations' member

Economic and Social Factors. As shown inAnnex 2, the Caribbean Region is unique in having
characteristics o f both developed and developing countries. Moreover, the Caribbean Region is
characterized by disparity among its countries, states, and territories andby regional vulnerability
and income volatility. Recent data indicate the range o f variation in the region in terms o f
absolute size o f the economy, population, Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita, andHuman
Development Index (HDI). For many countries inthe Caribbean, the size o f the economy i s as
important a constraint as poverty. As shown, the GDP o f the majority o f the countries in
CARICOM was less than US$ 1billionin2000, andthe populationwas lessthan 500,000 people.
Economies o f this size cannot support highly specialized HIVlAIDS prevention and care
programs inthe absence o f a great deal of external support. GDP per capita inthe region ranges
from US$ 14,960 in the Bahamas to US$ 510 in Haiti. Similarly, the ranking o f countries
according to the HDI ranges from 31 for Barbados to 146 for Haiti. The majority of the
PANCAP countries are in the middle range o f countries ranked by HDI, five are in the highest
range, and only Haiti i s inthe lowest category.

The economies o f CARICOM member countries experienced declines in economic output for
most o f 2002 on account o f depressedaggregate domestic demand and the lacklustreperformance
o f the tourism sector after the September 11,2001 events. The consequences o f these events are
especially severe for the mono-crop economies o f the smaller islands. Countries depending on a
single export crop, such as bananas, experienced tremendous pressures. This is evident in
Dominica, where revenuesplummetedby 80% duringa one-year period.

Poverty remains widespread inthe region affecting the aged, women and young people inthe 15-
29 age group (in which the highest levels o f unemployment are experienced). World Bank and
Caribbean Development Bank (CDB) studies over the last several years have shown that close to
one-third o f the population is many o f the countries o f the region live inpoverty. Access o f the
poor to HIVlAIDSprevention andcare and support services is extremely limited.

Mobility is often linkedto increasedrisk o f HIV infection. Caribbean people are, ingeneral, very
mobile, travelling from islandto islandand outside the region for work, study, leisure, and family
reasons. In2000, for example, data from the InternationalOrganization for Migration(IOM) o f a
total population o f 268,792 inBarbados, 23,485 or 8.7% were foreign-born, 65% of whom were
from CARICOM countries. There are three main streams o f long-term or life-time migrants into
Barbados: the largest stream from the WindwardIslands, mainly from St. Lucia and St. Vincent,
with 28% o fthe foreign-born, Guyana was secondwith 18.5% and the UnitedKingdomwas third
with 13.2% of the foreign-born. Political instability and huge socio-economic inequalities
between neighbouring countries are important catalysts for migration. Inthe 1 9 9 0 for~ ~ instance,
the number o f Haitians entering the Dominican Republic was estimated at about 12,000 per year.
Flows o f migrants in the Caribbean include sex workers, tourists, business travellers, traders,
casual labourers and others.      Recent estimates from the Dominican Republic indicate that
approximately 60,000 Dominican women are engaged in commercial sex work in Europe and in
other countries inthe Caribbean Region. Interms of intra regional movement o f the commercial
sex workers (CSW), there is know to be a significant degree o f movement between Suriname,
Guyana and Trinidad and Tobago inthe industry. A recent study conducted by CAREC in 2000
estimated that some 16% o f female CSW inGuyana also pliedtheir trade inTrinidad. According
to recent data from the Caribbean Tourism Organization(CTO), in2001 about 1.4 million tourists


                                                 4

visited the different countries in the region; this number is expected to increase to more than 2
million in2004.

The fact that females increasingly dominate the major migration streams is o f particular note.
The predominance o f one gender in a migration stream indicates that the immigrants are not
moving with spouses or families. This presents opportunities for high-riskbehaviours such as
multiple sexual partners and the likelihood of engaging in or purchasing commercial sex. In
addition, economic circumstances in the region also influence the growth of the sex tourism and,
increasingly, new groups are being pulled into commercial and "transactiona1"sex (e.g., sex for
food, sex for school fees). They include schoolgirls, housewives and children. The region is also
characterized by a large number o f youth-at-risk, including poor and undereducated youth with
few or nojob prospects and tenuous family structures.

These economic trends and social factors have significant implications for health and the health
status o f the Caribbean population. Because o f high levels o f poverty, migration patterns, the
vulnerability o f the economy and highlevels o f unemployment, the labor force i s concentrated in
the service sectors and in the informal economy that provide fewer social safety nets and may
result in more vulnerability to HIVlAIDS. A study by the World Bank explained that the
economic consequences of HIVlAIDS itself may not yet be apparent because o f the long latency
period o f the disease. However, some preliminary studies have suggested that consequences o f
the HIVlAIDS epidemic will include increasedexpenditures for AIDS and AIDS-related diseases,
reduction inproductive investments, and declines inGDP per capita.

B. Strategic Context

1. Sector-related CountryAssistanceStrategy (CAS)goal supported by theproject (seeAnnex
1)

The World Bank's strategy for addressing HIVIAIDS inthe Caribbean was part o f the assistance
strategy adopted by the Steering Committee o f the Caribbean Group for Cooperation inEconomic
Development (CGCED) duringits meeting inJune 2000. This strategy is stated inthe documents
"Toward A Caribbean Vision 2020: A Regional Perspective on Development Challenges,
Opportunities and Strategies for the Next Two Decades," o f June 2000, that serves as the
framework for development and assistance programs for the Caribbean, and "HMAIDS in the
Caribbean: Issues and Options, o f June 2000, prepared by the World Bank in conjunction with
                                  "
CARICOMand UNAIDS.
Participation in the proposed project is also consistent with the June 2001 CAS for the Eastern
Caribbean Sub-Region (Report No. 22205-LAC).           The CAS identified the need to promote
human development for long-term growth, to be accomplished through health and education
programs that emphasize improved access, equity and quality and the need for regionally
coordinated actions on issues when in-country solutions could be significantly disruptedby "off-
shore" events. The CAS also outlined an exception to the Bank`s graduation policy, which
permitted IBRD financing for the HIVIAIDS prevention and control program in Barbados. That
exception was basedprimarilyon the public goods and externalities rationale for a communicable
disease (such as HIVIAIDS) and Barbados' leadership role in these activities in the Eastern
Caribbean. In essence, Barbados was seen as a sub-regional hub with advanced economic and
social infrastructure providing these services to some o fthe less equipped countries o f the Eastern
Caribbean. This has indeedoccurred since 2002.
Similar goals as in the Eastern Caribbean Sub-region CAS were also outlined in the CAS for
Jamaica (Report No. 21187-JM) o f November 2,2000; Dominican Republic (Report No. 19393-
DO) o f June 9, 1999, for Haiti (Report No. 15945-HA) o f August 13, 1996, and for Trinidad and
Tobago (Report No. 14028-TR) o f March 7, 1995, as well as in the Guyana Policy Framework
Paper, 1998-2000 (Report SecM98-499) o f June 18, 1998, and the Belize, Jamaica and Trinidad
and Tobago's MediumTerm Strategy 2000-2002 prepared for CGCED.

World Bank Corporate Strategy for HIVIAIDSPreventionand Control

The strategy for addressing HIVIAIDS inthe Caribbean is also consistent with the World Bank's
corporate strategy for fightingthe epidemic world-wide. HIVIAIDS poses an unparalleledthreat
to global health and well-being. With more than 42 million people worldwide currently living
with HIV, the epidemic is eroding human welfare, socioeconomic progress, productivity and
social cohesion. Since the 1980s when the epidemic began, the World Bank has been one o f the
largest providers o f financial support for HIVIAIDS programs in developing countries with
approximately US$1.5 billion committed through loans, credits, and grants for HIVIAIDS
activities worldwide. The Bank's mission o f reducing poverty and supporting development
necessitates action on HIVIAIDS. As such, fighting HIVIAIDS is a core strategy o f the World
Bank Group's development agenda. To meet the AIDS crisis, the World Bank's strategy is to:

Status of HIVlAIDSInthe Region

The Caribbean is the second most affected region in the world behind sub-Saharan Africa, with
an adult HIV prevalence rate o f 2.4% in2003. More than 500,000 persons were estimated to be
infected with the HIV virus, including 20,000 children (given the high level o f underreporting,
the total infected population is estimated to be higher). The number of deaths from AIDS is
42,520, including more than 5,000 children, and the number o f orphans is 251,450,          In 2001
alone, 60,000 persons were newly infected with the HIV virus. Primarily, the virus is transmitted
heterosexually and through men who have sex with men(MSM). For disaggregated country data
see Tables inAnnex 2.

Mirroring global patterns, persons aged 15 - 44 are most vulnerable to the disease, especially
young women. CAREX notes that in 1999, approximately 70% o f AIDS cases reported were in
this age cohort. The exceptionally young age at which young persons become sexually active,
along with the occurrence of girls having sex with older men, exacerbate the pandemic, As a
result, C A E C reports that young women aged 15 - 24 exhibit an HIV prevalence rate 2 - 4
times higher than all other female age cohorts, and 3 - 6 times higher than males in that age
group, Women are at a greater risk o f contractingHIV inbothbiological and sociological terms,
as they are both physically more vulnerable and often have little or no power to negotiate safer
sex practices with male partners.

Inseveral countries HIV/AIDS hasbecomethe leadingcauseofdeath. The economic impactcan
be devastating. A study conducted by CAREC and UWI's Health Economic Unit estimatesthat,
respectively, 4.0% and 6.4% o f the GDP o f Trinidad and Tobago and o f Jamaica, respectively,
could be lost due to AIDS by 2005.

IDA countries inthe Caribbean region are beingparticularly hardhitby the epidemic. Haiti with

Many o f the most potent tools in preventing HIVlAIDS are "public goods", particularly
behavioral research, specialized laboratory services, antiretroviral (ARV) drugs, community
mobilization, and epidemiological surveillance.       These effective tools are costly in terms o f
financial as well as the required specialized scientific and technical personnel. Mobilizing these
tools i s generally beyond the means o f the smaller and o f the IDA-eligible countries in the
Caribbean.    These are the countries that would benefit most from a regional approach.
Conducting epidemiological research, building political consensus, combating stigma and
discrimination, promoting cultural change and attitudes, and providing specialized laboratory
services would be carried out collectively on a regional basis allocating scarce resources more
efficiently, gaining economies o f scale and enhancing quality.

Macroeconomic Impact of HIVIAIDS. As recently shown in a landmark study conducted by
the World Bank on the long-term economic costs o f AIDS in South Africa (Bell, Devarajan, and
Gersback, 2003), most o f existing estimates o f the macroeconomic costs o f AIDS, as measured
by the reduction inthe growth rate of GDP, are modest (e.g., they range between 0.3 and 1.5 per
cent annually in Africa).     The reason is that these estimates are based on an underlying
assumption that the main effect o f increased mortality i s to relieve pressure on existingland and
physical capital so that output per head is little affected. With a more plausible view o f how the
economy functions over the long run, the economic costs o f AIDS are almost certain to be much
higher. Not only does AIDS destroy existing human capital, particularly in the most productive
age groups as shown in the Caribbean by killing mostly young adults, it also weakens the
mechanism through which knowledge and abilities are transmitted from one generation to the
next; for the children o f AIDS victims will be left without one or bothparents to love, raise and
educatedthem.

3. Sector issues to be addressedby theproject and strategic choices

Early efforts to prevent and control the spread o f HIVlAIDS assumedthat the epidemic could be
contained quickly at a national level. Early interventions focused largely on ensuring the
adequacy o f the national responses. It was soon realized, in Africa as well as in the Caribbean,
that factors such as mobile populations, and economic and cultural interdependence facilitate
cross-border spread, and that coordination, common action and trans-national programming and
intervention are required. A special feature inthe Caribbean is the geographic dispersion inherent
to islandcountries, however good sea, air and telecommunications facilitate regional coordination
efforts. The strategic option of relying solely on an aggregate o f national responses will not be
sufficient for effective control across the region. Therefore, the Caribbean region has adopted a
mixed strategy of national and regional actions supported by world leaders in the fight against
HIV/AIDS.

1. Economic

Although the spread o f HIVlAIDS is a result, mainly, o f individual behavior and choices, there i s
a compelling economic benefitfrom public action to prevent it and to control its spread. A study'
conducted by the University o f West Indies suggests that economic losses due to HIVlAIDS
would amount to about five percent o f GDP in Jamaica and Trinidad and Tobago by year 2005.
The estimated GDP loss is driven by the number o f HIVlAIDS cases and the average loss o f
incomeloutput associated with those cases.          The study forecasts the economic impact of
HIVlAIDS by modeling the impact on five blocks: output, labor supply, employment, savings and
investment, and spending on HIVlAIDStreatment, An increase in HIVlAIDS-related deaths and
morbidity adversely affects labor supply causing wages to increase.            Raising wages and
decreasing labor supply translate into lower levels of employment. The increase in expenditure
associated with increased HIVIAIDS incidence diverts funds away from productive savings,
which inturn affects the levels of investment that can be achieved. Depressedlevels of labor and
capital affect the levels o f output from the various sectors andtherefore overall GDP.

Gersbach, 2003), using a "human capital" model: in the absence o f AIDS, the counterfactual
benchmark, there is modest growth, with universal and complete education attained within three
generations. Ifnothing i s done to combat the epidemic, however, a complete economic collapse
will occur within three generations. This outcome is postulated to be the result of: first, AIDS
killsldestroys human capital, especially adults in their prime; second, AIDS wreck the
mechanisms that generate human capital formation: decreases quality o f child-rearing due to

To date, there has been an active consultation and participation o f national and regional agencies,

partners. That meeting underscored the need for a well-coordinated, multi-sectoral expanded

Caribbean Task Force on HIVIAIDS, with a formal mandate from the Ministers o f Health o f the
participating countries to coordinate and strengthen the regional response to the HIVIAIDS
epidemic.   Chaired by the CARICOM Secretariat, members include national experts in key
HIVIAIDS programming areas, the Caribbean Network for People Living with HIVIAIDS

Under the APL each country that undertakes a national program assumes responsibility for
meeting its national prevention and control objectives. However, each country also agreed that

Mobile Populations:       The term migrants and mobile populations are sometimes used
interchangeably. According to the UnitedNations, migrant is defined for statistical purposed as a
person who enters a country other than that o f which helshe is a citizen for at least 12 months,
after havingbeen absent for one year or longer. Mobilepopulation, inturn, are described broadly

The above priorities recognize that the movement o f Caribbeanpeopleacross nationalboundaries
i s an historicalmainstay o f Caribbean cultures and economic survival. The economic viability of
the Caribbean region is dependentupon the mobility o f people into, out of, and within the region,
given its limited human resources base. Mobility within the Caribbean has three different
features: a) educational mobility (e.g., young people travel between countries to attend tertiary
education institutions); b) economic mobility (e.g., both formal and informal worforces; and c)
cultural mobility (e.g., to participate in a variety o f events such as Carnivals and Jazz Festivals).
Mobility is expected to increase as the regional policies o f Freedom o f Movement and Single
Market and Economy are fully adopted by the CARICOM countries.

Annex 2.a An Overview of the Caribbean

        Box 2a-1. Overview of the Caribbean Region

   Definitions o f the territorial scope o f the Caribbean vary. Probably the "sociallcultural" definition o f
   the "wider" Caribbean region is most relevant as i t pertains to the HIVIAIDS epidemic within the
   region. The "wider" Caribbean region includes the following:
        1. The sovereign-state members o f the Caribbean Community (CARICOM), including both
            island-nations (Antigua and Barbuda, The Bahamas, Barbados, Dominica, Grenada, Haiti,
            Jamaica, Montserrat, Saint Kitts and Nevis, Saint Lucia, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines,
            and Trinidad and Tobago) and the mainland countries o f Belize in Central America, and
            Guyana and Suriname inSouth America.
        2.  Spanish-speaking Cuba and the Dominican Republic.
        3.  The semi-autonomous states o f the Kingdom o f the Netherlands (Aruba and the Netherlands
            Antilles islands of Bonaire, Curacao, Saint Marten, Statia, and Saba).
        4.  The British-dependent territories o f Anguilla, Bermuda, British Virgin Islands, Cayman
            Islands, Montserrat, andthe Turks and Caicos Islands.
        5.  The US.commonwealth o fPuerto Rico andterritory o f the U.S. Virgin Islands.
        6.  The territories o f the Republic of France consisting o f French Guyana, St. Marten,
            Guadeloupe, and Martinique.
   The Caribbean is a multiethnic region with many cultural differences. There are English-speaking
   countries (e.g., Trinidad and Tobago), Spanish-speaking countries (e.g.,       the Dominican Republic),
   French-speaking countries (e.g., Haiti), and Dutch-speaking countries (e.g., Suriname). The majority
   o f the population is o f African descent, but there are also people o f European, Hispanic, and Asian
   ancestry (e.g., East Indians inTrinidad andTobago andGuyana).
   The mainland states o f Belize, Guyana, and Suriname, which by virtue o f language and cultural
   heritage, form part o f the Caribbean region, are much larger in land mass than the island states o f the
   Caribbean: Belize (29,963 km2,population 215,000), Guyana (219,470 km2,population 813,000), and
   Suriname (163,820 km2,population 437,000).          The island states o f the Caribbean vary in size and
   population from Anguilla (91 km2and 8,000 inhabitants) to Jamaica (11,424 km2and a population of
   2,447,000).
   Historically, the Caribbean region has been strongly influenced by Europe and the United States.
   Many o f the English-speaking Caribbean countries have modeled their educational system, the legal
   system and the political system on the United Kingdom. The countries o f the English-speaking
   Caribbean have a combined population o f around 6.7 million scattered over the vast Caribbean Sea,
   whose farthest points span about 3,500 kilometers between the coasts o f Belize and Guyana. The
   Bahamas and the Dominican Republic are economically reliant on the United States. France and
   Holland also have strong links with some o f the non-English speaking countries, for example
   Martinique and Curacao. Therefore, there has been much migration from these countries to the
   Caribbean. The Caribbean is also a major tourist destination, attracting visitors from many parts o f the
   world.   Similarly, over the past 40 years, for economic reasons, many Caribbean citizens have
   migrated primarily to the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom.             There is also much
   businesstravel within and outside the Caribbean.
   The geographic, political, cultural, and linguistic diversity o f the Caribbean region underscores both
   the complexities inunderstanding the patterns o fHIV contagion and successful responses. Given that
   human movement throughout the Caribbean and between it and other geographic areas has been the
   basic foundation of this region's existence since its formative days o f "triangular trade" o f slavery and
   colonization, it is clear that an appropriate response to the HIVIAIDS epidemics must recognize the
   contributing factor o f its geopolitical heterogeneity and the complete disregardby HIV o f geopolitical
   boundaries.

B. SettingPriorities
In order to generate the highest possible benefits in terms of averted infections and reduced
mortality, a subset o f interventions must be chosen from the menu o f possible initiatives. Once it
has been decided that public intervention i sjustified for HIV/AIDS (on the basis o f market failure
arguments), then information about cost-effectiveness can help the policy-maker determine which
interventions to prioritize to achieve maximum impact.
Maximum impact is achieved by giving financing priority to those interventionswith the highest
cost-effectiveness. The current consensus seems to be that the most cost-effective interventions
are those targeted towards CSWs (peer education) and other high-risk groups (MSM and high-
risk heterosexual men), the management of sexually transmitted infections, and the use of anti-
retroviral drugs to prevent MTCT.
Inall these cases, groups with a highHIV reproductive rate are reached at low costs. The HIV
reproductive rate itself hinges on the amount o f time a person remains infectious, the risk o f
transmission per sexual contact" and the rate o f acquisition o f new partners (high among CSWs
for example). Therefore, all other things being equal, preventing infection in someone with a
high rate of partner change and with risky sexual contacts will indirectly avert many more
infectionsthanpreventinginfection insomeone with low-risk behavior'*. This "multiplier" effect

E.Caveat

It should be clear that there is an element of conjecture in all projections.          The Caribbean
estimates were arrived on the basis o f critical inputs such as the infection probabilities estimated
through a sexual behavior survey that precedesthe econometric estimation.

There are two reasons why the economic estimates are important to the response to the
HIVlAIDS epidemic inthe Caribbean: the essential fragility o f these economies and the need to
make the case for resource mobilization for the response, inreference to the expected benefits o f
a successful response.

On the issue o f fragility, it is useful to remember that by and large, the economies o f the small-
islandCaribbean states are monoculture systems, depending for the mainon one source o f foreign
exchange- a source that is also the main driver o f the entire economic system. What this means
i s that, in the absence o f buffering or compensatory sectors, even the slightest unfavorable shock
has the potential to trigger a downward economic spiral, The fact i s that, by simultaneously
compromising both the labor productivity and savings pillars of the economic system, HIV/AIDS
has all the features o f an unfavorable shock. So what is at stake for the economies o f the



                                                  81

Caribbean is not simply a negative economic experience, but the very capacity o f the economic
system to holdtogether.

As recently shown ina landmark study conducted by the WorldBank on the long-tenn economic
costs o f AIDS in South Africa (Bell, Devarajan, and Gersback, 2003), most o f existingestimates
of the macroeconomic costs of AIDS, as measuredby the reduction in the growth rate of GDP,
are modest (e.g., they range between 0.3 and 1.5 per cent annually inAfrica). The reason i s that
these estimates are based on an underlyingassumptionthat the main effect o f increased mortality
is to relieve pressure on existing land and physicalcapital so that output per head i s little affected.
With a more plausible view o f how the economy functions over the long run, the economic costs
o f AIDS are almost certain to be much higher. Not only does AIDS destroy existing human
capital, particularly in the most productive age groups as shown in the Caribbean by killing
mostly young adults, it also weakens the mechanism through which knowledge and abilities are
transmitted from one generation to the next; for the children ofAIDS victims will be left without
one or bothparentsto love, raise and educatedthem.

With respect to resource mobilization for responding to the epidemic the case for a national
reallocationo f resources is really made much stronger when economic impact estimates are used
to support the argument. Avoiding the lossesprojected becomes the greatest source o f benefit to
the country facedwith HIVIAIDS.
