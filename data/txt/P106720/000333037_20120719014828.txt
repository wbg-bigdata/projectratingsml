F. Results Framework Analysis

Program Development Objectives (from Program Document)

   A. Fiscal Management
     Increase sustainability and transparency of fiscal policy
     Make tax system more equitable, transparent, neutral and stable
     Strengthen budget reporting and planning
     Increase equity in intergovernmental transfers
     Improve efficiency and impact of public spending on a national, sub-national and sectoral
      level
   B. Competitiviness
    Make public sector processes more transparent, accessible, and agile
    Reduce transaction costs for the private sector to enter, operate in, and exit markets /
      Expand and deepen international trade
    Promote sustainable financial deepening

Revised Program Development Objectives (as approved by original approving authority)

1. Program Context, Development Objectives, and Design2

1.1 Context at Appraisal

1.     In mid-2000s, Peru became one of the best-performing economies in Latin America.
Based on sound macroeconomic policies and strong demand for Peruvian commodity exports,
GDP grew by 4 percent per year, on average, between 2000 and 2005, and gradually reached 6.4
percent in 2005. That growth was all the more impressive considering the external shock of the
region in the early 2000s as well as the political instability that prevailed at the start of the
decade. Peru was putting behind years of instability and looking optimistically toward the future.

2.      The new Government that took office in 2006 intended to build on the achievements
of past administrations and to establish the basis for sustainable long-term growth. During
the previous five years, the Government of Peru (GoP) focused successfully on disciplined fiscal
and monetary policies, and in 2006 the challenge was to move beyond laying the foundations for
macroeconomic stability to the second generation of reforms. These included strengthening fiscal
management, improving public expenditure quality, and enhancing competitiveness to achieve
sustainable and broader-based economic growth. Nevertheless, significant challenges remained.
Half of the population was living in poverty, the private sector faced numerous obstacles to
economic activity, and many government institutions were inefficient and poorly managed.

3.       Maintaining high rates of economic growth, improving social development, and
modernizing public sector institutions were government’s key priority areas. Those
priorities were reflected in the 2007 budget, which called for an increase in public investments,
with a particular emphasis on removing key obstacles to economic activity, while encouraging
private investment. Key among these measures were finalizing the Free Trade Agreement (FTA)
with the U.S., offering concessions on regional airports, approving a timetable for bidding on
infrastructure projects, and unilaterally lowering tariffs on imported capital goods and
intermediate inputs. The Fiscal Management and Competitiveness Development Policy Loan
(FMCDPL) programmatic series, along with a series of Social sector DPLs and of Environmental
DPLs, was designed in close partnership with GoP officials and in line with their vision.

4.      Already under the previous administration, the GoP established a broad consensus
on its policy program with the legislative branch and society at large, a process that the
Bank actively supported. During the transition period after the elections, high level officials
from the previous and new administrations met together with the Bank’s country team to discuss
the policy notes collected in Perú: La Oportunidad de Un País Diferente—Próspero, Equitativo
y Gobernable, and there was considerable agreement among participants on the principal areas of
reform needed in the country. The government plan was approved in Congress by a large
majority in August 2006. The National Accord (Acuerdo Nacional), first signed in 2002, was
reactivated as a forum for discussion of long-term objectives agreed among representatives of all
political parties and various segments of civil society.

5.      To achieve faster and more equitable economic growth, the GoP plan called for
strengthening the efficiency and quality of fiscal management, and improving
competitiveness. On the fiscal side, it looked for a more efficient tax system, greater control
over spending, and a better management of public debt. But beyond the quantity, the GoP also
wanted to focus on the quality of public accounts, implementing more rigorous monitoring and
evaluation of spending programs using results-based budgeting techniques and simplifying and
rationalizing the tax system. To improve competitiveness, the GoP intended to enact policies to
support the private sector, such as public investments in key areas, removing numerous obstacles
in administrative procedures, export promotion and greater credit to small and medium-sized
companies.

6.       In the view of the GoP, fiscal management and competitiveness were mutually
reinforcing areas which could offer a significant payoff in terms of economic growth, social
development and poverty reduction. A solid fiscal management is a desirable end in itself, but
it also supports country competitiveness by generating confidence in the private sector on overall
macroeconomic stability, and the ability to undertake long-term investments and planning. At
the same time, greater country competitiveness would improve the fiscal situation by generating
increased tax revenues and reducing the need for government intervention to stimulate economic
growth. In addition, the combination of high growth with prudent fiscal/financial management
was expected to provide flexibility and fiscal space to address social needs.

7.       The programmatic series of FMCDPLs was at the core of the Country Partnership
Strategy (CPS) for the FY2007-11, discussed by the Board of Executive Directors on
December 19, 2006. The CPS described several thematic ―clusters‖ forming the basis of the
Bank’s partnership with Peru, and the FMCDPL series directly addressed aspects of: maintaining
macro stability; accelerating growth and widening the base of growth; and modernizing
institutions. The series consisted of four DPLs (see Table 1), with the second operation in th
series in form of a deferred drawback option (DDO), and a supplemental DDO that was
approved at the onset of the global financial crisis to provide financing in case of need. Policies
which form part of the FMCDPL series include, among others, simplifying and improving tax
collection, controlling sub-national debt, designing incentives for greater sub-national revenue
collection, promoting performance-based budgeting, expanding e-governance, streamlining
administrative obstacles faced by the private sector, and taking advantage of opportunities
offered by international trade. All of these directly promoted the goals stated in the CPS.

1.4 Original Policy Areas Supported by the Program (as approved)

15.     The operation responded to the GoP’s request for Bank support in two main areas:
(i) Efficiency and quality of Fiscal Management, and (ii) Competitiveness. After discussing
the desirability of including both components in the Program matrix, on October 30, 2006 the
Regional Operations Committee (ROC), endorsed the government’s view. The ROC also
requested that ―prior to appraisal, the team revise the program document to clarify how and why
the fiscal and competitiveness components are substantively linked and to strengthen the
rationale for the proposed content of the first operation‖. The new government chose a path of
gradual reforms, and the Bank endorsed that approach too.

16.     The programmatic approach provided the flexibility requested by the GoP to be
able to adapt to changing conditions. By incorporating flexibility into the FMCDPL series
within a defined policy framework, the Bank responded to the needs of Peru, a middle-income
country with strong macro-economic fundamentals and implementing second and third
generation reforms. While the broad outlines of the policy areas and directions were established
from the start of this operation and remained throughout the series of FMCDPLs, specific policy
reforms have evolved along with Peru’s changing political and economic circumstances. The
triggers and prior actions stipulated in the operations were designed jointly with the GoP.


17.     Several areas were not selected for inclusion into this DPL series, but were
addressed in two other DPL series: on social inclusion and environmental issues. In parallel
with FMCDPL series, separate series were also designed. The Results and Accountability Policy
Loan program (REACT) included governance reforms for equity and accountability in the social
sectors and standards and accountability for improved outcomes in education, health, and
nutrition. The Environmental Development Policy program included reforms on environmental
governance and on mainstreaming principles of sustainable development in the mining, urban
transportation, and fisheries sectors. These three series were coordinated between teams, with the
bulk of discussions taking place on cross-cutting issues, such as results based budgeting (RBB).

Efficiency and Quality of Fiscal Management

18.     A central element of the administration’s policy agenda was to maintain and
strengthen macroeconomic stability and fiscal prudence, while improving the quality of
public expenditure. The GoP had set clear targets related to this objective, which are reflected
in the development objectives of the FMCDPL series. Policy areas to achieve these goals
included strengthening fiscal rules as well as public debt management and tax policy; improving
the efficiency of budgetary accounting; implementing efficient, performance-based budgeting;
and increasing transparency and agility in public processes, including procurement and e-
government.

Competitiveness

19.     Strong macroeconomic and financial conditions are necessary but not sufficient for
sustainable growth, without decisive and comprehensive reforms to boost the investment
climate and global competitiveness. Peru’s strong macroeconomic performance had not
translated yet into similar success on the competitiveness front. The World Economic Forum
ranked Peru 86th in its Competitiveness Index, compared to 26th place for Chile.
Competitiveness was affected by low human capital, high logistical and transportation costs,
weak quality standards, cumbersome business regulations, very low research and development
(R&D) investment, and fragmented supply chains. The consequences of weak competitiveness
were reflected in low total factor productivity, high logistic costs, low quality standards, limited
technological development, fragmentation of supply chains, and relatively low export levels.
These were bottlenecks to sustainable economic and regional development. The FMCDPL
addressed several of these bottlenecks, including trade policy, procedure simplification, e-
government, and expansion of private credit, especially to Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises
(MSMEs).

1.6 Other significant changes

21.     The key elements and thrust of the Program remained remarkably constant
throughout the four DPLs of the series. This was largely due to the fact that the FMCDPL
series supported the Program of the government, and accompanied its implementation during
five years of the Garcia administration.

22.    The main change in the FMCDPL was the availability of additional financial
support to face the international financial crisis in 2008. The Bank had just approved the
second DPL of the series, and reacted quickly to the government’s request by preparing and
presenting to the Executive Directors a Supplemental DDO that nearly doubled the amount of
DPL-II. The government was very appreciative of the Bank’s rapid and substantial response and
to having the ability to draw on the DDO instrument in case of need. Eventually, Peru was able
to navigate the crisis better than other countries in the region and the government officials
pointed out that having those funds available, despite not having to call on them, helped calm the
markets and restore confidence.

2. Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes

23.     The Program enjoyed strong ownership of the government that intended to use the
policy matrix as a means for coordinating actions throughout the public sector. During the
discussions in preparation of the DPL-I, the government insisted that both fiscal management
and competitiveness are necessary components of the operation. Based on previous extensive
analytical work and on joint work by solid government and Bank technical teams, the operations
contributed substantively to implementation of reforms in both areas. The FMCDPL series was
complemented by two Technical Assistance Loans, one on capacity building, and the other on
decentralization. In parallel, the Bank approved a Programmatic DPL for the social sectors, and
later another Programmatic DPL for environment.

24.     In response to the government’s interest in flexibility, in addition to the intellectual
and technical support from the Bank for implementing its reform program, part of the
program was approved as a DDO. The economy of Peru was performing well, and financing
requirements declined, however, the government wanted to keep engaged with the World Bank
as it was working to improve its sovereign investor grade. Use of an instrument that would allow
keeping engaged while accumulating only limited debt was an attractive option in these
circumstances. DPL-II was approved as a Deferred Drawing Option (DDO) in August 2008
(Table 1). Later on, DDOs turned out to be an excellent instrument to mitigate the potential
impacts of the global financial crisis as well: the quick approval of the supplemental DDO for
DPL-II played a key role in the Government’s ability to respond to the crisis.

25.     Following the Lehman Brothers’ collapse in September 2008 and subsequent loss of
confidence in international markets, the focus of government changed overnight towards
dealing with the deteriorating financial environment and the growing international crisis.
Given the liquidity crunch in the international credit markets, it became essential to gain access
to foreign exchange to restore calm to domestic markets and to maintain credibility. The
government requested additional resources from the Bank, and the Bank reacted expeditiously by
preparing and approving a Supplemental to DPL-II within three months. Since FMCDPL-II had
been just approved, there was no need for the Supplemental operation to have another policy
matrix, and the government’s measures to address the crisis were reflected in DPL-III that was
under preparation at the time. The Supplemental operation was approved as a DDO as well, and
so far Peru has not needed to draw on it.

2.1 Program Performance

27.     The Program performed well and achieved planned and substantial progress in
most development objectives. Probably most noticeable progress was achieved in the fiscal
management area. By sparking the move towards Results Based Budgeting (RBB), the FMCDPL
was instrumental in shifting the focus of the budget from inputs towards outputs and outcomes,
which enhances efficiency and impact of public spending. The new methodology made headway
in nutrition and health programs, and by 2011 nearly 14 percent of the budget, net of interest and
pension payments, had outcome indicators; that figure is expected to rise to over 30 percent in
2012.

28.     Results Based Budgeting has had an impressive impact on the effectiveness of health
and nutrition programs. In 2008, allocation of resources among regions to improve child
nutrition had no relationship to their real needs. For example, Pasco and Amazonas, plagued by
high levels of chronic malnutrition, received virtually no funds. Implementation of RBB
produced meaningful results in two years. Between 2009 and 2010, the number of children
under 2 years of age covered by the vaccination program increased by 254 percent, while the




                                                9
number of children under 3 years of age covered by the program for children’s growth and
development increased by 432 percent.6 Further, funds were redirected towards regions with the
highest levels of chronic malnutrition, such as Huancavelica, Huanuco, Apurimac, Cajamarca,
Ayacucho and Cusco. Significant improvements were achieved in these poorest regions, which
are described in more detail below in the section on poverty impacts.

29.    Budget reporting and planning improved substantially, as did the financial
management of public accounts, coupled with enhanced transparency of fiscal policy. The
website of the Ministry of Finance provides real time fiscal figures both at the central and
regional levels. The Treasury Single Account (TSA) facilitates financial management and
allows a more efficient administration of public cash balances.

30.     Equity of intergovernmental transfers improved somewhat, but there is still a long
way to go. The government established special funds to increase transfers to poor regions that
have no extractive natural resources. This was partly done to offset the increase in the value of
transfers (so-called canon) to regions and municipalities producing oil and minerals that had
resulted from the recent surge in prices of oil and minerals. In particular, the government
established compensating funds, such as FONCOR and FONIPREL, which favor local
governments that receive little or no canon transfers. The criteria for allocation of FONCOMUN
funds to Municipalities was modified to better address the basic needs of the population. The
mining taxation reform approved in September 2011 is expected to raise additional fiscal
resources which could be used to increase equality in the transfer system.

31.     On the other hand, little progress was achieved in eliminating tax exemptions as
part of the objective to make the tax system more equitable and transparent. Those
exemptions are estimated at about 2 percent of GDP, and little progress has been achieved in
curtailing them. The largest revenue losses due to the exemptions are for agricultural products
and those to the Amazonia region. While some progress has been achieved in eliminating
exemptions to the San Martin region (these were abolished in exchange for an equivalent amount
of funds made available for investment projects), new tax exemptions were granted to projects in
areas over 2,800 meters above the sea level. However, decrees were subsequently passed to
prevent creation of new exemptions.

32.     In the competitiveness area, major progress was made in opening the economy and
expanding international trade through the signing of FTAs with its major trade partners.
The FTAs were a major break-through in opening up the economy. The Doing Business Report
shows that transaction costs for the private sector to enter, operate in, and exit markets were
reduced, and financing to micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) increased
substantially under prudent and sophisticated financial regulation. That process started with
specialized financial institutions that served mostly local areas, but recently commercial banks
have also entered into that market. On the other hand, there was little progress in e-government,
largely due to lack of leadership and cooperation among public sector institutions.

2.2 Major Factors Affecting Implementation

34.     The Program performed well and achieved most of its development objectives.
Three main reasons were at the core of the program’s success: (i) the strong ownership and
continuous commitment of the government to implementation of the Program; (ii) the strong
commitment and responsiveness of the Bank team, especially during the global financial crisis;
(iii) the soundness of the background analysis supporting the operation and lessons from
previous engagement with Peru; (iv) the policy dialogue developed between the Government of
Peru and the Bank.

35.     Exemplary government ownership of the reforms agenda, and the responsiveness
and commitment of the Bank teams were key to the success of the program. Government’s
commitment to reforms was the driving force behind the success in areas of increasing
sustainability and transparency of fiscal policy, making the tax system more stable, and in
improving the efficiency and impact of public spending, as well as in reducing transaction costs
for the private sector, deepening international trade, and financial sector. Working together with
their counterparts, the World Bank teams have also shown deep commitment to the reform
program. This commitment was tested when the supplemental DPL-II for the crisis response was
put together and approved in less than three months after the onset of the crisis (see Table 1).


36.     The Fiscal Responsibility and Transparency Law (FRTL) provided both the
framework for fiscal prudence as well as the flexibility to respond to the crisis. This Law,
enacted in 1999, sets two limits: (i) the fiscal deficit is not to exceed 1 percent of GDP, and (ii)
the growth of current expenditures of the central government is not to exceed 3 percent p.a. in
real terms. Article 5 of the Law states, however, that in cases of national emergency or
international crisis the legislature can, at the request of the executive, grant exceptions to the
fiscal rules for a period of up to three years. As the crisis erupted, the administration proposed
legislation—which the Congress approved— to increase the limit on the fiscal deficit to 2
percent of GDP in both 2009 and 2010. In addition, the limit on the real growth of current
expenditures by the central government was also temporarily increased to 10 percent in 2009 and
8 percent in 2010. Finally, some fiscal rules for sub-national governments were also modified,
the most important being that for non-financial expenditures the deficit limit was raised from 3
percent to 4 percent, excluding investment. The FRTL has been the anchor of fiscal policy in
normal times, and it served Peru well also in times of crisis.

37.     However, the onset of the global financial and economic crisis of 2008-09 forced the
government to put some of the reforms on the back burner, while it concentrated on
maintaining macroeconomic and fiscal stability. As a result, and as discussed above, progress
slowed down in comparison to initial expectations in areas such as eliminating tax exemptions,
increasing equity in intergovernmental transfers. The complex political economy and central-
local fiscal relations have aggravated the situation. Largely due to its solid fiscal situation and
previous structural reforms, Peru was able to navigate the crisis better than other countries in the
region, and returned to pre-crisis growth rates and fiscal stability in 2010-11.




                                                14
38.    The lack of strategic vision for the improvement readiness for e-government slowed
somewhat the progress in the area related to making public processes more transparent,
accessible and agile. The e-government agenda also suffered from lack of cooperation among
public sector institutions, as well as lack of leadership to push that agenda forward. However,
substantial progress was achieved in improving procurement both at the national and sub-
national levels of government.


2.3 Monitoring and Evaluation Design, Implementation, and Utilization

Design

39.    The GoP and the Bank agreed to monitor progress in the FMCDPL program
regularly, including annual reviews of the Country Partnership Strategy. It was agreed that
the Sectoral Loans Coordination Unit (UCPS) at the Ministry of Finance would be in charge of
Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) of the loan and of collecting the appropriate data to follow
the proposed indicators. In addition, the Bank was going to monitor actions and review
implementation progress using various sources, published by government agencies and by
independent institutions.

40.     The matrix of outcomes and indicators reflected the broad scope of the program,
and was adjusted to better suit its purpose. While DPL-I included the outcomes and targets
alongside the program matrix, during the preparation of the DPL-II, significant improvements
were made in the outcomes monitoring (see section 1.3 in this document for more detailed
discussion of these changes). As a result, Annex 4 in the PD of FMCDPL-II included a matrix of
Outcome Indicators derived from the expected outcomes by the end of the Program, which was
replicated in DPLs III and IV.

Implementation and Utilization

41.     In practice, the M&E functions, which included updating the indicator matrix, were
undertaken concurrently with preparation of the successor operation. Since the PDOs
remained unchanged throughout the Program, preparing a follow-up operation involved
assessing progress towards achievement of the PDOs, moving the process one step forward, and
updating the indicator matrix.

42.     The tracking of the indicators and the improvements made in the monitoring matrix
were essential for gauging progress towards the program’s development objectives. This
process also helped design the following operation every time. Having several indicators for
some prior actions allowed for better assessment of the progress and for better understanding of
the complex cross-sectoral reforms, such as the implementation of the results-based budgeting,
one of the highlights of the program.




                                              15
2.4 Expected Next Phase/Follow-up Operation

43.     An operation that is an exact continuation of the FMCDPL program has not been
envisioned, but one dealing with fiscal and governance issues is being discussed and is part
of the new CPS. There is also an ongoing discussion on the avenues for advancement of the
reforms started during the FMCDPLs, especially those on the fiscal management and taxation
side. The issue of whether additional resources would be required, given the strict fiscal
management and discipline that were imposed to rein in the stimulus and maintain low external
debt, remains uncertain, as the international economy is still struggling to bounce back after the
global financial crisis.

3. Assessment of Outcomes

3.1 Relevance of Objectives, Design and Implementation

44.     The objectives of the FMCDPL Program were highly relevant when the operation
was designed and they remain relevant to date. Having achieved a remarkable sound and
solid fiscal and macroeconomic position, coupled with unprecedented rates of economic growth,
the challenge for Peru was, and still is, to translate its potential in terms of natural and human
resources into sustainable development, taking care of its many social needs. An efficient and
effective public sector, which is one of the main objectives of the FMCDPL, can play a central
role in that process. In addition, Peru adopted a strategy of opening to international trade and
signed Free Trade Agreements with its main trade partners. In order to take advantage of those
agreements, it is essential to maintain improvements in the environment for business, the second
main objective of the FMCDPL program.

45.     The programmatic structure of the FMCDPL was appropriate for achieving the
development objectives. Moreover, it allowed for flexibility in choosing the means, while
maintaining the overall objectives of the Program. The design of the Program matrix reflects
both the government strategy of gradual reforms as well as its view that including items in the
policy matrix helps to garner internal support for implementing those reforms. The benefits of
built-in flexibility were evident at the time of the global financial crisis, when the objectives
related to fiscal stability, transparency and strengthening the budget process gained relative
importance.

46.      The flexibility built into the program matrix as well as that shown in the design of
financing instruments were essential for the success of the program. The ability to time the
prior actions leading to the development objectives, such as for example, the introduction of the
fiscal rules to deal with the crisis impacts and the introduction of the stimulus package (DPL-III),
helped maintain focus during the crisis and return to the broader program implementation more
quickly in its aftermath. The use of Deferred Drawback Options as an instrument for delivering
financing during the crisis has been extremely valuable. The use of the DDOs gave the program
the needed flexibility both during the good times to ensure that resources were available in case
of need, and in the face of the global crisis when an urgent increase in the availability of
financial resources was needed.

3.2 Achievement of Program Development Objectives

47.     Most of the development objectives of the program have been achieved successfully.
As assessed during the ICR preparation, overall, out of the 39 outcomes that were identified in
the DPL-I, 31 were achieved (29) or partially achieved (6), and four indicators were not achieved
(10 percent). This section provides a review of actual outcomes for each of the main policy areas
of the FMCDPLs, compared to the original and modified targets of the Program, and discusses to
what extent those outcomes could be attributed to actions supported by the Program.

Fiscal Management

48.    The improvements in the fiscal position and debt sustainability of Peru over the past
five years have been remarkable, as evidenced by the quick economic recovery from the
2008-09 slowdown. Peru is one of the very few countries in LAC that showed positive growth
in 2009. The achievement of a comfortable fiscal position in the early years of the program
enabled the speedy implementation of a stimulus package at the time of the global financial crisis,
which probably prevented a recession. Further, as soon as economic activity picked up, the
government tightened up and restored fiscal prudence, bringing the fiscal accounts back to

3.3 Justification of Overall Outcome Rating

52.     The Outcome of the overall Program is rated Satisfactory. As Tables 5 and 6 show,
most expected outcomes were achieved at the end of the Program. The momentum in the
progress towards the program development objectives remained strong. The most successful
PDOs include: increasing sustainability and transparency of fiscal policy; strengthening budget
reporting and planning; improving efficiency and impact of public spending on the national, sub-
national and sectoral levels; expanding and deepening international trade; reducing transaction
costs for the private sector to enter, operate, and exit markets and expanding and deepening
international trade; and promoting sustainable financial deepening. The Program was less
successful in making the tax system more equitable, transparent, neutral, and stable; on
increasing equity in intergovernmental transfers; and e-government to make public sector
processes more transparent, accessible, and agile. However, there is renewed interest and effort
in these areas at this time, reflected in drafting of proposal to Congress for the elimination of tax
exemptions and a renewed and more strategic management of the e-government office.

53.    FMCDPL-I played a key role in defining the programmatic series and setting the
stage for its implementation.             The Program reflected lessons from the previous
Decentralization and Competitiveness DPL, particularly paying attention to ownership of the
Program by the authorities and ―choosing reform areas carefully and not pressing the
government to make too many changes too fast‖ (PD, paragraphs 71, 73). This allowed the time
needed to implement complex technical processes, account for capacity constraints, and
overcome resistance by groups affected by the reforms. This DPL set the basis for the adoption
of Results Based Budgeting (RBB), which was a key element of the Program, as well as for
reforms to facilitate international trade. Not all of the actions that were envisioned for the future
during the FMCDPL-I preparation were implemented in the end of the series, however. Those
include the establishment of a fully functional fiscal committee, unifying budgetary and


                                                     25
accounting classification systems, and implementation of the electronic signature. The first ISR
for this operation was rated as Moderately Unsatisfactory for the implementation progress.
According to the ISR, this was related to an inadvertent mistake in the already signed legal
documents that took time to correct and delayed the disbursement. In the same ISR, the PDO
rating was Moderately Satisfactory; but the ISR also mentions that team’s assessment of the
progress of the program at the time was positive and lists progress made on several key reforms.
These issues did not repeat later in the program, and did not seem to have caused a slowdown in
the actual implementation of the program, which proceeded smoothly after that.

54.     FMCDPL-II pursued implementation of the reforms started under its predecessor,
as well as provided vital, timely support through the Supplemental DDO after the global
financial crisis hit. In particular, the main DPL piloted four executive evaluations of outcomes
of four public sector programs by external evaluators, thus advancing the move towards RBB. It
contributed to finalizing the new budget classification system in line with international standards,
the modernization of the public procurement system, and simplification of procedures of
international trade. At the same time, it fell short in other areas, including the redesign of fiscal
rules for regional and local governments, and the reduction of tax exemptions. As mentioned
above, both FMCDPL-II and its Supplement DDO had a major impact in reassuring the markets
that Peru had access to financing to mitigate the effects of the international financial crisis that
erupted in the second half of 2008. The financing acted as a signaling device and helped Peru
recover rapidly from the real and fiscal effects of the crisis.

55.      FMCDPL-III provided additional financial support as Peru was recovering from
the impact of the international financial crisis. It recognized the Economic Stimulus Plan,
which the government had launched to mitigate the impact of the crisis (in lieu of the trigger of
fiscal rules for regional government). To advance RBB, it promoted institutional rather that inter-
institutional programs as well as the multi-annual budget. It continued to support modernization
of public procurement (in lieu of a wider e-government agenda) and expanded online access to
tax administration services. It further supported the consolidation of accounts into the TSA, and
making the one-stop-shop for imports and exports (VUCE) fully operational. On the other hand,
while FMCDPL-III was being prepared, dismantling the tax exemptions was put on hold.

56.     The main contribution of FMCDPL-IV, the final operation of the series, was to keep
the reform momentum going. It advanced several specific policy measures on local
governments, including a new methodology for allocating FONCOMUN resources to
Municipalities, to partly offset the unequal distribution of canon, and conditional cash transfers
to Municipalities. It also advanced transparency of fiscal accounts and simplification of customs
procedures. On the other hand, it left out the trigger included in FMCDPL-III on implementing
the interoperability platform to enable data integration across government agencies that feed the
online applications for government services.

57.    Despite bottlenecks in some areas of policy reform, the broad scope of reforms
achieved and sustained under the FMCDPL program resulted in the majority of the
outcome indicators being reached by the end of the program.

3.4 Overarching Themes, Other Outcomes and Impacts

(a) Poverty Impacts, Gender Aspects, and Social Development

58.     The actions supported by the FMCDPL have had a positive impact on poverty
reduction and social development. On the fiscal side, the sound fiscal policy framework
supported by the FMCDPL series has allowed the GoP to increase attention to social spending.
In particular, the government implemented Juntos, a conditional cash transfer program for the
poor, undertook a sizeable emergency fiscal stimulus program to mitigate the effects of the
global economic downturn on Peru, helping it to sustain the social and economic gains of recent
years. The FONIPREL investment fund and changes in the criteria to allocate FONCOMUN are
increasing the fiscal transfers to the poorer parts of the country and to regions with high poverty
levels and limited natural resources. Most of FONIPREL’s investments have been in the
country’s six poorest regions. Actions to improve the performance orientation of budgeting
processes began with pilot projects in the nutrition and health areas, which have already
improved the impact of social spending on the poorest sectors of the population.

59.     Results Based Budgeting achieved an impressive impact on the ground through
improvements in effectiveness of health and nutrition programs. As funds were redirected
towards the poorest regions and towards regions with the highest levels of chronic malnutrition,
such as Huancavelica, Huanuco, Apurimac, Cajamarca, Ayacucho and Cusco, significant
improvements were achieved in improving health-related outcomes. In Huancavelica, for
example, there was a notable increase in the number of personnel providing health services,
particularly technicians and nurses, and in the quantity of services provided. As a result, the
number of children under 12 months with all their vaccinations increased, between 2009 and
2010, in Huancavelica from 44.4 percent to 69.5 percent; in Cusco from 25.1 percent to 67.9
percent; and in Cajamarca from 32.5 percent to 57.6 percent. In 2007-2010, child malnutrition
dropped in Huancavelica from 52.2 percent to 44.7 percent; in Huanuco from 41.5 percent to 31
percent, and in Cajamarca from 37.3 percent to 32 percent.9

60.     Competitiveness policies have contributed to the impressive economic growth of the
country in the past several years, which in turn has boosted employment creation and
poverty reduction. This has been particularly notable in the growth of micro, small and medium
enterprises and the non-traditional export sector, which contribute more to employment creation
than the traditional commodity and extractive export sectors. The long-term strategy for reducing
barriers to international trade through reductions in tariffs and export subsidies and pursuing free
trade agreements with Peru’s major trading partners has led to high rates of export growth and
overall economic activity, which has positive poverty and social impacts. In addition, consumers
have benefited with lower prices for many goods.

61.    Fast economic growth in recent years has been accompanied by poverty reduction.
Between 2006 and 2010, poverty fell from 44.5 percent to 31.3 percent, while extreme poverty
was reduced from 16.1 percent to 9.8 percent. The continued reduction in poverty is remarkable

62.    There were no specific gender aspects to this operation.

(b) Institutional Change/Strengthening

63.     The FMCDPL contributed substantially to modernizing and strengthening
institutions. The move towards Results Based Budgeting is a major institutional change, as it is
shifting the focus of public spending from inputs towards outputs and outcomes. The creation of
the Treasury Single Account facilitates public financial management, improves efficiency in cash
management, and increases transparency of public accounts. A new public procurement law has
been approved, the Supervisory Unit for State Procurement (OSCE) has been reorganized, and
most public procurement is already done online. The one-stop-shop window for international
trade (VUCE) has been established and is facilitating trade activities. Also, reforms are being
implemented to reduce transaction costs for the private sector to enter, operate in, and exit
markets and to expand and deepen international trade. All these institutional changes would help
make high economic growth rates and poverty reduction more sustainable.

(c) Other Unintended Outcomes and Impacts

64.      The potential environmental impact of the FMCDPL is not clear, but the fact that it
was accompanied by a DPL on environment mitigates potential risks. The Program
Document of FMCDPL-I states the following: ―Policies supported by this DPL are expected to
entail significant effects on the environment‖ (paragraph 96), while the PDs of the following
operations state that they are ―not likely to have any significant effects on the environment,
forests, and other natural resources‖ (e.g., FMCDPL-IV, paragraph 110). PDs argue that the
operations would have positive effects on the environment by improving monitoring and
evaluation of public expenditures, including environmental expenditures. Results Based
Budgeting could help in that aspect. On the other hand, expansion of economic and trade
activities would lead to further development of infrastructure, which could negatively affect the
environment. In fact, that would depend to a large extent on the way in which investment
projects are executed, and thus it is hard to anticipate which effect would dominate. As
mentioned above, the FMCDPL was complemented by a programmatic DPL on environment,
which addresses specific issues in this area.

4. Assessment of Risk to Development Outcome

65.     The risk to development outcome is rated Moderate for the entire program.
FMCDPL-I, II, III, and IV all are rated equally as they contributed to the same development
objectives, and are being rated on the basis of the assessment of risk at the time of writing.




                                               28
66.     The reform program has been associated with unprecedented economic growth and
steep declines in poverty levels, which lowers the risk that reforms would be reversed. The
program accompanied the reform plan of the previous government, but the new government that
took office in 2011, is staying the course and continues implementing the program. In fact,
increased transparency of fiscal accounts, improved public procurement and simpler procedures
for imports and exports, for example, can be seen as public goods, and thus they are likely to be
sustainable over time. Reforms of the transfer system to sub-national governments are also
ongoing, although they are slowed by the existing political constraints. The opening of the
economy coupled with the FTAs with major trade partners are promoting growth and poverty
reduction, but there are sectors of the population that are somewhat skeptical of those policies.
However, there have been expressions of social dissatisfaction in the past few years that are
related to the management of the natural resources, and while these are not directly linked to the
development objectives of the FMCDPLs, they could be indirectly related to a perception of
unfairness in the distribution system as well. Strong fiscal management and growth-oriented
development strategy are ensuring the macroeconomic stability and reinforcing the achievement
of the poverty reduction outcomes.

5. Assessment of Bank and Borrower Performance

67.    Bank performance is rated Satisfactory for the entire program. This rating is based
on a combination of ratings for ensuring quality of entry and supervision.

5.1 Bank Performance
(a) Bank Performance in Ensuring Quality at Entry
Ratings: Satisfactory

68.     The FMCDPL-I team designed the program in close collaboration with the new
government that took office in 2006. The Bank organized a large seminar in Lima on Results
Based Budgeting, bringing experts from other countries, to share both the theoretical as well as
the practical aspects of this methodology with the new authorities. The second DPL of the series
introduced several changes in the structure of the Program, to facilitate its monitoring and
evaluation. In particular, it revised two PDOs to focus their scope, and introduced many changes
in the Outcome Indicators (Section 1.3), to better define and sharpen them. It also added an
annex to the PD (Annex 4), which presented all the OIs in a comprehensive table, which was
useful for monitoring progress of the program. The third and fourth DPLs consolidated progress
achieved under the program, and acknowledged areas where objectives were not achieved. The
PD for the FMCDPL-IV included a thorough discussion of the impact of the program on poverty,
as recommended by the ROC and required by BP 8.60.

(b) Quality of Supervision
Ratings: Satisfactory

69.     Supervision of FMCDPLs I, II and III were undertaken together with preparation
of the following operation, since PDOs remained fairly constant throughout the Program.
That is shown by the discussion of progress made under the program and the updated Outcome
Indicators table (Annex 4) in the Program Documents of FMCDPL-II, II, and IV. The initial



                                               29
matrix of outcomes was detailed and expanded during the preparation of DPL-II and progress on
all indicators was tracked yearly after that. Two ISRs were prepared for DPL-I, seven ISRs for
DPL-II, three ISRs for DPL-III, and two for DPL-IV; each of these tracked progress on four or
five key indicators on approximately 6-monthly basis. Issues identified in the very first ISR were
immediately addressed and did not reappear later on in the series (see paragraph 53 of this report
for more detail).

(c) Justification of Rating for Overall Bank Performance

70.     The Bank managed transitions and team coordination reasonably well. Task Team
Leaders of the FMCDPL changed twice during the life of the project, but there was overlap
between the TTLs, and officials at the Ministry of Economy and Finance pointed out that the
transition was very smooth. During the preparation of the first DPL, the internal coordination
among the sectors at the Bank was set up and it worked well thereafter (see paragraph 17 of this
report for more detail).

71.     The FMCDPL helped the government implement its own program, thereby
increasing sustainability of the reforms. As shown in Tables 5 and 6, most targets of the
program were achieved by the closing date even though adjustments to the targets were made
along the way, when the targets were achieved earlier than expected. Adjustments to the
indicators were also made to make them more clear and concrete (see paragraph 14 of this report
for more detail).

72.     The Bank performance during the crisis and the preparation of the Supplemental
DDO after the FMCDPL-II deserves a special mention. When the government requested
additional financial support to face the international crisis in September 2008, the team was
ready to go and prepared a Supplement DDO in record time. This additional support was made
available in less than three months and helped the government to calm the markets, thus
mitigating the impact of the crisis.

5.2 Borrower Performance

(a) Government Performance
Ratings: Satisfactory

73.    There has been strong commitment to reform during the life of the Program, which
was the driving force behind the success of the program. While there were changes at the
helm of the Ministry of Finance, which coordinated implementation of the Program, and some
changes at the technical level of the Ministry, there was broad continuity. The government
performance is evaluated jointly for all the DPLs of the series.

74.     The government stayed the course and pursued implementation of the reforms
throughout the life of the program. Major institutional reforms have taken place in budget
reporting and planning, increasing sustainability and transparency of fiscal policy, improving
financial management in the public sector, as well as in facilitating private sector activities and
creating a more open and competitive economy. Solid fiscal and macro policies were



                                                30
consolidated in those years, and allowed the government to respond swiftly and effectively to the
2008-09 crisis and to recover to the pre-crisis fiscal position and level of tax revenues by 2011.


75.     To cope with the global crisis, however, less attention was devoted to other parts of
the program, such as elimination of tax exemptions. The crisis changed international and
domestic conditions and forced the government to concentrate on some issues and postpone
others. Focusing on macroeconomic sustainability while remaining fiscally sustainable paid off
by a relatively mild slow-down during the crisis and a swift recovery afterwards. However, this
meant that some reforms that already faced complex political economy constraints had to be
postponed. While some progress was achieved in stopping new exemptions from being
introduced, little progress has been made on the elimination of the existing ones. In the area of
increasing equity of intergovernmental transfers, the sharp rise in commodity prices contributed
to increased inequality, even though the program made solid progress in establishing funds to
increase transfers to the poorest regions, which partly offset the unequal distribution of the canon
system. There were also areas where slow progress was due to reasons unrelated to the global
financial crisis: the e-government agenda progressed slower than initially anticipated as it
suffered from lack of cooperation among public sector institutions, as well as lack of a champion
to push that agenda forward. 10 At the time of ICR preparation, however, there has been a
renewed interest and steps are being taken to eliminate tax exemptions and to restart reforms in
the area of e-government.

6. Lessons Learned

76.    The experience of the FMCDPL points to several lessons which could be useful for
future operations:

     i.   Flexibility and strong client commitment from the Bank team allowed to respond
          quickly when conditions changed. When the global financial crisis erupted in
          September 2008 and the government asked the Bank for urgent financial support, the
          Bank was able to come through in record time. The capability to respond quickly to
          changing country and/or international conditions is essential for maintaining the
          relevance of the Bank and for its relationship with its clients, particularly with the
          middle-income countries.




10
     The delays are described in the PDs for DPL-III (page 32-33) and DPL-IV (page 30-31).



                                                31
 ii.   The DDO was the right instrument in the changing circumstances. Initially, the DDO
       was chosen as an instrument that would allow Peru to keep the Bank engaged without a
       large impact on the debt levels, as the country was trying to improve its investment grade.
       But it turned out to be the right instrument during the crisis as well: the use of the
       supplemental DDO was a crucial factor in Peru being able to weather the global financial
       crisis well, because the availability of financing from the World Bank helped in calming
       the markets immediately after the onset of the global financial crisis. Eventually, this also
       facilitated the recovery of the economic activity.


iii.   Taking calculated risks is important for pushing a reform agenda forward. The
       design of the policy matrix balanced ambition and breadth with a focus on areas with
       strong government commitment. Striking such a balance at the beginning of the series is
       not an easy task. On the one hand, setting out to do only the reforms that are most likely
       to be achieved would make for a more satisfactory performance at the end of the
       program. On the other hand, it is most beneficial if the program can be used to push
       forward difficult reforms. While a small fraction of the outcomes in the FMCDPL
       program were not achieved, resulting in only Satisfactory performance, the work that
       took place in the less successful areas had moved the agenda forward and prepared the
       ground for future reforms, which are being continued and implemented at this time.

           Programmatic Fiscal Management and Economic Growth Loan
                                 Closing Report

                                  The World Bank
   I.      Introduction

The Government of Peru signed with the World Bank a series of four operations as part
of a Programmatic Fiscal Management and Economic Growth Loan:

   -    First, in the amount of US$200 million, was approved in 2007 by DS No. 035-
        2007-EF (published on 03/28/07).
   -    Second, reached US$700 million, of which US$70 million were freely available
        (approved by DS 138-2008-EF, published on 11/30/08) and US$630 million for a
        contingent line (approved by DS No. 138-2008-EF and DS No. 180-2008-EF).
   -    Third, in the amount of US$150 million, was negotiated, approved (by DS 277-
        2009-EF, published on 12/04/09) and disbursed the end of 2009.
   -    Fourth, and final transaction, was negotiated, approved (by DS 188-2010-EF,
        published on 09/09/10), and agreed on 2010 in the amount of US$100 million.

The program aimed to support the consolidation of fiscal management and to promote
sustained economic growth based on increased public and private investment, improve
the country's infrastructure, and promote the inclusion of the most vulnerable social
sectors. In this sense, the loan has improved the efficiency and quality of fiscal
management and public expenditure, and has developed policies aimed at
strengthening competitiveness.

The sectors of the government involved in the reform process implemented by the
program were:

Ministry of Foreign Trade and Tourism (MINCETUR)
Ministry of Production (PRODUCE)
General Directorate of Public Budget (DGPP)
General Directorate of Fiscal Decentralization and Social Affairs (DGDFAS)
General Directorate of Investment Policy (PGD)
General Directorate of Public Revenue Policy (DGPIP)
General Directorate for International Economics, Competitiveness, and Productivity
(DGAEICP)
General Directorate of Debt and Public Treasury (DNETP)
Banking and Insurance Superintendence (SBS)
National Customs and Tax Administration (SUNAT)
National Supervisory Commission of Companies and Securities (CONASEV)
Development Finance Corporation (COFIDE)
Supervisory Agency for Government Procurement (OSCE)
National Office of Electronic Government and Information Technology (ONGEI)



                                         51
   II.    Loan Objectives and Results

The Program is a series of fiscal reforms in line with government objectives, which
were embodied in various commitments the Government assumed to improve the
quality of public spending. Thus, the main results that helped to meet the program
objectives are as follows:

   Sustainability and Transparency in Fiscal Policy

The Economic Stimulus Plan (ESP) was promoted to address the impact of the
international financial crisis on economic growth, employment, and social inclusion. The
Framework Law Public Private Partnerships (PPPs) was approved for the generation of
productive employment and regulations were issued for speeding up the process of
promoting private investment.

In order to create conditions for sustained growth of the local economy, the Municipal
Modernization Program (PMM) for 2010-2013 was created.by Law No. 29465, Law on
Public Sector Budget for fiscal year 2010, The PMM is intended to encourage
municipalities to increase the municipal tax collection through improved financial
management, and to create favorable conditions for the business climate through
improved provision of public services, basic infrastructure, and simplification of
procedures

Similarly, in order to encourage local governments to improve standards for the
collection of municipal taxes, the execution of investment spending, and the reduction of
chronic child malnutrition nationally, the Government creates (through Law No. 29332)
the Incentive Plan to Municipal Management Improvement (PI), which is implemented
through     the distribution of resources from the Municipal Compensation Fund
(FONCOMUN) for the fiscal year 2010.

Subsequently, Law No. 29812, Law on Public Sector Budget for fiscal year 2012,
provides that the Incentive Plan to Municipal Management Improvement hereinafter be
called “Municipal Management Improvement and Modernization Incentive Plan." This
plan is an instrument of Results Based Budgeting (RBB), whose main objective is to
promote reforms that achieve growth and sustainable development of the local
economy and to improve its management, under the decentralization process and
competitiveness improvement.

   A more stable and neutral Tax System

The Approval of the Regulation of the Law for the Promotion of Competitiveness and
Development Formalization of Micro and Small Enterprises (MSEs) and access to
decent employment, demonstrates the continued progress in tax simplification
measures with emphasis on the MSE, encouraging growth in the domestic and foreign
markets.



                                           52
In this context, measures were adopted to facilitate the filing and payment of taxes by
implementing the simplified declaration for MSEs by Superintendence Resolution No.
120-2009/SUNAT. The simplified filing seeks to facilitate tax compliance by simplifying
the filing of returns and payment of taxes and removes physical preprinted forms.

In addition, the National Superintendence of Tax Administration (SUNAT) created the
Consultation and Disclosure Payment via the Internet with the objective of facilitating to
the taxpayer the search of previous declarations and payments. The new
disaggregation in the search criteria (by form, period, or filing date range) allows more
accurate results, consuming fewer system resources.

 Also, the improvement in the query option of returns and payments in Virtual SUNAT,
provides taxpayers a review of their statements and/or payments, entering a single
module when making the request, and allowing to save the information consulted.

Finally, the SUNAT created the new payment website generating an additional payment
option to taxpayers using the simplified virtual forms that are implemented in Virtual
SUNAT, which allows the increase in the number of Internet payment transactions and
thus a reduction of banking charges.

   Planning and Budgetary and Financial Transparency

Regarding the process of implementing the Public Treasury Single Account (CUT),
progress was done with the liquidation and closing of bank accounts that the Executive
Units of the National Government, Regional Government and budget specifications of
Local Government maintained dormant in the Bank of the Nation (Banco de la Nacion).

Furthermore, in order to strengthen fiscal management, new budget classifiers were
incorporated to the Integrated Public Financial Management System (SIAF-SP) and the
2009 Budget Act specified the use thereof. The new classifiers have been upgraded to
international standards that can provide a sufficiently complete and timely information
base to generate reports required by different users, both internal and external.
Specifically, the new budget classifiers of revenues and expenses are aligned with the
Manual on Public Finance Statistics 2001 of the International Monetary Fund (MEFP
2001) to ensure its usefulness in the management of public finances and provide
greater transparency to the management of public resources.

   Equity of intergovernmental transfers

Evaluations of the allocation of the Fund for the Promotion of Regional and Local Public
Investment (FONIPREL) in 2008, identified that the funds were concentrated in high-

 Significant progress was made in the use of Results Based Budgeting (RBB) as a tool

 Finally, the Law on Public Sector Budget for Fiscal Year 2010, includes the design of

   Transparency, accessibility, and agility of public sector processes

The actions considered in this component were the creation of the Supervisory Board of

procurement system by optimizing and integrating the technical processes of public
acquisitions, imposing financial penalties on entities that violate the rules of
procurement, and that contracts performed by public sector entities are made on time
and under the best conditions of price and quality .

   Deepening the financial sector

Changes were made to the Bank’s law demanding higher risk controls adjusting them to

Indebtedness of Small Debtors was approved in order to preserve quality in the process

In order to encourage the development of the mortgage market for the benefit of the

Finally, the Development Finance Cooperation (COFIDE) has created the Peru

   III.   Lessons Learned

The four Programmatic Fiscal Management and Economic Growth Loans (I, II, III and

The Ministry has the greatest incentive for successfully complying with the program, so

   a.     On the Bank performance

The Bank has provided on-going technical assistance and support to the teams of the

However, there were exceptional cases in which commitments had to be reviewed

   b.     On the performance of the Beneficiary

The good performance achieved by government agencies participating in the program is

Also, thanks to the role of the MEF as a partner in the definition, monitoring, and

and proposals of the Bank.

II.   Objetivos y Resultados del Préstamo

El Programa constituye una serie de reformas fiscales planteadas en concordancia con
los objetivos del Gobierno, las mismas que fueron plasmadas en diversos compromisos
que éste asumió para la mejora de la calidad del gasto público. De esta manera, los
principales resultados obtenidos que permitieron cumplir con los objetivos del Programa
se muestran a continuación:

Sostenibilidad y Transparencia de la Política Fiscal

Se impulsó el Plan de Estímulo Económico (PEE) para hacer frente al impacto de la
crisis financiera internacional sobre el crecimiento económico, empleo e inclusión social.
Se aprobó la Ley Marco de Asociaciones Público Privadas (APP) para la generación
del empleo productivo y se dictaron normas para la agilización de los procesos de
promoción de la inversión privada.

Con el objeto de generar condiciones que permitan un crecimiento sostenido de la
economía local, mediante la Ley Nº 29465, Ley de Presupuesto del Sector Público para
el año fiscal 2010, se creó el Programa de Modernización Municipal (PMM) para el
período 2010-2013. El PMM tiene como finalidad incentivar a las municipalidades a
incrementar la recaudación de los impuestos municipales a través de la mejora de la
gestión financiera, y generar las condiciones favorables al clima de negocios a través
de la mejora en la provisión de servicios públicos, infraestructura básica y simplificación
de trámites

En el mismo sentido, con el objetivo de incentivar a los Gobiernos Locales a mejorar los
niveles de recaudación de los tributos municipales, la ejecución del gasto en inversión y
la reducción de la desnutrición crónica infantil a nivel nacional, a través de la Ley Nº
29332, el Gobierno crea el Plan de Incentivos a la Mejora de la Gestión Municipal (PI),
cuya aplicación se genera a partir de la distribución de los recursos del Fondo de
Compensación Municipal (FONCOMUN) correspondiente al año fiscal 2010.

Posteriormente, mediante Ley N° 29812, Ley de Presupuesto del Sector Público para el
año fiscal 2012, se dispone que el Plan de Incentivos a la Mejora de la Gestión
Municipal se denomine en adelante “Plan de Incentivos a la Mejora de la Gestión y
Modernización Municipal?. Dicho plan es un instrumento de Presupuesto por
Resultados (PpR) cuyo objetivo principal es impulsar reformas que permiten lograr el
crecimiento y el desarrollo sostenible de la economía local y la mejora de su gestión, en
el marco del proceso de descentralización y mejora de la competitividad.

Sistema Tributario más estable y neutro

La aprobación del Reglamento de la Ley de Promoción de la Competitividad,
Formalización y Desarrollo de la Micro y Pequeña Empresa (MYPE) y del Acceso al



                                            59
Empleo Decente, demuestra que se continúa avanzando con las medidas de
simplificación tributaria con énfasis en las MYPE, incentivando su crecimiento en el
mercado interno y externo.

En este contexto, se dictaron medidas para facilitar la declaración y pago de impuestos
implementando la declaración simplificada para MYPEs mediante Resolución de
Superintendencia Nº 120-2009/SUNAT. La declaración simplificada busca facilitar el
cumplimiento tributario simplificando la presentación de las declaraciones y pago de
impuestos y elimina los formularios físicos preimpresos.

Asimismo, la Superintendencia Nacional de Administración Tributaria (SUNAT) creó la
Consulta y Declaración de pago a través de Internet con el objetivo de facilitar al
contribuyente la búsqueda de las declaraciones y pagos que haya realizado. La nueva
diferenciación en los criterios de búsqueda (por formulario, periodo o por rango de
fecha de presentación) permite obtener resultados más precisos, consumiendo menos
recursos del sistema.

También, la mejora de la opción de consulta de declaraciones y pagos en SUNAT
Virtual, facilita a los contribuyentes la consulta de sus declaraciones y/o pagos
efectuados, ingresando a un solo módulo donde realizar la consulta y con la facilidad
de guardar la información consultada.

Asimismo, la SUNAT creó el Nuevo portal de pagos generando una opción de pago
adicional a los contribuyentes que utilizan los formularios virtuales simplificados que se
implementan en SUNAT Virtual, lo que permite incrementar la cantidad de
transacciones de pago por Internet y en consecuencia, disminuir las transacciones
bancarias de pago.

Planificación y Transparencia Presupuestaria y Financiera

En cuanto al proceso de implementación de la Cuenta Única del Tesoro Público (CUT),
se avanzó con el proceso de liquidación y cierre de las cuentas bancarias que las
Unidades Ejecutoras del Gobierno Nacional, Gobierno Regional y los pliegos
presupuestarios de los Gobiernos Locales mantenían sin movimiento en el Banco de la
Nación.

Asimismo, con el objetivo de fortalecer el manejo fiscal se incorporaron los nuevos
clasificadores presupuestarios al Sistema Integrado de Administración Financiera del
Sector Público (SIAF-SP) y la Ley de Presupuesto 2009 especificó el uso de los
mismos. Los nuevos clasificadores han sido modernizados bajo estándares
internacionales que permiten obtener una base de información lo suficientemente
completa y oportuna para generar los reportes requeridos por diferentes usuarios, tanto
internos como externos. Específicamente, los nuevos clasificadores presupuestarios de
ingresos y gastos están armonizados con el Manual de Estadísticas de Finanzas
Públicas 2001 del Fondo Monetario Internacional (MEFP 2001) para garantizar su




                                           60
mayor utilidad en la gestión de las finanzas públicas y dotar de mayor transparencia a
la gestión de los recursos públicos.

Equidad en las transferencias intergubernamentales

Las evaluaciones de las convocatorias 2008 del Fondo de Promoción a la Inversión
Pública Regional y Local (FONIPREL) identificaron que los fondos se concentraron en
los Gobiernos Distritales de alta necesidad y menores recursos, y que las áreas que
más se beneficiaron por el Fondo son las áreas de infraestructura agrícola, educación
básica, infraestructura vial y saneamiento.

De esta manera se puede verificar que se han logrado los objetivos del fondo al
aumentar la equidad en las transferencias intergubernamentales y reducir las brechas
en la provisión de los servicios e infraestructura básica, con mayor impacto posible en
la reducción de la pobreza y la pobreza extrema en el país.

Con el objetivo de continuar con la política de equidad en las transferencias
intergubernamentales, mediante Decreto Supremo N° 060-2010 se aprobaron los
criterios, procedimientos y metodología para la distribución del FONCOMUN, que
comprendió el cálculo de la asignación geográfica por provincia y sobre esa base una
distribución entre todas las municipalidades distritales, incluyendo la municipalidad
provincial. Esta metodología consiste principalmente en el reemplazo de variables
como la tasa de mortalidad infantil por otras variables que reflejan mejor las
necesidades básicas insatisfechas en la población objetivo.

Eficiencia e impacto del Gasto Público

Se lograron avances importantes en la utilización del Presupuesto por Resultados
(PpR) como herramienta de gestión pública para mejorar la eficiencia e impacto del
gasto, generando indicadores de desempeño y evaluaciones de programas
estratégicos. De esta manera se completaron en el 2009 las evaluaciones de los
siguientes programas: i) Conservación o Mantenimiento de Carreteras; (ii) Juntos –
programa de transferencias condicionadas; (iii) Programa Integral de Nutrición..iv) Agua
para Todos, v) Construyendo Perú, vi) Electrificación Rural, vii) INDECI, viii) Programa
de Mantenimiento de Locales Escolares, ix) Materiales Educativos, x) Programa
Nacional de Movilización por la Alfabetización, xi) RENIEC, xii) Seguro Integral de
Salud, y xiii) Tolerancia Cero.

Finalmente, se incluyó en la Ley de Presupuesto del Sector Público para el año Fiscal
2010 el diseño de seis nuevos programas presupuestales relacionados con los
siguientes fines: i) Enfermedades no transmisibles, tuberculosis, VIH y enfermedades
metaxénicas y zoonosis, ii) Logros de aprendizaje en educación primaria y educación
básica alternativa, iii) Trabajo infantil, iv) Violencia familiar y sexual y seguridad
alimentaria, v) Sostenibilidad del medio ambiente, y vi) Ampliación de la base tributaria.
Los programas presupuestales son unidades de programación de las acciones del




                                           61
Estado que se realizan en cumplimiento de las funciones encomendadas a favor de la
sociedad.

Transparencia, accesibilidad, y agilidad de los procesos del sector público

Los acciones consideradas en este componente fueron la creación del Organismo
Supervisor de Contrataciones del Estado (OSCE), la publicación de su Reglamento de
Organización y Funciones y la aprobación de la Ley de Contrataciones del Estado,
cuyo objetivo es la mejora del sistema de contratación pública, optimizando e

que las contrataciones que realicen las Entidades del Sector Público se efectúen en
forma oportuna y bajo las mejores condiciones de precio y calidad.

Expandir y Profundizar el Comercio Exterior

El Gobierno, con el objetivo de mantener una política de incentivo al desarrollo del
comercio exterior en todas sus modalidades, así como ampliar el universo de
exportadores el cual debe incluir a las micro y pequeñas empresas, ha implementado
proyectos que facilitan la actividad exportadora, los cuales vienen generando un
incremento en las exportaciones de las micro y pequeñas empresas a través del
servicio postal. Por lo tanto, se hizo necesario aumentar el monto del valor FOB de las
mercancías que se exportan mediante este sistema; así como dictar otras normas
complementarias respecto a esta modalidad de exportación.

Asimismo, de acuerdo a lo dispuesto en la Ley General de Aduanas aprobada por el
Decreto Legislativo Nº 1053 y su Reglamento, el proceso de despacho anticipado en el
régimen de importación para el consumo, rige a partir del 29 de marzo de 2010 en la
Intendencia de Aduana Marítima del Callao y del 12 de abril de 2010 en la Intendencia
de Aduana Aérea del Callao. En este sentido, se aprobó el plan y el cronograma para la
implementación en forma progresiva, del nuevo proceso de despacho aduanero en las
intendencias de aduana antes mencionadas y en las intendencias de aduana de
Arequipa, Chiclayo, Cusco, Iquitos, Postal del Callao, Pucallpa, Puerto Maldonado,
Puno, Tacna, Tarapoto, Tumbes y la Agencia Aduanera La Tina.

Se firmó un tratado de libre comercio con China de fecha Abril 28 de 2009 y se
aprobaron leyes y reglamentos para facilitar el comercio en general y en línea con el
Acuerdo de Promoción Comercial con los EEUU. Se aprobó el plan y el cronograma
para la implementación en forma progresiva, del nuevo proceso de despacho aduanero
en diversas intendencias de aduanas. Además, con la implementación de la Ventanilla
Única de Comercio Exterior (VUCE) se logró ordenar, simplificar e integrar los procesos
de comercio exterior, lo que permite reducir los costos operacionales de las empresas,
incrementando nuestra competitividad frente a otros países, así como una mayor
calidad, transparencia y rapidez en los procesos relacionados con los trámites de
comercio exterior.

Profundización del sector financiero

Se realizaron cambios a la ley de Bancos exigiendo mayores controles de riesgo
ajustándolos a la normativa de Basilea II, y se estableció el régimen general de
provisiones pro-cíclicas incluyendo la regla de activación o desactivación, logrando
fortalecer las reglas para la administración del riesgo crediticio, de manera que se
promueva un crecimiento sostenible del crédito y se reduzca el riesgo de
sobreendeudamiento. Asimismo, se aprobó el Reglamento para la Administración del
Riesgo de Sobre Endeudamiento de Deudores Minoristas con la finalidad de preservar
la calidad en el proceso del otorgamiento de los créditos y mantener la solidez del
sistema financiero.

Con el propósito de fomentar el desarrollo del mercado hipotecario en beneficio de los
sectores que aún no cuentan con vivienda propia se aprobó la Ley N°29637, Ley que
regula el tratamiento aplicable para la emisión de bonos hipotecarios cubiertos (BHC)
como valores mobiliarios que confieren a su titular derechos crediticios respaldados con
los Activos de Respaldo. Estos activos pueden ser créditos hipotecarios para vivienda,
dinero en efectivo, instrumentos emitidos por el Banco Central de Reserva del Perú
(BCRP), valores emitidos por el Estado Peruano, BHC y otros que establezca la
Superintendencia de Banca, Seguros y AFP (SBS). La calidad de los activos de
respaldo permite que el BHC sea atractivo para los inversionistas, a la vez que hace
posible una administración flexible de estos activos por parte de los emisores, frente al
vencimiento de las cuotas de los créditos.

Finalmente, la Cooperación Financiera de Desarrollo (COFIDE) ha creado el Programa
Perú Factoring que tiene como objetivo facilitar el financiamiento de las actividades de
las micro, pequeña y mediana empresas (MYPE) proveedoras de bienes y servicios,
mediante el adelanto de sus facturas por parte de las instituciones del sistema
financiero nacional, supervisadas por la SBS, que han suscrito con COFIDE un
convenio de incorporación. En este sentido, COFIDE ha firmado convenios de
incorporación al Programa con el Seguro Social de Salud - ESSALUD, Gloria S.A. y
Banco de la Microempresa S.A.- MIBANCO.

III. Lecciones Aprendidas

Los cuatro Préstamos Programáticos de Gestión Fiscal y Crecimiento Económico (I, II,
III y IV) han tenido un desempeño satisfactorio, dado que desde el inicio del programa
se mantuvieron las líneas de reforma establecidas, de manera que los compromisos
fueron consistentes entre sí y fueron temas que forman parte de la agenda pendiente
de los sectores.

En este sentido, podemos resaltar que el Gobierno Peruano desde el inicio del
Programa se comprometió a través de los sectores en llevar a cabo las reformas
asociadas al mismo, lo que se vio reflejado en el cumplimiento de las acciones de la
matriz de políticas durante las cuatro operaciones que comprendió el Programa.

De otro lado, la utilidad del préstamo programático para el cumplimiento de los
compromisos, que es uno de los principales aspectos que destacan en la evaluación
del Programa, se ha visto reforzada por la presencia del Ministerio de Economía y
Finanzas (MEF), a través de la Unidad de Coordinación de Préstamos Sectoriales
(UCPS) como interlocutor ante el Banco y los sectores del Gobierno.

El Ministerio tiene los mayores incentivos para que el programa se cumpla con éxito,
por lo que la continuidad institucional del MEF ha permitido garantizar la consistencia
entre los préstamos y compromisos a lo largo del período 2007-2010. Asimismo, la
participación activa de los sectores responsables en los procesos de negociación,
quienes son los que proponen y validan al interior de sus respectivas instituciones los
compromisos que se acuerdan en la matriz de políticas, también ha reforzado los
resultados logrados con el Programa.

a. Sobre el desempeño del Banco

El Banco ha tenido una labor permanente de asistencia técnica y apoyo al equipo de
los sectores de Gobierno involucrados en el Programa, con el fin de identificar temas
críticos y buscar estrategias que promuevan su solución, por lo que consideramos que
la labor el Banco ha sido satisfactoria. Asimismo, el acompañamiento que brindó al
programa fue cercano y constructivo, brindando su experiencia y recomendando
estrategias implementadas de manera exitosa en otros países para que sean aplicadas
por los sectores.

En general, el Banco abordó los temas de la matriz de manera tal que permitió que la
relación con el equipo técnico de los sectores del Gobierno fuera fluida, así se pudo
detectar de manera oportuna los obstáculos o restricciones que pudieran afectar los
compromisos, y se pudo plantear salidas adecuadas para que los compromisos sean
cumplidos a cabalidad.

Sin embargo, hubo casos excepcionales en los que se tuvo que revisar los
compromisos debido a que las metas planteadas inicialmente no eran factibles de ser


                                          64
alcanzadas, en estos casos el equipo del Banco mostró predisposición para analizar la
situación y encontrar soluciones de modo adecuado y oportuno.

b. Sobre el desempeño del Beneficiario

Se resalta el buen desempeño logrado por las agencias de Gobierno que participaron
en el Programa, de esta manera se pudo contar con un Programa consistente a lo largo
del tiempo y se pudo cumplir de manera oportuna con los compromisos de política
establecidos, logrando implementar los temas relevantes de la agenda fiscal.

Asimismo, gracias al rol del MEF como interlocutor en la definición, seguimiento y
cumplimiento de los compromisos de política, se pudo lograr una fluida articulación y
comunicación con las instituciones responsables, de modo que los sectores
involucrados mantengan los compromisos de matriz a lo largo del programa. De esta
manera, se cumplió con el objetivo de lograr la coincidencia de la agenda de políticas
del Gobierno con las iniciativas y propuestas del Banco.
