                                     I. INTRODUCTION

1. This program document presents a proposed credit to the Republic o f Uganda for
SDR 83.3 million or US$125 million equivalent to finance the Sixth Poverty Reduction
Support Operation (PRSC6).

2. The proposed PRSC6 is the second annual budget support operation in a
programmatic development policy lending (DPL) series that supports Uganda's
thirdPovertyEradicationAction Plan(PEAP'), launchedinMay 2005 andcovering
the period 2005-2008. The third PEAP took stock o f more than a decade o f
achievements in economic reforms and poverty reduction, presenting a strategy with
renewed focus on accelerating growth. It also established a comprehensive results and
policy framework, and envisaged an Annual PEAP Implementation Review (APIR)
which was undertaken for the first time in February 2007. The separate PRSC results
framework was replaced by the PEAP framework starting with PRSCS. The PRSC
operations will continue to be the core o f the Bank's support for Uganda as spelled out in
the Uganda Joint Assistance Strategy (UJAS).2

3. The proposedPRSC6 supports four of the five pillars of the third PEAP: health,
education and water sector programs (since PRSCl), public sector management and anti-
corruption efforts, and the rural growth agenda (since PRSC2), programs related to
gender and infant and maternal mortality, and the strengthened results orientation
(PRSC4), and accelerated growth through stronger rural development and a better
investment climate (new in PRSC6). The Program Document describes reforms to be
supported by PRSC6 and planned areas o f focus for PRSC7, which will be the last inthe
current series o fPRSCs.

4. The proposed reform program, including prior actions, has been developed
jointly with the Government of Uganda (GoU) in close cooperation with other
Development Partners, including the African Development Bank (AfDB), Belgium,
Denmark, the European Union, Germany, the IMF, Ireland, the Netherlands, Norway,
Sweden, and the United Kingdom. Budget support from AfDB and Germany is directly

6. The success of the PEAP depends on enabling the poor to share the returns to
current investments in human and physical capital stock. Providing them with
incentives to participate and influence outcomes, to set aside savings to generate even
quicker and more tangible returns, and to empower them to demand accountability on

will contribute to this strategy by bringing together the key findings from ongoing and
planned studies - such as the Country Economic Memorandum and the Public
Expenditure Review - and by supporting the creation o f a forum for identification and

MACROECONOMIC         ENVIRONMENTAND DEBTSUSTAINABILITY
11.The macroeconomic environment remains largely stable, but the exogenous
shocks mentioned above could pose a threat. Domestic inflation has been slightly
above the target o f 5 percent for the third consecutive year due to inflationary pressures
from adverse weather conditions, power shortages and energy price shocks. The shilling
depreciated by 4 percent against the US dollar due to higher demand for foreign exchange
to finance the import bill. The Nominal Effective Exchange Rate (NEER) and Real
Effective Exchange Rate (REER) exhibited a depreciating trend, with the REER rate
depreciating by 6.2 percent during the first half o f 2005/06. Uganda's terms o f trade
improved thanks to a rebound in export prices during 2005/06, but the external position
deteriorated due to higher imports and a reduction inofficial grants. Grant disbursements
o f 5.2 percent o f GDP were almost half o f what was realized in 2004/05, although net
donor financing remained above 8 percent. International reserves slightly declined to 5.5
months o f import cover in2005/06.

12. The financial sector is sound and stable, but needs to generate higher domestic
savings and lendingand to addressthe gaps in longtermfinance and ruralfinancial
services. Domestic savings stood at 12.1 percent o f GDP in 2005/06 and credit to the
private sector at 8.8 percent o f GDP, which are well below the Sub-Saharan Africa
average. Capital market financing is limited and the pension system which i s a promising
source for long-term financing requires an overhaul.

13. Government has maintained a prudent fiscal management regime by gradually
improving revenue performance and restraining expansion in government
expenditures. Ongoing reforms at the Uganda Revenue Authority (URA)have improved
domestic revenuecollections to 13.3 percent o f GDP in2005/06 or a full percentage point
higher than the pre-reform period. The fiscal deficit (excluding grants) i s estimated to
have declined to 7.5 percent in 2005/06.* Concerns remain about overruns in public
administration expenditure and some accumulation o f arrears toward the end o f financial
years. The energy crisis has also had a significant fiscal impact, with Government
allocating about Ush 100billion to the energy fund.

14.External Debt Sustainability: Uganda's risk o f debt distress has been greatly
reduced through the Multilateral Debt Reduction Initiative (MDRI) which cancelled 100
percent o f eligible debts to the IMF, ADF, and IDA. The MDRIreduced the net present
value (NPV) o f the external debt-to-exports ratio from a pre-MDRI level o f 229 percent
in2004/05 to 46 percent in2005/06, and this is estimated to have declined further to 33
percent in 2006/07. The reduction in the risk o f debt distress notwithstanding, prudent

Principle1:Reinforce Ownership
Uganda has been a pioneer in country ownership o f its development strategy. Uganda's first Poverty
Eradication Action Plan (PEAP) in 1997 provided the model for PRSPs worldwide, and it has since been
revised twice. Government's current program is contained in the third PEAP, whlch was approved by
Cabinet in November 2004, following broad consultation. The PEAP has been instrumental in shaping
sector-wide approaches to investment andreform, and the Bank has played a supportingrole inthis process,
including via its analytical work-the     (forthcoming) Country Economic Memorandum, for example, i s
assisting Government inidentifyingpriority policy actions to facilitate accelerated growth, given the greater
emphasis on growth inthe revisedPEAP.

Principle 2: Agree up front with the government and other financial partners on a coordinated
accountabilityframework
Donor harmonization and alignment with Government priorities is advanced in Uganda. Government
developed an annualized policy action matrix for the third PEAP, and requested that Development Partners
use the PEAP matrix and the findings o f the Annual PEAP Implementation Review (APIR) indetermining
their assistance. Budget support donors, including the Bank, are in the process of developing a Joint
Budget Support (JBS) mechanism anchored on the PEAP, which would essentially formalize and build on
the existing collaborative arrangement.

Principle 3: Customize the accountability framework and modalities of Bank support to country
circumstances
Policies supported by the PRSC represent actions that Government believes will lead to the desired PEAP
outcomes, and thus reflect country preferences. They are selected inconsultation with donors, including the
Bank, from the larger PEAP policy matrix. The stocktaking report for Uganda PRSCs 1-3 concluded that
the PRSC i s the single best vehicle for helping Uganda advance its system-wide reform process, and for
making progress on service delivery and crosscutting issues. Government has expressed that it would like
PRSC operations to be structured so that financing i s approved before the national budget is submitted to
the Parliament; it is proposed that the PRSC timetable be adjusted over a two-year period to be better
synchronized with national monitoring and budgetingprocesses(see below).

Principle4: Chooseonly actionscriticalfor achieving results as conditionsfor disbursement
Prior actions for PRSC6 are based on those envisioned at the beginning o f the 3-year programmatic cycle o f
budget support (i.e. under PRSCS). The PRSC triggers represent actions and outputs that are considered
critical by Government to the success o f its reforms. Several o f the PRSC prior actions are based on
satisfactory completion o f undertakings agreed inexisting sectoral review processes (e.g. ineducation), thus
avoiding the creation o fnew and overlapping review structures and indicators.

financial support

LESSONSLEARNED
26. The stocktakingreport for PRSCs 1-3 concludedthat the PRSC was perceivedto
be the single best vehicle for helping Uganda advance its system-wide reform
process, and for makingprogresson service delivery and crosscuttingissues. It also
identified challenges for the instrument, including the need to (i)  focus more on income
generation; (ii) clarify a graduated response, especially in the case o f reforms being off-
track; (iii)avoid being overloaded; (iv) take into account the weak capacity o f local
governments; and (v) identify and measure results.         Many o f these challenges were
addressed during preparation o f PRSC6. First, PRSC6 increases the focus on income
generation and growth by addressing issues related to the implementation o f the PMA,
improving the investment climate, and highlighting gender issues related to growth.
Second, discussions among Development Partners as part o f the design o f the JBSO have
centered around developing a mechanism with an increasing focus on performance using
the APIR to provide a common assessment framework. Third, PRSC6 begins to expand
reliance on annual sector reviews in the areas o f decentralization, public service reform,
anti-comption and investment climate, in addition to education, health, and water-
deriving the prior actions from sector undertakings. Fourth, comprehensive policy
reforms across sectors at the local government level are undertaken with support from the
Local Government Development Project, which runs inparallel with the PRSC process.
Fifth, the GoU and Development Partners have worked to improve the monitoring and
results framework, culminating inthe PEAP matrix andthe APIR, the National Integrated
MonitoringandEvaluation Strategy (NIMES), andthe statistical master plan.

27. Implementation completion reports (ICRs): Recent ICRs for the first series of
PRSCs (1-3) and the stand-alone PRSC4 provide a further basis for translating lessons

                                              11

into improved focus, strategies, and mechanisms for future PRSCs.             These ICRs
highlightedissues o f absorption capacity and fiscal sustainability, a tendency for PRSCs
to set ambitious long-term goals, and the need to assist the GoU in developing a long-
term financing strategy. To take better account o f implementation capacity, each prior
action under PRSC5 and PRSC6 included a detailed description o f the rationale, content,
and policy expectations, clarifylng the joint expectations among all actors with regard to
what the actions entail and to what they will achieve (see Annex 4). They also suggested
increasing resident technical expertise that would provide support during implementation
o f the PEAP. In response, the Bank has located the core task management and sector
specialists in the country office, enabling a year-round consultation with GoU and its
development partners.

28. World Bank global stocktaking of PRSCs. A global stocktaking of PRSCs (2005)
identified four objectives: flexibility; donor harmonization; resource predictability; and
increased country ownership. The PRSCs also signal the Bank's support for countries
with a proven track record, strong financial management, and robust commitment and
capacity to undertake structural and social reforms. Inmanyways, Uganda's PRSC series
has been the model for PRSCs in other countries, having embraced these objectives early
on and designed a comprehensive approach that encompasses a large number o f sectors
andawide range o fstakeholders.

ANALYTICALUNDERPINNINGS
29. The analytical underpinnings for the PRSC that are especially relevant to PEAP Pillar
1include the 2005 Public ExpenditureReview (PER), which covers linkages betweenthe
budget and re-orientation o f the pro-poor growth strategy and service delivery. Previous
PERs focused on the budget preparation process, budget execution, and the efficiency o f
public expenditures. The 2005 PER expanded the scope to examine how the revised
PEAP could be better reflected in the budget, through analysis o f the 2005/06 budget
allocations, the MTEF, and the LTEF. This analysis underpins the PRSC support
provided for macroeconomic management.

30. From a more strategic standpoint, the Country Economic Memorandum (CEM) aims
to assist the Government - after the 2006 elections - in developing a prioritized, time-
bound and consistent set o f policy actions, investments, and interventions to unleash a
new acceleration in economic growth (given that growth has a more prominent role inthe
third PEAP than in the first two). The CEM also aims to help refocus Bank and donor
operations and policy dialogue towards supporting economic growth. Under the CEM,
the Bank has developed a SAM-based CGE model (MAMS) as a tool to simulate the
impact on household groups o f specific investments, policies and various economic and
price shocks. This could provide a usefbl tool for ex ante PSIA in future. Further on the
macro side, a Debt Sustainability Analysis was performed jointly by the Bank and Fund
in 2006 and the draft External Debt Strategy 2006 has been prepared and is being
discussed bythe Government and stakeholders.

37. Among the outcome indicators, strong performance was achieved on reducing the
fiscal deficit (from 11.3 percent in 2005 to 7.5 percent o f GDP in 2006) and increasing
private investment (which has reached 18.7 percent o f GDP). The rural development
agenda i s also yielding results: Ugandan agriculture i s becoming increasingly more
commercial and shifting to higher value agricultural enterprises, and i s also showing
continued strong growth in output. In the social sectors, primary school enrollment
figures have already reached the PEAP target, andprogress has been registered on several
key health indicators, such as increasing the proportion o f deliveries in health care
facilities. However, raising some other indicators (e.g. primaryschool completion rate) i s
proving more o f a challenge, as discussed indetail inthe sectoral summaries below.

38. Progress under Pillar 4 (Good Governance) is more difficult to quantify than progress
under the other pillars due to the nature o f the targets, which are related to buildingmore
effective, transparent, and accountable governance at central and local levels.
Investments in public financial management processes and systems have resulted in a
rapid increase in the share o f ministries preparing regular financial statements in
accordance with regulations.      Corruption is measured through the National Integrity
Survey every two years but further work i s needed to agree on a relatively reliable interim
measure (Uganda's score on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index

39. The prior actions under the current series o f PRSCs are designed to support the
achievement o f the PEAP outcome indicators shown in Table 2, either directly or by
ensuringthat requisite environment is inplace.

     Budget execution: The PEAP outcome indicator on reducing the fiscal deficit i s on
     track, but may be affected by the on-going energy crisis. The prior action helps to
     focus budget allocation toward priority areas by containing the expansion o f other
     non-priority expenditures.
     Private sector competitiveness: The likelihood o f meeting the outcome indicator on
     increasingprivate investment i s high. The prior action seeks to support a coordinated
     sector-wide approach to ensure that reforms to improve investment climate and
     investments to upgrade the essential physical infrastructure are undertaken through a
     coherent and non-duplicative strategy based on agreed undertakings and measurable
     benchmarks.
     Agricultural production: The PRSC prior action is formulated from undertakings
     identified during sector reviews. For PRSC6, the prior action placed emphasis on land
     use policy, land registry, water for production, agriculture census, and sector public
     expenditure review.
     Public financial management: The PRSC6 prior action and possible triggers for
     PRSC7 (to be derived from the Government's FINMAPmatrix) will help to increase
     the number of spending units at the central and local government levels following
     financial reporting and auditing requirements. However, the PEAP outcome indicator

    o f 99 percent o f spending units meetingthese requirements may not be possible given
    resource and capacity constraints at the local government level.
    Reduced corruption: The scope o f the anti-corruption measures under PRSC6 was
    broadened with the aim o f achieving tangible outcomes through better enforcement. A
    more rigorous enforcement, which is visible to the public, may lead to an increase in
    the perceived incidence o f corruption in the short term, but will help to bring about a
    cultural change with regard to public's tolerance o f corruption. The National Integrity
    Survey which i s planned for FY2007/08 will need to be designed in a way that
    distinguishes perception changes due to increasing availability o f information from
    those due to genuine increase incorruption at service delivery points.
    Public sector performance: The Public Service Reform Program aims to improve
    performance through a more consistent set o f policies on remuneration, incentives,
    and use o f public assets. The PEAP outcome indicator o f increasing satisfaction with
    civil service performance may not be easy to achieve, as the agreed medium-term pay
    targets have not improved the incentives inthe civil service. The long-term pay target
    i s likely to prove unattainable without measures to streamline and right-size the
    government. The prior actions under PRSC6 and the trigger for PRSC7 look for ways
    to strike a balance between performance targets, resource constraints, and strategic
    staffing levels.
    Local government service delivey: The funding gap created by the abolition o f the
    graduated tax severely limited the local governments' ability to deliver service on the
    ground. The prior action for PRSC6 placed an emphasis on developing alternative

    government so as to prevent a collapse o f local service delivery. The PEAP outcome
    indicators o f increasing own-revenue funding o f local government budget are likelyto
    be unachievable inthe short-term.

43. PRSC-supported medium term outcome 1.1.1: Reduce public expenditure as a
percentage of GDP through budget rationalization. The prior action on budget aims to
support Government in ensuring that budget resources are executed in line with the
priorities identified in the revised PEAP. Three broad areas o f budget execution are
monitored: (i)overall expenditures to remain within the approved Budget and
programmed levels, adjusted for any within-year domestic revenue performance; (ii)   PAF
expenditures performance, at a minimum o f 95 percent o f the approved budget; and (iii)
Government to limit supplementary requests to Parliament by the security and public
administration sectors to those that can be accommodated within the resources
appropriated to those sectors inthe approved budget (presented in June and approved by
Parliament). The one-time expenditures inFY2005/06 which were the major cause o f the
overrun o f public administration (electoral) were adjusted in the FY2006/07 budget and
the Government agreed to a gradual reduction inthe share o f public administration inthe
GoUbudgetby 0.5 percentage points per annum duringFY2006/07-FY2008/09.

44. Controlling the growth of and over-spending of the public administration budget
has remained problematic over almost a decade, with over-spending averaging
about 9 percent. These overruns were on top o f an average growth in approved
allocations to public administration o f 11.5 percent per year in nominal terms over the
last decade. In fact, the overruns in each year during the last decade effectively led to
increase in the allocation for the public administration sector in the subsequent year.
Therefore, it can be postulated that either (i)
                                              overruns become entitlements in subsequent
budget years, or (ii)the public administration budget is consistently understated requiring
in-year supplementaries. Either way, these overruns have served as an impediment in
shifting Government expenditure to areas with a large impact on growth and poverty
reduction. As a consequence o f the growing concerns about public administration
expenditures, the Bank plus other budget support development partners reduced their
support duringFY2005/06.

46. Overall revenue and expenditures performed as planned in FY2005/06, except
for some notable deviations in some expenditure categories, particularly due to
elections. Domestic tax revenue performed at slightly over 100 percent, but non-tax
revenue performed below target (Table 5). Total expenditures performed at 2.3
percentage points above the approved budget. Overall, expenditure performance in the
wage and non-wage recurrent categories remained slightly over budget due to new hiring
and the creation o f new districts. Large overruns were recorded in the public
administration andjustice, law and order (JLOS) largely due to the elections and election
monitoring expenses which were not adequately budgeted for.

47. The total additionalspending in FY2005/06 amounted to Ush 172 billion above
the approved budget-in part due to a reduction in donor budget support, but also as a
result o f unanticipated additional spending for the national elections. However, this did
not negatively affect the overall fiscal deficit targets. The overall fiscal deficit (excluding,
and including grants) performed at 7.5 percent and 0.9 percent o f GDP, remaining inline
with targets. Giventhe cuts indonor budget support andthe one-time cost o frunningthe
elections, the over-spending in FY2005/06 does not seem to indicate a relaxation o f the
Government's       commitment to gradually reducing the deficit over the PEAP
implementationperiod.

48. In FY2005/06, public administration overspending amounted to Ush 86 billion,
or 18 percent. The Bank and the Fundhave used a narrower definition in programs to
monitor public administration overruns in order to avoid constraining some categories o f
spendingthat are thought to be conducive to better service delivery. On this basis, the
PRSC-defined public administration recorded an overrun o f Ush 68 billion, or about 24
percentage points above the approved budget. Election-related expenses accounted for
about 54 percent o f the overrun in public administration, but these are expected to even
out during the remainder o f the P E N implementation period. Overspending was also
recorded inthe Parliamentary Commission, State House, Office o f the President andmass
mobilization.

PEAPstrategic objective 2.1:Efficient and competitive private sector.

52. PRSC supported medium-term            outcome 2.1.1: Increased private           sector
competitiveness. Policies in support o f this objective have been anchored in three key
programs (the Medium-Term Competitiveness Strategy - MTCS, Strategic Exports
Program - SEP, and Plan for Modernization o f Agriculture - PMA) seeking to enhance
production, competitiveness and incomes by increasing private sector efficiency. Sub-
objectives are aimed at addressing key constraints in infrastructure, the financial sector,
and the commercial legal justice system, and by improving factor markets. Government
also has in place ongoing programs to strengthen the Uganda Revenue Authority, land
andbusiness registries, and commercial laws.

53. As a landlocked economy with poor transport infrastructure and storage facilities, the
cost o f doing business inUganda i s high, with difficult logistics. Inadequate provision o f
energy, compounded by below-normal hydroelectric generation due the cyclical low
water level in Lake Victoria as well as highinternational oil prices, makes it difficult for
firms to operate competitively in global markets. Low firm-level productivity and
responsiveness to consumer preferences, along with weak entrepreneurial capacity, results
in slow development ofproductive clusters. Inadequate infrastructure and the increasing
cost o f imports and exports have also negatively affected investment decisions.
Inadequate skilled labor force, limited management skills, the prevalence o f informality
in the labor market, and low production standards are key constraints leading to low
productivity and low capacity utilization. Recent studies undertaken for the Country
Economic Memorandum point to a severe mismatch between the growth o f jobs in the
formal sector and the number o f people expected to enter the work force. The accelerated
growth o f MSMEs, together with greater mobility from informal to formal status, i s key

                                            28

element in reducing poverty-in     particular income poverty--through expanded and more
productive employment.

54. While the Medium Term Competitiveness Strategy has been successful in improving
the investment climate, Uganda's private sector, and in particular the M S M E subsector,
has been slow to respond to market opportunities relative to other countries in Africa or
Asia. The key constraints affecting the public-private sector interface are the inadequate
provision o f services in the land and business register, an outdated legal framework in
selected areas, and inefficiencies in trade-related support institutions, including customs
andstandards.

55, Therefore, a better harmonized and coordinated implementation o f government
programs to remove various constraints i s critical:

    Improved dialogue and coordination is needed to harmonize activities under SEP,
    PMA, and MTCS, and to prioritize investments to remove key infrastructure
    constraints. Better co-ordination and prioritization are key to achieve the desired
    impact on growth and competitiveness.

    Inorder tofurther improve the business environment, Government is implementing
    a series o f reforms in the commercial legal justice sector including the reform o f
    commercial laws and regulations (companies act, insolvency act, etc.), the companies'
    registrar,commercial courts, andsimplification o fcourt procedures.

   Access to long-term finance needs to improve as leasing, rural, and agricultural
    finance remain key constraints to growth. Government is implementing an outreach
    plan for microfinance, strengthening micro deposit-taking institutions, funding
    microfinance services through cooperatives, and developing a credit reference bureau.

    Current limitations in factor markets and infrastructure services need to be
    alleviatedto foster growth. To achieve an efficient allocation o fland, the land sector
    strategy calls for revitalization o f land registries, and operationalization o f the Land
    Tribunal and the National Land Use Policy. To facilitate the private sector's effort to
    reduce costs, Government has begun mobilizing more resources for investments in
    transport services and energy provision (from private and public sources) with a view
    to meeting current and future demand. To ease accessibility to serviced land for
    investors, a modern business park i s to be constructed at Namanve, and several other
    parks are under review.

PEAPstrategic objective 2.2: Increasedand more efficient agricultural production.

58. PRSC supported medium-term outcome 2.2.1: Increased agricultural production.
The revised PEAP focuses on increasing household incomes through transforming the
current widespread subsistence farming to more market-oriented production or
commercial farming through coordinated multi-sectoral interventions under the PMA.
The interventions include (i)agricultural research and technology development; (ii)
agricultural advisory services; (iii)agricultural education; (iv) rural financial services; (v)
agro-processing and marketing; (vi) sustainable natural resource use and management;
and (vii) supportive infrastructure. Joint Annual Reviews (JAR) o f the PMA take stock of
progress inimplementation and guide the activities to focus on duringthe following year.
Since FY 2003/04, PMA prior actions for the PRSC have been derived from the
undertakings agreed upon at the PMA JARS.

59. Some notable examples o f achievements so far made through PMA implementation
include rollout o f the NAADS program to almost two-thirds o f the country (533 sub-
counties out o f the current total o f 960 sub-counties), establishment o f a new and
reformed National Agricultural Research System (NARS), incorporation o f the National
Agricultural Education Strategy into the Education Sector Strategic Plan, development o f
the marketing and agro-processing strategy, and passage o f the Warehouse Receipt
System Act by Parliament. Other achievements expected in the next six months include
the finalization and implementation o f the Environment and Natural Resources (ENR)
Sector Strategy and Investment Plan and the adoption o f the various policies and
legislative bills relatedto landissues.

60. The primary challenge to sustained growth in productivity in Ugandan
agriculture is the rising population. In its current structure, the agriculture sector
cannot absorb such large increases inworkers and dependents. Inthe past, with available
land, growth was sustained through area expansion.            In recent years yields have
contributed to output growth, but area expansion still remains the major source o f growth.
Now the limits to environmentally sustainable area expansion in Uganda have probably
already been reached, except perhaps in the North. Without significant possibilities for
further area expansion, future growth will have to rely on a combination o f more
intensive agriculture and - crucially - the movement o f labor out o f own-account
agriculture to urban and rural non-farm activities.       The latter will be important, as
population pressure i s already leading to uneconomical farm sizes, subsistence orientation
of households and reduced adoption o f productivity enhancing technologies because o f
higher risk aversion.

mechanisms for accountability. However, the anti-corruption agencies remain under-

This, in addition to pending court cases, lengthy approval and implementation processes
for legislation, have ledto slow progress inimplementing the anti-corruption agenda.

114. Population growth and infant and maternal mortality: Rapid population growth
creates a challenge to Uganda's poverty eradication efforts. The National Population and
Housing Census o f 2002 found the total population o f Uganda to be 24.4 million, an
increase o f 8 million since the last count in 1991 (Figure 3), making Uganda the third
fastest growing country in the world with a rate o f growth o f 3.2 percent. At this growth
rate, Uganda's population was projected at over 27.6 million in2005 and 39.3 million by
2015. By 2050, Uganda's population is projected to be 103.2 million, making it the 4th
most populous nation in SSA (UNWorld Population Prospects, 2002). Uganda also has
the third highest fertility rate inthe world: an average Ugandanwoman bears 6.9 children,
and one o f highest dependency ratios in the world o f 1.11.26 High total fertility, short
birth intervals, limitedaccess to contraceptive services and low age o f first pregnancy are
the key drivers o f Uganda's high population growth rate.                        Poor and less educated
households in Uganda experience higher fertility and higher dependency ratios. A
discussion paper by MoFPED estimates that if no action is taken to reduce fertility, an
additional 1.3 million Ugandans, approximately 28 percent o f the total population, will
live inpoverty by 2013.27 It recognizes population growth as an impediment to poverty
eradication, as a contributing factor to highmaternal and infant mortality, as a hindrance
to attaining education and employment objectives, and as an unsustainable pressure on
natural resources. It identifies key determinants o f lower fertility priority interventions as
secondary education for girls and access to family planning. The paper concludes that a
"reduction in the growth rate o f 1 percentage point could boost economic growth in the
mediumterm by 0.6-2.8 percentage points per year." Klasen (2005) notes that ifUganda
began a period o f sustained fertility decline, it could boost its per capita income growth
rates by between 0.5-0.6 percentage points per year in the medium term, and the

POVERTYAND SOCIAL IMPACT
118. Poverty and Social Impact Assessments (PSIAs) have allowed Government to
analyze the distributional impact o f policy reforms on the well-being o f different
stakeholder groups, with particular focus on the poor and vulnerable. Uganda has a long
tradition o f analysis o fpoverty and social impacts o fpolicies, andmost core diagnostic or
sector work address these issues even though the final product i s not presented in as a
PSIA. A large number o f such studies have been undertaken by Government, the Bank
and other partners to analyze poverty and social impacts o f policies, including the PSIAs
on the abolition o f user fees inhealthclinics (2004) and on the Strategic Exports Program
(2003). A comprehensive poverty assessment has been undertaken in2006 for the period
1992/3-2002/3. A PSIA on lands in Northern Uganda is being conducted jointly by the
MinistryofLands andtheWorld Bank.An ongoingPSIA onlocal government taxationis
expected to shed light on the impacts o f different options for local revenue collection in
two dimensions. The first o f these is the distributional impacts (taking into account both
revenue and expenditure effects). Options for local taxation tend to suffer from a
contradictory dynamic. Ifthe measures considered are progressive (e.g. property taxation,
taxation o f formal sector transactions or incomes) then they tend to be ineffective at
raising revenue inpoor and marginal districts. Ifrevenue can be raised at the local level in
rural areas it also enhances the chances that resources will be available for utilization at
the parish (LC1) level, which has been starved o f funds since the abolition o f the
Graduated Tax. It i s important therefore to consider both the revenue and expenditure
dimensions o f taxation options. The second dimension will be the impacts o f different
revenue options on the accountability o f local government to citizens - which i s widely
perceived as having suffered since the abolition o fthe graduated tax.

119. The assessment o f the poverty and social impact o f the measures proposed by the
PEAP and supported by the PRSC is carried out in the context o f Uganda's PEAP
monitoring system and in particular the biannual household surveys that analyze, among
other things, poverty trends and dynamics. The surveys which cover the entire country
provide a rich source o f information on household socio-economic characteristics,
including consumption expenditures, employment, and access to social services, etc. The
surveys are complemented by the more qualitative participatory poverty assessments.
Emphasis i s increasingly being placed on outcome in the PEAP monitoring and on
indicators which provide direct information on the status o f poverty inall its dimensions,
including gender-related issues (for example, a baseline assessment on women and land
was recently completed). Monitoring o f these indicators i s based on a broad range o f
surveys and administrative data systems. In 2005/06, the Government supported by the
World Bank carried out the "Moving out o f Poverty" as part o f an international program
to assess the determinants moving inand out poverty.

R I S K S AND RISKMITIGATION
132. The risks are discussed briefly below with a particular emphasis on those that are
more relevant for the PRSC program, and the PRSC efforts to mitigate the risks. In
addition, specific risks to the implementation o f core areas the program are also
identified.

Risks that reflect the broader context in Uganda

133. Policy Environment. The FY2006/07 National Budget Framework Paper included
new programs for rural community development and rural micro-credit initiatives
fulfilling the pledgesmade duringthe 2006 national elections. Inrecent months, a number
o f directives regarding transfer o f landto investors also brought strong reactions from the
media and the National Forestry Agency. There are concerns about possible policy
reversal and state interventions in productive sectors, potentially with undesired
outcomes. Inrecent years, the risk o f deviation from established policy, or emergence o f
new initiatives without sufficient time for consultations, has been mitigated through
policy dialogue at the highest level, and also through the PRSC and IMF's PSI, by the
continued strengthening and buttressing o f institutional and administrative processes. In
the two rural initiatives mentioned above, donor reactions during the budget process
persuaded the Government that a structured discussion was needed before committing
large budgetary funds. A strong and stable Government apparatus, with transparent and
consistent decision-making and priority setting processes will help mitigate this type o f
risks.

134. Power Shortage. The cyclical decline inthe Lake Victoria water levels has curtailed
hydroelectric production at one third o f capacity. Emergency investments in thermal
generation have started constraining resources allocated to other sectors. With rising cost
o f doing business due to expensive and unreliable electricity, there is a risk that private
investmentwill not flow into Uganda as before or that existinginvestors choose to move
plant and employment to other countries. The potential impact on growth and poverty
alleviation i s likely to be unfavorable, as fewer resources would be available for
upgrading o f physical infrastructure and human capital. Inconjunction with the ongoing
sectoral Public Expenditure Reviews, the PRSC budget dialogue is being strengthened in
order to assist the Government to achieve better value for money for its resources and to
achieve efficiency savings in sector programs. At the same time, a new area o f dialogue
on improving the investment climate has been opened up duringPRSC6.

135. Potential resolution o f conflict in the North. On-going peace talks with the rebel
group, Lord's Resistance Army, produced a temporary cease fire and talks are continuing
on the eventual disarmament o f the LRA forces. If the improved internal security
situation cannot be maintained, it would hinder achievement o f national and local
development objectives. The socio-economic challenges posed by the conflict in the
North and the cattle-rustling related insecurity in the East have affected a large segment
o f Uganda's poor, heighteningregional disparities. The destruction o f the livelihoods o f
the communities living in these areas has resulted in very high or rising (as in the East)


                                               57

poverty levels and displaced some 1.6 million persons. On the upside risk, a return to
peace will entail large resource requirements and stretch the capacity o f the Government
to effectively facilitate recovery inthis region. There i s likely to be added pressure on the
budget and donor aid resource envelope due to a massive resettlement and rehabilitation
costs. The Bank, working with other partners, will continue to support the provision of
better social services inthe areas ravaged bywar andinsecurity.

136. Poor governance undermines confidence. Slow progress in reducing corruption
could harm Uganda's external credibility towards potential private investors and
development partners. Corruption also threatens public confidence, and could reduce the
impact o f Government programs.           The Bank is working with Government and
Development Partners to support a more vigorous implementation o f Uganda's national
anti-corruption strategy and its related action plan in conjunction with public service
reform measures. The PRSC6 i s supporting the government's legislative and institutional
strengthening programs to broaden the Government's fight against corruption through a
multi-pronged approach inorder secure more tangible andquick results.

137. Weak institutional and public sector capacity, particularly at sub-national levels.
Uganda needs to address the pervasive public sector weaknesses particularly at the district
levels. Strong ownership o f the PEAP agenda by the entire public sector and civil society,
and ongoingpublic sector reforms, should contribute to sustained improvements inpublic
service capacity. In addition to public service reform, the PRSC6 includes a prior action
on decentralization, with emphasis on securing adequate funding for local government
operation.

138. High mortalitv and poor reproductive health outcomes. Notwithstanding Uganda's
gains in the fight against HIV/AIDS, high infant mortality and maternal mortality, and
high fertility rates remain serious challenges. A multisectoral taskforce has been
analyzing the determinants o f infant andmaternal mortality andhas proposed strategies to
tackle them. The population secretariat i s looking at measures to address population
questions, and actions are proposed in the third PEN. Development Partners have been
involved in the health, water, and education sectors in the context o f PRSCs and other
stand-alone operations, and are supporting infant and maternal mortality-reducing
initiatives.

Risks specific to thePRSC:

139. Slow prowess in the implementation o f public service reforms. Slow progress in
implementation o f the public service reforms could adversely affect several o f the
outcomes that are supported by the PRSC. The many interacting dimensions o f public
service reforms, including: strategic management o f the public service, human resources
management including pay reform and other incentives, capacity building for better
performance, effective institutional practices, removal o f overlap and duplication o f
efforts in both core and more peripheral agencies- are all issues that are intrinsically
linked, strongly influenced by politics and organized interests, and cut across all sectors.
The PRSC will continue to support government in the implementation o f the public

                                              58

service reform program, ensuring that efficiency and performance are achieved in a
sustainable manner.

140. Over-expansion o f government structures. Recent rapid creation o f new districts
and proliferation o f autonomous agencies and commissions are likely to present
significant financial problems in the future. Simplification o f administrative structures
and personnel or progress toward self-sustaining local governments would need to be
considered, and provide localities with a disincentive to become new districts. Some o f
the over-spending observed under public administration also relates to the proliferation o f
autonomous agencies and commissions, and this i s one o f the areas where policies are
expected to be reviewed inthe context o fthe PRSCprior action on budget execution.

141, Over-committing on priority interventions.     Capacity and resource constraints in
key social service sectors such as education and health may undermine the Government's
efforts on quality improvement. The demographic factors point to potential
unsustainability o f current programs, and thus, new programs will need to be considered
carefilly with population pressure duly taken into account.

Annex 3: PRINCIPLESFORDETERMINING                    PRIOR ACTIONS
Principlesfor Prior Actions inthe Uganda PovertyReductionStrategy Support Credit (PRSC)
Programs. The following principles have beendeveloped during PRSC4 to guidethe development ofprior
actions for the PRSC programs:

1. About the Principles
These principles concern the establishment o f Prior Actions (conditions) for disbursement o f the World
Bank-supported PRSCs inUganda. The principles are intended to be complementary and subordinate to the
agreed "Partnership Principles between Government of Uganda and its Development Partners,"
Kampala, September 2003,

2. The PovertyReductionSupport Credit (PRSC)
a.   The PRSC is a core operation to implement the objectives o f Uganda's Poverty Eradication Action
    Plafloverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PEAPPRSP), and the Bank Group's Country Assistance
     Strategy (CAS).
b. The PRSCs are sequential annual credits, and each PRSC is seen as an annual step in a three-year
    medium-termreformprogram.

3. Prior Actions
a. Each PRSC i s based on a set o f conditions ("prior actions") that the government h l f i l l s before the
grandcredit i s presentedto the World Bank Board. These prior actions are based on shared expectations
between Government o f Uganda (GoU), the World Bank (WB), and other development partners.
b. Prior actions should be based upon policy dialogue, and aligned with Uganda's Poverty Eradication
Action Plan and country assistance priorities. Prior actions should normally correspond to all the major
reform areas (pillars) o f the Poverty EradicationAction Program. The starting point for discussion i s the set
o fprior actions o f the precedingPRSC.
c. The flexibility inherent inthe PRSC comes not from defining vague or easily-met prior actions, but from
agreeing on specific and monitorable milestones and then measuring progress against them, with reasoned
judgments allowing for disciplined adaptation.
d. Agreement on prior actions is reached between the GoU, the Bank, and other Development Partners
shortly after pre-appraisal andbefore appraisal. Prior actions are at this stage considered binding, but are in
exceptional circumstances adaptable inthe face o f uncertainties inside and outside o f the program.
e. Completion o f the prior actions i s a condition for proceeding to the World Bank Board for approval o f
the grandcredit.
f.Whenprior actions are not met bynegotiations, there are three alternatives: (i) support; (ii)
                                                                                    reduce               delay
program; and (iii)  release Credit intranches.

4. AnticipatedPrior Actions
a. Each PRSC also includes a notional set o f tentative prior actions that are presented in the program
documentation. The tentative prior actions are not binding for the next PRSC.
b. As one PRSC becomes effective, and the preparation of the next commences, the tentative actions
identified under the first help shape and form the basis for preparation and agreement o fprior actions under
the next. I t is important for the reformprogram to have a predictable and sustained approach.
c. The anticipated prior actions should normally be discussed, and agreement on broad areas to be covered
should be reached at the pre-appraisal o fthe precedingPRSC.
d. Exact areas and precise wording of the anticipated prior actions should be agreed during appraisal and
negotiations o f the precedingPRSC.
e. Where tentative prior actions may have to be revised, the fault may lie in a poor choice o f tentative
actions, unexpectedly weak execution o f elements o f the reform program, faster than expected
implementation of elements o fthe reformprogram, or changing circumstances outside the reformprogram.

                    PRIORACTION 2: INVESTMENT CLIMATE

Introduction

The private sector, including agriculture, is the major source o f employment o f an
estimated 12-million-strong workforce. Non-farm enterprises in the formal sector,
estimated at 160,000 firms, employ over 444,000 workers, with the majority o f workers
employed in MSMEs. There are no accurate employment figures in Uganda, but it is
widely acknowledged that an estimated 300,000 new job seekers per year are absorbed by
the informal sector, which together with MSMEs account for an estimated 1.5 million
workers. The accelerated growth o f MSMEs, together with greater mobility from
informal to formal status, has the potential to reduce poverty, in particular income
poverty, through expanded and more productive employment. The formalization o f
MSMEs would, among other benefits, increase their access to financial and technical
services. The lack o f a skilled labor force, limited management skills, the nature o f the
entrepreneurial behavior (what is ofien termed an "informal enterprise" approach), and
poor production standards are key constraints leading to low productivity and low
capacity utilization.

While the Medium Term Competitiveness Strategy has been successful inimproving the
investment climate, leading to a reduction in the processing time for legal cases,
improvements in investor support services and improvements in telecommunication
services, these improvements have only led to growth in traditional agricultural and
manufacturing sectors. Uganda's private sector, inparticular MSMEs,has been slower to
respond to market opportunities relative to other countries inAfrica or Asia. Despite the
progress made, the business environment still lags behind other investment locations.
Requisite institutions are not able to provide services that support new sources o f growth
or enable the private sector to compete in global markets and promote trade. Low firm-
level productivity and responsiveness to consumer preferences, along with weak
entrepreneurial behavior and strategies, result inslow development o fproductive clusters.
Inadequate infrastructure and increasingcosts o f imports and exports have also negatively
affected investment decisions.

The key constraints affecting the public-private sector interface are the inadequate
provision o f services in the land and business register, an outdated legal framework in
selected areas, and inefficiencies in trade-related support institutions, including customs,
standards, andtrade policy and export promotion institutions.

In the second pillar of the PEAP, Government seeks to enhance production,
competitivenessand incomes, by increasingprivatesector efficiency. Sub-objectives
toward this overarching goal are aimed at addressing the above mentioned constraints by
strengthening infrastructure and the financial sector, increasing efficiency in the
commercial legaljustice system, and improving factor markets.

                            PRIOR ACTION 11:EDUCATION

                                A. BetterEducatedUgandans
                                          Summary

 Government o f Uganda continues to make steady progress towards achieving PEAP
targets andMillenniumDevelopment Goals (MDGs) through implementing its Education
Strategic Investment Plan 1998 - 2004, recently reviewed to extend from 2004 to 2015.
It is committed to achieving the MDG o f ensuringthat all boys and girls alike complete
primary schooling, and elimination o f gender disparities.      Government continues to
provide about 30 percent o f its discretionary recurrent budget to education and over 65
percent o f this i s spent on primary education. The Net Enrolment Rate (NER) at primary
increased to about 90 percent with equal numbers o f boys and girls. Although survival
rates for the post-UPE cohort have improved, dropout rates are still high. This threatens
achievement o f the MDGs. Results o f the National Assessment o f Progress inEducation
indicate that learning competencies, although still low, are beginning to improve. To
improve quality o f learning, Government continues to hire additional teachers and
improve their conditions o f service, construct more classrooms, purchase learning
materials, and is carefully implementing the roadmap for reviewing the primary
curriculum, including instruction in the mother tongue at the lower grades. Inaddition,
Coordinating Centre Tutors (CCTs) have been deployed to support continuous
professional development for teachers. Specific attention is paid to girls and districts
with poor indicators.
To increase access to secondary education, Government began to implement the Plan for
Post Primary Education and Training. Gross Enrolment Rate for secondary education
improved from 18 percent in 2004 to 21 percent in 2005. Government also began to
implement the Strategic Plan for Higher Education, including a stronger focus on
teaching science and technology at universities, and proposals for divesting itself from
financing student feeding and accommodation. The proportion o f students enrolled in
science related disciplines increased from 15 percent in2004 to 23.3 percent in2005.
Despite the progress recorded, several challenges remain: Gender disparities remain,
particularly at post primary levels, regional inequalities remain and delivery o f education
intheNorthremainsamajor challenge.
Comprehensive sector reviews, that include all stakeholders, will continue to be the
forum for reviewing progress on sector targets and establishing undertakings that
Government i s committed to achieve in order to reach the PEAP objectives and MDGs.
Successhl implementation o f undertakings agreed during the Education Sector Reviews
i s the anticipatedprior action for PRSC6 - 8.

                  PRIORACTION 13: WATER AND SANITATION

The annual Joint Sector Review (JSR) o f the water and sanitation sector was held Sept.
25-29, 2006, and assessed progress in relation to the 10 key sector performance golden
indicators and the 5th JSR undertakings. The 7 undertakings agreed on duringthe JSR in
September 2005 have been largely achieved, with the exception o f the filling o f posts in
DWD, which is being accelerated and is on track for achievement by June 2007.
Government i s pursuing forward-looking subsectoral reforms that include the
development o f an effective policy framework for managing water resources;
decentralized implementation o f rural water supply and sanitation through local
governments; the introduction o f professional utility management and private-sector
participation in urban water and sanitation; and enhanced sector coordination and
stakeholder consultation.

Resource flows to the sector have risen dramatically-from   Ush47.4 billioninfiscal year
1996/97 to a ceiling o f around Ush 126 billion in FY 2002/03 onwards. However, the
Review noted with concern the reduction inresource allocation to the sector fimding as a
share o f the national budget from 2.8 percent to 2.6 percent, despite increasing demands
due to population growth and persistent dry spells. The sector looks forward to
achievement o f the long term framework o f 5 percent o f the national budget as
recommended inthe PEAP. Rural water coverage decreased slightly from 61.3 percent in
2005 to 61 percent in 2006 as the intervention could not keep pace with population
growth. There i s therefore need for GOU to increase budget allocation for the water
sanitation sector, and for improved efficiency within the sector. Operation and
maintenance i s also a persistent issue inthe sector.

Reform strategy to be supported by PRSCs 6 and 7: The focus will be on aligning sub-
sector budgetary allocations to sector outcomes, ensure better value for money in sector
actions and investments, speeding the implementation o f urban water and sanitation
institutional reforms, promote Integrated Water Resources management, scale up o f
sanitation and hygiene promotion, and pursue vigorously sector response to emergency
situations particularly in Northern Uganda. Progress will be monitored at future sector
reviews. Also, six specific undertakings for the upcoming year were adopted at the 2006
JSR to be implementedinthe coming year.

Annex 5: GENDER    ISSUES INTHE OPERATION

BackgroundandRecentDevelopments

Uganda has shown exceptional leadership in addressing gender issues in the 2004 PEAP
revision, and inthe accompanying PRSCs. The 2005-2008 PEAP strongly articulates the
relevance o f gender for growth, poverty reduction, and meeting the MDGs, noting
specifically that ".,,removing constraints caused by HIV/AIDS, environment, and above
all gender inequalities is key to achieving Uganda's poverty eradication goals." The
gender-focused initiatives launched under PRSC4 were extended inPRSCS, and are now
being consolidated and strengthened under PRSC6, which will pay increased attention to
cross-cutting issues affecting growth and poverty reduction in the country. Key
accomplishments in tackling the various gender-focused elements included in the PEAP
Policy Action Matrix, and supported bythe PRSC, include:
   +  Gender and equity budget guidelines. The gender and equity budget guidelines
      prepared by MOFPED have been attached to the Budget Call Circular each year
      since 2004/05, and a trainee manual on implementing the guidelines has been
      developed; a manual for trainers is being finalized. Some progress has been made
      by MFPED, in collaboration with the Forum for Women and Democracy
      (FOWODE) and others, to develop the capacity to implement these guidelines
      effectively. This has focused on 5 priority sectors (agriculture,             education,
      WATSAN, health, and JLOS), alongside support to local government in6 districts.
      Six workshops were held in FY2006/07 for ministry staff, with a total o f 120
      participants trained at the national level, and 240 trained at the local level. Training
      activities will continue in FY2007/08 aimed at enabling compliance with the
      guidelinesduringpreparationo fbudget framework papers for FY2008/09.

   +  Work program on gender and growth. MOFPED, in collaboration with
      MGLSD, has actively pursued work on gendedgrowth linkages in Uganda.
      MOFPED commissioned a "Gender and Growth Assessment" (GGA), focused on
      identifying legal and regulatory obstacles to women's entrepreneurship, which was
      published in early 2006. A Gender and Growth Coalition (comprised o f civil
      society, public, and private organizations) has been established to support the
      implementation o f its recommendations, and i s now actively lobbying for their
      adoption. GGA recommendations have been incorporated into Uganda's Private
      Sector Development Strategy 2005-2009 (uP3), and have led to re-drafting o f
      several related laws, including the Companies Act and the Chattels Act. MOFPED

SECTION1: INTRODUCTION

The Government o f Uganda (GoU), development partners and other key stakeholders
held the 4thNational Health Assembly on 24th- 25th October 2006 and thereafter the 12th
Health Sector Joint Review Mission (JRM) on the implementation o f the Health Sector
Strategic Plan (HSSP) from 26" - 27th October 2006 in Kampala under the theme
"Community mobilisation and investing in health for achieving the Millennium
Development Goals ".

The mainobjective o fthe 4thNational HealthAssembly was to provide a forum for wider
participation o f all the major health sector stakeholders and with Local Government
representation, to review the performance o f the health sector and identify priorities for
the third year o f the second Health Sector Strategic Plan.

The main objective o f the JRM was to review progress o f implementation o f the Health
Sector Strategic Plan. The specific objectives o fthe JRM are:
0   To review the performance o f the health sector during the first year o f the HSSP 11
    (2005/06);
    To discuss the financial resource envelope for the following year (FY 2007/08);
    To review progress, consolidate and internalize the Undertakings o f the October 2005
    N H N J R M and review PEAP matrix inrelation to HSSP II;    and
    To discuss and agree on health sector priorities for the following year (FY 2007/08)
    including Undertakings.

The Aide Memoire records the proceedings o f the 4th NHA and 12thJRM including all
agreed actions and recommendations. In addition, the Aide Memoire records the key
sector undertakings agreed for achievement before the next JRM inOctober 2007.

The 4th National Health Assembly was officially opened by the Hon Minister o f Health,
Dr. Stephen Mallinga, MP. About 600 delegates participated inthe NHA. The delegates
included key officials from districts, District Chairpersons, Chief Administrative Officers,
District Directors o f Health Services, Medical Superintendents from Regional Referral
Hospitals, General Hospitals and in-charges from Health Centre IVs. Officials from
central government ministries, development partners, health related autonomous
institutions and other key stakeholders also participated.

The 4" NHA and 12thJRM were preceded by one week o fjoint monitoring field visits to
six districts by six teams composed o f officials from government and development
partners. The districts visited include Kalangala, Kapchorwa, Mukono, Kyenjojo, Arua

Annex 8: UGANDAAND THE MDGs

Uganda has made strong progress towards achieving the MDGs. Two targets have already
been met - gender equality in primary school enrolment and reversal o f the spread of
HIV/AIDs. IfPEAP implementation and the reform agenda stay on track, Uganda should
meet four more targets by 2015          - halving o f extreme poverty and hunger, universal
primary education, environmental sustainability and strengthening global partnerships.
Progress has been slow on two targets which may not be met child and maternal -
mortality. According to the UN Millennium Project Report (2005), Uganda i s one o f the
countries that requires additional resources to reach all the MDGs.

Given the steady progress in poverty reduction, Uganda i s on track to meeting the income
poverty MDG. Government commitment and leadership, and timely and coordinated external
support have also ensured Uganda i s making strong progress to meet the MDG for education,
although primary completion rates remain disappointing. Currently about 90 percent o f primary
school age children are enrolled. At the primary level (although not at secondary), wealth and
gender biases in access have largely been addressed. Children inthe lowest wealth quintile are as
likely to go to primary school as the highest wealth quintile.

Maternal mortality and under-5 mortality MDGs will be difficult to achieve. The last survey
of child and maternal mortality rates in2000 presented a disappointingpicture. Since then, sector
reforms have strengthened drug distribution, improved immunization coverage, addressed human
resources for health and increased utilization. Results of the Uganda Demographic and Health
Survey (2006) to update the status o f this indicator are expected later this year.

Malaria incidence appears to have increased with resistance to existing treatment. However, the
successful home-based management o f fever program should have an impact upon mortality, and
efforts are underway with support from the Global Fund to scale up use o f bednets. The data
available for monitoring the prevalence o f malaria requires strengthening.

The MDG for reducing HIV/AIDS was met in 1996 due to early intervention, political
leadership, strong focus on prevention and application of a multi-sector approach. Large scale
resources from PEPFAR and the Global Fund have been secured and the coverage o f ARV

treatment i s increasing rapidly. But the Uganda A I D S Commission requires strengthening to
effectively carry out its coordinating role.

Uganda i s likely to achieve the access to safe drinking water MDG target, but i s currently o f f
track to meet other targets under MDG 7 on environmental sustainability.The revised PEAP
recognizes environment and natural resources (ENR) as key issues affecting the livelihoods,
health and vulnerability o f the poor. Environmental degradation i s likely to increase the costs
incurred by government in achieving wider PEAP objectives in addition to being a constraint on
sustained growth.

As highlighted above, progress against the MDGon gender equality and empoweringwomen
has been registered in the context o f reducing inequalities in primary education. Inequalities in
secondary education have also been reduced, but remain significant. More generally, gender
analysis o f poverty in Uganda has drawn attention to inequality in access and control o f
productive resources; women's overall work burden; and the problem o f domestic violence. A
gender team has been established under the leadership o f the Ministry o f Gender, Labour, and
Social Development to actively pursue gender mainstreaming through PEAP implementation.
This will require, importantly, a clearer focus on gender and equity issues in the setting o f the
government's budget. Policy progress has been made, but this needs to be translated into action.

IMF Executive Board Completes the First Review under the Policy Support
Instrumentfor UgandaandApproves a New Three-Year Policy SupportInstrument

The Executive Board o f the International Monetary Fund (IMF) today completed the first
review under a 16-month Policy Support' Instrument (PSI) for Uganda. The PSI was
approved on January 24,2006 (see Press Release No. 06/14).

The Executive Board also agreed to Uganda's request to replace the current 16-monthPSI
with a new three-year PSI, to support their near-and medium-term policies. The new
program includes structural measures to spur private sector activity, lower business costs,
and improve Uganda's competitiveness. Macroeconomic policies would continue to
emphasize stability and sound management o f aid flows. Uganda remains a strong
candidate for a PSIwith its longstandingtrack record o fprudentmonetary policies, ample
foreign exchange reserves, and low external debt, particularly after the recent Multilateral
Debt ReliefInitiative (MDRI).

The IMF's framework for PSISi s designed for low-income countries that may not need
IMF financial assistance, but still seek close cooperationwith the IMFinpreparation and
endorsement o f their policy frameworks. PSI-supported programs are based on country-
owned poverty reduction strategies adopted in a participatory process involving civil
society and development partners. This i s intended to ensure that PSI-supportedprograms
are consistent with a comprehensive framework for macroeconomic, structural and social
policies to foster growth and reduce poverty. Members' performance under a PSI is
reviewed semi-annually, irrespective o f the status o f the program (see Public Information
Notice No. 05/145).

Following the Executive Board discussion, Mr. Takatoshi Kato, Deputy Managing
Director andActing Chair, made the following statement:

"Sound macroeconomic management, structural reforms, and substantial donor assistance
supported Uganda's long record o f high economic growth and poverty reduction. Per
capita growth, however, has tapered off inrecent years. To maintain high, sustainable per
capita growth, there i s a need to scale up investment in infrastructure, deepen the
financial sector, andcontinue to strengthen governance.

"In addressing Uganda's infrastructure bottlenecks, careful attention must be paid to
financing and macroeconomic considerations. Infrastructure projects need to be well
planned and financed to the extent possible through grant scaling up, domestic savings,
and concessional borrowing.



                                             135

"Uganda's financial sector needs deepening to build on recent improvements in the
banking sector. The government is developing new supervision and legal arrangements
for the provision o f long-term capital and i s beginning to promote financial services in
rural areas. To sustain these initiatives, it will be important for the government to avoid
directed lending.

"Sustaining the country's fiscal performance will require m h e r efforts to boost revenues
and strengthen expenditure management. In this context, caution in the government's
efforts to introduce tax incentives to attract investments i s warranted. The way forward
for Ugandawould be to use the country's scarce resources to improve its infrastructure.

"As regards structural reforms, the authorities are encouraged to press ahead with their
ambitious agenda, covering the public finances, the financial system, and governance. In
addition, the recently established East African Community (EAC) customs union should
help promote regional trade and provide a forum for discussion o f trade policies and
advancement o f regional integration.

"The first scheduled review under the Policy Support Instrument (PSI) is completed. As
envisaged, the current 16-month PSI i s being replaced by a three-year PSI. The latter i s
based on the approved 2006/07 budget, the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework, and
Uganda's Poverty Eradication Action Plan. The program-which           meets the standard of
upper credit tranche conditionality-aims          at maintaining macroeconomic stability
through continued prudent fiscal, monetary, and exchange policies, while preserving debt
sustainability. As in the past, these would set the basis for private sector-led growth and
continued poverty reduction," Mr.Kat0 said.
