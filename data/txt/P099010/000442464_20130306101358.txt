F. Results Framework Analysis

Program Development Objectives (from Program Document)

The specific development objectives of the PRSC 7-11 series were to support the acceleration of real
growth and a reduction in the incidence of poverty in Burkina Faso through three pillars:

  (i)   Improving the investment climate and promoting exports: The series focused on several actions to
        strengthen the competitiveness of the cotton sector and reform the investment climate.
  (ii) Improving/Protecting access to quality basic services: The series helped strengthen
        decentralization in basic sectors, such as education and water, improved access to basic services,
        and ensured greater impact of public expenditures on human capital formation.
  (iii) Promoting efficiency, transparency and accountability in the use of public resources: The series
        supported better public financial management and governance through increased fiscal transfers
        to local governments and better budget information.

Revised Program Development Objectives (as approved by original approving authority)

The program development objectives remained consistent throughout the entire series and no new
objectives were added. However, minor revisions were made to the supporting pillars to help the country
to cope with the impact of the global economic downturn and the impact of exogenous food, fuel, and
financial shocks. Pillar II was altered slightly in PRSC-10 so that access to basic social services would be
“protected? as such access had already been “improved? through the previous credits in the series. In
PRSC-11, Pillar II was removed as that objective had already been achieved earlier in the series and well
covered by the Bank (Fast Track Initiative I for basic education) as well as other donors.

1. Program Context, Development Objectives and Design

1.1   Context at Appraisal

1.       Burkina Faso has maintained a good macroeconomic track record despite significant economic
vulnerability and recent exogenous shocks. Over the last ten years, strong reserves of gold and a vibrant
cotton sector have supported a respectable annual economic growth rate averaging 5 percent. At the
sectoral level, continued growth is projected to come from a moderate expansion (4-6 percent per annum)
of cotton and agriculture, and through above average growth in the industry and service sectors (above 8
percent per annum). As measured by Country Policy and Institutional Assessment (CPIA) scores, Burkina
is a relatively good performer in terms of the quality of its policies and institutional arrangements. The
CPIA score rose from 3.7 in 2007 to 3.8 in 2011.

2.      Despite its good macroeconomic performance, Burkina Faso still remains one of the poorest
countries in the world. At US$447, per capita income remains significantly below the low-income
countries average of US$590. In a social context, the country’s indicators also rank below Sub-Saharan
averages. In 2005, literacy rates were 30 percent and life expectancy at birth was 43 years, compared to
35 percent and 46 years for the Sub-Saharan Africa region. According to the 2006 Human Development
Index (HDI), published by the United Nations Development Program (UNDP), Burkina Faso ranked 174
out of 177 countries in the index.

3.      Fiscal policy has been broadly in line with the targets set under the authorities' program and the
Government has complied with successive IMF fiscal targets and programs. Four successive programs
under the IMF’s Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) were implemented, with the last one
completed in September 2006. Despite a rise in public expenditure, partly due to exogenous shocks and
the limited domestic resource mobilization, the fiscal deficit (excluding grants and on a cash basis) was
11.6 percent of GDP, 2 percentage points less than the program target. External debt indicators worsened
in 2007 and the net present value of external debt reached 118 percent of exports in 2007, up from 97
percent in 2006. However, substantial and higher than expected inflows of foreign aid, increasingly on a
grant basis, helped contain Burkina Faso’s current account deficit. With the support of World Bank and
the European Union, grants rose to close to 7 percent of GDP in 2008.

4.       During the 2000-2006 period, the Government progressively increased the budgetary allocation
for basic services, in line with PRSP priorities. Poverty-reducing expenditures have increased by 21
percent, from CFAF 162 billion in 2005 to about CFAF 173 billion in 2006, of which education
represented roughly 42 percent. As was the case with the first PRSC series, the successful implementation
of the second PRSC series (PRSC4-6) has given room for deepening reforms in the economic growth
front and improving social indicators including governance in Burkina. In addition, these two previous
PRSC series were seen as providing an anchor for the Bank’s overall support to Burkina’s policy and
institutional reforms and public expenditure priorities.

5.      Against this background, a third series (PRSC 7-11) was approved in July 2007 to provide
budgetary support over the period 2007-11. The series was set in a context in which Burkina’s
macroeconomic performance had been broadly on track over the past years, but significant challenges to
macroeconomic and fiscal stability had emerged. The overall reforms program expectations under the
PRSC7-11 series were to help Burkina Faso’s government to have a significant positive impact on growth
and poverty reduction. This operation seeks to maintain macroeconomic stability through the cotton
sector and maintain the momentum of reforms to achieve critical developmental objectives of the PRSP-I.

1.2      Original Program Development Objectives (PDO) and Key Indicators

6.      The specific development objectives of the PRSC 7-11 series were to support the acceleration of
real growth and a reduction in the incidence of poverty in Burkina Faso through three pillars:

  (i)      Improving the investment climate and promoting exports: The PRSC series focused on actions to
           support the cotton sector and carry out longer term reforms to improve the sector’s overall
           competitiveness.

  (ii)     Improving/Protecting access to quality basic services: The PRSC series further operationalized
           decentralization in basic education and water and sanitation and by developing municipal
           investments to make progress toward the MDGs, and by improving access to basic services and
           ensuring greater efficiency of public expenditures on human capital formation.

  (iii) Promoting efficiency, transparency and accountability in the use of public resources: The PRSC
        series supported public financial management and governance through increased information on
        budget execution and transfers to local governments and through stronger external control
        institutions.

7.      The key indicators for such development objectives are stated in Annex 1: Project Development
Outcome Indicators. Objectives, policy reforms and indicators for the PRSC-7 to 11 series were based on
and aligned with the PRSP II, respectively the Priority Action Program (PAP, a policy document, which
updates the PRSP on an annual basis with all the stakeholders), and the CGAB joint performance matrix.

1.3 Revised PDO and Key Indicators, and Reasons/Justification

8.      The program development objectives remained consistent throughout the entire series and no new
objectives were added. However, minor revisions were made to the supporting pillars to help the country
to cope with the impact of the global economic downturn and the impact of exogenous food, fuel, and
financial shocks. Pillar II was altered slightly in PRSC-10 so that access to basic social services would be
“protected? as such access had already been “improved? through the previous credits in the series. In
PRSC-11, Pillar II was removed as that objective had already been achieved earlier in the series.

1.4 Original Policy Areas Supported by the Program

9.       Investment Climate and Competitiveness: The focus on improving the investment climate for
private sector reflected the findings of the Investment Climate Assessment (ICA 2006), and the 2006
Doing Business Report in which Burkina was ranked 154 out of 175 countries. These reports concluded
that the business climate was not generally favorable to private sector development in Burkina. The most
significant constraints were insufficient provision of infrastructure and high operating costs (electricity
and transport), regulatory barriers, dysfunctional factor markets and corruption. A preliminary DTIS trade
report, also showed that key reforms were necessary to increase and diversify exports by expanding
beyond cotton to reducing economic vulnerability.

10.     Service Delivery and Decentralization: The focus on social services reflected Burkina Faso’s
goal of achieving the eight Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) related to (1) eradicating extreme
poverty and hunger, (2) achieving universal primary education, (3) promoting gender equality and
empowering women, (4) reducing child mortality, (5) improving maternal health, (6) combating
HIV/AIDS, malaria and other diseases, (7) ensuring environmental sustainability, and (8) developing a
global partnership for development. In addition, the country’s decentralization efforts reflect the need to



                                                     2
increase poverty reduction efforts by strengthening the effectiveness and equity of service delivery
through results-based management and institutional capacity building.

11.     Public Financial Management and Governance: The focus on governance was intended to help
improve transparency, accountability and efficiency in budget processes, empower decentralized
structures to get more recognition and resources from the central level, and generate resources for
sustainable development.

1.5     Revised Policy Areas

12.      As the series moved from PRSC-7 through to PRSC-11 the main policy areas remained largely as
originally designed. There were some minor modifications to originally indicated prior actions and
triggers; however, these were not significant and did not represent substantive divergence from the
originally envisioned actions and triggers. In the overall series, about seven new indicators were
introduced to better align with the evolving monitoring framework for government’s PRSP in the form of
the Priority Action Program (PAP) and the joint Matrix of the CGAB-CSLP. These revisions reflect
efforts by the government and donors to gradually improve the link between policy actions and the PRSP
monitoring and evaluation framework by increasing the realism of the outcome and impact indicators.

1.6 Other Significant Changes:

13.    There were two significant changes in addition to the relatively minor policy modifications noted
above:

14.      The first significant change was additional resources of US$ 20 million under the Pilot Crisis
Response Window (CRW), which were set aside to help the Burkinabe authorities address the impact of
the global economic crisis. The global economic crisis has significantly affected the Burkina’s economy,
jeopardizing the viability of the cotton sector, weakening the country’s fiscal position, deepening poverty
incidence, and threatening to set back liberalization efforts of key productive sectors as a result of reduced
interest by potential foreign investors. Moreover, the impact of the global economic crisis was amplified
by severe floods in September 2009 in the Ouagadougou area. The inadequacy of existing social
protection institutions and policies, including social safety nets, has not helped the poor cope with recent
shocks.

15.     Without external financing to cover a significant portion of the economic crisis impact including
flood related financial demands, the fiscal position could have deteriorated further putting in jeopardy not
only the IDA-supported program under the PRSC-10, but the IMF program as well. Achievement of
several performance indicators under the PRSC-10 would clearly have been at risk in the absence of a
rapid and comprehensive response, including indicators related to: education outcomes, health outcomes,
agricultural production, and access to safe drinking water. While these indicators were, in all probability,
negatively affected by the disaster regardless of the adequacy of the response, a financial response from
the Bank was intended to limit under-performance and more importantly, reduce the medium and longer
term negative impacts that might be exacerbated by a prolonged crisis.

16.      The second change was related to an over optimism in some expected results selected at the
inception of PRSC-7. As price taker on the international market, Burkina Faso’s cotton exports are
subject to exposure to price fluctuation as well as harvest conditions. Therefore, some targets were below
those expected in the first year of the series. This was not linked to poor implementation of the actions
supported by the PRSC series, but because of unfavorable global economic conditions and other factors
well beyond the scope of the PRSC series. For the cotton sector and total export, this led to a restating of
expected results for PRSC-11.Given that the original results appeared to be well in-hand, the new results



                                                      3
were now significantly more ambitious but began to lag due to factors exogenous to the series, including
the food and fuel price crises, the global financial crisis and in 2009 the devastating floods.

2.      Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes

2.1     Program Performance

17.     Overall, the PRSC series clearly had strong value added as a successful instrument in: (1) helping
the government get financing support to operationalize its PRSP; (2) promoting support to authorities to
manage terms of trade shocks and weather crises; and (3) providing a framework for policy dialogue
between the Government and the Bank on key issues and providing for multi-donor forum to discuss
Burkina development challenges.

18.     Consistent with the 2005 CAS progress report, PRSC 7-11 were two tranches programmatic
development policy operations. The amount increased over the period from US$67.5 million equivalent
for the PRSC-7 to US$125 million for the PRSC-11. While PRSC-7 had a composite tranches of credit
(US$22.4 million) and grant (US$67.5 million), PRSC-8 was entirely a credit of US$100 million. PRSC-
9, 10 and 11 were grants of US$100 million, US$90 million and US$125 million respectively. Since
2007, development policy lending to Burkina Faso rose from around 45 percent of IDA commitments to
around 54 percent.

19.      All prior actions were met and show a consistent unfolding of the reform program designed to
support the implementation of the PRSP, ensuring a high level of continuity from operation to operation.
As the series moved from PRSC-7 through to PRSC-11 the main policy areas remained largely as
originally designed. There were some minor modifications to originally indicated prior actions and
triggers; however, these were not significant and did not represent substantive divergence from the
originally envisioned actions and triggers. In the cases where originally set targets were partly missed,
such as in the cotton and the private sector, the government either achieved the set targets shortly
afterwards (cotton sector), or made credible efforts to achieve them (number of foreign investors attracted
- where the complexity of issues took time to be resolved).

2.2      Major Factors Affecting Implementation:

20.     External Factors: As a landlocked country, Burkina Faso was subject to a number of external
shocks over the PRSC 7-11 implementation period that negatively affected performance. These external
influences were not evident at the outset of the series, were not expected and would have been difficult to


                                                        6
predict. These external influences included the fluctuation of the harvest over the period weakening the
agriculture performance mainly the cotton production. The appreciation of the euro during the period and
cotton price volatility internationally also had an impact on the cotton sector performance. The food and
fuel price increases of 2007/08, the global financial and economic crisis, and the political crisis in Ivory
Coast have been partly responsible of Burkina Faso’s economic downturn. Adding to these difficulties,
unprecedented social unrest in earlier 2011 resulted in a slow-down in the pace of reform.

21.     These factors had a considerable direct impact on outcome indicators. First, cotton production
levels were influenced considerably by global food prices, encouraging a shift away from cotton toward
food commodities. Second, high global petroleum prices increased the cost of agricultural inputs, most
importantly fertilizer prices, and further reduced yields and planted area in 2009 and 2010. Third, serious
flooding in 2009, despite an increase in cotton acreage planted, further reduced cotton production. Fourth,
the number of foreign investors attracted declined in 2009 as a result of the weak economy. Finally, the
protracted crisis in Cote d’Ivoire—historically the main transit route for Burkina Faso to global markets
and a source for remittances from migrants—contributed to high transport costs for the country’s trade,
had a negative impact on worker remittances, undermined competitiveness, and added to the pressures on
the balance of payments.

22.     Political Factors: While the political context was relatively stable over the period, the
entrenched political system in place was not necessarily conducive to the implementation of an ambitious
reform effort. There is degree of ‘political locking’ in the current political system in Burkina Faso
whereby the President and ruling party, the Congrès pour la Démocratie et le Progrès (CDP), have
maintained centralized control over the political arena for more than twenty years. The CDP holds an
overwhelming majority in the National Assembly and nearly three-quarters of the decentralized local
council seats. The likelihood of alternation and the competitiveness of the electoral process have also
been limited by the lack of an effective and credible opposition and low and declining voter turn-out.

23.      The Authorities have been successful in neutralizing opposition through cooptation, including the
allocation of ministerial posts to other parties and the integration of cultural leaders (ethnic and religious)
into the political leadership, with several serving as public officials elected or appointed to prominent
position. Burkina’s opposition is fragmented into over 140 parties with only a handful represented in the
National Assembly. The State Bureaucracy enjoys little political insulation and persistent clientelism has
fostered an environment that is conducive to corruption and under-performance as well as lack of
accountability on the part of civil servants. The judiciary and parliament exercise relatively weak controls
on executive action, and both institutions lack independence.

24.     In such a context, deep-seated reforms that had the potential to impact upon vested interests
received little to no engagement on the part of the government. These reforms included the introduction
of comprehensive Anti-Corruption legislation; the privatization of SOFITEX, the creation of a
Disciplinary Chamber within the Court of Accounts, and the abolition by hinterland countries of the
queuing system and other distortions in the transport sector.

25.     The social unrest in 2011 represented an unprecedented display of discontent with elements of the
existing political system. The incident destabilized the position of the incumbents and influenced the
adoption of institutional reforms, including the creation of a Senate and the inclusion of a judicial
representative on the Judicial Council. The 2011 unrest combined with the emergence of a new political
opposition party headed by a former Minister of Finance and internal changes to the composition of the
ruling party could have an impact on the outcomes of future legislative and municipal elections. A re-
balance of power at the legislative and municipal level could provide a better operating environment for
more ambitious reform efforts in the future.




                                                      7
26.      Donor Coordination: The design and implementation of this series of PRSCs were increasingly
coordinated with the budget support provided by other donors. This coordination took place through the
Cadre Général d’Organisation des Appuis Budgétaires (General Framework for Budget Support, CGAB)
established in 2005 and revised in 2011, which succeeded a less formalized coordination approach.
CGAB annual progress reports on PRSP implementation and the joint performance matrix provided a
framework to harmonize the efforts of Burkina Faso’s development partners with government’s strategy
and the close coordination between the government and CGAB members allowed for early entry points in
the policy dialogue. Examples would be the privatization process for SOFITEX but also the complex
decentralization reforms. Furthermore, close and continuous dialogue with the government allowed
CGAB members to better understand the constraints on the reform process (e.g., for cotton reform) and
build government ownership on the reform program. However, the restructuring of the Ministry of
Economy and Finance undertaken in 2007 has weakened the follow up of the line ministries reforms.

2.3     Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Design, Implementation and Utilization:

27.     The monitoring and evaluation framework for the PRSC-7 to 11 series was based on the PRSP
three-year forward looking Priority Action Program (PAP) and the annual assessment reports on PRSP
implementation (Annual Progress Reports, APR), issued by the government technical team, the SP-PPF.
The SP-PPF relies on such monitoring primarily for the Ministerial Steering and Monitoring Committee
(CMOS) which draws on the services of sector and thematic commissions (CSTs) to establish up-to-date
information on policy implementation and related statistical data. The CSTs in turn draw on decentralized
agencies at the regional Consultative Council (CCRD). Following an independent assessment after ten
years of PRSP implementation, the Government has adopted a revised monitoring and evaluation
framework with the objective to strengthen it and also insure the ownership by the line ministries. The
main change of this revised framework was the improvement in the dialogue with civil society and private
sector. The objectives and measures of the PRSC series were also increasingly aligned with the CGAB
performance matrix. Discrepancies between the two matrices indicate inconsistencies between the
preparation calendar of the PRSCs and the multi-donor review process (see lessons learned), a problem
which is being addressed. The PRSP and PRSC monitoring and evaluation framework drew on work
undertaken and reports issued by the National Institute for Statistics and Demography (INSD). While the
most recent household survey is from 2009/2010, information is updated through annually conducted
surveys on Core Welfare Indicators (CWIQ). Other sectoral data on agriculture, education, health, water
and other areas are either produced as part of a regular schedule by the government. In Primary education,
evaluation of learning outcomes assessment is done on bi- annual basis since 2005 and has been improved
to consider gender issues. Concerning health, the Demographic and Health Survey (DHS) has been
updated in 2010.

28.      Regarding the agricultural sector, a permanent survey is done on an annual basis to assess the
level of farmers ‘equipment, crops production and productivity. In relation to financial management side,
two review meetings held by the steering committee composed by government’s representatives, donors
and civil society representatives lead to an annual review providing key elements in assessing public
financial management. All these survey and reviews were used to help evaluate PRSP implementation. In
addition to that two mandatory sessions are reserved per annum for a joint assessment by CGAB
members and the government on reform measures and their status. One formal session is held at the time
of the annual review of the PRSP progress report in late April. A second session is held during late
September/early October to allow for a mid-term review. Beyond these two sessions, a high level
dialogue between the Prime Minister and Donors is organized on an annual basis to deal with strategic
issues such as good governance and procurement, among others.

29.     Despite the merging of the Ministry of Economy and Development and the Ministry of Finance in
2007, which has resulted in weakening the monitoring and follow up of the PRSP framework, the annual
reviews of the PRSP have continuously been taking place. The government has restructured the Ministry


                                                    8
of Economy and Finance by strengthening its policy planning unit to make it more functional and fulfill
accountability demands from internal and external stakeholders. However, the M&E have improved the
next year under the PRSC-9 to 11 with respect to coverage and quality. The revised CGAB-CSLP
framework signed jointly by the Government and the Donors has improved the dialogue of the PRSP
implementation. Moreover government’s ownership in the PRSP process greatly contributed to improve
the M&E framework. Good quality APRs have been completed for each year during PRSC
implementation. Also, the alignment of the PRSCs’ outcome indicators with the government’s monitoring
process, in the form of the annual PRSP review/PAP within the CGAB framework, facilitated monitoring
of the series implementation for all participants and strengthened government ownership of the reforms
supported by the PRSC series. Other improvements in M&E implementation include better dissemination
of the APRs to Parliamentarians, regional planning and operation officers, and rural communities.

30.     However, following the independent evaluation of the ten years implementation of the CSLP, the
government has taken the lead in moving forward with the M&E system. The main change was the
emphasis on sectoral dialogue. A guide for sectoral strategies was adopted by the Council of Ministers
aiming at consolidating policy dialogue at this level. On donors’ side, a revised CGAB-CSLP framework
has been signed jointly by the Government and the nine donors providing general and sectoral budget
support to Burkina by 24th February 2012. A ruling troika has been set up as principle guiding the
dialogue between the Government and the donors. These principles have been mentioned in the revised
CGAB framework signed jointly by Government and Donors providing budget support as aid modality to
Burkina. The new M&E system has been applied to the first annual review of the SCADD.

31.     Going forward, further improving the M&E framework will require strengthening the capacity of
the (Cadre de Dialogue sectorial) CDS. The commission members rely on work done by research and
planning units in ministries or other relevant government units. The quality of the work in these units
varies, with some data not being provided on a timely basis. The government has recognized this
weakness and taken action in 2011 to improve CDS operations. In addition, the M&E framework for the
next series of PRSCs could gradually increase its focus on qualitative improvements (such as efficiency
for basic services and agricultural production) as well as further streamline the indicators coverage.
Finally, the annual statistics on poverty need to be documented and support the achievements of the
SCADD.

2.4     Expected Next Phase/Follow-up Operation (if any):

32.      Building on the PRSC-7 to 11 series, the CGC1-to 4 progressively focused on challenging growth
objectives, notably diversification, competitiveness and efficiency, as well as tackling governance issues.
The series is being followed by a fourth series of four annual one tranche CGCs (CGC 1 to 4) covering
the period 2012-2014. The new series continues to be closely aligned with the SCADD as the objectives
consisting in deepening the reforms introduced in this series by catalyzing private sector growth and
employment, improving governance and public resources management, and build resilience and reduce
vulnerability are well aligned to both the government strategy and the CAS framework. The first
operation in the series, CGC1-4 (US$90 million equivalent), was approved by the Board in May 2012.
The design of the new series was coordinated with other donors of the CGAB framework. This CGC
series incorporated key lessons from the previous PRSC as noted in this ICR.

3. Assessment of Outcomes

3.1 Relevance of Objectives, Design and Implementation

33.     The overall objectives of the PRSC7-11 were highly relevant and consistent with the country
global development prospective “Burkina 2025? and the poverty strategy CSLP endorsed by the
parliament in 2004. The triggers and subsequent prior actions supported were also directly relevant to the
development challenges facing Burkina Faso and the Government’s efforts to address these challenges.
The PRSC-7 to 11 objectives supported 3 out of 4 objectives of the CAS FY06-09 and the CAS FY10-12
and were closely aligned with PRSP objectives. Particularly notable in this respect was the series’ support
for implementing the second phase of the national action plans in education and health sectors, as well as
the government’s Program to Strengthen Financial Management.

34.     In addition to its alignment with the country priorities, the design of PRSC-7 to 11 was also
increasingly aligned with the budget cycle and the support provided by other donors through the General
Framework for Budget Support in Burkina Faso (CGAB). The CGAB coordination framework allowed
for close monitoring and assessment of the PRSC series’ objectives and outcomes, thus ensuring
continuous relevance of PRSC objectives and measures supporting such objectives.

In terms of implementation, prior actions show a consistent unfolding of the reform program designed to
support the implementation of the PRSP, and ensuring a high level of continuity from operation to
operation. In the cases where originally set targets were partly missed, such as in the cotton and the
private sector, the government either achieved the set targets shortly afterwards (cotton sector), or made
credible efforts to achieve them (number of foreign investors attracted - where the complexity of issues
took time to be resolved). The channeling of a large share of resources through the budget, instead of
through projects, strengthened decision-making by the Ministry of Finance, in conjunction with sector
Ministries, Cabinet, and Parliament, and enhanced sound financial management and accountability.
Against legislative and presidential elections respectively in 2007 and 2010, implementation continued
broadly as satisfactory showing the high degree of ownership and the appropriately chosen design for the
series.

titling issuance law, implementing the pilot land tenure security program in 26 targeted provinces,
adoption of text to enhance flexibility in the hours and schedule work including a removal of the
limitation in renewal of terms contracts in some sectors; adopting perennial legislation for land titling
issuance and execute the new dispositions through the establishment of a one-stop shop for land titling
and a one-stop shop for construction permits; supporting to business association of Burkina (Maison de
l’Entreprise du Burkina), improving services to private sector through a decentralized units of CEFORE,
and adopting the PPP strategy facilitating freight transportation industry while preventing overloading of
trucks so as protected roads infrastructure and improving road safety. The investment lending operations
under the Competitiveness and Enterprise Development Project (PACDE) and the Burkina Faso
Agricultural Productivity and Food Security Project (PAPSA), and to some extend the Community Based
Rural Development Project (PNGT), have been supportive through technical assistance as well as studies
that have contributed an improvement in the investment climate in Burkina. The complementarity
between the investment lending and the development policy operations was exemplary in Burkina in light
of the achievements in this area.

39.      The objectives of improving the investment climate measured by the number of foreign investors
attracted, processing time for legally starting a business, and number of newly established firms were
satisfactorily achieved. For the first indicator, a benchmark of 214 foreign investors has increased to 327
in 2011. In the same vein, almost 5000 new firms have been created in 2011 compared to 3,189 in 2007.
Processing time for starting a business declined satisfactorily from 34 days and 8 procedures in 2007 to
just 14 days and 4 procedures in 2011. In the energy sector, some improvement was noted in the public
electricity company (SONABEL) and rural electrification has been broadly accessible to populations
which has widened the scope of electrification from 27.42% in 2011 against 26.10% in 2010.

40.      All these improvements have led to a better ranking Burkina as one of the top reformers of the
Doing Business Indicators (163/175 in 2007, 161/178 in 2008, 148/181 in 2009 147/181 in 2010 and
150/183 in 2011). According to the 2013 Doing Business Report, Burkina Faso ranks 153 out of 185
countries, reflecting no major improvements as compared to last year where the country undertook
significant reforms in four indicators, especially in terms of closing a business, where the country jumped
11 spots. All these reforms have resulted in ranking Burkina Faso among the 4 most consistent
reformers in the world.

41.     In terms of export diversification, the prior actions and triggers supporting Pillar I were
appropriate and timely. By establishing a new transitory VAT arrears reimbursement mechanism for
exporting enterprises, by restructuring the management of the refrigerated slaughterhouse in
Ouagadougou, including strong reforms in cotton sector, the real exports grew from -1.4% in 2007 to
37.5% in 2011. Exports also improved of key tradables, including gold which increased from 220 Kg in
2007 to 9,523 Kg in 2011, meat which increased from 2.0% in 2007 to 11.2% in 2011, and cotton which
increased by 38.2 percent in 2009 (before falling by 16.8 percent in 2011).

42.      The outcome on cotton is also appreciable as the Government has implemented key reforms to
achieve competitiveness even though some of the prior actions were reformulated. Cotton sector reforms
has progressed based on the consolidation of the price-setting mechanism through the contracting of an
independent agency taking full responsibility for implementing the formula and calculating the farm gate
price each year; the contracting of a credit rating agency to provide rating advice for SOFITEX; the
production of certified accounts for SOFITEX’s activity in 2008; and the implementation of SOFITEX‘s
restructuring plan to reduce the company’s operating costs, as adopted in 2008.

44.      Progress in opening the economy and developing the private sector has led to annual growth rates
of over 5 percent over the past five years. However, systemic weaknesses in business freedom, property
rights, and investment freedom persist. Extensive regulations hinder entrepreneurial activity, and the
weak judicial system fuels corruption. Also, a corruption perception under Transparency International
index ranked 3 as a result of complaints against judiciary, police, and customs.

3.3 Justification of Overall Outcome Rating

PRSC-7-11: Satisfactory

58.      The overall outcome rating of the PRSC 7-11 series is satisfactory. As measured by the
indicators, the operations’ development objectives were broadly achieved. Objectives, design,
implementation, and outcomes were relevant to country circumstances and development priorities, as well
as to the Bank strategy for the country. The government has continued implementation of the reforms in
the context of the five series of PRSCs, and the sustainability of the reforms is likely over the medium
term.

59.      However, given the economic, social and political context at the moment of implementation, a
series of significant external shocks affected the extent to which development outcomes were reached.
While almost all of the indicators were reached or showed improvement, the influence of these external
factors has had a dampening effect on the extent to which progress was made. As a result, prior actions
and triggers were modified over the series to account for some of these dislocations. Although it is
difficult to assess, had the PRSC series not been in place, the effects of the exogenous shocks may have
more severely affected the country’s growth and poverty reduction goals.

3.4 Overarching Themes, Other Outcomes and Impacts

(a) Poverty Impacts, Gender Aspects, and Social Development

60.     The main objective of PRSC 7-11 was to promote real growth and to reduce poverty. The target
in terms of the global poverty incidence was 44% for 2010. It follows from the Burkina Faso Poverty
Note that there has been significant reduction in poverty between 2003 and 2009/2010. The national
poverty incidence was estimated at 46.7% in 2009/2010, which represents a decline by 4 percentage
points compared to 2003. Declines were also observed in poverty gaps and the severity of poverty, with
about 4 and 2.5 percentage points respectively. The rural areas have benefited most from the decline
while poverty rates appear to have stagnated in urban ones. The national level estimates of inequality
suggest that inequality decreased slightly between 2003 and 2009, respectively from 43% to 40%. In
general, urban inequality seems higher than rural inequality, which might explain the persistence of urban
poverty during 2000s despite the steady growth. Moreover, data from INSD show that unemployment rate
(based on ILO definition) in urban areas increased slightly from 8.6% in 2007 to 9.2% in 2010. This
might be another reason why poverty did not decline in urban areas. The Human development index
(HDI) confirms the downward trend of poverty since this index increased monotonically from 0.313 in
2007 to 0.331 in 2011.

61.      The agricultural sector remains the most affected by poverty with a poverty incidence of 53.8%
against only 7.6% for public sector. Vulnerability is another issue influenced by the fact that economic
activities are highly dependent on exogenous shocks (insufficient rainfall, drought, food shortage and
famine, price increase), especially in agricultural sector. A vulnerability analysis in Burkina Faso shows
that, in 2009, price increase was the main shock for about 40% of urban households; however, in the rural

                                                   17
areas the main issue was drought and insufficient rainfall. The analysis shows that, out of the 46.7% of
poor, about two thirds, which represent 31% of the total population, are likely to suffer from chronic
poverty. Also, from the 53.3% of non-poor, a certain proportion representing 14% of the total population
have a great probability to fall into poverty. This means that about 45% of the Burkina’s population were
vulnerable in 2009. It also follows from the vulnerability analysis that severe food insecurity, based on
self-reported measure, was increase by 3.2 percentage points from 2003 being at 21.4% in 2009.

(b) Institutional Change/Strengthening

62.     The institutional development impact of the policy reforms actions implemented under the PRSC
7-11 series was marginal. The series has been continuously implemented despite some weaknesses in
line ministries and the discrepancies in household survey to assess the poverty impact of the reforms.
Nevertheless, the capacity of the Ministry of Economy and Finance to identify and monitor key policy
reforms across sectoral ministries was strengthened during the 2009 revision of the M&E system.
Accordingly, donors have implemented the Accra’s agenda throughout the CGAB framework by aligning
and harmonizing their support to the country’s priorities and budget cycle. Also, the series supported
important institutional changes with the involvement of private sector in the cotton sector and the setup of
Maison de l’Entreprise to improve private sector coordination. The decentralization process, which
improved the delivery of basic social services at the local level, has been progressively implemented.

None

4. Assessment of Risk to Development Outcome

Rating: Moderate

63.    There are several external risks that may affect the sustainability of the PRSC 7-11 development
outcomes, which based on their likelihood and impact, are rated as moderate.

64.     Macroeconomic Risk: Burkina Faso is exposed to economic risks from a recession in Europe
both through changes in commodity prices and through potential volatility from its exchange rate peg to
the Euro. Agriculture, cotton and mining remain vulnerable to adverse exogenous shocks. Since Burkina
is heavily dependent on revenues from gold and cotton exports, commodity price shocks affect export
revenues. An increase in oil imports may also put pressure on the country’s balance of payments position.
Moreover, since the CFAF is pegged to the euro, a shock to the euro system can have ripple effects on the
CFAF economies. The recession in Europe increases the risks of a decline in bilateral aid, which may
generate negative effects on public investment, growth, and debt sustainability.

65.      Political Economy Risk: The regional crisis in Mali is having a strong effect on Burkina Faso,
its southeastern neighbor. First, there have been about 37,000 refugees that have left Mali and are being
harbored in Burkina Faso as of February 2013, aggravating existing problems with food security and
putting strong pressure on scarce humanitarian resources. Second, the conflict in Mali can adversely
impact Burkina via greater inflows of illegal arms into the country. A third risk could emerge in the
medium-term if the Constitution were to be revised to end presidential term limits as this could create
political tensions.

66.     Climate Change Risk: Climate fluctuations have a marked effect on Burkinabe agriculture,
particularly cotton, and therefore on rural livelihoods. As a landlocked country situated in the Sahel,
Burkina Faso suffers from an extreme and variable rainfall with the possibility of both flooding and
drought within a few months. Thus, weather-related shocks may affect the speed of implementation of the


                                                    18
reforms. The 2012 food crisis due to Sahelian drought took its toll on the country. Sustainable
management of the price smoothing fund and actions taken to improve food security will help to mitigate
these risks.

67.     To deal with the external shocks, Burkina has committed to relevant reforms, and the President
continues to be engaged with the parliament. Also, donor commitment remains strong, which augurs well
for sustained inflows of external assistance. The donor community has mobilized funds to provide the
Burkina government the requisite financing to handle the conflict and address the humanitarian crisis.
The increasing involvement of civil society in the PRSP and the SCADD process and, the growing use of
participatory M&E, make sustainability of the development outcomes more likely. Future budget support
operations will help strengthen state capacity to deal with the shocks.

6. Lessons Learned

77.      The main lesson from the PRSC series in Burkina is that budget support can be an effective
instrument for governments to finance their development programs and to weather economic shocks. In
the context of diverse exogenous shocks, it is particularly necessary to maintain operational flexibility in
light of changing environment which enhances the team’s ability to adequately respond. As a result of
reforms in the public sector, the Government was able to maintain a stable macroeconomic framework,
with low inflation and strong growth. A review of the PRSCs from 2007 to 2011 indicates a positive
contribution to the implementation of the PRSP through the financing of government annual programs
and the promotion of sectoral reform. The main contributions include cotton sector and mining sector
reforms, support to public sector reform that resulted in increased government ownership, and gains in
priority sectors (health, education, and rural development).

78.     A second lesson in the success of sectoral reforms is linked to understanding the political
economy. Cotton reform was successful because of a positive consensus between the cotton ginning
companies, the farmers associations, and the government ministries. Similarly, mining sector reform has
benefited from shared consensus between the government and the companies regarding the development
of the sector. Finally, public sector reform has benefited from a group of technocrats who have been
invested in reforming the system to improve its efficiency.

79.      A third lesson from the PRSC series in Burkina Faso is the need to be selective and strategic in
scope - narrowing the choice and number of policy reforms to support - but ambitious in depth of reforms
and their significance for economic growth, employment, and poverty reduction. The PRSC did an
important job in ensuring that there has been reform momentum. The institutional foundations for reform
have been quite successful, particularly in relation to strengthening the cotton sector and improving the
management of public finances. However, a lesson from policy in Burkina Faso in recent years has been
that in the absence of broad-based economic growth and job creation, macro stability and social programs
will not on their own have sufficient impact on poverty indicators. The lesson is that more focus needs to
be oriented on obstacles to private-sector job creation, such as financing for small and medium
enterprises, managing the volatility of input costs in the labor-intensive cotton sector, as well as
supporting ambitious reforms with far-reaching consequences for economic governance, for example
through improved justice sector performance. In terms of poverty reduction, it is necessary to focus on



                                                    21
strong capital formation in agriculture, greater competitiveness along the transit corridors, enhanced
safety and stability, and accelerated regional integration.

80.     A fourth lesson is that more focus needs to be put into statistical capacity-building in order to
strengthen monitoring and evaluation systems. There is a lack of annual survey data to better track
poverty trends. Statistical capacity can be strengthened at the INSD, the key statistical institution. The
country needs well-targeted training and capacity building programs to help develop better monitoring
and evaluation, which in turn can improve the efficacy of government programs.

81.      A fifth lesson from past lending is the importance of trying to provide finance linked to the
government’s own budget cycle. Past operations have at times been subject to delay, with the result that
financial resources could arrive late in the government fiscal year, hindering government planning and
resource use. Responding to this concern, the proposed series is designed to deliver two operations in
swift succession (subject to the pace of reforms being maintained) and to allow the series to better align
its operations with the beginning of the government’s budget cycle.

82.     A sixth lesson relates to the importance of donor coordination. The experience of Burkina
suggests that a well-institutionalized system of donor coordination can facilitate the aid management for
the country and ensure better development outcomes. The current framework allows the aid agencies to
reach a consensus on the aid strategy and prior actions by working closely with each other and with the
government in order to ensure a harmonized effort.

    I-      CONTEXTE DE LA MISE EN PLACE DES CASRP

Les premiers crédits d’appui à la stratégie de réduction de la pauvreté de l’IDA, à l’instar des autres
programmes d’appui budgétaires des autres pays développés engagés au début du 3ème millénaire, ont été
conçus pour appuyer le Cadre stratégique de lutte contre la pauvreté (CSLP) adopté en 2000 par le
Burkina Faso pour servir de cadre de référence pour les interventions du gouvernement et de ses
partenaires en la matière.

La première génération de ces financements a eu du mal à s’ancrer dans l’administration publique en
raison d’une part, d’une préférence marquée de la plupart des secteurs ministériels pour les projets alors
en grand nombre dans le pays, avec quelquefois la complicité des chargés de projets au sein des agences
des pays donateurs et des institutions multilatérales de développement. Cependant la ténacité des
pionniers de cette modalité d’aide a fini par convaincre les acteurs de part et d’autre de son efficacité en
tant qu’outil de financement du développement, notamment en raison de son caractère non ciblé et des
résultats enregistrés sur le terrain.
L’appropriation des programmes a constitué la principale faiblesse des CASRP, en raison notamment de
résistances rencontrées tant au niveau du gouvernement qu’au sein des équipes de l’IDA.
Du côté de la Banque, et tout particulièrement des équipes sectorielles, habituées depuis toujours aux
projets dont l’existence est l’essence de leur emploi, l’appui budgétaire venait en quelque sorte remettre
en cause le fondement de leur présence à la banque, avec des risques réels de réduction voire de
suppression de privilèges acquis.
Du côté du gouvernement, les multiples unités de projets mis en place ça et là ont créé une nouvelle race
d’agents publics, avec des privilèges assurément exorbitants au regard du statut de la fonction publique.
Pour cette catégorie, le développement de l’appui budgétaire constituait également une menace pour les
avantages acquis et toute action de nature à freiner cette nouvelle modalité d’aide était la bienvenue.
L’avènement de la déclaration de Rome sur l’harmonisation de l’aide en 2000 et, plus encore, celui de la
Déclaration de Paris sur l’efficacité de l’aide auxquelles le Burkina Faso a souscrit, avait suscité un grand
espoir parmi les adeptes de l’aide budgétaire. Saisissant cette opportunité et à la faveur de la signature en
2005 du Cadre général d’organisation des appuis budgétaires en soutien à la mise en œuvre du cadre
stratégique de lutte contre la pauvreté (CGAB-CSLP) entre le gouvernement et l’ensemble de ses
donateurs d’aide budgétaire, l’IDA a été interpellée sur la nécessité de veiller à assurer une cohérence et
une coordination de ses futurs programmes d’appui budgétaire avec les grands principes du CGAB-CSLP
auquel elle était partie prenante.
Il s’agissait d’éviter les processus parallèles dans le profilage du programme d’AB qui constituaient
véritablement une source d’affaiblissement du cadre de dialogue sur l’appui budgétaire (CGAB-CSLP) et,
de façon plus générale, du dialogue sur les finances publiques à travers le dispositif de pilotage de la
stratégie de renforcement des finances publiques au niveau sectoriel.


    II-     ACQUIS DE LA TROISIEME SERIE D’OPERATIONS

La troisième série des CASRP, engagée avec le septième financement, a visé trois objectifs principaux i)
améliorer le climat des investissements et promouvoir les exportations ; ii) améliorer l’accès aux services
sociaux de base décentralisés et iii) promouvoir l’efficacité, la transparence et la responsabilité de
l’utilisation des ressources publiques.
Sur la base des données disponibles, les mesures et actions soutenues par les différents financements de
ladite série ont permis d’apporter des réponses idoines aux préoccupations essentielles dans les domaines
visés.

                                                     32
Ainsi, le climat des affaires et de l’investissement, déjà en nette amélioration avec les réformes
entreprises dans le cadre du programme ″doing business better″, a connu de nouvelles avancées avec entre
autres l’assouplissement par le gouvernement des conditions de délivrance des titres fonciers et des
permis de construire avec la mise en place de guichets uniques à cet effet (GUF ou guichets unique du
foncier et CEFAC ou Centre de formalités des actes de construire) et la déconcentration de la maison de
l’entreprise pour mieux répondre aux besoins des créateurs d’entreprises. On peut également signaler les
initiatives prises pour assurer plus de flexibilité du marché du travail dans des secteurs comme celui des
mines, en plein développement, et réglementer la production et la distribution d’énergie électrique.
Pour la promotion des exportations, diverses mesures ont été mise en œuvre. Il convient de relever la
révision du mécanisme de remboursement des crédits de TVA pour les entreprises exportatrices, etc.
Concernant ce dernier volet, il semble, selon les bénéficiaires, que le dispositif en place actuellement au
Burkina Faso serait l’un des plus performants de la sous-région ;
Dans le secteur coton secouée depuis 2007 par des crises d’une profondeur sans précédent, la présence
constante de la Banque aux côtés des acteurs de l’association interprofessionnelle a été un catalyseur dans
la mobilisation des pouvoirs publics et des autres partenaires de la filière pour assainir l’environnement de
la filière à travers d’une part sa restructuration et le soutien des producteurs, et d’autre part la révision du
mécanisme de détermination du prix au producteur pour l’aligner davantage aux cours mondiaux.
Concernant le deuxième objectif visé, à savoir améliorer l’accès aux services sociaux décentralisés,
l’action de l’IDA a été décisive pour impulser le transfert effectif des compétences et des ressources aux
collectivités territoriales, en poussant au démarrage du processus dans les domaines de l’éducation de
base (préscolaire et primaire) dans les quarante neuf (49) communes urbaines communes. Le processus
qui a été étendu par la suite au secteur de la santé et à celui de l’eau et de l’assainissement, couvre à ce
jour, dans les trois domaines concernés, l’ensemble des trois cent cinquante et une communes du pays.
Par ailleurs, le montant des dotations directement transférées à ce titre aux communes, qui s’accroît
d’année en année, est passé de 8,2 milliards de francs CFA en 2010 à 12,5 milliards en 2012 et est prévu à
plus de 20 milliards en 2013. Ce transfert de compétences et de ressources a contribué à l’amélioration
des indicateurs dans les secteurs sociaux de base enregistrée au cours de la période couverte par la
troisième série de CASRP.
Dans le domaine de la gestion des finances publiques, la banque mondiale a, depuis la première série de
CASRP, soutenu le gouvernement dans ses efforts pour accroître l’efficacité, la transparence et la
responsabilité dans la gestion des ressources publiques. C’est en effet avec son appui dans le cadre d’un
projet d’appui à l’administration que le gouvernement a engagé au milieu des années 90 le processus de
modernisation de ses outils de gestion budgétaire. Cet engagement de l’institution a continué au début des
années 2000 avec un soutien pour la mise en place des instruments de programmation et d’évaluation des
dépenses publiques (Cadre des dépenses à moyen terme, budget programme, revue des dépenses
publiques, etc.). L’appropriation de ces instruments par l’administration économique et financière a
permis d’améliorer considérablement le processus de préparation du budget et de renforcer
progressivement le lien d’une part, entre le budget et les priorités des politiques et stratégies sectorielles,
et d’autre part, entre les moyens mis en œuvre et les résultats attendus.
Un autre volet des finances publiques qui a retenu une attention particulière de la banque dans le cadre de
la mise en œuvre de la troisième série de CASRP est le secteur des marchés publics, avec notamment
l’opérationnalisation de la grande réforme entreprise en 2008 qui s’est traduite en particulier par la
séparation des fonctions de régulation et de contrôle, dévolues respectivement à l’Autorité de régulation
des marchés publiques (ARMP) et à la Direction générale des marchés publics (DGMP). Cette réforme
est marquée en outre par la déconcentration de la DGMP dans les ministères et les régions et la création
de personnes responsables de marchés au sein des ministères, pour permettre plus de célérité dans le
traitement de la commande publique. La banque a activement soutenu ce processus de déconcentration
qui a véritablement contribué à améliorer l’exécution de la dépense publique en général.
Le respect de l’obligation de compte rendu a été également enregistré des progrès sous l’impulsion des
mesures soutenues par les CASRP. Au nombre des actions majeures pouvant être relevées à cet égard

                                                      33
figurent la production régulière des rapports publics des institutions supérieures de contrôle,
respectivement juridictionnel (Cour des comptes) et de l’ordre administratif (Autorité supérieure de
contrôle d’Etat), le renforcement du contrôle du service fait par les structures de contrôle a priori, etc.
Initialement prévue pour trois opérations comme les précédentes, la troisième série de financement a
été prolongée avec la mise en place de deux nouvelles opérations exceptionnelles respectivement en 2010
et en 2011.
L’opération de 2010 visait à soutenir les efforts du gouvernement dans la gestion des effets des
inondations du 1er septembre 2009 et de la crise économique et financière mondiale, à travers un plan
d’action consolidé élaboré à cet effet articulé et comportant entre autres, la réhabilitation des
infrastructures détruites, la relance de la production agricole, la mise en place d’un programme spécial
pour l’emploi des jeunes et la mise en œuvre d’un programme de filets sociaux au profit des ménages
vulnérables. L’intervention de la banque a offert au gouvernement un espace budgétaire élargi pour une
prise en charge des importants besoins de financement du plan d’action.
Quant au cinquième financement de la série, Il a été mis en place au moment où le gouvernement
finalisait son nouveau référentiel de développement, à savoir la Stratégie de croissance accélérée et de
développement durable (SCADD). L’IDA, qui a soutenu activement l’élaboration de ce référentiel qui
met en avant la nécessité de réaliser une croissance forte et soutenue dans le temps pour parvenir à une
réduction significative de la pauvreté, a décidé à travers cette opération de s’aligner sur l’agenda du
gouvernement dans la mise en œuvre de la SCADD. C’est donc tout naturellement qu’elle a adossé son
financement à des mesures dans le sens de conforter les acquis enregistrés des quatre précédentes
opérations et de favoriser la réalisation des ambitions de la SCADD.


    III-    APPRECIATION DE LA MISE EN ŒUVRE DES CASRP

L’instrument CASRP, bien que basé sur des mesures de politique, a grandement contribué à ancrer la
culture du résultat dans les administrations publiques impliquées dans sa mise en œuvre. L’utilisation
aujourd’hui généralisée des CDMT et du budget programme dans le processus budgétaire a
considérablement amélioré la qualité du budget de l’Etat et renforcé ses liens avec les priorités des
politiques sectorielles. L’instauration de mécanismes d’évaluation périodique de la mise en œuvre des
plans d’actions des politiques et stratégies nationales et sectorielles a permis de renforcer l’obligation de
compte rendu au citoyen, qui peut désormais apprécier l’impact des interventions du gouvernement.
A l’évidence, la compréhension de l’instrument CASRP a positivement évolué tant au niveau du
gouvernement qu’au sein de l’IDA, ainsi que l’illustre la régularité, le nombre et le volume des
opérations. L’administration publique s’est largement approprié l’appui budgétaire en tant que modalité
de délivrance de l’aide, et plus particulièrement le CASRP et sa mécanique de mise en place.
Si l’on peut se réjouir de la flexibilité du CASRP qui la caractérise par rapport aux autres instruments
d’appui budgétaire, il y a lieu de noter que sa prévisibilité s’est réduite au fil des ans, en raison
principalement des difficultés rencontrées dans la définition des déclencheurs, pas toujours alignés avec la
matrice de décaissement convenue dans le cadre du CGAB. L’on perd beaucoup de temps dans le design
des opérations, conduisant à des incertitudes sur les décaissements. En raison du calendrier d’arrêt des
opérations budgétaires du fait du principe d’annualité du budget, la non mobilisation à bonne date des
ressources des CASRP a des conséquences néfastes sur l’exécution du budget et, partant, sur l’atteinte des
objectifs du gouvernement, en particulier dans les secteurs sociaux. La banque et le gouvernement se
doivent de travailler à remédier à cela dans le cadre des opérations futures.

    I-      CONTEXT FOR THE IMPLEMENTATION OF THE PRSC

The first Poverty Reduction Support Credits from IDA, similar to other budget support programs of
developed countries involved at the beginning of the 3rd millennium, were designed to support the Poverty
Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP) which was adopted in 2000 by Burkina Faso to serve as a reference
framework for the government’s actions and its partners in the field.

The first generation of these funding has struggled to become established into the public administration
due to, on the one hand, a clear preference from most of the government sectors for projects in great
numbers in the country, sometimes with the complicity of task team leaders within the agencies of donor
countries and multilateral development institutions. However, the tenacity of the pioneers of this support
modality has finally convinced the stakeholders on both sides of its effectiveness as a financing tool for
development, particularly because of its non-targeted nature and the results achieved on the field.
The ownership of the programs was the main weakness of the PRSC, partly because of the resistance
encountered at both the government and the IDA team levels.
As for the Bank, and especially the sector teams, which were accustomed from the beginning to projects
whose existence is the essence of their employment, budget support was somehow questioning the basis
of their existence in the Bank, with real risks of reduction or even removal of the privileges granted.
On the government’s side, several units of projects implemented here and then created a new breed of
public officials, with outrageous privileges certainly in terms of the status of the public administration.
For this category, the development of the budget support was also a threat to the acquired advantages and
any action likely to stop this new type of support was welcome.
The advent of the Rome Declaration on Aid Harmonization in 2000 and, more importantly, that of the
Paris Declaration on Aid Effectiveness to which Burkina Faso has subscribed, had raised great hopes
among followers of the budget support. On this occasion and for the signing in 2005 of the General
Framework for Budget Support for the implementation of the Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (CGAB-
PRSP) between the government and all its budget support donors, IDA was questioned on the need to
ensure coherence and coordination of future budget support programs with the main principles of CGAB-
PRSP in which it was involved.
The aim was to avoid parallel processes in the streamlining of the budget support program which
constituted a genuine source of weakness for the framework for dialogue on budget support (CGAB-
PRSP) and, more generally, the dialogue on public finances through the steering group of the strategy for
the strengthening of public finances at the sector level.


    II-     ACHIEVEMENTS OF THE THIRD SERIES OF OPERATIONS

The third series of PRSC, which started with the seventh funding, had three main objectives, namely i)
improving the investment climate and promoting exports; ii) improving access to basic and decentralized
social services; iii) promoting efficiency, transparency and accountability in the use of public resources.
Given the available data, the measures and actions supported by the different funding of the
aforementioned series enabled to provide answers suitable to the key concerns in the areas targeted.
Thus, the business and investment climate, which has already shown a significant improvement with the
reforms carried out within the program entitled "Doing Business Better", has seen new progress with,
amongst others, the easing by the government of the conditions for issuing land titles and building permits
with the establishment of one-stop shops for this purpose (GUF or “guichets unique du foncier? and
CEFAC or “Centre de formalités des actes de construire?) and the devolution of the Maison de


                                                    36
l’entreprise (a one-stop shop for business registration) to better meet the requirements of entrepreneurs. It
may also include the steps taken to ensure a greater flexibility of the labor market in sectors such as
mining, which is booming, and regulate the production and distribution of electrical energy.
Different measures have been implemented for export promotion. The revision of the reimbursement
mechanism of VAT credits for exporting companies, etc. should be noted. Regarding this last point, it
seems that, according to the beneficiaries, the system currently in place in Burkina Faso is one of the best
in the sub-region.
In the cotton sector, which has been affected by unprecedented crises since 2007, the constant presence of
the Bank alongside the stakeholders of the interprofessional association has been a catalyst in mobilizing
governments and other partners in the sector to improve the environment of the sector through, on the one
hand, its restructuring and the support from the producers, and, on the other hand, the revision of the
mechanism for determining producer prices to further align it with world prices.
Regarding the second objective targeted, namely to improve access to decentralized social services, the
action from IDA was critical to encourage the efficient transfer of skills and resources to local authorities,
by pushing at the beginning of the process in the areas of basic education (preschool and elementary) in
forty-nine (49) shared urban municipalities. The process, which was later extended to the health sector, as
well as to the water and sanitation sector, covers to date, in the three areas affected, all three hundred and
fifty-one municipalities in the country. In addition, the amount of grants directly transferred as such to
municipalities, which increases year after year from 8.2 billion CFA in 2010 to 12.5 billion in 2012 and is
expected to reach more than 20 billion in 2013. This transfer of skills and resources contributed to the
improvement of the indicators in the basic social sectors registered during the period covered by the third
series of PRSC.
In the area of public finance management, the World Bank has, since the first series of PRSC, supported
the government in its efforts to increase efficiency, transparency and accountability in the management of
public resources. It is indeed with its support to the administration that the government undertook the
process of modernizing its budget management tools in the mid-90s. This commitment of the institution
continued in the early 2000s with support for the implementation of programming tools and the
evaluation of public expenditure (Medium-term Expenditure Framework, budget program, public
expenditure review, etc.). The appropriation of these tools by the economic and financial administration
has considerably improved the budget preparation process and gradually strengthened the link, firstly,
between the budget and the priorities of sector policies and strategies, and, secondly, between the
measures implemented and the results expected.
Another aspect of public finances that has attracted a particular attention from the Bank as part of the
implementation of the third series of PRSC is the area of public procurement, including the
operationalization of the large scale reform which started in 2008 and which was reflected in particular by
the separation of the regulation and control functions, respectively assigned to the Regulatory Authority
for Public Procurement (ARMP) and the Directorate-General for Public Procurement (DGP). This reform
is also marked by the decentralization of the DGP in the departments and regions, and the creation of
people responsible for markets within the departments, to enable more rapid processing of public
procurement. The Bank has actively supported this decentralization process which has really helped to
improve the implementation of public spending in general.
Compliance with the obligation to report has also achieved progress at the instigation of measures
supported by the PRSC. Among the major actions that can be identified in this regard are the regular
production of public reports of Supreme Audit Institutions, respectively judicial (Court of Auditors) and
administrative (Supreme Audit Authority of State), and the strengthening of the service control done by
the a priori control structures, etc.
Originally scheduled for three operations like the previous ones, the third series of funding was
extended with the introduction of two new exceptional operations respectively in 2010 and 2011.



                                                     37
The 2010 operation was aimed at supporting the efforts of the government in managing the effects of
floods on September 1, 2009 and the global financial and economic crisis, through a consolidated action
plan developed for this purpose and articulated to include the rehabilitation of destroyed infrastructure,
the boosting of the agricultural production, the establishment of a special program for youth employment,
and the implementation of a program of social safety nets for the benefit of vulnerable households. The
intervention of the Bank offered a broader fiscal space to the government to address the important
financing requirements of the action plan.
As for the fifth funding of the series, it was set up when the government finalized its new reference
framework for development, namely the Strategy for Accelerated Growth and Sustainable Development
(SCADD). IDA has actively supported the development of this reference framework which highlights the
need for a strong and sustained growth over time in order to achieve a significant reduction of poverty.
IDA decided through this operation to align on the agenda of the government in the implementation of the
SCADD. For this reason, it has backed by its funding to measures so as to consolidate the gains of the
four previous operations and promote the goals of SCADD.


    III-    ASSESSMENT OF THE PRSC IMPLEMENTATION

The PRSC instrument, although based on policy measures, has greatly contributed to establish the culture
of performance in public authorities involved in its implementation. Today, the widespread use of MTEF
and program budget in the budget process has significantly improved the quality of the Government
budget and strengthened its links with the priorities of sector policies. The establishment of mechanisms
for periodic evaluation of the implementation of the national and sector policies and strategy action plans
has strengthened the obligation to report to citizens, who can now assess the impact of government
intervention.
Clearly, understanding the PRSC instrument has evolved positively both at the government level and
within IDA, as illustrated by the regularity, as well as the number and volume of transactions. Public
administration has largely adopted budget support as a modality of aid delivery, particularly the PRSC
and the workings of its implementation.
If we can be satisfied with the flexibility that characterizes the PRSC compared to other budget support
instruments, it should be noted that predictability has diminished over the years, mainly due to difficulties
encountered in the definition of triggers, not always aligned with the agreed disbursement matrix under
the CGAB. We lose a lot of time in the design of operations, leading to uncertainty about the
disbursements. Due to the timetable for cessation of budget operations as a result of the principle of
budget annuality, the unsuccessful mobilization of the PRSC resources in due time has negative
consequences on the budget implementation, and hence, on the achievement of the government
objectives, particularly in the social sectors. The Bank and the government must work together to address
this issue in future operations.
