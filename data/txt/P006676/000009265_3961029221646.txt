I. BACKGROUND AND SECTORAL CONTEXT
A. Macroeconomic Background
1.1         Over the last decade, Chile has experienced one of the most sustained
macroeconomic performances in Latin America. Since 1982, exports tripled and the
economy grew for 11 consecutive years. From 1984 to 1994, driven by the remarkable
annual growth rates in investments and exports, averaging 14.3 percent and 9.9 percent,
respectively, GDP increased by an average annual rate of 6.8 percent. During the same
period, inflation declined from 23 percent to 8.9 percent. At end-1994, Chile had a
relatively small public sector (general government expenditures totalled 23.3 percent of
GDP), high investment rates (25.6 percent of GDP), a strong reserve position, a relatively
flexible labor code, and an open trade regime.
1.2         The steady resolve of successive Administrations to carry out a comprehensive
reform program aimed at redefining the role of the state brought about the sound economic
performance. Starting in the mid 1970s, Chile began reducing trade protection sharply,
privatizing firms and restructuring public enterprises along commercial lines, decreasing
Government subsidies, and decentralizing responsibilities to the municipal level. An
innovative pension reform program, designed to increase domestic savings, offered social
security payers access to newly established pension funds.
1.3         In 1992, the economy reached its highest growth rate (10.4 percent) in 27
years. Real 1993 GDP growth declined to 6.3 percent due to a deterioration in Chile's terms
of trade, reflecting a 15 percent decline in international prices of its major exports (copper,
paper products, fishmeal and fruit) and a restrictive monetary policy that had been adopted to
cool the economy. A substantial rebound in copper prices, along with strong demand from
Chile's markets turned the economy around in the second quarter of 1994. As a result, the
trade balance improved and the current account deficit dropped to 0.9 percent of GDP in
1994. Chile's balance of payments continued to benefit from strong increases in capital
inflows induced by prudent macroeconomic management, deep capital markets, and well-
defined legal and regulatory frameworks. As a consequence, the Central Bank accumulated
ample reserves, up to US$13 billion (equivalent to 9.8 months' imports) by end-1994, and
the peso appreciated by 7.1 percent in nominal terms in 1994. While a restrictive monetary
policy succeeded in reducing annual inflation to 8.9 percent in 1994, GDP growth slowed to
4.5 percent. As a result, unemployment increased to 6.5 percent, up from a record low 4.5
percent in 1993.

B. Agriculture in the Economy
Policy Framework
1.4          The macroeconomic policy reforms of the mid-1980s, complemented by
sectoral policy adjustments, liberalized domestic and foreign trade agricultural markets and
eliminated distortions in the credit system. Most agricultural products have a uniform 11
percent tariff and are free of non-quantitative trade restrictions. Price bands for a few basic
staples, i.e. wheat and sugar, minimize the destabilizing impact sharp changes in import
prices could have on domestic production. Private banks provide credit to commercial
farmers at real interest rates between 6.5 percent and 9 percent. Small farmers have access
to a special credit line, whose funds are available at interest rates linked to the cost of money
as determined by Central Bank operations. The current interest rate for small farmers is 7.5
percent in real terms.
Performance
1.5          The agricultural sector contributes approximately 8 percent of GDP, employs
18 percent of the country's economically active population and generates 35 percent of its
merchandise exports. Chile is a highly urbanized country, with under 15 percent of its 13.6
million inhabitants living in rural areas. Between 1983 and 1989, agricultural GDP
(including forestry and fisheries) grew at an average annual rate of 5.8 percent, with export
crops playing a leading role. Between 1989 and 1992, declining world prices for fruit and
horticultural exports and decreased wheat production caused the annual agricultural growth
rate to drop to 3.0 percent. During the past two years, the gradual appreciation of the peso
has affected exports. Although agricultural export growth will continue, sector growth will
be determined by the capacity of producers to improve productivity and orient their
production towards highly profitable crops.
1.6          Within the sector, there are marked differences in performance and natural
resource management among regions, crops and producers. Regional differences reflect
agro-ecological variations. Among crops, contrasts are explained by diverse and dynamic
market opportunities. The most pronounced differences in productivity and profitability are
found between commercial and small farmers. Reconciliation of this duality, compounded by
the diversity within the small farmer sector, ranging from those with agricultural potential to
those with limited or no agricultural potential, will be this Government's most serious
sectoral challenge.

C. Rural Poverty
1.7             In the 1980s, over 40 percent of the population lived in poverty - with
insufficient income to cover their basic needs.' Half of these were extremely poor - unable
to satisfy their minimum food requirements. In 1992, while 80 percent of the poor were
urban dwellers, the incidence of poverty in the rural areas (34 percent) was greater than in
urban areas (30 percent). In the project area, covering Regions VI through VIII, the
incidence of rural poverty was greater than 40 percent. In 1991, 84 percent of the country's
poorest municipalities (comunas)2 were rural.3 The rural poor tend to be small farmers,
older and having a lower education than the rest of the population. They are confronted by
deteriorating soils and limited access to credit and critical social services.
1.8           Between 1990 and 1993, the Solidarity and Social Investment Fund (FOSIS)
financed more than 2.100 projects, valued at US$34 million, to improve the welfare of the
poorest sectors.4 Between 1990 and 1993, the national poverty level fell from 40 percent to
33 percent due to sustained economic growth, increased social expenditure and greater
poverty alleviation investments. During that period, the incidence of urban poverty declined
by 23.8 percent and rural incidence fell by 20 percent. However, poverty incidence in the
project area decreased by less than 12 percent.

Forestry Development
1.13         The National Forestry Corporation (Corporaci6n Nacional Forestal - CONAF)

Natural Resource Conservation
1.14         The Agricultural and Livestock Service (Servicio Agricola y Ganadero - SAG),
a Santiago-based decentralized entity under the Ministry of Agriculture, is responsible for
agricultural crop and animal health and sanitary control and renewable natural resource
protection. Because of budget constraints, natural resource conservation programs only issue
environmental regulations and do not actively promote rational resource use. It supervises
compliance of rules governing seed and ethyl alcohol production and foreign trade of
agricultural products. SAG has 12 regional offices and 2,000 employees.
E. Bank Experience in the Agricultural Sector
Rural Poverty Alleviation and Agricultural Development
1.15         Rural projects are at the crux of Bank operational objectives, addressing, at the
same time, poverty alleviation, natural resource conservation, macroeconomic and sectoral
policy rationalization, beneficiary participation, private sector development and gender
issues. As a result, rural sector lending is complex - demanding sound, innovative
approaches .9
1.16          Successful rural development projects require six elements.'° First, sound
macroeconomic and sector policies, as reflected in a country's public expenditure program,
are critical to create an enabling environment which reduces tendencies to overburden
projects with multiple objectives and strengthens project implementing agencies. Second,
intensive consultation with beneficiaries is needed to build consensus and support for project
objectives. Third, a phased approach, focusing only on sub-sectors vital for small farmer
development and poverty alleviation, is most likely to succeed. Fourth, project scale needs
to conform to demonstrated institutional and funding capacity. Fifth, while design should be

1.17          The Colombia Rural Development Investment Project (Ln.3250-CO) is an
example of a decentralized, demand-driven program of diverse rural investments, having
well-defined objectives, intense community participation and minimum Government
involvement. Communities, with Bank support, directly contract firms to provide small

ensures the participation of poorer communities.
1.18         Socio-economic development funds, such as the Bolivia Emergency Social
Fund, are increasingly used to finance micro-enterprise development and social infrastructure
to alleviate poverty and/or tide the poor through periods of adjustment. Experience
demonstrates the value of flexibility, precisely defined objectives and investment criteria,
sound management, rapid procurement procedures and high calibre staff.'3 Poorer
communities have often required assistance to prepare sub-projects proposals. In the
Honduran fund, credit was successfully supplied to rural micro-entrepreneurs by using NGOs
as financial intermediaries. Market interest rates were charged to final beneficiaries, and
NGOs assumed full credit risk. Recovery was ensured by group solidarity, rather than
physical or financial guarantees.
Natural Resource Management
1.19         The Bank should help countries to incorporate natural resource management as
a central element in national development and poverty alleviation strategies."4 Project
conditionality cannot substitute for economic and social policies which specifically benefit the
poor in ecologically-sensitive areas. Incentive structures need to reinforce project natural
resource goals. Resource users should be actively involved in project design and
execution. '" Finally, projects must be technically solid. For example, agro-ecological
zoning has become the sine qua non for successful public investment in environmentally
fragile areas; and, the failure to understand key agro-ecological issues can undermine a
project's expectation of agricultural and livestock improvements.

Lessons Learned from Involvement in Chile
1.20         The current lending portfolio in Chile, comprising 16 projects with an
undisbursed balance of US$529.2 million as of September 30, 1995, has performed well.
The Chilean experience highlights the importance of sound macroeconomic and sectoral
policies, complemented by a strong institutional framework with a skilled civil service.
1.21         The Bank has extended nine agricultural loans to Chile in the past four
decades, totalling US$260.0 million. Early projects provided credit for modernization and
expansion of agricultural infrastructure. Small-farmer development and poverty alleviation,
typified by the Small Farmer Services Project (Ln. 3473-CH) and the Irrigation Development
Project (PROMM, Ln.3528-CH), were approved in 1992. Lessons from these are the value
of (i) flexibility in project design; and (ii) beneficiary involvement. The project's flexible
design allowed beneficiaries to specify project activities.
-I. PROJECT BACKGROUND
A. Characteristics of the Secano 16
Natural Resource Base and Land Use
2.1          The area of influence of the proposed project is within the dryland areas of the
slopes of the Cordillera de la Costa [Coastal Mountain Range], known as respectively, the
"Coastal" and "Inland" Secano. The entire Secano stretches from Region V to Region X
(between parallels 320 20' and 420 30', S. Lat.), covering 5.7 million hectares. The project
area of influence would incorporate Region VI to VIII, covering 3.5 million ha. This area
covers four agro-ecological zones, with a climate that varies from mediterranean in the north
to marine in the south and annual rainfall ranging from 350 mm, in the north, to 1,200 mm,
towards the south.
2.2          Irrational use is degrading regional natural resources. Pasture area greatly
exceeds the area suitable for such purpose, less than 5 percent of which is improved pasture.
To avoid soil erosion, nearly 60 percent of the region should be under forest, yet only 10

work is their primary occupation. Less than 10 percent has permanent off-farm jobs.
Seasonal agricultural work is the second most important occupation for men. Women tend to
work in seasonal/occasional jobs or permanently in the service sector.
2.15         Small farmer Secano production systems are: (i) crops, alternating grain
legumes with wheat; or (ii) crops (wheat, legumes) combined with livestock, with a fallow
rotation followed by wheat. When the farm size permits, these are followed by three to five
years of natural pastures, mainly for draught animals and sheep. In the South, wine grapes
are important and, in the more humid zones, potatoes and beans are planted.
2.16         Small Secano farmers fall into three types defined according to income
sources.27 Type I corresponds to farmers who earn 65 percent or more of their cash income
from farm production, and who possess significant agricultural production potential. Type
II, the "transition farmer", obtains between 35 percent and 65 percent of cash income from
farm production. With appropriate support, these farmers could become efficient part-time
farmers, practicing sustainable natural resource management strategies. Finally, Type III,
the "off-farm producer", with the lowest commercial agriculture potential, receives less than
35 percent of cash income from farm production.

B. Development Potential and Constraints of the Small Farmer
2.17          The Secano's economic potential lies in the development of its forests and
pastures. With sufficient capital, investing in these areas could represent important economic
options for small farmers. Expanded irrigation could considerably increase crop production.
Given the region's topography, storing water run-offs with small basins could irrigate several
thousands of hectares. In those zones where irrigation is not feasible, production could be
enhanced through cost-effective rainfed farming techniques. Production and productivity
could be through land-intensive technologies which would reduce cultivated area, resulting in
land use patterns more suited to the available natural resources.
2.18          The poor natural resource base is a serious constraint to improving the
Secano's small farmers' household welfare. Farms are small with poor, fragile and degraded
soils. Insufficient water constrains production increases, with rainfall concentrated in the
period of least vegetative growth and disappearing during the period of crop ripening and
pasture growth. In many ares, the limited coverage and poor condition of the irrigation
system restrict the development of fruit, especially grape, production. In livestock
production-oriented micro-regions with relatively scarce rainfall, poor irrigation limits crop
production for home consumption. Throughout the Secano, training and technical assistance
could increase efficiency of on-farm irrigation.
2.19          Poverty limits small farmers' ability to adjust production patterns and cultural
practices in response to natural resource endowments and economic incentives. Household
survival dictates that highest priority be given to subsistence crop production, with little
regard for soil type or condition. Frequently, farmers need to cultivate steep slopes,
resulting in soil degradation and erosion. Poverty increases small farmers' reluctance to
experiment with novel cultivation practices, as such modification could fail, affect not only
the farm operations, but also the household's livelihood.
2.20          There is a dearth of ecologically viable production systems adapted to the
special Secano conditions. Despite INDAP's recent program shift towards subsistence
farmers, its coverage in the Secano is inadequate, dispersed and crop-specific. Poor
infrastructure, including post-harvest storage, processing and marketing, and all-weather rural
roads restrain regional development by impeding competitive market formations. Finally,
deficient social services, such as health. education, drinking water and electricity, depress the
welfare of the rural poor.

III. THE PROJECT
A. Origin and Rationale for Bank Involvement
3.1          Despite its substantial agro-forestry potential, the Secano is economically
depressed, with the highest incidence of rural poverty in Chile.28 The region has remained
beyond the reach of both the national economic growth and most social programs. Targeted
programs, together with economic growth, are needed to alleviate regional rural poverty.
3.2          The project's strategy would increase economic returns to land by promoting
soil conservation and production technologies capable of sustaining increased production and
income. Assistance would be given to farmers in the design and execution of production
systems compatible with land capabilities and natural resource conservation practices. In
zones with sufficient water resources, the project would promote technically and
economically viable irrigation works, crop intensification and diversification. The project
would: (i) establish cultivated pastures or improved natural pasture management; and (ii)
create forest plantations and enrich existing natural forests. Project-financed investments
would generate sufficient additional income to allow farmers to invest in their farms, on
better lowland soils, pastures and forestry.
3.3           The project's poverty alleviation strategy would combine on- and off-farm
activities to reverse natural resource deterioration and increase household income and welfare
on a sustainable basis. The relative importance of one type of activity over another would
depend on the beneficiaries' socio-economic characteristics and survival tactics. The strategy
would generate employment. thereby increasing incomes for under-employed rural families
and reducing their need to abuse natural resources. While beneficiaries would select project
off-farm activities, likely activities include handicrafts. cottage industries and small agro-
industries. Beneficiaries would be assisted in the preparation of employment-generating
investment proposals and, subsequently, provided resources for their implementation.
Project-financed training, complementing the development of off-farm activities, would equip
beneficiaries with skills for non-agricultural labor markets and developing micro-enterprises.
The project would rehabilitate rural feeder roads and address community-identified marketing
bottlenecks.
3.4          The project's poverty alleviation activities would address the needs of the
Secano's three small farmer types (see para 2.16). For Type I, households which draw most
of their income from on-farm production, the project would improve production patterns.
cultural practices and natural resource management. For Type II, households which combine
on-farn production with off-farm activities, the project would support the development of
small-scale forest plantations to improve natural resource management and appreciate land
assets. Additionally, off-farm activities would be promoted, utilizing surplus family labor to

from off-farm activities, the project would raise productivity in the limited land areas suited

3.5           The project would support research for the development of production

methodologies, such as gender analysis, would ensure that project-advanced activities would
not increase their burdens, but rather, their opportunities to earn additional household
income.
3.7           In support of the Government's decentralization effort, the project would
strengthen local government institutions, especially municipalities, citizen groups and
farmers' organizations, enabling them to assume project execution, monitoring and evaluation
responsibilities. The technical assistance provided to municipalities would strengthen their
capacity to prepare project proposals, facilitating their access to recently established
Government-financed Regional Development Funds.
B. Project Objectives
3.8           The Project would address rural poverty and natural resource degradation,
mutually reinforcing problems, through the development and validation of comprehensive
strategies which achieve sustainable rural poverty alleviation and economic growth through
increased family income and improved natural resource management.
3.9          The Secano's diverse and fragile resource base, along with a lack of effective
regional poverty alleviation strategies, argues for a project which can validate alternative
development approaches. The heterogeneity of Secano conditions calls for a micro-regional
approach, starting with pilot-scale sub-projects in representative areas to test the
technological, social and institutional actions considered to be most likely to succeed. The
project would develop viable strategies which would: (i) improve natural resource
management to reverse soil degradation and establish sustainable production systems; and (ii)

IV. PROJECT IMPLEMENTATION
A. Institutional Arrangements41
4.1         The Ministry of Agriculture (MINAGRI) would have overall project authority.
INDAP would be the Executing Agency, while its National Director, through the Project
Technical Unit (UTP), would manage the project. The UTP would provide technical,
managerial and administrative support in all project implementation matters. The UTP
would have staff responsibilities and functions established in the Implementation Manual.
The UTP would manage the Program for Financing Poverty Alleviation Activities (PFAP)
and would administer its resources. A Project Commission (Comite Superior de Proyecto,
CSP) would be established, whose permanent membership would include the Director of
INDAP, who would be the Commission's chairman, the Director of ODEPA, as well as the
Administrators (Intendentes) of the three regions covered by the project. Representatives of
various entities, such as MBN, MOP, CNR, INIA, CONAF and MIDEPLAN, would be
invited to participate in the CSP debates, as recommended by its chairman. The Project
Commission's responsibilities would be to provide guidance on the strategy for project
implementation and make recommendations on the Annual Work Programs (POAs) and their
corresponding budgets; rules, procedures and operational mechanisms of PFAP; and
guidelines for studies and technical assistance. The Commission would also review the
project's progress and its degree of beneficiary participation. The Minister of Agriculture
would have the responsibility and authority to reconcile differences among members of the
Commission. At negotiations, the Government presented to the Bank draft legal and
administrative arrangements for the establishment of the CSP and the UTP. Agreement on
these was reached at negotiations. Final arrangements, acceptable to the Bank, would be
forwarded to the Bank as a condition of Loan Effectiveness.

B. Program for Financing Poverty Alleviation Activities (PFAP)
4.7           This program would finance sustainable agriculture on-farm activities and

natural resource conservation practices. The off-farm activities would be economically
sustainable enterprises designed to generate local employment opportunities and alternative
income sources (See Annex D).
4.8           The Program's interventions would be highly targeted, as beneficiary
municipalities are among Chile's 100 poorest. In addition, the program's on-farm activities
would be primarily targeted to those farmers with less than 20 physical hectares (or less than
about 5 ha equivalent of Class I irrigated land).

V. PROJECT BENEFITS, IMPACTS AND RISKS
A. Benefits
5.1         The project would contribute to a coherent strategy for rural poverty
alleviation and sustainable resource management in the Secano. Financial support, technical
assistance and technology development and transfer based on the demands of small farmers
in selected, representative areas, would serve as a basis for the design of a rural poverty
alleviation strategy. If successful, the strategies could be extended throughout the Secano.
Given the project's small size relative to the magnitude to regional poverty, the project could
be viewed as a precursor to possible larger Government-financed programs needed to reach
the entire Secano population. The proposed project would increase incomes of beneficiaries,
through enhanced employment opportunities and agricultural production. The project would
encourage government decentralization, strong beneficiary and municipal participation in
discovering solutions for their priority issues, and coordination of the activities of various
agencies currently providing basic services in the Secano.
B. Economic Analysis
5.2           The project would improve the production patterns of about 22,000 ha of
1,100 small farmers (out of 2,425 within the seven selected micro-regions; see Annex 0 for
assumptions regarding adoption rates) and develop sustainable small farmer production
systems consistent with land capabilities. Area under annual crops would be reduced by at
least 3,400 ha, concentrated on better soils and diversified into higher-value crops. Project-
financed irrigation works on 1,300 ha would increase cropping intensities. On 3,700 non-
irrigable ha, the project would introduce rainfed cultivation practices aimed at proper soil and
water conservation. Additionally, the project would improve vineyard cultivation practices to
avoid soil erosion and introduce grape varieties with higher market value about 1,800 ha
under grapes, the Secano's most profitable crop, .
5.3         By promoting commodities for which there is strong domestic and export
demand, the project would integrate a relatively marginal region into the main economy.
The project area is expected to produce annually over US$2.6 million of vegetables and
pulses for domestic markets. In addition, US$3.0 million of wine and nearly US$1.0 million
of lamb would be produced, an important share of which would be exported. Additionally,
project-promoted forestry activities would produce US$36.0 million of roundwood or posts
for local fruit industry, and US$27.0 million of pulpwood for the pulp industry, throughout
the project's life.



- 31 -
5.4           Estimated internal economic rate of return (IERR) for the natural resource
management and sustainable agricultural development component and the rural feeder roads
sub-component (including the costs of the technology transfer program and the UTP) is 20.7
percent. The IERR includes economic benefits derived from: (i) crop productivity gains,
resulting from improved practices; (ii) crop diversification into higher-valued crops; (iii)
decreased production costs; and (iv) reduced marketing margins, due to better rural
infrastructure. These were applied to indicative farm models, representative of project
beneficiaries in the Secano.48
5.5           During the first year of implementation, participation in the project's natural
resource component of between 5% and 10% of the small farmers in the selected micro-
regions is expected. Participation would increase in subsequent years, until the project's fifth
year, when an estimated 47 percent of small farmers in the selected micro-region would
participate in the project. Relatively high participation rates are expected due to the: (i)
farmers' interest expressed in the participatory diagnosis carried out during project
preparation; (ii) attractive incentive packages offered; (iii) relatively low risk the production
packages represent to farmers' subsistence; and (iv) high probability of successful
implementation due to system-oriented technical assistance. Moreover, the project's
flexibility in implementation would allow for design adjustments to remove constraints to
farmers' adoption.
5.6           This IERR is very conservative as it does not consider important non-
quantitative benefits, such as those stemming from (i) reduced soil run-off and erosion; and
(ii) containment of progressive productivity losses due to declining soil fertility.
Furthermore, the analysis did not capture "without project" productivity loses (as a project
benefit) due to declining soil fertility.
5.7           For the off-farm investment component, as beneficiaries would determine
which activities would be financed under this component, an ex-ante estimation of costs and
benefits was made by analyzing a sample of likely off-farm projects. The preparation team
found that most proposals would be small, ranging between US$25,000 and US$100,000,
and economically highly feasible, having estimated internal economic rates of return
exceeding 25%. Since only technically and economically sound proposals would receive
project finance, this component would likely increase the project's internal economic rate of
return. To ensure economic viability, a minimum threshold of an estimated internal
economic rate of return would be applied as a selection criteria to sub-project proposals.
5.8           Significant benefits would be derived from other project components (technical
assistance, institutional strengthening, research and studies), including improved beneficiary
capacity to implement off-farm economic activities, support to Government decentralization,
as well as research for developing the Secano. But, because of the longer-term realization

C. Environmental Impact
5.9         As improved natural resource management would be the project's most
important poverty alleviation mechanism, a significant project benefit would be the positive
environmental impact stemming from more rational land use. Where agricultural production
would be intensified, chemical use would be scrutinized, minimizing potential negative
impacts. Virtually all road works under the project would involve improved drainage,
bridges and resurfacing, conforming to acceptable engineering standards and having
consequent positive environmental impact.
5.10        The project has been rated "B" on the grounds that it may be necessary to
design mitigative measures for environmental protection against project activities.
Environmental impact assessments (EIA) of natural resource management and agricultural
development programs have been carried out by specialized consultants, contracted with
Bank-acceptable TORs and under the supervision of an experienced environment specialist
(See Annex F). The EIAs indicated that further environmental impact studies were
unnecessary. As specified in the Project Implementation Manual, the UTP Monitoring and
Evaluation Department's, through contracted firms, would perform environmental M&E of
project activities, giving particular attention to environmental implications of PFAP-financed
investment proposals.
5.11        For purposes of consultation, a draft summary environmental report would be
prepared and circulated for comment to CONAMA, to national NGOs and, later, to each
Local Development Committee. Based on response, the UTP could convene with interested
parties, especially NGOs.
D. Impact on Women and Youth
5.12        Eager to participate in income-generating programs, Secano women often
cannot due to logistical constraints and their pressing responsibilities at home. The project is
expected to strengthen women's position within the community, increase their access to job
opportunities within the micro-region and elsewhere, and improve their skills for developing
micro-enterprises. High-value land-intensive crops (tomatoes, flowers, strawberries), and
small artisan shops and "cottage" industries are especially likely to help women earn more
income as many of these activities can be integrated into their regular home and family
obligations. While not exclusively targeted towards them, women are expected to be the

Characteristics of the Secano'
A. Natural Resources
Climate
1.    Rainfall in the Secano is poorly distributed, with a dry period lasting one to eight
months, becoming shorter as one moves south. Average annual rainfall is between 350 mm
and 1,200 mm, occurring in the winter when lower temperatures limit plant growth. Intense
initial rains provoke erosion. Rainfall varies greatly from year to year. Periodically, there
can be up to three consecutive years of drought, incurring great agricultural production
losses. The sea moderates temperatures in coastal zones, extending 15 km inland. Humid
and frost-free conditions in these areas are relatively favorable for agricultural development.
Soils
2.    In the Secano, soils are: granitic (32%), metamorphic (32%), marine terraces (18%)
and alluvial (18%). Granitic and metamorphic soils, without vegetative protection in the dry
summer months are vulnerable to erosion by the intense first rains. This, along with its
uneven topography, explains the Secano's high incidence of erosion.
3.    With few exceptions, Secano soils have low fertility and are mostly suited for
forestry. According to USBR soil classification, 62% of soils is Class VII, 15% is Class VI
and only 19% is arable land, i.e., soils in Class I to IV. Only the soils of the marine
terraces have medium or greater depth and good moisture-retaining capacity. With adequate
management and heavy fertilization, farmers obtain reasonable yields on these soils.
Water
4.    The main rivers in project area are the Aconcagua, Maipo, Rapel, Mataquito, Maule,
Itata and Bio Bio. The Aconcagua river irrigates the Aconcagua valley, while the remaining
rivers and their tributaries irrigate Chile's Central Valley. These rivers generally go through

South.

deep and narrow valleys, irrigating only small strips of alluvial soils along the river banks.
Most pernanent irrigation water rights from these rivers have been allocated. Future
agricultural development programs could use water for irrigation from these rivers only
during surplus periods. Some of the areas identified by the National Irrigation Commission
(Comisi6n Nacional de Riego - CNR) as having irrigation potential, such as Convento Viejo,

5.    The best sources of small volumes of water for irrigation in the Secano are the small

construction of small dams to harvest winter and spring rainfall. The area's topography
offers suitable places for the small dams, using run-off from mini-basins of up to 30 km2.
While underground water sources are apparently scarce, they could be important for the

MOP's General Directorate of Water, Secano's groundwater resources are along the rivers

material. Wells drilled along the Maipo, Itata and Mataquito rivers are between 13 and 14

the Hydro-geologic Atlas registers areas of high to medium relative hydro-geological
importance located in Regions VI and VIII. These groundwater resources are generally
found in sandy strata above sea level.

C. Small Farmer Production Practices in the Secano
17.   Small farmer production systems in the Secano are: (i) crops, normally alternating
grain legumes (lentils and chickpeas) with wheat, and (ii) a combination of crops (wheat and
legumes) and livestock, with a fallow rotation the first year, and wheat as the main crop in
the second year. Usually small extensions of grain legumes are cultivated. When farm size
allows, these crops are followed by three to five years of natural pastures, mainly to feed one
or two draft animals and for the production of sheep for meat and wool. In Regions VII and
VIII, wine grapes are an important crop for the small producer and, in the low and humid
parts, potatoes and beans are also planted.
18.   Land is ploughed in early spring and left fallow until the first winter rains arrive and
wheat is planted. This practice provides: (i) weed control; (ii) soil preparation for quick
sowing when the first rains appear; and (iii) theoretically, subsoil water storage. But the
practice causes erosion by (i) leaving soil without vegetative cover, exposed to the intense
force of first rains, and (ii) dispersing raindrops on the soil surface, water infiltration rate is
reduced and run-off increased.
19.   Agricultural research is needed to compare this practice with alternative tillage
methods. Such investigations should take into account: (i) chemical weed control
alternatives; (ii) vertical farming equipment and minimum tillage systems adapted to small
units, allowing quick sowing; (iii) the net effect of fallow on subsoil water retention; and (iv)
reduction in erosion losses due to delaying the fallow and keeping soil covered with
vegetation during the rainy season.

20.    Soil preparation in small farms is normally done with teams of oxen using
moldboard ploughs and tine and branch harrows. Fertilization is limited to nitrogen in wheat
cultivation. Sowing is done by broadcasting and seed is covered with branch harrows.
Broadcast sowing of grain legumes renders subsequent weed clearing with animal traction
impossible. Wheat harvest consists of manual reaping and transport, and threshing with
stationary machines. Grain legumes (lentils and chickpeas) are pulled, threshed and selected
manually.
21.    For the cultivation of potatoes and beans in the humid parts or floodplain of Regions
VII and VIII, small producers use their own seeds, generally of poor quality. Particularly in
the case of potatoes, this practice transmits viruses and other diseases. Fertilizer applications
and phytosanitary controls are minimal. Beans are harvested in the same manner as
chickpeas and lentils. Potato are harvested manually, with specially adapted animal-drawn
ploughs.
22.    For the small farmer, wine grapes are the crop with the highest profitability. But as
the vigorous and drought-resistant common stock, the "Pais", yields wine of lower
commercial value, the development potential is reduced. Vineyards, occupying limited areas
of the farms, are generally over 30 years old. The narrow space between the vines, together
with low head formation and the absence of trellises, hinders weed control and disinfections,
and makes harvesting difficult. Fertilization levels are low.

Socio-economic Analysis of Secano Small Farmers
A. Rural Poverty
1.    Despite the strong economic performance Chile achieved in the last decade, currently,
more than 4 million people (about a third of the population) live in poverty. Although
in absolute terms most poor are in the urban areas, the incidence and severity of poverty are
greater in rural areas, (Table 1) with municipalities in Chile's Secano area having the highest
incidence of rural poverty. Sixty-five percent of Chile's poorest municipalities are in the
Secano.
Socio-Economic Characteristics of Secano Small Farmers
2.    To study the socio-economic characteristics of the Secano farming population and
define small farm survival strategy topologies, the preparation team: (i) conducted a survey
based on a simple random sample of 440 small farmer households (with a population of
2,032 individuals); (ii) investigated in detail representative cases of each farm type; and (iii)
organized male agricultural producers, female agricultural producers and young people into
separate groups to discuss alternative development strategies for their micro-region.
3.    In rural Secano municipalities, there are significantly fewer women than men. The
male/female ratio was 1. 178:1, more than 20% higher than the ratio for the Chilean
population. This is due to the migration of young women from rural to urban centers,
seeking urban employment in the service sector. The age of the adult population is
concentrated between 30 and 50 years (average age 36 years), with the household heads'
average age being 53 years. Family size ranged between 3.7 and 5.7 persons. The average
household size of 4.3 persons is greater than the national average (4.05), but approximates
the rural household average of 4.41 persons. The formal education level reached by the
population over 18 years of age was 5.12 years, well below the national rural area average
level of 6.2 years.
4.    More than 60% of the rural households surveyed live below the poverty line. Region
VII had the highest poverty incidence, with over 70% of its rural population living in
poverty. Off-farm and non-agricultural activities have become increasingly important for
supporting small farm families. For many, home consumption of farm production
supplements off-farm earnings, leaving only limited marketable surplus. Nevertheless, for



- 43 -                               Annex B
poor families, farm production for family consumption is critical to family survival. As a
result, without secure off-farm employment, many households cannot risk moving out of
agriculture. Less than 10% of the people surveyed held permanent jobs, while 8.5%
declared seasonal/occasional work.
B. Farm Size and Land Tenure
5.    Farm size in the seven selected micro-regions ranged from 0.2 ha to 72 ha, with an
average of 20.5 ha. Many farms (44%) are between 10 ha and 20 ha. Nevertheless, 10%
are below 2 ha and 31 % are below 5 ha. Precarious land tenure arrangements are prevalent,
with share-cropping being the most common. Twenty-seven percent of the farmers surveyed
were engaged in a sharecropping arrangement, either by working another's land, giving
another farmer livestock for grazing, or having sharecroppers on their farms. For many
producers, lack of formal land title seriously limits access to credit and services.
C. Secano Producer Types
6.    Farmers surveyed were classified into the following three groups:
(a)   Type I: On-farm producers, corresponds to farm units obtaining 65% or
more of their cash income from on-farm production and whose survival
strategy is based on farming. Within this group, there are three sub-groups:
(i)   crop-predominant, which draws more than half the on-farm income
from crop production;
(ii)  livestock-predominant, which draws more than half the on-farm
income from livestock production; and
(iii)   mixed type, which combines livestock, crop and other on-farm
activities, each representing less than half of on-farm income.
(b)   Type II: Transition farmers, corresponds to farmers who obtain between
65% and 35% of their cash income from on-farm production. This type
moves between on-farm and off-farm activities, depending on employment and
income prospects. This type has three sub-groups, those whose incomes are
derived primarily from: (i) crop production; (ii) livestock operations; and (iii)
wages.
(c)   Type III: Off-farm producers, includes households obtaining more than 65%
of their monetary income from off-farm sources. Three sub-types are: (i)
households in which more half the off-farm income is derived from wages; (ii)
households in which the main income sources are subsidies, transfers and
remittances; and (iii) small entrepreneurs, households in which petty commerce
and rent provide at least half the off-farm income.

7.    Reflecting different agro-climatic conditions and economic survival strategies, annual
family income varied substantially among micro-regions and farm types, ranging from
Ch$960,000 (US$2,340) to Ch$3 million (US$7,500). For Type I and II producers, on-farm
income represented about 90% and 58% of the household's total cash income, respectively.
The relative importance of on-farm income declined to less than 10% of cash income for
Type III producers.
F.    Most on-farm income came from agricultural products. Wages, representing 40% of
households' off-farm income and about a fifth of monetary income, was the most important
off-farm income source. About 45% of households surveyed received wage income, with
more than 40% of these households with annual wage earnings exceeding Ch$400,000
(US$1.000). Wage were primarily related to seasonal work, between November and March,
in the fruit export sector. State transfers, such as child benefits, pensions, and
supplementary income for poor families, represented almost a third of off-farm income. The
relative importance is greatest among the poorest households, with the share of subsidies
reaching 60% or 70% of household income.
9.    The agriculturally-based strategies of the Type I and II farmers yielded similar
household, per capita income and per capita on-farm consumption levels. Type III
households, whose survival strategies are non-agriculturally oriented, had the lowest income
levels. Farm size of Type I crop-oriented producers were smaller than those of Type I
livestock-oriented farmers. Type III farms tended be the smaller than Types I and II.
Compared with Type II, Type I used land more intensively, cultivating higher-valued
products, such as orchards, vineyards and fruits as well as subsistence crops for home
consumption. Type I crop-oriented producers tended have relatively large families, to be
slightly older and closer to urban centers than other Type I producers.
10.   Type I livestock-oriented producers had a well-defined upwardly mobile development
strategy. They were likely to be engaged in sharecropping arrangements or rent land to
other producers. Cattle breeders were concentrated in Region V and sheep producers in
Region VI. Most cattle breeders tended to sell low-weight animals for further fattening, or
buy low-weight animals and sell them after reaching market weight. Sheep producers sold
off-spring every year, retaining those needed to maintain herd size. Farm infrastructure is
poor.
11.    As attractive on-farm alternatives diminish, wages have become increasingly
important to the Type II crop-oriented producer. The relatively older Type II livestock-
oriented producers lack household labor to cultivate their farm, and capital to expand and
manage properly their livestock herd. They rely increasingly on state transfers, engaged in a
continuous struggle to survive. The wage-dependent Type II sub-type, with farms on
marginal lands, uses farm production for home consumption. While about 35 % of family
labor in this group earns wages, household labor is under-utilized.

D. Project Development Strategies
13.   The project's challenge is to address the poverty and lack of natural resource
management strategies that have progressively deteriorated the environment. Potential
beneficiaries consider that the greatest constraints to Secano development are the lack of
capital, especially for irrigation, roads and local enterprises, and limited economic
alternatives. Another constraint to growth is the poverty-induced reluctance of these small
farmers to risk experimentation with innovative production systems and unfamiliar crops.
The project would overcome this by increasing household income, effective extension
programs and risk-sharing. Effective introduction of production changes would need to (i)
clearly demonstrate results to producers; (ii) assume part of the initial investment costs and
risks; (iii) develop strong marketing links; and (iv) foster producers' organizations.
14.   Sustainable agricultural production and increased off-farm economic opportunities can
increase household incomes of each farm type. Types I and II could intensify sustainable
agricultural production, as well as increase pasture and forest lands. Type III could
substitute agricultural production with forest plantations. Extension and technical assistance
programs would promote sustainable production systems and natural resource management.
The project would generate investment to develop small agro-industries and other off-farn
employment opportunities.
15.   Type I producers would be most receptive to irrigation and crop diversification
programs. Success in crop intensification would encourage producers to make long-term
investments in forestry and improved range lands. Thus, the project would increase
economic viability of the farms. releasing non-agriculturally suited land from crops, and
gradually introducing sustainable natural resource management.
16.   Type II producers would be less inclined to adopt innovations requiring significant
changes in labor use. These relatively wage-dependent households, with few spare workers.
would alter farming practices once convinced that the income generated from the on-farm
innovation more than compensates for the associated lost wages. Provided they do not bear
full establishment costs, these producers might be inclined to develop forestry-range systems
on their marginal lands, having a preference for rangelands improvements. Based on
INDAP's experience, these producers are more inclined to adopt livestock production
improvements over crop-intensification systems.

there is a secure market for surplus production. This would require significant participation
of women, and care would be needed to ensure that the innovations do not overburden them
with additional labor.
18.    The project's very important "demonstration effect" on the late adopters will continue
after the project's conclusion. Therefore, it would be important for Mid-Term Reviews and
Evaluations to focus less on statistics (number of hectares and beneficiaries), and more on
assessing the improvements in the participating household's economic welfare.

Institutional Aspects
I. Background
1.    The project's implementation institutional framework was designed based on the
following guidelines:
(a)   INDAP has been designated as Executing Agency for this project, with its
National Director responsible for project execution.
(b)   The Project Technical Unit (UTP) would provide technical, managerial and
administrative support with respect to project execution. The UTP, within the
Institute for Agricultural Development (INDAP), would manage, coordinate,
supervise, monitor and evaluate the project. As its staff would be small, much
of these activities would be performed by consultants or contracted firms.
(c)   The Project Commission (CPS) would provide guidance and make
recommendations regarding project implementation.
(d)   A project-funded municipal professional would be assigned to each
municipality to act as a liaison among project beneficiaries, the UTP, the CDL
and the municipality.
(e)   Specific mechanisms to ensure beneficiary participation in decision-making
process and implementation would be incorporated in the project design,
including, (i) an adequate representation in CDL, particularly of women and
youth; and (ii) direct involvement in the preparation of off-farm development
plans.
(f)   To avoid duplication of development efforts in the micro-regions and lead to
better coordination and complementarity, public agencies (such as MOP, CNR,
INIA, CONAF and INFOR) would participate, in their respective areas of
competence, in project activities' supervision and technical evaluation. Road
rehabilitation works, designed according to MOP standards, would be
supervised by MOP or designated consultants.

Program for Financing Poverty Alleviation Activities
A. Introduction
1.    Small producers in the Secano operate in a fragile natural environment, face
declining agricultural productivity due to resource degradation. Most are subsistence farmers
living below the poverty line. Since satisfying survival needs is so demanding, they make
investmenit decisions based on a short-term time horizon. As a result, land is cultivated
intensively, often in ways incompatible with its capabilities, resulting in greater resource
deterioration and further impoverishment of the small farmer.
2.    The development strategy of the proposed project would introduce improved natural
resource management and sustainable production systems; as well as, expand off-farm
activities providing additional income sources and boosting regional development. Special
measures, including guarantees and incentives, would be provided to reduce the risk to poor
airmers of a modifying their farming practices.
3.    The strategy's success would depend on two elements: (i) producer's acceptance of
the project proposals; and (ii) a financing mechanism responsive to beneficiaries' demands
and willing to assume a portion of the risk involved. The first element, addressed during
preparation. would be pursued further during implementation through active beneficiaries
involvement in designing and executing farm development plans and off-farm investment
proposals. The Program for Financing Poverty Alleviation Activities (PFAP) would address
(hie second element, by financing beneficiary-proposes investments.' PFAP would assume
parn of the investment risks by providing grants for some activities and co-financing the rest.
I .    The types of investment that would be financed through the PFAP are summarized below, and detailed
in the Implementation Manual.

B. General Characteristics
4.    PFAP would finance on-farm activities2 promoting better natural resources
management and sustainable agricultural development, as well as off-farm activities, such as
small agro-industries, cottage industries, apiculture, mushroom processing, and handicrafts.

Environmental Aspects
1.     The project would improve natural resource management, emphasizing soil and
water conservation, upgrade pastures and grasslands, and reforest and enrich native
woodlands.
2.    Prior to colonization, the northern Secano was covered with espino savanna, and its
southern regions, were covered with sclerophytic vegetation and nothofagus forest. In the
early 1700s, deforestation initiated and accelerated after 1760 with wheat area expansion. By
1900, most forests from the moderately sloping areas had been removed. Two centuries of
poor resource management has deforested over 90% of the area. Even the remaining
woodland patches, on the remote steep slopes, have been degraded by extraction and
overgrazing.
3.    Soils are severely eroded, suffering structural and fertility losses. In areas with
greater rainfall, some farmland has been converted into eucalyptus and pine plantations.
Large to medium farmers cultivate the irrigated valleys. While poor farmers (the project's
beneficiary group) over-cultivate and over-graze their small plots on eroded slopes. Without
targeted efforts to improve their conditions, small farmers will be forced to continue to
deteriorate their natural resources to the point of abandonment.
Environmental Impact Assessment
4.    With the concurrence of the Environmental Unit (LATEN), this project has been
classified as a "B" category project for purposes of environmental assessment (EA) on the
grounds that it may be necessary to design mitigative measures for environmental protection,
particularly in the interest of avoiding: (i) threats to bio-diversity which may result from
agricultural area expansion; (ii) conflicts over water supply rights where irrigation expansion
is contemplated; and (iii) threats to human health resulting from possible air and water
contamination due to increased agro-chemical use.' Since the project would alleviate poverty
through better natural resource management, environmental improvement is an explicit
project objective, rather than an externality. The application of proper environmental
1.    "Environmental Data Sheet - Chile: Secano Agricultural Development", February 17, 1993; and
Minutes of the IEPS Review Meeting, February 26, 1993.

screening, evaluation and impact assessment procedures, as defined in the Project
Implementation Manual, would mitigate the possibility of negative environmental impacts.
5.    Biodiversity. Since the micro-regional programs do not displace native woodland
with agriculture or plantations, there would be no threat to endangered or vulnerable flora
and fauna species,2 nor would contemplated forestry plantations or improved pastures on
degraded brush threaten fauna. Project-generated income increases would reduce pressure to
exploit fragile areas. The proposed micro-regions do not include, nor are adjacent to, the
forty CONAF identified sites within Regions V to VIII which contain significant
concentrations of endangered or rare flora species.
6.    Agro-chemicals. Increased fertilizer use is planned for crops in dryland and irrigated
rotations. In addition, expansion of specialty crops, such as, grapes, other fruits and
vegetables, would require additional pesticides and herbicides. The agricultural micro-
regional development programs comply with the Bank's Agricultural Pest Management
Guidelines. Due to limited areas of increased agro-chemical use, significant negative
impacts, through contamination of streams and aquifers, are not expected .
7.    Native woodlands. Four of the seven micro-regions contain an estimated 2,500 ha of
degraded native woodlands. The local population exploit these woodlands for fuelwood and
building materials. The project would not attempt to directly regulate this exploitation.
Instead, project forest conservation measures would be indirect, as project-related income
gains would reduce pressures to abuse the woodlands. In addition, the project proposes pilot
enrichment planting of native species in two micro-regions to test this forest management
technique.
8.    Roads. The programs for the seven micro-regions call for rural feeder road
improvement and rehabilitation. There would be no new road construction. Road
rehabilitation works would not destroy native vegetation or valuable crops. Negative
environmental impacts are expected to be insignificant, but would require M&E by the UTP.
9.    Assessment. The project would improve the environment by reducing erosion and
increasing soil fertility, without threatening native flora and fauna. The project would
introduce such soil conservation measures as, contour cultivation, gully stabilization,
substitution of extractive annual crops for perennial crops or pasture, and improved soil
cover and selective fertilizer use. These measures would stabilize water flows and improve
downstream aquifer levels in the dry season. Increased income generated by the project
should reduce pressures on native flora and fauna, thus increasing their regeneration
prospects compared with the "without-project" scenario. Higher income levels could also
have positive environmental impacts through improved sanitation, health and nutrition.
Under full compliance with project procedures for sustainable agriculture, no mitigative
measures for environmental protection would be needed.

Micro-regional Programs
10.   The micro-regional programs, evaluated as independent sub-projects, have been

investigation of the native flora and fauna; ecology, soils (erosivity, erosion rates, fertility

11.   Navidad. This micro-region covers 8,300 ha, over 90% of which has soils classified
VI and VII. The livelihood of half the population of 2,100 (465 farm families) depends upon
agricultural pursuits. The program calls for: improved agricultural practices with fruit

12.   La Estrella. This micro-region covers 9,700 ha, of which 75% has soils classed as

Technology Transfer
A. Background
1.    The National Institute for Agricultural Development (Instituto Nacional de Desarrollo
Agropecuario, INDAP), an autonomous public institution linked to the Ministry of
Agriculture, is the most important public provider of technology transfer and credit to small
farmers. INDAP's programs for eligible small farmers' and their families, are intended to
improve their productivity and family income, their living conditions and integration into the
national economy. INDAP extension programs are organized within agro-climatic zones with
rural municipalities displaying similar socio-productive characteristics.
2.    INDAP assists two kinds of small farmers: (i) those with sufficient resources and
capital, and/or technical capacity to increase their productivity and integrate into the national
economy in the short-terrn; and (ii) those with farms of 5 HRB (see footnote no. 1) or less,
having limited potential to increase family well-being. The relative importance of the latter
group to INDAP's program has increased from 53% in 1990 to about 75% in 1994. In
1988/89, INDAP, through its Technology Transfer Program (PTT), adopted a two-pronged
approach, which served the better-off clientele through the Integrated Technology Transfer
Program (PTTI), while placing greater emphasis on the poorer farmers through the Basic
Technology Transfer Program (PTTB). The World Bank's Small Farmer Services Project
(Ln. 3473-CH), which became effective in 1992, supports INDAP's assistance to farmers
with 5 or fewer HRB.
3.    PTT is executed by private consulting firms contracted directly by beneficiary farmers
and paid with INDAP subsidized voucher/coupons (bonos). A consultant can earn up to a
limited number of bonos and the bono's face value varies regionally. Participating firms,
included in a national registry of consultants, are selected through competitive bidding.

arrangements.

Land Titling
A. Background
General
11.   Despite Government efforts to address the issue during the 1980s, lack of land titles is
widespread among small farmers in Chile, particularly in the Secano. Most tenancy
problems arise from undivided inheritance and de facto land possession.' By the end of
1990, 108,000 rural tiles and 64,000 urban titles had been regularized by the Ministry of
National Property (Ministerio de Bienes Nacionales, MBN) and 72,000 titles by INDAP on
indigenous lands. However, it is estimated that 130,000 non-indigenous and 6,000
indigenous rural families remain without proper land titles. Although informal arrangements
are effective, these are not legally binding and limit small farmers' access to services and
benefits, such as: (i) the forestry subsidies under the Forestry Development Program; (ii)
irrigation water rights and subsidies for small irrigation works; (iii) subsidized housing
construction under Ministry of Housing programs; and (iv) private lending institutions.
12.   MBN is responsible for developing, conducting and enforcing the governmental policy
on issues related to the: (i) acquisition and control of public properties; (ii) maintenance of
an updated register of public goods; (iii) layout of specific development policies for fiscal
lands; and (iv) regularization and consolidation of small agricultural property.
13.   The MBN has a Bank-financed program, the Small Farmers Services Project (Loan
3473-CH), to expand its normal land titling activities and regularize 43,500 new titles over a
four-year period, which began 1992.' However, the land titling program has fallen behind
schedule, as indicated by recent figures issued by MBN3 (Table 1). The titling program thus
far has concentrated on irrigated area and producers along main trunk roads, effectively
excluding the Secano small farmers from the program.

14.   Several factors contributed to the program's poor performance. First, beneficiary

15.    Secondly, public competitive bidding for selecting MBN contracted firms to perform

24.   This component would ensure that most small farmers in the project area would have

sources. With secure tenure, the small farmers would be more likely to increase on-farm
investment and, therefore, not only improve their economic situation, but also increase the
market value of their land. Regularized land titles would promote an open land market.
Granting land titles would encourage sustainable natural resource management strategies as
small farmers with clear property rights would be keener to adopt conservation practices.
Risks
25.   Granting land titles would stimulate the development of the land market at the micro-
regional level which could threaten the small farm family. Although this is a necessary step
toward the consolidation of small properties into sustainable family farms, it may also
concentrate land into large corporate farms (mostly forest industries), threatening the survival
of viable, sustainable, family farms. There is also the risk that some farmers, upon
receiving land titles, may not participate in other project components. However, an
agreement between the potential beneficiaries and the UTP whereby the beneficiaries commit
their participation in other project component, particularly the forestry and silvo-pastoral
activities would mitigate this risk.
26.   Continued fragmentation of properties after land titles have been granted poses a
serious risk. Without measures ensuring minimum farm size, granting land titles does not
deter further fragmentation of small farms among heirs. In the Secano, the harsh economic
conditions and the deteriorated state of its natural resources require a minimum farm size for
the development of sustainable production systems and natural resource management
strategies.

III. INSTITUTIONAL STRENGTHENING FOR NATURAL RESOURCE
MANAGEMENT AND CONSERVATION
15.   The study, carried out by a consulting firm, in consultation with the Ministry, would
analyze the natural renewable resource (NRR) situation and a NRR management system of
the Ministry of Agriculture (MINAGRI). The consultancy would:
(a)   Define the institutional framework responsible for the NRR, i.e., soil, water,
flora (including forests) and fauna. Prepare a strategy and program, including
legal aspects, to establish the proposed framework.
(b)   Identify and propose NRR management instruments.
(c)   Design programs to strengthen NRR programs of concerned agencies.
16.   The consultancy would define the institutional framework in MINAGRI to orient and
administer NRR management and conservation. The study would analyze alternative
institutional arrangements.
17.   The proposed institutional framework would have the following functions:
(a)   maintain the monitoring systems with the purpose of having updated
information on the state and condition of the NRR;
(b)   propose and maintain policies at the central level, with regional development
for NRR conservation;



- 94 -                               Annex K
(c)   permanent evaluation of the impact on resource conservation of programs and
policies of a productive character, suggesting the corresponding adjustments;
(d)   maintain close links with production-related agencies and institutions, public
and private.
(e)   promote and coordinate research in NRR;
(f)   provide training and technology transfer of sustainable use of soil to
institutions, extension workers and farmers.
18.   The study would propose a strategy and program to establish the institutional
framework. The strategy would take into consideration the current institutional framework's
deficiencies as well as the complexity of the tasks, such as legal changes, program
restructuring or strengthening. When pertinent, legal modifications or drafts should be
19.   A review of instruments regulating NRR management and conservation is needed to
recommend modifications and new instruments which can provide the proposed institutional
framework the capacities to carry out its functions.
20.   The study would:
(a)   Review the present legislation and determine which aspects connected with the
NRR are properly covered by the laws and regulations in force and identify
which ones are not.
(b)   Establish deficiencies in terms of laws, regulations and rules, in order to cover
those aspects related to the NRR which are not oriented or controlled by the
present rules or by existing initiatives in public agencies (e.g., CONAMA).
(c)    Evaluate the existing instruments in terms of the NRR conservation, and
propose those considered necessary to cover the deficiencies.
21.   The study would determine for each MINAGRI institution or technical agency which
aspects require modification or strengthening to fulfill their NRR responsibilities. Human,
financial, infrastructure and equipment resources would be evaluated. Programs to overcome
their deficiencies would be proposed.
22.   The study would begin during the first year of project implementation and would
require 6 months. UTP would prepare the study's terms of reference. The study must be
performed by a consulting firm with experience in this work in Latin America and a legal
team familiar with Chilean legislation and institutional framework.
23.   The estimated cost of this consultancy is US$150,000.

Supervision Plan
Schedule
1.    A project launch workshop would be held to consolidate collaboration of public and
private entities at the national, regional and municipal levels required for successful project
execution. Representatives of Ministries and agricultural entities, regional and community
authorities, representatives of beneficiaries' associations, and NGOs would participate. This
workshop would be held immediately after Board approval. Subsequently, two supervision
missions would take place each year. The first, held in March/April, would focus on the
implementation progress of the previous year's POA, as described in the annual progress
report, submitted to the Bank by March 31. The second mission, held in August/September,
would review the proposed annual budget and work plan, included in the draft POA,
submitted to the Bank two weeks prior to mission departure. Both missions would include
field visits to project micro-regions, together with meetings with beneficiaries, community
mayors, regional officials and others.
2.    During the first year, supervision would cover organization, team development and
operational issues (i.e., procurement, disbursement and auditing procedures) with a view
toward strengthening the UTP. Bank supervision missions would assist in the selection of
implementing entities, helping to ensure the project's technical, financial, social, and
environmental soundness.
3.    The tentative supervision timetable and proposed Bank staff (supported by local
consultants) requirements are as follows:
Mission No. 1
Project Launch Workshop
January 1996.
4.    This mission would review the planned budget and implementation schedule for 1996
and focus on policy, regulations, administration, procurement, disbursements and auditing
procedures. In addition the mission would examine UTP, its establishment, functions,
including specific responsibilities (with detailed job descriptions) and clear rules of
communication. Particular attention would be paid to the establishment of the monitoring
and evaluation (M&E) unit, whose role in a pilot project is critical to continuously monitor



- 105 -                             Annex M
implementation progress and periodically assess the project's achievements. The mission
would comprise the Task Manager, a natural resource management specialist, an
agriculturalist, a forestry specialist and an institutional/organization/management specialist, as
required. Total 4-8 SW.
Mission No. 2
First Annual Budget and POA Review
March-April 1996.
5.    Full supervision mission would assess project start-up, budget review, implementation
planning and procurement program for 1996; analyze project's performance (compared with
agreed POA and performance indicators), especially with regard to institutional
arrangements, contracting for micro-regional programs, research activities and other
implementation issues. Mission participants would be the Task Manager and two to three of
the above specialists, as required. Total 3-6 SW.
Mission No. 3
Second Annual Budget and POA Review
August-September 1996.
6.    This mission would focus on budget and proposed POA review for 1997, paying
particular attention to important implementation issues. The mission would consist of the
Task Manager and one to two specialists, as required. Total 2-4 SW.
Mission No. 4
First Annual Progress Evaluation
March-April 1997
7.    This mission would analyze project performance during 1996 (compared with the
agreed POA and performance indicators). It would consist of the Task Manager, and one or
two specialists, as required. Total 2-4 SW.
Subsequent Supervision Missions
1997-2000
8.    Subsequent supervision missions would follow the pattern set by missions No. 3 and
4, respectively, except mission No. 5, scheduled for March-April 1998, which would
coincide with the project's Mid-Term Review.
9.    The estimated supervision input envisaged for the five year project implementation
period would be 25-50 staff weeks in the field. In addition, about 12 staff weeks would be
required annually for review and approval of project documents, implementation, follow-up
and correspondence. Supervision effort would be greatest during the first year and gradually
decrease as implementation became routine. To increase efficiency and contain supervision
cost, the continuity of the preparation team and task manager is essential.

SECANO RURAL POVERTY ALLEVIATION AND
NATURAL RESOURCES MANAGEMENT PROJECT
Economic Analysis
A. Benefits and Costs from Natural Resource Management and
Sustainable Agricultural Development and Rural Infrastructure Components
1.    The project would improve the production patterns of more than 22,000 ha of 1,345
small farmers (out of 2,845 within the seven selected micro-regions; see para. 13) and
develop sustainable small farmer production systems consistent with land capabilities. Area
under annual crops would be reduced by at least 3,400 ha, concentrated on better soils and
diversified into higher-value crops. The wheat area is conservatively estimated to decline
from 2,750 ha to about 930 ha. Once new production systems are fully developed and
farmers no longer rely on their on-farm production for subsistence, wheat production in the
project area would likely disappear. Project-financed irrigation works on 1,500 ha would
increase cropping intensities. On 5,000 non-irrigable ha, the project would introduce rainfed
cultivation practices aimed at proper soil and water conservation.
2.    Additionally, the project would increase area under grapes, the Secano's most
profitable crop, to about 2,100 ha, improve vineyard cultivation practices to avoid soil
erosion and introduce grape varieties with higher market value. Land released from crop
cultivation would be directed to pastures and forestry. At pilot project completion, it is
expected that there would be over 6,100 ha of properly-managed natural pastures, 3,570 ha
of improved pastures, 3,400 ha of silvo-pastoral production, 4,500. ha of planted forest and
100 ha of woodland renovation.
3.    By promoting commodities for which there is strong domestic and export demand, the
project would integrate a relatively marginal region into the main economy. The project area
is expected to produce annually over US$2.6 million of vegetables and pulses for domestic
markets. In addition, US$3.0 million of wine and nearly US$1.0 million of lamb would be
produced, an important share of which would be exported. Additionally, project-promoted
forestry activities would produce US$36.0 million of roundwood or posts for local fruit
industry, and US$27.0 million of pulpwood for the pulp industry, throughout the project's
life.

B. Internal Economic Rate of Return (IERR)
4.    Natural resources and rural infrastructure components. The internal economic rate of
return for these components is 20.7%. Working hypotheses of likely beneficiary choices
were developed to estimate the internal economic rate of return of the project's natural
resource management and sustainable agricultural and rural infrastructure components.
5.    This estimate incorporates the costs of on-farm investments (US$13.0 million),
technology transfer (US$2.0 million), rural infrastructure (US$2.4 million) and the Project
Technical Unit (US$1.1 million). The IERR includes economic benefits derived from: (i)
crop productivity gains, resulting from improved practices; (ii) crop diversification into
higher-valued crops; (iii) decreased production costs; and (iv) reduced marketing margins,
due to better rural infrastructure were taken into account and applied to indicative farm
models, representative of project beneficiaries in the Secano.' This estimate is conservative,
since it does not consider the benefits accruing from reduced soil run-off and erosion.
Furthermore, the analysis did not capture "without project" productivity loses (as a project
benefit) due to declining soil fertility.
6.    Off-farm investment component: As beneficiaries would determine which activities
would be financed, an ex-anzte estimation of costs and benefits of the off-farm investment
component can be made by analyzing a sample of likely off-farm projects. The preparation
team found that most proposals would be small, ranging between US$25,000 and
US$100,000, and economically highly feasible, having estimated internal economic rates of
return above 25%. Since only technically and economically sound proposals would receive
project finance, this component would likely increase the project's internal economic rate of
return. To ensure economic viability, a threshold of a minimum estimated internal economic
rate of return would be applied as a selection criteria to sub-project proposals.
7.    Technical assistance, institutional strengthening and training components: Technical
assistance would help beneficiaries execute off-farm poverty alleviation activities and support
Government decentralization efforts through institutional strengthening. The economic
impact of these interventions was not evaluated as the portfolio of off-farm investments
would be defined at implementation by the beneficiaries and the benefits arising from
facilitating rural municipalities in the execution of their responsibilities, as well as
beneficiary training, are not quantifiable.
8.    Research and studies components: These components' benefits would stem from: (i)
research for increasing the economic development of the Secano, given its special social and
ecological circumstances; (ii) an improved Governrment natural resource management
monitoring system; and (iii) an analysis of local commodity markets. Preliminary estimates

C. Economic and Financial Considerations
9.    The internal economic rate of return used base costs plus physical contingencies, less
taxes and price contingencies. The opportunity cost of capital was 12%.
10.   Pricing. The rate of exchange of the Chilean peso is determined in the open market.
As there are no major trade restrictions, domestic prices tend to correspond to border
economic values. For the economic analysis, the criteria and assumptions used by the
Ministry of Planning (MIDEPLAN) were applied. Border prices were used for all tradeables
produced by the project, based on the projections of MIDEPLAN and exporting firms
(which, for wheat and maize, are lower than Bank projections). A conversion factor of 0.90
was used for inputs and machinery.
11.   Labor. While the project would increase on-farm and off-farm employment in the
selected micro-regions, unemployment and under-employment would not be eliminated.
Therefore, MIDEPLAN's labor cost assumptions were used, including unskilled and semi-
skilled labor shadow prices, estimated at 0.66 of the market wage rate. The market rate for
skilled labor was assumed to reflect its opportunity cost.
12.   Yields. In the without-project situation, yields were assumed to remain constant,
although there are indications that, as a result of declining soil fertility, yields have been
declining.
13.   Adoption Rates. During the first year of implementation, project participation of
between 5% and 10% of the small farmers in the selected micro-regions is expected.
Participation would increase in subsequent years, until the project year five, when an
estimated 47 percent of small farmers in the selected micro-region would participate in the
project. Relatively high participation rates are expected due to the: (i) farmers' interest
expressed in the participatory diagnosis carried out during project preparation; (ii) attractive
incentive packages offered; (iii) relatively low risk the production packages represent to
farmers' subsistence; and (iv) high probability of successful implementation due to system-
oriented technical assistance. Moreover, the project's flexibility in implementation would
allow for design adjustments to address constraints to farmers' adoption.
14.   Farmers' Income. The project is expected to more than double family on-farm
income. Nevertheless, given their impoverishment, even with these on -farm income gains,
a significant proportion of the families would continue to live in poverty. Farmners with off-

farm oriented survival strategies would benefit least from increases in on-farm earnings. But
these farmers' incomes would increase through project-financed off-farm activities.
15.   Inappropriate cultivation practices, combined with soil fertility losses and low natural
pasture yields, depress Secano most on-farm income below the "extreme poverty line".
According to MIDEPLAN, the annual per capita "poverty line" income is US$723 and the
"extreme poverty line" income is US$362.2 Applying these benchmarks,3 most farmers in
the project area have on-farm incomes substantially below the "extreme poverty line". Only
small farmers (i) with farms larger than 20 ha4 (less than 25% of the total) and/or (ii)
engaged in vineyard and intensive horticulture cultivation have higher on-farm incomes. In
the absence of remedial action, the generally low on-farm Secano incomes would decline
further due to the deterioration of the region's natural resources.
16.   Applying indicative farm models' to assess the project's impact, farms with vineyards
would obtain the highest income levels, about twice the poverty level. Farms, combining
agricultural (with 0.5-0.8 ha under irrigation) and livestock production, are likely to earn an
on-farm income ranging between the poverty line to twice the poverty line. On-farm income
of farms, combining agro-livestock production with silvo-pastoral activities, would
approximate the poverty line.
17.   On-farm income earnings for farms in areas with highly degraded natural resources
would be derived primarily from forestry, reserving agricultural production for home
consumption. Although the project would increase on-farn incomes, the level would remain
below the "extreme poverty line". Therefore, the project would increase these farmers'
incomes through the PFAP-financed off-farm investments. Additionally, the project would
generate annual employment for 1,000 wage laborers in agriculture, livestock and forestry.
