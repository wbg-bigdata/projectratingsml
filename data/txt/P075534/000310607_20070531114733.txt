A. STRATEGIC CONTEXTAND RATIONALE

                                        1.Country andSector Issues

1.       Profile of the Jordan Rift Valley: The Jordan Rift Valley is an integral part of the Great Rift
Valley andprovides a globally critical landbridgebetweenAfrica, Europe, andAsia that supportsa large
variety of ecologically diverse habitatsofinternational importanceandfunnels millions ofmigratingbirds
between these continents each year. The Valley is of strategic economic importance, linking the five
countries of Egypt, Israel, Jordan, the West Bank, and Syria, which share many o f its natural resources,
including the Jordan River, Dead Sea, and Gulf of Aqaba. Its critical geographical location and great
scenic interest, combined with the most productive agricultural land resources in Jordan has made it a
focal area for infrastructure and tourism development and land conversion, and climate change all of
which threaten its unique ecological and cultural values. The Government of Jordan (GOJ) has long
recognizedthis dilemma and is seeking ways to secure the Valley's economic and ecological integrityfor
the benefit of its people.

2.       The concept of Intenrated EcosystemManagement (IEM): Inthe context of this project, IEM i s
defined as a holistic and participatory approachto land use that balances and manages ecological, social
and economic components of ecosystems to ensure that biodiversity and ecological processes can be
sustainedunder developmentpressure and social change. This approachwill be applied inthe JordanRift
Valley through small-scale, targetedinterventions inthe current landuse planningframework andthrough
the settingup ofanetwork ofconservationsites that are developedas models ofthe IEMapproach.

3.       Sector issues: There have been a number of individual environmental and resource protection
projects inthe JordanRift Valley (JRV). However, most ofthese havenot beenbiodiversity-centered and
have not accommodated all three `pillars' of IEM: ecological, social and economic. Furthermore, with
one notable exception, there has beenno attemptto view the RiftValley indevelopmentterms as a single
ecological system. This one exceptionis the Rift Valley Master Plan, developedin2004, which is a large-
scale designation map that defines preliminary development zones and shows key conservationsites (see
component 1).While the planrepresents a major milestone in development control, it currently lacks the
consultativeplanningprocesses that would enable it to function effectively. As a result, the degradation
of key habitats continues, with increasing loss o f biodiversity. Tourism development, in particular, is
intensive, especially alongthe Dead SeaandRed Sea coasts, andis the reasonfor most ofthe large-scale
infrastructure projects and recent depletion of water resources. Ironically, the Rift Valley has enormous
potential for eco-tourism and this could be harnessed to support the project objectives. Most local
communities throughout the Rift Valley rely on agriculture and pastoralism and many have become
marginalized in the face of development pressure, which has reduced and dissected grazing areas,
depleted water resources and generally degradedlargetracts of land. Nature- relatedbusiness enterprises
offer potentially better livelihood options for the future.

4.       Strategic choices in project design: The Jordan Rift Valley is an economically important area
under heavy developmentpressure and used by a multitude of stakeholders. Any intervention to secure
its ecological integrity must therefore be realistic in scope and targeted at measures that are critical to
retainingkey ecologicalfunctions. Giventhis scenario, the project designwas basedaroundthe following
strategic choices:

            (a) Protection of key biodiversity sites: The remaining, leastdisturbedareas ofnaturalhabitat
                 inthe RiftValley arethe `skeleton' ofits ecologicalintegrity. Theyarethemainreasonit
                 continuesto hold populations of typical species and to host millions of migratory birds.
                 The protection and management of these habitats was therefore considered to be a
                 priority inthe project's design. A network of sites is to be establishedalong the whole


                                                       1

               length of the Valley, consisting of four high status protectedareas, meetingnationally
               agreedcriteriafor protectedarea (PA) designation, and seven Special ConservationAreas
               (SCAs), which are generally smaller and have more specific ecological roles (see
               component 2). Creating this network is seen as achievable becausethe sites are already
               earmarked on the Rift Valley master plan, they incorporate no private land and the
               implementingagency, RSCN, has a 40-year history of protectedarea establishment and
               management.

           (b) Targeted intervention in the Rip Valley land-use planning system: The protected area
               networkwill not function efficiently ifeach site lies inamatrix of developed or degraded
               land; the well recognized "island effect" will limit their ecological viability. Influencing
               land use around and between protected areas to safeguard or create supportingnatural
               features and preventdamaging activities, is importantto keepingthe conservation value
               of the JRV intact. Rather than trying to revolutionize land use planning throughout the
               JRV, which would be difficult to achieve, the project limits its interventionto supporting
               the embryo master plan, which already earmarks the key biodiversity sites for
               development control. Itwill assist the JRV to develop a consultativeplanningprocess in
               support ofkey site protection(see component 1).

           (c) IEM as the approach to land-use and protected area management: As development
               pressure will likely continue inthe JRV for many years to come, the applicationof IEM
               principles to land use and protected areas will help to ensure that biodiversity
               conservation is not viewed as inimical to development but part of it. This inturn will
               encourage greater public and political support.          It will also encourage greater
               participationof localpeople and enablethemto have some influence over the fate oftheir
               natural resources. This is particularly important for the marginalized agricultural and
               pastoralcommunities inthe RiftValley who areunlikely to benefitgreatly fromthe large
               plannedinfrastructureprojects.ForthemPASand SCAs offer more appropriate andlong-
               term opportunitiesfor improvedlivelihoodsthrough eco-tourismand other nature-based
               businesses.

           (d) Climate proojng of the conservation planning and management as part of the IEM
               approach: There i s "very high confidence" (as laid down by the IPCC) that climate
               change is already affectingliving systems. The responses ofbothfauna andflora spanan
               array of ecosystems and organizationalhierarchies, from the species to the community
               levels. Recent scientific research on global meta-analyses revealed significant range
               shifts averaging 6.1 krn per decade toward the poles (or meters upward), and significant
              mean advancement of spring events by 2.3 days per decade. Jordan is vulnerable to
               climate change, and some features of projected climate change in Jordan include: (i)
               increase in temperature of 1-2 degrees centigrade by 2030-2050; (ii)            increase in
               evaporation accompaniedby soil moisturereduction; (iii)    diminishedrechargeofaquifers
               and oases, (iv) projectedshrinkage of grasslands (which extend overlo% of Jordan); and
               (v) projectedshift of semi-aridrangeland (which extend over 80% of Jordan) to become
              arid desert. Improved conservation planning and management in the JRV, based on an
              assessment of climate change implications on biodiversity is both prudent and a
              necessity.

5.    World Bank and GEF engagement: The JRV is suffering from severe landscape degradation
because of unsustainable naturalresources managementpractices. This inturn has ledto a worseningof

the local communities' livelihoods.The World Bank involvement inthis project i s intendedto restore the
ecologicalintegrity ofthe JRV, which will help restore socio-economic livelihoodsand inturn contribute
to globalenvironmentbenefits.

6.       This project will have the potential to inform the proposed Red-Sea Dead Sea Study. The
                                                                                 -
objectiveofthe Study is to investigatethe feasibility of water transfer fromthe RedSea at Aqabailat to
the DeadSea inorder to reversethe rapid environmentaldegradationof the DeadSearegionas well as to
examine options for drinking water production and electricity generationfor economic growth. Three of
the proposedprotectedareas inthe project(Fifa, JabalMa'sudaandQatar), andassociated SCAs lie south
of the Dead Sea. The ecological and socio-economic assessments, coupled with the holistic and
participatoryapproachto landuse beingpioneeredusingthe IEMapproachwill informthe environmental
andsocialassessmentofthe Study.

7.       InJordan, the World Bank and GEF have beeninvolved inthe successful implementationof
previouscompletedandongoingprojectsinthe field ofbiodiversityconservation. Some examples include
the Conservationo f the Dana and Azraq ProtectedAreas project; andthe Conservationof Medicinal and
Herbal Plants project. GEF involvement in this integrated ecosystem management project allows for
valuable lessons learnedfrom previous andongoingprojectsto be incorporatedinto the proposedproject.
It further provides the opportunity for an exchange of ideas and cross-fertilization with other GEF
projects (such as the Soaring Birds Project) thus giving the possibility for the creation of an integrated
ecosystem management network, including surrounding countries and regions. Thus far, national efforts
to introduce, regulate, andinstitutionalizeintegratedecosystemmanagement are very limited. Inaddition,
there i s little integration in Jordan between conservation and rural development activities. Training
programs addressing the issues and enhancing the knowledge base hardly exist. Similarly, the
involvement of communities and local stakeholders in ecosystemmanagement and land use planning is
very limited, requiringawell-plannedawareness-raisingprogram.

8.       The projectand countrypriorities: The establishment ofprotectedareas inthe Jordan RiftValley
supports the recommendations of the National Biodiversity Action Plan, adopted by the government in
2003, and the earlier National Environment Strategy (NES) (1992). It also helps Jordan to meet its
obligations under the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), the UN Framework Convention on
Climate Change, and the Convention on Migratory Species (CMS) by furthering national strategies for
biodiversityconservation. The InitialCommunicationReportto UNFCCCrecognizedthe needto expand
Jordan's protectedareas (as identifiedinthe NES), and alsothe needto estimate impactofclimate change
on the water resources of Jordan. These plans and strategies also reinforce the importance of securing
communityparticipationand communitybenefits inbiodiversity programs, principlesthat are an integral
partofthe project's IEM-centeredapproach. The project's attentionto socio-economic programslikewise
supports the policies of the National Poverty Alleviation Strategy (2002), which emphasizes the need to
create more employmentopportunitiesinrural areas. Furthermore, the intentionto capitalize onthe eco-
tourism potentialof the proposedprotectedarea network will support the Ministryof Tourism's recently
adoptednationaltourismstrategy, which encouragesfurther development ofthis nichesector.

9.       The project is also in line with the Jordan Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) for the period
2006-2010, which includesprogramclusters focused on supporting local development throughincreased
access to services and economic opportunities.Issues ofgender, environment, water and energy are cross-
cuttingthroughallthe programclusters.Byaddressing environmentalissues andpromotinglinkages with
local development, the project will show that, with proper capacities and appropriate financing
mechanisms, biodiversity and conversationefforts can have positive impacts on the livelihoods of poor
populations.

                                    3. Summary of ProjectComponents

 18.     More detailedcomponent descriptions, withtheir rationales, are presentedinAnnex 4.

Component 1:Assessment and Planningfor IntegratedEcosystemManagement

 19.     The objective of this component i s to lay the groundwork for integrated ecosystemmanagement
in the JRV by introducing biodiversity conservation and community participation measures into the
existing landuse planningframework, usingthe proposedprotected areas and special Conservationareas
as pilot sites. It will also support the development of national policies for protected areas inJordanthat
will embracethe principles ofIEM(see sub-component la).

20.      Buildingonprevious survey data, detailedassessmentswill be undertakeninthe PASand SCAs
to determine their ecological, social and economic status and to finalize their boundaries. This
information will be usedto update and develop the existing Land Use Master Plan (LUMP) for the Rift
Valley, prepared by the Jordan Valley Authority (JVA) during 2004, which delineates known
conservation sites (It will also be vital for shaping the management strategies under component 2). In
particular, it will be usedto formulate the guidelines and procedures necessaryto make the master plan
designations part of a consultative planningprocess. RSCN will partner with the JVA to develop these
mechanisms and apply them to approved conservationsites, building on its current role as a member of
the Joint Steering Committee for the National Land Use Strategy'. By targetingthe intervention inthis
way, the project's landuse aspirations are achievableandthe process will strengthen the JVA's planning
capabilities. It will also ensure that the network of conservation sites, vital for securing the ecological
integrity of the Rift Valley, is mainstreamed into development control and given recognition and
protectionunderthe planningsystem.

Sub-componentla: ProtectedArea Policy Development

21.      Jordandoes not, as yet, havean officially endorsedpolicy onprotectedareas. There is, however,
a draft policy framework produced under the auspices of the Ministry of Planning in 1996. This
framework was prepared with technical guidance and financial support from USAID. Its preparation
involved a number of workshops with relevant Jordanian organizations, both government and non-
government, inorder to secure consensus on key policy issues andpriorities. The Rift Valley project will
facilitate the review andupdatingofthis policy initiative inorder to produce a revised set ofpolicies for
adoption through the Ministry of Environment. A clear set of policies will provide a much needed
national vision for protected areas and a frame of reference for future strategies and legislation; and, in
particular, it will enable the government to mainstream the community and IEM approaches it is
pioneering inthe region. The project team would work jointly with the staff of the MOE, which has
recently establisheda policy unitunder its natureconservationdirectorate.

Component 2: Development of a network of biodiversity conservation sites, embodying the
principles of integratedecosystemmanagement

23.      The objective ofthis component is to legally establish the network of protectedareas and special
conservation areas defined under the land use planning framework above, and to develop management
systems for these sites that becomeworking models of IEMprinciples.

24.      A total of four new protected areas and seven special conservation areas will comprise the
network, representing a cross section of all key habitats and ecosystemtypes inthe Rift Valley. The PAS
cover atotal area o f 56,950 hectaresandare all delineated onthe JVA's landuse masterplan.As for the
SCAs, the delineations andtotal areas willbeagreeduponwith consultationwithrelative stakeholders.

25.      The proposed protected areas from north to south are: (1) the oak-clad valley o f the Yarmouk
River; (2) the sub-tropical vegetation communities at Fifa; (3) the arid mountain systems of Jabal
Masuda; and (4) the mudflats of Qatar. They are all of national or international importance and meet
agreed national criteria for PA designation. They will be legally established by RSCN through the
Ministry of Environment, under the Environment Law. RSCN will apply the lessons learned from
previous GEF experiences and involve key stakeholders in the designation process and use socio-
economic programs to gain the support and involvement of local people. It will also apply the business
and marketing approaches recently developed under its `Wild Jordan' division to pioneer the use of
economic incentives to pave the way for site designation. These income generating initiatives would
include eco-tourismactivities and might include havingproducts suchas handicrafts, organically-farmed
fruits and herbs, jewelry, textile and others. Some income generation initiatives will runparallel to the
designation process to demonstrate that PAScan be economic assets if developed inthe right way. Such
economic initiatives will be integrated with conservation and social programs, in line with IEM

                         5. Alternatives Considered and Reasonsfor Rejection

49.     The other options considered inthe project's formulationbut abandonedwere:

(a) A larger roleinthe land-useplanningprocess

The project intended to establish a legal and institutionalfi-amework for biodiversityconservation inthe
national planning system. However, this was considered to be beyond the scope and capacity of the
project and effort was targeted at developing consultativeplanning processes only for the existing Rift
Valley landuse master plan, where it specifically relates to designatedbiodiversity sites (see component
1).This was regardedas achievable by the project partners, while still havingthe potentialto influence
nationalpolicies.

(b) A largernumber ofprotectedareas

Originally, six protected areas were proposed for inclusion in the project but this was reduced to 4
following a review during the preparationphase. Four was considered to be the maximumnumber that
could be established effectively within the time fi-me and resources of the project and without over-
burdening the capacity of RSCN as the management agency.

(c) Conservationplans for SCAs

All seven SCAs identifiedinthe RiftValley were to have community-drivenconservation plans prepared
under the project but this was later reduced to four to avoid overstretchingproject resources and PMU
capabilities and to provide scope for replication and upscaling. Recognizing the value of SCAs for
establishing ecological contiguity, the seven sites will all be assessed and designated but the four sites
selectedfor conservationplans will eachreflect auniquecontextfor learning.

(d) Capacity-buildingprograms

A large capacity program was originally considered, involving many agencies and many generic
programs, especially inland-useplanning, agriculturaldevelopment andthe water sector: it also included
the development of an extension service for Rift Valley farmers. Following the rationalizationofproject
objectives and the consultation exercises carried out for the project document, the capacity building
elements were scaled down, integrated within each component (rather than a standalone component) and
targetedto perceivedneeds. They are now focused on the innovative elements ofthe project where little
in-country experience exists; Le., IEMprinciples inland-use planning and protectedareas management,
community-drivenconservation planning, conservation training of agricultural extension staff, business
planningandprivate sector partnerships.

(e) ImplementationArrangements for Specialized `Conservation' Activities

Some ofthe `specialized' conservationactivitiesrelatedto ecologicalbaseline assessments, preparationof
management plans, training needs assessments relatedto the implementationof management plans, and
capacity-building and raising awareness on the application of the integrated ecosystem management
approach will be undertakenby RSCN.The option of recruitinganexternalfirmto undertake these were
rejected for the following reasons: (a) RSCN has a repository of skills related to these specialized
conservation activities with a proven track record, both nationally and regionally. These skills can be
effectively applied and utilized by the project, while at the same time building incremental capacity for
themto take onthe additionalchallenge of anextendedprotectedareanetworkfor long-termmanagement
of these sites. Conversely, outsourcing these activities will require a fragmented approachto these issues


                                                     12

which will undermine the quality of product, advice and effectiveness; (b) these `specialized'
conservationactivities are integrally linked to the long-term management of these protected areas. The
capacity built through the process of engagement in these activities is critical to the long-term
management of the protected areas (which is dynamic and evolves over time). Outsourcing of these
activities will result in products (reports) but will not engender the capacity and dynamics needed for
long-term managementoftheprotectedareas; and (c) repeatedlessons from conservationhaveshownthat
institutional sustainability is jeopardized when these core activities are outsourced. Jordan has been
forward looking inthis respect, and RSCN has been accordedthe legal remit by MOE, approved by the
Council ofMinisters, as the institutionto establish, develop, andmanageprotectedareas. The project will
seek to fortify this. To ensure oversight on quality of outputs/adviseproduced by RSCN, there will be an
oversightkafeguard function exercisedthrough the Steering Committee and IUCN (World Conservation
Union).

            JORDAN: Integrated EcosystemManagement inthe Jordan Rift Valley

                         Annex 1:Country and Sector or Program Background


Jordan Rvt Valleyphysical and ecologicalcharacteristics

1.       The JordanRift Valley i s partofthe Great Rift Valley, and extendsfrom Yarmouk inthe north,to
the Gulf of Aqaba inthe south, over a lengthof 370 km (see map, Annex 19) It is about 10 km wide in
the north, narrowingto 4 km inthe central section, and widening againto about 20 km inthe south. The
central section cradles the Dead Sea, a large and highly saline water body, lying morethan 400 mbelow
sea level: the lowest water body on Earth. For the purpose of the project, the Jordan Rift Valley (JRV)
includes the valley floor along the whole length of the Kingdom up to the country boundary and the
adjacent escarpment onthe Jordanian side.

2.      As an integralpart ofthe Great Rift Valley, the JRV plays a crucial role inthe function ofthis
globally importantecologicalcorridor. Itprovidesthe landbridgethat funnels millions ofmigratingbirds
betweenthe continents of Africa and Europe each year. The sharp physicalboundaries of the JRV are
clearly visible from the air andprovidea navigationalguiding systemfor migratingbirds. The habitatsit
contains alsoprovidevitalrestingandrefuelingstations, without which they would beunableto complete
their long journeys. Not surprisingly, Birdlife International's document on Important Bird Areas inthe
Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan (2000), suggests 27 sites for Jordan of which 17 are located in the Rift
Valley.

3.      Apart from its significance for birds, the JRV holds many large and internationally important
ecosystems, including desert, mountains, wetlands, sea and forest. These includethe Gulf of Aqaba, the
Jordan and Yarmouk river systems andthe DeadSea. The Dead Sea itself is notedas one ofthe World's
"biodiversity hot spots" because its extremely harsh environment has engendered a high level of
endemism. There are also numerous habitats of national and regional importance that are vital to
maintainingthe Valley's ecological integrity, including six notablewetlands (The Directory ofWetlands
of the Middle East, Budieri, 2005).

Sector IssuesAffecting Biodiversity of the Jordan Rvt Valley

Developmentandloss ofbiodiversitv

4.      Runningthe full lengthof the country, the JRV is a strategicallyimportant corridor not only for
ecologicalreasons but also for Jordan's economic development. It is a primary communications corridor,
linking Jordan's only coastal resort with the capital, Amman, and with other tourist attractions like the
Dead Sea and Baptism site. It also provides the border crossing points into Israel and Palestine. The
northernpart has beenintensively developed for agricultureandgrows large quantities of vegetables and
fruit and parts of the Dead Sea coast have recently beentransformed into 5-star tourist resorts. Further
south, there are industrial complexes harvesting minerals from the Dead Sea and making a range o f
chemical products. Other major development projects are being considered, include the `Red-to-Dead
Canal', which proposes to link the Red Sea to the Dead Sea via a large pipeline in order to generate
electricityfor desalinatingseawater.

5.      Increasing development pressures within the JRV, combined with population growth, are
acceleratinghabitat degradation, climate change, species loss and the general depletion of key natural
resources, especially water. They are also affecting the livelihoods of local pastoral and agricultural
communities, who now find traditional grazing areas, agriculturallands and hunting areas dissected and


                                                   23

degraded by infrastructureandurbanexpansion. As a result, overgrazing, over huntingand deforestation
are widespread, adding more pressure on remainingnaturalareas. Previous GEF studies inthe country
have revealed the presence of many globally threatened species, including several endemics. A full
descriptionofthe biodiversityofthe JRV is given inAnnex 20

ProtectedAreas andEcologicalIntegrity

6.       As a single, linear system, the protectionofthe remainingnaturalhabitats alongthe lengthofthe
JRV is critical to maintaining its ecological integrity. A number of studies have identified the most
important habitats within the Valley, of which the most comprehensive are the Important Bird Area
initiative, carried out by Birdlife International's regional office in 1999 and the Protected Area Review
camed out by RSCN between 1997 and 2000. These studies were drawn upon for the Jordan Country
Study on Biological Diversity (2000) and for the National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (2003),
both of which highlight the exceptional ecological value of the JRV and recommend the setting up of a
networkofprotectedareas.

7.       Under the project's preparationphase, consultants reviewed and rationalizedthe recommended
sites with RSCN and other stakeholders, arriving at a network consisting of six protected areas of
national/ internationalimportance (i.e. meetingagreed nationalcriteria for PAS)and nine important bird
areas (IBAs), as shownonthe map inAnnex 19. Two ofthe PASare already establishedand operational,
including the Dana Nature Reserve, the renowned model of sustainable biodiversity conservation
developed under an earlier GEF program. Two of the IBAs also fall within the boundaries of these
existing protected areas. All critical habitat-types are represented inthe proposed network, from arid
mountainsystems, fast and slow flowing rivers, sub-tropical vegetation communities, specialized forest
types (e.g. deciduous oak forest), mud flats and small freshwater wetlands. All of the sites occupy
government landandthere is no issue ofprivatelandtenure. Furtherdetails ofall sites are giveninAnnex
20.

8.       RSCNhas a national mandate to set up and manage protected areas, and the designation of the
four sites inthe RiftValley would follow well-tried andtested procedures. IBAs have not, to date, been
officially recognized by Government, except by their inclusioninthe Rift Valley Land Use Master Plan
(see below). As sites of lesser status to nationalprotectedareas, the intentioni s to pioneertheir protection
through the planning system and through community empowerment, thus helping to mainstream
biodiversityconservation intothe generaldevelopment process.

LandUse Planninginthe RiftVallev

9.       The need for Land Use Planning(LUP) capacity and for a nationalland use plan in Jordan has
already been stressed inthe NationalEnvironment Strategy (1991) andthe NationalEnvironmentAction
Plan (1996). The Jordan Country Study on Biodiversity (1998) states inthis regard: "A comprehensive
land use scheme is lacking on the national, regional and local levels. Lack of land use planning creates
serious threats for the environment as well as for public health." This is reiterated in the National
Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (2003), which strongly promotes integrated landuse planningand
related capacity building for a "biodiversity-oriented" society. Progress towards meeting these
aspirations, however, has been slow at the nationallevel and experience inconsultativelanduse planning
processesis stillvery limited.

10.      Some progress, however, has been made in the Jordan Rift Valley. In 1994, the Jordan Valley
Authority carried out a study on Integrated Development of the Jordan Rift Valley (World Bank
supported), which was the first planning exercise to recognize the Rift Valley as a single ecological
feature. However, its recommendations were largely site specific and it did not detail ways of integrating


                                                     24

natureconservationinto broaderlanduse strategies. More recently (2004), the JVA identifiedthe needfor
"land zoning" inthe Rift Valley and contracteda consultantto preparea LandUse Master Plan(LUMP)
for the JVA mandate area, with support from USAID. The LUMP includes an Atlas and provides the
near-future landdesignationpattern, by land use category, for the Strategic Plan 2003-2008 period. This
planis highlyimportantto the project becauseit delineatesallthe keyconservation sites mentioned above
and provides a basic framework for the mainstreaming biodiversity conservation into the Rift Valley's
planning system. At present, however, it is essentially a designation map and consultative mechanisms
haveyet to be developedthat enable stakeholdersto participate inthe planningprocess.

11.      LUP atthe locallevel is virtually nonexistent.M O Ais currently involvedina government funded
program of on-farm agricultural development through preparation and implementation of conservation
plans, farm plans, water harvestingmeasures, andagricultural roads. RSCNhas alsotaken some landuse
management measures, notably in relation to Protected Areas, including community-brokered grazing
agreements. Both sets of measures were based on (mostly ecological) resource surveys but only the latter
have been the result of consensus-building with local stakeholders. The process leading to these
arrangements is incompliance with standards of local landuse planningbuthasnot been institutionalized
bythe relevantplanningauthorities.

Local Communities

12.      There are small villages and towns scattered throughout the Rift Valley, with a greater
concentration in the north, where agriculture is more productive. There are also nomadic communities,
which seasonally move their livestock between the valley bottom and the mountain ridges. All of the
identifiedconservationsites havevillages immediately aroundthem or closebyand, withthe exception of
Fifa and Yarmouk (both in military zones); they are used to greater or lesser extents to support
subsistence livelihoods for settled and nomadic populations. Faced with development pressures, land
fragmentation and changing economic conditions, these subsistence communities are finding it more
difficult to maintain sustainable livelihoods. This has led to overgrazing of rangelands and illegal
woodcutting and a general degradation of the resource base. They have been largely left out of the
planningprocesses implementedso far andgenerally marginalized indevelopmentdecisions.

13.      Clearly, there is a need to develop alternative livelihoods for many communities in the Rift
Valley that are more sustainableand compatible with the protection of biodiversity. Positive experiences
withthe development and introductionof alternative livelihoodactivities canbe drawnfrom the Dana and
Azraq GEF Project (1994-1997) and subsequent GEF and USAIDprojects, which have pioneeredthe use
of protected areas as economic assets for local people. Valuable lessons are available with regard to the
creation of products and services targeted at tourists, an important consideration in the JRV, as it has
extraordinary scenic and cultural attractions. The GOJ is anxious to promote the expansion of the eco-
tourism sector (National Tourism Strategy 2005 -2010) and opportunities exist to build on the RSCN
facilities andprogramsalready operational inMujibandDana onthe RiftValley escarpment.

IEMandCapacity-Building Needs

14.      So far there have been no developmentprojects inthe JordanRift Valley (or elsewhere) that are
models of IEM, i.e., where biodiversity protection is fully integrated with social and economic
development. The closest examples are RSCN's protectedareas, most of which now have outreachand
socio-economic programs linked to Conservation management. They are, however, still weak at the
community involvement level, tendingto limit participation to employment and smallbusiness initiatives,
rather than the community management of resources.       Other NGOs have carried out successful social
developmentprojects inthe Valley, notably JOHUD, and are more skilledthan RSCN inthis area, but
have yet to integrate biodiversity considerations into their programs. There is therefore a general


                                                   25

weakness inIEMapproaches and especially inthe full integrationof local communities into the process.
No community group in Jordan has yet been enabled to manage important biodiversity resources
independently. At the planning level, the JVA has made important progress in mapping and land use
zoning but it has yet to develop the processes that ensure biodiversity issues are routinely reflected in
planningdecisions andthat givecommunitiesthe opportunityto beconsulted andparticipate.

Climate Change Impacts

15.     There i s "very high confidence" (as laid down by the IPCC) that climate change i s already
affecting living systems. The responses of both fauna and flora span an array of ecosystems and
organizationalhierarchies, from the speciesto the community levels. Recent scientific researchon global
meta-analyses revealed significant range shifts averaging 6.1 kmper decade towardthe poles (or meters
upward), and significant meanadvancement of spring events by 2.3 days per decade. Jordanis vulnerable
to climate change, and some features o f projected climate change in Jordan include: (i)     increase in
temperature of 1-2 degrees centigrade by 2030-2050; (ii)   increase in evaporation accompanied by soil
moisturereduction; (iii)diminishedrechargeof aquifers and oases, (iv) projectedshrinkage ofgrasslands
(which extend overlo% of Jordan); and (v) projected shift of semi-arid rangeland (which extend over
80% of Jordan) to become arid desert. Improved conservation planning and management in the JRV,
based on an assessment of climate change implications on biodiversity is both prudent and a necessity.

Component 1:Assessment andPlanningfor IntegratedEcosystemManagement
(Total USM$1.43: GEF USM$0.61 and Co-Jnancing USM$0.82)

3.       The objective of this component is to lay the groundwork for integratedecosystemmanagement
in the JRV by introducing biodiversity conservation and community participation measures into the
existing landuse planningframework, usingthe proposed protectedareas and special conservationareas
as pilot sites. It will also support the development of nationalpolicies for protectedareas inJordanthat
will embracethe principlesofIEM(see sub-component la).

4.       A landuse masterplanfor the Rift Valley was commissionedby the JordanValley Authority in
2004 and it delineates eleven key conservation sites, includingfour protectedareas ofnationaland global
importanceand a range of other sites of lesser status, which include importantbird areas and important
wetlandareas (see Annex 20). These secondarysites, to be definedas Special ConservationAreas (SCAs)
under the project, are critical to maintainingthe ecologicalintegrity of the Rift Valley as a whole, since
they conserve valuable habitat aroundthe backbone of protected areas. This concept of SCAs is totally
newto Jordanandthe projectwill bethe first attemptto makeitpart of aplanningprocess.

5.       At present, the RiftValley master planis principally a designationmap, andthe procedures and
guidelines required to make it part of a consultative planning process have yet to be developed. This
component will therefore focus on developingthese guidelines and procedures, using the principles of
IEMas the inspirationandframeworkinorder to ensurethat biodiversity issues (ecological) are factored
inalongside socialandeconomic considerationsindevelopmentcontrol; andthat stakeholderconsultation
procedures become the norm. The PASand SCAs will be the pilot sites for the introduction of this
biodiversity-centeredplanningprocess, since their protectionis a priority for securing the Rift Valley's
ecologicalintegrity andthey have already beenearmarked onthe landuse master plan. The JVA has also
clearly indicated it's willingness to work with RSCNto develop better protectionmechanisms for these
key sites. Bytargetingthe interventioninthis way, the project's landuse planningobjectiveis achievable
and the process itself will help to strengthen the JVA's planning capability. The JVA itself will be
encouraged to adopt responsibility for the process development activities, with RSCN acting as the
facilitator

6.       A prerequisitefor component 1(and2) are detailedassessments ofthe PASand SCAs interms of
their ecological, socialand economic status andpotential.These assessmentswillbuildonthe preliminary
surveys carried out by RSCN and be geared to gatheringthe information required for designationand
management. They will include (a) full species inventories, with details of distributionand status to aid
zoning and other conservation strategies; (b) social and livelihood profiles of local communities and
assessments of their dependency on the resources of PASand SCAs; and (c) assessments of alternative


                                                    35

livelihood opportunities and other income generation possibilities. Another major activity will be
delineatingthe boundaries of PASand SCAs so that the process of official designationcanproceed. This
will be camed out in full consultation with the key stakeholders. It will also benefit from the synergy
provided by the GEF supported regional project "Mainstreaming Conservation of Migratory Soaring
Birds into Key Productive Sectors alongthe RiftValley / Red Sea Flyway". It should be noted here that
RSCNhas a legal mandateto establishprotected areas and has a long experience oftranslating boundary
surveys into officialdesignations.

Sub-component la: ProtectedArea PolicyDevelopment

7.       Jordan does not, as yet, have an officially endorsedpolicy on protectedareas. There is, however,
a draft policv framework produced under the auspices o f the Ministry o f Planning in 1996. This
framework was prepared with technical guidance and financial support from USAID. Its preparation
involved a number of workshops with relevant Jordanian organizations, both government and non-
government, inorder to secure consensus on key policy issues andpriorities. The Rift Valley project will
facilitate the review and updating ofthis policy initiative inorder to produce a revisedset of policies for
adoption through the Ministry of Environment. A clear set of policies will provide a much needed
national vision for protectedareas and a frame o f reference for future strategies and legislation; and, in
particular, it will enable the government to mainstream the community and IEM approaches it is
pioneering in the region. The project team would work jointly with the staff of the MOE, which has
recently establishedapolicyunitunder its natureconservationdirectorate.

Component 2: Development of a network of biodiversity conservation sites, embodying the
principlesofintegratedecosystemmanagement
(Total USM$9.12:GEF U M$4.12 and Co-jnancing USM$5.00)

9.       The objective ofthis component i s to legally establish the network ofprotected areas and special
conservationareas defined under the land use planning framework above, and to develop management
systems for these sites that becomeworking models of IEMprinciples.




                                                     36

 10.     The proposed network consists of four protected areas and seven special conservation areas,
representinga cross section of all key habitats and ecosystemtypes inthe Rift Valley (Annex 20). They
were selected on the basis o f previous survey data collected by RSCN, Birdlife International and
independent consultants andthey are delineated on the JVA LandUse Master Plan, andthus recognized
bythe GOJ. Each ofthe proposedprotectedareas is known to support more than 20 globally significant
species, including threatenedbirds, mammals and rare plants and fish. The special conservationareas are
smaller sites that have special ecological roles, either as surviving wetlands or `stop over' habitats for
migrating birds. Collectively they cover over 57,000 hectares and, if well protected and managed, will
create a matrix of sites along the length of the Rift Valley, fundamental to maintaining its ecological
integrityandproviding major opportunitiesfor nature-relatedeconomic development.

 11.     Underthis component, the four protectedareas ofYarmouk, Fifa, Jabal MasudaandQatar will be
officially designated through the government channels used successfully by RSCN over its 40 year
history. RSCNwill also apply lessons learned from its more recent experience o f GEF projects, notably
Dana and Dibeen Forest, by involving key stakeholders in the designation process and by using an
incentive basedapproachto gain support for site protection. Over the last 12 years, the Society has built
considerable experience of developing viable nature-relatedbusiness and socio-economic programs in
andaroundprotectedareas. Ithas createdthe `Wild Jordan' division (andbrandname), which has a team
professional staff (many from the private sector) to develop and market its nature-related enterprises.
This project will build on Wild Jordan's expertiseto pioneer the use of economic incentives as pathways
for the effective protection ofthe PASinthe Rift Valley. TakingJabal Masudaas an example, the project
intends to develop a high quality eco-tourism operationas a lead inventure to the involvement of local
communitiesinthe establishment ofthis protectedarea, since it lies only a short distance from the tourist
hot spot of Petra. This will runparallel to the designation process and will demonstrateto local people
that protectedareas canbe economic assets if developedinthe right way, thereby creating a constituency
of support for the concepto fprotectedareas. The project will provide funding for workshops andtraining
facilities for the development of community enterprises, including the services of marketing consultants
to promote community products at sales outlets. The project will cover the per-diem costs of the
communities and project staff duringtraining and product development. These nature-based community
enterprises will seek to provide alternative livelihoods and options for income generationto vulnerable
communitiesinthe PASand SCAs.

 12.     The economic development o f the proposedprotected areas will be integrated with conservation
strategies and social programs, in line with IEMprinciples, through the preparation and implementation
of managementplans. These will require new on-site staffteams to be recruited andtrained and essential
infrastructure and equipment to be provided, including facilities for eco-tourism and other socio-
economic programs. PA management will also be supported by the construction of essential
infrastructure, including staff offices, rangerposts, visitor centres, andothertourism-related facilities

13.      The SCAs are a new concept to Jordan and represent a departure from the more conventional
notion ofprotected sites inthat they are (a) anchoredinthe planningsystemratherthanunder Ministerial
by-laws; and (b) are intendedto be managedby local communitiesratherthanby established andremote
agencies. Seven SCAs are recognized on the Rift Valley Master Plan (see component 1) andthe project
intends to work on four of these sites to prepare community centered conservation plans. Two of the
selectedsites are contiguous with protectedareas andwill act, ineffect, as bufferzones. The proximity o f
the protectedareas will influence the managementoptions availableto localcommunities Incontrast, the
other two sites will be `stand alone' SCAs, situated in different habitat types, providing different
challengesandexperiences.

14.      Like the PA management plans, the Community-Driven Conservation Plans will embody the
principles o fIEM.Participative approaches, suchas PRAs, will beusedwith local communities to define


                                                      37

available resources and options for alternative livelihoods and the sustainablemanagement of important
biodiversity. Community-basedmanagement structures will be developed and mechanisms devised for
integrating community action with the land use planning processes developed under component one.
RSCNwill facilitate these pilot programs, in concert with the Jordan Hashemite Fund for Development
(JOHUD), a well respectedNGO with decades o f experience in community based enterprises. Through
this partnership, the lessons learned from the pilot sites will be used to promote the approach to all
relevant agencies and to institutionalize the process inthe two leadNGOs so they provide reservoirs of
expertise that can support the extension of community-managed SCAs to other areas. This will be the
first time the two NGOs have collaboratedon a major program.

 1.      The Royal Society for the Conservationof Nature (RSCN) i s a non-governmental organization
created in 1966 under the patronage of H i s Majesty late King Hussein. During the 1970's RSCN was
entrustedbythe Government ofJordanto establish andmanagea nationalnetwork ofprotectedareas and
to enforce the wildlife protection laws. Since that time RSCN has achieved and maintained a high
credibility infulfilling its mandatethrough effectively managing 6 protectedareas representingover 1%
of Jordan land area. One of these areas, the Dana Nature Reserve represents a worldwide model for
integratingbiodiversityconservationwith economic development.

2.       Over the last 40 years RSCN has accumulated a wide range of knowledge and experience in
subjects related to biodiversity conservation and protected areas management. This extraordinary
experience enabled RSCN to become a regional model for integrating biodiversity conservation with
economic development. In 1999, RSCN established a regional training unit, which organizes training
program in subjects relatedto biodiversity conservationand protectedareas management using RSCN
staff as trainers and facilitators for other countries inthe region. Ithas successfullydeliveredtraining in
Lebanon, Yemenand others.

3.       The scope of the "Integrated Ecosystem Management in the Jordan Rift Valley" project falls
within RSCN's national mandate and builds on its key competencies and strengths. RSCN will be the
implementingagency for this project. It will work closely with the Ministry of Environment, and other
relevantagenciesto deliverthis project.

Financialmanagement
Bank policies require that grant proceeds be used only for the purposes for which they were made
available, with due regard to economy, efficiency and the sustainable achievement of the project's
development objectives. Therefore, the recipientofthe grantis requestedto maintain, among other things,
an appropriate and adequate financial management system, including records, accounts and financial
statements, which adequately reflect the operations, resources and expenditures of the project. The
complete assessment ofthe financial management arrangements for the proposedproject was undertaken
inNovember 2006 with the purpose of assessingwhether the entity implementingthe project, the Royal
Society for the Conservation of Nature (RSCN), has acceptable financial management arrangements
includingits financial management systems, accounting, reporting and auditing arrangements as well as
its internal controlsandflow offunds.

The project will be implemented by RSCN, an environmental organization registered in Jordan and
following the law ofsocieties and socialbodies inJordan. RSCNpreviouslyimplementedaGEF financed
project and is still implementing donor financed projects from USAID. In addition to grants, RSCN's
main sources of revenue include earnings from an endowment fund, revenues from conserved sites and
wild Jordan as well as financial support from the Government. Its main expenses are salaries and the
operating expensesofconservedsites.

The Society's maintains acceptable financial management arrangements which have been enhanced over
the lasttwo years whenthe Society: (i)  implementedanew accounting system, (ii)      hiredErnst&Youngto
document its financial policies andprocedures, (iii) established anindependent audit committeereporting
to the boardand (iv) established a modern internalaudit function. The Society's financial statements are
yearly auditedby anindependent external auditor.

The project's financial management arrangements will follow the policies and procedures in place at
RSCNin additionto: (i)    creating an accounting module linked with RSCN's main system to separately
follow up and report on the project, (ii)develop a detailed chart of account by function and category for
the project, (iii)generating quarterly reports covering the project's activities and commitments and (iv)
engaging an independent external auditor to review the project's accounts on a semi annual basis and
auditthe project's accounts onayearlybasis.

MOPIC will have the ministerial oversight over the project and will be presented with a copy of the
quarterlyfinancialreports as well as acopy ofthe semiannual auditreview ofthe project's accounts.

To ensurethat funds are readilyavailablefor project implementation, a US dollars Special Account (SA)
will be opened at the Central Bank of Jordan. The management of this SA will be entrusted, by the
MOPIC, to RSCN. Counterpart funds will be made available by RSCN, MOE and other financiers.
MOPIC will ensure the financiers commitmentto the project and its implementationby requestingeach
financier to formally committhe counterpart funds needed.

Budgeting
Currently, RSCNprepares ayearly budget reflectingthe Society's plans for the coming year. The budget
process is usually initiatedby the finance department inRSCNwho circulateguidance onhowto prepare
the budget andthe timeframe. Basedonthis guidance and on eachdepartment's priorities, the department
headspreparethe first draft ofthe budget. This draft is discussedwith the acting Director General andthe
finance department and compiledto reflect the budget of RSCN. The budget is presented to the audit
committee andthe boardfor approval.

The budget is monitoredonaquarterlybasis andprovidesdetailed informationonthe forecastedrevenues
andexpenditures. The finance department also prepares aquarterlycashflow forecast to monitorthe cash
neededto runthe society.

Budget relatingto donor financed projects is prepared separately and discussed with the donors. When
approved, this budget is attachedas anannexto the RSCN's budget.

A yearly budget will be prepared for this project by RSCN's finance department. This budget will show
the expected sources of funds by financier against the forecasted uses of funds. This budget will also be
monitoredonaquarterlybasis and variations above or below 5% will be monitoredand investigated.The
budget will be posted inthe system andthe budget monitoringreport will show the expected sources and
uses offunds as comparedto actual.

Accounting
RSCN follows the modified cash basis of accounting where revenues are recorded when received and
expenditures when incurred. For the purpose of the project, the RSCN will follow the cash basis of
accounting where sources and used of funds are recorded when cash is received or when payments are
made. RSCNwill develop a chart of account for the project where the project's sources of finds will be
brokendownby financier andthe expenditures will bebrokendownby category, component and activity.

RSCNcurrently uses an accounting software (General Alpha) to record all financial transactions and to
issue various reports (balance sheet, income statement, detailed statement of account). The Society will
explore with the software provider the option of creating a module in RSCN's accounting system to
separately account for and report on the project's activities. RSCN is also exploring with its IT
departmentthe option of creatinga simple inhouse systemto follow up onthe project. Both options are
acceptable to the Bank. RSCN's decision on which option to use will be communicated,to the Bank by
project's effectiveness.

The project's accounts will be maintained using the double-entry bookkeeping principles and
commitments will be monitoredandtrackedusinga contractmodule. Supportingdocuments will be kept
by RSCN.

The society maintains a fixed asset register where all assets are identified and numbered. Assets relating
to the project will be identified inthe register andphysicallyto ensure proper counting and maintenance
for them.

I K Process Frameworkfor Reseitlement

Introduction

42.      Conservation projects that restrict access to legally designated parks or protected areas (PAS)
without acquiringthe land outright require a process framework (OP 4.12, para. 7).The purpose o fthe
framework is to describe the process by which potentially affected communities will participate in
planning. Inthese projects, the participationofthe affected population indesigningthe restrictions, as
well as inproposing the mitigation measures, i s critical for success.

43.      The process framework describes how any action plan will be developed with the local
population. Once developed, the action plan, which may be part o f a natural resources management
plan, is submittedto the Bankfor approval.

Background

44.      The Jordan Rift Valley is an integral part of the Great Rift Valley and provides a globally
critical land bridge between Africa, Europe, and Asia that supports a large variety o f ecologically
diverse habitats o f international importance and funnels millions o f migrating birds between these
continents eachyear. Pleasesee PAD for more detailed information.

45.      The proposed Integrated Ecosystem Management (IEM) Project inthe Jordan Rift Valley i s
being undertaken by the Jordanian Royal Society for the Conservation o f Nature (RSCN). The
development and implementation of this project is in general associated with enhancement o f
environmental and social conditions since it will implement environmentally sound management
activities and deal with socio-economic initiatives incollaboration with local communities. For more
detailed information about the project, please see PAD.

Description of Project Component Relevant to ProcessFramework

46.      Out ofthe four componentscomprisingthe project, Component 2: "Development ofa network
of biodiversity conservation sites, embodying the principles of integrated ecosystem management" i s
the one relevant to Process Framework. The objective of this component is to legally establish the
network of protected areas and special conservation areas defined under the land use planning
framework above, and to develop management systems for these sites that become working models o f
IEMprinciples.

Policy Trigger

47.      The ProcessFramework (PF) will be implemented inaccordance withthe World Bankpolicy
on involuntary resettlement (OP 4.12). The PF covers restriction of access to legally designated parks
and protected areas that may result in adverse impacts on livelihoods o f the affected persons. Good
practice has demonstrated that the objectives o f the policy can be better achieved through a
participatoryprocess.

ProcessFramework

48.      The criteria and procedures outlined inthe Process Framework will be an integral part ofthe
project. Incases where project-induced involuntary restriction o f access to natural resources may lead
to adverse livelihood impacts, it should be ensured that eligible affected persons are assisted intheir
efforts to restore or improve their livelihoods in a manner which strengthens the environmental
sustainability o f the PNSCA in question.. Specifically, the PF outlines the participatory approach by
which:

    (a) specific components of the project were prepared and willbe implemented;

                                                   77

    (b) the criteria for eligibility ofaffectedpersonswillbe determined
    (c) measures to assist the affected persons in their efforts to improve or restore, to pre-
        displacement levels, their livelihoods (e.g. creation of new livelihoods such as handicraft
        businesses, alternative grazing areas etc) while maintaining PAJSCA environmental
        sustainability; and
    (d) potential conflicts involvingaffected persons will beresolved.

The PF also providesa descriptionofthe arrangementsfor implementingandmonitoringthe process.

ProcessFollowedDuringPreparation

49.     A preliminary SA was conductedduring the project preparationphase. This was followed by
PRA surveys. Boththe SA and PRA were carried out by both national and international experts and
covered all areas of focus in this project. A representative sample of rural households in selected
communities in and adjacent to the four PASand their contiguous SCAs were interviewed. The
objectives ofthe SA were to:
    (a) Assess the existing social conditions of local communities living inside and around
        PAsISCAs.
    (b) Evaluatethe project'spotential environmentalrisks and impacts ontheir areas of influence, if
        any:
    (c) Prepare an Environmental Management Plan and, if needed, a Resettlement Policy
        Framework, inorder to ensure that any potential adverse social impacts will be mitigated as
        appropriate; and
    (d) Inform the ongoing sub-component design

50.     The preliminary SA found that the likely overall impact of the proposed project would be
positive since participation of localcommunities inpreparationand management of PAs/SCAs would
beincreasedandalternativelivelihoods would be supported.

51.     The preparation of Community-driven Conservation Plans (CDCPs) and subsequent
managementplans would follow a participatory approach, inwhich the roles of local communities in
the management of these sites would be strengthened, and appropriate measures would be identified
with local communities to enhance the sustainability of PAJSCA resource-based livelihood activities
and manage pressures on them. In addition, a community-based nature cohervation sub-component
would assist the four PAs and seven SCAs develop their community-basedapproachto conservation
through support to:

    (a) decreasing the threats from overuse of natural resources through targeted demonstration of
        sustainable, appropriateandpracticalconservationapproaches
    (b) providing training and technical assistance for the identification and demonstration of
        alternativeincome generatingtechniques as alternativesto the use of criticalnaturalresources.

(a) FullSocialAssessment

impacts and mitigating potential adverse effects. Some components could nevertheless adversely
affect the livelihoods o fpersons living inthe SCAs.

The selection of local communities for demonstration purposes would be based on PRA findings,

naturalresources- which also frequently include the poorest sections of the population- are likely to
be targeted as a first priority by the project.

Conflict ResolutionMechanisms

54.      The risk of conflicts arising between communities, villages and households during the
implementationo fthis processframework is realand mustbe addressed. Inthis context bothproactive
and reactive elements for resolutionof conflicts, disputes and grievances would be established.

Proactive approach
55.      Conflicts  often arise as a result o f differences in understandings or perceptions, and a
proactive approach will be adaptedinan effort to avoid conflicts before they start. This would include:
    (a) wide-spread disclosure ofproject background information;
    (b) clarification ofthe criteriaofeligibilityfor assistanceunderthe process framework;
    (c) clarification o fthe duties andresponsibilities o f all stakeholders inthe process; and
    (d) community conservation training and capacity building regarding the values of protected
         areas, threatsto these andoptions for mitigation.

Reactiveapproach
56.      As a first step inthis process, an effort should be made to settle disputes amicably through
mediation at the community level. When conflicts do arise, these should be dealt with by the
appropriate LCC. Ifresolution is not possible at the local community level, the LCC should seek the
advice of the Ministry o fthe Environment through the PMUBSCN. It is critical for the success o f the
project that dispute solving mechanisms are fair and give localconcerns adequateattention

Context and Development Goals

The JordanRiftValley forms part ofthe Great RiftValley connectingAfrica andNorthernEurope. It i s a
major fly way between the two continents and used by millions of migrating birds each year. Birdlife
InternationaVRSCN's report on ImportantBird Areas inthe HashemiteKingdom of Jordan, lists twenty-
sevensites for Jordan, ofwhich seventeenare locatedinthe JordanRiftValley.

Apart from its significance for birds, the Jordan Rift Valley also holds many large and internationally
important ecosystems, including desert, mountains, wetlands, sea and forest; e.g. the Dead Sea, the Gulf
of Aqaba andthe Jordan and Yarmouk river systems, as well as numerous specialised or unique habitats
ofregionalimportance suchasthe Quercus aegilops oak forests ofYarmouk. To date only one wetlandof
internationalimportance has been designated, inJordan, namelythe Azraq marshes inthe centraleastern
part ofthe country, well outside the RiftValley. The Directory ofWetlands ofthe Middle Easthowever,
recognizesat least six notable wetland sites inthe Jordan RiftValley. Furthermore, the DeadSea itself is
the lowest and most saline water body on Earth and is noted as one of the World's "biodiversity hot
spots" becauseits extremely harshenvironment has engenderedahighlevel of endemism.

Previous GEF activities inthe country have revealed the presence of many globally threatened species,
including several endemics. The persistent causes of habitat degradation and loss are deforestation,
overgrazing, inappropriateagriculture, urbanizationand population growth. The growth inmass tourism
has also been cited as a `new' threat to environmental quality inthe JRV (NEAP Working Paper 1995).
Habitat degradation and species loss in the JRV is serious and accelerating, largely as a result of
increasing developmentpressure, inappropriateagriculturalpractices andpopulationgrowth.

The GOJhas realisedthe threats to andfragility ofthe ecosystem inthe JRV, andrecognisedthe potential
loss of biodiversity. Ittherefore is supportingpolicies and investments that can strengthen conservation
and environmentalprotection and is interested in establishing a framework that effectively integrates
(economic) development activities and sustainable naturalresources management standards into an IEM
andLUPsystem. At the sametime it is recognisedthat communityparticipationis an essential element in
the integrated ecosystemmanagementapproachthat needsto be developed. Despitethis recognition, little
headwayhas so far beenmade inthe area of IntegratedEcosystem Management and LandUse Planning,
for the following reasons:
         Absence of coherent conservation-oriented integrated ecosystem management and land use
         strategies
         Lack of participatory integratedresource management approaches and activities that provide for
         economic development and sustainableresourceuseto localcommunities
         Weak legal and institutional framework for integrated ecosystem management with limited
         capacity andknowledge

The overallproject development objective of the GEF Project"Integrated EcosystemManagement inthe
Jordan Rift Valley" is to pioneer the application of integrated ecosystem management to land use
planning and protected area management in the Jordan Rift Valley in order to mainstream
biodiversity conservation into land use practices and create a network of conservation sites that
meets localecological, social and economic needs.

The primary vehicle to achieve this objective will be financial and technical support for community-
driven, integrated ecosystem management plans and subprojects that can simultaneously address local
developmentneeds andlocal environmentalchallenges.

Baseline Scenario

Inthe baseline scenario or the 'withoutprojectsituation,' very few activitieswilltake placeinthe areaof
IEM and LUP as experience in comprehensive land use planning, which takes due considerationto
biodiversity conservation and habitat protection is still limited and capacity in IEM is inadequate.

KEY ISSUES

1.      Scientific and technical soundness of the project

The project is well structuredand the contents of its five components are consistent with its goal: To
securethe ecologicalintegrity ofthe JordanRiftValley as aglobally importantcorridor. The components
are also in accordance with the project's development objective, which is to mainstream integrated
ecosystemmanagement (IEM)practicesinsevenpilot areas the JordanRift Valley.

A core issue inthis projectis the safeguarding ofglobally significantbiodiversity andrestoringecological
integrity along the Jordan Rift Valley. In order to achieve that, it will mainstream biodiversity
conservationinto the land-use planningprocess andthus introduce and applythe principles of integrated
ecosystem management inthe Rift Valley. This is coherent with the current trends in conservationthat
stress the importanceofprotectingecosystems rather thanisolatedareas.

Inthat sense, the proposalof expanding the ProtectedAreas Network by creatingfour new areas inthis
ecosystem and including them in the project seems logical and absolutely necessary. The proponents
make itclear that, althoughit wouldbepositiveto createmore areas, the numberoffour is a compromise
between reasonable increase in demands, on the one hand, and a minimum number to achieve the
establishmentofa system ofprotectedareas, onthe other hand.

Thus, the project will focus on seven demonstrationsites: Jordan River Area, Mujib North Area, Mujib
South Area, Yarmouk River Area, Fifa Area, Qatar Area, and Ma'suda Area. These last four are the
proposednewprotectedareasto becreatedduringthe project's implementation.

Another important considerationis that the project draws on important lessons learned from the Dana
Nature Reserve project, carried out in the 1990s and considered highly successful. Particularly, the
component dealingwithAlternative livelihoods will be introducedbased onthe experience gainedinthat
project, which hadanexplicit policy of employinglocalpeople as staffinprotectedareas.

The integrated ecosystem management approach intends to strike a balance between the interlinked
objectives of biodiversity conservationand sustainable natural resources use, while keeping inmind the
fair distributionand equitable sharingofbenefitsarisingfromthese resources.

Inaccordance with that objective, the currentprojectintends to apply a strategic "bottom-up" approach
with great emphasis on communityparticipationand capacitybuilding. The proponentsacknowledge the
importance thatthis livelihoodprogrambe sustainable, so that it does not damagethe conservationvalue
of the sites included. In order to control the project's achievements and impact, a well structured
participatory monitoring and evaluation system has been includednot only as a control instrumentbut
also as a mechanismfor improvedplanningand implementationof the program incooperationwith the
stakeholders.

The weak institutional and legal framework for integratedecosystem management with limited capacity
and knowledge is also addressed by the proponents, who have includeda strategy to improve the legal




                                                   93

framework and fill the gaps that limit the full participation o f communities inthe management of their
environment.

Although it is an ambitious project, the duration of 6 years seems adequate to achieve the stated
objectives.

2.       Globalbenefits ofthe project

The support of this project will have a positive impact on the environment and humanpopulations of the

JordanRiftValley.



This valley is anintegral partofthe Great Rift Valley andprovides a globally critical landbridgebetween
Africa, Europe, and Asia that supports a large variety of ecologically diverse habitats of international
importancewithmillions ofmigrating birdsvisitingeachyear.

As stated inthe project, it is also of strategic economic importance, linkingthe five countries of Egypt,
Israel, Jordan, the West Bank, and Syria, which share many of its natural resources, including the Jordan
River, Dead Sea, and Gulf of Aqaba. Its critical geographical location, combined with the most
productive agricultural land resources in Jordan, has made it a focal area for development and land

The project's goal to secure the ecological integrity of the Jordan Rift Valley as a globally important
corridor is clearly of worldwide importance. Seeking ways to ensure the Valley's economic and
ecological integrity for the benefit of its people also seems of great importance to improve the
conservation of this fragile habitat that harbors more than 20 globally significant species, including
threatenedmigratory birds, rare plants and fish, andthreatenedmammals. Inaddition, the creation ofthe

                      Annex 2 0 Biodiversity ofthe JordanRift Valley



1.       Introduction

The Jordan Rift Valley is part of the Great Rift Valley, and extends from Yarmouk in the
north, to the Gulf of Aqaba inthe south, over a length of 370 km. The Jordan River has a
basin of 18,194 kmzand flows southwards for a total length of 230 km through Lebanon,
Syria, Israel, West Bank and Jordan, and finally into the Dead Sea. The Jordan Valley in
Jordan consists of the Northern Ghor (11,586 ha), Middle Ghor (7,875 ha) and the Southern
Jordan Valley (11,500 ha). The Jordan Valley is about 10 km wide in its northern part,
narrowing to 4 km in its middle section, and widening again to more than 20 km in its
southern part. The elevation of the Jordan River drops from 212m below sea level at Lake
Tiberias, to more than400 mbelow sea level at the DeadSea.

South ofthe Dead Sea, the Jordan Rift Valley is drainedby the Wadi Araba, whichflows ina
northerly direction when in spate. This southernsection is about 160 km long, and up to 25
km wide (it is at its widest between Jabal Fidan and Umm Muthla). The valley bottom is
bordered by highland (or Jabal) ranges that run parallel to the Jordan valley. In local
geomorphologicalterms, these are known as the Mountain Ridges and Northern Highlands
East ofthe Rift.These highlands are morethan 50 kmwide inthe north, but narrow to about
10kmnear Aqaba. For the purpose ofthe Project,the JordanRiftValley willbe consideredin
its broadest sense, and includes both the valley floor (i.e. the Jordan River Valley, Wadi
Araba, and its extension up to Aqaba) and the adjacent highlands parallel to the valley
bottom.

The Great Rift Valley is a globally important ecological corridor and the Jordanian section
representsa strategically crucial component, since it is amajor trackingroute between Africa
and northern Europe used by millions of migrating birds each year. Systematic surveys
conductedat bottlenecksites since the mid-1960shaverevealedthat over 1.2million birds of
prey and over 300,000 storks pass along this route each year on their annual migrations
between breeding grounds in Eurasia and wintering grounds in Africa. The sharp physical
boundaries of the Jordan Rift Valley, clearly visible from the air, provide a navigational
guiding system for these birds and the habitatsit contains provide vitalresting andrefuelling
stations, without which they are unable to complete their long journeys. Not surprisingly,
Birdlife International's report on Important Bird Areas for the Middle East (Evans, 1994),
lists seventeensites for Jordan, ofwhich ten are locatedinthe Jordan Rift Valley.

Apart from its significance for birds, the Jordan Rift Valley also holds many large and
internationally important ecosystems, including desert, mountains, wetlands, sea and forest;
e.g. the Dead Sea, the Gulf of Aqaba and the Jordan and Yarmouk river systems, as well as
numerous specialised habitats of regional importance such as the Yarmouk forest.
Furthermore, the Dead Sea itself is the lowest and most saline water body on Earth and is
notedas one ofthe World's "biodiversity hot spots" becauseits extremely harshenvironment
has engenderedahighlevel of endemism.




                                             119

2.       HistoryofProtectedArea developmentinthe RiftValley

Due to the importance of the Jordan Rift Valley for conservation of globally significant
biodiversity, the area has received a lot of attention from boththe national and international
conservation community. Although this has generated a lot of interest in protecting the
biodiversity of the region, this attention has to date not resulted in the establishment of a
comprehensive network of protected areas, sufficient enough for safeguarding viable
populations ofterrestrial wildlife, and adequate as a network of steppingstones for migratory
birds. Becauseofpressures onlanduse and competitionfor land, protectedareas are seenas a
vital component, but not as a definite solution for safeguarding globally significant
biodiversity, and therefore the present project focuses on both strengthening the protected
areas (PA) network, and establishing integrated (sustainable) land use practices in
intermediateareas.

The first recordedrecommendations for the establishment of a network of protectedareas in
Jordan were made in 1963by aBritishexpedition(Mountford, 1963; quoted inClarke, 1979).
For the Jordan RiftValley, their suggestionsincluded a 2200 km2areathat includedPetraand
the Wadi Araba section of the Rift Valley, a smallreservedarea ofMediterranean-typeforests
near Ajlun, and a small reserve at the Zarqa Ma'in hot springs on the east bank of the Dead
Sea. Littlehappened, however, until 1968, when PetraNP was established, but this measured
only 122 km2and did not include the Rift Valley section as originally proposed. The
establishment of protected areas was also hampered by the fact that legislation was not in
place - a national parks law had been drafted in 1970, but this was not incorporatedinto
nationallegislation.

In1979, the IUCN/WWF report onDevelopment of Wildife Conservation in Jordan II:A
Proposalfor Wildlife Reserves in Jordan (Clarke, 1979) recommendedthe establishment of a
network of PAS based on an evaluation of ecosystems and land types. In the Jordan Rift
Valley they recommended the establishment of two sites: i) Mujib Wildlife Reserve (220
                                                             the
km2),located from the eastern shore of the Dead Sea and including adjacent escarpment
habitat; and ii)Jabal MasudaWildlife Reserve (460 km2),which was to include Rift Valley
desert habitat at Wadi Araba, along with escarpment habitat. In addition, three PAS were
proposed that are locatedadjacent to the Rift Valley, namely i)the Zubiya Wildlife Reserve
(31 km2) located in the northern highland area near Irbid; ii)Dana Wildlife Reserve (100
km2),which was proposed as a western highland reserve area; and iii)Ram (Wadi Rum)
Wildlife Reserve (510 km2),which was to consist of eastern desert (Hisma) habitat. All of
these sites were consideredto be secondor thirdpriority sites at the time (Clarke, 1979).

In 1980s and 199Os, six of the twelve sites proposed by Clarke (1979) were formally
designated: Dana Wildlife Reserve (308 h2),      Ajlun (Zubiya) Woodland Reserve (12 km2),
Azraq Wetland Reserve (12 km2), Shaumari Wildlife Reserve (22 km2), Mujib Wildlife
Reserve (215 km2)andWadi RumProtectedArea (540 km2).

However, the total area registered in the Rift Valley is small. Also, as indicated in an
evaluation on ProtectedAreas Review by RSCN (undated; around ZOOO), many habitats are
not adequately protected. Using the IUCN criterion that a minimum of 4% of each
habitathegetation type should be included inthe PA system, only sand dunes, saline areas
and open waters are adequately protected in Jordan. Other habitat types are offered some
protection, except for Aleppo pine forest and deciduous oak forest, which are not included in
any ofthe existing PAS.

4. Site Selection: criteria and finalselection

With GEF support, RSCN, inconsultation with the General Corporation for Environmental
Protection" (GCEP) and with guidance from the World Conservation Union (IUCN)
undertook a full review of the protected areas in Jordan in 1999-2000 (RSCN, 1999). The
main aim of the review was to ensure that all natural habitats in Jordan are adequately
representedinthe PA system, as it had become obvious by the mid-1990s that this was not
the case. The main objective of the review was to propose a new list of candidate protected
areas, in addition to the other already suitably proposed sites (Clarke, 1979), which rejlect
thefindings of the review and the current criteria on which the value of protected areas are
measured. This objective was underpinned by three underlying sub-objectives, i)re-
assessment of protected areas, to determine if these still represent the full spectrum of the
country's natural heritage; ii)an evaluation if any important areas were neglected by the
original proposednetwork (Clarke, 1979) and need to be added to safeguard the country's
natural heritage; and iii)to determine the extent to which the sites identified previously
(Clarke, 1979) but not yet designated, have been irretrievably damaged and are no longer
appropriateas candidate sites.

RSCN's criteria for selectingindividual sites within the broad ecosystemhabitat types were
compiled from a number of sources, including IUCN guidelines and Jordan's Draft Park
Policy. A ranking system using 14 criteria was used for evaluating proposed sites, these
criteria were arranged intwo groups: one of `determining factors' that contribute strongly to
site selection, and a second of `non-determining factors' that contribute less and are not as
strongly weighted

Based on this ranking system, RSCN's evaluation arrived at the following order of
importance for the sites locatedinthe Jordan Rift Valley: i)Dibbeen (now designated); ii)
Yarmouk ,iii)Fifa, iv) Baptismsite, v) Qatar, vi) Masudaandvii) Aqaba mountains.

For a GEF biodiversity project, it is of first and foremost importancethat the protection of a
particular site contributes to the protection of globally significant biodiversity. Firstly, a site
may harbour globally significant species, unique assemblages of species, or uniquehabitats.
Secondly, a site may be essential for the survival of a globally signtfkant species. Thirdly, a
site may be important for its cumulative importance to globally significant biodiversity: not
the individualsite, but a network of protectedareas alongmay be requiredfor the survival of
globally significant biodiversity ina particular area. The latter i s particularly the case inareas
where migratory species needto be protected, and creatingnetworks ofprotectedareas along
flyways is ofvital importance.

Basedonthe above, a revisedset of selectioncriteria has beendevised, basedonconservation
value and viability score. Two new criteria under conservation value were added, the
contribution to the survival ofa globally significant species andthe cumulativevalue of a site
for globally significant species.

Applying the criteria on the sites, it became obvious that two sites should definitely be
included inthe final four sites to be targeted by the project, namely Yarmouk and Fifa, as
both hadthe highest conservationvalue, and a good viability score. It was also important to
assess how representative the sites are - i.e. are the major habitat types inthe country well
representedby these sites, or do they simply duplicate one another?From this information it
was obvious that while Qatar and Jabal Masuda add new habitat types to the PA system
(mudflat and Juniper forest, respectively), the Aqaba Mountains add more of the Acacia-
Rocky Sudanianhabitatalready representedat JabalMasudaand Qatar.

From this, it is obvious that protecting these sites will help safeguard globally significant
biodiversity. Some of these sites harbour unique species, such as the plant Cypsis
schoenoides,which has a very restrictedrange and is knownfrom one site inJordan only, or
the fish Tilapiagallileae that is endemic to a few streams inJordan. Other sites are critical in
the lifecycle of some species. The mudflats of Qatar, for example, seasonally harbour more
than 1% of the world's population of both white stork Ciconia ciconia and black stork
Ciconianigra.

However, much of the globally significant biodiversity listed at each site is commonto more
than one site, and it may seem that efforts to conserve this overlaps to a large extent. It must
be remembered, though, that the sites must also be seen inconjunction with each other. The
Jordan Rift Valley is widely regarded as one of the most important flyways for migratory
birds inthe world, and a network of suitable-and preferably protected-areas alongthe Rift
Valley is deemed essentialfor the survival ofthe many ofthese species. Preservinga network
ofappropriatesites, at a distance from each other, serves to provide a series of safe havens as
"stepping stones" alongthe migrationroute.

The effectiveness of such a system of stepping-stones also depends on land use activities in
the intermediateareas, andthis will betackledby the landuse component ofthis GEF project,
andinthe parallel programonmanagementof Jordan's network of IBAs.
