                                      1. INTRODUCTION

1.1     This program document proposes a Post-Conflict Reconstruction and Recovery
Grant for the Republic of Côte d’Ivoire in the amount of SDR 93.9 million (US$ 150 million
equivalent). The proposed grant provides a bridge between past DPOs and future operations,
pursuing reforms initiated in previous grants while responding quickly to the emergency
resulting from the recent crisis and the need to relaunch economic growth. Côte d’Ivoire’s
political situation suffered a sharp reversal after the long-awaited Presidential elections of
November 2010. Elections were held peacefully and in a manner generally considered to be free
and fair. However, the Constitutional Court disqualified a large number of ballots and awarded
President Laurent Gbagbo the victory, while the UN insisted that Alassane Ouattara had won. A
political stalemate resulted and eventually led to a short-lived civil war; five months, many lives
and much economic wealth were lost. An estimated 3,000 persons died, over 100,000 fled the
country, and approximately one million were internally displaced. Three months of fiscal
revenues were lost and the country accumulated new arrears with domestic and external
creditors. The new government faces a daunting task of promoting reconciliation while providing
justice and rebuilding the economy.

1.2     This one-tranche development policy operation is a key component of the World
Bank strategy to assist the new government in addressing the immediate challenges of
reconciling and rebuilding the country. It is a special operation with multiple objectives. First, it
provides emergency financial assistance to maintain macroeconomic stability, sustain key public
services, and generally support the authorities’ efforts to consolidate the peace. Second, it
ensures that the progress made on essential governance and economic reforms under the three
previous Economic Governance and Recovery Grants is maintained. Specifically, this grant will
deepen policy reforms in public financial management (PFM) and the key cocoa, finance and
energy sectors, and promote recovery and job creation in the wake of the debilitating political
crisis of early 2011. Third, this operation supports the new government in identifying its
priorities and moving to HIPC completion point. The Grant is an integral part of IDA’s Country
Partnership Strategy for Côte d’Ivoire for the period FY10-FY13 and is closely coordinated with
assistance provided to support the program of Government’s reforms through the ongoing
Governance and Institutional Development Grant (US$13 million equivalent), the Enhanced
HIPC Initiative, and the IMF-supported Rapid Credit Facility (RCF) program. It supports the
implementation of the Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP) adopted in February 2009, and
the new Government’s emerging agenda.

1.3      The operation would provide urgently-needed financial support to the government
in the context of an economy which virtually ground to a halt for two months. Thus, the
amount of this grant has been increased from US$75 million equivalent to SDR 93.9 million
(US$150 million equivalent). Since Côte d’Ivoire is in debt distress, IDA resources are provided
on grant terms, per the stipulations of the grant allocation system agreed with IDA donors for the
IDA 16 period (FY12-FY14). Côte d’Ivoire is also eligible for annual exceptional post-conflict
allocations from IDA. Following the lessons emerging from experience with DPOs in fragile
environments, a stand-alone DPO is proposed in order to give the new government sufficient
time to define its medium-term strategy. A follow-up DPO, proposed for early in FY13, could be
the start of a programmatic series, as the government establishes a strong track record of reform.

1.4      The economy has been severely impacted by trade sanctions, the closure of the
banking system and extensive looting. There has been a sharp fall in revenues from import
duties, cocoa export taxes and petroleum sales while those revenues which were collected in the
first three months of 2011 are not available to the new Government. At the same time, the
authorities must honor salary and debt service obligations, coupled with extra spending
requirements as a result of the crisis. Thus, the Government is in urgent need of external funding
to avoid arrears to employees and suppliers which would have a devastating effect on economic
recovery and job creation. At the same time, it needs to reassure the population of its
commitment to good governance through pursuit of public financial management reforms, and to
create the foundations for long-term development and poverty reduction through promotion of
the private sector.

1.5     The new government is well aware of the need for greater social accountability,
stronger institutions, and more active participation by civil society. Therefore, it is moving
forward with a multifaceted approach involving: (a) national reconciliation through dialogue
with civil society and the private sector, a “truth and reconciliation commission?, and outreach to
the diaspora living abroad; (b) improved public service delivery and rehabilitation of social and
economic infrastructure, notably targeting the least served areas and the most vulnerable
populations; (c) enhanced accountability and transparency in budget execution; (d) provision of
jobs, especially for the unemployed youth, at first through temporary work programs but more
sustainably through the expansion of the private sector and formal job creation; and (e) provision
of justice in a manner that is deemed efficient, transparent and fair.

1.6     The proposed PCRRG reflects the orientations for Bank engagement in states
emerging from conflict as outlined in the 2011 World Development Report on Conflict,
Security and Development and in the new Africa Strategy. First, it is rapid-response support
aimed at breaking the cycle of repeated violence that has been observed in many post-conflict
states. Second, while its objectives are modest, in recognition of the limited time available for
the new Government to define its priorities, the operation promotes incremental institutional
change to improve governance and build confidence. Third, the areas supported by the proposed
operation reflect a high degree of selectivity on a small number of reforms that are feasible given
the complex political situation. In addition, the rapid delivery of budget support through this
proposed operation enables the government to prepare a credible budget and achieve some quick
wins such as re-establishing key public services, and clearing private sector arrears to promote
job creation. Consistent with the Africa Strategy, the proposed PCRRG addresses the foundation
of Côte d’Ivoire’s growth and poverty reduction, governance, while contributing to one of the
two pillars, namely competitiveness and employment. Associated projects and policy dialogue
emphasize public sector capacity, competitiveness and job creation in more depth. This operation
is part of a broader package of Bank support, within the context of the Country Partnership

Strategy, including a new youth employment project, the restructuring of an existing project to
promote labor-intensive public works, and the rapid re-launching of the Bank’s portfolio.

       2. CÔTE D’IVOIRE’S DEVELOPMENT CHALLENGES AND PROSPECTS

2.1     Côte d’Ivoire, a medium-size country with a population projected to reach 21
million in 2011, is the third largest economy in West Africa, after Nigeria and Ghana, and
is critical to the overall development of the sub-region. Côte d’Ivoire has an important
economic influence over the rest of the region, given its size (accounting for 40 percent of GDP
in the WAEMU group of countries), its relatively high income per capita (US$1,050 in 2010 as
per the Bank’s Atlas methodology), and its role in transit trade for landlocked neighboring
countries. With its large immigrant population, Côte d’Ivoire has also become the largest source
of net remittance outflows in sub-Saharan Africa, benefiting poorer neighboring countries such
as Burkina Faso, Mali and Guinea. The country is the largest exporter of cocoa and raw cashew
nuts in the world, the largest African exporter of rubber and palm oil, and the third largest
exporter of non-oil products in sub-Saharan Africa (after South Africa, and Nigeria). It has a
relatively diversified agricultural economy as well as significant manufacturing, energy and
service sectors.

2.2     Peace in Côte d’Ivoire will also be critical for the political stability of the sub-region.
It borders on five countries, two of which are very fragile states (Liberia and Guinea), and one
other one which has recently experienced significant social unrest (Burkina Faso). The conflict
in Côte d’Ivoire attracted mercenaries from Liberia who returned home with arms and pose a
renewed threat in a country slowly recovering from decades of crisis. Burkina Faso and Mali
suffered from rising import prices when transit trade was disrupted. Political normalization in
Côte d’Ivoire is essential to stabilize this fragile sub-region.

2.3     The country has suffered ten years of political instability stemming from the
leadership’s lack of legitimacy and social exclusion. The election of President Gbagbo in
2000 was considered by many to have been seriously flawed due to widespread fraud and the
exclusion of Alassane Ouattara’s candidacy on the grounds of his disputed nationality. This
reinforced a sense of disenfranchisement among northerners, who already felt neglected in the
delivery of public services, and among immigrants and their descendents who felt threatened by
the concept of “Ivoirité?. The resulting civil war of 2002-03 divided the country in two,
hardening the lines of social division and further depriving the north of services. Failure to hold
presidential elections on schedule in 2005, and their repeated postponement, further undermined
the legitimacy of the Gbagbo regime, though the creation of a coalition government in 2007
offered a temporary solution for some.

2.4     This political instability has taken a heavy toll on the economy and social outcomes.
During the years of crisis, growth was very weak, and the incidence of poverty rose from 38.4
percent in 2002 to 43 percent in 2008.1 The level of rural poverty is estimated at 63 percent,
rising to 85 percent in the north. The contribution of agriculture to GDP increased, reflecting a
decline in the manufacturing and services sectors. Côte d’Ivoire is now ranked 166th out of 179

countries according to the 2008 UNDP Human Development Index. While average per capita
income is still among the highest in mainland West Africa, it is unevenly distributed, with a Gini

points below the sub-Saharan African average.
2.5     The country experienced a new setback in the post-election crisis of 2011. When the
UN, ECOWAS and the African Union endorsed Alassane Ouattara’s claim to victory, he
requested that the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO) cease to cooperate with the
Gbagbo government. This soon led to the closure of all major commercial banks, a shortage of
cash, and the layoff of most employees. At the same time, trade sanctions imposed by the
European Union and the United States stopped most external trade. The modern economy
ground to a halt. The situation was further aggravated by extensive looting and vandalism when
the crisis entered its final stage with the conflict in Abidjan. Some observers suggest that up to
three-quarters of the small and medium enterprises in Abidjan suffered material or financial
damage. As a result, fiscal revenues dropped dramatically and what was collected was diverted
to military purposes by the Gbagbo regime. Civil servants and suppliers were unpaid. The new
government is committed to honoring such commitments, yet fiscal revenues will fall far short of
normal levels in 2011.

2.6     Poverty may have risen by 3 to 5 percentage points in 2011. Analysis of poverty
trends between 2002 and 2008 suggest that a one percent change in growth leads to a two percent
change in poverty. Thus a drop of GDP by 6 percent would lead to a 12 percent increase in
poverty above the baseline of 43 percent (or 5.2 percentage points). Considering only the
displacement of one million persons, and assuming only half of them are new poor, the poverty
rate would increase by some two percentage points. A return to normalcy and renewed growth
would reverse this trend.

2.7     Political instability has been fueled by, and resulted in, a deterioration of
governance, transparency and social services. The public expenditure management and
financial accountability review (PEMFAR) conducted in 2008 revealed a deterioration in
budgetary discipline, financial management, and procurement processes and procedures. Budget
allocations in the social sectors (health and education) have significantly declined since 2000 and
resources were diverted away from poverty-reducing sectors, and the center-north-west (CNW)
part of the country. Oil revenues were diverted for extra-budgetary spending on the new capital,
and corruption deprived farmers of a fair share of cocoa revenues and needed investment.
Several smaller banks were created with weak governance structures, while the microfinance
sector suffered from lack of oversight after the withdrawal of foreign partners. Road blocks set
up by public officials, warlords and opportunists reached epidemic proportions. The business
climate has deteriorated and private investment has been discouraged. Total public and private
sector investment totaled a mere 10 percent of GDP, half the average rate in the sub-region.

2.8    The previous Gbagbo Government started efforts to improve the allocation of
public resources. Public financial management reforms were begun, public services were slowly
expanded across the country, and pro-poor spending increased as a share of the total budget.
Beginning with the 2007/08 academic year, the education system returned to normal though

                                             -4-
learning outcomes had declined to the level of Chad. The rehabilitation of health care facilities
was underway and vaccination programs were resumed with help from multilateral agencies.
However, the recent crisis has set back the provision of social services once again, particularly in
the CNW region, but this time also in Abidjan.

2.9    Due to severe budget constraints and neglect of the social sectors during the crisis,
achievement of most MDGs by 2015 will be extremely difficult. The crisis had a considerable
negative effect on the implementation of poverty targeted activities, with security and defense
spending taking priority, while demobilization, re-integration, and election preparation have
required significant resources. As a result, service quality remains well below pre-crisis levels.
Gender equality suffered during the earlier conflict with massive human rights violations and
abuses, and gender-based violence was once again a major problem in the crisis of 2011.

                            A. RECENT POLITICAL DEVELOPMENTS

2.10 Alassane Ouattara was sworn in as President on May 6, after several months of
political impasse and a short civil war. After the second round on November 21, the
Independent Electoral Commission announced that the remaining opposition candidate, Alassane
Ouattara, had won over the incumbent President, Laurent Gbagbo, with 54 percent of the vote.
The UN Office for Côte d’Ivoire (UNOCI) confirmed this result, which ECOWAS and the
African Union also accepted. However, the Constitutional Court ruled that a large number of
ballots in the northern part of the country were invalid, with the result that President Gbagbo
won by 51 percent. Numerous international diplomatic efforts to negotiate an agreement failed
and sanctions of increasing severity were gradually imposed by key trading partners. Eventually
the former rebels, the Forces Nouvelles, launched military operations from their base in the
North, and they quickly took control of most of the country. However, fighting intensified in
Abidjan where forces loyal to President Gbagbo made a final stand. After ten days of fighting,
including interventions by UN and French troops, the outgoing President was taken into custody
on April 11. Peace gradually returned to Abidjan and on May 6, Alassane Ouattara was formally
sworn in as President. His official inauguration took place on May 21, attended by 21 Heads of
State.

2.11     Côte d’Ivoire now faces a huge challenge of reconciliation, with justice and security.
A truth and reconciliation commission has been set up along the lines of the one created in South
Africa at the end of apartheid. The International Criminal Court has been asked to investigate all
crimes against humanity, regardless of which side may be implicated. The former President is
being held under house arrest in a northern town. President Ouattara is trying to reach out to the
46 percent of the population who voted for Gbagbo, while responding to the legitimate calls for
justice. His election has helped address the single most important source of conflict – the sense
of exclusion among northerners, immigrants and Moslems. But now his government must avoid
actions which are perceived by the losing side as discriminating against them.

2.12 The new government must also restart the process of integrating former rebel forces
into a national army, while disarming those who are not suitable for the military and
helping them re-enter civilian life. The former should be manageable since the majority of the
army voted for President Ouattara, and there was not much actual fighting between the two sides.
However, the latter will prove more challenging as the economy does not yet offer many
opportunities for ex-combatants and the government’s resources to pay for re-integration are

                                             -5-
very limited. Those who are not retained in the army will be tempted to hold on to their weapons
and resort to crime if there is no better alternative. Economic recovery and job creation thus
takes on added urgency.

2.13     President Ouattara has promised legislative elections by the end of 2011. Since the
mandate of the national assembly expired in 2005, it has not been officially in session, though it
has continued to function on an informal basis. It will be important to normalize this situation
and re-establish the role of the legislature in approving the budget and other laws. However, this
election may be the source of further tensions, as both sides could use this opportunity to remind
voters of past grievances. Much of the leadership of Gbagbo’s party (FPI) is currently under
house arrest. If this situation is still in effect at the time of the elections, or if guilty verdicts have
been reached, the political discourse could deteriorate into violence. The timing of the elections
may also prove controversial. If President Ouattara pursues the current tight schedule, it may be
difficult to enlarge the voters’ list to the satisfaction of the opposition. On the other hand, by
delaying the elections the President could expose himself to criticism of undemocratic rule. The
President has already acknowledged the latter risk and has pledged to push hard to meet the
stated deadline. Meanwhile, these elections will add extra expenses at a time when fiscal
revenues are seriously depleted. The proposed operation will help ease the fiscal constraint and
reduce the risk of inadequate election preparation.

2.14 The political implications of the new regime are likely to be mixed for the economic
reform agenda. The new government represents another coalition, but this time without the
participation of the FPI. Gbagbo’s party was invited to join, but they opted to remain in the
opposition, while rebuilding the party. President Ouattara will have a more cohesive cabinet, but
a vocal opposition, while having made a conciliatory gesture. The return to a legitimately
elected legislature could also impose additional constraints on the government. At the same time,
the coalition is likely to enjoy a solid majority and the presence of a functioning national
assembly is expected to improve its credibility, and to create the conditions for greater
transparency and good governance, provided that the announced democratic agenda is pursued.

                              B. RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS

2.15 The economy was slowing down even before the latest crisis. GDP growth reached
3.8 percent in 2009, helped by high export prices for oil and cocoa in particular. However,
renewed political uncertainty and extensive electricity blackouts in early 2010 resulted in a
downward revision of GDP growth projections. After a 21.5 percent fall in crude oil production
and the post-election crisis, GDP growth is estimated to have fallen to 2.4 percent for 2010.

2.16 The fiscal deficit remained moderate and the current account balance remained
positive in 2010. The fiscal deficit is estimated at -2.3 percent of GDP, slightly lower than the
program target of -2.0 percent, due to a fall in revenues by 0.7 percent of GDP compared to
projections. The primary balance was -0.2 percent of GDP. Pro-poor spending amounted to 7.8
percent of GDP, slightly lower than the 8.1 percent target. This was due to the disruption caused
by the elections and their aftermath, which took place in the last quarter of the year when public
investment typically accelerates. The trade balance was positive though lower than in 2009, due
in particular to falling crude oil exports, partially offset by higher world prices.

2.17 The economy went into freefall in the first four months of 2011. With the imposition
of trade sanctions in January, official imports and exports through the two ports gradually ground
to a halt. 490,000 tons of cocoa, worth over US$1.2 billion, was stranded at the port or up-
country. As much of the crude oil is extracted by Western oil companies and much of the
petroleum products are sold to Nigeria, these were also affected by sanctions. Government
salaries were only partially paid in February. The situation worsened in late February when the
cessation of central bank operations and fear of sanctions forced the major commercial banks to
close. It is estimated that 70-80 percent of formal sector employees were laid off by mid-March
due to lack of cash. Government salaries were not paid in March. Thus aggregate demand
dropped sharply. At the same time, the prices of some key consumer goods such as gasoline,
cooking gas, and staple foods doubled, due to a combination of external trade sanctions and the
deterioration in the domestic transport of food. Looting has also been extensive. Estimates of the
number of enterprises affected range from 30 to 50 percent, and higher, and some will not be
able to recover. While little reliable data is available, it is likely that GDP declined by roughly 15
to 20 percent in the first four months of the year.

2.18 The impact on public finances has been equally dramatic, as revenues collapsed
while most spending obligations remained. Cocoa export taxes, customs duties and VAT
taxes on imports gradually dried up, while the closure of enterprises and the major banks
disrupted internal taxes. Arrears were accumulated on civil servant salaries, supplier payments
and debt service. Given the temporary cessation of operations by the central bank, the closure of


                                                    -7-
the main commercial banks, and the disruption of work in the Ministry of Economy and Finance,
it is impossible to determine the net effect with any precision. The authorities and the IMF have
agreed to work on the basis of a table of financial operations for the last three quarters of 2011.
For this reason, budget estimates for 2011 shown in Table 2.1 are provided for Q2-4, as well as
on an annual basis for comparison with other years. Furthermore, the immediate cash flow
situation is so dire that monthly estimates of revenues and expenditures have also been deemed
necessary. The government is basically running on a cash budget for the rest of 2011.

2.19 Cocoa export taxes and crude petroleum sales will be the main sources of revenue in
the first months of the new government. Fortunately, the large cocoa stocks did not suffer
serious damage and with the high world prices prevailing, export tax revenues should exceed
US$200 million. Pending receipt of these taxes, the authorities have arranged for bridge
financing from the two largest commercial banks. The state has also arranged with the state-
owned petroleum company, PETROCI, to receive its share of crude oil exports immediately,
rather than spreading it out over the year. Customs duties will also start. Enterprises should
begin paying profit taxes for 2010, but it is not clear how many will be in a position to pay in the
short-term.

2.20 Consistent with WDR 2011, a significant, rapid and coordinated response from the
international community has been critical to reestablish some order in public finances. The
French government was first to respond with a loan of €350 million, of which the first €200
million was disbursed in late April. This enabled the payment of two months of arrears on civil
servant salaries, injecting badly needed cash into the struggling economy. Disbursement of the
remainder of the loan is expected in July now that the IMF has confirmed that an emergency
short-term macroeconomic framework is in place. However, this loan has a grant element of only
1 percent (interest rate of Eurobor plus 50 basis points, 3-year grace period, 9 years to repay), so
an assessment by the Non-Concessional Borrowing Policy will be requested in order to
determine the need for any remedial measures. However, the loan does not have any significant
effect on the country’s debt dynamics (see paragraph 2.32). The African Development Bank
contributed a grant of about US$150 million in mid-June. The IMF has terminated the program
supported by the Extended Credit Facility and has approved a single-tranche Rapid Credit
Facility. This arrangement has no structural benchmarks and enables the Fund to disburse 25
percent of quota (approximately US$129 million) in July.

                 C. MACROECONOMIC OUTLOOK AND DEBT SUSTAINABILITY

2.22 The short-term macroeconomic outlook is not good but should improve significantly
by the end of 2011. Real GDP is projected to fall by 6.3 percent in 2011, though it is important
to recognize the wide margin for error under current circumstances. The impact of the recent
crisis is still being assessed, while the speed of the rebound is difficult to predict. Security
continues to be a major problem, and up-coming legislative elections add an element of political
uncertainty, which together will discourage private investment in the short-term. Crude oil
production is expected to fall as old wells approach the end of their life. Cocoa and food
production are unlikely to grow from the high levels enjoyed in 2010 due to favorable weather.

2.23 The medium-term prospects are very good, providing the political situation
stabilizes and the new government demonstrates a firm commitment to reform. A simple
recovery from the disastrous events of 2011 should permit GDP growth in the order of 9 percent
in 2012: 6 percent to offset the loss in 2011, plus a return to the 2009/10 trend of 3 percent
growth. Post-conflict countries typically experience a robust rebound for a few years and Côte
d’Ivoire is exceptionally well-placed for such a recovery, since there was very little destruction
of physical infrastructure. Growth would come from renewed private sector confidence and
investment, the return to normal levels of capacity utilization in industry, normalization of the
situation in the central, northern and western parts of the country, the rehabilitation of public
infrastructure, and expected reforms in the investment climate and the energy and agriculture
sectors. The economy has suffered from 10 years of under-investment and there is a sense that
both domestic and foreign investors are just waiting for the situation to stabilize in order to re-
enter the many sectors with pent-up needs (oil and gas, electricity, construction,
telecommunications) or new opportunities (rubber, oil palm, cashew, cotton, rice).
Rehabilitation of public infrastructure will be particularly critical to help domestic and external
trade. Restoration of capacity in the electricity sector will be essential for the recovery of
manufacturing. Longer-term growth is projected to average 5.5-6.0 percent per annum, sustained
by diversification of the economy, increased investment, and expanded trade to regional and
global markets. CPI inflation is expected to remain at 2.5-3.0 percent during 2011-2014. A
possible downside scenario would limit growth in 2012 to only 6 percent, as investor confidence
takes longer to return. This would represent no more than a recovery in GDP lost in 2011. The
government would need to reduce public investment or increase borrowing, which may be

                                             -9-
feasible if HIPC completion point is reached as planned in mid-2012. Some of this lower growth
would likely be offset by higher growth in 2013.

2.24 The trade balance is expected to remain positive but decrease and turn negative in
the next few years. Export and import volumes are projected to grow at similar rates but the
terms of trade are expected to deteriorate after 2011. Cocoa sales are unlikely to grow in the
medium term due to aging plantations, while exceptionally high world prices may decline if the
political situation stabilizes in Côte d’Ivoire. Oil exports are expected to decline over the next
few years, although developments in neighboring countries suggest that new reserves may be
identified and brought into production in the longer-term. With significant outflows for service
payments and private transfers, the current account balance is therefore expected to turn
negative.

2.25 The RCF-supported budget calls for a fiscal deficit of 8.5 percent of GDP for the last
three quarters of the year, although this represents a smaller deficit of 6.5 percent for the full
year. The difference is explained largely by the payment of salary arrears. Total revenues
(excluding grants) are projected to fall from 19.2 percent of GDP in 2010 to 18.4 percent for Q2-
Q4 of 2011, or only 17.6 percent of annual GDP. Current expenditures, on the other hand, are
expected to increase to 22.8 percent for Q2-Q4 compared to 18.6 percent in 2010 due to the
payment of salary arrears for March and additional emergency needs. Electricity subsidies will
continue to be a major burden (0.8 percent of GDP). Public investment as a share of GDP will
fall from already low levels, with an even sharper drop in nominal terms.

2.26 Fiscal policies are focused on stop-gap measures for 2011 while re-establishing a
viable program for the coming years. The revised 2011 budget emphasizes post-conflict
needs, pro-poor spending, and support to the private sector. It includes tax concessions to assist
the recovery of the private sector and consumers, and additional spending for the re-integration
of ex-combatants, creation of a new army, and the repair and replacement of public property.
Taxes have been reduced on petroleum products in order to maintain prices at the pump in the
face of rising world prices. Similarly, no increase in electricity tariffs is considered feasible for
the moment. Free health care was provided for the first three months after the crisis. Pro-poor
spending is planned to represent 38.5 percent of the national budget in 2010 up from 35.4 percent
in 2010. Spending on health and education is planned to reach 28.6 percent of the budget. Pro-
poor spending will be reinforced by the President’s Emergency Program which calls for US$110
million in spending in education, health, water, electricity, and urban waste disposal, allocated
equitably across all regions of the country. A government seminar was held July 5-6 at which
each Minister presented their action program for the next six months, and priorities were
established. Ministers are to be held accountable for delivery of the agreed plans.

2.27 The share of the wage bill is high relative to falling revenues. Wages and salaries
were contained at 41.5 percent of fiscal revenues in 2010, but their share will grow on an
exceptional basis to 55.9 percent for the nine-month budget of 2011, as the wage bill increases in
the face of falling revenues. No wage increase is planned, but the budget includes payment of
arrears from the first quarter, as well as some hiring to re-establish services in neglected parts of
the country, and to integrate elements of the Forces Nouvelles into the new national army. The
authorities plan to gradually reduce the wage bill to approach the WAEMU target of 35 percent
with the help of a census of civil servants (initiated in 2010 and to be completed in 2011),
followed by the implementation of an integrated payroll information system. This will help

                                             - 10 -
control the future wage bill by identifying ghost workers, those who are being paid twice or
receiving unwarranted benefits, and other abuses. This issue will likely be further pursued in the
context of a future IMF-supported ECF program.

2.28 In the medium-term, the Government intends to return to primary basic surpluses,
which would help it to remain current on debt service. Fiscal space for pro-poor spending should
come from a modest increase in the revenue/GDP ratio, containing the wage bill, reducing
sovereignty and other non-essential spending, and an increase in concessional project financing.
Total revenues (excluding grants) are projected to reach 20.1 percent of GDP in 2014.
Government expenditures are projected to increase to 23.0 percent of GDP by that year, with
falling current expenditures providing fiscal space for a recovery of public investment to 5.2
percent of GDP (from 3.1 percent in 2009-10). This will be funded fairly equally from domestic
and foreign sources.

2.29 Côte d’Ivoire reached the HIPC decision point in March 2009. Possible HIPC
Initiative debt relief was estimated at US$3 billion in end-2007 NPV terms. Côte d’Ivoire had
already received about 55 percent of its estimated HIPC debt relief including pre-2002 excess
debt relief provided by the Paris Club and London Club creditors, and the concessional arrears
clearance operations undertaken by IDA and the AfDB. The May 2009 agreement with the Paris
Club reduced debt service to bilateral donors by 92 percent (US$4.3 billion) during the ECF
program period, but this is no longer in effect under the RCF and will have to be renegotiated.
External debt rescheduling discussions were also concluded with London Club commercial
creditors, whereby Brady bonds were exchanged for a new U.S. dollar denominated Eurobond
having a term of 23 years and a six-year grace period.

2.30 Côte d’Ivoire is in debt distress, as witnessed by the accumulation of external
arrears in 2011, and the medium-term fiscal challenges remain large. External arrears
amounted to approximately US$180 million by end-April 2011, of which over half was due to
the collapse of the Paris Club restructuring when the ECF went off track. The authorities should
be able to negotiate a new Paris Club agreement after a new IMF-supported ECF is in place
(tentatively planned for late 2011). Approximately US$45 million in arrears to the AfDB and the
World Bank was cleared in late May, and other external multilateral arrears should be paid by
the end of 2011 if the economy picks up as expected. The authorities plan to repay all
outstanding arrears on the new Eurobond issue early in 2012, No further debt restructuring has
been requested, but only some flexibility on debt service for 2011. External debt service will
then fall well below the critical threshold of 25 percent of revenues.,

2.31 Upon reaching the HIPC completion point, Côte d’Ivoire would qualify for relief
under the Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative, which is estimated to reduce debt service on debt to
IDA, the IMF, and the AfDB by just over US$2 billion (in nominal terms), as well as additional
debt relief from bilateral creditors. The HIPC completion point triggers are given in Table 2.3.
The authorities now hope to reach completion point in 2012. The proposed operation provides
support to move the country toward HIPC completion point by facilitating the return to
macroeconomic stability, clearing the backlog of the certification of draft budget execution laws,
continuing progress toward EITI compliance, and reducing cocoa taxes. Reaching completion
point will represent a powerful signal to domestic and international creditors and investors that
Côte d’Ivoire has finally regained control of its finances and has rejoined the community of


                                            - 11 -
creditworthy nations, and for that reason it is a top priority for the authorities and indeed the
population.

2.32 However, debt service payments could actually increase after completion point due
to the restructuring of Paris and London Club debt, though they would remain below the
critical threshold of 25 percent of revenues. Under the generous London and Paris Club
agreements, debt service only amounted to some 5 percent of revenues, so some increase would
be normal. As described in the recent DSA, however, additional post-cut-off debt relief from the
Paris Club will be necessary to avoid a sharp jump in debt service. Most of this debt is owed to
the French government. It announced in July 2011 that all outstanding debt would be treated
either through debt forgiveness (roughly €1 billion) or through the conversion of debt service
payments into projects (approximately €2 billion). While the latter will still entail major short-
term cash flow outlays, it will provide significant funding for planned development and pro-poor
spending.

               3. THE GOVERNMENT’S REFORM PROGRAM AND THE
                           PROPOSED OPERATION

3.1     The broad objectives of the new government’s program remain in line with the
Poverty Reduction Strategy. The immediate priorities are security, reconciliation and
reconstruction. These were already a prominent feature of the PRS which focuses on (i)
restoring peace and security and promoting national reconciliation and reunification of the
country; (ii) rebuilding basic infrastructure and social services in the country; (iii) reestablishing
fiscal and debt sustainability; and (iv) reigniting economic growth through structural reforms in
key sectors such as energy, agriculture, manufacturing and financial services. The strategy will
be pursued by the new government but is being adjusted and refined to meet immediate needs
after the recent conflict and to reflect its priorities.

3.2    The previous Gbagbo Government took significant measures to improve
transparency and consultation with stakeholders, with support from the World Bank. The
Poverty Reduction Strategy was developed after extensive consultations throughout the country.
The national budget has been published in the main daily newspaper and key economic
information is now published on the website of the Ministry of Economy and Finance. The EITI

national committee, which includes representatives from civil society, is responsible for
reviewing and approving the annual reports on oil and gas payments to the government. The
cocoa reform committee recently held four consultations with farmers’ organizations and various
private sector groups to obtain feedback on the proposed new cocoa strategy.

3.3      Since 2008, noteworthy steps have been taken to implement reforms in the areas of
public financial management and in the cocoa, energy and financial sectors with the support
of the three Economic Governance and Recovery Grants and the IMF-supported ECF program.
Budget execution improved to reduce discrepancies between approved budgets and actual

by the Forces Nouvelles. In the cocoa sector, the government replaced the leadership of the main

delivery of basic social services and rehabilitate critical infrastructure throughout the

of basic technical and management skills and the vigorous promotion of SMEs will also be
pursued. Agriculture and related rural activities will be supported to promote income-generating
activities and slow down migration to the cities. The free movement of persons and goods
throughout the country will be assured through the elimination of most roadblocks. Large
infrastructure programs in the energy and transport sectors will be launched within a year,
notably through public-private partnerships.

                    A. IMPROVING GOVERNANCE, TRANSPARENCY AND EFFICIENCY IN
                                 PUBLIC EXPENDITURE MANAGEMENT

3.7     Since 2006, the government has implemented public expenditure management
reforms targeting the return to the normal budget cycle, improvements in the execution of
expenditures, the strengthening of financial management and procurement practices and regular
preparation of budget execution reports. Reforms recently implemented by the Government or
on-going include: (i) return to a normal budgetary cycle with the adoption of the 2008, 2009 and

                       B. STRENGTHENING GOVERNANCE, TRANSPARENCY
                             AND EFFICIENCY IN THE COCOA SECTOR

3.18 The cocoa sector, which provides the main source of income for one fifth of the
Ivorian population, has been saddled with excessive taxation and governance problems.
Côte d'Ivoire is the world’s largest cocoa producer, with about 40 percent of total world
production; cocoa accounts for one quarter of its exports and roughly 17 percent of public
revenues. Approximately 700,000 small holder families (about 4 million people) depend on the
sector for their main source of income. However, inadequate producer incentives and poor
management of the sector’s agencies have limited cocoa’s contribution to rural growth and
poverty reduction. Between 2001 and 2007, it is estimated that a staggering CFAF 3.2 trillion
(US$6.4 billion equivalent) were extracted from the sector as fiscal (US$5.0 billion) and quasi
fiscal levies (US$1.4 billion). In relative terms, overall taxation represented about 30-40 percent
of the CIF price leaving a small share - 35 to 40 percent of the CIF price – to farmers. As a
result, roughly 60 percent of cocoa growers now live below the poverty line, and these
households accounted for some 28 percent of the poor nation-wide.

3.19 The cocoa sector has been remarkably resilient during the past ten years of political
crisis. Production has held steady, fluctuating between 1.1 and 1.4 million tons depending

                                            - 20 -
largely on the weather. Revenues and taxes collected have grown as the world price has risen to
near-record levels, driven in good part by anxiety over the state of the Ivoirian cocoa sector.
This in turn is based not only on governance issues but also emerging signs of productivity
erosion due to aging trees, spreading disease and farmers replanting with alternative, more
remunerative crops.

3.20 The previous government launched a series of cocoa sector reform. The authorities
took initial steps to streamline cocoa sector management by: (i) gradually reducing fiscal and
quasi-fiscal levies; (ii) channeling part of the quasi-fiscal levies to a rural investment fund
(Fonds d'investissement en milieu rural, FIMR), established to help finance rural infrastructure
to benefit cocoa farmers, and whose disbursements require joint approval by the Ministers of
Economy and Finance, and of Agriculture; (iii) improving sector transparency and financial
management, by allocating another component of the quasi-fiscal levies to a fund for investments
in productivity enhancement, with improved reporting requirements; (iv) arresting the leadership
of the four institutions in charge of the sector and replacing them with a Cocoa/Coffee Sub-
sector Management Committee; (v) launching a census of cocoa farmers in order to permit the
creation of a representative producers’ organization; and (vi) conducting external audits of the
four institutions. The new committee developed a program of short-term actions to improve the
quality and quantity of cocoa production, and combat disease, including some joint projects with
external partners. This committee has been retained by the new government, but with
considerable change in leadership.

3.21 Lower taxation coupled with higher world prices have improved producer prices.
As shown in Table 3.1, the farmgate price as a share of world prices has steadily increased from
41 percent in 2005-06 to 53 percent in 2009/10. This is partly due to declining fiscal and
parafiscal tax rates, supported by past DPOs, and partly due to rising world prices, since most of
these taxes were specific in nature (charged per kilo rather than as a percentage of the export
price). However, it is important to move to an ad valorem tax system so that when cocoa prices
decline, the government will absorb some of the loss and farmers will not bear the entire burden,
as in the past. In addition, more efficient and competitive domestic marketing is needed to reduce
the costs of transporting from farm to port. But most important, improved productivity at the
farm level will enable farmers to significantly increase their total income. For these reasons, a
new strategy is essential.

 3.22 Efforts to develop a new cocoa strategy began in 2009. A separate committee was
 established to focus on longer-term reform of the cocoa sector and make proposals regarding
 productivity, marketing (domestic/external), domestic transformation, financing, institutional
 set-up, and environmental and social sustainability. Various drafts were shared with the World

 elections. This committee has not been reinstated as of yet, but the new government is well
 aware of the urgent need to clarify a vision for the future of the cocoa sub-sector. The final
 strategy will need to include: (i) a clear definition of the Government’s responsibilities and limits

 interventions to raise farmer productivity.

 Prior Actions under the proposed EGRG and follow-up reforms

 3.23 The proposed PCRRG would support two prior actions to improve governance and
 efficiency in the cocoa sector. The authorities have agreed to convert all cocoa taxes to ad
 valorem taxes at a rate of no more than 22 percent of the CIF price by the beginning of the
 2010/11 crop season. The authorities chose to set export taxes at 19.6 percent of the CIF price,
 and levies going directly to the management committee at 2.4 percent of the CIF price. The rates
 for cocoa beans processed locally are slightly lower.


                                                    - 22 -
3.24 The reduction in taxation and its conversion to ad valorem rates is considered to be
one important part of a medium-term plan to improve the well-being of farmers, based on
the analysis in studies by the World Bank and the EC on cocoa taxation in Côte d’Ivoire. Not
only would it reduce the total level of taxes in a given year, but it would also ensure that the
burden of any fall in world prices would be more equitably shared by the government. Until
2010, the main cocoa tax was a specific tax which was fixed on the basis of volume and not
value (CFAF 210/kg in 2009/10). This meant that this tax did not change if prices declined, and
hence the supply chain had to absorb the loss – and most probably the farmers. This tax reform
will not have a negative effect on government revenues this year because world prices are very
strong. Any future declines should be compensated by other tax reforms under way.

3.25 The second prior action concerns the publication of summaries of the cocoa audits.
This is seen as a critical measure to improve transparency in the sector and help ensure that the
abuses of the past, as outlined in these audits, are properly addressed in the new institutional
structure. The previous government had committed to their publication but was reluctant to do so
while a presidential election was under way for fear of politicizing the issues. There were also
concerns that publication might prejudice the on-going judicial proceedings against the past
leadership of the cocoa entities. As these proceedings have not been completed yet, the
authorities have proposed to publish summaries of these audits which provide the key
information without identifying specific persons or positions.

3.26 Other cocoa sector reforms have been identified as a HIPC Completion Point
trigger to improve the efficiency of the sector and the living standards of the many Ivorians who
depend on it. These are reduction in the overall indirect taxation of the cocoa sector to no more
than 22 percent of the CIF price, adoption by the Government of a new institutional and
regulatory framework for the cocoa/coffee sector, and satisfactory implementation of the
functions under the government’s responsibility for at least six months immediately preceding
the HIPC Completion Point, based on its new strategy for development of the sector.

             C. STRENGTHENING FINANCIAL SECTOR SOUNDNESS AND TRANSPARENCY

3.27 The financial sector had made considerable progress before the most recent crisis.
Starting in 2007, banks began to reengage in the Center, North and West of the country. A new
Nigerian bank, United Bank of Africa, started operations in Côte d’Ivoire and a second one,
Access, acquired 75 percent of the capital of an Ivorian bank. The entry of these new foreign
banks, significantly more capitalized than the Ivorian banks, improved the banking landscape. In
aggregate, the sector is relatively well capitalized due to the strong performance of foreign
private banks which account for about two-thirds of total banking assets. However, government-
owned and private domestic banks are undercapitalized. Overall, liquidity levels are adequate
and the banking sector is profitable.5

3.28 Key challenges in the sector are: (i) a mixed performance in the banking system, with
some large commercial banks in good financial standing, and other smaller ones not in
compliance with prudential standards; (ii) a microfinance sector in challenging conditions (net
worth is negative for the sector as a whole); (iii) a savings bank (CNCE) in a difficult financial

does not contribute to the respect of contracts or debt recovery, and there are weaknesses in the
supervision of financial institutions. The government plans to develop a strategy and action plan
for financial sector reform on the basis of the FSAP, supported by the IMF and the World Bank.

3.29 The recapitalization of small commercial banks which had negative equity in June
2008 has progressed slowly. Two of them have had recapitalization plans approved by the
Banking Commission of the BCEAO. However, two others have been recapitalized by the state,
on the grounds that their specialized mandates, which focused on small and medium enterprise
and agriculture, represented strategic priorities. This intervention is supposed to be temporary as
the state intends to privatize both banks once their financial positions have been strengthened.
Nonetheless, the risk remains that at least one of the banks may not be salvageable and may need
to be liquidated. The previous government committed not to inject any more funds into these
banks after an initial contribution of CFAF 43 billion in 2009.

3.30 The Caisse Nationale des Caisses d’Epargne (CNCE), the dominant savings
institution in the country, was transformed into a commercial bank in 2010. The
commercial banks have not keen to expand their services to small towns and rural areas in light
of the significant costs involved and the limited volume of activities. CNCE, because of the size
of its network (180 branches and service outlets across the country), could fill that gap. The
CNCE inherited a difficult financial position at the time that postal and financial activities were
split, and was accumulating losses. The government’s plan is to restore the financial position of
the CNCE and improve its profitability by obtaining a banking license and developing lending to
public and private sector wage earners. As recommended in the Financial Sector Assessment
Program (FSAP), CNCE will need to proceed cautiously in order to gain the necessary
experience and avoid accumulating non-performing loans.

3.31 The microfinance sector is in crisis and requires urgent reform, as documented in
the FSAP. The sector has been growing quickly and now counts more members (over one
million) than the number of customers reported by all the banks together. Between 2004 and
2008, the number of beneficiaries doubled, deposits increased by 77 percent, and lending by 36
percent. The largest entity is a network of 160 credit unions, the National Union of Savings and
Credit Cooperatives (UNACOOPEC), which represents 85 percent of the microfinance sector.
Unfortunately, many of the institutions in the sector are poorly managed to the extent that one
fourth of customer deposits have been wiped out and the sector as a whole has negative equity.
Insider lending and generally poor governance has become widespread, jeopardizing the critical
role played by UNACOOPEC in providing services to the low-income population. In many
cases, the mandates of officials have been extended without elections, allowing corruption to
continue without sanctions. The withdrawal of traditional development partners because of the
crisis of 2002-03 has deprived the sector of essential technical and financial support, while the
National Microfinance Commission has been unable to properly supervise the sector and control
its expansion. Given its dominant role, UNACOOPEC represents a systemic risk to the sector
and requires immediate attention.

3.32 Some progress has been made in the microfinance sector. A strategy and action plan
for the sector was approved by the Cabinet in October 2008, and the operating budget of the

                                            - 24 -
Microfinance Directorate in the Ministry of Economy and Finance was tripled between 2004 and
2008 in order to improve supervision. The Ministry conducted a joint mission with the BCEAO
in September 2009 to assess the situation of some of the major micro finance institutions (MFI).
Two of the five largest MFI networks have been placed under provisional administration, 22
credit unions are in the process of losing their license, and 8 others have already had their
licenses revoked. Significant recovery of non-performing loans was achieved, notably in
UNACOOPEC. However, this progress seems to have been reversed in 2010 when the ratio of
non-performing loans in the sector as a whole jumped from 6.6 percent to 11 percent. The
impact of the most recent crisis has not yet been determined but it does not seem to have
fundamentally affected the situation.

3.33 The authorities have launched an in-depth restructuring of the microfinance sector.
This is being done on the basis of the national microfinance sector strategy and will require a
comprehensive audit. Particular attention is being given to UNACOOPEC to bring its net worth
back to a positive level through intensive loan recovery, cutting overhead costs, and closing
some cooperatives. On this basis, it is expected that UNACOOPEC could generate a small
surplus in 2011 and be recapitalized over two to three years.
Prior action under the proposed PCRRG

3.34 The proposed PCRRG would support the following action judged to be critical to
the soundness and transparency of the microfinance sector, and important for job creation.
The Government will begin implementation of the restructuring plan for UNACOOPEC, by
renewing the Board of Directors, reducing staff levels in the head office, and closing at least 10
cooperatives. These cooperatives will be merged with better-managed entities so that no deposits
are lost. These changes will reduce the financial burden of the head office on the network,
increase the likelihood of reform, and arrest the accumulation of losses in unsustainable
cooperatives. This will send a powerful message to elected officials that the rules will be
enforced and corrective action taken when necessary. As such, these are key elements of a larger
strategy to protect the savings of depositors, ensure their continued access to credit, and thereby
encourage job creation through enterprise development.

                        D. IMPROVING GOVERNANCE, TRANSPARENCY AND
                               EFFICIENCY IN THE ENERGY SECTOR

3.35 Sustained by growing demand for electricity and high oil prices, the energy sector
has experienced strong growth in recent years. Côte d’Ivoire is a modest net oil exporter,
exporting its own heavy crude oil, while importing lighter crude to be refined locally for the
domestic and sub-regional markets. It relies on a combination of imported oil, and domestically-
produced natural gas and hydropower to satisfy internal electricity demand. It has been one of
the three largest providers of electricity in West Africa, servicing Mali, Burkina Faso, Benin and
Togo in the recent past. However, rising domestic demand and a lack of investment in supply
capacity is resulting in reduced exports, while the declining share of hydropower as a source of
energy has changed the cost structure of the sector. Meanwhile, oil and gas reserves are reaching
the end of their productive life and oil production is already falling.

3.36 The electricity sector is threatened by years of minimal maintenance and an
investment freeze, which have resulted in growing technical losses. This has also raised the risk


                                            - 25 -
of major technical breakdowns and an inability to face rising domestic power demand. As a
consequence, Côte d’Ivoire suffered serious power shortages in the first half of 2010, despite
cutting exports sharply. New generation projects will not be commissioned before 2013 due to
difficulties faced by some private project developers in raising the needed funds. Furthermore,
the transmission and distribution system, which is the responsibility of the state, also needs an
extensive overhaul, but this is not likely to happen in the short term because of the sector’s
financial deficit. Due to rising fuel costs, greater reliance on thermal power, limited power tariff
adjustments, technical losses, fraud and difficulties in billing in the areas formerly held by the
Forces Nouvelles, the electricity sector has suffered increasing financial losses amounting to an
estimated CFAF 110 billion (1 percent of GDP) in 2010.

3.37 The price of gas used to generate electricity has increased dramatically in the past
three years since it is directly linked to international oil prices under existing supply
contracts. These contracts do not include any cap on the level of potential increases, and prices
are based on the last 12 months. In 2010, the gas bill of the electricity sector represented about
70 percent of total electricity sales revenue. In order to cope with the situation, the government
foregoes its share of the gas revenues to which it would normally be entitled as a part owner of
the gas production operations, which is only partly compensated by arrears accumulated by the
state on its electricity bill. Such arrangements are clearly suboptimal and need to be resolved
quickly by aligning contractual arrangements to international standards.

3.38 Progress has been made in increasing revenues. The electricity company plans to
regularize billing in the northern zones of the country. After a five-year tariff freeze, prices were
increased by 16-18 percent in mid-2008 for industrial and commercial clients and by 10 percent
in early 2009 for better-off residential customers accounting for 60 percent of residential
consumption. However, electricity prices remain below those in neighboring countries and more
significant increases are required over the coming 2-3 years to help eliminate subsidies from the
national budget. An ongoing tariff study is expected to be completed by October 2011. A
Poverty and Social Impact Assessment study is also planned to complete the tariff structure
reform.

3.39 Oil exports have started to decline in volume terms, though their value has been
buoyed by high world prices. In 2010, Côte d’Ivoire produced 17 million barrels but this is
expected to fall to only 14 million barrels in 2011. Gas production remains stable but there is an
urgent need to develop new reserves given the long lead time required and the dependence of the
electricity sector and many large industrial enterprises on natural gas as their energy source. The
country has been relatively less successful than its neighbors in attracting sizeable investment for
the development of its deep-offshore oil potential and gas reserves, but recent initiatives taken by
the Government and PETROCI aim to attract serious operators to seek and develop oil and gas
prospects in the deep and ultra-deep offshore.

3.40 PETROCI is hampered by its lack of full autonomy and accountability. It lacks the
experience in promoting its business overseas that an oil company would normally gain by
establishing bona fide arm's length contractual relations with international oil companies (IOCs).
PETROCI requires more operational autonomy, with the government withdrawing from its day-
to-day operational business, as done in the refining industry. This would transform PETROCI
into a pro-active local industrial partner and a more knowledgeable operator. New state-of-the-
art Production Sharing Contracts (PSCs) are also needed to help develop new prospects without

                                             - 26 -
requiring any initial capital outlay from PETROCI or the Government. The contracts involve
signing with an IOC at a percentage to be negotiated, but the IOC carries the risks, and shares the
rewards with PETROCI if oil or gas is found. This should allow PETROCI to either contribute in
cash or in kind to the development and production stages of the new field, according to its share
in the PSC, including the option of foregoing part of its profit-oil. Meanwhile, the Government
would continue to receive its royalties and taxes, in line with oil and gas production levels. It
was such PSCs which led to the recent major oil discoveries in Ghana.

3.41 The Government has taken steps to adhere to the Extractive Industry Transparency
Initiative (EITI). The Membership Agreement was approved by the Cabinet in January 2008
and signed by the President the following month. A National Committee (CN-EITI) was
established, an administrator appointed, and the EITI reports reconciling payments made with
revenues received in 2006 and 2007 were finalized and accepted by the CN-EITI in March 2010.
The validation report of the entire EITI program in Côte d’Ivoire was submitted to the
international secretariat of EITI in October 2010. However, as has been the case with many other
candidate countries, the international secretariat recommended some additional work to
strengthen the document by preparing a second reconciliation report for 2008 and 2009. This
work began with World Bank funding in 2010 before it was interrupted by the post-election
crisis and the suspension of World Bank operations. This report will also be needed in order for
the country to comply with one of the triggers for the HIPC completion point.

3.42 The Government adopted a letter of sector development policy for electricity
(LSDPE) in January 2009 which spells out its vision for the sector over the medium term and
places emphasis on: (a) restoration of full financial viability by 2012; (b) ensuring that supply
and demand for power are in balance while increasing the population’s access to electricity; and
(c) improving the sector’s institutional framework. The action plan resulting from the LSDPE
includes: (i) streamlining the entities functioning in the electricity sector, by clarifying their role,
eliminating overlapping responsibilities and, thus, reducing their operating costs; (ii) transfer of
the sector’s assets to SOGEPE’s accounts to enable it to mobilize long term funding for the much
needed sector investments; and (iii) strengthening the regulation of the electricity sector by
changing the role and status of the regulatory agency ANARE, with the aim of ensuring its
autonomy and granting it real regulatory authority. A road map was defined in April 2009 to
restore the financial equilibrium of the sector and phase out operating subsidies by end 2011. The
authorities intend to draw on all potential revenue sources by: renegotiating gas contracts, raising
export prices, reducing losses from technical inefficiencies and fraud, improving bill collection
throughout the country, and increasing electricity tariffs. The current context of painful recovery
from the latest crisis limits some of these options in the short term. Nevertheless, and while
putting in place a credible financial recovery plan, the government needs to address increasing
arrears to IPPs and gas operators to enable them to finance the operational expenses required to
ensure electricity availability and avoid a possible shortfall in capacity. This will also reassure
investors considering the development of new, badly needed IPP capacity already under
negotiation.

3.43 Having clarified SOGEPE's legal status as the electricity sector asset holding
company, the Government intends to draft a new sector law to operationalize the
institutional changes announced in the LSDPE. The new law would give a coherent regulatory
framework and provide for a transparent decision making process as far as investment strategy


                                               - 27 -
and financing, financial management and operation supervision are concerned. The IDA-
financed Urgent Electricity Rehabilitation Project (US$50 million equivalent) provides funds to
recruit consultants to review all existing sector legislation and prepare proposals for
modification.

Prior actions under the proposed PCRRG and follow-up reforms

3.44 The government remains committed to the Extractive Industry Transparency
Initiative. The proposed grant will support the submission by the government of a validation
report for 2006 and 2007 to the International Secretariat of EITI, reconfirming its commitment to
early compliance with the conditions required for full membership of EITI.

3.45 The government is also committed to other reforms judged to be critical to advance
reforms in the energy sector, namely: (i) defining the roles and responsibilities of key
stakeholders in the oil and gas sector through a) adoption of a Hydrocarbons Code, and b)
signature of a performance contract with PETROCI; (ii) adopting a new draft electricity law
defining the role of the different actors and a new institutional and regulatory framework for the
electricity sector; and (iii) completion of the negotiations with the gas operators and the Ivorian
Electricity Company, and implementation of a financial plan to reduce the operating deficit of
the sector. However, there was insufficient time after the restoration of peace in April 2011 and
the creation of a new government in early June to allow for completion of these measures, so
they will be supported by a possible future operation.

3.46 Two additional energy sector actions have been identified as HIPC Completion
Point triggers aimed at improving transparency on financial flows in the mining, oil and gas
sectors: (a) regular public reporting of payments to, and revenues received by, the Government
for the extractive industries (mining, oil and gas) in line with the EITI criteria, with a recent
annual report during at least the year immediately preceding the completion point; and (b) annual
publication, within seven months of the end of the fiscal year, of the certified financial
statements, in accordance with international standards, of PETROCI for at least the year
immediately preceding the completion point. Both of these actions are on track.

        A. LINK TO THE COUNTRY PARTNERSHIP STRATEGY AND THE AFRICA STRATEGY

4.1      A Country Partnership Strategy for the period FY10-FY13 was discussed by the
Board in April 2010. Four key pillars were identified: (i) strengthening economic governance;
(ii) infrastructure renewal; (iii) agricultural production and export promotion; and (iv) private
sector development. Under the first pillar, a number of development policy operations (including
the proposed PCRRG) were envisaged to help deepen the reforms begun under previous EGRGs
and detailed in Section 3 and in the PRSP. The proposed reforms are essential to the
Government’s ability to use public resources efficiently and effectively to support the country’s
economic and social recovery. Complementary technical and financial support for these
governance reforms would continue to be provided by the ongoing Governance and Institutional
Development Grant.

4.2     The CPS is being reoriented in the light of the latest crisis and rapid reengagement
strategy being formulated. This strategy, which will be formalized in forthcoming months as
part of the progress report on the ongoing CPS, aims to improve the relevance, timeliness and
significance of the Bank’s response, in line with the recommendations of the 2011 WDR and the
Africa strategy. It aims to help government deliver essential services and accelerate growth,

4.3    This approach and the proposed operation are fully consistent with the Africa

governance. They are designed to address the immediate needs of a state that is extremely

economy’s competitiveness, while accompanying investment projects focus on employment
generation. Improving governance is the over-arching theme.

                     B. COMPLEMENTARITY WITH OTHER BANK ACTIVITIES

4.4     The proposed PCRRG is closely coordinated with and is complementary to
Government’s reforms supported by several IDA-financed ongoing or planned operations

Section 3 and Annex 3).

4.5     The ongoing Post-Conflict Assistance Project (US$120 million) has been
restructured to respond more directly to the most immediate needs of the new government.
It is supporting labor intensive public works, creating up to 10,000 new temporary jobs in crisis
affected areas including Abidjan and the West, while continuing its pre-existing operations in the

                                             - 29 -
North. Thirteen service platforms for youth employment promotion are being established, in

4.7      The proposed PCRRG complements or leverages the support of other development

4.8      IMF and IDA staffs have coordinated closely on the identification of the respective

4.10 The AfDB and IDA staffs have coordinated closely in supporting the Government’s

                                       E. LESSONS LEARNED

4.15 The design of this operation has benefited from the guidance provided by OPCS,
lessons from other development policy operations, the specific lessons from previous Economic
Governance and Recovery Grants, and the 2011 WDR:

      Resist pushing for ambitious policy and institutional reforms at this particular stage of the
       country’s transition. The new government has not had time to define a detailed reform
       agenda, yet it is in urgent need of financial support. Overly ambitious prior actions are
       likely to delay the operation, or encourage the authorities to adopt measures that they do
       not fully own.
      Concentrate on high priority sectors which will be key elements of any government’s
       strategy to improve the degree of national ownership and the likelihood of follow-up.
       The sectors covered by this operation are key pillars of the economy as confirmed in the
       Poverty Reduction Strategy.
      Deepening reforms within the same sectors from one operation to another is preferable to
       changing the sector focus. Only gradual progress can be expected between any two
       operations, especially in a fragile, post-conflict situation, and the needed reforms
       typically require several steps over a medium-term time frame.
      The effectiveness of Bank-supported development policy operations can be enhanced if
       complemented by other Bank-financed operations and the efforts of other donors and
       lending agencies. This operation benefits greatly from the parallel activities implemented
       under the Governance and Institutional Development Grant. Close coordination with the
       IMF-supported ECF and RCF and the HIPC completion point reform agenda also serve
       to maintain a consistent and focused dialogue with the authorities.

                    B. OTHER ACTIONS UNDER THE GOVERNMENT’S PROGRAM

5.5     Many of the reforms identified in EGRG III for future support remain valid and
indeed urgent. This is particularly true of the energy sector, where the electricity sub-sector
crisis continues and the oil and gas sub-sector has begun to decline. The cost of gas used to
produce much of the electricity needs to be renegotiated to break its link to the world price of oil,
and start to reduce the burden on the budget. A new electricity law is needed to clarify the roles
and responsibilities of the different actors. In the oil and gas sub-sector, a new hydrocarbons law
and a performance contract with PETROCI will be necessary to improve governance and
promote the new exploration urgently needed to offset the decline in existing fields. This is
important not just because this sub-sector could be a major source of exports and growth, but
also because it is difficult to import natural gas to replace domestic supplies on which much of
local industry depends. Completion of the civil service census and the unified personnel
database, also identified in EGRG III, remain critical as a means to control the size of the wage
bill.

5.6     In addition, it will be important to stimulate growth and employment in other
sectors through private sector development. There are promising agricultural sub-sectors like
rubber, oil palm, cashew and cotton which need a stronger regulatory framework and better
coordination between actors in order to stimulate investment by existing players, and bring in
new investors in a way which strengthens the sub-sector rather than disrupting it. A new law is
being developed to define the role of associations of sub-sector stakeholders known as “inter-
professions? and it should be adopted once a few outstanding issues are resolved. Small
enterprise could be encouraged by introducing a single window for the creation of new
enterprises, or the formalization of informal ones. Currently Côte d’Ivoire is ranked 172nd out of
183 countries in Doing Business on the ease of starting a business. The authorities will be
encouraged to learn from Senegal where their ranking has improved to 101st. Finally, the
training fund, which had been supported by the training levy on private enterprise, was changed
several years ago to help finance the Ministry of Labor. It will be important to re-establish this
fund so that the levy is used for its intended purpose, ensuring that the process is demand driven
by the private sector.

5.7      These actions will be further supported by investment projects to assist the private
sector. These include the SME Revitalization and Governance Project (US$15 million
equivalent) and a new Employment and Skills Development Project (US$50 million). The
former is developing a pilot project on SME funding, supporting reforms to improve Doing
Business indicators, tackling the problem of roadblocks, developing commercial courts, and
promoting public-private dialogue. The latter will address the skills shortages faced by the
private sector. Other projects will focus on energy and other infrastructure needs, as well as trade
facilitation at the port. An Investment Climate Survey Report has been conducted and
disseminated and is being supplemented by a follow-up survey for the center-north-west which
could not be covered by the initial exercise.

                        Box 5.1: Good Practice Principles on Conditionality
Principle 1: Reinforce ownership
The proposed PCRRG focuses on reforms which were already started under previous government and which the
new government has confirmed as continuing priorities. It avoids introducing subjects which the new government
has not yet been able to examine and build consensus. The unit responsible for the PCRRG program within the
Government is the Ministry of Economy and Finance, where the key actors remain the same. In order to reinforce
ownership at the sector level, the Ministry of Economy and Finance has coordinated the dialogue with working
groups and teams including other government agencies and organizations (e.g., Ministry of Agriculture, Ministry
of Mining and Energy, and the BCEAO).

Principle 2: Agree up front with the Government and other financial partners on a coordinated accountability
framework
The Government’s reform program supported by the PCRRG is summarized in the policy matrix in Annex 3. The
core of the program was developed during intensive policy dialogue with the authorities and in consultation with its
key financial partners during 2009 and 2010. The program has been coordinated with the IMF, AfDB, EC, and
Agence Française de Développement (AFD). As IDA has been the only regular provider of budget support, a
unified policy matrix and results framework has not yet been established, but this is planned in the coming year as
other partners prepare for more regular support. The expected results of the program are detailed in para. 5.8.

Principle 3: Customize the accountability framework and modalities of Bank support to country circumstances
The policy matrix has been simplified, with an emphasis on reforms started in 2010, in recognition of the urgency
of financial support and the limited time available for the new government to define its reform agenda. Actions
requiring approval by the Council of Ministers have been avoided, since the full government had not been
announced at the time of the preparation mission and would require some time to establish its work program.

Principle 4: Choose only actions critical for achieving results as conditions for disbursement
The policy matrix uses a limited set of seven prior actions for Board presentation. The focus on improved public
financial management is essential for a budget support operation. The cocoa sub-sector is the most important pillar
of the economy, and the one with the poorest people. It and the oil sub-sector are the ones which have suffered the
most governance problems in the last ten years. Microfinance is the component of the financial sector which has
the greatest direct effect on the poor and where the need for restructuring is most urgent. Several of the actions
also help the authorities move closer to HIPC completion point.

Principle 5: Conduct transparent progress reviews conducive to predictable and performance-based financial
support
IDA staff keeps in close touch with the authorities through its country office and regular missions from
headquarters. They also participate actively in all IMF missions, where many of these issues are raised.
Appropriate results indicators are discussed, as well as the type of follow-up reforms which the authorities are
considering and the extent to which they might be suitable for a future budget support operation. Going forward,
IDA staff will continue to monitor program implementation, and will move toward a programmatic approach as
soon as the new government has a medium-term strategy in place.

                           6. OPERATION IMPLEMENTATION

                                   A. COUNTRY OWNERSHIP

6.1     The proposed operation is supported and owned by the authorities, as indicated in
the Government’s Letter of Development Policy (LDP). The authorities have clearly
underlined their commitment to the sectors and reforms covered by this operation. Improved
budget effectiveness and accountability have been reiterated as top priorities, and the authorities
have requested a new PEMFAR to extend and deepen their reform program. The Ministry of
Energy and Mines organized a major international conference, June 9-10, within six weeks of the
end of the crisis to discuss the energy sector’s problems, draw up an action plan, and attract new
investors. Cocoa reforms are recognized as politically important as well as the most challenging
outstanding requirement to reach HIPC completion point. Microfinance is also potentially
politically explosive with over one million members at risk of losing their deposits. Thus, the
new government has reaffirmed its commitment to implementing the microfinance restructuring
plan. These areas also emerge from the PRSP, which provided an opportunity to foster national
consensus across the Ivorian society and among Government representatives.

6.2     The economic governance reforms supported by this operation will only be
sustainable if ownership goes beyond the government to include the many beneficiaries and
private stakeholders affected by the changes. The increased transparency and accountability
that will accompany or result from many of the reforms are one aspect of improving
communications, understanding and ownership of reforms. The Bank has supported open
consultation around the preparation of its Country Partnership Strategy and has encouraged the
authorities to increase their dialogue with stakeholders. As a result, the cocoa reform committee
carried out a series of consultations on the draft cocoa strategy in 2010, and further discussions
will be encouraged in 2011.

6.3    The Governance and Institutional Development Grant provides technical assistance
to help implement the reform program, including a component to support a
communications program. The project is supplemented by a State and Peace-building Fund
(SPF) grant signed in November 2009 to increase communications around the reform program.
The broader objective of the grant is to help build the communications capacity of government,
media and civil society organizations (CSOs) in regard to promoting understanding of the
economic recovery and reform program. The grant is financing the preparation and
implementation of a government communications strategy, to build on activities started with the
TA operation (newsletters, web site). The SPF grant has three components: (1) assisting


                                            - 38 -
government with a communications program around three themes: (a) PFM, focusing on the
transparency of budget formulation and execution, tracking/reporting on pro-poor expenditures,
and revenues and expenditures from natural resources; (b) the petroleum sector, including
supporting implementation of EITI; and (c) the coffee/cocoa sector – monitoring improved
governance of the sector and producer revenues; (2) strengthening non-government/CSO
capacity to participate in and monitor economic governance reforms; and (3) strengthening the
capacity of the media to report on issues related to PFM, oil and gas, coffee/cocoa, corruption,
civic participation and the PRSP implementation. The Bank completed a governance diagnostic
in early 2011, which strengthens the knowledge-base on the quality of public services and
perceptions of corruption. The findings of the diagnostic are useful in determining the demand-
side governance activities the Government and Bank could support in the future.

6.4      Separately, the Country Office in Abidjan is implementing a communications and
outreach program with a broad array of stakeholders to further support a peaceful and smooth
political transition and economic recovery during and following the electoral period. These
efforts seek to raise awareness and understanding of the economic reform issues on the part of
public opinion leaders while also providing a platform for exchanging views on the country’s
priorities. In addition, the Bank has prepared a communications strategy for its lending and non-
lending activities to strengthen broad-based understanding and support for its activities, as well
as to strengthen two-way channels of communication with stakeholders.

                                    B. FIDUCIARY ASPECTS

6.5     Strengthening Government systems. The fiduciary standards in Côte d’Ivoire suffered
from the crisis of the 2000s but remains strong relative to those in other WAEMU countries in
some respects, notably in the preparation of budget execution bills and in financial management
systems. Nevertheless, there is the need for further improvements in fiduciary standards. This is
a central objective of the public financial management component of the proposed operation and
the Government has made encouraging progress in implementing measures to enhance public
resource management and transparency. These early measures provide a platform for deeper
reforms in the context of the proposed operation and complementary ongoing and planned IDA
and other donors’ interventions (see Section 4).

6.6     These measures include the full reinstatement and application of normal budget
preparation and execution procedures; regular publication of budget execution reports;
clearance of the backlog of outstanding draft budget execution bills to the Chamber of Accounts;
and implementation of public procurement reforms. Building on the PEMFAR findings and
recommendations, the authorities have adopted and are implementing an action plan to further
strengthen budget formulation, implementation, reporting, cash management and control. The
authorities conducted a PEFA self-evaluation in mid-2010 which was then reviewed by two
external consultants. This confirmed that performance had improved in the following areas: the
timely and participatory nature of budget preparation, access to budget information by the public,
transparency in the obligations of taxpayers, and treasury and debt management, all of which
were rated A. While there was some slippage during the crisis of 2011, there have not been any
fundamental reversals, and recent changes in some staff should further strengthen the
implementation of reforms. The PEMFAR update requested for 2012 will deepen the reform
agenda. These measures are accompanied by ongoing efforts to enhance transparency and
efficiency in the energy sector, notably through the follow-up of the recommendations of the

                                            - 39 -
financial, technical and operational audits of the energy sector and by joining the Extractive
Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI). Cocoa sector reforms have also strengthened the
management of para-fiscal levies on cocoa exports.

6.7     In 2005, the IMF staff carried out an on-site safeguards assessment of the Central
Bank of the West African States (BCEAO), the common central bank of the West African
Economic and Monetary Union, which includes Côte d’Ivoire. The assessment found that
progress had been made in strengthening the BCEAO’s safeguards framework since 2002 when
the last safeguard assessment was undertaken. The BCEAO now publishes a full set of audited
financial statements and improvements have been made to move financial reporting closer to
International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). IMF staff noted that the BCEAO has
improved the explanatory notes to the financial statements and further changes are scheduled for
the next fiscal year, with a view toward a gradual alignment with International Accounting
Standard, as adopted internationally by other central banks. Furthermore, an internal audit
charter has been put in place, mechanisms for improving risk management and risk prevention
have been established and follow-up on internal and external audit recommendations has been
strengthened. The assessment identified a number of areas where further steps would help
solidify the progress made in recent years. The main recommendations relate to: (i) improving
the external audit process by adopting a formal rotation policy and further enhancing the
transparency of the financial statements by adopting IFRS in full; and (ii) enhancing the
effectiveness of the internal audit function by further strengthening the reporting to management
of the BCEAO. The status report of the implementation of recommendations, received from the
Bank in March 2007, indicates that some progress was achieved.

                 C. GRANT ADMINISTRATION, DISBURSEMENT AND AUDITING

6.8     The proposed operation would consist of a single tranche grant of SDR 93.9 million
(US$150 million equivalent) to be available upon effectiveness and disbursed on the basis of
a withdrawal application. The grant will follow the Bank’s disbursement procedures for
development policy operations. Once the grant becomes effective, the Government of Côte
d’Ivoire will submit a withdrawal application to IDA requesting that the proceeds of the grant be
deposited in the BCEAO into a dedicated account that forms part of the country’s official foreign
exchange reserves. Within five working days upon the deposit of the grant into said account, the
Recipient shall ensure that an equivalent amount is credited in its budget management system, in
a manner acceptable to the Bank to finance management expenditures, and it will also report to
the Bank on the amounts deposited in the foreign currency account and credited to the budget
management system. Disbursement would not be linked to specific purchases. When the funds
are disbursed from the dedicated account in order to finance government expenditures, the
official exchange rate for that day will be used. If the proceeds of the grant are used for ineligible
purposes as defined in the Financing Agreement, IDA will require the Recipient to refund an
amount equal to the amount of said payment to IDA promptly upon notice from IDA. Amounts
refunded to the Bank upon such request shall be cancelled. The Bank will retain a right to seek
an audit of the dedicated account where funds have been disbursed, by independent auditors
acceptable to the Bank.

                                  D. ENVIRONMENTAL ASPECTS

6.9     Under Operational Policy 8.60 on Development Policy Lending, the Bank is
required to determine whether specific country policies supported under the operation are
likely to cause significant effects on the country’s environment and, in the event of such
likely effects, assess country systems for reducing any such adverse effects and enhancing such
positive effects. In the event of significant gaps in the analysis, or shortcomings in the country
systems, the Bank is required to identify how such gaps or shortcomings would be addressed
before or during program implementation, as appropriate.

6.10 The specific reforms supported by the proposed development policy grant are not
likely to have significant negative effects on the country’s environment, forests and other
natural resources. Any likely adverse effects are expected to be minor and manageable through
the existing Bank framework. The reforms supported aim primarily to strengthen economic
governance at the national government level. The measures to improve management of public
finances and the cocoa sector are not considered to carry environmental implications. It is not
expected that the export tax reductions included in the current operation will have any significant
impact on the environment. If there is an effect on production, it will likely be to slow down the
exodus of farmers from the sector, which is already happening, rather than to promote any
expansion in the area under cultivation. For the sector to thrive in the medium-term, it will be
necessary to increase productivity per hectare so that farmers can make an adequate living on the
land they already have. But marginal farmers are still likely to leave the sector, either for the city
or other more profitable crops, such that the total area under cocoa declines over time. The new
strategy under development is expected to improve the manner in which current environmental
issues are addressed.

6.11 A Social and Environmental Assessment (SEA) has been launched with support
from the ongoing Governance and Institutional Development Grant. It will identify potential
negative impacts of the energy sector, with particular emphasis on offshore petroleum
developments, and determine their likely size and probability. It will also examine the
institutional capacity to deal with such impacts, and propose regulatory, procedural and
institutional improvements for the prevention and management of the potential social and
environmental impact of petroleum operations. The study started in February 2010 and the
validation workshop for the draft final report was held in September 2010. The final report was
submitted to the Bank on September 29, 2010. Preparation of the follow-up Environmental and
Social Management Plan is ongoing.

6.12 The Bank is also supporting the preparation of a Sectoral Environmental and Social
Assessment of the cocoa sector. The approach will be an institution-centered one and the
implications for forest-dependent communities, whose welfare may be impacted from
intensification and/or expansion of cocoa plantations, will be analyzed. The issues of pesticide
use, child labor, and climate change will be addressed. The terms of reference of the Sectoral
Environmental and Social Assessment have already been agreed with the National
Environmental Protection Agency. The study will start when there is a permanent institutional
structure and strategy for the sector.

6.13 The Bank has completed a post-conflict Country Environmental Analysis in 2010
looking at the forestry, mining, urban, and environmental governance sectors. The study

                                              - 41 -
reveals the following challenges: (i) weak or almost non-existent mechanisms for the collection,
analysis and dissemination of environmental information; (ii) weak intersectoral collaboration of
the Environment Ministry with ministries responsible for water resources, transport, fishing,
agriculture, and energy; and (iii) low funding available to the sector both from Government, the
private sector and development partners. The Public Environmental Expenditure Review (PEER)
of the CEA estimates that the environment sector allocation is around 0.2 percent of the national
budget. Far reaching recommendations were proposed including assisting the Government to
develop an action plan for the forestry sector for the period 2008-15 and establishing a national
environmental information system.

                                    E. POVERTY AND SOCIAL IMPACT

6.14 The proposed PCRRG is expected to have a significant positive direct impact on
poverty reduction. It builds on the findings of the June 2006 World Bank report “Côte d’Ivoire
– Contributions to the analysis of poverty?,6 the analysis of the 2008 household budget survey in
the PRSP, and the 2010 Côte d’Ivoire Poverty Assessment. These found poverty and inequality
increasing against the backdrop of a continued decline in per capita incomes. The emphasis on
cocoa reforms is particularly important given the large number of poor rural households
producing cocoa, which are estimated to account for 28 percent of the poor in Côte d’Ivoire.
Both partial and general equilibrium analysis confirms that reduced cocoa taxation will improve
the incomes of cocoa producers.7 By one estimate, a 10 percent reduction in cocoa taxes would
increase the value of cocoa output by 16 percent. Furthermore, improvements in the cocoa
sector will raise the rural wage, thereby benefiting rural laborers who are generally among the
poorest and most difficult to assist. A new study has been conducted by the authorities with
support from IDA which confirms that tax and price changes are generally transmitted to the
producer, while recommending ways to further improve the transmission mechanism.8

6.15 Other elements of the proposed grant are also pro-poor. There are likely positive
distributional effects from improved public financial management, transparency in oil and gas,
and reforms in microfinance. Specifically, efforts to improve the capacity of the State to
implement its budget priorities and strengthen public financial management would enhance
efficiency, transparency and accountability in public resource use, and increase the country’s
absorption capacity for external resources. The adoption of medium-term expenditure
frameworks in health and education should increase the quantity and quality of resources going
to these sectors. The financial support provided by the proposed operation, together with
improved governance and transparency in the energy sector, should further strengthen
Government fiscal space for such social services. Increased education spending will enable the
government to enroll some of the 24 percent of primary school-age children not currently in
school, and extend the school feeding program.9 Greater health expenditures will serve to
eliminate the arrears owed by the Public Pharmacy, thereby enhancing access to medicine in
public health centers and hospitals.

                              F. MONITORING AND EVALUATION

6.17 In line with the arrangements under the three EGRGs, the administration of this
operation will be the responsibility of the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF). Day-
to-day monitoring and evaluation of the program and all outcome indicators will be the
responsibility of an Inter-ministerial Economic team appointed by the MEF and composed of the
General Director of the Economy (DGE), the General Director of the Budget and Finance
(DGBF), and the General Director of Planning (DGP). This Economic team will be chaired by
the Cabinet Director of the Minister of Economy and Finance and will coordinate the activities
of all government agencies involved in program implementation. This arrangement was dictated
by the strength and continuity of the economic team and the dialogue on policies carried by
them. The arrangement proved satisfactory under the three EGRGs and the experience gained
will help fine-tune monitoring arrangements for the PCRRG. The Government will provide
quarterly reports to IDA on implementation progress measured against the agreed performance
indicators detailed in para. 5.7. The overall reform effort will be reviewed by the Government in
close coordination with regular IDA missions to ensure continued implementation of the
program within an adequate macroeconomic policy framework.

                                G. RISKS AND RISK MITIGATION

6.18 In the current environment of the country emerging from conflict, the proposed
operation presents significant country and fiduciary risks. The Bank’s active engagement in
Côte d’Ivoire during this period of recovery is a high-risk, high-profile and potentially high-gain
enterprise. The greatest risk, however, is failing to support the peace and recovery process and
missing a window of opportunity for helping to stabilize a key country in an unstable region.
Thus, while there is no guarantee that conflict will not erupt again or that the process of inter-
Ivorian dialogue will not undermine the Government’s commitment to development, the risks of
inaction are much greater. The specific risks that could jeopardize the expected outcomes and
benefits of the proposed grant relate to political, governance, macroeconomic performance and
fiduciary matters.

6.19 Political and security risk. The recent elections have exposed and amplified divisions
within Ivorian society which will take some time to mend. Almost half of the participating
voters supported the out-going president, and the subsequent stalemate and violence reinforced
the animosity on both sides. The role of external actors in securing the victory of President
Ouattara has undermined his legitimacy in the eyes of some. The losing party, the FPI, has
refused to participate in the new government, and upcoming legislative elections could provoke

                                            - 43 -
renewed conflict. Arms, which were already widespread, increased significantly in early 2011 as
both sides prepared for the showdown. A resurgence of violence could set back the peace and
reengagement process. This could delay the authorities’ reform program, and impede progress in
addressing the most pressing social and economic problems, leading to further instability and
adversely affecting the business climate and macroeconomic management. The Government is
pursuing efforts to diffuse political tensions and improve security. Notably, it is implementing a
disarmament, demobilization and reintegration program and creating a new army which
combines the former rebel force with the previous national army. A truth and reconciliation
commission has also been created, modeled after the successful example in South Africa.

6.20 While mitigation of the political and security risk falls outside of the direct scope of
this operation, this DPO should help reduce these risks. Its reforms to improve transparency
in public procurement, cocoa and oil and gas should help reassure the public that public
resources will be used more equitably. The closure of some microfinance cooperatives should
also have positive political repercussions, as their members will be reallocated to better-managed
cooperatives with no loss of deposits, while a clear message is sent about the implications of
poor governance. Continued international support for the peace process, including through the
ongoing World Bank Post-Conflict Assistance Project (see para. 4.5), would help mitigate the
security risk. The United Nations and bilateral donors are actively involved in this process. The
financial support provided by IDA through the proposed operation will help clear domestic
arrears and thereby stimulate the private sector growth and employment urgently needed to
secure the peace.

6.21 Governance and commitment to reform risk. Poor governance and weak
accountability have become ingrained features of the Ivorian economy and it will take a long
while to change behavior through strengthened institutions. The new government has not
completely changed the political class and some members have vested interests in maintaining
the status quo, while others may seek new opportunities for personal gain. It is still too early to
know what their vision is for the critical cocoa and energy sectors. The government continues to
be a coalition, though less broad than before, which could make it difficult to develop a
consensus on some reforms. However, the new government has a strong economic team and
initial announcements have given top priority to improving public expenditure management and
economic governance. To deepen the coalition for overall reform, IDA, IMF, AfDB, and donors
are working to ensure that (i) top priority is given to the proposed reforms; (ii) key basic services
are improved; (iii) targeted interventions for demobilization and reintegration continue; and (iv)
Government implements an effective consultation and communication strategy. The Government
plans to launch a new ECF program in September and is committed to rapid attainment of the
HIPC completion point. The strong desire to reach completion point provides some leverage,
notably related to the outstanding trigger related to a new cocoa strategy. Intensive
communication efforts on the Government’s reforms will be supported by the Governance and
Institutional Development Grant to better inform the public and help build demand for
governance and transparency reforms. Trust funds have been obtained from the State- and
Peace-Building Fund to finance demand-side governance activities which will aim to strengthen
the capacity of the media to cover economic reforms and conduct investigative reporting. A
Governance Diagnostic Survey has just been completed providing government officials and
other stakeholders with a framework and tools to promote good governance.



                                             - 44 -
6.22     Macroeconomic risk. The country’s macroeconomic performance represents another
important risk to the reforms supported by the proposed operation. The political crisis of early
2011 knocked the IMF-supported ECF program off track, necessitating its replacement by a
temporary RCF. Economic recovery will depend critically on the Government’s ability to adopt
a new medium-term macroeconomic framework elaborated in collaboration with the IMF and the
World Bank. In addition, while the world price of cocoa and oil remains strong, the volume of
production is falling in the latter, and may do so as well in the former. The recent violence has
further discouraged foreign direct investment, at least in the short-term, and created new external
arrears with the Paris and London Club creditors. Mitigating the above risks are Côte d’Ivoire’s
recent track record in macroeconomic management, the stability of the team in the Ministry of
Economy and Finance, and the Government’s commitment to starting a new ECF and the rapid
attainment of the HIPC completion point. The IMF and IDA will continue to closely monitor the
Government’s macroeconomic performance.

6.23 Fiduciary risk. The financial management assessment has concluded that fiduciary risks
are substantial mainly because of political instability and governance issues. This risk is
mitigated by the focus of the proposed grant on the improvement in fiduciary standards and
continued attention of IDA and other donors on public financial management and public
administration which will help to improve budget execution processes over time. The
government has been actively pursuing the action plan resulting from the PEMFAR, with
technical and financial assistance provided under the ongoing Governance and Institutional
Development Grant. The PEMFAR will be updated in 2011-12, in cooperation with the EU and
the AfDB.

6.24 In IDA’s assessment, the potential benefits of the proposed operation outweigh the
residual risks and warrant IDA’s assistance for implementing critical reforms and policy actions
in a coordinated fashion with other donors, while supporting risk mitigation actions to maximize
the sustainability of the reform agenda.

Subject: Letter of Development Policy


Dear Mr. Zoellick:

1.      This Letter of Development Policy (LDP) describes the socioeconomic situation in Côte
d'Ivoire in 2010 and the outlook for 2011. It sets out the specific objectives and policies that the
authorities intend to implement within the framework of the Government program in 2011. In
view of the difficult circumstances in Côte d’Ivoire, the authorities are counting on assistance
from the international community, particularly the World Bank, in the form of general budget
support, through the Post-Conflict Reconstruction and Recovery Grant (PCRRG).

I.     The economic and political context

2.      After a slow start, Côte d’Ivoire succeeded in organizing democratic and transparent
presidential elections. The mobilization of the population as well as Ivorian political figures and
the international community within the framework of the Ouagadougou Political Accord led to
significant advances in the process of exiting the crisis, particularly as a result of the organization
and smooth progress of the first round of the presidential election, in a situation of calm, on
October 31, 2010.
3.      However, the announcement of the results of the second round of the presidential election
of November 28, 2010 plunged the country into a serious post-election crisis, beginning in
December 2010, which lasted about 6 months and affected the country’s political, economic and
social situation. The crisis displaced Ivoirians from their homes; banks were closed for two
months with damaging consequences for both businesses and households; the European Union
imposed economic sanctions, particularly an embargo on the country’s two ports; offices,
businesses, residences and factories were destroyed and looted; and heavy armed combat broke
out in the country’s economic capital. The consequences persist in the form of continuing


                                                - 54 -
insecurity, increased racketeering and the closing of thousands of small and medium-sized
enterprises, despite a perceptible improvement since early May.
4.      The Government’s immediate priority is to consolidate peace through economic
normalization and recovery. Accordingly, the Government is working hard to (i) restore security
throughout the national territory and to combat racketeering as well as road harassment, (ii)
ensure that administrations function properly, (iii) consolidate peace and provide an immediate
response to social demand in the fight against precariousness; (iv) rehabilitate socioeconomic
infrastructures, and (v) promote economic recovery, particularly by improving the business
climate and strengthening good governance.
5.      The Government’s economic and financial program for 2009-2011 was suspended as a
result of the post-election crisis. As soon as the crisis was over, the Government began settling
its arrears and prepared an emergency program pending preparation of the new three-year
program designed to bring the country to the HIPC Initiative completion point.


II.    Progress of Reforms in 2010

6.      Economic activity in 2010 was impeded by several shocks, particularly the energy crisis
at the start of the year, the decline in crude oil production and the post-election crisis. These
developments led, in 2010, to: (i) real GDP growth of 2.4% instead of the target of 3%; (ii)
annual average inflation of 1.8% in 2010 compared to 1.0% in 2009, attributable to the
difficulties of supplying the markets in the aftermath of the post-election disturbances and a
budget deficit (including grants and excluding the reduction of external arrears) of 2.3% of GDP,
versus a target of 1.5% of GDP. In general, progress was made in implementing structural
reforms in the areas of governance, transparency, public finances, the financial sector, the energy
sector, the coffee-cocoa sector, general government, and the business environment. The
implementation of reforms slowed in the third quarter of 2010, however, because of the
sociopolitical crisis.

       a) Public expenditure management reforms

7.     Progress was achieved in implementing the reforms recommended in the Public
Expenditure Management and Financial Accountability Review (PEMFAR). The National Public
Procurement Regulatory Authority (ANRMP), established by Decree No. 2009-260 of August 6,
2009 on the organization and functioning of the National Public Procurement Authority, is in
operation. The Authority’s responsibilities are clearly defined, in accordance with the applicable
WAEMU directives. Operating with full independence, the ANRMP is a special agency that
performs the function of regulating public procurement. Its independence is assured by its
balanced composition, the multiplicity of its financing sources and the method of electing its
President. Notable among its activities in 2010 are the inauguration of its headquarters in
September, the holding of regular Board meetings, the establishment of the “hot line? system and
the production of quarterly activity reports.

8.      As part of the process of restructuring and privatizing public enterprises, operational
audits and inspections were stepped up. With a view to increasing the staff of the Directorate of


                                            - 55 -
Participation and Privatization, nine new employees were hired in 2010 to strengthen the
supervision and monitoring of State enterprises through operational audits and inspections. Thus,
the number of inspections rose from 11 in 2009 to 27 in 2010. With regard to public enterprise
audits, the final reports on the work of all the firms are now available. In addition, the reports on
the audits of the four units responsible for crisis-exit programs have been completed.
9.      In the context of regional integration, the States are required to transpose into their
domestic legislation, no later than December 31, 2011, the Directives adopted in March and June
2009 by the WAEMU Council of Ministers. The Directives cover the code of transparency in
public financial management, the budget law, the State budget nomenclature, the general
regulation on government accounting, the State chart of accounts and the State Financial
Operations Table. Draft decrees and orders concerning the transposition of these public finance
Directives have been prepared and are on hold pending adoption of the organic laws setting out
the transparency code and the public finance law. The latter are in process of preparation prior to
their submission to the Government for adoption.
10.     Moreover, within the framework of efforts aimed at controlling the wage bill, the
Government undertook a census of State officials and employees in order to gain better control
of the wage bill. The Technical Committee chaired by the Minister of the Civil Service launched
the census of State officials and employees in April 2010. The pilot phase covering all three
ministries has been completed.
11.    The internal assessment carried out in August 2010 revealed significant progress in the
implementation of public financial reforms based on the PEMFAR. Additional efforts are
needed, however, to ensure a satisfactory assessment in 2012.

       b) Energy sector reform

12.     The Government continued its energy sector transparency and reform efforts with a view
to achieving greater efficiency and contributing to State revenues, with World Bank support.
      The final report for 2006 and 2007 conforming to the criteria of the Extractive Industries
       Transparency Initiative (EITI) was made available in April 2010 and published on the
       website of the Ministry of Economy and Finance (www.finances.gouv.ci). It was
       submitted to the international EITI for validation. In addition, a firm was selected to draft
       the 2008 and 2009 report for subsequent validation and publication.
      Also on the subject of transparency in the hydrocarbons sector, quarterly reports
       specifying the respective shares of the beneficiaries of each lifting were produced
       regularly in 2010 by the Committee responsible for Monitoring Crude Oil Liftings, which
       is chaired by the Directorate-General of Taxes.
      A draft hydrocarbons code was finalized and forwarded to the Secretariat General of the
       Government for adoption by the Council of Ministers.

       c) Cocoa sector reforms

13.    Steps were taken to reduce coffee-cocoa sector taxation while improving transparency in
the quasi-fiscal taxation of investments. Indeed, in the interest of boosting producer incomes, the


                                             - 56 -
Government continued to reduce the levies on the coffee-cocoa sector. At the start of the
2010/2011 crop year, it converted all levies (fiscal and quasi-fiscal) on the cocoa sector into an
ad valorem tax with a rate of 22% of the CFAF price. Similarly, the Coffee-Cocoa Sector
Management Committee (CGFCC) worked to ensure the success of the 2009-2010 and 2010-
2011 crop years by instituting a minimum remunerative price for producers, rationalizing the use
of quasi-fiscal resources, financing social investments (Rural Investment Fund - FIMR) and
implementing the Quantity-Quality-Growth (2QC) program, which includes combating the
diseases that affect coffee and cocoa. In addition, the Government continued including quasi-
fiscal revenue from coffee/cocoa investments (FIMR) in the budget.

       d) Financial sector reforms

14.     Following the Financial Sector Assessment Program (FSAP), the Financial Sector
Reform Committee began preparing for the development of a sectoral strategy. Strategic
orientations and an action plan for reform of the sector produced in the September 2010 seminar
were proposed, based on the analysis and recommendations of the 2009 FSAP. However, in light
of the impact of the post-election crisis on the sector, a new assessment is needed.
15.     Individual measures were taken to improve the governance of microfinance institutions,
including the suspension in June 2010 of certain managers because of their accountability for
mismanaging the institutions they led and for failing to comply with the injunctions of the
National Microfinance Commission (CNM). With a view to improving portfolio quality,
injunctions were issued to all microfinance institutions under temporary administration or under
surveillance by the CNM, so that the necessary steps could be taken to recover all delinquent
loans.
16.    With regard to pension plans, a draft law on reform of the CNPS (National Social
Security Fund), as well as draft implementing legislation, was prepared and submitted to the
Secretariat General of the Government. In addition, the results of the social dialogue undertaken
by the Interministerial Committee for Reform of the Public Pension System (CIRPP/CGRAE)
with the various partners led to the preparation of draft reform legislation that was submitted to
the Government in 2010.

       e) PRSP and HIPC process

17.     The consultation that the Government organized with sectoral actors led to the design of
priority action matrices and sectoral plans, the requirements of which were quantified in the final
quarter of 2009. An annual progress report on PRSP implementation in 2009 was prepared
following adoption of the full PRSP in March 2009. The Government will continue
implementing the PRSP in 2010, in particular by increasing the budget appropriations for “pro-
poor? expenditures and improving their execution.
18.     Of the CFAF 924.4 billion in pro-poor expenditures planned for 2010, a total of
CFAF 885.2 billion (7.8% of GDP) was executed, yielding an execution rate of 95.8%. This
level of execution, although short of the established target, represents a marked improvement of
5% over 2009. The Government also prepared Medium-Term Expenditure Frameworks (MTEF)
for the social sectors (Health and Education-Training), including performance targets and
indicators.


                                            - 57 -
III.   The 2011 Program

       a) General objectives
19.     The main objective of the emergency program is to set the stage for economic recovery
and respond to social demand in the wake of the post-election crisis. The program aims to
stabilize the macroeconomic framework, enhance security, improve the humanitarian situation,
rehabilitate infrastructures, support the private sector and reequip departments. The economic
recovery program also includes a targeted investment component embodied by the Presidential
Emergency Program (PPU). The PPU targets the priority sectors of safe drinking water, health,
education, electricity and urban health, thus confirming the program’s focus on satisfying the
essential needs of the population.
20.     Following a sharp decline in economic activity between January and April 2011, the
trend of real GDP is projected at -6.3%. The rate of inflation is expected to approach 3% by end-
2011. The overall budget deficit (excluding arrears clearance grants) is estimated at 8.5% of
GDP, up from 2.3% of GDP in 2010, as a result of the contraction of domestic revenues and a
fixed volume of expenditures, particularly wages, social expenditures and debt.
21.     To attain these objectives, the Government also plans to implement fiscal measures and
pursue: (i) restructuring of the coffee-cocoa and energy sectors; (ii) reform of general
government to improve efficiency; (iii) reform of social security in the context of fiscal
consolidation; and (iv) financial system strengthening. Particular emphasis will be placed on the
promotion of good governance and the deepening of legal and regulatory reforms in order to
strengthen the rule of law and guarantee security and the free movement of goods and persons.

       b) Governance, transparency, and structural reforms
              Governance and transparency
22.     The Government intends to implement its action plan for improved governance.
Consequently, it will complete the reforms launched in 2009, particularly the national good
governance plan, the ethical charter, the code of good conduct and the law on illegal enrichment.
The Government will also strengthen the system of supervision, inspection and assessment in the
public administration by conducting operational audits of public enterprises, strengthening the
capacities of agencies responsible for supervision (IGF–Inspectorate-General of Finance, and
DPP–Directorate of Planning and Programs) and reactivating the State Inspectorate-General.
23.      The Government will focus in particular on: (i) capacity-building and increasing the
accountability of managers in the financial and tax administrations and in the parapublic sector;
(ii) closer supervision and (iii) improved communication (including publication) of public policy
outcomes.
              Public finance reforms
24.     The 2011 budget covers the period April 26 to end-December 2011. Government
financial operations carried out before that period in 2011 are being validated prior to their
possible inclusion in a supplemental budget. The fiscal revenue targets of the 2011 budget are
below the actual figures of prior years owing to the impact of the post-election crisis on the
economy. Expenditures are up sharply and target the priority actions of the Government,
particularly dealing with the humanitarian and social impacts of the crisis, improving the security


                                            - 58 -
environment, supporting the private sector, as well as fixed costs (public debt service, personnel
expenses). The overall budget deficit (excluding arrears clearance grants) is expected to amount
to 8.5% of GDP during the period.
25.     The improvement of financial management will continue with the preparation of a code
of conduct for public procurement and the introduction of the “hot line? to combat fraud and
corruption in public procurement. In addition, in the context of meeting the WAEMU
convergence criterion on the wage bill, the Government will resume the census of State officials
and employees in order to better control the wage bill. It also plans to adopt in the Council of
Ministers the Draft Law creating the Audit Office and to take into account in the revised budget
the sectoral MTEFs for education-training and health. Finally, the Government plans to request a
technical assistance mission to update the PEMFAR, in coordination with the African
Development Bank, the European Union, and possibly the AFD (French Development Agency).
              Energy sector reforms
26.     With regard to the transparency of the hydrocarbons sector, the Government plans to
reexamine the draft code, which is now available, before it is adopted by the Council of
Ministers and submitted to the National Assembly for a vote. Following Côte d’Ivoire’s
accession to the EITI, the State is committed to following the procedures for conforming to the
EITI validation framework, which is managed by the National EITI Council, particularly by
producing and publishing annual reports in accordance with the EITI criteria. Moreover, the
National EITI Council plans, on behalf of the Government, to complete the remaining work on
the payments and revenue reconciliation report for 2008 and 2009, in order to make the
validation report satisfactory to the EITI International Secretariat.
27.     The adoption of an action plan to reduce electricity subsidies is a priority. The
negotiations undertaken with natural gas operators will continue in 2011, with a view to
establishing floor and ceiling prices for the sale of natural gas. A financial effort to reduce the
remuneration of the power distribution concession holder is expected, in order to bring balance
to the sector. The concession holder will be required to resume the collection of electricity
payments in the former central, north, and west (CNW) regions. The Government expects to
complete the draft electricity code with a view to its implementation. Finally, the rate study will
be completed in 2011 and, on that basis, the revised electricity pricing structure will be adopted.
              Cocoa sector reforms
28.    The new Coffee-Cocoa Sector Management Committee (CGFCC) plans to implement the
Quality, Quantity and Growth (2QC) program, with World Bank support, and to resume the
census of coffee-cocoa sector farmers. The Committee will perform management audits,
covering the period September 2008 to March 2001, of all entities in the sector as well as the
escrow account. In addition, a summary report of previously completed coffee-cocoa sector
audits will be published.
29.    The Government will finalize the coffee-cocoa sector reform in 2011. Implementation of
the new strategy defining a new legal and regulatory framework, as well as a new marketing
mechanism, will begin during the 2011/2012 crop year. Moreover, for that crop year, the
Government is committed to keeping the ad valorem tax on cocoa exports at a level equivalent to
no more than 22% of the CFAF price during the 2011/2012 crop year.




                                            - 59 -
              Financial sector reforms
30.     The financial sector was weakened by the post-election crisis but continues to play a vital
role in the financing of economic activities. To take stock of the economic losses jeopardizing
the financial soundness of the banking sector, an assessment of each bank’s prudential situation
will be conducted by the Banking Commission as soon as possible. The State will ensure that the
necessary steps are taken to ensure proper functioning of the sector through observance of the
prudential standards and appropriate financing of the economy by banks.
31.    With regard to microfinance institutions, the Government will pursue the restructuring of
UNACOOPEC (National Union of Savings and Credit Cooperatives) with (i) the closing of
cooperatives in that network; (ii) replacement of the Board of Directors; and (iii) reduction of the
number of apex staff.
              PRSP process
32.     The Government intends to adjust the PRSP to take account of both the impact of the
post-election crisis and its new priorities, as well as the Government program of the President of
the Republic. To that end, the Priority Action Plan that initially covered the 2009-2013 period
will be adjusted to the 2011-2015 period. A donor roundtable will also be organized to mobilize
the funds needed to finance the plan to update the PRSP. Finally, the Government will prepare a
report on PRSP implementation covering 2010 and 2011. This report will be evaluated by the
IDA and the IMF as part of the HIPC Initiative completion point and multilateral debt relief
process.

IV. Monitoring and assessment

The Ministry of Economy and Finance will be responsible for implementing all programs
concluded with the World Bank. Daily monitoring and assessment of the program will be the
responsibility of the Strategic Committee of the Secretariat of the Economic and Financial
Program Monitoring Technical Unit.
I hope that the Government’s policies and commitments will impart much greater impetus to the
resumption of financial cooperation with the World Bank and all other multilateral and bilateral
partners, and in so doing enable it to benefit from the 2011 Post-Conflict Reconstruction and
Recovery Grant (PCRRG) general budget support grant.
