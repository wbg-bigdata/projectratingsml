                                         I. INTRODUCTION


1.       The proposed Fourth Programmatic Poverty Reduction Support Operation (PRSO IV)
i s the last in this series o f four budgetary support operations for Georgia. The PRSO series
has been        the cornerstone       o f the Georgia       Country Partnership  Strategy   (CPS)
(September2005).       PRSO IV, in the amount o f US$22.7 million equivalent supports the
Government's medium term reform program, designed within the framework o f the Poverty
Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP Update o f August 2006 or EDPRP Update as it i s known in
Georgia).   '
2.        The objectives o f Georgia's medium-term reform program include achieving and
sustaining rapid economic growth, halving both overall and extreme poverty by 50 percent or
more by 2015, and re-orienting the role o f the state and its relationship to the private sector in
order to achieve these outcomes. The reform program also reflects important features o f
Georgia's Action Plan under the European Neighborhood Policy (ENP).

3.        Within the wide range of reforms being implemented by the Government, the PRSO
program focuses on key governance, growth and poverty reduction areas in which the World
Bank Group has a comparative advantage and complements the work o f other development
partners.      For operational purposes, the program is organized into four pillars: (i)
strengthening public sector accountability, efficiency, and transparency; (ii)improving
electricity and gas sector services; (iii)improving the environment for private sector
development; and (iv) improving social protection, education and health care services. In
addition, the PRSO programmonitors the Millennium DevelopmentGoals (MDGs).

4.       The proposed PRSO IV i s designed to build upon the reforms supported under
PRSO 1-111 and timed to maintain the direction o f reforms inthe context o f a more rapid than
expected pace o f implementation in some policy areas. All but one expected action o f PRSO
IV have been met, and there are several areas in which reforms have exceeded original
program expectations (see Table 1).

5.       The Georgian Government has maintained a record o f strong growth and
macroeconomic stability since early 2004, which it has achieved through an adequate mix o f
sound fiscal and monetary policies and implementation of in-depthstructural reforms. The
IMF's Sixth and final Review of the PRGF program in August 2007 and the report from the
Staff Visit o f March 2008 provide supporting analysis. These achievements notwithstanding,
an equally positive trend in income poverty reduction in Georgia has not yet taken hold.
Furthermore, over the last two years Georgia has faced several external shocks. These have

included especially sharp energy price increases, with the price o f imported gas rising from
US$65 in 2005, to $110 in 2006 and, further to US$235 per 1000 c/m from January o f 2007.
Although the rise in import prices o f gas in 2007 was more limited by less expensive gas
imports from other sources, consumers were faced with large gas tariff increases inMay 2007.
The Government put inplace an electricity lifeline tariff system to protect vulnerable groups,
as well as a gas subsidy for the two last winters. The negative impact o f these shocks on the
extreme poor has also been reduced by the timely introduction o f a well targeted social
assistance program, which was designed as part o f the PRSO I,introduced with the support o f
PRSO IT,  and extended its coverage under PRSO I11and IV. The Government also provided
one time additional financial support to all pensioners and other vulnerable groups in the last
quarter o f 2007 to ease the impact o f the higher electricity and gas prices. Monthly pension
was increased from GEL 38 to GEL 55 starting from December 1,2007 and further to GEL 76
inMarch 2008.

6.      At the beginning o f the PRSO series, the then new administration faced enormous
challenges, and the primary objective o f the PRSO series was to support selected reforms
necessary to pull Georgia out o f a failing state scenario and to mitigate the risks o f derailment
in of the reform process. As shown in Annex 111, and highlighted in what follows, results
achieved under the PRSO program have been substantial and have exceeded expectations in
several areas.

7.      Table 1 presents the prior actions (triggers) implementedunder the PRSO program to
date, and a summary o f progress with respect to the prior actions o f PRSO IV. As shown in
the last column of Table 1, all expected major conditions of PRSO IV have beenmet with the
exception o f the enactment o f the draft law on the Chamber of Control.             Although the
Executive sent the draft law to the Parliament in late Fall 2007, with Presidential elections
having been brought forward to January 2008, the heavy workload o f Parliament due to
discussion of a broad package o f financial sector legislation submitted for consideration in late
January 2008, and the fact that the legislature i s no longer in session in anticipation of the
forthcoming (and also advanced) May 2008 Parliamentary elections, it has not been possible
to have the draft law on the Chamber o f Control considered and approved. However, as noted
inthe Letter of Development Policy, the Government is determined to work on the enactment
o fthe draft law as soon as the newly elected Parliament i s able to take up this issue.

8.      Public Sector Accountability,Efficiency, and Transparency (Pillar I;see Section
V-A). Georgia has made substantial progress in curbing corruption, improvingtransparency
and accountability in the public sector. As shown in Figure 1, Business Environment and
EnterprisePerformance Survey (BEEPS) 2005 reports an impressive turnaround from 2002 to
2005, which i s echoed by other indicators on corruption by international think tanks. Notably,
a opinion poll in 2006 reported that corruption was no longer among the five top concerns o f
Georgians. At the same time, the same poll reported that Georgians considered improvements
inthe provision of some basic public goods and services, such as roads and electricity, to be
among the major successes o f the Government. Furthermore, Georgia i s in a much stronger
macro financial position today owing in part to public finance reforms and anti-corruption
reforms undertaken over the past several years.



                                                 2

9.      Reforms supported by the PRSO program have contributed to some o f these positive
results. Annex I11 summarizes program outcomes to date based on monitorable indicators,
which in turn draw on baseline data from the periodjust before PRSO Ibegan. The progress
i s measured under PRSO I,PRSO 11, and PRSO I11and then assessed at the end o f the PRSO
program with PRSO IV.

10.     Public sector reforms under Pillar Ihave exceeded program expectations in four areas
including: (i)public expenditure policy reform; (ii) finance accounting reform; (iii)
                                                       public
intergovernmental financial relations reform; and (iv) the implementation o f the National
Anti-Corruption Strategy.

11.     (i)The 2007 budget coverage of sectoral strategy submissions by the ministries
improved significantly compared with the previous year. At the beginning o f the PRSO
program, this was envisaged for a small number o f pilot ministries and with the expectation to
expand gradually over time. By the time o f PRSO 111, the coverage had expanded to all
spending units, and there has been large improvement in the quality o f these submissions for
the 2008 budget preparation. DuringPRSO IV the quality of sectoral strategies continued to
improve gradually; and the Treasury Single Account continued to provide accurate financial
information on budget execution. Taxes, fees, and other payments (as well as state budget
expenditures by the state) to the state can be carried out at commercial banks which register
these receipts in accounts which are linked on real time to the Treasury Single Account
providing real time information on state revenues and expenditures.

12.     (ii)The initiation of the Accounting Reform Strategy covered a whole range of
reforms in accounting and reporting standards that consolidated several PRSO policy
measures which were envisaged over the entire three year program. As part o f this strategy,
measures were taken in introducing a GFS 2001 budget classification based on functional
classification for the 2007 and economic classification for the 2008 budget; a process that was
not foreseen until 2009. Similarly, the pace o f adopting treasury rules and procedures in cash
planning, the real time settlement system linked to the National Bank o f Georgia, the reporting
standards and producing consolidated cash and commitment reports for the central
government, Tbilisi and four other large cities, are all achievements that were beyond
expectations in such short period o f time.

13.     (iii)Reforms inthe area of local governments originally envisioned inthe PRSO
program aimed at clarifying the rules for transfers from the central government by the end o f
the program and some consolidation of local governments could start at the end of the PRSO
program. The law clarifying the budgetary rules and process for local self-government units
was prepared under PRSO I1and enacted under PRSO 111. The enacted law on budgets o f
local self-government units i s being implementedunder PRSO IV. Furthermore, the Georgian
authorities implemented a major inter-governmental relations reform by consolidating the
entire structure o f local governments from 1100local units to only 72. This achievement also
involved the adoption o f the law on Local Self Government Budget which introduced new
rules on intergovernmental fiscal relations to ensure a better mechanism for the allocation o f
tax revenues and size o f transfers between the various levels o f government. This will have
significant impact on the provision o fpublic services by the local governments.


                                               3

14.     (iv) Implementation of the National Anti Corruption Strategy has also been among the
reforms where the Government has succeeded in organizing its efforts swiftly and effectively.
A report on the implementation of the strategy is preparedon a regular (six month) basis to
closely monitor and evaluate progress, with findings and recommendations which are then
reflected to revise and improve the strategy. The report has been used as a monitoring tool to
keep track o f which government entity is responsible for which actions and the timetable on
specific anti-corruption areas.

15.     At the same time, other areas, such as civil service reform didnot proceed as originally
expected. At the start of the PRSO program, there was a view that a centralized civil service
reform would be implemented during the program. The first step was to take stock o f rules,
provisions, staff, and their salaries-and   this was done under PRSO I.However, with a deep
redefinition o f the functions o f the state, the restructuring o f civil service continued to be
pursued in a decentralized approach.         The common elements have been to restructure
government units as per their newly defined functions, re-staffing units under new salaries,
and rules for hiring and firing. New staff are hired provided they pass an aptitude test and
after re-training, a skills test.

16.     These areas are expected to improve gradually over time through continuous efforts to
develop further technical and managerial capacity in public institutions. The World Bank's
public sector reform project, together with extensive technical assistance from development
partners, has supported the PRSO IV reforms.

17.     Energy and Gas Sectors Reform (Pillar 11; see Section V-B). The main objective o f
these reforms has beento move the energy sector towards efficiency and sustainability. At the
start o f the PRSO series, Georgian consumers did not receive electricity and natural gas at
acceptable service levels.       Owing to non-payment, low tariffs, energy theft and pervasive
corruption in the sector, in 2003 the national average o f electricity service per household per
day was 7 hours (collection rates were under 40 percent). This meant that the capital city o f
Tbilisi had some electricity some ofthe time, and blackoutswere frequent. Outside of Tbilisi,
service was even less reliable and collection rates were under 35 percent.

18.     Overall, the reforms in the electricity and gas sectors have surpassed what was
originally envisioned in the program which aimed at reaching a collection rate o f 65 percent
and gradually improve service and reduce blackouts. Today, nearly all paying costumers have
24 hours per day/7 days per week electricity service; collection rates are above 90 percent; and
blackouts are infrequent. Prudent investmentsover the last several years as well as significant
improvement in the management o f electricity units were central to the turn around o f the
sector. In addition the Government implemented large tariff increases in 2006, which was a
critical step on the road to a financially sustainable energy sector, while well targeted
electricity and gas subsidieshelpedprotect the most vulnerable.

19.     The PRSO supported reforms have contributed substantially to this impressive
turnaround.     Privatization o f electricity sector assets was completed more rapidly than
originally envisioned. Domestic hydro-generation has substantially increased as a result o f
rehabilitating hydro power stations, and constructing additional thermal power station.
Energy prices in Georgia have reached levels comparable to those in international markets.
                                                 4

Georgia has diversified its sources o f gas supply. Commercial debt has declined and with the
completion o fthe privatizationprogram, the legacy o f debt has been removed from the sector.

20.     The private sector i s now the main player in producing and distributing energy
services, while the public sector needs to focus on developing its ability to regulate the energy
sector. PRSO IV supported further progress towards the efficiency and sustainability o f the
energy and gas sectors, and supports developing an effective regulatory capacity.

21.     Improving the Environment for Private Sector Development (Pillar 111; see
Section V-C). In 2007, Georgia ranked among the top 20 countries in the Bank's Doing
Business Report o f 2008, moving from 37`h place to lSth place out o f 178 countries. The
authorities are determined to implement reforms that will continue to improve the business
environment. Overall the reforms o f Pillar I11are on track; all triggers have been met and in
some dimensions program expectations have been exceeded. PRSO IV supports reforms
aimed at improving revenue collection administration by the newly established Revenue
Service Department within the Ministry o f Finance. The emphasis i s on developing a culture
o f voluntary compliance as the underpinning of the government reform agenda in tax and
customs administration.    In addition, PRSO IV policy measures support follow up on the
implementation o f the new customs code, and the strengthening o f the Government reform
agenda on inspection, permits, certification, accreditation and standardization.

22.     Improving Social Protection, Education and Health Care Services (Pillar IV; see
Section V-D). The proposed PRSO IV supports the Government's reforms o f the social
protection system and education and health care services.             In social protection, the
Government's main objective i s to provide adequate support to the neediest population. To
this end, the Government first cleared arrears inthe pension system and increased the level of
pensions (under PRSO I);    and then introduced in the Fall o f 2006 a well-designed targeted
social assistanceprogram (PRSO II), they subsequentlyexpanded (PRSO I11and PRSO
                                        which
IV). While Georgia had several social assistanceprograms, these have been based mainly on
status and social category (such as internally displaced people, veterans, the disabled, orphans,
and so on). Incontrast, the poverty benefit program is targeted to the extreme poor, measured
by household surveys utilizing consumption data.

23.     The development of Georgia's new targeted social assistanceprogramhas includedthe
following preparatory activities: (i)adoption o f an agreed poverty measurement methodology
(supported by the Bank's 2006 Programmatic Poverty Assessment); (ii)      designo fthe targeting
formula; (iii)preparation and issuance o f basic documents including an operational manual
for benefit administration; (iv) establishment o f the Employment and Social Services Agency,
which administers the program; (v) recruitment and training o f the Agency's staff; (vi)
computerization o f the databases o f the applications and files o f beneficiaries at the district
level; and (vii) implementation o f a financial transfer mechanism. Under PRSO IV, the
Georgian authorities, supported by PHRD resources, have initiated a detailed assessment o f
the targeted poverty benefit program and its sub-components.

24.     Education Reform.       The Government has made significant progress towards its
objective o f improving education system efficiency, transparency, access and quality. This
has included adoption o f transparent university entry examinations; implementation o f
                                                5

measures to participate in the current Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study
(TIMSS) and Progress for International Reading Literacy Study (PIRLS); adoption of new
laws and regulations which provide all Georgian schools with a high degree o f autonomy;
introduction o f a per student financing mechanism; design o f accreditation mechanisms; and
implementation o f quality control and financial accountability instruments. DuringPRSO IVY
the focus has been on improving education finance, developing quality enhancement
mechanisms, and modernizing the management infrastructure inthe sector. These reforms are
also supported by the Bank's investment lending operation in education, and technical
assistance i s being provided by development partners.

25.     Health Care Reform. The health care sector faces significant challenges. First, as in
other transition economies, Georgia inherited a sector with a physical and staffing
overcapacity which it has been unable to maintain properly. Second, Georgia has the lowest
health service utilization in the Europe and Central Asia Region, with less than 2 outpatient
visits per capita and less than 5 inpatient visits per 100 people. Out-of-pocket expenditures
are required to receive care, and therefore 73 percent o f the chronically ill cite lack o f
resources as one o f the main reasons for not utilizing health services.

26.     To address the most urgent needs, the Georgian authorities defined a Basic Benefit
Package o f health care services with universal coverage for all o f the population and
introduced a supplemental package o f health care services for the poor. In parallel, the
Government increased budgetary allocations to the health care sector inthe MTEF 2007-2010.
It also created a Presidential Commission for Health Care Reform which is an ambitious effort
to align better the number and capacity o f hospitals and primary health care facilities with
demand patterns, and engage the private sector actively in modernizing and operating health
infrastructure. PRSO IV has focused on supporting implementation o f a primary health care
(PHC) master plan with special emphasis on the monitoring and evaluation o f early results.
The PHC reform implementation has been expended to Samegrelo, Guria, and Lentekhi
regions, and Tbilisi, where a total o f 30 primary health clinics have been rehabilitated and
equipped with physicians and trained nurses. While continuing with the human capacity
building activities, the Government has privatized the ownership o f the PHC clinics under a
framework that aims at ensuring access to clinics for all Georgians.

27.     Addressing the Importance of Implementation. A critical factor in the success o f
any reform program i s that its design pays adequate attention to the government's
implementation capacity. Prior to 2004, although Georgia attempted to undertake a number o f
structural reforms, actual implementation was poor and underminedby widespread corruption,
poor governance, weak public finances, and extremely limited institutional capacity.        In
supporting the far-reaching reforms following the Rose Revolution, the current PRSO
program has been sequenced to first put in place the strategies and legal frameworks in
priority reform areas and then to focus increasingly on implementation o f the strategies and
action plans.    Building capacity to support implementation has thus become increasingly
important as the PRSO series has progressed. The design o f the PRSO has focused on those
areas in which Bank operations and the activities o f other donors have been able to most
effectively    support    capacity-building,    in parallel      with   the  reform    efforts.

efficient and transparent enforcement o f the law, modernizing the judiciary, and further
integrating with the world markets.                   Assuming continued prudent macroeconomic
management and sustained implementation o f structural reforms, Georgia's development and
poverty reduction prospects are positive.

                                 11.   COUNTRY CONTEXT

A.       Political Background

31.      The political environment is evolving. Georgia enjoyed a relatively stable period
during the first four years following the Rose Revolution in November 2003.                The
Government has beenundertakingsignificant economic reforms and anti-corruption measures
widely supported by the population. At the same time, tensions beganto emerge due on the
one hand to the desires o f some stakeholders for greater consultation and involvement in
policy decisions, and the appearance o f heavy-handedness by the Government in applying the
rule o f law on the other hand. These tensions were further exacerbated inlate 2007 by the use
o f force against opposition demonstrations, the closure o f a TV station, and a brief state o f
emergency.

32.      To ease the situation, Presidential and Parliamentary elections were advanced in order
to gauge the extent o f the Government's mandate to continue with its program. President
Saakashvili won a second term in the January 2008 elections. Further demonstrations took
place and then halted as the opposition focuses on the upcoming Parliamentary elections in
late May 2008. Because o f these events, the second term will likely see more diverse
participation in the Parliament and hence a greater need for negotiations/consensus-
building/coalitions. This could slow the very rapid reform momentum o f the first term, but
would be a natural and welcome part o f the political development process.

33.      The geo-political environment remains a challenge for the Georgian authorities. The
conflicts related to regions o f Abkhazia and South Ossetia remain, but the Government i s
committed to resolving them in a peaceful manner. Georgia i s also pursuing NATO
membership over the next several years.

34.      Georgia has received strong financial support from the international community,
exceeding full delivery on commitments made during the June 2004 Consultative Group in
Brussels. In addition, disbursements o f grants from the Millennium Challenge Corporation
(MCC) started in 2006 and are expected to reach US$200 million over the three years o f
2007-09. At the same time, Georgia's relations with the EU have been strengthened by the
ENPAction PlansignedinNovember 2006.

35.      Duringthe secondhalfof2006 the authorities accelerated the implementationofmajor
political and institutional reforms. As describedinthe Government's PRSP Update o f August
2006, with the local elections of October 2006 the Government implementedmajor territorial,
administrative, and budgetary decentralization reforms. This included consolidating about
1,100 very small municipalities into 72 local self-government units. During 2007, the
budgetary process o f local self-government units was initiated as per the new budget law o f
self-government and has remained on track through the preparation of 2008 budget.

36.      Despite the impressive pace and breadth o f reform, important challenges remain.
During the Governance and Anti-corruption (GAC) consultations in January 2007,
representatives o f the civil society stressed that the Government needs to: (i)improve its
consultations and communications with the public on the reform strategy; (ii)focus on
                                                16

strengthening the rule o f law and avoid perceptions o f selective use o f enforcement
mechanisms; and (iii)adopt a modern approach to revenue collection (i.e., more balance
between incentives for compliance and enforcement).


B.      EconomicBackground

37.     During much of the first decade of transition after the break-up of the former Soviet
Union, Georgia was one o f the poorest performing economies in the Europe and Central Asia
region.   During the mid-to-late 1990s, there were some periods of optimism-several
structural reforms were implementedand there was a short period o f macroeconomic stability.
While important laws, regulations, and institutional reforms were approved on paper, actual
implementation was poor.         By 2003, Georgia had an increasingly weak governance
framework, corruption was rampant, selective reform efforts were not being sustained, and the
country was drifting rapidly toward `failed state' status.

38.     Not surprisingly, during 1989-2003 Georgia also had one of the lowest tax revenues to
GDP ratios among the transition economies. Many Georgians hadto pay government officials
but these payments did not reach the Treasury, and hence the state did not have the resources
to provide basic public goods and services. This situation reflectedwidespread corruption and
significant weaknesses in the rule o f law. Moreover, the deterioration in living standards was
further exacerbated by non-payment o f pensions and wages, a sharp deterioration in public
services (in particular electricity), and the growing impact o f corruption on everyday life.
These conditions created a fertile environment for the peaceful Rose Revolution in late 2003.


39.     Upon taking office in January 2004, the new Government responded decisively to its
mandate to curb corruption and improve Georgia's development prospects. First, its anti-
corruption efforts and the agenda to establish strong mechanisms o f accountability and
transparency cut across all areas o f government. Second, the reform strategy has aimed at
developing a dynamic and competitive private sector as the main engine o f growth, with the
state playing a supportive role by providing basic public goods and services. And third, the
Government has undertaken complementary reforms to ensure that the population benefits
from growth through improvements in education and health care delivery and by developing
an effectively targeted social safety net to protect the extreme poor. These institutional and
structural reforms are being supported by prudentmacroeconomic policies.


C.      RecentEconomic Developments

40.     Macroeconomic management in Georgia continues to show solid performance and the
ability to mitigate internal and external shocks. The political events o f late 2007, continued
strong capital inflows, increases in energy and food prices, and the continued loss o f part o f
Georgia's traditional export market resulted in higher fiscal and external current account
balances and inflation in 2007 than were earlier anticipated.        As a result of strong tax
performance, the fiscal deficit on a cash basis was 2.6 percent o f GDP during 2006 and moved
into a surplus during the first half o f 2007. However, a spike in expenditures late inthe year
resulted in a deficit o f 4.3 percent o f GDP for 2007. The authorities intend to move to a


                                                17

balanced budget in the medium term. New legislation adopted in 2008 has introduced a
rule-based approach to balancing the budgetwhich will help strengthen fiscal discipline.

41.      The external current account deficit which had widened in 2006 to 13.8 percent of
GDP i s estimated to have reached 18 percent in 2007. This deficit has been fueled by large
inflows o f foreign direct investment (FDI) which reached 15.8 percent o f GDP in 2006.
Notwithstanding the political uncertainties in the last quarter o f 2007 and its negative impact
on capital inflows, FDIinflows remained highat 16 percent o f GDP for the year as a whole.

42.      Core inflation has been maintained in single digits in 2004-2007. However, headline
inflation was at single digits through the first half o f 2007, but increased to 11 percent by end
o f 2007 as inflationary pressures from large capital inflows and rapid expansion o f credit to
the private sector were exacerbated by rising energy and food prices and rapid money growth
in the second half of 2007. Moreover, sharp increases inthe state regulated prices of natural
gas (by 32 percent, due to the doubling o f the price for gas supplied from Russia), garbage
collection fees (by 137 percent), water rates (by 80 percent), and public transport tariffs (by
100 percent) put additional pressure on the overall price level in 2007. The authorities intend
to tighten fiscal and monetary policies and moveto inflation targetinginthe mediumterm.

43.      The record of growth in Georgia in recent years has been impressive. As a result o f
macroeconomic stability and substantial structural reforms, real GDP growth was 9.6 percent
in2005, 9.4 percent in2006, and is estimated at 12.4 percent in2007. This rapid growth has
been led by construction, financial intermediation, communications and, more recently,
manufacturing.     Average growth in manufacturing has been robust at 14.3 percent while
growth o f agriculture has been uneven due both to droughts and floods and the loss o f some
traditional export channels.

44.      Manufacturing growth picked up in 2005 and 2006 and despitethe trade restrictions on
exports to Russia, growth and diversification o f exports have been strong. Although the
economy showed a remarkable ability to expand export access to other markets, the
restrictions have taken their toll on rural exporters in 2006 and 2007. Exports to markets
other than Russia and Turkmenistan, while starting from a small base, have exhibited growth
in excess of 30 percent since the second quarter of 2006. Over the medium-term, export
capacity will improve gradually as Georgia upgrades the skills o f its labor force and its
infrastructure for long distance trade, and improves its institutional framework for growth and
investment.

45.      Strong capital inflows, including foreign direct investment (FDI), have also played a
significant role in supporting growth in Georgia in recent years. The external current account
deficit widened in 2006 to 13.8 percent o f GDP and i s estimated to have expanded further to
18 percent in 2007, resulting from higher cost o f imported food and energy combined with
continued increase in consumer imports. With a much improved business environment, FDI
flows were even larger than anticipated and fully financed the external current account deficit,
in addition to the accumulation o f international reserves in 2006. FDI flows have remained
strong during 2007 and are expected to be large enoughto finance the external current account
deficit and the accumulation o f international reserves.

                                                 18

46.     As a result of tax reform and deregulation (which reduced the informal economy), and
particularly measures to curb corruption, tax revenue collection has increased from 14.1
percent of GDP in 2003 to 21.9 percent in 2006 and is estimated to have increased further to
25.3 percent in 2007. This major achievement allowed the Government to start addressing
critical social and infrastructure needs. Social spending has increased by about 5 percent
points of GDP to date and now represents more than one-third o f the budget.               Social
assistance policy has been targetedto assist the most vulnerable insociety.

47.     First, pension arrears were eliminated and pensions have been tripled since 2003.
Second, the Government introduced the targeted social assistance program-a          cash-benefit
program targeting the extreme poor-in        AugustBeptember 2006. Third, the Government
introduced the Basic Benefit Package for health care, which includes a supplemental package
for the poor. Fourth, inorder to reduce the negative impact o f energy price increases resulting
from external shocks, temporary budget subsidies were provided to vulnerable consumers.
The benefits o f these measures are well acknowledged by the population given the negative
impact o f the external shocks.

48.     In addition, the Government has implemented large strategic investments in the
transport and electricity sectors which inturn have contributedto a strong growth performance
by the private sector. Public capital expenditures increased from 2.4 percent o f GDP in2004
to 5.5 percent in 2006 and are expected to have increased further to 7.0 percent in 2007.
External shocks, particularly the large energy and food price increase, had a significant impact
on the economy and on the budget. The significant improvementintax revenue collection led
to the introduction o f four budget amendments during 2007 with large allocation to social
sectors particularly in light of the political events towards the end o f the year. However, the
under execution o f the plannedstate appropriations one month before year end is estimated to
have resulted in a much lower overall fiscal deficit o f 4.3 percent o f GDP than earlier
anticipated.

49.     Prudent macroeconomic policies and effective anti-corruption measures have
increased confidence on the part o f businesses and consumers. Simplified procedures for
registering a business, reforms in customs administration and the border police, and more
business-friendly tax and labor regulations have significantly improved the business
environment. Georgia was the top reformer in 2006 according to the Bank's Doing Business
survey, vaulting from 112th place in 2005 to 37th place in 2006, an unprecedented one year
change among the 175 countries inthe sample. Georgia's rank improved further to 18thplace
in2007, whichis second only to Estonia (17thplace) inthe ECA region.

50.     The Government's commitment to pursuea stabilization reform agenda, along with its
strong macroeconomic performance, has increased confidence on the part o f businesses and
consumers. As a result, monetization inthe economy is improving as reflected by increases of
M3 to GDP from about 12 percent of GDP in 2003 to 19.5 percent in 2006 and to an
estimated 21 percent by end March 2007. At the same time, albeit from very low levels, there
has been a rapid expansion of credit from 8.6 percent o f GDP in 2003 to 20 percent in 2006
and to an estimated 24.3 percent by end 2007. This expansion of credit is beingaccompanied
by a rapid expansion of construction and by increases in asset prices. These monetary and

                                                 19

financial developments pose important challengesto Georgia's supervisory capacity and to the
macroeconomic managementof credit boom-cum-assetprice increases.

51.      Going forward, key reforms in the banking sector include: (i)upgrading banking
supervisory policy; (ii)strengthening the implementation of Base1 Core Principles for
Effective Banking Supervision, particularly the requirements for Fit and Proper Criteria for
bank administrators and large shareholders; (iii)strengthening banking sector transparency;
and (iv) continuing the closing and/or merging of commercial banks that do not comply with
prudentialregulations. The developments and challenges inmonetary policy and the banking
sector were being addressed primarily by Georgia's PRGF program and now with the post-
program surveillance of the IMF. These reforms are central to reducing vulnerabilities to the
changing structure of external capital flows.      The Parliament, before adjourning for the
upcoming elections passed revisions to 26 laws dealing with the financial sector, intendedto
make Georgia an international financial center and to consolidate responsibility for
supervision of financial institutions into one autonomous Financial Supervision Agency
(FSA).

52.      Debt burden indicators continue to improve. The stock of public external debt was
substantially reduced from 26.6 percent of GDP in 2005 to 21.7 percent in 2006, following a
second round of Paris Club rescheduling in mid-2004 and the discipline of refraining from
contracting non-concessional debt over the last three years. The Georgian authorities have
completed their negotiations with all of the non-Paris Club members, excluding Kazakhstan,
for which negotiations are ongoing on US$27.7 million outstanding debt. The remaining
arrears on Turkmenistan debt have been reduced from an end-2005 amount of US$101.0
million to US$4.65 million by the end of 2007. External debt service to exports of goods and
serviceshas fallen from 7.2 percent in2005 to 6.7 percent in2006.

53.      With Georgia's prudent macroeconomic policies over the last three years and with
debt indicators improving, the sovereign debt was rated at B+ (outlook positive) by
Standard&Poors in 2007, and BB- (outlook stable) by Fitchin 2008. Georgia issued its first
sovereign bond in April 2008 inthe amount of US$500 million with its proceeds expectedto
be placed in two new funds-the        Stable Development Fund and the Future Generations
Fund-established to guard the economy against unexpected shocks in the case of the first
fund, and for long term financing in the case of the second. In March 2008 Parliament
approved a new set of laws to modernize its financial sector including: (i)   establishing a single
Financial Supervisory Authority (FSA) to supervise the banking and non-banking sectors
(capital markets and insurance); (ii)strengtheningthe independence of the Central Bank with
first steps towards inflation targeting, and (iii)creating the above mentioned two funds, one
for stabilization and the other for long tem financing.       In addition, the new legislation
facilitates the development o f an international financial center to attract foreign funding; and
this will require close monitoring.

54.      Overall, macroeconomic management in Georgia in recent years has beeneffective in
safeguarding stability and growth and has shown an impressive ability to mitigate shocks.
Furthermore, the quality of public finances is improving in terms of its transparency, and
accountability, and also in terms of its being supportive of national priorities. Specifically,
public expenditures are supportive of growth, and of the development of a fiscally sustainable
                                              20

     social safety net, and are also starting to address the large basic infrastructure needs. As a
     result, the medium-termprospects are positive.

     57.       Georgia faces a number of significant but manageable macroeconomic risks and
     vulnerabilities. First, substantial capital inflows have fueled domestic demand and generated
                                                         21

a large and growing external current account deficit, as well as pressures for inflation and
exchange rate appreciation.      The authorities have responded by intervening heavily in the
foreign exchange market to dampen appreciation o f the Lari and they have also sterilized part
o f the inflows through domestic open market operations. While the policy response thus far
has been sound, the large and widening external current account deficit does represent a risk
o fthe need for adjustment to any shortfall incapital inflows inthe medium term.

58.      Factors that could generate such a shortfall include a fall in oil prices (since much o f
the inflows are from energy-rich countries), negative developments inreal estate markets, or a
deterioration in political or regional stability. The policy response to any reversal in capital
inflows would likely require fiscal adjustment, which would be particularly difficult to
achieve during an election year and with the continued significant social and infrastructure
expenditure needs. These risks are manageable and they are mitigated by the Government's
strong commitment to safeguarding growth and stability and its demonstrated track record o f
pursuing a sound mix o f macroeconomic policies.

59.      The above medium term scenario i s based on continued prudent macroeconomic
policies and further progress with structural reforms in the public sector, including the
strengthening o f state institutions, improved governance, the revitalization of public
infrastructure, maintenance o f stability in energy supplies, and further enhancement o f the
environment for private sector development. Services are expected to continue their rapid
expansion, as the economy develops its financial services, wholesale and retail sectors, and
tourism-driven services including hotels and restaurants. Construction and manufacturing are
both expected to contribute to growth in the industrial sector. Volatility in the agriculture
sector i s likely to persist, along with the process o f labor-shedding. Although sustained high
growth rates are expected to generate employment and reduce poverty inthe medium to long
term, a complementary and effective social safety net will also be required. The introduction
o f the poverty benefit targeting the extreme poor has been a watershed step inthis direction.

60.      Inflation (at 8.8 percent at end December 2006) was maintained insingle digit territory
through the first half o f 2007, but crept into double digits late in 2007 (11 percent, end-of-
period for 2007) largely due to rapidly rising energy and food prices, as well as inflationary
pressures related to large capital inflows. While inflation may tend to remain high during
2008, it i s expected to moderate to single digit levels during 2009-11 as the government
moves to a program o f inflation targeting.

61.      The monetary program projects a continued increase in money demand of around 15
percent and targets a growth o f reserve money consistent with the objective o f a single-digit
inflation rate. However, inflationary pressures, including those already resulting from higher
oil and energy prices and a higher than expected impact on inflation by monetary aggregates,
need to be kept in check. Indeed, the Government is now facing the challenge o f prudently
managing privatization proceeds, inflows from the MCC, and increased fiscal revenues from
oil pipeline transit fees.

62.      A continued strong fiscal policy is essential for 2008.        The enhanced legal and
institutional framework for tax and customs administration resulting from the merger o f tax,
customs and financial police to a single revenue administration, together with the new tax
                                                 22

amendments to reduce income tax rate from 25 to 15 percent are expected to further boost tax
and customs revenues over the medium term. Specifically, total revenues and grants are
expected to increase to 28.5 percent o f GDP in 2008 and stabilize around 26.5 percent by
2011.    The Government is also progressing towards better fiscal planning through the
accelerated implementation o f a Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF) with
improved revenue forecasts. This macro outlook is consistent with the government BDD and
MTEFcovering the 2008-2011period.

63.     Large FDI flows are expected to continue dominate developments on the external
current account. The external current account deficit o f 18.0 percent o f GDP in 2007 is
expected to narrow to 16.6 percent o f GDP in 2008. Export capacity i s expected to grow
further in the medium term and is expected to benefit from the new trade agreement with
Turkey (whose share of Georgia's exports is close to 15 percent). Georgia remains vulnerable
to changes in energy prices although this vulnerability has been somewhat mitigated by the
agreements Georgia has with Azerbaijan and Iran. Over the medium term, however, imports
are likely to continue to be dominated by Georgia's energy needs for some time to come,
while other imports are likely to increase as businesses update their capital investments and as
imports o f consumer goods grow together with the purchasing power o f consumers. While
the reforms being implemented are expected to expand Georgia's export capacity, this is
likely to develop at a slower rate than the growth in imports. As a result, the external current
account deficit is projected to remain highat around 11.5-14 percent o f GDP during 2009-11.

64.     The external current account deficit is expected to continue to be financed largely by
inflows o f FDI, as well as by private capital flows and donor assistance. Recent estimates o f
FDIreflect the success of the Government's efforts to enhance the business environment and
there i s no indication that this has been affected by negative relations with Russia. The
political uncertainties o f late 2007 had a temporary impact on the size of the FDI in the last
quarter, but foreign inflows have remained strong in recent months and are expected to
continue at strong levels after the Parliamentary elections are over. Inresponse to the large
external current account deficit, the Georgian authorities have indicated that they are prepared
to tighten fiscal and monetary policies. In addition, the proceeds from the recently issued
US$500 million Eurobond will be placed intwo new funds-the          Future Generations Fundand
the Stable Development Fund-to          guard against unexpected shocks and for long term
financing.

65.     Given Georgia's projected external current account deficit and financing mix, the
stock o f external public debt i s projected to increase in 2008 with the government issuance o f
the $500 million bond, raising the debt from 16.7 percent of GDP in2007 to 18.5 percent in
2008 before starting to decline in the medium term. Debt service to exports is projected to
decline from 6.7 percent in 2006 to 5.5 percent in 2007 and to an average o f 3 percent by

A.      Povertydevelopmentssince the RoseRevolution

67.     Favorable economic performance since 2004 has increased incomes for many
Georgians, mainly in the middle and upper ranges o f the income distribution. Assessing the
impact o f strong economic growth and social spending on the lower range o f the income
distribution is work in progress.    According to the annual national Household Surveys,
poverty in Georgia has remained steady at about 30-31 percent since the Rose Revolution.
Preliminary analysis of the results o f a Living Standards Measurement Survey (LSMS)
conducted as a cross check in late Fall 2007 suggest a somewhat lower level o f poverty. A
full Poverty Assessment inearly FY09 will discuss the differences and suggest strategic policy
and investment options for poverty reduction efforts going forward. However, some
observations can be made at this time. The rapid increase in social spending (by 5 percentage
points o f GDP between 2003 and 2005), including the introduction o f the targeted social
assistance program in September 2006, has likely improved the situation o f the extreme poor.
Moreover, poverty appears to be more concentrated inrural areas and some secondary cities.

68.     Although a sustained trend toward lower income poverty has not yet been established,
important non income dimensions o f poverty have improved, including significantly improved
access for the poor to electricity, natural gas, safe water, health, and higher education.
Between 2003 and 2006, the share of poor households with access to a 24 hour supply of
electricity increased from 19 percent to 66 percent. Over the same period, the share of poor
households with access to natural gas supply increased from 12 percent to 26 percent. Also,
in 2006, the government introduced a supplemental benefit package of medical services for
the poor, with approximately 650,000 people or 15 percent o f the population covered by the
program in 2007. Finally, the introduction of a transparent university admission examination
in 2006 resulted in more students from outside the capital city of Tbilisi entering the higher
education system.

69.     A number o f factors have challenged efforts to reduce income poverty in Georgia.
First, during the first 13 years of transition, Georgia experienced a deep contraction in
economic activity, and for reasons discussed above, the country was drifting towards `failed
state' status by 2003. Second, the productivity-enhancing restructuring process since 2004
has been associated with labor shedding. Third, during the last four years, Georgia has been
hit by a number of domestic and external shocks that have adversely affected poor generally,
and the rural poor in particular. Looking to the future, as the restructured Georgia economy
experiences sustained higher growth, employment should expand and income poverty should
decline over time.

70.     The very difficult initial conditions at the end o f 2003, particularly inthe agricultural
and non-farming rural sectors (from which large numbers o f the poor draw their livelihood),
have been one o f the key structural factors limiting the response of poverty to the recent
acceleration o f economic growth. In particular, a large share o f the population has been
attached to low productivity activities, particularly inrural areas. The contraction of output in
Georgia by 69.5 percent during 1989-98 was one of the most severe among transition
economies, and was also accompanied by very high inflation and loss o f savings during
 1989-95. In response, a major share o f the labor force moved to rural areas in order to
survive. Agriculture became the "employer o f last resort," with the share o f employment inthe
sector more than doubling during 1992-2004.            Plot sizes shrank, leading to declining
productivity. At the end of 2007, some 52 percent o f the labor force was in the low-
productivity agricultural sector.

71.     A second structural factor contributing to poverty developments in Georgia is the deep
productivity-enhancing process o f economic restructuring that has taken place since 2004 and
its effects on labor market performance. As in other transition economies, this process has
been accompanied by labor shedding, which has affected employment. As i s often the case in
countries undergoing deep economic restructuring, while average earnings have increased, net
employment has declined during 2003-06.           As the economy has restructured from very
inefficient activities to more efficient ones, and the state has been reorganized and downsized,
job losses have exceeded job creation. Between 2003 and 2006, the absolute number o f
employed declined by 70,000 people and the number o f inactive increased by 149,000 people.
Over the medium term, it i s reasonable to expect that the expansion o f employment in the
private sector will offset the shedding o f labor, but strong safety net arrangements will
continue to be necessary for some time.

72.     Poverty in Georgia in recent years has also been affected by a number o f domestic and
external shocks, which have been mitigated by prudent policy action. The elimination o f
pension and wage arrears likely had a favorable impact on poverty in 2004. However, the
unusually severe floods and the impact of external shocks (including the deterioration in
economic relations with Russia) in2005 took a toll on rural areas.

73.     Looking to the future, it i s importantto recognize that Georgia i s putting in place the
conditions necessary for poverty to decline. First, the reforms implemented to date have
generated four years of sustained high growth, which has already improved average incomes
and should generate additional employment going forward.              Second, as discussed, the
Georgian authorities have introduced a social benefit program targeting the extreme poor.
                                                25

The targeted social assistance covers over 120,000 households and i s being expanded this year
as potential beneficiaries apply and qualify for the benefit. The authorities, in close
collaboration with Bank staff, have carried out a survey to evaluate the effectiveness o f this
program, with a view toward adjusting it as needed to mitigate extreme poverty. As a result,
the risk o f income poverty i s becoming more resilient to shocks.

(1-v) StrengtheningPublicAdministration

131.     Key issues. Inno other area does the legacy o f a failed state weigh as heavily as inthe
area o f public administration.        In Georgia, the structure of the executive branch was
ineffective, the mandates o f the individual government agencies were not clearly
distinguished, the number o f public employees was very large, salaries were inadequate and
wage arrears common.           These factors, coupled with the low skills o f civil servants,
contributedto corruption practices inpublic service.

132.     Shortly after coming to power, the new administration adopted a LawI5to introduce a
cabinet style o f government. The executive branch was reorganized and streamlined. At this
stage, the reform o f the state was driven by rightsizing and redefining the role o f the state and
was beingimplementedon a unit-by-unit basis.

133.     Other ministries also undertook significant reshuffling and internal restructuring, in
some cases more than once. Over time, the Georgian authorities have found several common
principles that are being applied on a case by case basis as the role o f the state i s re-defined
and results in curbing corruption and improving public service are requiredfrom restructuring
and re-staffing reforms. First, the Government has restructured and re-staffed public entities
where corruption was pervasive such as the traffic police. Secondly, re-staffing is being
carried out by selecting qualified candidates. Since corruption was pervasive inthe education
system it i s difficult to assess potential candidates based on traditional curriculum vitae.
Hence, aptitude and technical tests are applied to identify potential candidates. Thirdly, new
staff i s provided some basic training and those that perform at average or better levels are in
most cases provided term contracts. A fourth relatively common element i s to provide much
higher salaries and strict penalties for misconduct and corruption.

134.     At the start of the program, the Government and Bank staff envisioned a rapid
conclusion o f rightsizing and redefining the role o f the state. Defining and implementinga
public sector-wide civil service reform was part o f the approach o f civil service reform.

E.      Poverty and Social Impact Assessment

234.    The PRSO is designed to improve living standards in Georgia by (i)strengthening
public sector accountability, efficiency, and transparency; (ii)improving electricity and gas
sector services; (iii)improving the environment for private sector development; and (iv)
improving social protection, education, and health care services. The two areas with the
greatest potential immediate impact on living standards are reforms in the power sector and
the social protection system.

235.    To assess the impact o f reforms in the power sector and the social protection system,
especially on the poor, a Programmatic Poverty Assessment program i s being implemented in
parallelwith the PRSO series.

236.    It should be noted that maintaining macroeconomic stability as a core of PRSO
program policy actions has safeguarded the purchasing power o f households, particularly
those whose main source o f income i s wages. More important, the targeted social assistance
to the extreme poor introduced under PRSO I1and expanded under PRSO 111-IV has been the
most direct measure for mitigating any negative impacts o f reforms on the extreme poor.

237.    In energy, the PRSO supports Government initiatives to attain financial viability for
sector entities. Payment collections inpower distribution have increased, mostly as a result o f
improved service delivery and the willingness o f consumers to pay for a more stable
electricity supply. Cost recovery, full collections, and control o f electricity theft have been
necessary to achieve sustainable electricity supplies at a service level adequate for economic
development and improved living conditions. The 2003 PSIA concluded that households in
Tbilisi had a power consumption level close to their basic needs and that demand was
inelastic, thus suggesting that tariff increases such as the one in June 2006 would be
associated with significant welfare losses. Under this PRSO, as energy reforms are targeted to
areas outside o f Tbilisi, welfare losses are likely to occur among this population group in the
short term and possibly in the medium term. To mitigate the impact o f higher tariffs on the
poor, the Government has adopted a bloc tariff based on the level o f consumption, and i s
providing additional energy assistanceto vulnerable groups.

238.    The bloc tariff system has predefinedand increased tariff levels for low, middle, and
high levels of consumption. Tariffs rates applied outside of Tbilisi are approaching those
charged inTbilisi. Many residential consumers in Tbilisi have individual meters, but outside
o f Tbilisi most residential consumers are paying on the basis o f communal meters that meter
consumption o f up to 70 apartments or houses. The Government has decided that consumers
on communal meters will pay the lowest bloc price. A program is under way to replace many
communal meters by individual meters, inwhich case monthly meter readings will tell which
block tariff to apply. As mentioned in Section ILA, access to electricity by the poor improved
significantly from 19.1 percent in2003 to 66 percent o fthe poor in2006.

239.    The social protection system is undergoing a major reform with the objective o f
reducing the incidence o f extreme poverty. The Government introduced a landmark targeted
cash family poverty benefit in AugustBeptember 2006. The benefit targets the 11 percent o f
Georgian families (about 400,000 people) that were estimated to be under the extreme poverty
                                                 64

line in 2005. Families found eligible for the benefit (based on an assessment o f their social
and economic status) now receive a regular monthly cash benefit.The specific benefit amount
depends on a household's assessed income and has the aim o f topping up existing income to
the extreme poverty benchmark.

240.    To assess the impact of the reform o f the social protection system, two activities are
being undertaken.First, the Government has initiated an evaluation o f the poverty benefit in
2007 to asses the program's first year o f implementation.            Second, as part o f the
Programmatic Poverty Assessment program, the effectiveness o f the reforms i s being
assessed.


F.      RisksandRiskMitigation

241.    Georgia faces significant but manageable macroeconomic risks and vulnerabilities.
On the domestic front these include: (i)  budgetary pressures and economic effects related to
the recent external shocks; (ii)further acceleration inthe growthofcredit to the privatesector;
and (iii)a limited response o f domestic investment and net job creation to the reforms.
External risks and vulnerabilities include: (i)further sharp increases in energy prices; and (ii)
disruption in some traditional trade patterns and slow progress in trade and investment
diversification.  The continued implementation o f prudent macroeconomic policies and
structural reforms should render these risks and vulnerabilities manageable.

242.    At the start of the program there was the risk of a limited capacity to coordinate and
implement reforms. However, over the last three years the Government has significantly
improved its capacity to coordinate, manage and implement the wide range of reforms in
which the authorities are simultaneously engaged. Specifically, the Cabinet has become the
central policymaking entity whose decisions are implementedby corresponding line ministries
or by dedicated teams in the form o f Presidential Commissions empowered with legal and
executive authority.

243.    Despite this progress, the Government faces important challenges to the effective
implementation of new regulatory frameworks in a wide range o f public functions owing to
vast needs to retrain public employees, and to the challenges o f developing new administrative
structures, enforcing accountability, and improving the delivery o f public services.      The
design o f the proposed PRSO IV proposes reviews and assessments o f the reforms
implementedand their impact, and the definition and implementation of remedial measuresto
improve performance. These measures are aimed directly at addressing the challenge o f the
effective implementation and strong ownership o f the reforms. Clearly identified monitoring
indicators o f the expected results complement the self-evaluative and corrective measures
proposed inseveral areas inPRSO IV.

244.    Inaddition, it should be noted that the PRSO programhasbeendesignedto focus only
on the key strategic elements o f the Government's broad program within a programmatic
approach.     Meanwhile, the Government i s taking direct initiatives to address capacity
weaknesses. It i s recognized that developing a small and effective public sector will take
time. The IDA is actively supporting these initiatives-for       example, through the ongoing
Public Sector Financial Management Reform Support Project. More generally, the PRSO
                                               65

program has a strong focus on areas in which donor technical assistance i s ongoing. For
example, the area o f improving governance in public finances (specifically, the public

 safety net, improvingbasic education and health care provision. We introduced the targeted
 social assistance program in August o f 2006 and it covered nearly all the extreme poor in
 Georgia in2007. Public capital expenditures have increased to 8.6 percent of GDP and the
enhancedbudget envelope has allowed us to address basic infrastructure needs. At the same
time, public debt declined from 50percent of GDP in2004 to 25.5 percent in2007.

7.      The real GDP growth in2007 constituted 12.4%, If compared to the same period of
2006 the fastest growing sectors of the Georgian economy in 2007 were banking and
financial sector, mining and quarrying, transport and construction. in 2007 FDI increased
by 41.3% compared to the previous year and constituted USD 1,6 bln. In 2007 trade
turnover increasedby 39.9% and constituted USD6,457 mln.

8.      Our commitment to maintain macroeconomic stability and decisive implementation
o f structural reforms has increased confidence on the par?of businesses and consumers.
According to the Doing Business reports of the World Bank Group, Georgia has been
among the top ten reformers for three consecutive years since 2005. Georgia improved its
ranking from llZth place in 2005, to 37`h place in 2006, to 18`h place in 2007. Non-oil
foreign direct investment has increased from US$139.9 million in 2004 to US$884.5
million in 2006 and US$1 553.3 million in 2007. The sovereign credit risk was rated at
BB-by Fitchandat B+withpositiveoutlook by Standard&Poor's in2007

9.      We believe that the combination of bold structural reforms, continued reductions in
social vulnerability, facilitation of foreign investment, liberalization of trade, sound
macroeconomic management and robust growth constitute the cornerstone of Georgia's
sustainable development, These reforms, implemented in the context of the Government's
unconditional adherence to the principles of democracy, are at the core of the realization of

                                               2

  Georgia's paramount foreign policy objective, which is integration into the Euro-Atlantic
  structures.

  10.     This Letter of Development Policy summarizes the elements of our progress
  supportedby PRSO IV in completing the PRSO program. The key policy anchors of our
  reformagenda will continue beyond the elections in 2008, We anticipate that Georgiawill
  build on the achievements to date supported by the four PRSOs as we continue to move
  forward.

     Pillar 1: Strengtheningpublic sectoraccountability,efficiency, andtransparency

  11.     With PRSO IV we have laid down solid basis for the efficient management of
 publicfinances, and for transparencyandaccountabilityinthe area. Oneof the key anchors
 ofthese reformsis the Medium-termExpenditureFramework(MTEF). Our strategy is now
 supported by the MTEF process in planning and executing the budget. MTEF and the
 budgetdiscipline that it implies has been extendedto local-selfgovernmentunits which are
 to implement orderly and transparent planning and execution of their budgets. We are
 determined to progressfurther in respect of capital budgetingand will continue our efforts
 towardsfurther improvingthe effectivenesso fthe public finance management.

 12.     The use o f the Treasury Single Account today allows us to know in real time the
 cash and commitment positions in Oeorgia's public finances. This principle is being
 extended to local government units. The accounting reform strategy is advancing, and we
 have already implemented both, the functional and economic budget classification
 accordingto the GFSM-2001.

 13.     The StocktakingNoteon Civil Servicereviewingthe periodof 2004 to mid-2007 is
 ready for discussion at the Cabinet level, Monitoring and reporting of our anticorruption
 actionplanhas beenmainstreamed,This action plan allows us to apply our anticorruption
 efforts effectivelyandtransparently.

 14.     Draft legislation on the Chamber of Control is under consideration in the
Parliament. It is aimed at providing Georgiawith the new legal framework consistent with
INTOSAI principles for development o f a modern and independent supreme audit
institution,The executive branch works with the legislature to consider this legislation as
soonas possibleafterthe Parliamentaryelectionsin late May 2008.

 15.     Insum, the reformsimplementedunderPillar 1 ofPRSOIV haveexceeded manyof
the targetswe envisionedat the start of the program, with the exceptionofthe Chamber of

Presidentialand Parliamentaryelections.

                   Pillar2: Improvingelectricity and gas sector services

At the start of the PRSO program in 2004, blackouts inTbilisi were frequent and most of
the rest of the country was in the dark, The power sector did not provide service at

 reasonable levels and collection rates were low. The debt overhang haunted electricity
 companiesandtheir assets deterioratedover time.

 16.     With the PRSO IV and the previousPRSOs, the reformsimplementedover the last
 four years have transformed the power sector. Service to paying consumers is 24/7,
 collectionrates are above 90 percent, and commercial debt has been reduced. The recently
 privatized assets have resuIted in private companies operating in the power sector free of
 the debt overhang. In2007, Georgiabecamenet exporterof electricity..

 17.    These achievementswere the result of concertedactions including appointment o f
 professional managers to the then state-owned electricity utilities, careful and prudent

 implementingmarketdiscipline.

 18.    Similarly, reforms inthe gas sector have beeneffective inimprovingconditionsby

          Pillar 3: Improvingthe environmentfor privatesector development

20.     At the start of the PRSO program, Georgia faced excessive and costly regulations
which resulted in widespread corruption and little private sector development. These
problems had to be addressed decisively if high sustained growth was to establish in
Georgia. With the PRSO-backed reforms, we have improved the business environment
significantly. At the start of the reform agenda Georgia was 112'hplace in the rankingof
Doing Business, today Georgia is in 18'h place. In 2003, non-oil related flows to Georgia
were less thanone percentof GDP, today these flows are closeto 15 percent ofGDP.

21.     The reforms implementedduring the PRSO program included a major tax reform,
de-regulation, and major trade liberalization. Furthermore, we have made significant
progressin the area of standardizationby adopting international standards and developing
implementation capacity. These reforms combined to provide incentives to businesses to
move from the informal to formal economy. More smaIl and medium enterprises are
entering the market; credit is being expanded to the private sector and foreign trade is
diversifyinggradually.

We are determinedto continueto implementreformswhichexpandand strengthenthe role
ofthe privatesector inthe economy, Furtherreformsare neededto movea relativelycash-
intensiveeconomy into a morecontract-driveneconomyandfoster further expansionand
soundnessofthe rapidlydevelopingfinancialsector.

        Pillar 4: Improvingsocialprotection,educationand healthcare services

 22.      In2003, the social protection systemwas close to collapsing. Substantial arrears in
 pensions had accumulated over several years. Education was under-funded, school
 infiastructure was crumbling, and teachers were poorly paid. Corruption was common.
 Quality o f education was poor and access was becoming a problem. The health care system
 also faced significant challenges. Despite a series o f attempts during the mid-1990s to
 improve the health care system, positive results were limited.

 23.      In 2004, we cleared pension arrears and then focused on better targeting of the
 public safety net resources on the neediest population in Georgia. To this end, under the
 PRSO-I we introduced proxy means-testingand formed a database o f socially unprotected
 families. Under the PRSO I1we launched the targeted social assistance program inAugust
 o f 2006 and have expanded the coverage of the program under PRSO I11and PRSO IV. In
 2007, minimum pension increased up to 55 GEL and we anticipate further increases each
 year,

 24.      With PRSO IV and during the period of the PRSOprogram, we have implemented
 major reforms ineducation. The education system inGeorgia today is highly decentralized.
 Inthis decentralized system we are giving special importance to monitoring both quality
 and financial accountability. For the allocation of resources we have adopted per capita
 financing system. The capitation model is being adjusted periodically to improve its
 effectiveness, On quality, we first introduced national university entrance examinations.
 In 2006, legal entity o f public law      -                                                  .
                                              "State Accreditation Agency of Educational
 Institutions" was established and accreditation o f Higher Education Establishments was
 conducted. The accreditation o f general and vocational educational institutions is foreseen.
 Georgian students participated inTIMSS and PIRLS. At the same time we are developing a
 general school teacher retraining and certification system. In 2007, the new centers o f
vocational education were established. In2005, National program for the rehabilitation of
 Georgian public schools was launched. In the framework of the program new schools are
built, existing schools are refurbished and rehabilitated, heating systems are being fixed,
school buses are acquired.

25.      Inhealth care reform, we have changed our approachbut not the main objective of
seeking to ensure access to health care for the whole population. Under the PRSO IV, we
have ensured that the extreme poor continue to have access to publicly funded health care
services. About 674,000 poor individuals receive publicly financed health services. The
implementation of the Primary Health Care reform is reaching five regions. New standards
for health care facilities and professionals were developed and are being implemented. The
National Drug Policy Framework was adopted and the draR law on pharmaceuticals has
been prepared. And we have developed a plan for health care insurance covering specific
segments ofthe population.

             Annex IV: Georgia: Statement of IMF Staff Mission, March 7,2008




Press Release No. 08/46                                        InternationalMonetary Fund
March 7, 2008                                                  Washington, D.C. 20431 USA



          Statement of an IMF Staff Mission at the Conclusion of a Visit to Georgia

An International Monetary Fund(IMF)missionvisited Georgia during February 29-March 7,
2008 to assess recent economic developments and prospects for 2008. The mission met with
senior officials and representatives o f the private sector. The mission issued the following
statement today inTbilisi:

"Georgia's economic performance in2007 was exceptionally strong, with growth o f real GDP
exceeding 12 percent. This performance was due mainly to private capital inflows, which
exceeded 20 percent o f GDP and included a large element o f foreign direct investment.
Although private capital inflows are subject to uncertainty, recent positive trends suggest they
will continue at broadly the same level as last year. Consequently, the missionprojects real
growth at about 9 percent in 2008.

"End-period inflation was 11 percent for 2007, which exceeded the authorities' target. Inpart
this was the result ofhigher global food and energy prices, butalso reflected the loose monetary
stance that prevailed during most o f 2007, with broad measures o f money supply increasing by
50 percent. Against this background, the mission welcomes the authorities' commitment to
reduce inflation to 8 percent by end-2008 and strongly supports the goals o f more moderate
monetary expansion, the move to inflation targeting, and greater exchange rate flexibility.

"Animportant element inreducinginflation in2008 will bethe authorities' plan for
implementinga tighter fiscal stance. Continued improvements intax and customs administration
should allow revenues to be maintained even as some tax rates are reduced. O f particular
importance to maintainingmacroeconomic stability is to resist new government expenditure
initiatives.
"Thebroad intent ofthe proposed macroeconomic and financial reform package now before
parliament i s welcome. Inparticular, the mission supports the efforts to strengthenfinancial
sector supervision and combat money laundering. The introduction o f well-managed and
transparent FutureGenerations and Stable Development Funds could be a useful tool for
restrainingbudgetary pressures.

"At the same time, the mission stressedthe importance o f avoiding any erosion o f central bank
independence. Inthis regard, the mission believes that requirements for frequent parliamentary
approval o fmonetary policy goals and instruments, combined with new provisions for the


                                                  92

automatic dismissal of the President of the National Bank of Georgia for missingthe inflation
target, would weakenthe operations o f the central bank.
"Themission understands the authorities' interest inaccessingnon-concessional sources of
external financing. Nonetheless, inthe absence of any financing need inthe context of the 2008
budget, it i s important that the uses o f such financing be clearly specified, to allow a careful
assessment of the benefits and the costs.
"The IMFmissionplansto visit Tbilisi again inJune 2008 to conduct discussions for the Article
IV consultation."
