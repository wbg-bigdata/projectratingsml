                                       I. INTRODUCTION

1.       This program document presents a proposed development policy grant to the Republic o f
Sierra Leone for SDR 6.8 million (US$lO million equivalent) to finance its Poverty Reduction
Strategy. The grant will provide quick-disbursing budget support for the 2006 budget in one
tranche. The proposed operation i s the first in a new programmatic series that will support
sustainable development in the context o f Sierra Leone's new Poverty Reduction Strategy. It i s
designed to help the Government make the transition from post-conflict recovery, financed in
part by four previous Economic Rehabilitation and Recovery operations, to sustainable
development in the context o f Sierra Leone's Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS). The program
supported by the proposed grant includes policies and actions that will promote this transition
through improved governance, better management o f public resources, decentralization o f
government andpolicies that support private sector-led economic growth.

                                   11.    COUNTRY CONTEXT

                      A.       RecentEconomicDevelopmentsin Sierra Leone

2.       Sierra Leone has embarked on a transition from a nation focused primarily on post-
conflict needs to a nation focused on the implementation o f a Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS)
aimed at broad-based growth and poverty reduction. The war ended in January 2002, just over
four years ago. Sierra Leone has made substantial progress inconsolidating social and economic
security for its people. This has been complemented by its efforts to promote transparent and
inclusive governance. While there i s a continuedneed for rehabilitation andrecovery, people are
now increasingly looking ahead to the economic growth andpublic service delivery that will help
them escapepoverty.

3.       There have been significant positive developments on several fronts since the Board o f
Executive Directors approved the Fourth Economic Rehabilitation and Recovery Grant (ERRG
IV) for Sierra Leone inMay2005.'           Implementation o f the first PRS began with the 2005
budget in conjunction with the distribution o f the 2005-07 Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper
(PRSP).2 Local councils (LC) began implementing their first budgets in 2005 following the
passage o f the Local Government Act and the election o f local governments in 2004 (the first
such elections in over 30 years).            Consultative Group meetings were held in London in
November 2005. Among other things, the meetings included commitments to move towards a
framework for multi-donor budget support in the context o f the PRSP. The United Nations

increase inpetroleum prices over the same period. The exchange rate depreciated to an average

42.       The design o f the proposed grant has benefited from a number o f lessons drawn from

                         - IV                                                                   Several key
lessons are highlighted here:

     Experience has shown that the potential benefits o f development policy operations during
     fragile transitions often justify the risks o f engagement2' (OPCS 2005). In some cases, the
     international community reaches a consensus that the benefits o f stabilizing a fragile
     transition are worth the risks and looks to the Bank to play a role in coordinating and
     channeling financial support inthese countries. Operations inthis context usually support an
     extended state-building and recovery process following a programmatic approach. When
     used flexibly, the programmatic approach can accommodate uncertainties as regards
     implementationcapacity andprovide a framework for policy development.

     A balanced approach is needed to support development policy. Theproposedgrantmaintains
     this balance with program support aimed at macro-stabilization,                    good governance,
     decentralization, selected aspects o f capacity buildingand stimulation o f the private sector.

     Lending for development policy should not rely on conditionality alone to catalyze and
     implement reforms. To be fully effective, the policy dialogue must be supported by other
     instruments that identify institutional weaknesses and help build country capacity step by
     step. (OPCS 2001) The proposed grant therefore seeks to capitalize, as much as possible, on
     existing project support and technical assistance. These supporting operations are referenced
     insections IV.Babove andV.Dbelow.

     Fragile states may require much more detailed programs than usually seen in other
     development policy operations. Where the administration lacks an effective work planning
     and monitoring system, it is unlikely that milestones will be achieved unless they are further
     broken down into actions and institutional responsibility is clearly assigned. Detailed action
     planninghelps the government and program partners track progress and adjust to delays in
     program implementation. Detailed action planning should not be confused with
     conditionality: milestones help guide a process o f reform internally, but are not necessarily
     triggers or conditions. However, such detailed operational planning and reporting in the
     context o f development policy operations i s best seen as a transitional arrangement while
     internalmanagement andreporting systems are put inplace (OPCS 2005).

67.       Recent Government action. The Government i s aware o f the highcost that the nation
paid for corruption under previous regimes. It therefore chose to put a spotlight on the problem
through the creation o f an Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC).                        The Anti-Corruption
Commission is meant to create accountability for those caught in corrupt practices although it
performs an educational role as well. Government also introduced a number o fproactive reforms
meant to reduce the opportunities for corruption. These include the Government Budgeting and
Accountability Act, procurement reform, strengthened internal and external audit functions,
better follow-up by the Public Accounts Committee and improved records management. The
details o f each are summarized invarious sub-sections below. Section V.D.3.f below on mining
also includes details about the Kimberly Process and the Extractive Industries Transparency
Initiative (EITI).

    Anti-Corruption Commission. The Anti-Corruption Act was passed in 2000 and the ACC
    was established inthe same year to carry out prevention and community awareness activities
     as well as investigate alleged or suspected corruption. A National Anti-Corruption Strategy
     (NACS) was published in February 2005 and an implementation plan was proposed. The
     intelligence and investigative capacity o f the ACC has been improved by the recruitment o f
     additional staff and intensive training. One constraint is that the ACC i s not responsible for
    prosecution: cases are referred to the Attorney-General and the Minister o f Justice. This has
     led to some frustration: as noted in a recent report to the UNSecurity Council, "not a single
     high-profile case has been fully prosecuted so far. There i s a general feeling among the
     population that the Commission is not able or willing to achieve tangibleresults.'126

    Anti-Money Laundering.           Sierra Leone's recent history o f conflict financed by "blood
     diamonds" and allegations o f money laundering motivated the creation o f anti-money
     laundering regulations in2002. These were extended andbrought into line with international
     standards when the 2005 Anti-Money LaunderingAct was adopted.

     Civil society and media. A program called "Enhancing Interaction and Interface between
     Civil Society and the State" (ENCISS) is now in its second year o f working to increase the
     capacity o f civil society to participate in, influence, contribute to and monitor the PRSP and
     local government policy, planning and implementation.               Focusing on the link between
     demand- and supply-side governance, ENCISS works with government institutions andwith
     civil society organizations. A recent review confirmed that it i s considered impartial and
     political neutral by both government and civil society organizations. Government endorses
     and works with ENCISS. ENCISS includes a media component for disseminating
     information on the PRS and decentralization. Government i s also promoting the
     strengtheningo fthe Independent MediaCommission.

        d. Decentralization.

89.       Current situation and challenges. Government believes that a contributing factor to the
ten-year civil war was the disaffection o f a large section o f the rural population, who were
effectively cut-off from political representation by the elimination o f local government in 1972
and cut-off from Freetown and the rest o f the world by the lack o f a fimctioning road
infrastructure. They were thereby marginalized from the political process and deprivedo f social
services and economic development opportunities. This was particularly a concern for those
communities far from Freetown. Marketing and communications were more practicable with
communities in Liberia or Guinea than with the population in Freetown. Household survey data
for 2003/04 are clear that the problem persists with large inequalities between urban Freetown
and the rest o fthe country. For example, the incidence o fpoverty inFreetown inthat periodwas
only 15 percent compared to 79 percent inthe rural areas.56 Similarly, in2000 about 74 percent
o f the population inurban areas had access to safe drinkingwater compared to 46 percent inthe
rural areas.57 More recent data from a 2004 pilot CWIQ survey shows increased disparity with
91 percent o f the urbanpopulationhaving access to safe water while only 45 percent o f the rural
population had such access.

        b. TradePromotion and Export Diversification.

113.    Current situation and challenges. Imports accounted for 42 percent o f GDP in 2005,
almost 20 percentage points higher than exports. This large trade gap i s the consequence of the
old anti-export policy bias prior to the war, war-time export losses and high post-conflict
resource requirements.     Sierra Leone i s a member o f two regional economic groupings: the
ECOWAS and the Mano River Union (MRU). The vast majority o f trade i s conducted with
Europe andthe UnitedStates o fAmerica, inbothdirections.

114.    Sierra Leone used to have a moderately diverse export base consisting o f unprocessed
diamonds, iron ore, bauxite, rutile, fish, palm oil, coffee, cocoa and tourism. Exports o f cash
crops fell, well before the war, as a result o f inappropriate policies. Accessible iron ore was
gradually exhausted while the rutile andbauxite mines were shut down due to the conflict. Thus,
recent export activity has been limited to diamonds with cocoa holding a distant second place.
Fishexports are also significant but little o f the revenue is captured by Sierra Leone. The level
and diversification o f exports is expected to improve following the restoration o f bauxite and
rutile operations in 2006. It should also be noted that Sierra Leone has leased some offshore
blocks for oil exploration.    More work remains to be done to restore cash crop exports to
historical levels and then to expand and diversify them.

        c. Finance

122.     Current situation and challenges. There i s strong anecdotal evidence that the lack o f
access to credit and capital markets i s constraining new business investments andjob creation.
There is an absence o f long-term bank credit in Sierra Leone. High and risk free Treasury bill
rates, combined with weak property rights and inadequate collateral, tend to reduce the incentive
for the commercial banking system to lend to the private sector. Net domestic credit to the
private sector was only 5 percent o f GDP in 2005 compared to an average o f around 17 percent
in Sub-Saharan Africa and65 percent inOECD countries. There is no credit reportingdatabase.
Interest rates are market determined. The end-year Treasury-bill rate was 16percent compared to
a year-end inflation rate o f 13 percent. The spread between bank loan and deposit rates remains
highand has been increasing due inpart to the risks posed by incomplete property rights and a
weak judicial system.      The capital market consists mainly o f short-term government debt
instruments. Only a few company equity shares have been issued and the only trading that has
taken place has been the issue o f rights that were issued in 2002 for a commercial bank and the
capitalization o f a brewery in2003.

123.     Most people do not have access to formal banking services. Those that do are mainly in
the urban areas and the quality o f service is often low. Four community banks have been
established and two more are planned. A National Policy on Micro-Finance was established in
2003 and a number o f micro-credit programs have been established. Progress towards a modern
payments system i s only beginning, with substantial review o f laws and regulations. There are
no formal barriers to entry for new banks. There are seven commercial banks operating. Three
are locally owned and four are foreign owned with one having entered as recently as late 2004.
Only one o fthe locally owned commercial banks is wholly private.

124.     The National Social Security Insurance Trust (NASSIT) controls the largest pool o f
investable funds inthe nation. At present, NASSIT i s heavily investedintreasury bills and is the
single largest non-bank lender to Government. One o f the challenges facing NASSIT is whether
and how to diversify out o f treasury bills and into domestic and international investments -
without undermining its ability to meet its obligations to pensioners. Another challenge is how
to manage its assets without distorting the market: recent transactions data from 2005 show that
large purchases or sales o f treasury bills by NASSIT can generate sharp swings in the domestic
interest rate.

125.     There are some risks posed bythe financial status o fthe BSL. The Bank o f Sierra Leone
Act o f 2000 requires the BSL to have a minimumpaid up capital o f Le 50 billion subscribed and
held exclusively by Government. The MOF has provided capitalization worth Le 24 billion, via
an interest free loan from the BSL, leaving Le 26 billion outstanding. As mention inSection II.C
above, this limits B S L income and reduces its capacity to fulfill its policy and oversight
functions.




                                                 44

126.     Inaddition, BSL capacityto mop up excess liquidityis decreasing due to its declining
share o f interest bearing securities relative to the overall liquidity inthe market. The only other
available option for mopping up liquidityi s the sale o f foreign exchange through the BSL foreign
exchange auction. This would have the effect o f appreciating the real exchange rate, an outcome
that would be harmful to the vast majority o f citizens who are engaged in rural agriculture:
exports o f emerging cash crops such as cocoa would become more expensive to market while
competition from imported food would increase.

127.     The Government may therefore wish to convert the non-interest bearing loan from the
BSL into bonds and start repaying them. This, however, could create a substantial fiscal cost for
Government and it could also undermine the incentive for commercial banks to lendmore to the
private sector when it is easier to invest inthe new, risk-free bonds (inaddition to treasury bills).

171.        Current situation and challenges. Sierra Leone i s making a significant progress inthe
education sector despite the substantial disruption caused by the war between 1991 and January

School fees for girls inthe Junior Secondary Schools in the Northern and Eastern Provinces are
also subsidized. It has enhanced its expenditures to cater for additional teaching and learning
materials, examination fees, provision o f subsidized textbooks for secondary schools. As a
result, the Government has made remarkable progress in doubling the number o f primary school
enrollments between the 2001/02 and 2004/05 school years to a current level o f over one million
children. The gross enrollment rate in2004/05 was 160 percent for primary schools, 44 percent
for Junior Secondary Schools, 14 percent for Senior Secondary Schools and less than 4 percent
for tertiary institution^.'^ Inequityinenrollment rates i s a problem at the secondary school level,
particularly for girls and those inrural areas. The primarycompletion rate (PCR) was 65 percent
in 2004/05, slightly above SSA average of 62 percent. The number of students sitting for the
National Primary School Examination has tripled between 2001 and 2005 to 78,000.                             The
percentage o f those passing fell to 72 percent (55,800) in2005 compared to 93 percent in2000.

180.      Current situation and challenges. Sierra Leone lost about half o f its health facilities
duringthe war andsubstantial numbers o fhealth sector personnel. The impact o f 10years o fwar
preceded by decades o f neglect i s apparent in the data assembled for the 2005 UN Human
Development Index. Sierra Leone has the world's highest maternal mortality rate (2,000 deaths
per 100,000 live births) and under-fivemortality rate (284 per 1,000 live births). The country
had also experienced a very highrate o f malnutrition among under-five children with 27 percent
underweight. The incidence o f tuberculosis is also very high: roughly 628 cases per 100,000
people versus only 495 cases per 100,000 for the region. The incidence o f Onchocerciasis (River
Blindness) i s among the highest in the region. The reported incidence o f malaria is high but
declining, from a morbidity rate o f 37.5 in 2003 and 2004 to 35.2 in 2005.97 The HIV/AIDS
prevalence is low at 4.9 percent nationally but no basis for complacency: the Western Area
prevalence rate i s 6.1 percent and47 percent o fHIVpositive cases are new infections.

183.        World Bank's Assessment. Empirical research from around the world shows that
declining health is one of the major reasons why families fall into poverty. Inthis context, the
health and nutrition outcomes, as described above, are intolerable. Yet, the available evidence
does show progress. For example, immunization rates are up: from 44 percent in 2000 to 70

184.        A national health policy is in place and is supported by the Government and
Development Partners. The policy is based on providing an essential package of services and
protecting the poor. An annual health review is heldeach year with a wide group o f stakeholders
at which time the policy i s reviewed and a detailed plan o f action agreed upon for the upcoming
year. The Government has made a commitment to provide free maternal and child health care,
expanded immunization programs and school health program. Financial protection for the poor
against the burdens o f illness is limited. This i s the case inmuch o f the developing world where
the public sector does not work particularly well and a majority o fhealth services are provided by
the private sector - often either missionary or traditional.                 Programs to prevent and treat

efforts are underway to expand the programs to the provinces and rural areas.

185.        A number o f issues still hinder the ability o f the system to deliver on national goals.
These are: a) low budgetary allocations andbudget execution rates; b) low household capacity to
pay "out o f pocket"; c) inadequate planning and management of the health sector's human
resources; d) the lack o f coordination among the various actors in the health sector; and e) the
constraints associated with the lack o f a functioning infrastructure for water, roads and
communications need to be recognized and addressed inthe development o f the health sector. In
this light, the health sector agenda should be closely coordinated with the infrastructure agenda
through the PRSP, MTEF and other processes.

186.        The health sector is also in transition. Many mission hospitals and NGOs, who served
during and just after the war, have recently reduced their activities as a result o f increased
security. The private for profit health system, just recently developing, remains weak and is
limited to the Western Region andFreetown, the capital city. The free drugpolicy introducedby
the government insome facilities and by some large international NGOs has put stress on private
providers.

187.       A small portion o f the management and functional review agenda remains unaddressed.
Key issues include procurement policies and capacity and human resource management. The
latter i s important: in2004, there were only 33 nurses, 4.7 midwives and7.3 physicians for every

211.      The proposed grant includes support for policies that are likely to have distributive
effects on the level and distribution o fpoverty. The data from the 2003/04 household survey and
pilot CWIQ show that the districts outside the Western Area where the capital o f Freetown i s
located generally suffer higher levels o f poverty and reduced levels o f public services. The
support provided to the decentralization o f public services should therefore have the effect o f
reducing inequality. The data also show that those households relying primarily on income from
artisanal miningare among the poorest o f the poor.loO Thus, the support provided to the mining
community, particularly extension services, should help reduce inequality. The support for
privatization in the NPA should help increase electricity supply and may reduce costs to the
extent that NPA can purchase power from the Bumbuna hydroelectric project, contributing
directly to job creation and poverty reduction.              The support for restructuring the SLRA to
improve road maintenance should help reduce rural poverty, and thus inequality, as farmers save
costs on inputs and gain access to new markets."' Further, road maintenance programs are often
a good source o fjob creation.

environment, health and livelihoods o f mine workers and adjoining communities, a majority o f

2.      Mr. President, as you are aware, Sierra Leone has made significant progress in
maintaining political and economic stability, implementing structural and institutional reforms
and improving service delivery as the country transitions from the post conflict emergency phase
to sustainable development and poverty reduction. These efforts were generously supported by
the international community including the World Bank. However, poverty remains pervasive
with high unemployment levels especially among the youth. To address the pervasive poverty
situation in a comprehensive manner, Government is implementing a comprehensive poverty
reduction strategy with appropriate macroeconomic, structural and sectoral policies inthe context
ofthe Sierra LeonePoverty ReductionStrategy (SL-PRS).

3.      The SL-PRS which i s in its second year o f implementation i s underpinnedby three main
pillars, which are closely interlinked and reinforced each other. Pillar Ifocuses on achieving
Good Governance, Peace and Security. The second pillar aimed at achieving Food Security, Job

RecentEconomicDevelopments

4.      The Sierra Leone economy recovered strongly during the post-conflict period (2001 to
2004), reflecting broad-based recovery in key sectors - agriculture, mining, manufacturing,
construction and services. Real GDP grew by 9.3 percent in2003 from 27.5 percent in2002 and
continued to expand by 7.4 percent in 2004. After a period o f low inflation during 2001,
reflecting prudent economic policies and improvement in domestic supply situation, inflation
rose during 2003 - 04 with the annual average inflation reaching 14.2 percent in 2004 from 7.5
percent at the end o f 2003. The increase was largely due to higher fuel prices but expansionary
fiscal and monetary policies and the depreciation o f the exchange rate contributed to the
inflationary pressures.

5.      External sector objectives were largely achieved, reflecting mainly the strong growth in
exports particularly diamonds. Imports also grew strongly explained inpart by the rehabilitation
o f the rutile and bauxite mines. As a result, the current account deficit, including official
transfers, narrowed from 17.4 percent o f GDP in2001 to 4.9 percent in2004. Gross international
reserves reached 2.9 months o f imports as at end 2004.

6.      Fiscal performance improved during the period mainly due to lower-than-budgeted
expenditures. The overall budget deficit on a commitment basis and excluding grants, declined
from 16.5 percent in 2002 to 12.5 percent in 2004. The domestic primary deficit declined from
7.7 percent o f GDP to 2.8 percent over the same period.

7.      Monetary policy followed development in the fiscal sector. The increase in net bank
credit to Government, in part due to shortfalls and delays in external budgetary support, led to
increases inmoney supply. Growth inbroad money and reserve money averaged 27 percent and
22 percent respectively between 2001 and 2004, though the rate o f increase slows down during
the period.

8.      The three-month Treasury bill rate increased from 14.7 percent in 2001 to 28 percent in
2004, reflecting the rise in inflation and higher domestic Government borrowing. The nominal
andreal exchange rate o fthe Leone to the U S dollar depreciated duringthe period.

9.      In 2005, economic activities continued to be buoyant with real GDP growing by 7.3
percent. Inflation rose inthe first three quarters o f the year before moderating towards the end o f
the year, owing to the rise inpetroleum prices and accommodating monetary policy.

10.     There were slippages in fiscal performance in 2005 but Government took corrective
measures in the last quarter o f 2005 to bring overall fiscal performance broadly in line with the
budget. Total domestic revenues were slightly less than projected (0.5 percent o f GDP) due to
shortfall in collection by Customs and Revenue as a result o f the shift incomposition o f imports
towards lower tariff goods. Total expenditure was less than budgeted due to lower donor

Medium-termMacroeconomicObjectivesandPolicies

15.     The main macroeconomic objectives underlying the medium term strategy include the
maintenance o f sustained high real growth and a stable macroeconomic environment. Hence,
agricultural expansion and food security are key medium-term objectives, reflecting in part
agriculture's importance for job creation.

16.     Private sector development and an improved investment climate will be vital for the
achievement o f high sustained output growth. Government will pursue reforms to raise private
investment, including in the mineral sector, in the coming years. The Government also plans to
allocate resources to power generation and infrastructure over the medium term to ease
bottlenecks that negatively affect business activities.

Fiscal policy

17.     Medium-term macroeconomic stability hinges critically on fiscal policy. The main thrust
o f the policy framework will be fiscal consolidation while shifting resources increasingly toward
spending on priority areas. In particular, a better balance between available resources and
budgeted outlays, as well as a move towards a broadly balanced primaryposition, is necessary to

18.      External shocks caused by the unpredictability o f external budgetary support are being

priority spending will be financed by well identified, predictable resources under the second
arrangement o f the PRGF.

19.      Government will focus on a stronger revenue effort in order to reduce dependency on

20.      Efforts will be made to maximize fiscal revenues from the mineral sector. Government

21.      Effective monetary policy will be critical in ensuring macroeconomic stability,
complemented by appropriate fiscal policy. Government will work together with the Bank o f

                            ANNEX 4: FUNDRELATIONS NOTE

IMFExecutiveBoardApproves US$46.3 Million PRGFArrangement for Sierra Leone
Press Release No. 06/94
May 10,2006

The Executive Board o f the IMF approved a three-year arrangement for Sierra Leone under the
Poverty Reductionand Growth Facility (PRGF) ina total amount equivalent to SDR 31.1million
(about US$46.3 million) to support the government's 2006-08 economic programs. An initial
disbursement o f SDR 4.7 million (about US$7 million) under the arrangement will become
available immediately.

The Board agreed to waive the nonobservance o f a performance criterion on the accumulation o f
new external arrears under the previous PRGF arrangement that expired in June 2005 (see Press
Release No. 05/130) that resulted ina noncomplyingdisbursement under that arrangement.

The PRGF is the IMF'sconcessional facility for low-income countries. It is intended that PRGF-
supported programs are based on country-owned poverty reduction strategies adopted in a
participatory process involving civil society and development partners and articulated in a
Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP). This i s intended to ensure that PRGF-supported
programs are consistent with a comprehensive framework for macroeconomic, structural, and
social policies to foster growth and reduce poverty. PRGF loans carry an annual interest rate o f
0.5 percent and are repayable over 10years with a %-year grace period onprincipalpayments.

Following the Executive Board's discussion on Sierra Leone on May 10,2006, Agustin Carstens,
Deputy ManagingDirector andActing Chairman, said:

"The Sierra Leonean authorities are to be commended for the satisfactory performance under the
previous PRGF-supported program, which helped consolidate the post-conflict transition and laid
the foundation for sustained real growth and poverty reduction, while ensuring continued donor
support.

"The authorities' economic program for 2006-08 draws on the government's Poverty Reduction
Strategy Paper's vision o f channeling more resources to poverty reduction, and places a welcome
emphasis on nation building, the restoration o f macroeconomic stability through sound fiscal
management and improved effectiveness o f monetary policy, and the creation o f an investment
climate conducive to private sector growth.

"Inthe fiscal area, the authorities' emphasis onrevenue mobilization is well placed. Measures to
improve revenue administration, increase collections from the minerals sector, and enforce tax
compliance will be critical for a successful policy implementation and help protect poverty-
related spending. It will also be necessary to contain the growth o f the wage bill and continue
applying the budgetary framework that allocates predictable fiscal resources to priority spending
areas.




                                               125

"Sound fiscal management will have to be supported by appropriate monetary and exchange rate
policies. For this purpose, the authorities intend to strengthen the central bank's operational
capacity for conducting monetary policy and apply a more flexible exchange rate policy.
This will have to be reinforced through financial sector reforms to enhance private savings,
investment, and growth.

"Structural reforms will help limit the challenges that lie ahead. The promotion o f good
governance, including the strengthening o f the government's anti-comption efforts, will remain
vital for the efficient use o f resources, the improvement in the investment climate, and the
mobilization o fmuch needed external support.

"Reaching the HIPC Completion Point and qualifying for the MDRIrelief will provide access to
additional resources for poverty reduction. However, Sierra Leone's external debt will remain
substantial in the coming years and adequate macroeconomic policies, a prudent external
financing strategy, and increasedhighly concessional donor support will remain essential to help
attain debt sustainability over the medium-term and to progress toward achieving the Millennium
Development Goals.

"The Executive Board reviewed the noncomplying purchases made by Sierra Leone under the
previous Poverty Reduction and Growth Facility Arrangement with the Fund, which arose as a
result o f misreporting on external arrears. In light o f the steps taken by the authorities to clear
these arrears and to avoid their recurrence in the future, the Executive Board decided to grant a
waiver for the non-observance o f a performance criterion on the accumulation o f new external
payments arrears that gave rise to the noncomplyingpurchase," Mr.Carstens said.

Recent EconomicDevelopments

Sierra Leone has made substantial progress with its post-conflict transition. Macroeconomic
performance during the last PRGF-supported program (2001-04) was on the whole strong, and
structural reforms sought to establish a foundation for sound public finances and the effective
conduct o fmacroeconomic policies.

During 2005, economic expansion continued to be robust, with output growth projected at
7.3 percent in 2005, reflecting healthy activity in agriculture, diamond mining, manufacturing,
construction and services. Inflation averaged 12.1 percent during the year, as a result o f higher
fuel prices and monetary expansion. There were fiscal and monetary slippages toward the end o f
the year, and the government intended to implement corrective measures andpolicies for 2006 in
the context o f the proposed PRGF-supported program. The exchange rate appreciated in real
terms in 2005 after a steady depreciation since 2002. While export performance continued to
improve, the external current account deficit (including official transfers) widened in 2005, to
7.3 percent o f GDP from about 5 percent in 2004 because o f strong import growth. External
gross reserves reached US$168 million by year-end, reflecting better than anticipated external
budgetary support.




                                                126

Manysocio-economic challenges remain, however. Poverty remainspervasive, particularly in
rural areas, and poverty-reducing efforts have yet to make notable progress.

ProgramSummary

The new PRGF program for 2006-08 aims at enhancing macroeconomic stability, with structural
reforms focusing on laying the ground work for scaled up aid inflows to support achieving the
Millennium Development Goals.

The medium-term macroeconomic framework projects that output growth will stabilize in the
range o f 6-7 percent by 2008, underpinned by broad-based sectoral growth. Inflation i s expected
to converge to single digits by the end o f the period, supported by prudent macroeconomic
policies. The external current account balance (including official transfers) i s projected to narrow
to 6 percent o f GDP by 2008. Gross official reserves are expected to stabilize at about 3 months
o f import cover.

Effective monetary and fiscal policies will be critical in ensuring macroeconomic stability.
Strengthening the B S L operational capacity, including by improving existing money market
operations and widening the menu o f instruments, will help to improve the effectiveness o f
monetary policy. Fiscal consolidation and the achievement o f a better balance between available
fiscal resources and budgeted outlays will be central for the policy framework under the PRGF.
The government intends to contain the primary deficit at about 2 percent o f GDP in2006 while
shifting resources increasingly toward poverty-reducing activities. Fiscal consolidation would
assist monetary policy in lowering inflation while ensuring that the domestic public debt would
remain at a sustainable level.

The main focus o f structural measures inthe PRGF-supported program will be on reforming the
public sector and civil service, strengthening capacity for data collection, analysis, and reporting
to support policy-making, and strengthening public finance management to assist the budgetary
process. Financial sector reforms will be aimed at enhancing domestic savings and investment.
Further efforts will also be made to fight corruption, including by strengthening the Anti-
corruption Commission.

Progress has also been made in implementing the Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper, which
should help Sierra Leone reach the HIPC Initiative completion point this year and gain access to
additional resources under the MDRI.
