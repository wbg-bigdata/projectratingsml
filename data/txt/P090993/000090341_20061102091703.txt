  A. STRATEGIC CONTEXT AND RATIONALE

   1. Countryand sector issues

   1. Economic context and background Argentina continues its rapid recovery from the deep
  economic crisis o f 2001-02. However, poverty levels have not recovered to pre-crisis levels and
  continue to be high, affecting 31.4 percent o f the population as o f the first semester o f 2006.'
  Duringthe upturn, the unemployment rate also decreased consistently from 22 percent in2002 to
   10.9 percent inthe first semester o f 2006.

  2. Since the peak of the crisis in 2002, the economic recovery has been robust; GDP growth
  averaged 9 percent between 2003 and 2005. The recovery was led by strong export earnings and
  increasing investment and was further aided by renewed consumer confidence. Prudent
 macroeconomic policies were key in the recovery, especially fiscal policies that generated
 primary surpluses for the consolidated government in 2004 and 2005 o f 4.8 and 4.2 percent o f
  GDP, respectively. Official forecasts for 2006 suggest a continuation o f growth in the order o f 8
 percent     2,  The current principal macroeconomic challenge i s to contain inflation, which has been
 running at an interannual rate o f about 11 to 12 percent. The Government is expecting that
  inflation will show an increase o f 10.7 percent in 2006 (according to the budget proposal recently
  sent to the Congress). The market expectations are at 10.1 percent for 2006 and 10.8 percent for
 2007.

 3. Health Outcomes: Argentina i s in an advanced demographic and epidemiologic transition
 with significant positive trends in key health status indicators. Infant mortality has returned to a
 steady decline after a brief increase due to the economic crisis o f 2001/2002 and today stands at
  13.3 per 1,000 live births (2005). Maternal mortality i s around 44 maternal deaths per 100,000
 live births. Life expectancy continues to increase (from 71.9 to 74.6 between 1990 and 2004).
 Today, the country's burden o f disease (Table 1) i s dominated by non-communicable diseases
 (NCD) and injuries (88%), primarily cardiovascular diseases, cancers and chronic respiratory
 diseases.

5. Inaddition to this horizontal fragmentation, Argentina's federal structure assigns most health
responsibilities (operational and policy) to the provinces. While this allows for greater efficiency
and local adaptation in the delivery o f individual health services, it has posed a complex
challenge for national health policy formulation and implementation. The National Ministry o f
Health (MSN) i s responsible for sector coordination (through the Federal Health Council,
COFESA4,,setting and enforcing quality standards and regulations, as well as the traditional areas
o f national public health (e.g. disease monitoring). But, until recently it lacked the tools to
influence provincial policy from the national level.

6. Crisis and Response:The Government responded quickly to the economic and social crisis o f
2002. The share o f the population without formal health insurance coverage increased as formal
employment declined.         Such increased demand, coupled with sharply higher poverty and the
inability o f large parts o f the population to afford basic medicines, put provincial health systems
under great pressure at a time in which most provinces were in a financial crisis. The Health
Emergency Program (HEP) which received assistance from the international finance institutions
consisted o f national provision of essential drugs (Remediar program) as well as basic medical
supplies (e.g. vaccines, fortified milk, anti-retroviral drugs) to the strapped public provincial
health systems'.      The national Government's rapid and effective response to the emergency
ensured a continued provision o f health services to the poor during the crisis and resulted in a
quick reversal o f the worsening o f health indicators (see Annex 1, and Annex 16).

7. The national Government also introduced important health policy reforms in the direct
aftermath o f the crisis. For example, the M S N substantially revised the drugs policy to increase
the availability o f generic drugs in the country. Similarly, it passed the Sexual and Reproductive
Rights Law which provided the basis for a much needed policy and regulatory framework as
Argentina had one o f the lowest levels o f family planning among middle-income countries inthe
region.

8. Despite the strong progress made in the health sector--especially inview o f the efforts needed
to address the impacts o f the economic crisis--Argentina's health sector still faces several
challenges. The first would be to further improve its health outcomes which lag behind countries
with similar incomes despite high spending on health and a high coverage o f health providers and
hospital beds. In 2004, although life expectancy in Argentina at birth was greater than the Latin
American average it was lower than Costa Rica, Chile, Uruguay, and Panama--countries with
similar or lower incomes than ArgentinaS6 Likewise, infant and maternal mortality i s higher
compared to comparator countries, and maternal mortality has plateaud with unsafe abortion as
the single most important contributor to maternal deaths (27% in 2003). In addition, teenage
pregnancies, although slowly decreasing, remain common (accounting for 14% o f all
pregnancies).

infant mortality rate i s three times higher in some provinces than in Buenos Aires, and the gap for

 12. Government's Health Sector Reform Program: Since 2003, the Government has been
responding to the new sector priorities through its Health Sector Reform Program (HSRP)."
which comprises an integrated package o f complementary policy reforms and actions intended to
increase the effectiveness o f public subsidies in improving the health status o f the poor. The
main HRSP objectives are to: (i)increase access o f the poorest mothers and children to basic
services; (ii)enable the National Ministry o f Health (MSN) in reassuming its stewardship and
regulatory functions in core areas o f public health (essential functions and programs); (iii)
consolidate regulatory reforms in the social health insurance system to avoid negative spillovers
in the public health sector in the provision o f access to services for the poor and uninsured; and
(iv) trigger significant changes in the relationship between the national and provincial
Governments, as well as between the provinces and health service providers.

 15. Essential Public Health Functions and Programs: The Government's FHP also highlights
the need to strengthening MSN's stewardship and regulatoryfunctions with respect to Essential
Public Health Functions and management of key Programs (see Box 1 for explanations), and has
prepared a $999.7 million equivalent program to be carried out at the national and provincial level
for this purpose. The FHP recognizes the strategic importance o f public healthfunctions (such as
monitoring, surveillance, and health promotion) to reducing the burden o f communicable diseases
and preventing non-communicable cardiovascular diseases, cancer, chronic respiratory diseases,
diabetes and hypertension. Well-functioning stewardship-including                      robust public health
surveillance-forms      the foundation o f a public health system by providing information on disease
outbreaks and patterns that Argentine policy-makers at national and provincial level need to make
better decisions about the efficient and effective allocation o f limited resources. Communicable
disease surveillance leads to timely responses to disease outbreaks, which in turn save lives,
reduce morbidity and disability, and in some cases protect against economic shocks. Both the
national and the provincial level have stewardship roles inthe health system and inthus carrying
out public health programs. The FHP defines these roles more clearly, including competencies
for each function, and development o f an accreditation system to assure that the functions are
being carried out at the provincial level.

            Box 1: Essential Public Health Functions,Programs and Public versus Private Goods in Health

     EssentialPublic HealthPromams and Functions: Essentialpublic healthfinctions refer to core elementsof public
health policy which apply across all activities, independent of specific diseases or levels of interventions. These
functions range from monitoring & evaluation to social participation and from health promotion to human resource
development. Essential public health programs refer to specific activities geared to specific diseases or levels of
intervention(e.g., tuberculosis, HIVIAIDS, vector-borndiseases, safe blood supply). Such programs are characterized
by a collectivehealthinterest andrepresenteither public goods or goods with importanthealthexternalities.

     Public Health Goods: Public Health Programs provide some goods and services that are almost "pure" public
goods. Public health goods are both "non-rival" in consumption (consumptionby one person does not reduce the
amount available for others) and "nonexclusive" (benefits cannot be restrictedto only those people willing to pay for
them). Individuals may benefit from the pure public health goods and services without even knowing that they exist
(for example, vector -mosquito- control or area fumigation campaigns).

     Other health programs are rival in consumption but nonexclusive in benefits.One example is immunization. A
largepart of the population must use this service if it is to be effective as a medical interventionfor the populationas
whole. Immunizationsmay provide widespread immunity, however, at a level of coverage lower than 100%. Thus,
they providebenefits (throughexternalities)to peoplewho choose not to be immunized.Becausethese services involve
inherent market failures, they require some form of Government intervention in determining where, how much, and
what will be provided.A strong rationaleexists for Government financingkey public health programsbecauseof their
public goods nature.

     Private Health Goods: Privategoods in health are those characterizedby both rival and exclusive consumption.
Private goods consist largely of curative (as opposed to preventive) forms of care. The Government can decide to
financeprivategoods for a variety of reasons, staring with health insurancemarket failures or assuringthe delivery of
vital (private) health services to the poor who could otherwise not afford them. Plan Nacer, for example, is a public
health insurancefor privatehealth goods (for mothers and children) for the most vulnerable populationthat do not have
formal health insurancecoverage.



16. The FHP also highlights the importance o f providing support to national essential public
healthprograms that respond to collective health interests and represent `public goods' inorder to
avoid backtracking on health status achievements (see Box 1). Public health priorities and goals
as established in the FHP are encompassed in essential public health programs, and most o f these
have been in operation for many years. Obstacles to success in reaching goals vary among these
programs. In general, however, implementation o f these programs reflects inequities between
and within provinces, which are partially related to variable capacity, quality of activities and
access to health services.

2. Rationalefor Bank involvement

18. The Bank has been engaged in a very close and productive health sector dialogue with the
Government in Argentina that spans more than a decade. Over the years, the Government has
faced a number o f important challenges, and has looked to the Bank for financial and technical
support to address these. The Bank's financial and technical assistance has supported improving
the efficiency o f Argentina's health system through a combination o f adjustment and investment
lending, and associated technical advice. Bank assistance has centered on several areas. First,
the Bank has supported provincial health insurance and system reforms by incorporating the
sector in a number o f Provincial Reform Loans, as well as in the Provincial Health Sector
Development Project. Second, the reform o f the national social health insurance system was
supported through adjustment and technical assistance lending. Third, support was provided
through several investment operations two o f them targeted on maternal and child health care,
including child nutrition, attention to reproductive health o f mothers and early childhood
development, and another on public health surveillance and health promotion at the central level.
As a result o f its long experience with Argentina's health sector, the Bank has significant
comparative advantages in supporting the remaining facets o f the Government's FHP through the
Essential Public Health Functions and Programs Project. Bank support for implementation o f the

58. Lessons from national experience: Four main lessons o f experience were drawn from the
Bank's support o f Argentina's health sector over the past years, which were incorporated in the

the poorest provinces, where most o f the uninsured live. The Government strategy for expanding
public health i s to extend the Project to all provinces. However, both the supplies provided and
the incentives for implementation would be allocated under well defined equity criteria,
reinforcing the focus on expanding health coverage to the poorest.

59. Second, the Bank's experience in supporting Argentina's health sector highlights the benefits
o f linking Bank financing to actual performance andor results. The MCHIP APL Iintroduced
significant innovations in output based lending, which are showing important positive results.
The proposed Project would introduce (i)a performance-based disbursement mechanism in

                                                                                           a
based financing mechanism in Component 3 through which M S N would provide financing to

indicators (triggers) o f provincial performance.

60. Third, the implementation o f the MCHIP APL Iprovided other preliminary lessons: (a) the

61. Finally, the Government derived important lessons related to procurement from the

5. Alternatives considered and reasons for rejection

62. Choice ofSIL:      In defining the most appropriate instrument to support the Government's
program through the proposed Project, both a single investment loan (SIL) and a horizontal
Adaptable Program Loan (in which provinces enter in different groupings like in the MCHIP
APL I) considered. The horizontal APL concept was not pursuedbecause ofthe indivisible
         were
nature o f the functions and programs that are being supported, both at the national level as well as
the provincial level (public goods nature o f the supported programs which makes them not
divisible geographcally or epidemiologically).         Dengue control, for instance, depends on
mosquito control, which only operates efficiently if carried out in the entire national territory
(including border regions). Likewise, vaccinations must cover all provinces since the objective i s
to ensure effective coverage o f the population in order to interrupt epidemiological transmissions
and take advantage o f positive externalities. For this reason, a single investment loan covering all
provinces was considered the most appropriate instrument.

63. Limiting the Number of Essential Health Programs: A strategic choice was made to limit the
number o f programs to be supported under the proposed Project. This was deemed necessary
given both the complexity o f sector functioning, as well as the proposed Project's goal to


                                                  16

introduce performance- and results-based financing as a management tool. The programs were
selected given their cost-effectiveness in contributing to lowering the BOD inArgentina.

64. Inclusion of both Communicable and Non-Communicable diseases (NCDs): A choice was
also made to address both communicable and non-communicable diseases, recognizing that in
Argentina non-communicable diseases are the primary BOD. One option would have been to
address only communicable diseases, but with an aging and increasingly urban population, the
vast majority o f the BOD is related to NCDs. Understanding current disease patterns and related
behaviors in Argentina and the long-term monitoring o f changes in these patterns and behaviors
over time i s critical to effective planning and execution o f appropriate public health activities.
Without adequate training and development, Argentina would face important challenges in
mounting any concerted public health response to these emerging chronic disease problems.
Therefore, an incremental, strategic approach to chronic disease surveillance and associated risk
factors was considered appropriate. The proposed Project would begin by setting priorities in
chronic disease control, determining staffing needs and implementing an appropriate training
program. The chronic disease and risk factors surveillance system would then be implemented
duringProject implementation.

65. Focus on Prevention: The proposed Project would focus on the prevention o f NCDs as the
most cost-effective strategy for reducing the burden o f disease. Effective strategies exist to
change life-styles and prevent NCDs that are strongly related to smoking, poor diet, inactivity and
obesity. While the more effective treatment o f NCDs to reduce hospitalizations and mortality i s
also important, it i s not nearly as cost-effective as prevention. Prevention requires increased
capacity in the areas o f regulation and health promotion (communication, education, cross-
sectoral activities health inthe workplace and schools), most o f which are public health functions.

D. APPRAISAL SUMMARY

      1. Economic and financial analyses

      1.1Economic Analysis

94. The proposed Program (and with it the Bank-financed Project) will produce economic and
social benefits due to its impact on both morbidity as well as mortality. Morbidity improvements
would be materialized both, due to the decrease o f disease incidence (e.g., for Chagas, dengue,
tuberculosis, and diabetes) as well as to improved lifestyles (e.g., reduction in nicotine
consumption, increase the voluntary donations o f safe blood).                     In addition to improve the
morbidity pattern (which i s very likely the largest benefit o f the Project), the Project i s also going
to reduce a number o f preventable deaths in Argentina- it would reduce mortality. The top risk
factor for causes o f preventable death which would be tackled by the project are overweight,
nicotine poisoning, and alcohol consumptions. The main pathologies or causes o f preventable
death which would be tackled by the project are Chagas, dengue, TB, HIV and ITS, and unsafe
abortions and others.

95. The application o f a straightforward economic analysis o f the project i s made difficult by two
factors. First, the major benefit from this project will come from a morbidity reduction.
However, no reliable burden o f disease (BOD) analyses exists in Argentina today, which would
allow for a calculation o f the gained `disability-adjusted life years' attributable to the project.
Second, the translation o f benefits expressed as Disability-adjustedLife Years (DALYs) or years
o f life lost to premature death (YLL) into monetary values i s not uncontroversial. The standard
assumption made in this conversion is to use the average annual salaried income as a proxy for
the economic benefit o f a year o f life saved. Not only the monetary conversion o f years o f life
saved but also the actual value used in the conversion (which would be lower in much o f the
developing world then the developed world) i s debated hotly inthe literature.20

96. Given the above restrictions, Annex 9 reports the years o f life lost to premature death (YLL)
due to the project investments. The marginal investment in public health financed with Bank
resources would lead to more than 345,000 life years saved, mainly due to reducing the risk
factors o f obesity (153,000) and nicotine poisoning (106,000) and to reduce mortality stemming
from pathologies like unsafe deliveries (29,000), HIVIAIDS (18,000) and dengue (12,900).                  As
mentioned above, these are likely going to be only a fraction o f the actual benefits o f the project
given that we could not include benefits from morbidity reduction. Further, additional benefits are
likely to arise due to (i)     the effect o f an increase inthe immunization coverage o f certain inmuno-
preventable diseases; (ii)cost reductions related to the fall in the incidence o f infectious diseases
and NCDs, as well as diseases attributable to the consumption o f tobacco (that present a highcost
o f hospital attention); (iii)   cost reductions related to public health service delivery improvements,
including the collection and distribution o f safe blood. If one were to apply the average income
evaluation to the Years o f Life Lost to premature death (YLL), the 345,761 YLL alone would
translate into a substantial benefit resulting from the marginal Bank-financed program
interventions inthe amount o f U S 2 4 6 million.

          Economic and Social context: The economic, social and political crisis o f 2001 exposed
 and exacerbated the preexisting and chronic structural problems o f the Argentine health sector
 that have contributedto its under-performance. Duringthe crisis, Gross Domestic Product (GDP)
 decreased by 20 percent, 11 percent in 2002 alone (Graph 1). The social impact o f the crisis was
 enormous and poverty increased dramatically (Graph 2). According to official                         statistic^:^ the
 share o f the population defined as poor increased to nearly 60 percent o f the population, almost
 doubling from 12 to 22 million intwo years between 2000 and 2002. Inthe same time period, the
 number o f extreme poor nearly doubled to 10.4 million, about 30 percent o f the population.
 Although the poverty rate fell to 38.5 percent in the first half o f 2005, it i s still above the pre-
 crisis level o f 30 percent. The unemployment rate has also fallen from 22 percent in 2002 to 10
percent in2005.

       Argentina i s now recovering from the economic crisis. After its peak, economic growth
remained steady at 9 percent from 2003-2005, resulting in a recovery o f GDP to near its pre-crisis
peak o f 1998. The recovery was led by strong export earnings, increasing investment levels and
 further aided by renewed consumer confidence. Prudent macro-economic policies were the key
inthe recovery, especially fiscal policies that produced primary surpluses inexcess o f4.5 percent
o f GDP during2004-2005. In2005, the Government restructured 75 percent o f its debt indefault
and in January 2006, prepaid its outstanding debt with the International Monetary Fund.
Forecasts for 2006 suggest a continuation o f growth o f 5-6 percent, with containing inflation
being the principal macroeconomic challenge.

         Overview of the Health Sector in Argentina and Current Challenges: Argentina i s inan
advanced demographic and epidemiologic transition. There are many positive trends inkey health
status indicators. Infant mortality has returned to a steady decline after a brief increase duringthe
economic crisis. Life expectancy continues to increase. Nevertheless, Argentina lags behind
countries with similar social and economic indicators despite high spending on health and a high
coverage o f health providers and hospital beds. Even after various reform efforts during the
1990s, serious structural flaws in the Argentine health sector result in interlintra provincial
inequities, high levels o f fragmentation, inefficiency and a weak regulatory framework impeding
its performance in response to the expectations and needs o f the population. Only recently some
o f these conditions have changed, in particular the regulatory framework has been installed and
reinforced under the current administration, creating both, a new legal framework in different
areas (tobacco, reproductive health, quality control), or creating capacities for the enforcement o f
the law through surveillance, and control. The health transition has presented an entirely new set
o f challenges to which the country i s just beginningto respond.

         Basic demographics: Argentina has a population o f 38 million. It i s a Federal Republic
with 23 Provinces and a capital city o f Buenos Aires, which is divided into 13jurisdictions. There
are 2190 municipalities. There are also five geographic regions (NOA, NEA, Centro, Cuyo y
Patagonia) that are distinguished by demographic, economic and socio-cultural characteristics.
Although there are regional variations, on average about 90 percent o f the population live in
urban areas with one-third (13 million) living in the province o f Buenos Aires.

         The population growth rate i s 1 percent. The total fertility rate has declined from 2.7
children per woman in 1996 to 2.4 in 2002 and i s expected to continue slowly drifting downward.
Life expectancy at birth i s 71 years for men and 78 for women. The long-standing drop in fertility
rate and increase in life expectancy mean that Argentina now has one o f the oldest populations in
Latin America with only 27 percent younger than 15 and 10 percent older than 65.

         The official moderate poverty line shows a large increase over the last thirteen years. The
headcount ratio computed using the moderate poverty line increased around 14 percentage points
between 1992 (19.7 percent) and 2005 (33.8 percent), which means more than 6 million "new
poor" individuals. About 3.5 million entered poverty during the economic growth period o f the
90s, another 3.5 million joined that group in the first phase o f the recession (1998-2001), and
around 7.5 million crossed the poverty line during the 2001-2002 crisis. The economic recovery
substantially reduced the number o f poor to about 8.5 million. Extreme poverty increased around
9 points between 1992 (3.8 percent) and 2005 (12.2 percent), with a peak in 2002 o f 27.6 percent
(10.5 million).

         Health condition of       the population: Argentina has essentially completed an
epidemiological transition. Nearly 90 percent o f deaths and BOD are due to non-communicable
diseases and injuries (Table 5). The most common causes o f death are cardio-vascular disease (33
percent), cancer (21 percent), and chronic respiratory disease (10 percent).

         Even if we consider the last efforts o f the M S N in the last years in health provision,
inefficiency in the health sector manifests itself in the provision o f health services and the focus
on high complexity / high cost care. Primary care generally accounts for less than 10 percent o f
provincial health budgets. Even within primary care the focus i s on curative care and little
attention i s paid to preventive services, particularly for adults. Population-based prevention and
health promotion programs have only recently been initiated inArgentina, primarily inthe area o f
tobacco control. An important contributor to this inefficient and inadequate health sector scenario
has been the dysfunctional relationship between the nation and the Provinces, and the lack o f
stewardship capacity to lead and assure effective implementation o f public healthprograms.

         Federal-Provincial Relations: The health sector in Argentina i s immersed within a
complex federal structure with important consequences for health policies, institutions and
administration. Despite minimal participation in direct provision o f services (the M S N executes
only 3 percent o f total health spending), the national Government plays an important role interms
o f stewardship and coordination, including the regulation and control o f national social security
insurers, national public health policy formulation, leadership o f national public health programs
and coordination o f international sources o f financial and technical assistance.

         Nevertheless, most health sector responsibilities (operational and on provincial and local
policy matters) are given to the Provinces. As the national Government has only limited legal and
administrative influence over provincial health sector policy, quality standards and norms have no
been adhered to by all Provinces. This has severely hampered the effectiveness o f national
programs and projects. Many provincial health authorities have also shown weak capacity to
coordinate their internal health care systems. Provincial health spending i s highly asymmetrical:
while the city o f Buenos Aires spends 25 percent o f its budget on health, other Provinces like
Santa Fe, Misiones, Cordoba, Comentes and Catamarca spend only around 8 percent. The city o f
Buenos Aires and the Provinces o f Santa Cruz, Tierra del Fuego and Neuqukn spend more than
270 Argentine pesos per capita, while Buenos Aires, Santa Fe, Misiones Cordoba, Corrientes and
Tucuman health spending doesn't exceed 80 pesos per capita. These factors, compounded by
socio-economic, educational and cultural differences, have contributed to wide variations in
health outcomes between Provinces. The social crisis and persistent, substantial inequities within
and between Provinces call for renewed prioritization o f public health within the heath sector
development agenda. This is especially the case for strengthening priority programs, developing
new activities (e.g. related to tobacco and injuries) and the establishment o f a solid framework for
stewardship including health intelligence, regulation, quality assurance and policy-making.

          Public health priorities: and goals as established in the Federal Health Plan are
encompassed in ten key public health programs (Box 3). Some o f these programs have been
runningfor many years while others are new. Obstacles to success inreaching goals vary among
these programs. However, in general they all exhibit inequities between and within Provinces

          Immunizations: As in all Latin American countries, Argentina has eliminated polio and
maintains a vigilant acute flaccid paralysis surveillance system. The last measles case was
reported in 2000.28 These successes are related to a reasonable immunization level. However, in
the recent past Argentina has had major problems with adequate vaccine procurement and as
recently as 2001 there was a critical shortage of vaccines that lasted for nearly a year. This was
exacerbated by the 2002 economic crisis. Emergency loans enabled the country to purchase
vaccines from PAHO's rotating fund during 2003-2005. Since 2003 Argentina has increased
vaccine coverage and introduced new vaccines including hepatitis A, B and Measles-Rubella
(MR). Overall coverage levels are greatly improved though they have still not reached the target
o f 95 percent. A significant problem i s the variable coverage rates between Provinces as well as
gaps in coverage at the local level. Improving these inequities is an important goal o f the Federal
Health Plan. The country has other ambitious targets including eliminating congenital rubella by
2010.

          Tuberculosis: With the exception o f an increase during the economic crisis, tuberculosis
incidence in Argentina has been steadily declining for the last decade. Nevertheless, it i s higher
than in countries with similar levels o f development. While this may be related in part to the
increase in A I D S cases, there continue to be chronic problems with the TB program. Timely
procurement o f TB medications has been an issue, and there was a several month gap in their
availability during the crisis. DOTS treatment began in the late 90s and coverage accelerated
prior to the crisis, but recently increases in coverage have been slow. Most o f the TB cases are in
the province o f Buenos Ares where there have been problems with implementing this treatment.
While the cure rate reached 80 percent in2005, the goal of 85 percent has still not been achieved.

         AIDSBTDs: The incidence o f H N / A I D S has stabilized over the last couple o f years.
Mortality rates have fallen since ARVs became available. The GOA has a policy of universal
coverage o f ARVs for AIDS patients. The AIDS program has invested in prevention efforts in
several areas including sex workers and widespread media campaigns supporting condom use.
Programs injails have been initiated. The Province o f Buenos Aires supports needle exchanges.
The most rapid growth of the epidemic appears related to N drug abuse. Reported STD rates are
low but this i s almost certainly due to under-diagnosis and under-reporting. The incidence o f
congenital syphilis i s unacceptably high suggesting that many cases are undiagnosed. This i s
likely true for other STDs as well.

dengue and leishmaniasis. Chagas, a disease with long-term cardiac consequences, i s primarily
related to poor housing (thatched walls) that permits the vector to come in contact with humans at
night. It has been eradicated in several Provinces and the goal is to eradicate it in all. The large
dengue epidemics that have affected many Latin American cities in the past decade have not yet
occurred in Argentina. But the presence o f the vector means the country i s at risk. The incidence
o f leishmaniasis i s relatively high in Argentina (282 cases in 2005).        The top priority i s to
improve surveillance of the vector, and increase capacity to diagnose and treat the disease. The
dengue program received funding during the health emergency and this may have contributed to

health authority (Box 4). This role encompasses multiple functions that are called essential public
health functions (PAHO, CDC). Most o f these have high positive externalities for the health o f
the population and/or constitute public goods in the field o f health. They are fundamental to the
work o f the health authority as the agency responsible for safe-guarding public well-being in
health (PAHO). The success o f public health programs depends on an adequate capacity to be
stewards o f these programs and to carry out the EPHFP (Figure 1). Each program may have a
greater or lesser emphasis on one o f the other o f the EPHFP. For instance, all programs require
monitoring and evaluation. Most programs involve some degree o f health promotion and
regulation though the importance o f these hnctions on the success o f the programs may vary (e.g.
health promotion i s critical to the success o f a risk factor prevention programs and reproductive
health, but may be less important for EPI or TB). The competencies and responsibilities for
EPHFP will also vary at each level o f government (national, provincial, local).

LessonsLearnedfrom World BankProjects:

         Three main lessons o f experience were drawn from the Bank's support for Argentina's
health sector over the past years, which were essential inthe design o f the EPHFP Project:

          First, focusing reform on the social insurance system proved insufficient to address the
health problems o f the poor. While structural reforms were moderately successful before the
crisis, coverage o f the uninsured did not expand significantly, and after, reaching the uninsured
became even a higher priority given the effect o f the economic crisis, which caused 12 percent o f
the insured to loose their health coverage. This loan, has the following mechanisms to target
poorest population: i)"public good" condition o f the P H A - universal provision, ii)important
externalities o f public health programs (vaccines, tobacco, etc), even if the poorest don't receive
services, iii)selected programs are cost-effective and highly oriented to reduce most important
Argentina's Burden o f Disease affecting the poorest, iv) self target o f the key PHP, such as TB or
Chagas disease, which majority affect the poorest.


         Second, previous projects and reforms did not reach the poorest Provinces where most o f
the uninsured live. The Government strategy for introducing the EPHP i s universal (all
Provinces).

         Finally, the Bank's experience in supporting Argentina's health sector, indicate the need
to more closely link Bank financing to actual results, output (actual delivery o f services to the
population) and outcomes (improvement o f final or intermediary health indicators). APL I
introduced significant innovations in output based lending, which are showing excellent effects in
their almost two years o f implementation since loan effectiveness. The EPHFP has take
advantage o f that experience on its design.

LessonsLearnedfromother Projects notfinancedby the WorldBank(under elaboration):

         On August 2003 the M S N created a National Coordination Unit for the "healthy network
municipalities" for supporting municipalities in developing and implementing its health
promotion strategies and activities. Ending 2005, the healthy municipalities' networks is
integrated by 124 municipalities, and have developed relevant activities in health promotion and
prevention, vector control and waste disposal. Important lesson from those activities, regarding
outputs and monitoring systems are being reflected in the design o f Component three. PAHO is
one o f the most important supporters o f this initiative.

         A decentralized Agency called SEDRONAR has developed a very well known and
feasible system to monitor results, and impact o f promotional and preventive activities, based on
biannual health surveys, which solves administrative data fragilities from annual measures. This
experience has been utilized in the design o f monitoring indicators, and will be a milestone for
impact evaluation protocol, as well as for Mid-term evaluation.

         The REMEDIAR Project, financed by the IADB has the purpose o f improve the delivery
and utilization o f generic drugs (medicines) for primary health care facilities. This project has
developed a remarkable system to supervise its correct storage, delivery and utilization on
medical facilities. This experience will be used for the development o f supervision and
monitoring system for Component two by the M S N on Public Health and medicines purchased
for the public health programs.

        Impact evaluation: Criteria for measuring the Impact Evaluation of the Argentina's
Public Health Functions and Programs Project

        There are two main challenges identified for the design o f an impact evaluation o f this
operation: i)the EPHF and Key programs project have national universal coverage but are
implemented in a decentralized fashion by provinces, and ii)the activities undertaken by the 10
PHP may have complementary and synergetic effects, which make the identification o f the
"intervention" difficult.

        To address these challenges, the impact evaluation proposed for the operation would
adopt a variety o f strategies and use triangulation methods to confirm impacts (Le. when several
methodologies find the same result, the credibility o f the result increases). The evaluations are
likely to face some limitations in coverage and may not all resolve the basic identification
question about the changes observed due to the project.

    National risk-factor survey:    the survey provides the baseline data for the Project. The
    Project would include funding for other round of the survey in year 4. It will not be possible
    to attribute changes in the prevalence o f risk factors to the activities o f the Project but it will
    provide the basis for comparisons between provinces. A specific module dealing with the
    prevention activities to be undertaken under the 10 key health priorities could be added. The
    information from this module could be used to assess variations incoverage.

    A prior step to designing the impact evaluation will be to assess the evaluability o f the
    programs in terms o f definition o f the interventions, targets and possible comparison groups,
    timing o f impacts, importance o f the function in terms of prevalence, money spent by the
    Government. The evaluation should focus on a few key programs or key areas (for example
    the Northern Provinces, where problems are more acute).

    While the programs are universal, there will be variations inthe way the provinces implement
    them. A key factor o f the design would be to understand roll-out plans - spatially and
    temporally -- to take advantage o f them to design impact evaluations in selected provinces for
    a sample o f interventions. While the results will strictly only be applicable to the selected
    provinces or areas, they will be indicative of tendencies in the rest o f the countries if the


                                                  57

     provinces are chosen in such a way that they reflect the variety o f situations in the country.
     The evaluation teams will need to secure commitment from program managers to stick to the
      roll-out plan.     Understanding roll-out plans will enable the evaluation team to identify
      different types o f comparison groups, such as individuals in territories not covered by an
      intervention for a while, individuals at the limit o f the target group (Le. children o f 60 to 63
     months for interventions targeting children less than 60 months o f age).

      If a province starts distribution of some medicines or supplies to pregnant women and
      children in some areas and has a plan for expansion, a baseline data collection could be
     undertaken in area A covered period 1 and in area B to be covered inperiod 2. At the end o f
     period 1, children in area A will have received the supplements, while children in area B can
     act as a comparisongroup for them. Inperiod 2, both groups will have receivedthe treatment
     but the duration o f exposure will vary. Ifthe areas are selected randomly, the analysis will be
     based on a quasi-experimental design, which will provide robust results. Otherwise, matching
     techniques and regression discontinuity techniques will be used.

      Similarly, for lifestyle related diseases where the intervention would mostly consist o f
     information campaigns, it would be adopted a similar approach to Dupas (2006).34 The

         Procurement for the proposed Project would be carried out in accordance with the
World Bank's "Guidelines: Procurement under IBRD Loans and IDA Credits" dated May 2004;
and "Guidelines: Selection and Employment o f Consultants by World Bank Borrowers'' dated

financed by the Loan, the applicable procurement methods or consultant selection methods, the

Procurementunder the Projectcomponents

         The Project includes four components. The first component i s the strengthening o f the
national health capacity to deliver health programs and will include studies, information
systems, health promotion, educational tasks and technical assistance, including accreditation,
and evaluation; the second component will improve resource allocation and preparedness for
health emergencies including supplies monitoring and procurement o f pharmaceuticals; the third
component will reimburse the participant provinces with a share o f public health activities. This
is a financial transaction with no procurement activities involved as the share o f the Bank-
financing o f each package o f public health activities i s dimensioned as to cover only for the
activities that are not subject to public procurement. The fourth component will finance project
management, administration and operational costs including office equipment, maintenance,
travel expenses, sundry items and actions towards results measurement and fiduciary
compliance. All the procurement actions under the project (including those under the contingent
health emergencies) will be carried out at the M S N in Buenos Aires thus guaranteeing
economies o f scale in the procurement o f supplies and medicine to be distributed at the
provincial level.

     This annex presents the economic evaluation o f the Project and the analysis o f financial or
 fiscal impact. The analysis tries to offer an approach o f economic rationality to the interventions

 Economicevaluation.

      As mentioned previously, the proposed Project will produce economic and social benefits
due to its impact on both morbidity as well as mortality. Morbidity improvements would be
materialized both, due to the decrease o f disease incidence (e.g., for Chagas, dengue,
tuberculosis, and diabetes) as well as to improved lifestyles (e.g., reduction in nicotine
consumption, increase the voluntary donations o f safe blood).          In addition to improve the
morbidity pattern (which is very likely the largest benefit o f the Project), the Project i s also
going to reduce a number o f preventable deaths in Argentina- it would reduce mortality. The
top risk factor for causes o f preventable death which would be tackled by the project are
overweight, nicotine poisoning, and alcohol consumptions. The main pathologies or causes o f
preventable death which would be tackled by the project are Chagas, dengue, TB, HIV and ITS,
and unsafe abortions and others.

     The application o f a straightforward economic analysis o f the project i s made difficult by
two factors. First, the major benefit from this project will come from a morbidity reduction.
However, no reliable burden o f disease (BOD) analyses exists in Argentina today, which would
allow for a calculation o f the gained `disability-adjusted life years' attributable to the project.
Second, the translation o f benefits expressed as Disability-adjusted Life Years (DALYs) or
years o f life lost to premature death (YLL) into monetary values i s not uncontroversial. The
standard assumption made in this conversion i s to use the average annual salaried income as a
proxy for the economic benefit o f a year o f life saved. Not only the monetary conversion o f
years o f life saved but also the actual value used in the conversion (which would be lower in
much o f the developingworld then the developed world) i s debated hotly inthe literature.

     Given the above restrictions, this Annex outlines the methodology used to calculate the
years o f life lost to premature death (YLL) due to the project investments. The marginal
investment in public health financed with Bank resources would lead to more than 345,000 life
years saved, mainly due to reducing the risk factors o f obesity (153,000) and nicotine poisoning
(106,000) and to reduce mortality stemming from pathologies like unsafe deliveries (29,000),
HIV/AIDS (18,000) and dengue (12,900).           As mentioned above, these are likely going to be
only a fraction o f the actual benefits o f the project given that we could not include benefits from
morbidity reduction. Further, additional benefits are likely to arise due to (i)the effect o f an
increase in the immunization coverage o f certain inmuno-preventable diseases; (ii)cost
reductions related to the fall in the incidence o f infectious diseases and NCDs, as well as
diseases attributable to the consumption o f tobacco (that present a high cost o f hospital
attention); (iii)cost reductions related to public health service delivery improvements, including
the collection and distribution of safe blood.

         Estimates o f the number o f Indigenous inhabitants in Argentina vary significantly,
illustrating the difficulties o f identification and the complexities o f ascertaining ethnic backgrounds.
Several sources have published estimations o f the number o f Indigenous inhabitants as around one
million (3 percent o f the population). A Population Census was carried out by the National Institute
for Statistics and Population Census (INDEC) in 2001, and for the first time, it includes a question
to households regarding Indigenous self-identification criterion for persons living in it. 2.8 % o f
households answered the question positively. The census included a complementary survey on
Indigenous households, carried out during 2004-2005. It includes 57 000 households that were asked
72 study questions addressing education level, distance to school, mother tongue, availability o f
transportation etc., to produce a systematic assessment o f their size and composition. It also
generated data on their living conditions and livelihoods. To reduce the level o f misclassification,
representatives o f each target population were included as trainers to sensitize the team o f research
workers to the cultural environment and norms. It was finished by December 2005, and showed an
estimated number o f 402,92 1 Indigenous inhabitants.

         This allowed for a systematic assessment o f the size and composition o f Indigenous
populations in Argentina. It also generated data on their living conditions and livelihood. This i s a
significant advancement as the produced disaggregated information by ethnic origins permits the
design o f programs usinggeographically targeted tools such as poverty maps. To reduce the level o f
misclassification, representatives o f each target population were included as trainers to sensitize the
team o f research workers to the cultural environment and norms. This i s important as stigma
associated with Indigenous people might cause some to reject the Indigenous identity increasing
type 2 errors inclassification.

         Census results on Indigenous households: The census data distinguishes between 18
Indigenous peoples groups (table l),        o f which Mapuche, Kolla and Toba are the largest accounting

Buenos Aires has the largest number (98,364) o f Indigenous households. Indigenous households are
categorized as such when at least one household member identifies hidherself as Indigenous or o f
Indigenous descent. Other Provinces that have large numbers (15,000-20,000)                            o f Indigenous
households are Santa Fe, Salta, and Jujuy. On average, the prevalence o f Indigenous households
according to these criteria is 2.8%. However, five Provinces in particular (Jujuy, Chubut, Neuquen,

Identification of Provinces that trigger the World Bank's Policy on Indigenous Peoples
(OP 4.10)

        Even though in the 23 Provinces which make up the Project there are members o f
Indigenous populations, not all o f them are within the bounds set by the criteria o f OP 4.10. to
be considered Indigenous peoples for its purpose. Many people have migrated to the cities for
economic reasons and are not members of communities, from the sociological standpoint. For
example, in the province o f Buenos Aires and in the Autonomous City o f Buenos Aires one can
find Kolla families andor individuals which ancestrally were located in Salta and Jujuy, as well
as Tobas from Chaco, etc.

        The procedure which was followed in order to define which Provinces trigger OP 4.10
was the following: A first necessary step was to identify those Provinces where, according to the
available information, there are Indigenous people who meet the criteria established in the OP
4.10 (para. 4). Three identified sources o f information were analyzed:

    1. National Institute of Indigenous Affairs - Instituto Nacional de Asuntos Indigenas
        (INAI): has registers o f Indigenous communities enrolled in the National Indigenous
        Communities Register (Registro Nacional de Comunidades Indigenas, RENACI),
        according to national and provincial level regulations.
    2. Several studies which describe the territories historically inhabitedby these people, and
        where currently communities remain and have a collective attachment to the land.
    3. Information from the 2001 National Census, particularly on self identification o f
        individuals as Indigenous.

        Based on the criteria established in OP 4.10, at the present time and with the available
information, the Provinces that trigger the policy are: Tierra del Fuego, Chubut, Rio Negro,
Neuquen, L a Pampa, Mendoza, San Juan, Salta, Jujuy, Tucuman, Catamarca, Chaco, Formosa,
Misiones and Santiago del Estero.

        Inthe Umbrella Agreement, to be signed with the MSNfor the implementationof the
Project, all Provinces, both the Provinces now triggering the application o f OP 4.10 and those
that might do so in the future, will be committed to proceed to the social evaluation o f the
Indigenous people meeting the identification criteria o f OP 4.10 in their respective Provinces
and to carry out the Indigenous Peoples Plans according to the terms o f the IPPF.

        On the basis o f the information provided by the 2001 Census, it is estimated that there
are 3 10.314 Indigenous people in the Provinces identified, corresponding to an estimated target
populationo f 173.176.

Generalguidelinesfor the preparation of IndigenousPeoplesPlans (IPPs)

         As stated above, in those Provinces where OP 4.10 is triggered due to the presence
Indigenous communities that meet the criteria o f identification of such policy, IPPs will be prepared
and implemented following the guidelines given in OP 4.10 (Annex B) and the IPPF. The IPPs will
be elaborated in similar terms o f the recently negotiated APL 11, creating synergies and
strengthening the national policy o f indigenous peoples.

         Specific Guidelines are included in the OM to prepare the provincial IPP such as: (i)  general
activities related to the legal framework; (ii)a detailed schedule o f common studies with Plan
Nucer; (ii)detailed activities for education and training strategies o f health personnel during the
project cycle; (iii)action plan for cultural adequacy o f priority programs for traditionally cultural
patterns o f indigenous peoples, and (iv) monitoring and evaluation strategies to be carried out
through sentinel observatories.

         In the preparation of the IPPs, the Project would take into account the need to develop
diffusion strategies and specific and adequate communication methods for Indigenous people, thus
guaranteeing their complete knowledge o f the Project. Ineach particular case the following will be
anticipated: (i)material in native language; (ii)  diffusion o f information campaign; (iv) consultation
and complaints mechanisms on a national as well as provincial level; (v) training for health service
provider's staff in order to assist the beneficiaries belonging to the communities and (vi) a specific
description o f EPHPP service delivery foreseen in each community.

         The IPPs in the APA could also include other activities that the province could develop for
or together with their Indigenous communities in order to improve the quality and access to health
for that population. Financing o f the IPP will be from proceeds o f the loan.

Diffusionof the IPPF

         For the diffusion o f the IPPF mechanisms o f participation and information will be used,
together with the national and provincial authorities, using the existing institutional framework
regarding Indigenous populations. A detailed description o f this institutional framework will be
included inthe IPPF presented by the Government. The diffusion must be registered and will serve
as input for the creation of the IPP for the identified Provinces.

         Taking into account the criteria established by the descriptive regulations o f each provincial
Government, the identified Provinces must design mechanisms for participation and prior
consultation on project implementation o f the Indigenous peoples in their respective Provinces. In
the same way they must anticipate some mechanism in order to disseminate IPPs that have been
prepared, with means for adequate verification (such as participative evaluation, agreements,
reports, interviews, photographic and audio records and others) that contain suggestions andor
demands that could arise from the different communities, in such a way that they could be used as a
basis for future training and medical supplies for the monitoring and evaluation system.

          The Project i s classified as Category B (activity where more limited environmental
analysis i s considered appropriate to address specific environmental issues) for two reasons: (i)
the Project would involve the provision o f medical supplies, which triggers concerns about
healthcare waste management (HCWM), and (ii)the team identified that minor constructions,
renovations, upgrades and additions o f health laboratories would need an environmental plan.
The Project will not finance pesticide^.^^

          Under Resolution 580/06, the Environmental Research and Development Unit from the
National Secretariat o f Environment and Sustainable Development was designated as the
executing group for providing technical assistance to the M S N in the diagnosis and elaboration
o f the Environmental Plan o f this Project. An Environmental Assessment Plan Framework
(EAPF) was prepared by the M S N to address the identified issues. The Plan Framework was
reviewed by the Bank and i s available in the Infoshop and on the webpage o f the MSN. Public
consultations o f the agreed Framework were held on September 18, 2006. Finally, an
Environmental Action Plan was prepared under the terms o f EAPF, to improve HCWM and for
screening and carrying out laboratory works set forth inthe OM.

          With regard to incremental HCWM, the Project will finance supplies and use o f
commodities that are already beingused in the health sector and thus no additional categories o f
health care waste will be generated by the Project.

          Argentina has comprehensive National legislation in place to guide H C W N practices.
(Law No. 24.051). Additionally, the Law No. 19.587 and its Regulation Decree establishes the
limits o f maximum pollutant concentrations in working spaces. Finally, all Provinces ingeneral
have hazardous waste regulations, either by adhering to a National Law or by having their own
regulations. As per the resolution o f SRNyAH No.413/93 a special unit for Hazardous Waste
was established under the MSN, which was a result o f a merger o f the National Registry for
Hazardous Waste and the unit for Management o f Hazardous Waste.

          Several assessments have been carried out to evaluate H C W M in Argentina in past
years, and all suggested that the Health Sector has a low risk rate for healthcare waste disposal.
Furthermore, the development and implementation o f the Argentinean national policy on
H C W M has been supported by preceding WB projects such as VIGIA and PROMIN 11.

         Additionally, under the PROMIN I1Project, following recommendations o f the QAGto
increment Bank efforts in waste management (QAG evaluated PROMIN I1 as highly

                                         Annex 13: HealthEmergency Program
                  ARGENTINA. EssentialPublic HealthFunctionsand Programs

          The economic crisis that affected Argentina, which reached its peak during 2002,
caused a sharply negative effect in the capacity o f delivering public services and goods in the
health sector. The crisis almost caused the interruption o f the normal implementation o f key
health interventions thus threatening to impact negatively on the health status o f the Argentinean
population and to increase out-of-pocket expenditures for health care services and goods thus
throwing more households into poverty or deepening their poverty levels.

          Although the socio-economic crisis had various consequences inthe provision o f health
care services and goods, the health sector was already facing both chronic and acute challenges
before the year 2002.

Chronic challenges: A report o f the Bank on the problems of the health sector in Argentina
(World Bank, 2003), summarized the major challenges o f the health system as a combination o f
inequity, inefficiency and weak institutional framework for the effective coordination o f
national and provincial programs that generated an extremely difficult environment to build
consensus for developing new and improved public policies to invest in human capital and
protect the poor.

Acute challenges: The Ministries o f Health o f the provinces and the Federal Government faced
increasing costs, shrinlung financial resources, and the reluctance o f providers to deal with
governmental organizations. This led to serious difficulties in procuring key medical supplies
for all types o f health services. The economic, social and political instability that ensued from
Argentina's default on its debt and the devaluation o f its currency caused a serious deterioration
o f the capacity o f the Government to sustain key health interventions for the most vulnerable
groups o f the population and essential public health services and programs for the entire
country.

            The crisis led provincial and national Governments to face an extremely tight fiscal
situation and a virtual paralysis o f payments that financed human resources and other key
medical supplies needed for key public health interventions (blood banks, prevention and
control programs for vector-transmitted and infectious diseases, nutritional support to pregnant
women and children etc.). However even when there were resources to purchase basic medical
supplies for the delivery o f health care services many suppliers were highly reluctant or plainly
refused to engage in business with the public sector due to lack o f credibility and uncertainty
regarding the exchange rates.

            As a result o fthese chronic and acute challenges some health indicators stagnated inthe
years previous to the crisis.

The Bank's response to the economic crisis in the health: Health Emergency Program
(HEP):
            HEP was a rapid response to the acute deterioration o f the delivery o f basic social
services, and consisted o f the restructuring o f already existing Projects. Therefore, there was no
S A R or PAD. The original objective o f the Social Emergency Program and o f the health
components were described in a President's Memorandum entitled "Argentina-Proposed
Amendment to Restructure the Portfolio to Support the Finance o f the Emergency Program"
dated 23 September 2002 (R2002-0189).

         The exceptional circumstances and complex organizational and institutional framework
within which the HEP was developed required a special implementation design. The
implementation design required aligning various key stakeholders and actors active inthe health
sector who also participated inthe financing and implementation o f the emergency program.

         The organizations and teams that were directly or indirectly involved in the emergency
response were the Inter American Development Bank (IADB); the teams o f the projects
through which the programwas implemented(VIGIA and PROMIN I1would be involved in the
planning o f the medical supplies required and on the supervision o f the distribution and
integration with the corresponding public health programs); the teams o f departments that
normally conduct the procurement o f pharmaceutical and other medical supplies within the
MSN; and the team o f the UTI-S the specialized unit that provided financial management and
procurement for PROMIN and VIGIA.

Overall the main objective of the Argentina Social Emergency Program that included
interventions in various sectors other than health was to provide the material support needed to
avoid the most deleterious impact o f the crisis on the poor and vulnerable population. Support
was provided to interventions in the following areas: health, education, nutrition, temporary
work, and income support and community activities.

         As stated in the President's memorandum, the general objective o f the health
component o f the Emergency Program was to protect the most vulnerable (groups o f the
population including mother and children) and to maintain essential public health programs.

         The HEP consisted o f two components that were implemented through the two existing
operations inthe health sector.

         The Mother and Child and Women's Sexual Reproductive Health Component was
implemented through the Maternal and Child Health and Nutrition Project (PROMIN). This
component was designed to ensure the provision o f services related to basic mother and child
care and women's reproductive health. It included material support for functioning o f public
hospitals network and key health interventions such as prenatal controls, anemia prevention,
child delivery, newborn reception, post-partum and child health care, through the provision of
pharmaceutical products, medical supplies and iron-fortified milk.

         The Public Health Programs Component was implemented through the Public Health
Surveillance and Disease Control Project (VIGIA). This component supported the financing of
important medical supplies for sustaining the following essential public health interventions:
immunization programs, functioning o f blood banks network, and disease control and
prevention for HIVIAIDS, tuberculosis, malaria, dengue fever, Chagas disease, and yellow
fever.

Achievement of Objective: The HEP achieved its objectives and the overall performance was
rated as satisfactory. The health interventions and public health programs implemented by the
Government were successfully sustained despite the acute and severe social and financial crisis
o f the past five years.

The financing o f key medical supplies for delivering basic health services to women and
children and disease and control programs thus provided support to the Government's efforts to
protect the poorest and most vulnerable groups o f the population from the worst effects of the
macroeconomic shock in the health sector.

Major factors affecting implementation and outcome: One o f the most important factors that
enhanced the implementation and outcome of this Project was the use o f existing arrangements
for procurement and financial management o f the health projects that were being implemented
at the time o f the development o f the HEP.

         In close coordination with all the stakeholders and actors o f the sector, the centralized
unit for procurement and financial management (UFI-S) of the M S N had to become rapidly
acquainted with the Bank's procedures and changing market conditions in order to support HEP
with the acquisition o f the required medical supplies. After an initial steep learning curve, UFI-
S, along with the teams from PROMIN, VIGIA and all the other departments from the MSN,
was able to comply with all the regulations o f the Bank to procure, contract and monitor the
nationwide distribution o f an unprecedented amount and scope o f medical supplies organized in
a centralized way.

         Another factor that affected positively the implementation and final outcomes was the
proactive initiative o f the M S N to build a consensus among the provincial Governments for a
quick response to the crisis and for implement the governmental response to protect the most



                                                 122

vulnerable groups o f the entire population. This provincial cooperation allowed the program to
overcome the challenges that a centralized organization posed to project implementation in all
provinces, not merely only in those where the Bank's projects (PROMIN and VIGIA) were
previously active.

        Various factors including internationally competitive mechanisms for acquisition o f
vaccines and drugs diminished the price o f key medical supplies acquired by the Federal
Government. Some o f these factors were: (i)the purchasing o f vaccines through PAHO, (ii)      the
introduction o f mandatory prescribing and acquisition o f generic instead o f brand-name drugs,
and (iii) expansion o f the scope o f interest bidders beyond national providers.
         the

        Among the negative factors that implementation o f the Project faced initially were those
related to uncertainty about socio-economic and political changes, which caused various
problems in the markets for the provision o f pharmaceuticals and other medical supplies. For
instance, uncertainty about potential further devaluation o f the currency and the fact that many
provinces resorted to various forms o f issuing alternative ways o f payment ("patacones")
increased the reluctance o f national and international providers to engage in commercial
transactions with public organizations.

        The strong reluctance o f providers to deal not only with cash-strapped governments but
with public organizations that did not have access to traditional mechanisms o f credit for
acquisition o f medical supplies was surmounted only with the public backing o f multilateral
organizations that provided the Ministry o f Health with access to hard currency and with the
reputation for transparency and regularity o f payments o f international organizations such as the
UNDP that providers considered crucial to count with during the worst months o f the crisis.

Sustainability: The GOA, incoordinationwith the Bank, developed various mechanisms for

phasingout the emergency program.

        Starting in fiscal year 2004, all the key medical supplies for public health interventions
        previously supported by the HEP were gradually transferred to the Government
        financial responsibility. For instance, the Government currently finances the purchase o f
        vaccines as it did before the period o f crisis. Currently all programs are financed by the
        Government.

        Inclose coordination with the Federal Government, the provincial Governments have
        been progressively taking on responsibility for procuring medical supplies for disease

        The GOA has negotiated a project with the Bank for increasing the efficiency and

        essential public health programs.

Lessons:

        (0 Prioritization of target populations and health interventions that promote
reduction of inequality in health status and access to basic health services: The Project
supported the efforts o f the government to finance the development and implementation o f a
package o f basic services and goods that focused on health prevention and primary health care
services targeting the poorest groups o f the population. With an increasing proportion o f the


                                                 123

depending on services provided by public facilities the government concentrated its efforts in
identifying an affordable and selected set o f services and goods that would effectively protect
and enhance the health o f the most vulnerable and at the same time preserve public health
goods. The magnitude o f the fiscal crisis and the sense o f urgency created by the rapidly
growing demand for public health services opened up the climate for a policy dialogue between
the federal and provincial Governments. The financing o f the Project along with resources from
the IADB contributed to provide leverage to the Federal Government to take the leadership in
reshaping the role o f the national Ministryo f Health and developing some initiatives to promote
a health financing model that focused on decreasing inequalities in access to basic health
services and preserving public goods.

         (ii) More efficient use of public resourcesfor theprocurement and distribution of key
inputs: The new role o f federal financial contributions that focused on reducing inequalities in
health status and access to basic health care services set an important precedent and provided the
basis for later developing nationwide interventions such as Plan Nacer. This signaled a shift
from a more passive and isolated role o f federal funding to a more proactive one that in close
collaboration with provincial Governments was able to shape policies that better targeted the
poorest strata o f the population.

         The Project resources not only supported the prioritization o f services and goods but
also contributed to set and enforce competitive procurement practices reducing the costs o f
some o f the medical supplies. The process o f planning, acquisition and distribution logistics o f
various medical supplies that had been previously carried out by a multitude o f agents in the
health sector became the responsibility o f federal authorities in close coordination with the
provinces and municipalities.

         The larger quantities required for national distribution permitted federal authorities to
take advantage o f economies o f scale in the purchasing o f various products. Additionally the
prices negotiated by the Federal Government were publicly disseminated thus eventually
serving as reference prices a practice that contributes improve transparency and to reduce large
variations in the acquisition o f inputs that typically occurs when the purchasing o f medicines
and other medical supplies i s carried out at lower scale or by local governments and health
authorities or the public facilities themselves.

         The Federal Government regained credibility among suppliers provided by the certainty
o f payments provided by the participation o f World Bank and the participation o f various other
international organizations such as Pan American Health Organization (PAHO), the Inter
American Development Bank (IADB) and the United Nations Development Program (UNDP).
The federal government used its purchasing power to use more frequently procurement highly
competitive procedures including international bidding.

         (iii) Greater accountability of the use of public resourcesfor some medical supplies:
Inorder to improve the planningofpurchasing ofmedicines and other medical supplies such as
nutritional supplements, vector control materials and laboratory reagents for laboratory tests, the
Government developed new information and monitoring systems. The transparency and
accountability requirements o f international financing provided the example and the incentives
to implement better control o f stocks, medical supplies planning and distributional logistics.

        The Federal Government outsourced to the private sector the logistics and distribution
o f basic medicines and some other medical supplies to public facilities at the provincial and
municipal level nationwide. This introduced state o f the art data systems that permit new and


                                                124

better controls o f the prescription practices, o f compliance with public sector standards and
norms, as well as o f the efficacy o f therapeutic approaches. The savings generated by reducing
expired stock o f medicines and other medical supplies losses provided the incentive for the
Government o f the province o f Buenos Aires to replicate the outsourcing o f medicines and other
medical supplies to the private sector.

Furthermore the Federal Government introduced across the country and consolidated the
practice o f prescriptions usingthe "generics".

         (iv) Strengthening of the stewardship of the federal MSN and improvement of the
organizational and institutional framework for coordination betweenfederal and provincial
Governments in the health sector: The crisis and the emergency response o f the Government
created a window o f opportunity for opening the dialogue between federal, the provincial and
local governments and the civil society to lay out the basis o f mechanisms to build consensus
regarding the priorities and policies that urgently needed to be implemented.

         The dialogue and practice o f consensus building forged during the worse months o f the
crisis led to a more permanent change in the relationship between the Federal Government and
the Provinces with a large impact in mechanisms for health policy decision making and the
implementation o f nationwide interventions. The agreements reached in March o f 2003 known
as the "Acuerdo Sanitario Federal de Sun Nicolas de 10s Arroyos" became a landmark o f the
processes o f consolidation and strengthening o f the partnership between the Federal and
Provincial Governments triggered by the crisis. The San Nicolas Agreements created the space
for the development o f new policy instruments such as a new Federal Health Plan for 2004 -
2007.
