                                      I.INTRODUCTION

1.      The Decentralization and Competitiveness Development Policy Loan (DCDPL) i s the
third and last of a series of programmatic development policy loans supporting Peru's reform
agenda under the current administration. When it took office in 2002, this administration made
decentralization and competitiveness two key priorities to secure sustainable and broad-based
growth and alleviate poverty. The Toledo Government started programs in those two areas in
2002, which the World Bank was asked to support through programmatic loans.
2.      The main achievements of the program have been to preserve fiscal discipline while
progressing with the decentralization agenda, and to promote increased competitiveness at the
national and regional level. Critical laws were passed to create regional governments, facilitate
fiscally sound decentralization, secure fiscal responsibility and transparency at the sub-national
level, strengthen local government capacity for revenue collection, establish accreditation criteria
for function and responsibilities devolution, introduce participatory local budgeting and initiate
public sector reform to accommodate to the demands of a newly decentralized setting. Budget
allocations have changed, reflecting the advances with decentralization.              Sub-national
governments were assigned 35 percent of non-financial expenditures in the 2006 draft budget, up
from 28 percent of total 2003 budget. Similarly, sub-national governments are playing a more
important role in public investment. The 2006 budget allocates more than half of public
investment resources to sub-national governments, compared to only 20 percent in2003.
3.       In parallel, the Government undertook significant legal, institutional and administrative
initiatives to enhance competitiveness. These include new laws to modernize the port sector, and
to facilitate science and technological development,     a national competitiveness plan and the
creation of the institution to implement it, measures to reduce administrative barriers and ensure
contract enforcement, programs to facilitate exports and to link small and medium enterprises
(SMEs) to supply chains, policies and actions to support infrastructure development and reduced
logistics costs-particularly      at the regional level-and      the creation of multiregional
competitiveness offices and centers for technological innovation. Most of those measures have
had a strong regional focus. Taken as a whole, these reforms are changing how Peru's public
sector operates and improvingopportunities and incentives for private sector development.
4.       These reforms have supported economic growth and, albeit with a lagged effect,
employment creation and poverty alleviation. Growth has remained strong and steady since
2002, averaging 4.8 percent per year during the period 2002-05. The strong export performance
has continued in 2005 and i s expected to increase by 35 percent over the previous year. This
export growth i s fueled not only by traditional commodities, but also by a rapidly-increasing
non-traditional export sector, which grew 33 percent in 2004 and another 29 percent in the first
half of 2005. The recent upswing in domestic demand has been supported by strong growth in
private investment, up 14 percent in real terms during the first half o f 2005. At the same time
fiscal discipline has remained a constant, in spite o f the spending pressures that have naturally
arisen during decentralization. The fiscal deficit is under control at around 1 percent of GDP.
Compared to the country's history o f low and erratic growth, the economy's performance during
the last four years i s a major accomplishment.
5.       The gains from a fiscally-sound decentralization and enhanced competitiveness are
becoming evident not only in the macroeconomic numbers, but also in the lives of ordinary



                                                 1

Peruvians, especially the poorest. The largest impact on poverty reduction has been felt in the
extreme poor group of housefiolds, in particular for those from rural areas. Between 2001 and
2004, extreme poverty fell 18 percent nationwide and 24 percent in the rural areas. Economic
growth in the regions has outpaced growth in Lima, a trend that should help close the large
income gap between Lima and the rest of the country. Employment growth is accelerating,
particularly in provinces. Urban employment has increased by 7 percent in the provinces other
than Lima during the first half of 2005, while national employment increased by 5 percent. The
number of exporting SMEs has increased by 30 percent and most of them are in the regions.
Moreover, during the period, near 10 percent of small enterprises grew into medium or large
enterprises. Thus not only i s growth becoming increasingly pro-poor, but it i s also being felt in
rural areas outside of Lima and other traditional economic centers.                         While this cannot be
attributed solely to the DCDPL program, these twin effects are exactly what the loans are
promoting. This sustainable and broad-based nature of Peru's growth offers an opportunity to
further alleviate poverty.
6.        Although the third DCDPL i s the last stage of the programmatic loans designed to
support decentralization and increase competitiveness (Box l),                        the Bank i s committed to
continue engaged with the GoP inthe implementation of the pending reform agenda as well as in
ensuring a smooth transition to the new administration in 2006. A multi-sectoral study focusing
on social sectors in a decentralized context that aims to provide conceptual support and dialogue
between the Bank and all levels of government in social sector. policies, called Rendici6n de
 Cuentaspara la Reforma Social (RECURSO), i s underway. Two technical assistance loans, for
capacity building in decentralization and decentralization of social sectors, are under
implementation. In addition, two pilot programs to assist local governments in the management
 and efficient use of natural resources income (mining canon and royalties) are being
implemented by International Finance Corporation (IFC) and the Extractive Industry
Transparency Initiative (EITI) in Cajamarca and Cuzco, respectively.

Box 1. Decentralizationand Competitiveness: Two Critical and Complementary Ingredients
          The two key challenges for Peru are to make the decentralization process work administratively and to
create the economic foundations for decentralized economic development through an improved investment climate,
better market access and the development o f internationally competitive industries. In order for regions to be the
engines of increased growth, employment and reduced poverty, an effective decentralization process must be
complemented with a regional and local competitiveness program. This i s the rationale for the subject choice of the
development policy program.

          Decentralization can be a powerful instrument to secure social, political and economic objectives. The
decentralization system will only be sustainable if based on economic realities, with regions and municipalities
building up their own growth potential through focused private sector development strategies. The ultimate success
of decentralization inPeru will be measured not so much by laws, procedures and processes, but rather by the extent
to which regional economic growth and employment are catalyzed. Regions must improve their competitiveness to
attract private investment and increase economic activity. Enhanced competitiveness requires: adequate and well
maintained infrastructure; low production and logistics costs; better access for regional products to domestic and
foreign markets; improved productivity in regional firms; a better mix of regional products; the adoption of quality
standards; greater technological capabilities and innovation; enhanced export assistance and trade facilitation; and
improved overall regional investment climates. Recent data indicates that economic growth of the past three years
has disproportionately benefited regions outside of Lima and other traditional economic centers, suggesting that
decentralization i s already bearing fruits for stimulating a broader-based platform for development inPeru.

7.      The Government has asked the Bank to increase the amount of the DCDPL I11loan, and
to continue supporting its reform agenda during 2006 with a new operation emphasizing public
sector reform. The Bank has reviewed Government progress not only in fulfilling the program
triggers for this loan, but also taking actions beyond what was called for inthe program to further
their decentralization and competitiveness reform agenda. Based on this review, the Bank
proposes that the financing amount for this operation be increased from the US$lOO million
planned in the Country Assistance Strategy Progress Report (CAS PR) discussed by the Bank
Board (December 7, 2004) to $150 million. This proposed increase fits within the total lending
envelope and exposure level envisaged under the high case lending scenario presented in the
CAS PR. The projected levels of IBRDexposure and debt service obligations to IBRDand other
creditors remain within the standards recommended by the Bank. The Bank will consider the
request for a follow-up policy-based operation, under the umbrella o f the new CAS to be
presented to the Bank Board in FY07. A series of policy notes planned for 2006 will help with
the design of this operation and will serve as an important tool during the transition to help
sustain policy continuity. The Bank has already started consultations with both civil society and
members of different political parties with the purpose of defining the priority areas of social and
economic development to be included inthese policy notes.
8.      This third loan i s co-financed with the KfW,the German Development Bank. As a result
of the Bank Donor Initiative for Decentralization established under the first programmatic loan
and the corresponding interest of the German Financial Cooperation to intensify its engagement
in supporting the Peruvian decentralization process, KfW has already co-financed the second
programmatic loan and plans to co-finance the proposed loan to the amount of 12 million Euros.
The co-financing arrangement serves as an important step to deepen donor coordination in Peru.
The Government will benefit by accessing additional resources, coordinated technical assistance
andreduced transaction costs through the use of common monitoring andreportingmechanisms.

        11. RECENTPOLITICAL,ECONOMICAND SOCIALDEVELOPMENTS

A. PoliticalContext

9.      President Alejandro Toledo has faced a difficult political context, although this has not
significantly affected the country's economic performance.               Political noise has centered on
corruption accusations against the President and members o f his party, but has never escalated to
the point where they have become a serious threat to governance. The Government lacks a
majority in Congress and the President's approval ratings are low. Cabinet turnover has been
high, with five Prime Ministers and five cabinets in the last four years. The last reshuffle
occurred in August 2005 due to political differences within the coalition.                      The political
environment has remained calm after the designation of Pedro Pablo Kuczynski (former Minister
of Economy and Finance) as Prime Minister.' Moreover, the appointment of Fernando Zavala,
former vice-minister of Economy for the last three years, as Minister o f Economy and Finance
was a signal for the continuation of sound fiscal management.
  10.   The focus of political actors in Peru currently i s on the upcoming 2006 elections. There
 are several potential candidates, but none has a clear advantage according to different nationwide

  11.     Social protests remain an issue in Peru, but they are generally isolated and limited to

  particularly inremote areas with limited state presence.

  B. EconomicDevelopments

  12.     Since the approval of the first programmatic loan, Peru's macroeconomic performance
  has been robust and main vulnerabilities have been reduced. Following the deceleration period
  between 1998 and 2001, growth rebounded to 4.9 percent in 2002, andhas remained steady since
  then. Gross Domestic Product (GDP) grew 4.0 percent in 2003,4.8 percent in 2004, and about 6
  percent in the first half of 2005 (Figure 1).GDP i s projected to be 5.5-6 percent for the whole
  year in 2005, in contrast to the projection of 4 percent at the time the second programmatic loan
  was prepared. Between 2001 and 2005, Peru's economy expanded by 21.1 percent inreal terms,
  compared to the Latin American average of 12.9 percent over the same period. Moreover, the
  economy i s experiencing diversified expansion on the supply side, with non-commodity
  industries, consumer-goods sectors and other areas growing robustly, along with traditional
  commodity production. Employment has begun to pick up (Figure 2). Urban employment has
  increased by 7 percent in the provinces other than Lima during the first half of 2005, while
  national employment increased by 5 percent.

14.     On the aggregate demand side, private investment and exports have been the two main
driving forces of growth in the last two years. Private investment, up 14 percent in real terms
during the first half of 2005, has remained strong, boosted by investment in the mining and
hydrocarbon sector, and lately by housing investment. Exports grew in 2004, aided by strong
international commodity prices, rising nearly 40 percent in dollar terms from the previous year to
reach a record US$12.6 billion. While Peru's exports are dominated by mining and other
traditional exports (about 70 percent of the total), non-traditional exports have also been vibrant,
increasing every month for the 38 consecutive months up to October 2005. The dollar value of
non-traditional exports grew b y 33 percent year-on-year in 2004, and another 29 percent during
the first half o f 2005 compared to the first six months of 2004. Peru posted a trade surplus of
US$2.8 billion in 2004, compared to US$835 million in 2003.                           The strong performance
continued during the first half o f 2005, as the trade balance came in at US$2.1 billion, almost
twice the surplus reached during the first half of 2004. The current account was in balance in
2004, an improvement from the 1.5 percent of GDP deficit in 2003, and official projections see
the current account showing a surplus o f 0.3 percent o f GDP in 2005, the first surplus in 16
years.
15.     Despite a small uptick in 2004, inflation has been at its lowest level in 40 years (Figure
3). Supported by the inflation-targeting framework adopted in 2002, inflation was at 2.5 percent
during 2003, and increased slightly to 3.5 percent in 2004. This figure compares favorably to
other countries in the region, such as Argentina (4.4 percent in 2004), Brazil (7.6 percent), and
Colombia (5.5 percent), although it i s slightly higher than Ecuador (2.7 percent) and Chile (2.5
percent). The currency has remained relatively stable thus far in 2005 (Figure 4), especially in
real terms.

19.     Following       the     economic
crisis of the late 1990s, Peru has enjoyed a period of sustained and, more recently, fairly strong
GDP growth. However, poverty reduction duringthe same period has been much more modest,
for three principal reasons. First, much of the growth during the first couple of years of the
economic recovery came from traditional commodity production, especially mining, which i s
capital intensive and thus has less direct impact on employment, wage and per capita income
levels. Second, after the investment boom of the early and mid 1990s, followed by the economic
downturn of the late 1990s, the economy had significant overcapacity that inhibited further
investment. And third, investment was also limited by the cautious attitudes of both domestic
and foreign investors, mistrustful of the sustainability of growth after so many boom-and-bust
episodes inthe past.
20.      However, since 2003 per capita incomes of the poor and the rates of poverty and extreme
poverty have started showing improvements, especially in rural areas, driven by broadening
economic growth. Average incomes of the poorest tenth of the population rose 33 percent
between 2001 and 2004, while the incomes of the wealthiest tenth of the population remained
level over the same period. Consumption shows a similar pattern: the poorest tenth of the
population saw their monthly average spendingincrease by 30 percent between 2001 and 2004,
while the wealthiest decile reduced consumption by 5 percent. Driven by this pro-poor shift in
income and spending, the consumption Gini improved from 0.44 to 0.41 between 2001 and 2004,
while the income Gini fell from 0.52 to 0.50 duringthe same period.
21.      Growth has had the most impact on extreme poverty rates, especially in rural areas (Table
1).Extreme poverty declined from 24 percent to 19 percent between 2001 and 2004 nationally,
butrural extreme poverty fell from 50 percent to 40 percent over the period, while urbanextreme
poverty declined from 10 percent to 8 percent. The results for poverty are somewhat similar,
though less dramatic (Table 2). At the national level, the poverty rate declined slightly from 54
percent to 52 percent. However, rural poverty declined from 77 percent to 72 percent between
2001 and 2004, while urban poverty fell from 42 percent to 40 percent.

22.     Thus, not only is growth becoming increasingly pro-poor, but it is also being felt inrural
areas outside of Lima and other traditional economic centers. These results, though still only
limited, give a sense that the patterns of income generation and distribution are beginning to
change in Peru as a result of several years of macroeconomic stability, a reform push toward a
more decentralized style of government, and steady economic growth that i s being felt beyond
the traditional products and geographic areas that have dominated economic activity in the past.
While this cannot be attributed solely to the DCDPL program, this is exactly the impact that the
loans are intendedto promote.
23.     Social indicators in Peru have improved significantly in recent years. In2000, the infant
mortality rate was 33 per thousand; 185 mothers died in every 100,000 births; 25 percent of
children were suffering from malnutrition; and only 51.3 percent o f children were at the age-
appropriate level in grade six. According to recent government surveys, infant mortality in 2004
was 23 per thousand, maternal mortality at 144 per 100,000 live births, and the number of
children at the age-appropriate level in grade six was 60 percent. Current numbers on child
malnutrition were not available. These improvements in social outcomes led the UNDP to
increase Peru's Human Development Index (HDI) ranking, from 85th out o f 177 countries in the
2002 HDIto 7gthout of 177 countries inthe 2003 HDI.

D.Economic OutlookandDebt Sustainability

24.     Peru's medium-term outlook i s favorable. Real GDP i s projected to grow at 5.5 percent
in2005, andbetween 4.5-5 percent in2006 and 2007. Peru's growth prospects couldbe affected
by the existing global imbalances and a potential deterioration of commodity prices in the near
future.    However, the growth estimates of 4 percent appear to be sufficiently robust to
accommodate the presence of adverse events. Large miningand hydrocarbon projects, as well as
infrastructure projects, will support growth in the coming years. The dynamism of the mining
sector will be sustained by a number of large-scale programmed investments in this sector. The
potential impact of the second phase of the Camisea natural gas project could be substantial.
Camisea I1 will attract investments for US$1.6 billion over a three-year period and when
operative it could radically alter the export profile by making Peru a major exporter of LNG and
end the country's dependence on imported oil. The pace of growth o f non-traditional exports
will face some risks as Andean Trade Promotion and DrugEradication Act (ATPDEA)6i s set to

25.      Peru's total public debt (external and domestic) has declined significantly inthe last three
years, from 48 percent of GDP in 2003 to an estimated 39 percent of GDP in 2005. Likewise,
external debt ratios have improved markedly. Total external debt to exports ratio has dropped
from 280 percent in 2003 to an estimated 160 percent in 2005. Public sector primary surpluses
and high economic growth rates, combined with active debt management strategy have
contributed to improving Peru's external debt position. In August of 2005, the Paris Club of
creditor nations agreed on a buyback proposal of US$1.6 billion. The prepayment operation
aimed to lower rollover and exchange rate risks. This operation lowers amortization payments by
about US$350 million a year up to 2009. The financing for this transaction was achieved
through international and domestic issuance of long-term sovereign bonds, helping deepen
domestic capital markets. While public external debt i s relatively high at 29 percent o f GDP,
public debt dynamics are unlikely to be a cause of concern in the coming years as shown in
Annex 7. Growth, fiscal consolidation, debt repayment and swaps will support a reduction of
total public debt to 34 percent o f GDP in 2008 (24 percent of GDP external and 10 percent of
GDPdome~tic).~

Projected external sources of financing for 2005 represent about 50 percent (US$2.2 billion) of
total needs, of which US$0.55 billion i s expected to be budget support loans from multilateral
organizations. The Bank plays an important role supporting the country's financing needs and
reform agenda through programmatic and investment lending operation as well as advisory
services.

                             111. THE GOVERNMENTPROGRAM

A. OverallProgram

27.    The Government has demonstrated considerable commitment to achieving its national
development goals, laid out in the Acuerdo Nacional (national accord)8 signed in July of 2002.
The accord reflects the common vision of the country's long-term development goals in a
number of areas and was prepared with the participation of political, religious and civil society
actors. For its short- and medium-term strategy, the Government prepared a Hoja de Ruta (road
map) 2004-06 which supports the National Accord goals with a set of specific policies. The road
map emphasizes the following main objectives related to the policy areas supported by this
development policy loan:

   0   Maintain prudent fiscal, monetary and exchange rate policies to promote growth.
   0   Complement growth with direct interventions to address inequality and poverty, focusing
       on excluded groups-particularly        indigenous people and Afro-Peruvians-and               at-risk
       groups such as youth and single mothers.
   0   Consolidate reforms in decentralization and social service delivery by buildingcapacity
       in sub-national governments and increasing transparency and oversight of public
        spending.
   0   Increase competitiveness by improving education quality, promoting public-private
       partnership investment in infrastructure, and reducing bureaucratic obstacles that hinder
       private sector investment.
       Deepen integration with the global economy by pursuing free trade agreements to
       maximize export opportunities and reduce risks through the diversification o f both export
       products and markets.
28.    The Government's economic program is supported by a SBA with the IMF, which the
authorities are treating as precautionary.'       The program for 2004-06 aims to consolidate
macroeconomic stability and strengthening the basis for sustainable growth, employment
creation and poverty reduction, while continuing to reducing vulnerabilities associated with
public debt and financial dollarization and increase priority social and infrastructure investment.
The Government has shown a good track record in implementing policies and reforms agreed
under this SBA, as evidenced in the two IMF Board reviews and the latest press release on the
Ih4F's October 2005 mission findings (See Annex 3).

                 IV. BANKSUPPORT TO THE GOVERNMENTPROGRAM

A. Linkto CountryAssistanceStrategyand Other BankOperations

33.     The central objective of the CAS i s to reduce poverty. It states three key mechanisms for
achieving that objective: (i) competitiveness and employment generation; (ii) equity and
                                                                                    greater
social justice, including access to health, education, culture and basic services; and (iii)   a more
efficient, transparent and decentralized state, with active private sector and civil society
participation.   The CAS and the CAS PR" both call for greater emphasis in the areas of
competitiveness and public sector management. Thus there i s a clear rationale for the DCDPL
program inpursuingthe CAS objectives.
34.     Inthe CAS PR, an overall lending envelope of US$615 million was envisaged for Peru
during the last two years of the Toledo administration. This included two planned Sector-Wide
Approach (SWAp) operations, one for US$50 million focused on increasing investment
resources for sub-national governments and the second for US$75 million to support the
decentralized management of public health services. After considering its needs and assessing
the different loan instruments available, the Government has requested that the resources from
the Sub-National Development Facility SWAP operation insteadbe folded into this third DCDPL
loan, increasing the loan amount above the originally-planned US$lOO million while still
remaining within the overall lending envelope for Peru and bank exposure level approved as part
of the CAS PR. The Government and the Bank considers the DCDPL a more appropriate
instrument for Peru's current needs, and one that fulfills many of the same goals of the planned
SWAp: increased quality of decentralized public service provision linked to improved results
monitoring andevaluation.
35.     The proposed third DCDPL loan fits into a series of Bank operations that support the
Government's decentralization and competitiveness reform program. The third DCDPL loan is
complemented by the Institutional Capacity for Sustainable Fiscal Decentralization-to         improve
fiscal planning, expenditure efficiency, and decentralized public investment-and            the Trade
Facilitation and Productivity Improvement Technical Assistance Loans (TALs)-to                  reduce
logistics costs and promote non-traditional exports, especially by SMEs.             In addition, this
operation is complemented by two technical assistance projects, one by the IFC in Cajamarca
and the second under the EITI in Cuzco, that aim to build capacity at the municipal level for
management of natural resource-based revenues and investments.
36.     The Bank also approved a Guarantees Facility Project in April 2005 that provides the
Government with a US$200 million envelope of guarantee funds to be used for infrastructure
project concessions. The facility will provide guarantee to backstop Government payments for
projects where the Government i s providing a financial contribution, and will strengthen the
environmental and social evaluation units and ensure that proper safeguards are in place. The
Facility closely complements the Government's regional competitiveness and development
agenda as it will be used to support critical regional infrastructure, including ports, regional
airports, roadnetworks and municipal water and sanitation infrastructure.

                              V. THE PROPOSEDOPERATION

A. Description

51.     The Government has developed a far-reaching decentralization and competitiveness
reform agenda. The Bank designed the program and the set of actions tailored to this agenda.
The aims of the Government's decentralization program are threefold: (i)protect fiscal
sustainability during Peru's transition to a more decentralized state; (ii)   enhance expenditure
efficiency and strengthen management and fiscal capacity at regional and local levels for the
progressive assumption of service responsibilities; and (iii)support national and regional
competitiveness by reducing logistics costs, promoting exports, and improving competitiveness
planning and quality management.




                                                 16

52.     The first DCDPL loan stressed fiscal sustainability, establishing a new fiscal
decentralization framework and ensuring a hard budget constraint alongside a series of related
competitiveness measures. The second DCDPL loan reflected an emphasis on supporting the
Government of Peru's competitiveness agenda. The loan also moved towards measures that
enhance efficiency and quality in service delivery. The thirdDCDPL loan aims consolidated and
strengthen many o f the reforms taken during the first two phases of the program, as well as
support further key reforms in the areas of decentralization and competitiveness, which are
addressedbelow.
Specific Objectives of the ThirdDCDPL
53.      This programmatic loan will reinforce guidingdecentralization principles that reconcile
fiscal neutrality with fiscal and financial incentives for the newly merged regions. The program
also strengthens public debt sustainability by supporting the regulation of decentralized
borrowing and debt reporting procedures for sub-national governments.               It supports the
implementation of a centralized (MEF-based) public expenditure evaluation system. The system
aims to effectively monitor, coordinate and assess public expenditure at all levels of government,
enhance the quality of budget decisions and ensure efficient and targeted service delivery. The
program also supports actions to smooth volatility of revenues for sub-national governments.
54.      Likewise, as the core institutions and legal framework for supporting competitiveness i s
already in place, the program has shifted towards strengthening institutions and initiatives at the
regional level. This loan seeks to improve the business environment and investment climate; and
assist the regions in developing capabilities to develop and implement competitiveness and
investment programs and to strengthen the regional business environment. This loan supports
efforts to facilitate increases in exports, particularly non-traditional ones, and to improve trade
facilities in the regions. Finally, the program supports actions to reduce logistics costs through
improved policy frameworks and service provision at ports and on the road networks, as well as
the facilitation of improved transportation services.

included in Annex 2, with triggers and indicators for the Government program to be supported

B. Results of Decentralizationand Competitiveness Development Policy Series

56.      Peru's decentralization and competitiveness enhancement program i s on course to
achieve program goals. The aim of this programmatic series regarding decentralization has
been to establish a conceptually coherent and fiscally sound foundation on which to build a
decentralized system of governance in Peru, a process that will take a number o f years. As the
experiences of other countries both in the region and around the world have shown, a poorly-
conceived or hastily executed decentralization can lead to major fiscal imbalances as well as a
confusion of functions and, consequently, a deterioration o f the quality and quantity of public
service delivery. The DCDPL series has supported the Government's efforts to proceed in a
cautious and fiscally sound manner, with a focus on building capacity before transferring
responsibilities to the sub-national level. Thus, while Peru has more to do to establish an
effective and efficient decentralized state, this loan series has helped ensure that its first steps in
that direction have been firm, and that the country has committed itself to see the process
through to completion.


                                                  17

57.     To date, many significant reforms in decentralization have been made with the support of
the DCDPL series. Regional governments, formerly appointed by the central government, are
now led by elected officials. Fiscal consolidation has been supported by an expansion of
revenues through tax policy and administration reform, especially at the regional and local
levels, and by a system of tax sharingthat reflects economic activity and poverty incidence. The
system of intergovernmental transfers has been fiscally neutral, ensuring adequate financing and
tax effort incentives at each level of government. The framework policy for decentralization,
including fiscal decentralization (covering revenues, assets, personnel, fiscal transfers, revenue
sharing, and accreditation process of sub-national governments) i s in place. This framework
ensures transparency and fiscal responsibility for both national and sub-national governments,
including debt policy for sub-nationals. As well, systems for budget preparation and execution
and investment planning are being implemented in local governments throughout the country.
58.      In parallel, significant legal, institutional and administrative initiatives were undertaken
to enhance competitiveness with ample support by the private sector. As with decentralization,
improving Peru's competitiveness will require years of effort. The Bank's support has been
instrumental in shaping the Government's overall strategy for moving reforms forward. One of
the most important steps taken under the DCDPL series was the creation and operation o f the
National Competitiveness Council and the development and approval of the National
Competitiveness Plan, with the input of government, private sector, and civil society. This will
provide a coherent framework and an executive agency to ensure continuity and progress on the
overall competitiveness reform agenda.
59.      Beyond the institutional framework, the DCDPLseries has supported a number of reform
actions to improve the climate for investment and business activity. These include new laws to
modernize the port sector and to facilitate science and technological development, a national
competitiveness plan and the creation of the institution to implement it, measures to reduce
administrative barriers and ensure contract enforcement, programs to facilitate exports and to
link SMEs to supply chains, policies and actions             to support infrastructure development
(particularly at the regional level) and reduction of logistic costs, the creation of multiregional
offices to promote competitiveness, and of centers for technological innovation and programs to
support norms, standards and quality awareness. Most of those measures have had a strong
regional focus. The institutional framework for promotion of private investment i s in place,
providing incentives for investment at sub-national levels.

volatile transfer and works towards the design of a more fair system of intergovernmental

79.     With regard to sectoral transfers, the Government has begunto implement the transfer of
expenditure responsibilities with a commensurate transfer in resources.          The social sectors,
which constitute the entry point to decentralization, have performed costing exercises to
determine the amounts of resources to deliver services in sub-national governments. Resources
are transferred from sector ministries to municipalities that fulfill certain requirements verified in
the accreditation process (addressed in a separate section of this program document). Funds are
ear-marked for these specific services. This proceeding has been one of they key elements for
Peru safeguarding fiscal neutrality.
80.     The strategy of the administration was to slow down the transfer o f responsibilities and
resources to departmental or local governments while the central government does more careful
sector and budget planning and better management and control systems are introduced at sub-
national levels.    Though this strategy might create political pressures from Congress and
departmental governments that wish to accelerate the pace of decentralization, it was the only
way to protect the quality o f services while at the same time observing fiscal neutrality. A few
responsibilities have been transferred already, primarily special social protection programs that
were being financed through earmarked sources, and some infrastructure projects that could be
easily identified inthe budget.

1.3 REGIONALANDLOCAL        BORROWING

84.     The Government has put in place a framework that ensures sub-national fiscal discipline
and budget constraints (See Annex 9, Fiscal Rules). The legal and institutional framework for
sub-national borrowing i s well advanced, with a number of regulatory and legal actions against
potential excessive borrowing by local and regional governments, including fiscal rules on
outstanding debt, debt services, primary surplus and annual expenditure increases in place. In
addition, the requirement that all debt operations above a given threshold need a credit rating of
the sub-national government was included in the general law on public sector borrowing,
reducing moral hazard problems and demands for bailouts by the central government.
85.     Program Triggers: Government establishes the legal framework which includes (i)
uniform debt concepts; (ii) limits to the amounts of guarantees, endorsements andor similar;
and (iii) the accounting registers of such guarantees, endorsements and trust finds created by
public sector entities; and the presentation of a proposal for a regulation of sanctions. The
General Law on Public Sector Borrowing recently approved, also incorporates uniform and



                                                  30

generally acceptable concepts of debt and liabilities, the introduction of annual limits on new
contingent liabilities, and a transparent registration of contingent liabilities and trust funds
created by public entities, as called for inthe program trigger. Inaddition, the MEFhas prepared
a by-law dealing with sanctions at the sub-national level and possible interventions by the
national authorities, that ex-ante will deal with delinquent behavior and a prompt correction. The
new laws will include a comprehensive definition for debt. Short-term debt would only be
excluded if it is related to operational distribution of functions within the Ministry. In an effort

A. Povertyand Social Impact

141.    The proposed loan would help reduce poverty by supporting the implementation of a
fiscally sustainable decentralization process and a competitiveness program to support economic
growth and improved service delivery, two essential components to reduce poverty and increase
the quality of life of Peruvian citizens, especially those living outside the Lima area. By moving
from a highly centralized system of government to a more decentralized system, Peru will
increase the effectiveness of fiscal resources, devolving spending authority to lower levels of
government that are more aware of local needs. This will allow resource expenditure to be more
tightly focused on addressing the varied needs different regions to improve the provision of
public services. At the same time, actions under the competitiveness component are aimed at
removing bottlenecks to economic development, thus improving the prospects for employment
and income generation. This proposed third DCDPL loan focuses on consolidating the gains
made under the first two phases of the program, thus ensuring the non-reversibility of reforms
duringthe transition to the next administration in2006.
142.    The implementation of DCDPL I11should result in an improvement in quality of living
for indigenous peoples and Afro-Peruvians, as the different actions in the policy matrix aim to
improve the conditions of all social groups, particularly those who are poorer and more
vulnerable. The monitoring and evaluation system supported by the DCDPL I11will help the
adoption of corrections in the delivery system to improve social inclusion. The program's
competitiveness component promotes a number o f incentives to increase economic opportunities

generation for indigenous peoples and Afro-Peruvians.

to the environment in Peru, intended to help shape the design of environmental policy. First, in
FY05 the Bank completed a detailed study on the environmental and social impacts of the
mining industry, offering specific recommendations to improve environmental oversight and
control, as well as improving community relations with mining companies and making the fiscal
income from mining more transparent. Second, the Bank put together a guidebook including
environmental and social principals that the Government should use in the design and
implementation of new infrastructure, as part of the Guarantee Facility approved in April 2005.
These guidelines clearly lay out Bank safeguard policy in the environmental and social areas.
And third, the Bank is preparing a comprehensive Country Environmental Assessment (CEA),
which i s expected to be completed before the end of FY06. The CEA will:

e   Identify the country's key environmental priorities that are linked to growth and poverty
    reduction;
e   Assess the adequacy of the institutional capacity to address priority environmental
    development concerns within the context of the Government's broader decentralization
    reforms; and
    Outline policy interventions to address environmental concerns that are both cost-effective
    and politically feasible for the Government at the national and sub-national levels.

E. RisksandRiskMitigation

154.    DCDPL I11i s not without risks. As the last stage of a four-year program, sustainability
becomes the most serious challenge. Political instability persists inPeru, and some of the policies
supported by this program may become the subject of sectoral pressures. The best protection
against sustainability risks i s ownership o f reforms by the Government. When government
officials have close involvement in the definition and implementation o f policies, they are much
more likely to support them when other sectors propose changes. In the case of Peru, ownership
has been relatively high, as policies and programs have been designed by the Government, with
the Bank in a supporting role. As in other reform programs, the commitment by authorities in
different areas has not been uniform, but even after many Cabinet changes, the Government has

continued to sustain the basic framework of DCDPL as the core of its fiscal decentralization and
competitiveness program, indicating that it will continue to do so after the DCDPL program i s
completed.
155.   The very low level of public approval for President Toledo i s another flag of attention
regarding program sustainability, all the more so now that he nears the end of his administration.
While it i s generally accepted that President Toledo will indeed finish out his term, it i s not clear
what ability he will have to move ahead with a reform program in the last part of his
administration. As has been the case throughout President Toledo's presidency, however, the
near-constant political turmoil has had little impact on the economy's good performance. And
while President Toledo has been forced to retreat in some areas of reform, his overall program
has moved ahead, and he has even been successful in closing the contentious and costly Ce`dula
Viva civil service pension plan. Opposition politicians can be expected to make as much hay as
possible against President Toledo, but they-and      the country as a whole-are     beginning to see
the long-term economic benefits of maintaining fiscal discipline and pushing ahead with
competitiveness reforms, and are increasingly loath to undermine that. Regional protests, most
especially those against mininginterests in the north and south of the country, could pose further
political difficulties, but these have been isolated to specific areas and specific issues, and show
no signs of becoming more generalized.
156.   There i s a risk that the next administration will reverse the course of decentralization and
competitiveness reforms andor return to more populist, unsustainable economic policies.
However, this risk i s increasingly remote. All the main expected presidential candidates are
promising to continue the broad outlines of economic policy established under the current
administration, despite their opposition to President Toledo in other areas. As well, while
divergences exist among different actors on the details of the decentralization process, broad
consensus exists on the overall thrust, and backtracking at this point would be difficult,
especially in the face of opposition by newly-empowered sub-national governments. The Bank
has been active in opening dialogue with the principal candidates, all of whom have expressed a
strong interest in working with the Bank should they win the presidency. The Bank is also
currently preparing a series of policy notes for Peru intended to offer the new administration
detailedpolicy proposals that can be usedto help design their plan of,government.
157.   The continuity of the decentralization and competitiveness agenda beyond the DCDPL
series i s supported, as noted in the section on Co-Financing Arrangements, Institutional
Coordination and Partnerships, by a number of other multi-lateral and bilateral cooperation
agencies, such as the IADB, USAID, and KfW, among others. As well, the Bank has already
begun discussions with current government officials as well as likely presidential contenders
about possible follow-up operations to support further reforms in these areas.
158.   The second principal risk faced by the program relates to macroeconomic stability. If
Peru's hard-won economic stability were to be reversed, this could have a negative impact on the
continuation of the reform program and on the country's ability to service its loan obligations.
The most serious threat to macroeconomic stability would come from a deterioration of the
currently favorable external economic conditions. A drop in the international prices of Peru's
main traditional exports, in particular minerals, which has powered a good portion of the
country's impressive export growth in recent years, would be a major blow to the export sector
and the economy as a whole. As well, the U.S. economy-the              largest market for Peruvian
exports-could slow down, reducing demand, and the ending o f preferential access to the U.S. in


                                                  50

2006 could hurt textile exports in particular. An increase in U.S. interest rates would impact the
ability o f both the Government as well as the economy as a whole to access credit on reasonable
terms.
159.     These external risks are being mitigated in a number of ways. Most importantly, while
non-traditional exports remain relatively low, they now comprise a significant portion of total
exports and are growing very rapidly, much faster than traditional exports.            Further, the
Government i s actively pursuingtrade agreements, with a focus on locking in preferential access
to the U.S. market through an Andean FTA, and also branching out in search of other export
markets in Europe, Asia, and elsewhere in Latin America. The combination of these two
trends-diversification   o f products and of markets-is  reducing the risk of major shifts in Peru's
terms of trade due to exogenous factors. Regarding interest rate increases, the Government's
disciplined fiscal performance in the past several years has greatly reduced country risk and
lowered interest rate spreads, meaning the cost of rising international interest rates will be lower
than previously. As well, the increasing liquidity of the local currency debt market for both
sovereign and corporate debt-supported       by a stable currency and low inflation-is   easing the
country's dependence on external credit.
160.     The most significant internal macroeconomic risks relate to increased pressure to relax
fiscal discipline. Political pressure exists for two principal reasons: (i) the unusually high tax
revenues o f 2004 and 2005 are generating calls to channel additional resources into the budget;
and (ii)  more importantly, the upcoming 2006 elections are fueling a clamor from different
political parties-including   the president's-to  increase public spending. This risk i s mitigated
in several ways. First,the Fiscal Responsibility and Transparency Law limits the level of fiscal
deficit. Second, the Toledo administration's track record on fiscal discipline has been excellent,
with deficits dropping every year since the president took office. The current Prime Minister,
Pedro Pablo Kuczynski, has demonstrated himself to be rigorous in holding the line against
excessive spending. H i s government plan between mid-2005 and the next administration, with
the same fiscal path, was approved by Congress with 60 votes infavor and only six against.
161.     Internal financial risks exist as well, particularly Peru's relatively high level o f public
debt. The country's public debt i s on a declining path, projected to fall to about 39 percent of
GDP at the end of 2005, down from 45 percent at end-2004. The pre-paymentof a significant
portion of Paris Club debt in 2005 and the continued good fiscal performance have combined to
see spreads on Peruvian sovereign debt fall to historically low levels, and the main rating
agencies are close to moving the country's debt to investment grade. The country's international
reserves were at US$15.4 billion in August 2005, up from US$8.6 billion in2001.
162.     Peru's banks have a very high ratio of dollar liabilities, while most of their assets are in
local currency, which could become a problem in the event of a banking sector crisis or a
currency devaluation. However, dollarization o f the banking system i s declining: between
December 2003 and September 2005, the dollarization coefficient for the Peruvian banking
system dropped from 62 percent to 54 percent. The Central Bank's growing international
currency reserves now amount to roughly double the country's money supply, significantly
decreasing the risk of a crisis due to dollarization. The continued expansion of the government
bond market in nuevos soles has contributed to lower financial dollarization. Furthermore, the
new regulation on credit risks in dollar credits to uncovered borrowers, unique inLatin America,
i s a first step to make banks internalize the credit risk associated with borrowers' currency
mismatches.

       floating and other short term debts, as well as future income securitization operations.

       IMFExecutiveBoard Completes Second ReviewUnder Peru's Stand-By
                                       Arrangement
The Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) has completed the
second review of Peru's economic performance under a 26-month Stand-By Arrangement
(see Press Release No. 04/112). The completion of the review makes available a total
amount equivalent to SDR 149.1 million (about US$219.8 million), but the Peruvian
authorities intendto continue treating the arrangement as precautionary.

Incompleting the review, the Executive Board also approved Peru's request for a waiver
of the nonobservance of a performance criterion and the modification and resetting of
another performance criterion. In addition, the Board also approved a rephasing of the
country's future disbursements into five equal amounts of SDR 27.6 million (about
US$40.7 million).

Following the Executive Board discussion, of Peru on June 8, 2005, Mr. Agustin
Carstens, Deputy Managing Director and Acting chair, said:

"Peru's economic performance has continued to be strong in 2004 and the first half of
2005. Inflationremains low and official reserves are at a comfortable level. The outlook
i s positive, as the economy is expected to continue to benefit from the global recovery. In
addition, Peru has built important buffers to mitigate vulnerabilities associated with
public debt and financial dollarization. Inthe context of the national elections scheduled
for early 2006, it will be important to maintain the consensus for policies aimed at
promoting sustained growth andreducing poverty over the medium-term.

"Prudent fiscal management is at the core of the authorities' economic strategy, and fiscal
policies are consistent with further consolidation in 2005. Given that part of the revenue
increase projected for 2005 i s expected to be temporary, the authorities need to avoid
boosting expenditures on a permanent basis. They are also encouraged to keep
expenditure in the supplementary budget to a minimum, and to use part o f any excess tax
revenue to reduce the fiscal deficit below program levels. This will help reduce Peru's
public debt-to-GDP ratio and further bolster investor sentiment. The authorities are
committedto strengthening the institutional framework to ensure efficient and transparent
use of scarce resources for public investment. In this context it is important that
procedures to identify projects eligible under the Private-Public Partnerships program be
strictly observed.

"The central bank remains committed to keeping inflation low. Allowing the exchange
rate to move more freely will be important to help insulate the economy against external
shocks and speculative capital flows. It i s also important that the central bank continues



                                               96

to gradually raise the limit on pension funds' investments abroad and further strengthen
domestic capital markets in order to help diversify the risks of these funds.

"The authorities are making progress in increasing the resilience and efficiency of the
financial sector. They are strengthening prudential regulations on lending to unhedged
economic agents and improving the supervision o f offshore bank subsidiaries. The
authorities also intend to discourage mortgage lending in foreign currency, and are
promoting greater efficiency in financial intermediation,           including through the
development of the domestic capital market. It will be important that the authorities
strictly observe the limit on consumer lending by the state-owned bank, Banco de la
Nacio'n.

                  Annex 7. PeruPublic Sector Debt and Sustainability

Debt Management and Debt Market Development. Peru is actively pursuing sound
and strategic debt management. Substantive progress has been achieved over the last
four years in the management of Peru's public debt. Cre`dito Pziblico's organizational
structure and division of responsibilities are in line with international sound practice.
Institutional capacity required to plan and start implementing an ambitious program to
develop the domestic debt market i s being built. And a strategy to increase the share of
domestic debt in soles, lengthen the maturities of fixed rate debt, and continue to develop
the markets for their international bonds i s being designedimplemented. To manage
refinancing risks, the Peruvian authorities are implementing a strategy centered in
developing a market for government securities as a means of mitigating the risk posed by
the sheer size of the external debt and overall exposure to the fluctuation of the exchange
rate. In addition, there i s an effort to continue to develop the markets for their
international bonds.

Despite these accomplishments, there i s more to be done in both debt management and
debt market development. Debt management strategy could be improved by the
introduction o f a systematic analysis of cost and risk. The debt simulation model would
benefit from a tool for more informed decisions in terms of cost-risk trade offs. On debt
market development, the Central Bank issues its own CDs with maturities of up to four
years. This i s a potential problem for consolidating a strategy covering the whole yield
curve and might fragment the small domestic market. Shortfalls in regulations and the
Delivery Versus Payment settlement could hamper the development of repos market,
which are key for providing liquidity to the secondary market. The Peruvian authorities
are working towards addressing these issues, for which the Bank i s providing technical
assistance.

Debt Sustainability Analysis, In 2005, Peru made significant progress in reducing its
public debt ratio from 45 percent of GDP in 2004 to 39 percent. Of its total debt,
domestic debt accounts for almost 10 percent of GDP, or 25 percent of total debt. Of its
external debt, about 35 percent i s multilateral, 26 percent i s bilateral, and 35 percent
international bonds. Moreover, the composition of domestic debt has improved to reduce
vulnerability to exchange rate shocks. In 2000, US dollar-denominated debt accounted
for over 80 percent of the domestic debt, by 2004 this percent had fallen to under 60
percent.
With its latest three debt issues Peru raised $1.535 billion, virtually wrapping the early
repayment debt to the Paris Club. Peru issued a 20-year $750 million Eurobond at
favorable terms on July 14th 2005. Similarly, Peru sold $785 million in 15-year domestic
paper. This liability management operation would generate NPV gains for Peru and
reduce the stock o f Paris Club debt from US$8 billion to US$6 billion. The most positive
feature of the Paris Club buyback operation i s that it would reduce Peru's external market
borrowing requirements-which in recent years have averaged US$l billion-by around
US$400 million per year for the next five years.
Declining public external debt and prudent and strategic debt management combined
with continued good economic performance are placing Peru's sovereign rating closer to


                                            104

investment grade, leading to upgrades from several rating agencies-including              the recent
Standard and Poor's upgrading to BB in June 2004. The Government o f Peru i s actively
pursuingdebt management strategy, lengtheningmaturities and hedging against currency
and interest rate risks.              The authorities have continued to reduce currency exposure
through increased government placements in local currency at lengthened maturity, along
with swap transactions to increase the local currency composition of existing debt. In
addition, in August o f 2005 the government completed the first interest rate-hedging
operation, covering all PDI Brady bonds that will carry a variable LIBOR-based rate in
2007. However, in comparison to other investment grade countries, Peru's high ratio of
external debt to current account (194 percent); high government debt compared to fiscal
revenues; less monetary flexibility due to high, though declining, dollarization; a more
narrow economy and a volatile political environment constrain the country's sovereign
rating. Still, the country risk, calculated usingthe by JP Morgan's EMBI              +index spread
over the U S Treasury bonds for Peruvian bonds rating, has dropped to a historic low of
1.5 percentage points over U.S. Treasuries by August 2005, down from 6.44 percentage
points when PresidentToledo took office.

for the 2005-2012 time period.                  Overall the exercise highlights that Peru's notable
progress in debt management, particularly in the past year, which has seen its debt ratio
decline by nearly 6 percent, has improved the country's public debt dynamics and
improved its resilience to economic shocks.                 In sum, Peru's public debt outlook is
sustainable, even under negative shock scenarios for the underlying macroeconomic
variables.

ANNEX 11. Progress on Participatory Budgeting, Civil Service Reform, and Public
Investment

1.PARTICIPATORY     BUDGETING
One of the key goals of Peru's approach to decentralization i s to make public policy more
inclusive and responsive to citizen's needs. It was recognized early on in the process that
decentralizationwould fail if it was not appropriated by the population at large, and more
specifically, the tax payers and civil society. The country set up a process by which
citizens can participate in the formulation and execution of local budgets. Peru follows
other countries, such as Brazil, in setting up a participatory budget process for the
allocation of resources. This will not only benefit local democracy and legitimacy, but
also allocative efficiency as local governments better cater to citizen's needs.
Peru has advanced considerably in setting up a participatory budgeting system
(presupuesto participativo) at local levels.    The legal framework has been adopted,
specifying the steps to prepare and implement the participatory budgets. Currently, all
regions and more than half of municipalities larger than 40,000 inhabitants-some       100
provincial municipalities in total-have      prepared the 2005 budget with participatory
budget rules. Likewise, 33 civic auditing cornmittees (Comite`sde Vigilancia) created by
participatory budgeting are tracking expenditure, procurement, and targeting of
decentralized food programs.      All provincial municipalities have set up procurement
committees.
The Government recognized that the role of Regional and Local Coordination Councils
needed to be strengthened in order for the participatory budgeting process to be organized
and implemented effectively.      This heightened role should not only refer to budget
preparation but also to reporting and monitoring of executed budgets.           The recently
published "Znstructivo MEF Presupuesto Participativo 2006" provides the corresponding
legal framework for the Regional and Local Coordination Council to play a stronger role
as it becomes the responsible agency for organizing the budget process.
The main challenge remaining for the participatory budget process from a fiscal point of
view i s how to reconcile accountability with more allocative efficiency.        The use of
participatory rules only for investment, and not current expenditures, is a limitation.
Discussion in participatory budget should be centered on how public services can be
improved, instead of predetermining the use of certain production factors. Recurrent
allocations should be reviewed also, in order to deepen budget debate and discuss
expenditure trade-offs. In addition, it i s important to ensure that participatory budgeting
does not create a fragmentation of expenditures in a highnumber of small-scale projects.
Other shortcomings that should be addressed include: coordinate budget planning with
other participatory mechanisms, including the mesas de pobreza; improve the
representation so that the budget process represents stronger groups from rural areas and
women; expand the participatory budgeting process beyond budget formulation to
include other phases of the investment cycle; strengthening the role of vigilance
committees and providing them with relevant information and methodological skills to
perform their job; encourage strategic debate with technical teams to avoid potential
investment fragmentation;       expand monitoring scope and capacity o f vigilance

committees beyond investment tracking to include all public expenditures; and bring
budget dialogue down to the communities using local media and versatile consultation
technologies.

2. CIVIL SERVICE REFORM.

The past fifteen years witnessed a severe deterioration of Peru's civil service with deep
implications for the fiscal accounts and the costs of the pension regime. The fiscal
burden of the public labor force i s very high and unsustainable. Wages in the social
sectors increased to 3.2 percent of GDP in 2004, from 2.7 percent in 2001, at the expense
of outlays in goods and services and in fixed investments, which dropped by 0.2 and 0.4
percentage points of GDP, respectively, during the same period. There i s an entrenched
disregard for the provisions of the existing civil service law and its associated regulations.
Civil servants are employed under a variety of legal regimes with a wide dispersion in
salaries for similar tasks. Until recent reform efforts (most particularly the closure of the
Ce'dula Viva public employee pension program in late 2004), the existing formal salary
structure and pensions had not been revised in over a decade, so base salaries were
increased through a variety of supplements and benefits in cash and in kind. Since the
ban on hiring into permanent positions more than a decade ago, institutions have resorted
to hiring on a contractual basis, a process that i s not subject to any required procedures
for recruitment and selection, and has major fiscal implications. All these factors have
contributed to create a work force that i s poorly prepared, poorly trained, poorly
supervised, and consequently of very low productivity.
Reforming civil service management practices i s of immediate relevance for the
decentralization agenda.         For a decentralized service delivery system to function
properly, the costs for service delivery must be transparent so that each level of
government i s aware o f the implied fiscal burden.                  Current management practices,
however, suggest that this principle i s not applied. Neither the central, regional nor
municipal governments knows, for instance, what the cost i s for delivering primary health
care, or secondary education, and so forth. Clearly separating accounts for different
services i s key for decentralization to achieve the expected efficiency gains and stimulate
accountability.
The Government has selected several entry points to reforming civil service. A central
one i s pension system, particularly with regards to the pay-as-you-go system Ce'dula Viva
which i s being phased out, following the program's definitive closure in late 2004.
Several reforms have been implemented that aim at scaling down the benefits provided
by the pension system. Interms of the retired pension, pensioners leveling payment with
active workers have been eliminated. There are also upper limits for pensions, and
pensions presently above the limit will be gradually adjusted. Inreturn, there will also be
minimum pension levels*'.          Other limits that have been introduced refer to survival
pension2' which i s less onerous. With regards to the military and police pensions, the

Another entry point to civil service reform was payroll management with the goal to

    Produce, systematize, and disseminate centralized information on personnel numbers
    and types of contracts, salaries and bonuses, and regularization of informal health
    workers (among others), before sector (expenditure) decentralization takes place.
    Clear the significant levels of arrears in contributions, particularly in the public
    sector; define a new taxing scheme for pension contributions. Make the system more
    flexible so as to allow AFPs to manage different funds with different mix of risk and
    profitability.

For many years, the public investment system worked within a centralized planning
scheme. It was then dismantled during the Fujimori administration, and only recently
been reintroduced. Initially sub-national governments were explicitly excluded from the
public investment law.          Then a directive allowing an exception for a group of
municipalities was passed. As a result, only 67 municipalities-mainly                      the largest
provincial and district municipalities of Lima with greater financial resources-were
incorporated to the National Public Investment System (SNIP). At the end o f June 2005,
only 190out of roughly 2000 local and provincial municipalities were part of the system:
67 had statutory membership, 99 joined voluntarily, and 24 were obliged to participate
due to the large size of their projects.    '

One area that the Government needs to improve to increase the quality of public
investment i s strategic planning. Inpractice, the budgetary process predominates over the
planning process and the strategic analysis of investments. Peru undertakes limited
strategic  analyses for decisions           on committing intersectoral, interregional, or
intergovernmental investment funds.21 The multi-year budget planning office in the
Ministry of Finance (DGPM) is not responsible for prioritizing projects that are
considered viable, so in practice the availability of cash flow determines the availability
of investment.
Capacity needs to be increased with regards to investment planning and execution. This
applies both to the sectors as well as sub-national governments. In 2004, the average
conformity achieved by the quality of the pre-investment studies in the sectors was
equivalent to 57 percent; and it is only 38 percent for the regions. In 2004 the regional
governments used only about 75 percent of their investment budgets.
Investments are fragmented, in part due to the recent decentralization of the investment
planning cycle. In 2003, the DGPM started to delegate the authority to certify the
viability of all projects involving investments less than or equal to S/. 6 million
(economic infrastructure) or S/. 4 million (social investment) to regional and local
governments.        Sub-national governments are still reluctant to work with a central
institution such as SNIP.           So in practice, these thresholds create incentives for
fragmenting investments.
Investment monitoring also needs to be improved. Responsibility to discontinue projects
rests completely with the upper hierarchy at the ministries and with the heads of regional
and local governments. But no norms were introduced to explicitly monitor projects with
low profitability and a long implementation period that had been executed prior to the
entry into effect of the SNIP law.

I.BackgroundandTradePerformance
Market-oriented economic policies adopted since 1990 have yielded a substantial
expansion of external trade, more than tripling exports in absolute terms between 1990
and 2004. Export growth has been particularly impressive since 2001, resulting in a
compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 21 percent between 2001 and 2004. In
comparison, the CAGR for 1990-2001 was 7 percent.22Robust growth has continued in
2005, as exports increased 36 percent during January to June compared to the same
period of 2004 (see Table 1).The number of S M E exporters has gone from 983 in 2001
to 1,292 in 2004, an increase of over 30 percent. Furthermore, 9 percent of these SMEs
became medium and large enterprise^.^^ However, despite all of these facts, there i s much
room for improvement in the Peruvian export sector.

percent higher than in 2003, they represent a low percentage of GDP (around 18 percent
in 2004) (see Figure l).24 is particularly striking when compared to other

Products: Traditional versus Non-Traditional
Despite the recent impressive trade expansion, the bulk o f Peru's exports are
concentrated in primary products (see Table 1). These traditional goods include mining,
fish meal, agricultural, and oil products and account for about 71 percent of all exports.
Mining comprises by far the largest share, at 55 percent of total exports. Although
traditional exports have enjoyed healthy growth rates r e c e n t l y 4 0 percent in 2003-
2004-they       tend to have less long-term growth potential than non-traditional products
and are highly dependent on volatile commodity markets. Further evidence of export
concentration i s shown by low levels of manufacturing exports,26which represented only
20 percent of manufacturing value-added in Peru in 2003. In contrast, manufacturing
exports in the Latin America and Caribbean region were 76 percent o f manufacturing
~alue-added.~~

A wealth of empirical experience points to the importance of diversifying a country's
export base to mitigate the impact of international price movements and increase value-
added levels where possible. Research emphasizes the negative correlation between
export concentration and economic growth, and the importance of diversification and
intra-industry trade on growth.28 The diversification of Peru's exports away from
traditional products i s a high priority. However, empirical measures of export
concentration show mixedresults over the last two decades.

Despite the opportunities for small farmers to engage in non-traditional export farming,
there are some challenges ahead. Global food markets are shifting toward more stringent
product and process standards. Growing concerns about food safety have made product
traceability and controlling the supply chain `from farm to shelf` a vital requirement in
highersegments of the market. This has ledto a concern that smallholder growers will be
marginalized by high quality control costs. In other words, increasing standards imposed
by food importers on premium suppliers would make it difficult for suppliers to afford
the necessary training and oversight for a large number of small growers. Working with
 smallholder farmers i s difficult for trading and processing companies. Quantities of
products are small and heterogeneous in quality, supply can be haphazard, and bulking-
up of volume into a steady stream of products of constant quality is difficult to achieve.
 Smallholders also face constraints in terms o f lack of knowledge o f modern markets,
technologies and inputs, and difficult access to capital which prevents them from
upgrading their operations. These factors constitute a serious constraint to supplying
high-end modem supply chains.30The key challenge for policy makers will be creating a
business environment in which small farmers can overcome financial and bureaucratic
barriers to formalization and growth and surmount technical barriers to upgrading their
 production standards.

are relatively but not excessively concentrated, with the United States and the European

Peruvian firms export a relatively small amount of their production. Data comes from
Peru's recent Investment Climate As~essment~~,which surveyed manufacturing
industries such as food products, textiles, garments, furniture and wood products where
Peru might be expected to take advantage of its rich natural resource base and have a
comparative advantage in export markets. Export firms were over sampled to better
understand the constraints to exports in Peru. Yet, in the sample only 21.6 percent of
sales were exported, which i s low compared to surveys with similar samples in other
countries. For example in Malaysia and Bangladesh, firms exported over 30 percent of
their production on average. It i s clear from the survey results that there are a few firms
that concentrate almost exclusively on exports. In fact, nearly half of the exporters in the
sample export less than 20 percent of sales and only 31 percent of the exporters (42
firms) in the sample export 50 percent or more o f their sales. Food products i s the one
outlier in this respect, with 86 percent of the sampled firms exporting 99 percent of
production at the median. There i s a clear focus on export among these firms. In a few
industries, such as garments and textiles, there are backward linkages to small and
medium sized firms who, by supplying exporting firms, become "secondary" exporters.
However the median Peruvian firm shows zero exports as a percent of sales
demonstrating the lack of focus on the export market.

11. Government Programsand Progress

Trade Agreements, Protection, and Regulation
The Peruvian foreign trade regime has been traditionally very protective against imports.
The new economic program implemented after 1990included a series o f positive reforms
of the foreign trade regimeincluding a significant reduction of foreign exchange controls.
As a result, the average tariff fell from 66 percent in 1990 to under 20 percent over the
next two years. Since then, average tariffs have declined slowly, reaching 13.5 percent in
2000, 10.9 percent in 2002, and 10.2 percent in 2004.38 Thanks to these changes, the
average nominal tariff level in Peru i s close to average protection in Latin American and
middle income countries. Several agricultural products still have a highrate o f protection
of up to 25 percent as a result of established additional duties (corn, powdered milk, rice,
sugar), although the high level for these products i s relatively common internationally.
Agricultural, textile and clothing imports are the most protected items, with average
tariffs close to 20 per~ent.~'

Peru's tariff structure was changed 11times between 2001 and 2004, affecting the duties
on imported inputs, sugar products, dairy, and some machinery imports. This had a large
impact on tariff deviation, which doubled from the low level of 3 percent in 2001 to 6.2
currently. Effective protection i s higher in lower value-added products like dairy,
footwear, apparel, leather, sugar, and textiles. Antidumping and safeguard mechanisms
have been used regularly in Peru since they were instituted in 1993, especially in 2002
and 2003.

The Government has moved aggressively towards negotiating and signing bilateral and
multilateral agreements in recent years, with an emphasis upon concluding the Andean
Free Trade Agreement (FTA) with the U.S., Colombia and Ecuador. If the US-Andean
FTA comes into force, average tariffs will decline to 1.5 percent by 2014. Peru has also
progressed with the negotiation of bilateral trade agreements with Mexico, Singapore,
Thailand, and regions within the E.U. through the Andean Community (CAN). As o f July
2005, Peru provisionally became part of the European Union's Generalized System of
Preferences Plus, which allows 7,200 types o f products to enter the E.U. duty free.
Examples of products include asparagus, artichokes, carrots, turnips, grapes, olives,
strawberries, and figs. Peru also receives duty free access to U.S. markets for about
7,000 products through the Andean Trade Promotion and Drug Eradication Act
(ATPDEA), although it is set to expire at the endof 2006.

Several changes have also been made to customs laws to benefit exporters. In January
2005, a new customs law was passed (the Nuevo Reglarnento de la Ley General de
Aduanas) that drastically reduced and regularized penalties for infractions. Previously,
there was little connection between the spirit of the infraction and the amount of the
sanction. Improvements have also been made to the drawback regime, making it easier
for small and medium enterprises to understand and take advantage o f the system. In
2004, roughly $US 30 million was returnedthrough the drawback regime.

Institutional Reform
The institutional framework to facilitate trade has been reformed.       The Ministry of
Foreign Trade and Tourism (MINCETUR) was created in 2002 and the Ministries of
Industry and Fisheries were merged under a single Ministry of Production (PRODUCE).
Similarly, agencies engaged in market research, privatization, concessions and FDI
promotion were merged into PROINVERSION. The Commission for the Promotion of
Peruvian Exports (PROMF'EX) within MINCETUR was strengthened and its functions
were enhanced to better coordinate the different institutions engaged in export promotion
and trade facilitation.

NationalStrategic Export Plan
In March 2003 the Government launched the National Strategic Export Plan (PENX),
formulated through consultations with public and private institutions involved in trade
promotion. The plan, to be implemented between 2003 and 2013, contains 20 distinct
export promotion strategies and 108 specific measures under four strategic pillars aimed
at: (i)developing a portfolio of high quality, value-added and diversified exportable
products; (ii) accessing targeted foreign markets; (iii)strengthening the legal framework
for trade promotion; and (iv) developing an export-oriented culture. A second phase of
the PENX i s currently underway with the objective of enhancing the export promotion
strategy. The Plan Maestro de Facilitacibn de Comercio Exterior was launched in April
2004 and focuses on operational strategies targeting specific sectors, regions, and final
markets. Key sectors included are textiles, jewelry, aquaculture and fisheries. The Plan
Maestro includes 152 different items, 23 percent of which had been implemented by
September 2005. Key items in the plan such as financing instruments and changes in
export laws are discussed below.

Financing Instruments
Access to financial markets i s a strong constraint for firms, particularly for SMEs. A
comprehensive-World Bank supported-sector adjustment program implemented by
Peruvian authorities between 1999 and December 2001 has led to positive structural
changes in Peruvian financial markets. Such changes have affected the financial sector,
which has experienced a substantial modernization of its regulatory framework, and the
banking system, which has improved its efficiency and liquidity. This, combined with
 entry of new players from Europe and the UnitedStates, has increased the potential of the
 domestic market to offer sophisticated export finance products and services.

 Notwithstanding this progress, the access to finance and to adequate export finance
 instruments has become more restrictive in recent years due to a generalized credit



                                           130

crunch in the economy. For example, domestic credit to the private sector fell from 29
percent o f GDP in 1999 to only 19 percent in 2004.40Not surprisingly, results from
Peru's Investment Climate Assessment indicate that limited access to adequate export
finance instruments i s a major problem of Peruvian exporters. This finding i s supported
by discussions with financial institutions and exporters, all confirming that only a few
mainly large exporters have access to working capital loans from the banking sector, and
even less to structured export finance products such as export insurance, international
leasing and others.

Several institutional deficiencies have been identified that contribute to this situation,
such as the lack of efficient mechanisms for insolvent companies to exit the market, or
the inflexible and deficient legal and judicial framework for executing and realizing
collateral. But there are also specific constraints for small and medium exporters to
secure credit that are related to deficiencies characterizing their productive system. For
instance, the fragmented structure of production and the lack o f integration of supply
chains make access to credit markets costly and difficult. Additionally, the low level of
"formalization" of SMEs in terms of accounting practices, management and corporate
governance structure, and quality standards prevents them from making use o f the
financial instrumentsnecessary to develop stable export activities.

To help address these deficiencies, new export-oriented financial instruments have been
launched by the Government targeting small and medium-sized firms. The Credit
Insurance for Exporters initiative, for example, provides a US$50 million reserve fund
allocated by the MEF to COFIDE via SECREX to cover 50 percent of the value of pre-
export financing operations offered by commercial banks to export businesses with firm
purchase orders. Approximately US$168 million had been invested through the program
as of July 2005, allowing new exports for about 150 businesses. A similar financial
instrument, FIXr (Programa de Financiamiento Zntegral para el Sector Exportado) had
assigned US$212 million by November 2004 to operations of foreign trade, investment
and human capital. Banks have subsequently used these resources to finance small and
medium exporters. The Government has also created a new risk capital fund (Fondo
Transandino Ped) endowed with US$15 million to support small and medium export-
oriented firms. The fund completed its first investment o f US$550,000 in early 2005. The
Plan Maestro de Facilitacibn de Comercio also contains other financing instruments that
are currently under development, such as lines o f credit for buyers of Peruvian exports.

111.MovingForward

Peruhas seen tremendous export growth inrecent years. However, much work remains to
be done if the country i s going to match the export levels (as a share of GDP) of its
neighbors and increase prosperity levels outside of Lima. Specifically, efforts should
proceed in the following areas: 1) coordination of export promotion and information
sharing; 2) improvements to the customs regime; 3) access to finance; and 4) export
diversification.

Coordination of export promotion
Within the government there are three major public institutions attached to different
Ministries performing similar export promotion activities that show a pronouncedlack of
coordination. These include the Commission for the Promotion o f Peruvian exports
(PROMPEX) under MINCETUR, the National Board of Trade under MINCETUR and
the Executive Office o f Economic Promotion under the Minister o f Foreign Affairs.
Additionally, PROMpyme, within the Ministry of Labor, aims to integrate small and
medium producers into the main supply and export chains. The duplication of roles and
functions and the lack of a clearly articulated country strategy on trade policy, export
promotion and foreign investment weaken Peru's export capacity and create unnecessary
costs for firms. Peru should consider consolidating its export promotion activities in a
single entity so that efforts are not duplicated and knowledge can be more easily shared
across different types o f products, firms, andregions.42

42 Private sector organizations could also potentially benefit from better coordination. Two entities are
particularly active-the  Association of Exporters (ADEX) and the Peruvian Association of ForeignTrade

activities, market analysis to identify business opportunities, provision of legal advisory services and
dissemination events. COMEXPERU, created in 1989, engages in similar activities, but it is primarily
comprised of larger firms that did not feel their interests were appropriately represented by ADEX.

A complement to this consolidation i s an integrated information system that makes all
manner o f market, technical, and procedural information related to exporting available to
firms on an easy-to-use Web portal. Currently, this information i s neither centralized nor
readily available, although a group headed by MINCETUR i s considering such a system
(SIICEX). A good first step i s the ventanilla tinica for government export and import
procedures which i s at a more advanced stage of development.

Improvements to the customs regime
Although there has been great progress in tariff reduction and simplification in recent
years, there i s still room to further simplify and reduce tariffs, decrease dispersion, and
reduce discretion regarding specific tariff policies. Customs operations could also be
made more customer-friendly, for instance by allowing on-line consultations and
complaints as well as increasing operating hours. Moving forward with the "exports via
the postal service" program would also make customs procedures easier for small
businesses.

Access to finance
Much progress has been made in terms of access to finance for exporting firms.
Examples include the Credit Insurance for Exporters program, FIXr, Fondo Transandino
Peru, and others. Yet, these programs have only reached a small minority of export
businesses. More needs to be done to facilitate access to working capital and pre- and
post-shipment credit, especially for S M E exporters in regions outside of Lima.
Otherwise, lack of financing will continue to be a barrier for small businesses to achieve
the scale and quality levels necessary to export.

Export diversification
Peru suffers from an acute lack of export diversification. This manifests itself interms of
concentration in traditional products (especially mining), concentration in the Lima-
Callao region, and concentration in a fairly small number of export firms. Diversifying
exports along all three dimensions will contribute to more equitable, sustainable
economic development.

As mentioned above, the hodge-podge of programs across ministries and agencies means
that activities are duplicated and resources are not maximized. Concentrating resources
 and expertise into a single organization with a coherent strategy would make it easier to
 generate and disseminate useful information and tools to encourage export
 diversification. For instance, surveys could be undertaken regarding what Peruvian
 exporting companies identify as potentially successful new products as well as
 constraints to production, distribution, marketing, or finance. Supply and demand
 conditions could be obtained from existing UN records (COMTRADE, UNDO, ITC,
 FAO) and case studies could identify changes in export patterns in other developing
 countries which have experienced a positive diversification process. An analysis of
 export performance in neighboring countries with similar endowments might convey
 useful information about products where Latin American countries are gaining market
 share. A more centralized collection of export knowledge would yield more informed
 programs including technology development, business training, dissemination o f foreign



                                              134

information, campaigns to create a positive image of the country, and internalization of
coordination externalities.

Improvements in infrastructure can also facilitate export diversification. Upgrades to
roads, ports, and airports can reduce the transactions costs associated with exporting
products and importing intermediate inputs from abroad. Lower transactions costs can
make it profitable to export a wider variety of products, especially from less developed
regions outside of Lima. As shown in a previous section, over 60 percent of Peru's
exports leave the country via the Lima-Calla0 region, while only 35 percent of the
country's exports are actually produced there. Infrastructure enhancements could either
reduce the cost of transporting goods to Lima-Calla0 for export or open up new points of
departure for exports closer to actual areas of production.

Many o f these recommendations are not new. In fact, Peru's National Competitiveness
Plan that was approved in July 2005 explicitly targets issues of consolidation of export
promotion activities, information integration, tariff reduction / dispersion, and
improvements to the customs regime to reduce transactions costs. While most of the
plan's goals have target dates within the next year, actually realizing the goals could
prove to be a substantial challenge.

While all o f the aforementioned trade facilitation strategies are important, they will not be
successful in isolation. Issues such as investment climate reforms, macroeconomic
stability, venture capital, worker training and education, bureaucratic red tape,
competitiveness, quality, foreign direct investment, innovation, and technology adoption
all affect firms' abilities to discover and develop new export products. However, since
these issues also affect economic development more generally, they are covered in other
sections of this report.
