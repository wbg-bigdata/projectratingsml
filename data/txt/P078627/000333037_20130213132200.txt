F. Results Framework Analysis

Project Development Objectives (from Project Appraisal Document)

The original PDOs were to increase the efficiency of Burundi's economic, financial and
administrative management by strengthening accountability and transparency through
improved procedures and controls. This in turn was expected to raise the effectiveness of
public expenditure for growth and poverty reduction, and help reduce transaction costs for
private economic activity.
                                           viii
Revised Project Development Objectives (as approved by original approving authority)

The original PDOs did not change.

The Results Framework presented in this Data Sheet is the one introduced during the project
restructuring of April 2007. It therefore incorporates the changes made at time of restructuring
and has been adopted in the post-restructuring period. Specifically, intermediate indicators
were revised and new indicators added (from 17 identified in the PAD to a total of 21). The
original Results Framework (from Approval) with output indicators by Component and their
respective achievement before project restructuring is attached in Annex 2A.

                are managed by the Ministry of the Civil Service, although they could be brought under
                control if payroll management responsibilities were transferred to the Ministry of
                Finance and Economic Development Planning.

                Wage bill/tax revenues: the wage bill has also increased with the teachers and National
                Police and Army raise. The high absorption of tax revenue to meet wages indicates that
                without the adoption of further tax policies, the bill in the medium or long term will
                eventually exceed total tax revenue

I.   Disbursement Profile

1. Project Context, Development Objectives and Design

1.1 Context at Appraisal

1.      The recurrent political instability and ethnic conflict suffered by Burundi since its
independence in 1962 and the subsequent outbreak of civil war in 1993 (following the
assassination of a democratically-elected president) had imposed a huge toll on the
country. Hundreds of thousands of people had lost their lives, were displaced or out-
migrated (including several high-skilled employees of the public administration).
Economic management had been especially weakened in Government where, in addition
to the brain drain of key personnel, the focus on emergency concerns gave capacity
building for governance a low priority. Accordingly, a consensus emerged both within
government and among donors on the urgent need to re-build state capacity by addressing
the severe disruption of, and inefficiencies in, the country’s economic management
apparatus.

2.      At that time (between 1999-2001 and 2001-03), because the conditions in Burundi
were not conducive to the elaboration of the CAS, Bank’s interventions took place under
the aegis of an IS and TSS, respectively. EMSP was proposed as a Multi-Sector
Capacity-Building Project and was planned for delivery in FY2003. However, project
preparation had been postponed to elicit adequate involvement of the new Government
structures that had emerged from the post-conflict political developments. The delay also
allowed the Bank team to take into full account the recommendations of the on-going
PER. One of the main conclusions of the PER was the need to address the dire state of
the budget process, with a focus on repairing the basics of budget coverage, budget
execution, and financial controls.

3.      As a first step, an IDF grant had been provided to GoB for strengthening the
capacity of central ministries for policy formulation and implementation. That initial step
needed to be scaled-up with a larger TA intervention. Thus, pending a full CAS, and
given the fluid political and economic context of Burundi, the proposition and
preparation of the EMSP fit within the Bank strategy characterized by three elements: (a)
focus on the urgent basic problems; (b) pragmatic adaptability to a changing situation;
and (c) close Bank-led donor coordination, especially for economic management.

4.      At appraisal, the financial and administrative management situation in Burundi
was stated as engendering grave concerns but mitigated by some enduring capacity and
public ethics levels in key agencies and the commitment of government to institutional
improvement. The EMSP sought to meet the situation across a broad front of identified
challenges. The fluidity of the change situation and the multifaceted and multisectoral
nature of institutional shortcomings strongly informed project orientation. Notably the
2002 Economic Rehabilitation Credit (P074602) had not provided for any technical
assistance (being a direct support to the budget), including in high priority areas such as
privatization. This represented a real functional void in support to the GoB reform effort,
which the EMSP aimed to address.



                                             1
1.2 Original Project Development Objectives and Key Indicators (as approved)

5.       The original PDOs were to increase the efficiency of Burundi's economic,
financial and administrative management by strengthening accountability and
transparency through improved procedures and controls. This in turn was expected to
raise the effectiveness of public expenditure for growth and poverty reduction, and help
reduce transaction costs for private economic activity.

1.3 Revised PDO (as approved by original approving authority) and Key Indicators,
and reasons/justification

6.      The original PDOs did not change. However, at the time of the restructuring in
2007, changes were made concerning: (a) new project activities; (b) revision of the
project components; and (c) the structure and functions of the project coordinating and
monitoring mechanisms.

7.      When the project was restructured in 2007, a revised set of 21 intermediate
indicators was formulated1, in order to more effectively monitor the expected outcomes
of the project activities, and to better reflect priorities identified by government
authorities as well as strengthen the links with the DPO series.

1.4 Main Beneficiaries

8.     Given the nature of the project - which focused on institutional development and
capacity-building both within and outside the government - the PAD explicitly identified
the primary target groups which included the Ministry of Development Planning and
Reconstructions, the Statistical Institute (Component 1), the Ministry of Finance
(Component 2) , the Ministry of Good Governance and Privatization (Component 3), the
Ministry of Justice and financial oversight structures/audit institutions (including the
Parliamentary National Committee on Finance and Budget, the General Inspectorate of
Finance, the General State Inspectorate and the Court of Accounts) - Component 4.

9.      Other specific institutions expected to benefit from the project included the
Tribunal of Commerce and the Private Arbitration Center (to be created under the
project). Finally, as an outgrowth of the institutional and capacity improvements
supported by EMSP, the Government of Burundi as a whole was expected to benefit from
the project in terms of improved capacity to link its budgetary allocations to the pro-poor
priority programs identified in the PRSP.

1.6 Revised Components

17.    The EMSP was approved on January 29, 2004 and the Credit became effective on
March 30 of the same year. After 2 years of project implementation, most of the activities
experienced substantial delays and slow disbursements. As of December 31, 2006, less
than 18 percent of the credit had been disbursed, and a significant part of it (about 37
percent) helped finance the Project Coordinating Unit (PCU). This was well below the
estimated disbursement (US$ 13 million, equivalent to 50 % of the total loan). Moreover,

18.      In October 2006, the mid-term review mission analyzed the main factors behind

the project, to ensure effective use of the credit for the remaining duration of the project
and create the conditions for the achievement of its intended results. Specifically, the
mission recommended that the scope of the project be revised by (a) aligning project
activities related to the preparation and implementation of critical macroeconomic, PFM,
and sectoral reform measures supported by the recently approved ERSG, including
triggers for future development policy lending; and (b) strengthening PCU mechanisms
leading to better supervision of the agreed reform process.

19.     In line with these recommendations, the project was restructured in April 2007.
While the original PDOs and its indicators were retained, changes were made concerning:
(a) new project activities; (b) revision of the project components; (c) the structure and
functions of the project coordination and monitoring mechanisms; and (d) measures
aimed at improving World Bank monitoring of EMSP operations. All together, these
actions contributed to the improved capacity of implementing agencies to prioritize
interventions and ensure effective utilization of the bulk of the loan. The improvements in
the disbursement rates are consistent with this interpretation (section 3 further elaborates
on this point).

20.      All new activities were in line with the original four components, while also
efforts were made to select specific activities aligned with the (then) recently approved

component of the ERSG was the reform of the export crop sector (coffee, tea and cotton).
The restructured EMSP was therefore expected to prioritize a series of diagnostic studies,
audits and other activities aimed at preparing and eventually implementing the
privatization of state-owned enterprises operating in the sector. The following paragraphs

priorities under each component.

2. Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes

2.1 Project Preparation, Design and Quality at Entry

27.     The EMSP aimed at economic reform implementation to support the country’s
post-conflict recovery and to lay the foundations for sustainable growth and poverty
reduction. The project identification, development and preparation process followed the
2000 Arusha Agreement which devoted a chapter to reconstruction and economic
development. The EMSP objective was to assist both the institutional and socio-
economic reconstruction effort and the capacity-building of institutions destroyed by a
decade-long civil war. Specifically, the key objective was strengthening economic
governance through transparency and accountability, a prerequisite for the resumption of
external assistance and for a post-conflict and post-transition government to regain the
confidence and trust of the citizens. In the context of political uncertainties, the
alternative of an ambitious large-scale budget reform program (including sector-wide
approaches to economic reforms) was considered premature. Accordingly, the project
team placed its focus on capacity-building and technical support initiatives targeting the
Ministry of Finance (various departments) as well as line ministries and other selected
state agencies, with the aim of laying appropriate institutional foundations for economic
turnaround.

28.     The Burundian economy management weaknesses and flaws were underlined by a
series of studies, particularly the Public Expenditure Review carried out in 2001. Based
on its conclusions, the Government engaged with the Bank under the Transitional
Support Strategy further backed in August 2002 by the Economic Rehabilitation Credit.
The ERC – as well as preparing the way for early HIPC access – was aimed at building
the foundation for (i) the improvement of public service delivery and basic social services
and (ii) Government’s efforts to deepen institutional and structural reforms for sustained
economic recovery and poverty reduction. The EMSP was designed and prepared to be
operationally complementary in this programmatic context, as in execution it was
expected to be instrumental to the succeeding series of Economic Reform Support
Grants. Furthermore, ESMP’s formulation dovetailed with elements of the emerging
GoB Poverty Reduction approach.

29.     The project was considered high risk, as applied with any project in the
circumstances then prevailing in Burundi. The first risk was political instability and
threat of violence due to the persistence of rebel movements and localized conflicts by
rebel. The second risk was the opposition from vested political and economic interests
militating against the reform program supported by the project. The third risk was the
endemic weakness of Burundi’s public administration, whose human capital had been
severely undermined by more than ten years of political crisis and civil war.

30.     A core dilemma existed in encompassing target agencies within the scope of
project design: numerous public bodies contributed to the broad framework for economic
management and growth but they all had very low capacity. Moreover, new institutions
were planned which would also need targeted technical assistance (e.g. OBR, GANEREC,



                                            8
and ONPR) and any number of necessary studies, policy and law drafts were backlogged.
A clear danger was dissipation of project effort and an activity trap. In the design
outcome, the team was well cognizant of the limitations of the EMSP project vehicle in
terms of actualization of sector reforms as against their priming and support.
Nevertheless, the sheer breadth of activity planned militated against concentration of
effort for results, even within these limits. This is best illustrated in the justice sector
where the depth and complexity of the required reform is recognized up-front but
diagnostic assessments and other TA initiatives were included across judicial efficiency,
accountability, access, affordability as well as M&E for the Legal and Judicial Reform,
all in circumstances of recognized “low competence and morale? and “low integrity?.
The timing also appeared overambitious: interventions in the justice sector were expected
to be concluded in the (original) project life, while in fact a sector reform strategy was
activated only in 2011.

31.      Further as regards the design perspective, the risk of widespread low capacity and
lack of human capital might have been more firmly predicted to run up against the
declared option to rely on country systems 5 for the implementation of the project
activities – with only partial emphasis on TA provisions – thus compounding the
operational challenge.

32.      In conclusion, while the team correctly itemized the most critical project risk
factors (including the above), little efforts were made to identify adequate mitigating
measures and incorporate them into the design (to the extent possible). Most notably,
during project preparation the Transitional Government was heavily involved in a
complex political agenda, being under constant pressure to manage the state-building
process and deliver the ‘peace dividends’ to formerly fighting groups. As a consequence,
macroeconomic and financial management issues continued not to be given priority as
the project got underway. Management would have been pre-warned by full exposition of
this critical risk in the PAD. As it was, very low disbursement rates were to flag the
problem down the line.

2.3 Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Design, Implementation and Utilization

41.     The need for M&E was not discussed in the PAD and credit documents. The RF
at design stage was poor and severely deficient, and there was no provision for an
effective M&E. Overall coordination was assigned to the TC, with a light reporting
structure indicated for the PCU. The project placed exclusive reliance on FM (using
IFMIS and IFRS), accounting, and external audits.

42.     Notwithstanding greater indicator substantiation in the post-restructuring phase,
concrete targets were omitted in all but a few instances. Some could be extrapolated from
broader descriptions of expected outcomes, but the opportunity for generalized positive
statements of performance was too readily provided and little efforts were evident on the
part of the Bank team to agree on new targets.

the new MoF building, no specific indicator was included concerning its construction. In
the event, long delays were encountered and a ‘single item’ additional year’s extension

2.4 Safeguard and Fiduciary Compliance

44.     Financial Management. The financial management aspects (budgeting,
accounting, internal control, funds flow arrangement, reporting and external auditing)
have experienced delays at the outset of the project (as reflected in the slow disbursement
over the period 2004-2006) but improved significantly during the late implementation
stage. The Financial Management system has been reviewed and found to be satisfactory
during the last supervision mission. The project external auditor - Eura Audit
International - issued a clean opinion on the financial statement for the last years.
Unaudited Interim Financial Reports (IFRs) for the project are submitted to the Bank on
time, reviewed by the FM specialist and found to be moderately satisfactory; there are no
overdue audit reports and interim financial reports from the project coordinating unit.

45.     The project uses the accounting software (TOMPRO) which helped to maintain
proper books of accounts; the project financial management manual details out key
internal control procedures from transaction initiation, review, approval recording and
reporting. The manual has been updated to take into consideration specific changes
during implementation. There is a clear separation of duties within the PCU, all fiduciary
functions are well staffed with one financial and accounting officer, one chief accountant,
one accountant and one procurement specialist; they have been all trained on the use of
World Bank fiduciary procedures.

46.     During the last supervision mission conducted in November 2012, the mission
team was informed about the allegation of mismanagement made by the Ministry of
Finance to the project coordinator through a letter sent to the Bank. As a result, the Bank
team conducted a due-diligence review looking at a sample of transactions to ensure that
project funds had been used for the intended purposes. The conclusion of the review



                                            12
found no evidence to support the allegation and concluded that overall World Bank
procedures had been followed (with minor exceptions). Also, it has been agreed to
conduct a more comprehensive assessment in coordination with the Procurement
specialist during FY13 (under the aegis of the PSD project, itself managed by the EMSP
PCU). A recommendation was also made to the Ministry of Finance to conduct its own
fiduciary and procurement review, using the state control institution of IGE (Inspection
General d’Etat).

47.      Procurement: the overall procurement system and process for the whole project
implementation period is rated moderately satisfactory. As per the project PDOs, the
procurement activities were related mainly to consultants’ selection in the area of
macroeconomic, financial, and administrative management, in addition to a high value
work contract for a new building of Ministry of finances. With regard to the latter, there
has been a long delay in processing and signing the final contract because of (i)
complexity in procurement process including the hiring and conducting technical studies,
(ii) late integration of pre-requirement on environmental safeguard, and (iii) the price of
winning bidder was significantly above the initial estimate and it required that an
additional financing from the government be agreed. The contract execution has been
also extended for works completion due to further delays in procuring construction
materials 8 (more on this below). The changes in PCU staffing (including the project
coordinator and the procurement officer) also impacted on procurement delays.

48.     Post procurement reviews conducted for the project in February 2010 (for the
period 2004-2009) and March 2011 revealed a number of weaknesses in procurement
planning, publications, bidding, evaluation, award and records keeping. Actions plan
have been agreed with the project and mitigations measures implemented, though the
remaining contracts were few at that time. Moreover, in March 2011 the procurement
specialist resigned and it took over six months to have a replacement. As a result, the
actual impact on the procurement process was negligible, and improvements benefited
more the follow-up project (PSD) managed by the PCU than the EMSP itself.

2.5 Post-completion Operation/Next Phase

52.    The main challenge is how to build on the achievements of the EMSP and
continue supporting government reform efforts and strengthen institutional capacity
while minimizing discontinuity of effort (see Risks section below). To this end, the
following streams of interventions are ongoing and/or currently under preparation (some
of which to be covered in the CAS indicated “EMSP follow-on?, FY14):

    a. Macroeconomic Management (component 1): There is no follow-up intervention
       under preparation. However, efforts are ongoing to leverage the support of other
       donors to support the development of a fully integrated national statistical system
       capable of producing reliable statistics on poverty reduction, and monitoring the
       implementation of national development initiatives, as envisaged under PRSP-II.

    b. Public Financial and Administrative Management (Component 2): In
       collaboration with the Government, the Bank is undertaking a Public Expenditure
       Review (PER 2013) to address inefficiencies in public expenditure management
       and strengthen country resilience to shocks. Also, the Bank will support


                                                  14
        interventions to strengthen public financial management and budget transparency,
        through the proposed programmatic development policy operation (DPO) series,
        Economic Reform Support Grant VI-IX, consolidating measures initiated under
        the earlier ERSG series while extending the reforms in scope and depth.

3.2 Achievement of Project Development Objectives

Rating: Moderately Satisfactory (MS)

56.      The general weakness of the RF is noticeable in the insufficiently tractable
hierarchy of objectives. In this regard, the weak links between several intermediate
indicators and the broadly-cast summary outcome indicators does not permit easy
identification of causal connections in any overall assessment. More importantly, as most
activities have been conceived as contributory to advancing more involved processes
(typical of TA projects) the issue of attribution inevitably arises at outcome level; even
more so when a whole series of ERSGs were being put in place during project life (as
well as other complementary initiatives, for example the PSD project). Factoring this
issue in and seeking to value the establishment of foundations for reform progress
through ESMP, the indicators at closure nevertheless still give a mixed picture only
partially illustrating successful completion.

57.     In summary, Component 1 scores rather well against intermediate indicators.
Macroeconomic management has improved—facilitated by the use of modeling,
computerization of data, training of key staff, and timely reporting of economic and
financial statistics. ISTEEBU is now also a well-equipped entity, and a member of
AFRISTAT from which it receives programmed support. Staff retention is a particular
concern however. Component 2 can be seen as a general zone of advance (with some
pending challenges): budget formulation and execution is taking place according to the
stipulations of a revised Organic Law. Noticeable achievements are evident in PFM.
The skills and systems building for better PFM accountability and transparency have
advanced impressively albeit some sub-component measures were not attained.
Component 3 on the other hand has a rather indefinite record. Progress on coffee
privatization is still halting though this reflects problems beyond the project’s area of
operation. Meanwhile capacity at SCEP, albeit improved, remains lacking. Essential
audits of parastatals have been carried out and important new business environment
regulatory texts introduced. Component 4 was also not consistently performing. The
audit functions have improved as has procurement administration (with some
reservations), so has transparency through external oversight and involvement of
parliamentarians and media. However, progress on the legal and justice sector reform
has been long drawn out.

3.3 Efficiency

73.     At appraisal, a cost effectiveness and financial analysis was not carried out
because of difficulties in identifying and quantifying the direct and indirect financial,
economic, and social benefits to capacity-building and institutional reforms, as well as
attributing specific outcomes to project interventions (See Annex 3 for further details).

74.     During project implementation, funds were used with the view to ensuring cost
efficiency and best value for money. This was evidenced by the fact that all financial
transactions were fully verified before disbursements were made for the acquisition of
goods, implementation of local civil works. Also, the adoption of competitive
procurement procedures became important key tools for helping the implementing
agencies to adopt the least cost approach in the administration of their budget.

75.    The delays in implementation meant that the project’s closing date had to be
extended from July 2009 to July 2012—a time overrun of three years. This has
contributed to supervision costs, whose total (265 staff-weeks, and 564,000 dollar amount,

frequency of supervision mission and the quality of implementation supported shown by
Bank team.

crop sector). Thus restructuring as such is not that fundamental (as conceded). What has
fundamentally changed for project execution is the buy-in of the newly elected GoB



                                            20
linked to the conditionalities of the Aug.'06 ERSG. The utility of the EMSP project
vehicle for the broad economic reform is accordingly enhanced but, in the same vein, its
initial internal cohesion ‘per se’ does appear less convincing. In particular, the grouping

78.      In the above context and also the declared orientation of ESMP to respond to fluid

outcomes of the project represent real success. Stand-out features relate to the greatly
strengthened institutional framework made possible through the project vehicle including
the promulgation of the Loi Organique; the establishment of the OBR; the introduction of
VAT and the customs code; the upgrading of financial controls and audits. Project
success admits discounting, however, deriving from the hindrance occasioned by the
flaws in design and the RF; insufficient internal project cohesion and activity overload;
the difficulties of attribution vis-a-vis other project (including separate donor)
interventions; and the abiding problems of institutional capacity and residual bureaucratic
inertia in critical agencies (attested to in the 2013-‘16 CAS and the project document for
ERSG VI both of late 2012).

5. Assessment of Bank and Borrower Performance

5.1 Bank Performance

(a) Bank Performance in Ensuring Quality at Entry

Rating: Moderately Unsatisfactory (MU)

86.     At the design stage, the project was conceived as a flexible operational instrument
to accommodate a complex political context and allow for adaptation to the changing
needs in the areas of macroeconomic management and structural reforms. However, the
quest for a flexible instrument undermined the efforts to clarify the project development
objectives and establish clear limits to the scope of the project design. The wide scope of
project activities, combined with the poor quality of the Result Framework (with lack of
baselines, measurable targets and inadequate PDOs indicators) is indicative of the
deficiencies at design, also reflected in the absence of a detailed action plan, which could
have informed the PCU management and monitoring role. These deficiencies in turn
affected the quality of implementation at the outset, being partly responsible for the slow
development of a pipeline of project activities.

87.      Moreover, the design was not flexible enough to incorporate activities identified
as priorities for the country’s economic development and yet not explicitly spelled out in
the original project description (the case of the reform of the export crop sector is
illustrative). As a result, the Bank team and government authorities had to engage in
time-consuming discussions to clarify eligibility of proposed new activities (with several
proposals being rejected by the Bank), contributing to further delays. In retrospect, the



                                            23
PDO and its components could have been less ambitious and the assignment of
implementation responsibilities delineated by agencies and with clear reporting and M&E
mechanisms.

88.      Based on lessons learned in other projects a conscious decision was taken to rely
on country systems and involve the functionally responsible agencies even if some of
their respective capacities were severely limited (as was the case in Burundi). The
intention was purposeful in not episodically substituting TA in a manner that likely
would not render sustainability. But it represented a trade-off between short-term needs
and long-term objectives, the hindering effects of which were not fully recognized in
terms of what could realistically be done by when. (Project extension and a rush of
activity at certain points were not just likely in these circumstances but probably pre-
determined).

89.     The overarching role of the steering committee was problematic at the outset and
expectations were probably too high. Project preparation took place under a transitional
government, and the expectation was that most of the political interlocutors would have
changed during implementation (EMSP became effective 1 year before election). Despite
this expectation, the project institutional and implementation arrangements assigned the
overall coordination and oversight of the project to a high-level inter-ministerial body
(Program Steering Committee – PSC) with 6 Ministers as mandatory members.

(b) Quality of Supervision

Rating: Moderately Unsatisfactory

90.      At the beginning, the first TTL (Country Manager) was not replaced in a timely
manner on his departure, and the acting TTL (Resident Economist) also left a few months

out that, out of 106 requests for non-objection sent to IDA by the PCU regarding
activities to be financed under the project. (as of February 28, 2006), 86 received a
response from the TTLs with an average delay of 53 days. About 25 percent of these
responses took about 16 days or less, while another 25 percent took 79 days.

91.    During the Mid-term Review in 2006, a number of recommendations were also
made with a view to new EMSP efficient operational dynamics, and to ensure day to day
project activities monitoring and dialogue with the counterparts. Specifically, direct
responsibilities were assigned to the local economists regarding various activities
supervision, in the context of the implementation of measures preceding the second
disbursement payment of the Economic Reform Support Grant in 2007. Various

6. Lessons Learned

108. Institutional strengthening and capacity building projects, though assessed
for their contributory value in a broader reform process, must still be constructed
with maximum internal cohesion and coherence. As facilitative mechanisms, the
ultimate utility of IS/CB projects will be judged in the robustness of the foundations they
set for heightened sectoral and national performance. But project components must still
be conceptually connected; activities realistically cast- situation, time and resource wise;
results demonstrably achievable; and monitoring procedures tractable in a well-defined
results framework.

109. The primacy of flexibility in project formulation does not diminish the
requirement for standard rigour in design. EMSP was constructed with justifiable
emphasis on providing a flexible project vehicle in the circumstances of Burundi’s fragile
situation and myriad institutional needs. Thoroughness in design has been questioned in
this ICR, however: a shortcoming not ameliorated by the fact that formulation spread
over an extended period of months.


17
  Specifically, ‘focal points’ claimed a bonus (top-up fees) for their services. This issue was submitted to
the World Bank project team but the bonus related expenditure was judged ineligible. Arguably, the fact
that the bonus was denied demotivated the staff and had an impact on the project activities implementation.
However, the variation in the performance of ‘focal points’ across beneficiaries suggests that individual
factors (capacity) also played a role, as well as the strength of the political and technical leadership in
holding focal points accountable for their work


                                                    28
110. An omnibus approach to the inclusion of a multi-agency range of IS/CB
interventions should not ignore selectivity. The issue of activity overstretch is explored
in this report. A positive orientation to maximizing the number of feasible interventions
needs to be tempered by a realistic assessment of the activity agenda including as regards
existing capacity. In this regard, it is instructive that the CAS FY13-FY16 cites key
lessons learned in the Burundian environment – albeit at the macro level - as “the need
for selectivity; avoidance of overly ambitious project goals; and capacity constraints?.

111. Reform in the legal and judicial sector invariably presents special
complexities. The separation of powers constitutionally means distinct governance
structures pertain in this sector. If added to this the judicial systems are under severe
strain and held in especially low regard by the population (as is the case in Burundi), it
must be questionable if the range of issues can be satisfactorily addressed in a project
encompassing diverse other sectors.

112. Greater rationalization of structure and number of components and sub-
components to be implemented under EMSP was required. Emphatically so in an
environment of restricted implementation capacity at the level of the borrower, and
sometimes weak supervision capacity at level of the Bank.

113. The results framework must always contain target values for PDO and
Intermediate Outcomes and the number of measurable indicators should not be
attenuated. Doing so will enable better implementation monitoring and make the ICR
preparation easier and more meaningful (in addition to any later evaluation).

114. Big infrastructure project should be part of stand-alone lending operations.
The supervision of big infrastructure works tend to be time-consuming for PCU, affecting
the quality of supervision for other activities covered under the project. As
recommendation for the future, the procurement process for such high value civil works
contract should be handled separately by infrastructures project.

115. Stability of TTL management is important in a post-conflict environment.
Both government officials and PCU Technical Committee remarked on the multiple
changes in TTL-ship, and the lack of a more stable presence of the Bank field in the field.
Particularly in the early stages of project implementation there was a diffuse perception
of disengagement, which weakened the Bank’s position vis-à-vis government
counterparts. Changes in TTLs should be kept to a minimum. When unavoidable join
hand-over missions (including the sector manager, if possible) should be carried out to
ensure smooth transition and minimize impact on the project18.

confusions in the lines of authority and were responsible of delays in implementations,

1.      Le programme de la Revue des Dépenses Publiques initié par le Gouvernement du
Burundi avec le soutien de la Banque Mondiale et du FMI a permis de mettre en évidence
les problèmes de visibilité dans la gestion des finances publiques et les difficultés de faire
les projections macroéconomiques à moyen et long terme.

2.     C’est à la suite de ce constat que le Projet d’Appui à la Gestion Economique
(PAGE) a été conçu dans l’optique d’une exécution cohérente des réformes préconisées
dans le cadre de la Revue des Dépenses Publiques et dont les programmes s’articulent
autour des axes ci – après :

Ź   Améliorer les outils de gestion macroéconomique par la disponibilisation des
    informations statistiques produites sur une base régulière et fiable ;

Ź   Renforcer la transparence et la discipline dans la Gestion des Finances Publiques
    aussi bien au niveau des dépenses qu’à celui des recettes ;

Ź   Appuyer les structures de contrôle telles que l’Inspection Générale de l’Etat-IGE, la
    Cour des Comptes –CDC et les médias.

3.     Des missions d’identification intervenues en décembre 2002 et début 2003 ont
permis de circonscrire le champ d’actions du PAGE. A l’issue des discussions entre la
mission de la Banque Mondiale, le Fonds Monétaire International et le Gouvernement du
Burundi, un consensus s’est dégagé sur les principales composantes du projet, les
principaux objectifs à atteindre, les réformes à initier et les actions prioritaires à réaliser.

OBJECTIFS ET COMPOSANTES DU PROJET

6.      Le Projet d’Appui à la Gestion Economique (PAGE) avait 4 composantes à
savoir : (i) l’appui à la gestion macroéconomique, collecte et analyse des données ; (ii) le
renforcement de la transparence dans la gestion financière et administrative ; (iii) le
développement du secteur privé et (iv) l’appui à la bonne gouvernance et justice. Cette
section analyse les objectifs spécifiques par composante en vérifiant d’une part, la
cohérence entre ces objectifs et la situation de référence décrit lors de l’identification du
projet, et d’autre part, se prononce sur l’atteinte des objectifs assignés à chaque
composante.

Composante 1 : Appui à la gestion macroéconomique, collecte et analyse des
données

7.       Les objectifs de la première composante sont repris ci- après :

     -   renforcer les capacités institutionnelles d’assistance technique en gestion
         macroéconomique ;
     -   renforcer les capacités du personnel dans l’élaboration et l’utilisation des modèles
         de prévision macroéconomique et sectorielle, l’analyse des politiques
         macroéconomiques, la mise à jour de l’élaboration des comptes nationaux, la
         gestion et l’analyse de la soutenabilité de la dette ;
     -   poursuivre et renforcer les réformes institutionnelles et structurelles en vue d’une
         croissance économique soutenue de réduction de la pauvreté afin d’établir un
         cadre de référence pour un accès rapide à l’initiative de réduction de la dette des
         Pays Pauvres Très Endettés (IPPTE);
     -   améliorer l’efficience dans la collecte et l’utilisation des données statistiques
         permettant à l’ISTEEBU d’établir une base de données informatisée et de
         compléter les bases de données spécifiques des autres secteurs décentralisés de
         l’Etat.
8.      Ces objectifs étaient pertinents dans la mesure où à l’issu des différentes missions
de préparation du projet, il a été relevé l’inefficacité des fonctions de programmation et
de projection macro-économique qui est largement attribuable aux difficultés d’accès à
des données crédibles et cohérentes par l’ISTEEBU. Le Ministère des Finances n’était
pas apte à préparer les projections macro-économiques multi-annuelles des recettes et des
dépenses publiques et continuait à dépendre dans une large mesure, des projections
périodiques établies par le FMI tandis que le Ministère de la Planification ne parvenait
pas à rendre disponibles, de façon régulière, les statistiques de base tels que les agrégats
de la comptabilité nationale sans lesquels de telles projections ne pouvaient pas être
élaborées. Par ailleurs, durant plusieurs années, il y a eu absence d'un mécanisme
permettant de traduire les objectifs de politique économique dans un ensemble cohérent
de projections macroéconomiques et financière, ce qui n'a pas facilité une gestion
efficiente des dépenses publiques.


                                             54
9.      Il faut cependant signaler que les objectifs assignés à la première composante ne
sont pas tout à fait cohérents avec la description de la situation de référence qui n’insistait
que sur la production régulière et fiable des données statistiques et la projection
macroéconomique. En effet, les objectifs de réforme structurelle, la gestion et l’analyse
de la soutenabilité de la dette, ne répondent à aucun problème soulevé au niveau de la
situation de référence. Ces objectifs ont été réalisés au niveau de la deuxième et troisième
composante du projet.

Composante 2 : Renforcement de la transparence dans la gestion financière et
                administrative

10.     L’objectif recherché par cette composante est d’instaurer la transparence et de
moderniser la gestion économique et financière de l’Etat, par la simplification des
procédures du circuit de la dépense, l’unification du budget de l’Etat, le renforcement du
recouvrement des recettes à travers l’instauration du système intégré d’informatisation de
la gestion des finances publiques.

Les objectifs spécifiques de cette composante sont repris ci après :
   - La modernisation et le renforcement du cadre légal de gestion des finances
       publiques pour assurer la transparence et clarifier le mode de gestion et les
       responsabilités des acteurs ;
   - L’amélioration de la préparation et de l’exécution du budget et la mise à jour plan
       comptable de l’Etat et du règlement général sur la comptabilité publique ;
   - L’amélioration de la passation des marchés publics pour instaurer la transparence
       et la rigueur dans la gestion des marchés publics ;
   - L’amélioration de la gestion financière et la reddition des comptes par la mise en
       place d’un système informatisé de gestion des finances publiques et de la masse
       salariale;
   - Le renforcement du contrôle et l’audit des finances publiques pour améliorer la
       transparence et l’obligation de rendre compte de la gestion des finances publiques.

11.     Ces objectifs sont pertinents dans la mesure où ils répondent aux différents
problèmes identifiés lors de la conception du projet. En effet, les principaux problèmes à
résoudre lors du démarrage du projet portaient sur la réorganisation de la collecte des
recettes et l’absence d’un système efficace d’identification fiscale, difficulté de
préparation et d’exécution budgétaire auquel s’ajoutent les comptes spéciaux pléthoriques
(comptes extrabudgétaires), la gestion de la solde et du personnel ainsi que les difficultés
liées à la comptabilité publique et l’information sur la gestion des finances publiques
(impossibilité de retracer toutes les opérations financières de l’Etat).
Composante 3 : Développement du secteur privé

12.     L’objectif de cette composante est d’une part d’améliorer l’environnement des
affaires, et d’autre part d’organiser la restructuration des Sociétés à Participations
Publiques-SPP dans le cadre d’un programme cohérent de restructuration et


                                              55
privatisation. Par rapport à ses sous- composantes, on distingue trois objectifs spécifiques
visant à :
    - L’amélioration de la compétitivité des filières agricoles afin d’augmenter les
        revenus agricoles des agriculteurs en appuyant le processus de désengagement de
        l’Etat des activités industrielles et commerciales ;
    -   La promotion et le développement du secteur privé par l’amélioration du climat
        des affaires;
    -   La Mise en place et soutien d’un programme cohérent de restructuration et
        privatisation des entreprises publiques dans le cadre du désengagement de l’Etat
        dans la gestion de ces entreprises pour améliorer l’efficacité économique et
        financière de ces dernières.
13.      Ces objectifs spécifiques correspondent à l’objectif du Gouvernement qui était de
réduire progressivement sa participation dans les SPP et d’établir un cadre légal et
institutionnel pour promouvoir le développement du secteur privé et accroître la
compétitivité. Ces objectifs, très ambitieux, n’ont été que partiellement atteints. C’est
ainsi que les activités y relatives ont été transférées dans un autre projet en cours
d’exécution par l’unité de coordination du PAGE à savoir le Projet de Développement
des Secteurs Financier et Privé (PDSFP), objet de l’accord de don n° H5360-BI signé le
16 février 2011 entre la Banque Mondiale et le Gouvernement.

Composante 4 : Appui à la Bonne Gouvernance et la Justice

14.     Les insuffisances constatées au niveau de la tenue des comptes et au niveau du
respect des délais pour leur reddition sont dues entre autres aux faiblesses d’exercice des
activités de contrôle des finances publiques.
Les contrôles formels exercés sur les finances publiques sont relatifs aux contrôles
administratifs internes, aux contrôles externes et aux contrôles parlementaires (contrôle
financier, contrôle de légalité et contrôle de bon emploi des deniers et des biens publics).

15.     La quatrième composante a comme objectif le renforcement des capacités des
structures de contrôle économique, notamment les structures chargées de la bonne
gouvernance (Cabinet du Ministère à la Présidence chargé de la Bonne Gouvernance, de
l’Inspection Générale de l’Etat et de la Privatisation), les structures de contrôle externes
notamment la Cour des Comptes et les Commissions Finances du Parlement et le secteur
de la justice.

EVALUATION DES PERFORMANCES

Evaluation des performances de l’Emprunteur

21.      Dans le cadre de la mise en œuvre du projet, le Gouvernement a réalisé de façon
satisfaisante ses engagements pour la bonne exécution du projet. Il a notamment assuré :
(i) le démarrage et mise en place de l’unité de gestion du projet, (ii) la préparation des
plans d’actions et des budgets de travail, (iii) la production régulière des rapports
d’activités et la conduite des audits, (iv) la communication sur les réformes économiques,
(v) la proposition des amendements au Bailleurs dans le cadre d’améliorer l’exécution du
projet. (vi) le payement régulier de sa contre partie convenue avec le bailleur pour couvrir
les dépenses non éligibles par le Crédit IDA, (vii) le pilotage institutionnel du projet
(comité de Pilotage, comité Technique de suivi et les Points focaux).

22.     Néanmoins, le démarrage qui s’est déroulé pendant la phase de longue transition
politique caractérisée par une instabilité institutionnelle (changement des autorités) a

Evaluation de la performance de la Banque Mondiale

23.    Les performances de la Banque Mondiale portent entre autres sur (i) sa

cadrage des activités du projet (iv) l’approbation des amendements de l’accord de crédit
proposés par le Gouvernement.

24.    Néanmoins, avant la revue à mi parcours, le projet a affiché des difficultés de
démarrage effectif liées à l’instabilité des Chefs d’équipe du Projet qui a anormalement
prolongé les délais de non objection entravant ainsi la réalisation rapide des activités.
Evaluation de la performance du Projet

25.     Les performances du projet sont globalement satisfaisantes. Tous les objectifs du
projet ont été atteints et 17 indicateurs sur 21 ont été totalement réalisés. Le reste des
indicateurs (4) ont été réalisés à plus de 90%. En plus, les audits du projet ont tous été
régulièrement réalisés et les opinions des auditeurs ont été sans réserve.

LECONS TIREES ET RECOMMANDATIONS

27.     La conception, la mise en œuvre et le suivi évaluation du projet PAGE ont permis
des expériences pratiques sur lesquelles le Gouvernement et la Banque peuvent
capitaliser pour la suite des réformes économiques susceptibles de promouvoir la
croissance et la réduction de la pauvreté.
Leçons tirées

28.    Les principales leçons tirées de la conception, la mise en œuvre et le suivi
évaluation du projet PAGE sont les suivantes:
    1) la mise en place d’une équipe compétente de gestion du projet dotée d’outils
       appropriés de pilotage constitue un facteur de réussite de l’exécution de tout
       projet ;
   2) l’implication des bénéficiaires nécessite des actions de mobilisation et de
      motivation pour l’exécution et la conduite des réformes ;
   3) la simplification des formalités de communication entre la Banque Mondiale et
      l’Emprunteur via les échanges informels comme les mails et le téléphone a
      accéléré la réalisation des activités du projet (requêtes et réponses des non
      objections) ;
   4) l’existence d’une structure gouvernementale solide de coordination des activités
      des bailleurs permet de mener des réformes de façon cohérente (en évitant des
      chevauchements ou doubles emplois) ;
   5) la régularité des missions de supervision de la Banque facilite la mise en œuvre
      des activités du projet ;
   6) les actions préalables aux DARE (Dons d’Appui aux Réformes Economiques) et à
      l’IPPTE(Initiative des Pays Pauvres Très Endettés) ont accéléré la mise en œuvre
      des réformes y relatives ;
   7) l’instabilité institutionnelle des cadres chargés de la mise en œuvre des réformes
      est un grand handicap à l’appropriation, à la pérennisation et à la réussite des
      réformes ;
   8) la résistance aux changements nécessite des actions de formation et de
      sensibilisation à tous les niveaux ministériels jusqu’aux exécutants ;
   9) la faiblesse de l’expertise nationale à conduire les études nécessaires à la mise en
      œuvre des réformes envisagées par le projet risque de fragiliser la pérennisation et
      l’appropriation des résultats atteints par le projet ;
   10) un pays en post conflit comme le Burundi éprouve des besoins énormes en
       matière de réformes économiques.




                                           63
Recommandations

29.     L’exécution du projet a permis d’atteindre les objectifs et les résultats qui lui
étaient assignés au moment de son démarrage. Cependant pour consolider les acquis du
projet et d’accompagner le Gouvernement dans sa politique de réformes économiques
visant la croissance et la réduction de la pauvreté, il est recommandé à la Banque
Mondiale ce qui suit :

   1) appuyer la mise en œuvre de la stratégie nationale de développement de la
      statistique ;
   2) renforcer le cadre institutionnel de suivi des indicateurs du CSLP IIème
      génération ;
   3) renforcer les capacités institutionnelles de prévision macroéconomique (CDMT,
      modélisation) ;
   4) appuyer les réformes économiques et financières visant la facilitation de
      l’intégration du Burundi à l’EAC ;
   5) appuyer la mise en œuvre effective de la nouvelle loi organique des finances ;
   6) développer le SIGEFI pour répondre aux besoins de la gestion décentralisée des
      finances publiques;
   7) appuyer la réforme institutionnelle et organique de l’administration publique;
   8) appuyer la mise en œuvre de stratégie de bonne gouvernance et de lutte contre la
      corruption ;
   9) concevoir et mettre en œuvre une stratégie nationale de communication et
      d’information sur les réformes économiques et financières.
   10) Poursuivre le soutien au Gouvernement dans la mise en œuvre et la consolidation
       des réformes économiques (pérennisation des acquis du projet).

Annex 10: List of People met during the ICR Mission

The ICR mission took place between December 10 and December 21, 2012. The mission
had a kick off meeting with the PAGE Coordinator and the project Technical Committee
(Comité technique du PAGE) on December 12. The event provided the opportunity to
introduce the objectives of the ICR mission and discuss the plan of meetings with key
actors within the government and the donor community.

During the first week the team carried out a first round of consultations and interviews
with key stakeholders across the project components. During the second week the team
took stock of the initial findings and engaged on a second round of consultations to
further probe our hypotheses and collect additional evidence on the progress and
achievements of PAGE.

The mission concluded with a wrap-up meeting at the MOF with the Permanent Secretary,
senior government officers from the project components and members of the PAGE
Technical Committee. The meeting provided an opportunity to discuss the ICR team's
preliminary findings, generating a lively discussion with the GoB counterparts. The list
of people met during the ICR mission and their respective affiliation is provided below.
