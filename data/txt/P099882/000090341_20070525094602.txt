                                       I. INTRODUCTION


1.       The proposed Third Programmatic Poverty Reduction Support Operation (PRSO 111)
represents the third in a series o f four budgetary support operations for Georgia. The PRSO
series i s the cornerstone o fthe Georgia Country Partnership Strategy (CPS) (September 2005).
PRSO 111, in the amount o f US$ 20 million equivalent, supports the Government's medium
term reform program described under the framework o f the Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper
(PRSP) Update o fAugust 2006 (or EDPRPUpdate, as it is known inGeorgia).*

2.       The objectives o f Georgia's medium-term reform program include achieving and
sustaining rapid economic growth (at least 5 percent per year), reducing extreme poverty from
11.4 percent in 2005 to 5 percent or less by 2015 and overall poverty from 32.9 percent in
2005 to half that figure by 2015, and re-orienting the role o f the state and its relationship to
the private sector in order to achieve these outcomes. The reform program also supports
important features o f Georgia's Action Plan under the European Neighborhood Policy (ENP)
and its IndividualPartnershipAction Plan(PAP)for joining NATO.

3.       Within the wide range of reforms being implemented by the Government, the PRSO
program focuses on key governance, growth and poverty reduction areas inwhich the World
Bank Group has a comparative advantage and complements the work o f other development
partners.     For operational purposes, the program i s organized into four pillars: (i)
strengthening public sector accountability, efficiency, and transparency; (ii)improving
electricity and gas sector services; (iii)improving the environment for private sector
development; and (iv) improving social protection, education and health care services. In
addition, the PRSO program monitors the Millenium Development Goals (MDGs).

4.       The proposed PRSO 111 and its follow-up (PRSO IV) are timed and designed to
maintain the direction o f reforms in the context o f a more rapid than expected pace o f
implementation in some policy areas, and to mitigate the risks to the reform agenda posed by
external shocks. Regarding pace, the PRSO 11, approved by the Board in October 2006,
documented in detail the areas inwhich progress had exceeded program expectations. These
areas include public expenditure policy and management reform, the functioning o f the
Chamber o f Control, tax and customs reform, reforms in the energy sector, and education
reform. This solid record o f reform implementation has continued under the proposed PRSO
111. Originally, PRSO I11reforms were expected to be completed'bySeptember/October 2007.
However, as shown in Table 1, all but four original prior actions o f PRSO I11have already
beenmet, and there are six areasinwhich reforms have exceededprogram expectations.

5.      The current Georgian Government has maintained a record o f strong growth and
macroeconomic stability since early 2004, which it has achieved through an adequate mix o f
sound fiscal and monetary policies and ambitious structural reforms. The IMF's FifthReview
o f the PRGF program in February 2007 provides supporting analysis. These achievements
notwithstanding, over the last two years Georgia has been hit by several external shocks.
These have included especially sharp energy price increases, with the price o f imported gas
rising from US$68 in 2005, to $110 in 2006 and, most recently, to US$235 per 1000 c/m as
January o f 2007. The financial impact on the Georgian economy has beenestimated to range
between US$300 and US$400 million and between 1 and 3 percentage points o f GDP growth.
The government has put in place an electricity lifeline and a gas subsidy for the first four
months o f 2007 to protect vulnerable groups. The negative impact o f these shocks on the
extreme poor has also beenreduced by the timely introduction o f the targetedsocial assistance
program, which was designed as part o f the PRSO Iand put in place with the support o f
PRSO II.

6.      Table 1presents the prior actions implementedunder the PRSO program to date, and a
summary comparison o f the original and updated prior actions o f PRSO 111. As shown inthe
last column o f Table 1, there are many areas where reform implementation has exceeded
program expectations. These include public expenditure policy reform, improvements in the
financial management system, improvements in intergovernmental fiscal relations, energy
sector reforms, solid progress inimproving the implementation and monitoring o f the targeted
social assistance program, rapid progress in customs reform, and additional measures to curb
corruption as per the findings o f GRECO.        In several areas the program adjusted the
conditionality for a closer follow-up o f implementation once new legislationhas been enacted:
for example, the Law on Local Self-Government Budget has been enacted so that the focus o f
the prior action for PRSO 111was on the implementation o f newly enacted rules and support
for the development o f monitoring systems. Similarly, with the significant progress made in
energy sector reforms, the monitoring o f power services has been consolidated into the Energy
Sector Medium-term Strategic Action Plan.

7.      By consolidating reforms in some areas, the PRSO IIIis able to support new reform
efforts in other key areas. The Georgian authorities have initiated a major reform inrevenue
administration by unifying the Tax Department, the Customs Department, and the Financial
Police into a single Revenue Service Department. This reform aims to improve revenue
administration, improve services to taxpayers, and achieve a better balance between
compliance and enforcement. Similarly, prior actions were upgraded in the reforms to the
financial management system, including the implementation o f accounting reform and o f the
recently issued financial and performance requirements o f the Legal Entities o f Public Law
(LEPLs).

8.      Public Sector Accountability, Efficiency, and Transparency(Pillar I;see Section
V-A). Georgia has made substantial progress in curbing corruption, improving transparency
and accountability in the public sector. As shown in Figure 1, Business Environment and
EnterprisePerformance Survey (BEEPS) 2005 reports an impressiveturnaround from 2002 to
2005, which i s echoed by other indicators on corruption by international think tanks. Notably,




                                               2

the last public opinion poll3reported that corruption is no longer among the five top concerns
o f Georgians. At the same time, this poll reports that Georgians consider improvements inthe
provision o f some basic public goods and services, such as roads and electricity, to be among
the major successes o f the Government. The poll shows that overall the public i s more
optimistic about the future in2006 than in2002 and 2003. Furthermore, Georgia is ina much
stronger macro financial position today owing in part to public finance reforms and anti-
corruptionreforms undertaken over the current government over the past several years.

9.      Reforms supported by the PRSO have contributed to some o f these positive results.
Annex I11summarizes program outcomes to date, based on monitorable indicators, which in
turn draw on baseline data from the periodjust before PRSO Ibegan. The progress achieved
i s measured under PRSO Iand then later under PRSO I1 and PRSO III.Annex 111also
projects expected end ofthe program outcomes.

10.     Public sector reforms under Pillar Ihave exceeded program expectations in four areas
(one o f which was introduced as a new trigger to focus on the implementation o f recently
enacted legislation) and have enabled the updating o f three triggers as part o f the
consolidation o f the program. Three reforms have been moved to PRSO IV and one trigger
updated as part o f the consolidation.           The four areas in which the authorities have
outperformed the original program include: (i)        public expenditure policy reform (Pillar I,sub-
component 1); (ii)public finance accounting reform (Pillar I,sub-component 3); (iii)
intergovernmental financial relations reform (Pillar I,sub-component 4); and (iv) the
implementation o fthe National Anti-Corruption Strategy (Pillar I,            sub-component 6).

11.     These areas are expected to improve gradually over time through continuous efforts to
develop further technical and managerial capacity in public institutions. The World Bank's
public sector reform project together with extensive technical assistance from development
partners will support the proposed PRSO 111reforms.

12.     Energy and Gas Sectors Reform (Pillar 11; see Section V-B). The main objective o f
the PRSO supported reforms under Pillar I1is to move the energy sector towards efficiency
and sustainability. Untilrecently, Georgian consumers did not receive electricity and natural
gas at acceptable service levels. Owing to non-payment, low tariffs, energy theft and pervasive
corruption in the sector, in 2003 the national average o f electricity service per household per
day was 7 hours (collection rates were under 40 percent). This meant that Tbilisi had some
electricity some o f the time and blackouts were frequent. Outside o f Tbilisi, most o f the
country was basically in the dark, and collection rates were under 35 percent. Overall, the
reforms inthe electricity and gas sectors have surpassedwhat was originally envisioned inthe
program with impressive results on the ground. Today, nearly all paying costumers have a
24/7 electricity service (24 hours per day/7 days per week); collection rates are above
90 percent; and blackouts are infrequent. Large tariff increases were implemented in 2006
and 2007, which were critical steps on the road to a financially sustainable energy sector. At
the same time, electricity and gas subsidies were introduced to protect the most vulnerable
population.

13.     The PRSO supported reforms have contributed substantially to this impressive
turnaround.    Privatization o f electricity sector assets i s progressing more rapidly than
originally envisioned. Energy prices in Georgia have reached levels comparable to those in
the international markets. Georgia has diversified its sources o f gas supply. As a result,
PRSO 111is consolidating the trigger on the monitoring and reporting o f electricity service to
consumers into the implementation o f the Updated Energy Sector Medium-term Strategic
Action Plan.

14.     With the completion o fthe privatization program this year, the private sector becomes
the main player inproducing and distributingenergy services while the public sector will now
need to focus on developing its ability to regulate the energy sector. PRSO IIIand the
subsequent fourth operation propose to support further progress towards the efficiency and
sustainability o f the energy and gas sectors and to support the first steps towards developing
an effective regulatorycapacity.

15.     Improving the Environment for Private Sector Development (Pillar 111; see
Section V-C). In 2006, Georgia became the number one reformer worldwide the Bank's
Doing Business Report o f 2007 (see Box 3), moving from 112`h place to 37`h place out o f 175
countries. The authorities are determined to implement reforms that will continue to improve
the business environment. Overall the reforms o f Pillar I11are on track; all triggers have been
met and in some dimensions program expectations have been exceeded. PRSO 111upgrades
the enactment and operationalizing o f the new Customs Code from a benchmark to a new
trigger to reflect a faster pace o f reform and to obtain a closer follow-up on implementation.
To support the new stages o f the reform, PRSO IV includes a new trigger to support reforms
aimed at improving the revenue collection administration by establishing a unified revenue
collection department within the Ministry o fFinance.

16.     Improving Social Protection, Education and Health Care Services (Pillar 111; see
Section V-D). The proposed PRSO I11 supports the Government's reforms o f the social
protection system and education and health care services.              In social protection, the
Government's main objective i s to provide adequate support to the neediest population. To
this end, the Georgian authorities first cleared arrears inthe pension system and increased the
level o f pensions (under PRSO I);     and then introduced in the Fall o f 2006 a well-designed
targeted social assistance program. While Georgia had several social assistance programs,
these have been based mainly on status and social category (such as internally displaced
people, veterans, the disabled, orphans, and so on). Incontrast, the poverty benefit program is
targeted to the extreme poor, measured by household surveys utilizing consumption data.

17.     The development o f Georgia's new targetedsocial assistance program has included the
following preparatory activities: (i)   adoption o f an agreed poverty measurement methodology
(supported by the Bank's 2006 Programmatic Poverty Assessment); (ii)      design o f the targeting
formula; (iii) preparation and issuance of basic documents including an operational manual
for benefit administration; (iv) establishment o fthe Employment and Social Services Agency,
which administers the program; (v) recruitment and training o f the Agency's staff; (vi)
computerization o f the databases o f the applications and files o f beneficiaries at the district
level; and (vii) implementation o f a financial transfer mechanism. Under PRSO 111, the
Georgian authorities, supported by PHRD resources, have initiated a detailed assessment o f


                                                 4

the targeted poverty benefit program and its sub-components. Completing the assessment and
defining and implementingremedial actions are the objectives envisioned for PRSO IV.

18.     Education Reform. The Government has made significant progress towards its
objective o f improving education system efficiency, transparency, access and quality. This
has included adoption o f transparent university entry examinations; implementation o f
measures to participate in the current TIMSS and P E S ; adoption o f new laws and
regulations which provide all Georgian schools with a high degree o f autonomy; introduction
o f a per student financing mechanism; design o f accreditation mechanisms; and
implementation o f quality control and financial accountability instruments. Under PRSO I11
and the subsequent fourth operation the focus i s on improving education finance, developing
quality enhancement mechanisms, and modernizing the management infrastructure in the
sector. These reforms are also supported by the Bank's investment lending operation in
education, and technical assistanceis beingprovided by development partners.

19.     HealthCare Reform. The health care sector faces significant challenges. First, as in
other transition economies, Georgia inherited a sector with a physical and staffing
overcapacity which it has been unable to maintain properly. Second, Georgia has the lowest
health service utilization in the Europe and Central Asia Region, with less than 2 outpatient
visits per capita and less than 5 inpatient visits per 100 people. Out-of-pocket expenditures
are required to receive care, and therefore 73 percent o f the chronically ill cite lack o f
resources as one o fthe main reasons for not utilizing health services.

20.     To address the most urgent needs, the Georgian authorities defined a Basic Benefit
Package o f health care services with universal coverage for all o f the population and
introduced a supplemental package o f health care services for the poor. In parallel, the
Government increased budgetary allocations to the health care sector inthe MTEF 2007-2010.
It also created a Presidential Commissionfor Health Care Reform which is an ambitious effort
to align better the number and capacity o f hospitals and primary health care facilities with
demand patterns, and engage the private sector actively in modernizing and operating health
infrastructure. PRSO 111 focused on supporting implementation o f a primary health care
master plan with special emphasis on the monitoring and evaluation o f early results.

21.     Addressing the Challenge of Implementation. The PRSO Iprogram document
indicated that, as the series progressed, the challenge o f effective implementation would
become increasingly relevant, and therefore the PRSO program focuses on areas in which
Bankoperations andthe activities o fother donors canbebrought to bear on capacity building.

                                 11.     COUNTRY CONTEXT

A.       Political Background

25.      President Saakhasvili started his fourth year in office in2007, after a landslide victory
o f the rulingNational Movement Party in the October 2006 local elections. Political analysts
note that the results o f this election indicate that the President and his party still enjoy broad
support. At the same time, tensions with Russia have subsided after having peaked last
October.      Since the peaceful Rose Revolution in November 2003, the President's
administration has been implementing bold reforms to fight corruption, reduce the burden o f
the state on the economy, move the energy sector towards efficiency and sustainability, lay the
basis for improved employment and standards o f living for Georgians, and develop a fiscally
sustainable social safety net.

26.      Georgia has received strong financial support from the international community,
including full delivery on commitments made during the June 2004 consultative group in
Brussels. In addition, disbursements o f grants from the Millennium Challenge Corporation
(MCC) started in 2006 and are expected to reach US200 million over the three years o f
2007-09. The U S has provided significant aid to Georgia which has regularly ranked among
the largest recipients o f U S aid in per capita terms. .At the same time, Georgia's relations
with the EUhas beenstrengthened bythe ENP Agreement signed inNovember 2006. And in
its bidto join NATO, Georgia is currently implementing its PAP.

27.      Duringthe secondhalfo f2006 the authorities accelerated the implementationo fmajor
political and institutional reforms. As described inthe Government's PRSP Update o fAugust
2006, with the local elections o f October 2006 the Government implemented major territorial,
administrative, and budgetary decentralization reforms. This included consolidating about
1,100 very small municipalities into 72 local self-government units. In December 2006 the
Parliament approved three constitutional amendments to advance presidential elections from
early 2009 to late 2008 so that they would coincide with parliamentaryelections.



                                                  13

28.     Despite the impressive pace and breadth o f reform, important challenges remain.
During the recent Governance and Anti-corruption (GAC) consultations, representatives o f
the civil society stressed that the Government needs to: (i)improve its consultations and
communications with the public on the reform strategy; (ii)  focus on strengthening the rule o f
law and avoid perceptions o f selective use o f enforcement mechanisms; and (iii)      adopt a
modem approach to revenue collection (i.e., more balance between incentives for compliance
and enforcement).     Political observers note that Georgia's active civil society plays an
important role inhelpingto strengthen democracy inGeorgia.

29.     President Saakhasvili continues to dominate the political arena, although there is a
small, incipient and still fragmented opposition.        Periodic polls underscore the broad
consensus that impressive progress has been made in many areas o f public life. They show
that corruption i s no longer the top concern o f Georgians and acknowledge that the major
improvements in education services and roads, and the provision o f reliable electricity
services, are among the major achievements thus far.


B.      Economic Background

30.     During much o f the first decade o f transition, Georgia was one o f the poorest
performing economies in the Europe and Central Asia region. Although there were some
periods o f optimism in the mid-to-late 1990s, by 2004 Georgia still had a weak governance
framework, corruption was rampant, selective reform efforts were not being sustained and the
country was drifting rapidly towards `failed state' status.

31.     Not surprisingly during 1989-97, Georgia had one o f the lowest tax revenues to GDP
ratios among the transition economies (averaging 3-8 percent compared with 26-3 1percent in
the Baltic and CIS countries). Many Georgians had to pay government officials but these
payments did not reach the Treasury, and hence the state did not have the resources to provide
basic public goods and services.         This situation reflected widespread corruption and
significant weaknesses inthe rule o f law. Moreover, the deterioration in living standards was
further exacerbated by non-payment o f pensions and wages, a sharp deterioration in public
services, (in particular electricity), and the growing impact o f corruption on everyday life.
These conditions created a fertile environment for the peaceful Rose Revolution inlate 2003.


32.     Upon taking office in January 2004, the new Government responded decisively to its
mandate to curb corruption and improve Georgia's development prospects. First, its anti-
corruption efforts and the agenda to establish strong mechanisms o f accountability and
transparency cut across all areas o f government. Second, the reform strategy has aimed at
developing a dynamic and competitive private sector as the main engine o f growth, with the
state playing a supportive role by providing basic public goods and services. And third, the
Government has undertaken complementary reforms to ensure that the population benefits
from growth through improvements in education and health care delivery and by developing
an effectively targeted social safety net to protect the extreme poor. The institutional and
structural reforms are beingsupported by prudentmacroeconomic policies.

C.      RecentEconomicDevelopments

33.     Macroeconomic management in Georgia continues to show solid performance and a
remarkable ability to mitigate internal and external shocks. Despite large increases in energy
prices and the loss o f part o f its traditional export market, fiscal and external current account
balances are in line with the program. The fiscal deficit on a cash basis was 2.9 percent o f
GDP in 2006 compared with 2.4 percent in 2005. With about one-third o f public spending
beingallocated to the social sectors, and another 12 percent allocated to infrastructure. As a
result public spendingis complementary to private investment and i s supportive o f growth and
poverty reduction. The external current account deficit widened in 2006 to 9.5 percent o f
GDP but remainedbelow the 9.9 percent expected inGeorgia's program with the IMF. With
a much improved business environment, foreign direct investment (FDI) flows were even
larger than anticipated and fully financed the external current account deficit and the
accumulation o finternationalreserves.

34.     GDP growth accelerated from 9.3 percent in 2005 to 9.4 percent in 2006. Growth
remained strong inthe first quarter o f 2007 (9 percent compared to 8.4 percent in2006 during
the same period).     The growth rate in Georgia for the last three to four years is quite
impressive, averaging to 8.9 percent through 2003-06, led by construction (280 percent
growth), financial intermediation (170 percent growth), and communications (110 percent
growth), while agriculture and manufacturing were generally stagnating. Since 2004 the
drivers o f growth started to shift toward the higher productive sectors owing to the active and
growing intervention o f external private capital, which allowed industrial production to
revive, particularly in2005 and 2006.

35.     Successful anticorruption measures in tax administration, measures to curb smuggling
activity at the Georgian borders, and improvements in the business environment resulted in a
significant improvement in Georgia's fiscal position and its performance. As a result o f the
reforms implemented, particularly the measures to curb corruption, over the last three years
tax revenue collection increased from 14 to 21 percent o f GDP. This major achievement
allowed the Government to start addressing social and basic infrastructure needs.

36.     Social spending to GDP has increased by about 5 percentage points to date and this
now represents more than one-third o f the budget. Social assistance policy has been targeted
to assist the most vulnerable in society-first      of all pension arrears were eliminated, and
pensions have been tripled since 2003. Furthermore, the Government introduced the targeted
social assistance program-a           cash-benefit program targeting the extreme poor-in
August/September 2006 as the most direct and effective means o f reducing extreme poverty.
In addition, the Government introduced the Basic Benefit Package for health care, which
includes a supplemental package for the poor. To reduce the negative impact o f energy price
increases on vulnerable segments o f the population, the budget covered the electricity lifeline
and the gas subsidy for the first four months of2006 and in2007 has substantially reduced the
negative impact o f external shocks. The benefits o f these measures are well acknowledged by
the population and contributed to a decline in overall poverty from 32.9 to 31 percent in
2005-06 andto a decline inextreme poverty from 11.4 to 10.7 percent duringthe same period.

37.      In addition, the Government has implemented large strategic investments in the
transport and electricity sectors which inturn have contributed to a strong growth performance
by the private sector. Public capital expenditures increased from 2.4 percent o f GDP in2004
to close to 4 percent in 2006 and the first half o f 2007. The external shocks, particularly the
large energy price increase had a significant impact on the economy and on the budget.
Despite these shocks, the Government contained the fiscal deficit at 2.9 percent o f GDP in
2006 compared to the 2.5 percent envisioned inPRGFprogram.

38.      Despite the inflationary pressures o f large capital inflows which contributed to a rapid
expansion of credit to the private sector, the Government implemented an appropriate
macroeconomic policy mix to safeguard growth and maintain inflation at single digit levels.

39.      The Government's commitment to pursue a stabilization reform agenda, along with its
strong macroeconomic performance, has increased confidence on the part o f businesses and
consumers. As a result, monetization inthe economy i s improving as reflected by increases o f
M3 to GDP from about 12 percent of GDP in 2003 to 19.5 percent in 2006 and to an
estimated 21 percent by end March 2007. At the same time, albeit from very low levels, there
has beena rapid expansion o f credit which has increased from 8.6 percent o f GDP in2003 to
18.9 percent in2006 and to an estimated 19.7 percent by end March 2007. This expansion o f
credit i s being accompanied by a rapid expansion o f construction and by increases in asset
prices. These monetary and financial developments pose important challenges to Georgia's
supervisory capacity and to the macroeconomic management o f credit boom-cum-asset price
increases.

40.      Going forward, key reforms in the banking sector include: (i)upgrading banking
supervisory policy; (ii)strengthening the implementation o f Base1 Core Principles for
Effective Banking Supervision, particularly the requirements for Fit and Proper Criteria for
bank administrators and large shareholders; (iii)     strengthening banking sector transparency;
and (iv) continuing the closing and/or merging o f commercial banks that do not comply with
prudential regulations. The developments and challenges in monetary policy and the banking
sector are being addressed primarily by Georgia's PRGF program with the IMF. These
reforms are central to reducing vulnerabilities to the changing structure o f external capital
flows.

41.     The external current account deficit widened from 5.4 to 9 percent o f GDP in2005-06
owing to large capital inflows, including privatization receipts. The widening o f the external
current account deficit was exacerbated by the real appreciation o f the lari. However, FDI
flows were even larger and fully financed the external current account deficit and the
accumulation o f internationalreserves.

42.     At the same time debt burden indicators continue to improve. The stock of public
external debt was substantially reduced from 27 percent o f GDP in 2005 to 24.7 percent in
2006 following a second round o f Paris Club rescheduling inmid-2004 and the discipline over
the last two years of refraining from contracting non-concessional debt. The Georgian
authorities have completed their negotiations with all o f the non-Paris Club members,
excluding Kazakhstan, for which negotiations are ongoing on US$27.7 million outstanding
debt. The remaining arrears on Turkmenistan debt have been reduced from an end 2005

amount o f US$101.0 million to US$39.5 million by the end o f February 2007. External debt
service to exports o f goods and services has fallen from 7.2 percent in 2005 to 6.7 percent in

indicators improving, the sovereign debt was rated at B+ for the first time by Standard&Poors
inDecember2005 andwas confirmed at this ratingat the endof2006.

43.      Insum, macroeconomic management has been effective in safeguarding stability and
growth and has shown an impressive ability to mitigate shocks. Furthermore, the quality o f
public finances is improving interms o f its transparency, and accountability, and also interms
o f its being supportive o f national priorities. Specifically, public expenditures are supportive
o f growth, and o f the development o f a fiscally sustainable social safety net, and are also
starting to address the large basic infrastructure needs.               As a result, the medium-term
prospects are positive.

(1-v) StrengtheningPublicAdministration

103.     Key issues. Inno other area does the legacy o f a failed state weigh as heavily as inthe
area o f public administration.           In Georgia, the structure of the executive branch was
ineffective, the mandates o f the individual government agencies were not clearly
distinguished, the number o f public employees was very large, and salaries were inadequate
and wage arrears common. These factors, coupled with the low skills o f civil servants,
contributedto the low level o f integrity inpublic service.

104.      Shortly after coming to power, the new administration adopted a new LawI4 to
introduce a cabinet style o f government.                The executive branch was reorganized and
streamlined. At this stage, the reform o f the state was driven by rightsizing and redefiningthe
role o fthe state and i s beingimplemented on a unit-by-unitbasis.

105.      Other ministries also undertook significant reshuffling and internal restructuring, in
some cases more than once. Over time, the Georgian authorities have found several common
principles beingapplied on a case by case basis as the role o fthe state is re-defined and results
in curbing corruption and improving public service is required from restructuring and re-
staffing reforms. First, the Government has restructured and re-staffed public entities where
corruption was pervasive such as the traffic police. Secondly, re-staffing i s being carried out
by selecting qualified candidates. Since corruption was pervasive inthe education system it is
difficult to assess potential candidates based on traditional curriculum vitas. Hence, aptitude
and technical tests are applied to identify potential candidates. Thirdly, new staff is provided
some basic training and those that perform at average or better levels are in most cases
provided term contracts. A fourth relatively common element is to provide much higher
salaries and strict penalties for misconduct and corruption.

106.     At the start o f the program, the Government and Bank staff envisioned a rapid

served as an input in to the policy deliberations within the Government Commission on State
Reforms on the options for the institutional restructuring o f Government entities and public
services. In addition, the Ministry o f Justice completed its functional review (with EU
support) and provided an action plan for its reorganization.

the role o f the state would take longer than expected. Having inherited an over-expanded

B.      Pillar 11: ImprovingElectricity and Gas Sector Services

118.    Key issues.         Until recently, Georgian consumers did not receive electricity at
acceptable service levels. Owing to a long period o f low tariffs, non-payment, energy theft,
and corruption in the energy sector, the financial flows from consumers to the sector were
insufficient to support adequate supplies of electricity to households and firms. Many years o f
neglect of maintenance and lack o f investments had caused the infrastructure to crumble,
which resulted in a situation in which in 2003 the national average electricity service per
household per day was 7 hours o f service. This meant that Tbilisi had some electricity some
o f the time and blackouts were frequent. The financial situation deteriorated to the point
where the Unified Electricity Distribution Company (UEDC)" and the power transmission
company the Georgian State Electrosystem (GSE), filed for bankruptcy protection in 2004.
Overall, the sector had accumulated a debt overhang constrained rehabilitation, maintenance,
investments innew capacity and, ultimately, operations.

This plan was updated under PRSO 11and now under PRSO I11to reflect the developments o f
the last several years which, through investments, improved operations and stronger financial
flows, have led to substantially better energy service to consumers (notably for electricity) and
have significantly reduced the risk o f a systemic collapse in the sector. The main companies
in the sector are now operationally viable owing to tariff increases, and operational
improvements, and privatizations are intendedto secure financial stability inthe future.

120.    As a result o f the concerted efforts o f the Government, the sector entities, the
management contractors, and the development partners, substantial improvements took place
in the sector. Most striking are the improvements in power supply andrevenue collections

only in the capital but in all regions o f the country, (see Annex III).     This enhanced service
level has been essential to the utilities' success in increasing collections, (in which, notably,
UEDC achieved outstanding results). A household survey in mid-2005 showed increased
satisfaction with electricity services, and UEDC surpassed 70 percent collections inDecember

C.      Pillar111: Improvingthe Environmentfor PrivateSector Development

133.    Key issues:The further development and promotion o f the private sector remains as a
key element in fostering growth and employment and in reducing poverty. Until recently,
significant problems remained regarding registration, licensing and permits associated with
establishing and operating a business, owing to highcosts, arbitrariness inprocedures, and/or
lack o f an enforcement mechanism. Widespread corruption, state overregulation, cumbersome

raise product and safety standards, and to remove technical barriers to trade and improve
competitiveness.

E.       Poverty and Social Impact Assessment

192.     The PRSO is designed to improve living standards in Georgia by (i)        strengthening
public sector accountability, efficiency, and transparency; (ii)  improving electricity and gas
sector services; (iii)  improving the environment for private sector development; and (iv)
improving social protection, education, and health care services. The two areas with the
greatest potential immediate impact on living standards are reforms in the power sector and
the social protection system.

193.     To assess the impact o f reforms in the power sector and the social protection system,
especially on the poor, a Programmatic Poverty Assessment program i s beingimplementedas
part o f the implementation o f the first three PRSOs. In addition, poverty monitoring and
assessment mechanisms have improved significantly during the PRSOprogram, which would
allow a much improved assessment o fthe impact.

194.     It should be noted that maintaining macroeconomic stability as a core o f PRSO
program policy actions has safeguarded the purchasing power o f households, particularly
those whose main source o f income i s wages. More important, the targeted social assistance
to the extreme poor introducedunderPRSO I1has been the most direct measure for mitigating
any negative impacts o f reforms on the extreme poor.

195.     Inenergy, the PRSO supports Government initiatives to attain financial viability for
sector entities. Payment collections inpower distribution have increased, mostly as a result o f
improved service delivery and the willingness o f consumers to pay for a more stable
electricity supply. Cost recovery, full collections, and control o f electricity theft have been
necessary to achieve sustainable electricity supplies at a service level adequate for economic
development and improved living conditions. The 2003 PSIA concluded that households in
Tbilisi had a power consumption level close to their basic needs and that demand was
inelastic, thus suggesting that tariff increases such as the one in June 2006 would be
associated with significant welfare losses. Under this PRSO, as energy reforms are targeted to
areas outside o f Tbilisi, welfare losses are likely to occur among this population group in the
short term and possibly in the medium term. To mitigate the impact o f higher tariffs on the
poor, the Government has adopted a bloc tariff based on the level o f consumption, and is
providing additional energy assistance to vulnerable groups.

196.     The bloc tariff system has predefined and increased tariff levels for low, middle, and
high levels o f consumption. Tariffs rates applied outside o f Tbilisi are approaching those
charged in Tbilisi. Many residential consumers in Tbilisi have individual meters, but outside
o f Tbilisi most residential consumers are paying on the basis o f communal meters that meter
consumption o f up to 70 apartments or houses. The Government has decided that consumers
on communal meters will pay the lowest bloc price. A program i s under way to replace many

                                                53

communal meters by individual meters, inwhich case monthly meter readings will tell ,which
block tariff to apply.

197.    The social protection system is undergoing a major reform with the objective o f
reducing the incidence o f extreme poverty. The Government introduced a landmark targeted
cash family poverty benefit in August/September 2006. The benefit targets the 14 percent o f
Georgian families (about 400,000 people) that were estimated to be under the extreme poverty
line in 2005. Families found eligible for the benefit (based on an assessment o f their social
andeconomic status) now receive aregular monthly cashbenefit.The specific benefit amount
depends on a household's assessed income and has the aim o f topping up existing income to
the extreme poverty benchmark.

198.    To assess the impact o f the reform o f the social protection system, two activities are
beingundertaken. First,the Government initiated an evaluation o f the poverty benefit early in
2007 to asses the program's first year. Second, as part o f the Programmatic Poverty
Assessment program, the effectiveness o f the reforms are being assessed.


F.      Risksand RiskMitigation

199.    Georgia faces significant but manageable macroeconomic risks and vulnerabilities.
On the domestic front these include: (i)   budgetarypressures and economic effects related to
the recent external shocks; (ii)further acceleration inthe growth o fcredit to the private sector;
and (iii)a limited response o f domestic investment and net job creation to the reforms.
External risks and vulnerabilities include: (i) further sharp increases inenergy prices; and (ii)
disruption in some traditional trade patterns and slow progress in trade and investment
diversification.  The continued implementation o f prudent macroeconomic policies and
structural reforms shouldrender these risks and vulnerabilities manageable.

200.    At the start o f the program there was the risk o f a limitedcapacity to coordinate and
implement reforms. However, over the last three years the Government has significantly
