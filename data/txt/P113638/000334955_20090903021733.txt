                                        I.       INTRODUCTION

     1.      The proposed Sixth Development Policy Loan (DPL-6) to Indonesia for USD 750 million
continues the series of annual single tranche DPLs that began in 2004. The first four DPLs, from 2004
to 2007, constituted a program anchored t o the FY04-FY08 Country Assistance Strategy (CAS). DPL-6 i s
the second o f a series o f three annual single tranche loans envisaged under the FY09-FY12 Country
Partnership Strategy. The first loan in this new series, DPL-5, was approved by the Board in December
2008. DPL-6 i s being presented t o the Board in late September 2009. In between, in early March, 2009, the
Board approved a U S D 2 billion contribution in the form o f a stand-alone D P L with DDO to the Indonesia
Public Expenditure Support Facility to provide contingent budget support in recognition o f the precautionary
steps Indonesia took to maintain the stability o f the financial system and sustain critical public expenditures
in the face o f the global crisis.

    2.       Like its predecessors, DPL-6 supports the Government o f Indonesia's continuing reform
efforts in three core areas: (i)improving the investment climate; (ii)strengthening public financial
management; and (iii)enhancing poverty alleviation and service delivery efforts. In particular, under
DPL-6, critical milestones will be accomplished in terms o f budget management with the procurement o f a
new treasury and budget system, important steps will have been taken t o establish a one-door integrated
investment licensing service and to further ease the burden o f tax compliance for Indonesian households and
firms, further progress will have been made in Indonesia's move towards performance-based budgeting, and
the allocation o f funding for the government's flagship community-based development program will have
been made even more pro-poor.

     3.       Indonesia's progress over the last year in the core policy areas that DPL-6 supports has
not been limited to the actions that were anticipated and explicitly included in the DPL policy matrix.
A new Public Service L a w was passed by Parliament in June, which represents an important step towards
civil service reform and promises to enhance service delivery. Amongst i t s other provisions, the law sets
forth the rights and obligations o f c i v i l servants, requires public agencies to formulate clear service
standards and establishes principles for handling o f complaints as well as meaningful sanctions for non-
performance or wrongdoing. The government has also maintained the momentum on the proactive and
preemptive approach to the global crisis it took in the last quarter o f 2008 and the first quarter o f 2009, and
has followed up, independently o f the DPL-6 discussions, on many o f the actions recognized and supported
by the Indonesia PESF (DPL-DDO). A draft presidential regulation on coordination o f national poverty
reduction efforts and the establishment o f a crisis monitoring and response team, which was circulated for
inter-ministerial consultations as one o f the prior actions for the PESF, has since been signed and issued by
the President. An inter-ministerial committee has been established t o formulate Indonesia's plan for
fulfilling the requirements o f EITI candidacy, and it has prepared and submitted to the Cabinet Secretariat a
Presidential Regulation for the implementation o f the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI) in
Indonesia. The regulation would require f i r m s in extractive industries t o report the amount o f revenues
conveyed to government, and government agencies at the central, provincial and district levels to report the
amounts o f such revenues received, with the results o f the reconciliation to be made public.

    4.       The steady progress on reforms, both those supported by DPL-6 as well as others, i s
noteworthy given the legislative elections in April and presidential elections in July as well as the
ongoing global crisis, all o f which dominated public discourse and demanded the attention o f policy-
makers. Though the legislative elections were, by all accounts, poorly managed by the National Election
Commission (KPU), with incomplete and inaccurate voter lists resulting in large numbers o f eligible voters
not being able to cast their votes, election observers also noted that there did not appear to be any systematic
disenfranchisement o f specific groups o f potential voters. And the results-a            tripling o f President
Yudhoyono's Democrat Party's 2004 vote share, the continuing decline o f Soeharto-era "legacy" parties and
poor results by Islamic parties-have been accepted by all parties. The July presidential election proceeded


                                                       1
relatively more smoothly. Approximately 72 percent o f Indonesia's 176 million registered voters cast
ballots. On July 25'h the KPU officially declared the incumbent President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono and
former Bank Indonesia Governor Boediono as winners o f the presidential election with 60.80 percent o f the
votes. President S. B. Yudhoyono's victory appears to be, not only nation-wide in scope, but equally strong
among almost all income, gender, ethnic and religious groups.

    5.       With a new government to take office in late October, discussions about a possible DPL-7
have necessarily been indicative, but the current government has mapped out a strong suggested
program of reform steps for 2010. The reform steps proposed by the current government as potential
indicative triggers for a possible DPL-7 further advance many o f the more focused reform aims supported
by the D P L series thus far. Amongst other steps, the government has proposed to continue i t s efforts to:

    strengthen investment service institutions by issuing detailed technical regulations on the
    implementation o f the One Stop Shops for investment licensing and establishing an operational online
    investment licensing system for certain key sectors
    streamline budget execution and improve cash management by completing the identification o f the
    relevant business process improvements needed to finalize the revision o f the Chart o f Accounts that i s
    t o be integrated into the new treasury and budget system
    improve the results orientation o f the budget process by using the new performance-oriented program
    structure in the preparation o f the 201 1 budget and in establishing indicative budget ceilings
    improve government accounting and audit functions by submitting a Presidential decree regulating
    government internal audit systems
    enhance the impact o f poverty programs by strengthening the role o f the National and Provincial
    Poverty Coordination Committees

     6.      Because o f the political calendar and the incumbent government's desire to secure
financing o f the 2009 budget prior to the transition, Go1 has requested earlier preparation and
approval o f the DPL than in past years. Given the progress on the prior actions and the strong likelihood
in the aftermath o f the July presidential elections that the direction o f reforms will remain unchanged and the
overall commitment to reforms will remain strong and possibly even intensify, the team has responded
positively to the Go1 and i s presenting DPL-6 to the Board in late September.

     7.      The Indonesian economy has proved quite resilient in the face o f the global economic
downturn and Indonesia's growth slowdown has come relatively later and been more moderate than
for most countries. Real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) growth in the year to the end o f the second quarter
o f 2009 was 4.0 percent, stronger than many market analysts had predicted and the third-highest in Asia
after China and India. Quarterly data suggest the economy has passed i t s trough. Growth was supported
primarily by domestic demand, with private consumption growing by 5.8 percent (the fastest pace this
decade), boosted by election-related spending and rising consumer confidence because o f declining inflation
and solid growth in agriculture. The latter two factors and the fact that for 2008 as a whole the Indonesian
economy expanded by 6.1 percent, only slightly below 2007's 6.3 percent pace, help explain why, despite
the marked growth slowdown in the fourth quarter o f 2008 and the first quarter o f 2009, the poverty rate s t i l l
fell by 1.2 percentage points from 15.4 percent in March 2008 to 14.2 percent in March 2009.

markets, there are signs o f returning market confidence. The rupiah has recorded one o f the largest
appreciations against the U S D in the region since December, while the stock market has risen by two-thirds
since January 1, the most amongst Southeast Asian stock markets. Yields on IDR-denominated government

bonds have fallen by over 400 basis points since the beginning o f the year. This reflects both external
developments, particularly improvements in global risk tolerance, as well as the Indonesia-specific factors o f
increasing market confidence in Indonesian policy makers' response to the crisis, the sustainability o f i t s
financing needs, and the orderly April parliamentary and July presidential elections. Yields on government

     9.      The global downturn has slowed Indonesia's economy, but the outlook i s better than for
many other economies. The deterioration in the external environment plus slower credit growth are likely
t o slow Indonesia's growth to 4 to 4.5 percent in 2009 before a gradual recovery in 2010 l i f t s growth to
around 5.5 percent. Indonesia's economy has been protected from the global slowdown by i t s relatively l o w
foreign trade-shares, robust domestic demand, and the impact o f earlier policy decisions to cut taxes and
increase social spending, which have been in effect in 2009. Meanwhile, the stabilization o f consumer prices
i s supporting consumer sentiment, which i s at recent highs. External financing needs, while considerable,
appear to be sustainable, although the risk o f significant capital flight remains. The increasingly positive
outlook was reaffirmed by the IMF's Article I V mission, which concluded in early June. The IMF raised i t s
projection o f 2009 growth to 3 to 4 percent (consistent with WB projections at that time) from i t s earlier
estimate o f 2.5 percent.

    10.     Despite the increasingly buoyant mood, the government remains vigilant and conscious of
the need for continued careful monitoring and management o f the economy in the face o f the risks
that lie ahead. Monetary policy has been eased, with Bank Indonesia lowering i t s policy rate by 275 basis
points between December 2008 and July 2009 to 6.75 percent, i t s lowest level under the current monetary
policy regime. The budget monitoring committee that was established as one o f the prior actions for the
PESF, has met and i s continuing to follow up on the actions needed to ensure effective implementation o f
the extraordinary measures that were adopted (under the PESF) to expedite budget disbursement. And the
central bank has entered into currency swap agreements with both China and Japan to buttress i t s foreign
exchange reserves.

     11.      Following W B Board approval o f the PESF on March 3, two o f the three other
development partners have now formalized their contributions to the PESF. In late March, the
Governments o f Japan and Indonesia signed the agreement under which Japan i s contributing the equivalent
o f U S D 1.5 billion to the PESF in the form o f guarantees for samurai bond issuance by Indonesia, backed up
by contingent budget support. Under this agreement, Indonesia successfully issued JPY 35 billion in 10-year
samurai bonds on July 17. The Asian Development Bank (ADB) Board, in early June, approved ADB's
contribution o f U S D 1 billion to the PESF in the form o f contingent budget support. The Government o f
Australia's U S D 1 billion contribution to the PESF i s expected to be finalized by late-August.

    12.     The likelihood o f a drawdown from the PESF this calendar year i s steadily diminishing.
The government had, as o f mid-August, raised over 82 percent o f the 2009 financing target indicated in the
financing plan issued in conjunction with the PESF. Yields on new issues have been falling since early
March, and the government has been able to sell more at longer tenors. Because it has exceeded i t s quarterly
financing targets thus far, the government has indicated that the earliest it "may" need t o request a
drawdown from the PESF i s after the end o f the third quarter. But even that appears increasingly unlikely
(though it cannot be ruled out), and senior government officials have begun to mention publicly the

                                                      3
possibility that the PESF may not be drawn down at all in 2009 but instead may be rolled over to provide
insurance against adverse market conditions in 20 10.

        13.     Despite these achievements, among others, which have contributed to the impressive
economic performance and remarkable transformation and consolidation o f the political system,
Indonesia needs to build on its current robust foundation and accelerate growth while ensuring that it
i s inclusive and sustainable. At the core o f most o f the challenges that constitute Indonesia's development
agenda today, there are a number o f key systemic processes and institutions for formulating and
implementing policy. These are the public financial management system public institutions rely on to
implement Government priorities and effectively spend public funds, the capacity and accountability o f the
c i v i l service, the decentralization framework determining the f l o w o f funds and the division o f
responsibilities between levels o f Government, the control o f corruption and the judiciary and legal system
which ensures the rule o f law. Over the next five years, the Government plans to continue implementingthe
reform agenda focusing on a subset o f these areas while increasing and improving the efficiency o f public
spending in priority sectors. Even though Bank support spans over a large number o f sectors and
Government systems, the DPL series has focused and will continue to focus i t s support on the reform o f core
central Government institutions and systems, namely, improvement o f investment climate, strengthening o f
public financial management system and systems for enhanced service delivery, key cross-sector
engagements under the new Country Partnership Strategy.

     1       COUNTRY CONTEXT
    A. Indonesia in 2009

     14.       Indonesia has made remarkable progress over the last decade in terms o f macroeconomic
 and political stability. Macroeconomic performance since the late 1990's has seen consistent output growth
 and rapid decline in external imbalances. Increasing tax revenues have contributed to the decline in the fiscal
 deficit. Increased growth has been grounded on increasing private sector investments and sustained domestic
 consumption. Under a scenario o f controlled inflation and moderate interest rates Indonesian citizens
                                                                           n
 managed to improve their living standards and reduce poverty levels. I addition, as the result o f a sustained
 decade o f relatively successful political and institutional reforms, Indonesia in 2009 i s a highly competitive,
 decentralized electoral democracy. Governments at national, provincial, district and city level are elected in
 free contests, with an incumbent loss rate o f about 40 percent (one o f the highest o f any competitive
`democracy). A system o f checks and balances between legislative, judicial, and executive branches i s
 increasingly in place, with no one branch o f Government able to dominate, and few institutional outcomes
 `guaranteed'. Where power was once radically concentrated around the presidency, it i s n o w shared with
 the legislature and where power was once concentrated in Jakarta, it i s n o w shared among 500 odd city,
 district and provincial Governments.

     15.     The foundation o f macroeconomic and political stability established in the last decade has
helped Indonesia weather the current global economic crisis. Indonesia's growth slowdown has come
relatively later and been more moderate than for most countries. But the global downturn, lower global
commodity prices, and tighter global financing will mean slower growth in 2009 and 2010 compared with
recent years. Public finances however remain in good health, with the budget barely in deficit in 2008 (-0.1
percent o f GDP) and public debt ratios continuing their secular decline. With a strong fiscal position,
Indonesia should be able t o cushion the adverse effects o f the global crisis on vulnerable groups, and
                                                           n
provide additional resources for development priorities. I the period 2010-14, the budget i s expected to
increase by more than 30 percent in real terms when compared to the 2005-2009 period (about US$500
billion in 2008 prices) creating significant opportunities to improve public services. But in order to seize
these opportunities Indonesia will need more effective and accountable institutions that can translate
available resources into better development outcomes. This will be particularly important as Indonesia


                                                        4
embarks on a period o f second generation reforms to provide, for example, more sophisticated services in
infrastructure, better education, and a sustainable health system.

B. The State o f the Indonesian Economy Entering Mid-2009

     16.     Indonesia's slowdown came relatively later and has been more moderate than for many
countries. GDP growth in the second quarter o f 2009 slowed to 4.0 percent y-0-y, stronger than most
commentators had expected and a relatively modest 2.4 percent points slower than in the year to the third
quarter 2008. (Figure 1) Quarterly data suggest that growth passed i t s trough by the second quarter. For
2008 as a whole the Indonesian economy expanded by 6.1 percent, only slightly below 2007's 6.3 percent
pace. Indonesia's growth held up longer, and the slowdown at the end o f the year, while sharper than
generally expected, was less dramatic than elsewhere in the region. (Figure 2). Nevertheless, the slowdown
late in the year was broad-based as private consumption, exports and investment all declined, with only
weaker imports and expanding Government spending providing some offset. Through the first half o f 2009,
the most externally-oriented parts o f Indonesia's economy continued t o be impacted by the bleaker global
conditions. But domestic demand recovered somewhat, led by private and Government consumption and the
industries supplying that demand, although this in at least part appears to be due t o spending related to the
parliamentary and presidential elections. A notable outlier to the general slowdown was the increase in
agricultural production o f 4.8 percent in 2008, the highest growth since 1992. While agriculture now
accounts for 14.4 percent o f total output, it continues to provide most or all o f the income for 42 percent o f
households and solid growth prospects in that sector appear t o be feeding into broader economic confidence.

     23.        Inflation has fallen sharply with the fall in commodity prices. Reductions in international
prices have passed into domestic and regulated prices (as fuel prices have been adjusted downward).
Consumer prices have stabilized: they barely increased between November 2008 and June 2009, and the
seasonal increases in July were less than usual, reducing the year-on-year inflation rate to 2.7 percent from a
peak o f over 12 percent in September 2008 (Figure 13). This i s despite the rupiah's deprecation o f up to
one-third during this period. The inflation rate has fallen even more for poorer households whose
consumption basket has a greater weight in food. Consumer inflation expectations are now at their lowest
level since early 2005, when actual inflation was running close to 5 percent. Upstream prices have been
falling since mid-2008: even by M a y 2009, as commodity prices had started to recover, wholesale prices
were s t i l l more than 8 percent below their July 2008 peak.

    24.        Indonesia's external position remains sound, though weaker than in 2007. Developments in
global commodity and financial markets buffeted Indonesia's balance o f payments throughout 2008. The
current account recorded a small surplus (USD 0.3 bn) over the full year, much smaller than the U S D 10 bn-
plus surpluses o f the previous two years. The bubble in o i l prices in the first half o f 2008 inflated first
Indonesia's fuel import bill and then i t s net income deficit as o i l producers transferred their profits abroad.
Portfolio outflows at the end o f 2008 moved the overall balance o f payments into a deficit o f
U S D 1.9 billion for the whole year, the first annual deficit since 2001 (Figure 14). The year-end portfolio
outflows were driven by both residents, who shifted U S D 2.7 bn into offshore bank accounts, and non-
residents selling down their holdings o f debt securities (net outflow o f U S D 3.8 bn from both corporate and
Government securities). These factors turned around in the first quarter o f 2009, with the balance o f
payment surplus recording a U S D 4.0 bn surplus. The current account surplus o f U S D 1.8 bn reflected a
large trade surplus more than offsetting small deteriorations in the net income deficit and workers'
remittances. Non-residents' purchases o f the Government's U S D and IDR bond issuance pushed the capital
account into a surplus o f U S D 2.4 bn. Other areas o f the capital accounts were relatively untouched by the
turbulence on financial markets, and Indonesia recorded i t s largest inflows o f foreign direct investment since
mid-2005 in the final quarter o f 2008 and the first quarters o f 2009.' External loans falling due were
generally rolled over (Figure 15). The overall balance o f payments remained in surplus in the second
quarter, according to preliminary estimates, with a wider current account surplus offsetting a capital account
deficit o f U S D 1.8 bn, and reserves stabilized above U S D 57 bn.

       26.    Indonesia's monetary and fiscal policy makers responded quickly to the deteriorating
global economy. Bank Indonesia shifted monetary policy from the anti-inflationary stance o f mid-2008,
cutting interest rates by 300 bps t o 6.5 percent between December and August 2009, their lowest level under
the current monetary policy regime. The Bank has signaled that i t s August rate cut i s likely t o be i t s last, and
i t s focus has shifted from supporting activity t o monitoring the outlook for inflation in 2010. B I has also
reduced the corridor between the interest rate banks receive on deposits at BI and pay on loans, and, during
the peak period o f financial market turbulence, injected liquidity into the inter-bank lending market.

     27.     The Government shifted fiscal policy to a more expansionary stance, including a 1.5
                                 n
percent stimulus package. I anticipation o f declining revenues and the need for fiscal stimulus,
Parliament approved an accelerated initial revision to the 2009 budget, with a 2.5 percent deficit target and
 1.5 percent fiscal stimulus. The revised budget seeks to stimulate demand by expanding Government
spending on infrastructure, and lowering taxes for specific sectors, in addition to the already-programmed
reductions in tax revenues. Given difficulties in programming and lags in spending, the Government views
tax cuts as the fastest and surest means o f short-term stimulus, and their large share in Indonesia's stimulus

subsidies compared with 2008. Domestic revenue reached IDR 346.7 tr or 40.9 percent o f the full-year
target, below the first half collection o f 43 percent o f 2008 revenue. With the cuts in tax rates, lower
commodity prices and compressed trade values, total first-half revenue was down 18.4 percent on a year
earlier. The excess o f revenues over spending created an IDR 8.8 tr surplus for the first semester 2009.

6 percent range, and especially strong results for agriculture, slower growth in food and fuel prices, and
especially the impact o f the Government's 2008-09 cash transfer program to poor and near-poor households
(BLT). With a continued slowing in growth, and the BLT ended in March 2009, poverty i s unlikely to fall
much further by early 20 10.

D. Political Outlook

     33.     As the result o f a sustained decade o f political and institutional reforms, Indonesia in 2009
i s a highly competitive, decentralized electoral democracy. Governments at national, provincial, district
and city level are elected in free and fair contests, with an incumbent loss rate o f about 40 percent (one o f
the highest o f any competitive democracy). Indonesia has a vibrant political party system, though ideologies
are not strong and candidate selection i s often subject to money politics. A system o f checks and balances
between legislative, judicial, and executive branches i s in place, with no one branch o f Government able to
dominate, and few institutional outcomes `guaranteed'. A vigorous free media thrives. Where power was
once radically concentrated around the presidency, the executive branch n o w has to share normal law-
making powers with the legislature. Where power' was once concentrated in Jakarta, it i s n o w shared among
500 odd city, district and provincial Governments.

    34.      Indonesian pride in ensuring high quality, international standard democratic elections
since 1999 was blunted by poorly managed 2009 national legislative elections. The central problem was
incomplete and inaccurate voter lists, resulting in millions o f eligible voters not being able to cast ballots in
the April Legislative Election Day, with the Supreme Court intervening at the last moment to allow voter
registration on site in the July Presidential contest.

     35.    I n the Legislative Election, data collected from civil society observers and party and
candidate poll witnesses illustrated the problems, showing a significantly larger number o f polling
stations and electoral districts reporting logistical problems, incomplete or inaccurate voter lists, and
intimidation or interference by party or candidate observers. In th e c o m p li c a te d legislative election,
eighty-percent o f polling places were reported to not have been fully equipped with all mandatory materials
(proper ballot boxes, correct ballot papers, clear instructions, finger ink, and tabulation forms).

    36.      Despite these outcomes the overall assessment o f the legislative elections was that the
election was not compromised in any meaningful, outcome-altering ways at the national level.
Election observers noted that while the election process was badly managed by the National Election
Commission, there was no systematic disenfranchisement o f specific groups o f potential voters. Throughout
Indonesia, observers witnessed h o w local officials and communities wrote out clear instructions for voters,
where they improvised secret polling booths, and where local communities closely watched and monitored
the process. This degree o f social control - massive nation-wide "buy-in" and "guaranteeing of'' the election

                                                       14
process cannot be overestimated and was a major factor in the overall success o f both this election, and
previous elections, in Indonesia.

    37.      I n the legislative contest there was a striking tripling of President Yudhoyono's Democrat
Party (PD) 2004 vote, the continuing decline o f the Soeharto-era "legacy parties" and poor results by
Islamic parties. Quite dramatic was the plunge for Golkar to about 14.5 percent (down from about 22
percent in 2004) and the continuing slide in support for former President Megawati's Indonesian Democratic
Party o f Struggle (PDIP) to about 14 percent also (down from 18.5 percent in 2004). The continuing (and
growing) appeal o f mainstream, more-or-less secular-nationalist parties, with fully 75 percent o f the vote
going to such parties, i s one o f the overall take-aways o f the legislative election.

    38.      The July 8 presidential election in Indonesia proceeded smoothly and peacefully.
Government and civil society organizations report relatively few problems in the voting and tabulation. The
National Election Commission's (KPU) voter lists, more accurate than in the April legislative election,
combined with a Constitutional Court ruling that helped unregistered voters to cast ballots, significantly
reduced the number o f citizens who may have otherwise not been allowed to vote. Approximately 72
percent o f 176 million registered voters cast ballots in over 450,000 polling stations.

     39.     On July 25'h the KPU officially declared the incumbent President Susilo Bambang
Yudhoyono and former Bank Indonesia Governor Boediono as winners of the presidential election
with 60.80 percent o f the votes. In addition, the President and his vice presidential running mate won over
50 percent o f the vote in at least 28 o f the 33 provinces. The President's victory appears t o be not only
nation-wide in scope, but equally strong among almost all income, gender, ethnic and religious groups.
According to post-elections reports the major post election shifts are likely to be: efforts by the President to
reduce the number o f party-held cabinet seats; and efforts to boost the strength o f the Office o f the President
vis-a-vis parliament and parties. The selection o f Boediono as the running mate vindicates the President's
inclination to choose technocrats and professionals over party figures.

    40.      The centrist, pragmatic, conservative approach o f most mainstream politicians has been
responsible for the best aspects o f Indonesian development in the past decade - the sensible, steady,
generally pe,aceful democratic consolidation and decentralization o f authorities - but this approach may
also slow o r blunt some o f the economic reform measures that are widely agreed as necessary. It i s this
Indonesian political preference for wide buy-in and moderation that has blunted the growth o f any radical
parties or allowed overly nationalistkhauvinist appeals to gain traction publically. However, it i s also this
conservative, `don't-rock-the-boat, we can work with anybody' approach to politics that has slowed or
blunted further some o f the economic reform measures or major infrastructure improvements needed.


E. Development Agenda            *
     41.     Despite impressive economic performance and remarkable transformation and
consolidation of the political system, Indonesia needs to build on its current robust foundation and
accelerate growth while ensuring that it i s inclusive and sustainable. Indonesia s t i l l has untapped
potential in commodity-driven sectors and despite the recent reversal o f the commodity prices, most
projections point t o a recovery, especially in emerging markets, which affords Indonesia the opportunity t o
attract investment in industries where recovery i s anticipated down the line. But a commodity-based growth
strategy will not be sufficient t o accelerate and sustain economic growth in the country. With i t s s t i l l young
population and rapidly growing labor force, Indonesia needs t o create jobs on a large scale by further
diversifying i t s export basket and facilitating market-led development o f new globally competitive clusters.
T o achieve this, Indonesia will need to address the key deterrents o f private sector development and

    42.      Higher levels o f growth have not translated, to the extent hoped for, into greater poverty
reduction. Despite recent signs o f progress, Indonesia lags behind i t s more prosperous neighbors in
producing higher value-added non-agricultural jobs. Employment growth has failed to match population

percent o f Indonesia's labor force s t i l l derived i t s livelihood from low-productivity activities in agriculture
and related areas, while only 30 percent o f Indonesia's growing labor force appears to have made the
transition to what are considered high-value added activities. Inclusive growth in Indonesia will also require
an increase in agricultural productivity and stimulation o f rural non farm employment. M o r e than half o f
Indonesia's population, and nearly two-thirds o f Indonesia's poor lived in rural areas in 2006. And even
though Indonesia's economy i s transforming rapidly with i t s steady and rapid urbanization, agriculture
remains the core for rural household incomes, employs the largest share o f labor and contributes the second
largest share (17 percent) o f GDP. Indonesia's agricultural product regulatory framework i s quite developed
but attention i s needed on capacity building and maintaining the integrity o f national systems with
decentralization. Because o f geographic and income-related disparities and the poor quality o f health, water
and sanitation and education service delivery at the local level, Indonesia's performance in terms o f human
development outcomes has been quite uneven over the last decade despite significant increases in public
expenditures. To reverse this, post-decentralization Indonesia needs to clearly assign functional roles and
responsibilities across different levels o f Government, resolve the issues around the unpredictability o f funds
and budget allocation adjustments and improve managements systems as well as generic and system-based
skills.

     43.     Inadequate environmental management i s leading to unsustainable degradation o f
Indonesia's considerable natural resources and increased vulnerability to natural disasters. T w o thirds
o f Indonesian's live in rural areas and are directly or indirectly dependent on communal land, and coastal
and environmental resources that are being depleted rapidly. The other third i s affected by environmental
conditions in urban areas, such as water and air pollution, flooding, congestion and noise. Given the strong
correlation between energy and GDP growth in Indonesia3, there i s tremendous pressure on the energy
sector to keep pace with economic growth. Indonesia's power demand i s likely to grow 7 t o 9 percent in the
next 10 to 15 years and nearly triple between 2005 and 2020, which will require a more sustainable
management o f energy resources. While in the energy sector improvements in the regulatory and
institutional front will be essential, sustainable environmental management in rural areas will require
addressing weaknesses in the judicial sector and in the rule o f law, including land use rights and land titling,
as well as capacity challenges. In urban areas, responding t o the challenges will imply a major effort o f
institutional strengthening o f local Government's ability to mobilize resources and manage expenditures,
including budgeting, development planning and management o f land and other municipal assets.

  1 1 THE GOVERNMENT'S REFORM PROGRAM AND BANK SUPPORT
    45.     The first five years after the 1998 Asian financial crisis were, understandably, about
restoring stability on both the macroeconomic and political front. The Government lacked the finances
to undertake many needed investments and the focus was necessarily o n fiscal consolidation and putting in
place the basic legislative framework for the new Indonesian polity. Within the space o f three years,
Indonesia passed a series o f laws completely remaking the state and undertook constitutional reforms, a l l o f
which were politically charged endeavors. On the economic front, the focus was on restructuring the
banking sector, which had collapsed as a result o f the crisis, and on restoring the fiscal position o f the
Government. There was little time or money available for efforts directeh at Indonesia's longer-term
development agenda. By the end o f the f i r s t five years post-crisis, however, macroeconomic stability had
been restored and, in the political arena too, things were stabilizing.

    46.      I n the last six years, as the hard-earned stability has been reinforced, fundamental
structural reforms have been initiated in several areas. A number o f new laws have been introduced or
existing laws amended and numerous regulations and decrees have been issued. To prepare for the end o f i t s
IMF program, the Government issued a White Paper in September 2003 that outlined plans for
macroeconomic and fiscal consolidation and a series o f structural reforms to restore confidence in the
Indonesian economy and boost investment. These plans were broadly maintained by the Government led by
President S.B. Yudhoyono who came to power in late 2004. This plan was incorporated into the
development agenda, articulated in the medium-term development strategies (Rencana Pembangunan Jangka
Menegah Nasional- RPJMN) 2004-2009 and the new RPJMN 20 10-2104 under development currently.
Finally, a history o f fiscal prudence has now provided the Government with the fiscal space needed to meet
Indonesia's rising development challenges.

    47.       The Government i s fully aware that if Indonesia i s to realize its potential as a competitive,
inclusive, sustainable and resilient middle-income country, it has to adapt the institutions and
mechanisms that govern the functioning o f the state and shape state-society interactions.The country's
authorities understand that the central challenges Indonesia faces today in realizing i t s development agenda
are all, in one form or another, o f an institutional nature. They entail furthering Indonesia's yet incomplete
governance transition by improving the effectiveness o f public institutions and processes through longer-
term institutional and process transformations. Even in areas where additional financing i s clearly needed -
for instance, from the private sector in the case o f many infrastructure sectors, or from the public sector if
the restructuring o f Indonesia's health system i s to be financially viable - institutional challenges need to
first be sorted out for that financing to be forthcoming and for it t o be effectively deployed.

A. Kev Reform Directions of Government Svstems and Institutions

A. 1 Investment climate

     48.     The Government has made significant effort$ to improve the investment climate. Notable
recent reforms include the passage o f the amended Income Tax Law, which reduces corporate and personal
rates4, simplifies administration and increases legal certainty; the passage o f the Tax Procedures L a w which
strikes a better balance between the rights and obligations o f taxpayers and the powers o f tax officials, an
issue of longstanding concern to the business community; the passage o f the law recognizing electronic
signatures which clears the way for multiple e-Government initiatives such as electronic registration and
filing o f tax returns; or the passage o f a landmark new Investment L a w providing a single legislative
framework for domestic and foreign investment which requires, for the first time, a single comprehensive
investment negative list. Economic policy packages covering the investment climate have been introduced
and major reforms o f customs administration are underway. Judged in terms o f the volume and substance o f

 In order to stimulate investment, the creation o f new companies and job creation Indonesia needs to encourage simpler

 time and costs in the export and import processes. Many economies have worked to introduce practices that enable
 them to reach those objectives by providing electronic filling o f trade documents (through electronic data interchange

 possibilities for paying bribes. But to avoid a dual electronic and manual customs clearance process, the new system

 documents. A recent study calculates the loss fiom export delays at around 1 percent o f trade for each extra trade day.
 For perishable agricultural products the cost i s nearly 3 percent o f the volume o f trade for each day's delay. Another
 study finds that each extra signature an exporter has to collect reduces trade by 4.2 percent.

B.2 Judiciary Reform

    61.     The judiciary and associated law-enforcement agencies are perhaps the weakest link in
Indonesia's governance and accountability system. Uncertainty about, and the arbitrariness of, judicial
interventions continues to be identified as one o f the most serious obstacles to investment. In the initial years
following reformasi a number o f independent judicial review and oversight mechanisms were established
along with several other bodies. But the momentum generated by these initiatives has not translated into
substantive progress and reform o f the national legal system has slowed considerably in recent years. There
appear to be t w o main and inter-related reasons for this. The first i s the resistance to reforms from
entrenched interests in the legal system while the second i s the growth in the number o f institutions with
judicial and oversight powers, which has led t o significant contestation between institutions. In the absence
of substantive reform at the national level, efforts are being made to strengthen accountability from the
bottom up by nurturing "demand for justice" at the local grassroots level. With support from donors, the
Government i s making efforts to enhance community legal awareness and to strengthen village level dispute
resolution processes and out-of-court mediation services, therefore, generating a demand for better
performance f ro m justice sector institutions at the local level. In this environment, the process o f creating a
normative framework at the national level with an appropriate system o f checks and balances i s likely t o be
a long-term process, especially when undertaken through trial and error, and to require substantial support.

D. Lessons L e a r n e d

    74.     Over the course o f the DPL program, several important lessons have been learned. These
lessons stem from the nature and evolution o f the relationship between the Bank and the Government, and
also from the characteristics o f the D P L program as a means o f offering policy support, together with the
changing policy environment on the ground. Some o f the lessons may be particular to the Indonesian case

                                                        27
because o f the unique nature o f the Bank's engagement with the Government o f Indonesia and the range o f
resources that the Wo rl d Bank program in Indonesia has available through trust funds.

     75.      Lesson 1: Strong Government ownership and committed counterparts are vital, but just
as important i s ownership over the pace o f the reform process. As a growing middle-income country,
Indonesia can access domestic and international capital markets, thus offering it a range o f financing
choices. While the Bank's financing i s attractive, the driving reason for the Government's engagement with
the Bank through the DPL program i s the utility o f the instrument in bolstering, locking in and accelerating
critical reforms. To succeed, DPLs not only need the commitment o f Government at the highest level but
also that o f mid-level officials responsible for implementing the reforms and following progress on a daily
basis. In areas where broad consensus i s s t i l l evolving, the pace o f implementation may be slower than
desired but their very inclusion in the DPL-supported agenda maintains a focus on the policy issue, prompts
on-going deliberation o f policy options, strengthens the resolve o f reform champions, and may eventually
lead t o reform breakthroughs.

     76.       Lesson 2: Progress on institutional reforms need not be linear and unidirectional and
there should be sufficient flexibility for modifications and increased complexity as the reform
program evolves. Unlike stroke-of-the-pen reforms, institutional reforms that involve changing the way the
Government works, especially in the face o f entrenched interests and organizational cultures, are
complicated, often unpredictable undertakings for which there are no simple recipes. Trial and error i s part
and parcel o f the process and there may be the occasional dead-ends that will require backtracking and
finding an alternate route. In a lively democracy, in particular, with multiple centers o f power and competing
interests, the momentum for specific reforms m a y ebb and flow. What i s needed in this context i s patience
and a continued sense o f urgency and preparedness so that when the right opportunity presents itself,
reforms that had otherwise been apparently slowed, may be advanced rapidly.

    77.     Lesson 3: DPLs are only one instrument among many that are available in a multi-faceted
engagement, and the choice of instrument should be contingent on the issue, the political context and
the institutional circumstances. This i s related to Principle 3 o f the Good Practice Principles on
Conditionality but i s particularly applicable in the Indonesian case because o f the size and scope o f the
W orld Bank program here. The Wor l d Bank program in Indonesia i s one o f the largest o f any country in
terms o f number o f staff and overall resources. Large teams are working closely on a daily basis with
Government counterparts at often very senior levels o n a wide range o f issues from public financial
management and trade and investment, finance, public expenditures and poverty. In this context the D P L i s
not the only instrument for supporting reform. In fact, at times, and on certain politically-sensitive and
contentious policy issues, such as subsidies reductions, it may be the wrong instrument.

     78.      Lesson 4: Mapping out a multi-year reform program at the outset, when possible, helps
provide meaningful direction, substantive results, and makes the reforms more sustainable. The D P L
 1 to 4 program was not at the outset envisioned as a four-year program. Instead it evolved into one as the
progress each year led to confirmation, often late in the process, that the series would continue the next year,
which meant that the program was continuously a work in progress. The commitment to furthering the
various reform objectives was always strong, but there was not always the time to map out in advance a
detailed strategy and a multi-year agenda under each o f the various reforms aims that the D P L program
eventually ended up supporting. As a result, under some o f the reform streams, the annual selection o f
triggers for the subsequent operation was sometimes a rushed exercise in determining what was feasible and
consistent with advancing the relevant reform a i m rather than a more strategic consideration o f what the
most critical next step might be. That represents a missed opportunity to realize the full potential o f the D P L
process.

    0   Strengthen Investment Service Institutions and Regulations

     93.        Investment negative list: While the July 2007 investment negative l i s t (DNI) was more

In 2007 a new credit guarantee facility - the People's Entrepreneurship Credit Program (Kredit Usaha
Rakyat) - was established to partially guarantee credits to SME's. Under the KUR scheme, the banks
assume 30 percent o f the credit risk and the credit guarantee facility undertakes the remaining 70 percent. In
addition, following the issuance, in January 2008, o f PP no2/2008 that allows for the establishment o f
national and provincial credit guarantee and re-guarantee companies, the Ministry o f Finance issued, in
December 2008, a regulation (PMK No. 222/2008) which clarifies, among others, the legal status o f these
companies, their ownership structure, reporting mechanisms and investment options. Under the new
regulation, not only new privately owned but also state-owned companies can be established both at national
and provincial level. The regulation also stipulates how to guarantee sharia based loans. Despite these
important steps, other constraints will s t i l l need to be addressed and it i s expected that after the ongoing
revision o f the KUR, new regulations can be issued to improve the Government credit guarantee programs.

B.2. Policy Area 11: Public Finance Management


     100.     The P F M reform program has progressed in key areas but challenges remain. A State
Budget combining the previous separate recurrent and development budgets has been institutionalized and
budget transparency improved. The implementation of a TSA as established by State Treasury Law No.
 1/2004 i s ongoing with the expansion in 2009 of daily sweeps o f Government revenue deposits to all
commercial bank branches. The public procurement environment has witnessed improvements in the last
few years with the establishment o f an independent agency - LKPP -which i s responsible for planning and
development of strategies, policies and regulations associated with procurement using public funds. The
GO1 i s preparing for the implementationo f an IntegratedFinancial Management System (SPAN) to replace
the multitude o f financial data processing applications currently used by Government institutions.
Government Regulation 60/2008 on Government's Internal Controls defines more clearly the roles and
responsibilities o f the different players in internal audit; and the external audit institution. The Financial and
Development Supervisory Board (BPKP) has received significant increase in its operating budget to fund an
increase in the number o f auditors as well as regional offices. Powerful independent oversight and
prosecutorial institutions such as the Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK) and the Anti-Corruption
Court have begun to deliver tangible results and parliamentary oversight o f budgets processes has been
strengthened.

     101.      I t will be crucial to ensure coherence and momentum in PFM reform. In order to be
effective, Indonesia's budget process should focus on results, particularly in those areas that are policy
priorities o f the Government. To achieve this, the budget needs to be structured along program lines, where
the policy priorities can be easily identified, and where the results o f the activities supported by the budget
can be monitored and assessed. In addition, the activities must be sustained over a number o f years. A
strictly annual approach to budgeting limits the Government's ability to plan and execute i t s budget in a
strategic way, in alignment with both the Government's political priorities and with overall fiscal
sustainability. It will also be necessary to ensure greater integrity and more effective management of public
funds through: (i)     continued implementation o f a TSA by automating the treasury payments system and
institutionalizing measures to prevent the proliferation o f off-budget bank accounts; (ii)     strengthening of
internal audit institutions, by establishing synergistic relationships between them while removing overlaps;
(iii) implementation o f a new treasury system, extending implementation of Government Accounting
Standards and re-engineering o f underlying business processes at spending agencies to ensure reliable
financial reporting; and (iv) widen the reforms throughout Government by extending modern financial
accountability and management processes to sub-national Governments bringing these fully in line with
emerging national standards. The current operation aims at supporting the Government addressing some of
those constraints as follows:

        Budget Formulation

    102.    Medium-Term Program Structure and Expenditure Framework: While the budget has
been unified for presentation and reporting purposes, the budget preparationprocess continues to suffer from
a de facto continuation o f dual budgeting, with few links between discretionary and non-discretionary
expenditure and less emphasis on expenditure prioritization. The 2008 budget included for the first time a
Medium-Term Fiscal Framework based on aggregate macro-fiscal forecasts o f both revenues and
expenditures for two out-years. However, budget plans continue to be prepared on a strictly annual basis and
medium-term public investment needs are not yet addressed in a systematic framework that reconciles
medium-term budgetary costs with aggregate resources. To improve the results orientation o f the budget
process, the Government has developed a revised program structure for the WJMN 2010-14 with
corresponding key performance indicators, which i s expected to be endorsed by December 2009, soon after
the new Government takes office. The National Development Planning Agency (Bappenas) and MoF have
developed joint manuals and a joint circular by both Ministers has been signed on June 19th providing

                                                       36
guidance to the 76 budget holders on h o w t o develop a revised program and activity structure in the process
o f preparing the 2010-14 RPJMN. Since the new program structure should also be used for the 201 1 budget
preparation, it i s expected that the indicative ceilings for the 201 1 budget will be established based on the
new program structure and this structure will be used in the new budget formats. Given that these are
difficult mind-set changes, trial and error type o f reforms and improvements will need, t o continuously be
made, based on lessons learned, it i s important for the D P L program to continue providing visibility and
support to this reform agenda.

     103. Performance-Based Budgeting: T o implement Performance-Based Budgeting (PBB) within
the context o f a Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF), DG Budget will need to collect and
analyze budget proposals from the line ministries in a framework different from what has been doing before.
While it has been comparing the information on routine expenditures received from line ministries against
standard costs, under a performance-based budget, DG Budget analysts will need to have the skills to
analyze the proposed programs, their expected outcomes, and make recommendations based on their
analysis. While this i s a long-term goal, a first step i s for DG Budget to request additional information from
line ministries in their budget submissions, including information on expected outputs and forward estimates
for at least three budget years. This i s already being asked for in the new MTEF-PBB preparation manuals
which have been finalized and officially launched on July 14'h. This i s only the beginning o f the reform
since the new budget formats will have to be tested, consultations with key stakeholders undertaken,
including the Parliament, the new framework approved and finally rolled out to all line ministries and public
budget entities.

    0   Budget Execution

      104.    Budget Disbursements and Reporting: 50 percent o f capital expenditures are still occurring in
the last quarter o f the fiscal year which disrupts project implementation cycles and increases under-spending
o n needed infrastructure investments. Slow and back-loaded disbursements are symptoms o f more severe
challenges that are encountered at each stage o f the public expenditure management cycle, symptoms of a
comparatively rigid budget execution process. Detailed input controls make it difficult t o alter the budget
during execution. Spending warrants (DIPAs), while n o w issued at the start o f the fiscal year, contain
excessive amounts o f detail leaving little flexibility for adjustments in the composition o f inputs needed to
carry out a given activity. Re-allocations across DIPAs from delayed programs to better performing ones
require lengthy revision, sometimes involving parliament. Budget users do not effectively use the authority
delegated t o them to vire budgets between certain budget items, either because the authority has not been
clearly understood or due to an inherent preference to pass on the responsibility to M o F (Directorate General
for Budget - DGB- and Directorate General for Treasury -DGT). At the same time, it i s expected that with
the implementation o f the automated SPAN system the timeliness, reliability and transparency o f budget
disbursement and reporting will be significantly increased. The contract for providing the SPAN turnkey
solution has already been signed on July loth and it i s projected to become operational in some pilot offices
o f the treasury beginning 201 1 and then rolled out to the State Treasury Service Offices (KPPNs) and pilot
line ministries starting 2012. Within one year it i s also expected that DG treasury will have identified and
completed improvements in the business processes and that a final version o f the Chart o f Accounts will be
available and integrated into SPAN. Given that SPAN will not address all the budget disbursement
constraints mentioned above, the D P L program process will continue to support discussions and
consultations regarding a comprehensive package o f measures to streamline budget execution.

    105.    Cash Management: Even though since 2007, the Treasury Single Account for expenditure
management has been made operational in all M o F Treasury offices, an annual census o f Government bank
accounts has revealed that a large number o f accounts are s t i l l outside the coverage o f the TSA. In addition,
the current T S A arrangement does not adequately cover bank accounts used for revenue collection. Poor
cash flow monitoring has perpetuated year-end bunching o f expenditure. The accelerated draw down of
Government cash balances in December perpetuates wasteful procurement practices and also impacts

                                                       37
monetary policy. There have been significant developments t o improve this situation and further consolidate
the Treasury Single Account (TSA). While the next day daily sweep o f revenue accounts into the T S A for
all commercial banks i s already happening, the recently issued M o F Decree (1 16/2009) mandates that by
January 2010 all revenue accounts maintained in collecting bank branches will be swept to the T S A by the
end o f the same day. The same day daily sweep pilot for some banks started on July 1'' in the Western
region. This reform coupled with the MOU between the M o F and B I requiring B I to pay interest on cash
balances in the TSA, will provide incentives to optimize cash balances. It will also help to better target
investment o f surplus cash. M o F i s also committed to continue the reform process by improving cash
forecasting. This will be accomplished through issuance o f a new regulation, the introduction o f
mechanisms to improve the performance o f Satkers in preparing budget forecasts and undertaking capacity
building programs for treasurers on cash forecasting. D P L 6 and D P L 7 will support the attainment o f these
actions.

    106.    Management o f State Assets: Government assets are not comprehensively recorded, properly
organized and fairly valued. An integrated and automated asset management system, which will enable the
Government to optimize the use o f i t s assets across ministries and regions, i s being implemented to facilitate
the attainment o f that goal. About 60 percent o f the Government budget holders have already been surveyed
and placed in the inventory (data collected from 12,800 o f 22,300 Satkers). A simultaneous evaluation o f
assets acquired prior to 2005 i s also being carried out. In addition, all current acquisition o f assets i s being
recorded through the application software installed in the 70 state wealth and auction service offices (KPK-
NL) across the country. U p o n completion o f the data collection process, the database will be launched.
Another important milestone was recently achieved with the issuance o f the M o F decree No. 102/2009
which requires Satkers, KPKNLs and KPPNs to reconcile their data on Government assets. In 2010 the
reform agenda will be pursued further with the initial establishment o f the database for state asset
information system and i t s integration with the Government accounting system (SIMAK-BMN). This
reform i s critical to promote better evaluation o f program outcomes as it provides a more realistic
framework for costing use o f resources.

      107.    Public Procurement: Despite some improvements over the last few years, the Government i s
aware o f the significant deficiencies in the public procurement system. One o f LKPP's (Lembaga Kebijakan
Pengadaan Barang/Jasa Pemerintah) or National Public Procurement Office priorities i s the improvement
o f the regulatory framework for public procurement, particularly important under decentralization, as it
provides an overall framework for all agencies implementing public procurement. T o achieve that, LKPP i s
currently working on two tracks - the preparation o f a new procurement law and the revision o f Presidential
Decree (Keppres) 80. LKPP has started working on the background documents for a draft procurement law
(academic papers) and expects to have the academic papers as well as a first draft o f the legal text o f the new
procurement law by September 2010. The enactment o f the law might, however, be a lengthy process as it
will require wide consultations and consensus building. Having the academic papers as well as a first draft
o f the legal text o f the new procurement law i s a proposed trigger for DPL7. In the meantime, the short term
objective was to revise Kepress 80/2003 with the intention o f mandating a set o f national standards bidding
documents to be used by all agencies. L K P P has recently submitted a draft o f the revised Keppres
no.80/2003 for inter-ministerial review. While the actual issuance o f the presidential decree might be
delayed, having a revised Keppres by October this year would represent an important reform in the way the
20 10 budget i s implemented. The development o f a clear professional stream o f procurement experts,
development o f a national procurement capacity building strategy, expanding the use o f e-procurement are
additional priorities for the coming years which will be supported through the D P L program.

    108.     Government Audit and Accounting Functions: According t o recent assessments, weaknesses
in internal control pose a significant risk to reforms introduced in others areas o f public financial
management. The internal audit function in the country i s classified as fragmented and characterized by
weak capacity. K e y reform areas identified for internal control and internal audit include the r o l l out o f
SPAN, the implementation o f COS0 control framework and strengthening o f the Government internal audit

                                                       38
function. SPAN, already mentioned before, i s expected to strengthen internal controls by introducing a
formal commitment control system in the line ministries, ensuring adherence o f the budget ceilings and
reducing the time lags in processing payments and budget revisions. Through Government Regulation (PP)
60/2008, the Government has not only adopted COS0 as i t s internal control framework but also given
BPKP the mandate for strengthening the internal audit function in the country. The key challenge will now
be to implement the framework and develop the required capacity in line ministries and Bawasdas in local
Governments. DPL-6 and DPL-7 will provide support to this crucial reform agenda, in particular to the
process o f issuance o f a PresidentialDecree regulating Government Internal Audit Systems as required by
article 5 8 o f PP60/2008 as well as to the process o f developing an internal audit strategy and internal control
system based on PP60/2008. I terms o f accounting systems DPL-6 and DPL-7 will also support the process
                                 n
for submission of the draft Government regulations on accrual-based accounting to the President. This will
lay the foundations for attainment o f the 2014 Government objective o f moving to full accrual accounting in
a l l line ministries and sub-national Governments.

    0   Civil Service Reform

     109. Bureaucratic Effectiveness: DPL5 supported the initiation o f bureaucracy reform in the
Supreme Court and Supreme Audit Agency (BPK) under the aegis of the National Committee for
Bureaucracy Reform chaired by MenPAN, and monitored by KPK, as well as the continued implementation
of the bureaucracy reform within the MoF. At the time no further follow-up actions were identified not only
because it was not clear as to when and how MenPAN intended to expand the reforms to other institutions
and/or regional Governments, including MenPAN itself, but also because it was felt as highly unfeasible to
expect major revisions in the legal framework for civil service reform during an election year. However,
since Government's commitment i s high and there are high expectations that the reform will receive a new
boost with the incoming President, discussions are ongoing regarding the possibility o f including Civil
Service Reform milestones in DPL7. This will be discussed and confirmed with the new Government.

B.3. Policy Area 111: Service Delivery

     110.    Beyond allocating more resources to poverty alleviation programs, several critical reform
initiatives are being pursued to enhance the delivery and effectiveness of these programs, and
maximize the impacts o f increased funding. These include improvements in targeting o f poverty
alleviation programs and measurement o f poverty, to ensure these programs reach the appropriate
beneficiaries, as well as improvements in the data and information which are required to make sound policy
decisions. Managing the impact o f the global financial crisis and subsequent inevitable slowdown of the
Indonesian economy on poverty reduction efforts will also require timely and reliable data, in conjunction
with improved targeting and delivery o f Government programs and services. This DPL will support
Government's reform objectives in terms of enhancing the pro-poor impact o f public expenditures by
increasing funding for poverty programs and by improving the targeting o f such programs as well as
improving poverty measurement and transparency in computing poverty.

    111. Increasing funding for poverty programs: The National Community Empowerment Program
(PNPM Mandiri), a program o f community block grants and the Government's flagship anti-poverty
program i s being scaled up in terms o f geographical coverage and the size of the average block grant i s
being increased at the same time. By the end o f 2009, the program will reach every sub-district (kecarnatan)
across Indonesia (6,400 sub-districts, o f which around 4,500 are located in rural areas) and full coverage
throughout the country will be maintained in 2010. Central Government funding for the entire PNPM
program i s planned to increase 23 percent, from Rps 10.3 trillion in 2009 to an estimated Rps. 12.7 trillion in
2010. The Government has also introduced poverty targeting to determine the size o f community block
grants, so that the increased funds can be allocated more effectively. During the coming year, the
Government also plans to analyze key issues regarding the future o f PNPM Mandiri and come up with
recommendations regarding quality improvement o f the sector poverty programs, the future role o f central

                                                       39
Government vis-&-vis local Governments in terms o f P N P M implementation, P N P M transition period,
linkage o f PNPM and the attainment o f the MDG's by 2015 among other key elements. This process will
also be linked to the discussions and development o f the new RPJMN around the new medium-term
Government's poverty strategy. The D P L will provide visibility and support to this core policy dialogue
while the technical teams will engage actively with the Government in the discussions as requested.

     112.    Improving pro-poor planning and budgeting systems: DPL-5 supported the publication o f
manuals with guidelines for pro-poor budgeting and planning for local Governments as well as i t s
dissemination to 400 local Governments, as one mechanism to improve pro-poor planning and budgeting
systems in the country. During 2008, Bappenas tested those tools and provided capacity-building in 18 pilot
districts. Over the course o f next year it expects to apply the lessons learned from the pilot experiments to all
local Governments. On the other hand, while the recently issued Presidential Decree on Coordination o f
National Poverty Reduction created local poverty committees, the Government recognizes that the
coordination between these local committees (TKPK-D) and the national committee (TKPK) needs to be
strengthened. One way to accomplish that will be to make Bappedas act as local focal points.

      113.     Improving targeting o f poverty programs: While aggregate funding for poverty alleviation
programs has steadily increased in the last few years, there are concerns that because poor households are
not identified and targeted accurately-whether eligible households are excluded or vice versa-the increase
in funding for poverty programs has not led t o more significant decrease in Indonesia's poverty rate. Many
Government poverty alleviation programs such as the unconditional and conditional cash transfer programs
(BLT and PKH), rice subsidy (Raskin) and health insurance (Jamkesmas), are directed to households and
families. However, they are currently implemented by different line ministries, each using an independent
database o f beneficiaries. To improve the effectiveness and coordination o f poverty programs, and reduce
duplication o f efforts in data collection, the Government intends to improve the accuracy o f information on
poor households, and move towards the use o f a single database. BPS has completed the pilot experiments
comparing different targeting methodologies, and the results are now being analyzed to determine an
improved the targeting design. At the same time the Government i s also conducting assessments to establish
the institutional mechanism required for the regular update o f the unified database. For 2010 it i s expected
that the unified database will be established and the institutional and financing arrangements finalized. Since
it i s crucial t o gain support from line ministries for the adoption o f the unified database, both D P L 6 and
D P L 7 will support that process.

     114.      Improve poverty measurement: Accurate information about poverty conditions across the
country i s needed to be able to make sound policy decisions and allocate resources effectively. Poverty
statistics, along with unemployment figures, are some o f the most controversial numbers released by BPS.
Part o f the controversy stems from the lack o f transparency on how the figures are obtained and calculated.
BPS intends not only to improve the current methodology for measuring poverty but also engage in a
consultative process with major stakeholders, both from Government and non-Government, prior to the
revision o f the methodology being finalized. The rationale for methodological changes, as well as their
impacts, will need to be well documented, disseminated and publicized, both before and after the changes
are implemented to promote ownership and understanding o f the reform. Given the need for such an
engagement strategy with stakeholders D P L 6 will support the process for undertaking the diagnostic study
o f the needed revisions to the methodology while D P L 7 will support the consultation process and the
revision o f the poverty measurement method itself.


C. Participatorv Processes and Consultations

   115.   Processes o f democratic consolidation and decentralization o f authorities in Indonesia
have translated in a political preference for wide buy-in and participatory, consensus-building
approaches to decision-making, not only in terms o f regulatory reforms but also planning and budgeting

                                                       40
processes. While this i s believed t o have slowed or blunted further some o f the reform measures needed, it i s
believed to mitigate against radicalism, steadily improve public participation and contribute to a more
democratic, accountable, professional and responsive governance. This commitment has been formalized
and endorsed through several regulations (e.g. L a w no.25/2004 on National Development Planning; Joint
Ministerial Decree 2006 and 2007 on Musrenbang), including those institutionalizing the creation o f multi-
stakeholder consultation forums - Musrenbang (Musyawarah Rencana Pembangunan) - at all levels,
concerning several time frames. Musrenbang i s the principal process for negotiating, reconciling and
harmonizing differences between Government and non-Government stakeholders and reaching collective
consensus on development priorities, including regulatory reforms, and budgets and while challenges s t i l l
remain, the instrument i s being increasingly adopted at all Government levels.

     116.    Beyond the officially recognized participatory processes, various aspects o f the reforms
supported by this operation have been subject to extensive specific stakeholders consultations. On the
investment climate area, the draft Presidential Regulation on the investment negative l i s t was intensively
discussed within Government through bilateral and multi-ministerial meetings but also with Kadin
(Indonesian Chambers o f Commerce and Industry) and Foreign Chambers; the One Stop Shops regulation
was also discussed through a series o f meetings between the Ministry o f Trade and Coordinating Ministry
for Economic Affairs and the relevant sectors including Kadin; a decision on the Indonesian National Single
Window operational model has and will continue t o require hard discussions among Government agencies
up to the expected plenary session o f ministers. On the public financial management and poverty alleviation
areas a large number o f seminars and workshops have been undertaken in the last twelve months among
different Government agencies but also jointly between the administration and other key relevant
stakeholders not only to discuss technical issues but also reach agreements on policies and reform activities.
The importance o f participatory processes becomes very clear in the implementation of, for example, the
revision o f Keppres no.80/2003, which requires wide consultation among Government and non-Government
agencies, and the revision o f the poverty measurement methodology were such processes are critical to
ensure ownership and implementation o f the new methodology.


D. The Future Propram

     117.    The policy focus of DPL6 i s closely aligned with the Government's current reform
priorities and the feasibility o f specific reforms. Through discussions over the last six months as part o f
the ongoing engagement with the core economic ministries, culminating in a series o f half-day meetings
with key counterparts in each o f the relevant areas or sub-areas, a roadmap o f milestones was developed that
conforms t o the Government's o w n reform agenda. The ideas that emerged from these strategy sessions are
laid out in the D P L program matrix in Annex 3 but will be adjusted and refined as progress continues to be
assessed and discussions mature. The matrix includes the prior actions o f DPL5, the current prior actions
(DPL6) and indicative actions for DPL7. For each year o f the program, the matrix also details a number o f
benchmark actions or milestones that represent important components or prerequisites in the reform process.
It should be noted that those for a DPL-7 are indicative and will be negotiated with the Government that will
be in power later in the year.

     118.    DPL6 and DPL7 represent, in most respects, a continuation o f the previous DPL program
series (1 to 4) as well as DPLS. Amongst the policy actions proposed are ones that are intended to improve
the investment climate through strengthened investment service institutions and regulations (revised
investment negative list, simplified investment licensing procedures), enhanced trade facilitation as well as
reduced tax burden and improved tax administration; to enhance public financial management through
improved results orientation in the budget process, streamlined budget execution and improved cash
management, strengthen management o f state assets, improved public procurement systems as well as
improved Government accounting and audit functions; to enhance poverty reduction and improving service

 delivery through increased funding and targeting o f poverty programs, improved pro-poor planning and
 budgeting systems and by improved poverty measurement.

      119.     Changes made to DPL-7 triggers since DPL-5 reflect a better understanding about the

 reform champions and anticipated accomplishments. While some actions became more specific or better
 defined, such as the benchmark action aimed at strengthening the management o f state assets or the follow-
 up actions to the signing o f the SPAN contract; others have been eliminated since DPL-5 because (i)            they
 are no longer immediate priorities in the reform roadmap (e.g. extension o f large tax-payer offices), (ii)

 the policy matrix because the reform momentum has been re-gained and new or previous reform champions
 have shown a strong interest t o be part o f the D P L program and indicated a clear roadmap o f actions that
 will be implemented over the next year (e.g. actions to improve budget and cash management within the
 central Government, t o improve accounting and audit functions or the provision o f I T services). Anticipated
 accomplishments include, among others, the r o l l out o f e-registration and e-filling for corporate taxpayers.

     120.     Some o f the new triggers included this year for DPL-7 even i f seemingly only incremental
 in scope, reflect important strategic steps o r milestones in terms o f attaining the Government's reform
 agenda. The proposal to undertake a systematic study o f a transition strategy for P N P M represents an
 important and much needed f i r s t step towards greater coordination and coherence in GoI's poverty reduction

 continues to be debate within Go1 about i t s future in the context o f an attempt to consolidate GoI's many

 accomplished.

B.1 Poverty and Social Impacts

     124.    I t i s the view o f the country team that the proposed DPL6 triggers are unlikely to harm
the poor while several triggers have the potential to deliver positive impacts on poverty over the
medium-term. Bo th the improvement o f the investment climate and strengthening o f public financial
management systems are expected to have significant poverty and social benefits. The measures aimed at
increasing investment, including foreign direct investment (FDI), and raising export competitiveness are
designed to support a virtuous cycle o f growth, employment generation and productivity gains. The
implementation o f a revised investment negative l i s t i s expected to have a positive impact o n poverty by
contributing t o removing current obstacles to investment. The simplification o f the business licensing
procedures by reducing the time and cost o f obtaining business licenses i s expected to contribute t o the
growth of smaller and medium sized businesses which can have a great impact on employment. The same
applies to the implementation o f the National Single Window and o f systems to improve the generation o f
certificates o f origin which enhance trade facilitation. Consumers, including the poor will, in principle,
benefit from lower prices o f goods as a result o f the greater openness o f the economy. With the tax reform
actions the Government plans t o improve the equity o f the tax administration, reducing overall income
inequality while contributing to increased revenue collections, generating additional fiscal resources which
can be used in poverty alleviation efforts. The implementation o f the Government's policy package as it
pertains to SME's also has the potential t o reduce poverty incidence by encouraging economic growth
through improved SME's access to finance. Improvements in budget preparation and budget execution
systems are expected t o lead to the allocation o f public resources according to policy priorities o f the
Government; to a reduction o f public under-spending or disruption in project implementation cycles in key
sectors; to a reduction o f corruption and increased Government outlays and thereby to an increase in private
investment and j o b creation in the economy as well as more efficient service delivery. The transparency and
quality o f public spending and the provision o f public services will allow social programs to have a greater
impact on the targeted population. Overall public savings through better fiscal management will allow the
Go1 to dedicate more resources to pro-poor programs. Programs which improve access to services, such as
transport infrastructure, will help a l l segments o f society t o participate in the opportunities offered by
economic growth.

     125.     The policy actions under the policy pillar aimed at enhancing poverty reduction and
strengthening service delivery have the potential to deliver positive impacts on poverty over the short
and medium-term. Further increasing the size o f the average community block grant provided through
P N P M while improving i t s poverty targeting i s expected to directly contribute to poverty reduction in the
short-term in particular amongst the poorest and more vulnerable groups in society". In 2010, the poorest
kecamatans will continue t o benefit from the program for the fourth consecutive year while their block
grants will be increased due to continuous improvements being done to the methodology. Improvements in
systems for better targeting o f poverty alleviation programs and measurement o f poverty are also expected
t o support the poor by ensuring that these programs reach the appropriate beneficiaries and by ensuring the
availability o f more reliable data and information required to make sound policy decisions.


loThe allocation o f community block grants i s done using the following methodology: poverty rates are estimated at the kecamatan

    126.     I t i s also the view of the country team that the proposed operation i s unlikely to have any

    127. There i s increasing recognition that environmental issues are indeed interlinked with

     130. The loan disbursement will follow the standard Bank procedures for Development Policy

used to finance budget expenditures, as the loan i s intended t o be used to support the general Government
budget. The borrower will provide to the Bank a written confirmation that this transfer has been completed,
and provide to the Bank any other relevant information relating to these matters that the Bank may
reasonably request. Disbursements o f the loan will not be linked to any specific purchases and no
procurement requirements have to be satisfied, except that the borrower i s required to comply with the
standard negative l i s t o f excluded items that may not be financed with Bank loan proceeds.

D. R i s k s

    131.  Continued strong performance by the Government on its reform agenda and the
achievement of medium-term objectives are subject to several risks:

     Political risks. The official presidential elections results indicate that the strategic direction o f the
current reform agenda will most likely not change. The remaining level o f political risk i s likely to be
associated with the pace and specific actions to be taken under the reform agenda, given the Cabinet
transition expected for later in the year. Potential political and cabinet shifts are addressed by providing
flexibility in future trigger selection whilst focusing on achieving the overall reform objectives. This risk i s
also minimized through the Bank's engagement in core reform areas through other instruments (AAA and
investment lending).


         External vulnerabilities. The main risk i s the possibility o f financial disruption because o f sudden
                                  n
(and large) capital outflows. I recent years there have been significant improvements in Indonesian
macroeconomic indicators which translate into improved resilience t o external shocks. But risks
nevertheless remain, especially during a period o f global financial turmoil. Indonesia's external position
remains sound with the current account ending 2008 in a small surplus o f U S D 0.6 bn. After declining by
close to U S D 10 bn in September and October, foreign reserves have subsequently been relatively stable,
rising slowly from U S D 50 billion dollars to U S D 57.6 by the end o f June, equivalent t o more than 4 months
o f imports and official debt repayments. I addition to i t s own official reserve holdings, Indonesia has also
                                             n
gained access to various bilateral and multilateral swap arrangements. B o t h Government and external debt-
to-GDP ratios have declined to the 30 to 35 percent range and Bank and Fund debt sustainability analyses
indicate that Indonesia should be able to withstand various crises, measured as a shock to the exchange rate
or interest rate while sustaining debt payments (see Annex 8). However, Indonesia i s a financially open
economy and there are significant foreign holdings especially in the stock market and Government bonds.
Portfolio outflows at the end o f 2008 moved the overall balance o f payments into a deficit o f
U S D 1.9 billion for the whole year, the first annual deficit since 2001. The year-end portfolio outflows were
driven by both residents, who shifted U S D 2.7 bn into offshore bank accounts, and non-residents selling
down their holdings o f debt securities (net outflow o f U S D 3.8 bn from both corporate and Government
securities). Indonesian corporations are believed to have reasonably well diversified finance and to not be
significantly leveraged, but, for historical reasons domestic capital markets are quite volatile. A significant
depreciation could affect some corporations and result in rising NPLs and erosion o f bank capital. In
addition, the Indonesian Government has large gross financing needs over the next few years and the
availability and cost o f this gross financing poses risks despite the generally strong fiscal position. Debt
service in 2009 plus mobile external bond and equity holdings begin to add up compared with reserves. A
prolonged recession with continued risk aversion episodes may pose challenges from a balance o f payments
perspective. Mitigating these risks will require solid cooperation between Bank Indonesia and the economic
ministries to reassure bond holders that their investments are safe (from inflation and exchange movements)
while crowding in private investment to the extent possible through the kinds o f structural reforms supported
by the DPL. The Government has moved very proactively to deal with the various threats.
         Subsidies. As international o i l prices decline subsides also fall but they remain a key risk as
commodity price reversions also remain possible especially as current global liquidity issues work
themselves out. There remain significant subsidies on fuel and electricity in Indonesia, despite the increase
in administered fuel prices (28.7 percent) in M a y 2008. At present, marginal changes in world prices are
budget neutral but fixed fuel prices and the subsidies they create represent a threat t o fiscal sustainability in
the longer term (not to mention their opportunity cost, regressivity and that they do not promote efficiency).
While a plan to move to market prices would be first best, this i s likely on hold until after the election in
2009 because o f the political risks involved. But as the Government's decision to raise administered fuel
prices last M a y demonstrated, if the economic situation warrants it, the Government has been willing to


                                                       47
make difficult decisions. In the meantime there are ongoing efforts to reduce subsidies in other ways. Most
immediately there i s a program t o introduce L P G (subsidized but less so) in urban areas while phasing out
kerosene (the most subsidized fuel). In addition, the budget for 2009 proposes that the regions share in the
cost o f fuel subsidies, thus reducing their impact on the central Government and shifting it to the regional
Governments that have been running surpluses.
        Fiduciary and reputational risks. In the past, the Bank has been associated with Indonesia's weak
governance environment. Concerns have also been raised by c i v i l society groups about the proper use o f
donor funds. The Bank's fiduciary assessment concludes that even though since 2000-01, there have been
considerable improvements in the overall fiduciary environment in Indonesia, risks remain. Indonesia has an
enormous public sector institutional reform agenda to implement to eliminate concerns over the quality o f
Indonesia's public financial management procedures and processes. The Bank's G F M R A P and other donor
programs are helping the Government t o lock in and implement institutional reforms in the P F M arena.

     ANNEX 2: APPLICATION OF GOOD PRACTICE PRINCIPLES ON CONDITIONALITY
Principle 1: Reinforce ownership. DPL 6 continues to support key policy and institutional reforms in line
with the Government's priorities. Policy actions for the Investment Climate pillar are largely based on the
2007 and 2008 policy packages, or implementing decrees related to approved laws. PFM policy actions stem
from the troika o f laws passed by parliament in 2003-04, namely on state finance, treasury, and audit - all o f
which provide the basis for modernizing public finances. Similarly, the procurement policy action i s borne
out o f the 2003 presidential decree. Lastly, policy reforms in service delivery to the poor are all based on on-
going efforts led by Government, including the Administration's priority t o scale up anti-poverty
community programs nationwide.

Principle 2: Agree up front with Government and partners on a coordinated accountability framework.
The policy matrix represents an agreed framework with Government and the country's budget support
donors, i.e. the Bank, the ADB and the Japanese Government. All development partners base their
disbursement decisions on mutual consultation and on the Government's fulfillment o f the same set o f
triggers. The policy matrix also sets out medium-term outcome indicators with targets to be achieved by the
end o f the program. These indicators and targets were developed j o i n t l y with the Government (and, in the
case o f macroeconomic triggers, with the IMF) and have been adjusted as the program has evolved.

Principle 3: Customize the accountability framework and modalities o Bank support to country
                                                                                 f
circumstances. The Bank has prepared this programmatic series in response to an explicit Government
request. The timing o f delivery o f this operation, aiming t o deliver disbursements before the end o f the
calendar year (3 months earlier when compared t o DPLS), i s driven by the current Government's
willingness to ensure the adequate financial resources for key reform programs in the 2010 budget, while the
political transition i s ongoing. The policy matrix does not pretend to capture the totality o f reforms being
implemented, nor to raise issues that are key t o growth and poverty alleviation but that, for various reasons,
may be counterproductive if part o f a high-profile program, such as the DPL. Hence, although the Bank i s
engaged in dialogue across a number o f sensitive policy issues, many policy actions are left outside the
scope of the D P L to take into account country circumstances and the political economy.

Principle 4: Choose only actions critical for achieving results as conditions for disbursement. While
country ownership and the criticality o f policy issues to stated objectives are the foremost criteria in
selecting D P L policy actions, the Bank and development partners also base the selection on the likelihood o f
accelerating implementation where vested interests or bureaucratic hurdles could slow the pace o f reform.
Other criteria include identifying strategies and plans that are critical first steps in a longer reform process.

 Principle 5: Conduct transparent progress reviews conducive to predictable and performance-based
financial support. Development partners agree to notional commitment amounts and policy triggers during
 the prior year's D PL . Donor commitments are further refined, based on Government confirmation o f
 program lending needs by the first quarter o f the calendar year. During this time, Government and
 development partners agree to specific criteria on assessing triggers. Given the decentralized Bank program,
 discussions with Government counterparts and development partners are done on a regular basis.

Policy Area I:Improved Investment Climate

Despite the global economic downturn, in 2008 Indonesia recorded the largest inflows o f foreign direct
investment since mid-2005. The Investment Board reports that realized foreign investment increased to an
all time record o f $14.9 billion in 2008, up 44 percent from 2007. Business surveys suggest that improved
perceptions o f macroeconomic stability and economic policy certainty partially explain the investment
trends but major legal and regulatory achievements, including reduced corporate tax rates and improved tax
administration, have also played a role.

Policy sub-area: Improving the regulatory environment for investment

Under DPL-5 the Government's PEP1 Secretariat completed a review o f the investment negative l i s t o f areas
closed t o foreign investment to clarify and enhance the effective implementation o f the Investment L a w
(Law N o . UU 25/2007). The revised investment negative l i s t was issued as Perpres 111/2007 in December

Policy sub-area: Enhancing trade facilitation and promoting exports

The National Single Window for imports and exports has been the most important trade facilitation initiative
underway in Indonesia. The Indonesian National Single Window (INSW) i s being established in the
framework o f several A S E A N agreements as an initial step toward an eventual A S E A N Single Window for
international trade. During 2008 the Government continued the implementation o f the National Single
Window by expanding participation in the National Single Window from 5 to 15 government agencies and
from 100 priority lane companies to 143 companies and by rolling out the National Single Window in four
additional ports. A pilot project was also initiated for exchanging data on Certificates o f Origin between
Indonesia and Malaysia as a step towards development o f an A S E A N Single Window.

Integration with the global and regional markets i s an important part o f Indonesia's economic strategy to
improve production efficiency and to exploit opportunities in foreign markets based on comparative
advantage. T o support those objectives, under DPL-5 Indonesia continued t o implement a tariff
harmonization program by simplifying the structure o f import tariffs and reducing the number o f tariff
brackets. The program i s scheduled to take place from 2005 t o 2010 when the l i s t o f tariffs will be

Policy sub-area: Reducing the tax burden and improving tax administration

Under DPL-5 DG Tax took important steps t o improve taxpayer services and monitor tax offices'

Policy sub-area: Strengthening and deepening the financial system

The experience o f the banking crisis o f 1997/1998 has been calling for an improvement in the financial
sector safety net including the development o f an integrated system t o supervise the financial sector and a
framework for closely monitoring financial sector stability. Steps were taken in this direction under DPL-5,
in particular the operationalization o f the Financial Sector Stability Forum by appointing a chairman and
staff from the relevant agencies and establishing teams to develop a financial system crisis management
protocol, scenarios for crisis simulation exercises, and early warning indicators for various sub-sectors.

Despite the implementation over the years o f several government programs aimed at improving access to
bank credit by M-SME's, there are s t i l l significant numbers o f such enterprises with feasible business
models but no access to bank credit. Several reasons are cited t o justify this situation including the lack o f
collateral, a limited understanding by the banks' credit officers about M-SMEs business risks and inadequate
skills of M-SMEs management in filling credit documentation or administrative requirements. An additional
constraint has been the lack o f clarity by credit guarantee companies about their legal status, borrowing
options, ownership structure and supervision. The government has solved this uncertainty through the
issuance o f PP No. 2/2008 in January 2008 that allows for the establishment o f provincial credit guarantee
companies and the development o f re-guarantee companies. The implementing regulations were issued
under DPL-6.

Policy Area 1 : Strengthening public financial management

After the adoption of a new regulatory framework in 2003-4, the Government of Indonesia has been
in the midst o f implementing public financial management reforms as legislated. Pursuant to the new
legal framework, progress has been achieved in the way public finances are managed and in increasing
transparency and independent oversight. In particular, the role o f Indonesia's parliament and the government
(through the Ministry o f Finance) and i t s spending agencies have been reformed and progress has been made
on streamlining the budget formulation process and documentation as articulated in new P F M laws. There
has been a strengthening o f the position and capacity o f the external audit institution - the Supreme Audit
Agency (Badan Pemeriksa Keuangan or BPK), whose constitutional mandate has been clarified and
budgetary resources increased to enable it to function more effectively. The government i s also beginning to
address civil service reforms with a view to providing an enabling framework for more effective public
institutions. These achievements provide a firm foundation for further progress, but significant challenges
remain across the public financial management cycle including budget formulation, execution, reporting and
auditing with a focus on adopting processes and procedures to the new regulatory environment.

Policy sub-area: Strengthening budget formulation and M&E systems

As part o f the move toward program and performance-based budgeting, under DPL-5 DG Budget in the
M o F started to review and refine the budget submission templates (RKA-KL) with a view to simplifying
budget submissions and link them more closely to programs. In addition to performance indicators, the
templates added narrative information on program strategies and objectives in order t o provide a clearer link
between program objectives and funding levels and also contained columns for forward estimates o f
expenditures. In parallel with this process, Bappenas, in coordination with the MoF, started to review the
existing program structure, which will culminate with i t s adoption in the preparation o f the new medium
term development plan (20 10-20 14).

The DPL series was envisioned at a time when the country was transitioning both politically and
economically. Politically the country was moving from autocratic rule to democracy while, economically,
the country's fundamentals were improving markedly as the impact o f the Asian crisis was left behind. In
2003, the country had successfully graduated from i t s post-crisis IMF program, growth was strong and
inflation down. The timing was right for the government's economic program to shift from a short-term
post-crisis stabilization framework t o a longer-term strategic growth and poverty reduction agenda. It was in
this context that the D P L was established to support the government's reform agenda in the key areas of: i)
sustaining macroeconomic stability; ii)   improving the investment climate; iii)strengthening public financial
management and governance; and later iv) improving service delivery. Overall, 54 prior actions were
enacted, covering a wide range o f sub-areas within the 4 pillars.

Pillar I:MacroeconomicStability

Overall macroeconomic fundamentals remain sound and fiscal consolidation has improved with the
expansion o f the tax base and improved efficiency. Economic growth averaged a substantive 5.6 percent
in the period 2004-07, debt-to-GDP declined nearly 25 percentage points, budget deficits have declined and
high reserves provide the country with a comfortable liquidity cushion. Increasing non-oil and gas revenues
to GDP were supported by a prior action to expand the modernization o f tax offices and extend the tax base,
including to small and medium size taxpayers. These taxpayers are now responsible for the largest share o f
Indonesian taxes. Additional reforms t o improve tax analysis, by the creation o f a Tax Policy Office, are
expected to generate improvements in revenue collection. Other Bank contributions under the D P L focused
on improving debt and fiscal management in order to underpin the government's medium-term economic
management capabilities.

On the expenditure side, reductions in non-targeted fuel subsidies allowed greater social spending but
                                                   n
untargeted subsidies remain burdensome. I 2005, the government raised fuel prices, resulting in the
decline o f fuel subsidies from 4 percent o f GDP in 2004 to 2.7 percent in 2007, freeing up U S 1 0 billion a
year. This amount was rolled out into cash transfers targeted t o the poor and also higher health and
education expenditures. As o i l prices continued t o set record highs throughout 2008, fuel subsidies rose
sharply causing the government to spend an additional Rp.50 trillion (US$5.4 billion or 1.2 percent o f GDP)
in the first part o f the year. In M a y 2008, the government again increased fuel prices by almost 30 percent
and accompanied this with a widely publicized and improved cash transfer program. This move i s expected
t o reduce energy subsidies - which were surpassing both central government capital and social spending - by
4 percent o f GDP. Even with the recent increase, Indonesia s t i l l has some o f the world's lowest fuel prices,
with 45 percent o f the fuel subsidies going t o the top income decile.

Rating agencies responded favorably to Indonesia's lower debt ratio and reform packages. In a
reflection o f the country's sounder economic footing, all three international credit agencies have raised their
credit ratings at least 3 notches since 2003. Standard and Poor's upgraded Indonesia's credit rating from
CCC+ in 2003 to BB- by 2008 while, in this same period, Moody's raised i t s ratings from B 2 to Ba3 and
Fitch raised theirs from B to BB. In supporting their ratings upgrade, the agencies cited debt reduction as the
primary cause, but also cited the government's commitment to broad-based reform, including changes to
investment laws, customs and tax reforms as well as fiscal reforms, especially in treasury and budget -
measures supported by the DPL.

Pillar 3: Financial Management and Anti-Corruption

T o improve the efficiency, transparency and accountability o f public finances, the DPL series focused on 3
key areas o f reform: i)modernizing public financial management systems; ii)strengthening anti-corruption
institutions and, later; iii)civil service reform. The progress achieved in this area was also strongly
supported by the Bank's GFMRAP.


Changes in the legal and regulatory architecture are now largely complete and the focus i s on
implementing a modern PFM system. Before the start o f the D P L series, the Indonesian government's
financial operations were based on outdated laws (from 1925) that had been updated with a series o f
inadequate decrees. The D P L supported various regulatory and implementation measures needed t o create
the framework for a more modern P F M system, including laws on state finances, treasury and audit laws and
a presidential decree on procurement.

Budgeting procedures have improved but execution still remains problematic. The D P L program has
helped accelerate reforms to re-engineer business processes across the budget cycle and the national budget
i s n o w compatible with the international standard Government Finance Statistics (GFS) classification. Fiscal
reporting i s more comprehensive and timely, while elements o f a Medium-Term Expenditure Framework
have been introduced, although this i s not fully operational in the budget system. The planning and reporting
side o f the budget process has improved but budget execution remains weak, with about 50 percent o f
capital expenditure being spent in the last quarter.

Of the budgeting reforms, a key achievement under the DPL has been the implementation of the
Treasury Single Account (TSA) to improve cash management o f public finances. At the start o f the
DPL series, more than 18,000 commercial bank accounts - and other accounts not even known to the

                                                     72
Treasury - handled government funds. As an initial step, D P L 1 supported addressing the regulatory
deficiencies in the P F M system, including the passage o f several laws defining the regulatory framework. A
reorganization o f the M o F was also done to begin the process o f establishing a TSA, and to clean up the
cash management issues the government faced. DPL 2 then supported the implementing regulations
underlining these laws and adopted an implementation plan. D P L 3 piloted the T S A to have zero-balance
accounts in over 50 KPPNs. D P L 4 extended the TSA coverage t o nearly a l l government expenditure
accounts, although there were some timing issues - e.g. the training for local agencies t o implement the TSA
occurred later than scheduled. Progress on covering revenue accounts has been more complicated, but i s
expected t o be covered as part o f the triggers for D P L 5.

Procurement reform, although slow to take off, now has the potential to exceed original expectations.
The original trigger had aimed only at strengthening the existing National Procurement Office; instead the
government has turned it into an independent office responsible directly to the President. As an independent
agency with adequate resources, authority and strong political backing, it will be in a stronger position to
drive public procurement reform. Because o f the lag in strengthening the NPPO, however, there was a lack
o f progress on other procurement reforms, especially in developing a comprehensive national public sector
procurement law. This law i s needed to address the numerous inconsistencies resulting f i o m decentralization
reforms which have enabled various levels o f government to issue their o w n procurement procedures. Other
deficiencies o f the system include slow progress in the passage o f regulations and in the development o f
standard tools such as bidding documents and user manuals.

T o address corruption, several institutions now exist to investigate and prosecute corruption cases.
Since 2003, numerous anti-corruption institutions have been established and given considerable autonomy
and authority, and even existing institutions have become more active. The number o f cases prosecuted has
risen and high-profile corruption investigations have been launched at every level o f government and even
among some SOEs. M o r e than 100 (DPL 3) investigations have been approved by the President targeting
mayors, governors and ministerial level officials.

Initial steps to address civil service reform have been launched. A lack o f administrative capacity and
distorted incentives in the government bureaucracy have constrained policy implementation as well as
delivery o f public services. As an initial step to reform the civil service, the D P L supported the development
o f a comprehensive reform o f the structure and remuneration framework for high-ranking state officials. A
task force was set up to examine the entire compensation package with the goal o f creating a more
transparent, systematic and coherent framework for pay and allowances based on a comprehensive j o b
evaluation and pay grading. Building on this, the M o F has developed a comprehensive civil service reform
plan and action plan for MoF, which i s to be piloted before being extended to other agencies.

Pillar 4: Service Delivery

The groundwork for improving poverty targeting o f basic services has been initiated. Prior to the 2005
fuel price rise, Indonesia's social protection system was characterized by emergency short-term safety net
programs and price subsidies. The country's 3 targeted programs accounted for less than 0.5 percent o f
GDP, compared t o almost 4.0 percent o f GDP for the untargeted fuel subsidies, o f which less than 0.2
percent o f GDP went to the poorest quintile. As the fuel price hike made available more funds for social
spending, the D P L supported building capacity t o create a more evaluative institutional framework that
would improve the targeting o f these released funds. The 2005 fuel hike was accompanied by a new cash
transfer program to help alleviate the impact on the poorest households - and this represented a substantial
shift from the ineffective and inefficient general subsidy program. Evaluative assessments were conducted
to improve targeting and performance o f the cash transfer program, and the lessons learnt were also
incorporated into the new cash transfer program that accompanied the M a y 2008 fuel price increase. The
government has undertaken significant steps t o set up an effective and comprehensive monitoring and


                                                       73
evaluation facility, including the establishment o f a unit to undertake performance evaluations, with the
intent o f having the findings used as an input to the government's planning and budgetingcycle.

Indonesia issued a new investment negative l i s t in July 2007 as Presidential Regulation 77 o f 2007. This
revision, the first since 2001, was required by the 2007 Investment L a w which states that a l l business
activities are open t o investment except those stated t o be closed o r restricted in a Presidential Regulation.

The structure o f the 2007 investment negative l i s t was different f r o m previous lists. In the past the negative
l i s t had been a simple document o f 3-5 pages showing a small number o f restrictions. However, past lists
were not comprehensive. Government ministries and agencies were free to make up their o w n restrictions
on an ad hoc basis. Often such restrictions were poorly documented, contradictory, lacked a clear legal
basis, and could be applied on a case-by-case basis, allowing wide discretion by government officials. For
example, prior to 2007 the investment negative lists did not mention courier services and n o l a w or
regulation explicitly prohibited majority foreign ownership o f courier companies behveen 2003 and 2007.
But in practice license applications from the big four (UPS, Fedex, TNT and DHL) were rejected and they
were forced to operate through domestic agents or as minority shareholders, even though the Investment

I.               Background and Analytical Underpinnings

The DPL-6 operation sets out to provide general budget support to the Indonesian Government and will be
executed through i t s public financial management systems. With a view to assess the attendant fiduciary
risks and proceeds o f this development loan, this annex summarizes the current state o f these systems, maps
the reforms efforts being conducted, and provides an outlook with regards to overcoming current reform
challenges.

Public Financial Management reform, as previously outlined in the 2001 White Paper, continues to be at the
center of attention o f Indonesian policy-makers, with the global economic downturn having added further
urgency t o the agenda: Indonesia anticipates a substantial reduction in both tax revenues and foreign
exchange reserves resulting from the pending trade deficit. The exchange rate has weakened, driving up the
cost o f foreign debt servicing, and the unemployment rate i s likely to increase, which will adversely impact
the poverty rate.

The wave o f crisis i s fortunately tempered by a decline o f world o i l prices. The decrease in o i l prices has
resulted in a reduction o f o i l subsidy, and thus provided more fiscal space in the budget for the fiscal
stimulus necessary to counter the effects o f the global economic downturn. Also, as a result o f the ongoing
efforts among Indonesian key stakeholders, well-sustained reforms have mitigated the impact o f this
downturn on the country's economy. Nonetheless, P F M systems in the country now face unprecedented
challenges t o ensure efficient and effective budget allocations and disbursements, in line with the policy
responses t o the global and domestic economic turmoil.

 n
I face o f these challenges, the commitment o f key stakeholders in the budget process to vigorously pursue
the reform agenda remains unwavering and strong. The evolving agenda continues to be broad-based,
comprising (i)   improving results-orientation in state budget planning and development, (ii)    improving
budget and treasury management, (iii)   strengthening monitoring and evaluation o f public expenditures and
programs, (iv) improving the public procurement systems, and (v) improving Government accounting and
audit functions. I sum, the agenda i s designed to improve how revenues are raised, how funds are allocated,
                  n

DPLs 1-5 have had public financial management as an area o f focus and have contributed to many o f the
significant improvements made in the past few years. With PFM improvement remaining one key focus also
for DPL 6, this loan will be an equally crucial instrument for the Government to flexibly, and in a self-

     1. Improving Results-Orientationin State Budget Planning and Development

To facilitate policy-basedbudgeting, the Indonesian Government continues to prioritize the implementation
of Performance-Based Budgeting (PBB) and the Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF). MoF and
BAPPENAS have been working together in developing a road map for the implementation of PBB in

coherence of policies and priorities, and their alignment with the state budget.

Budget submission templates (RKA-KL) are currently being revised in order to simplify budget submissions

     5. Improving Government Accounting and Audit Functions

Accounting and Reporting on Budget Execution

Even though the aggregate Government Annual Financial Statements have been prepared in a timely manner

The annual financial statements are prepared on an accrual basis and there i s a plan to move to full accrual
accounting for line ministries and sub national Governments by 2014. The accounting standards for accrual
accounting have already been drafted and are currently undergoing a review process.

Thus, even though there i s considerable improvement in the quality o f accounting and financial reporting,
and the future direction seems to be good, human resource issues continue to be a significant constraint.

    6. Reforming Regional Public Financial Management

Sub-national P F M reforms have failed to produce their expected results. Decentralization has provided sub-
national Governments with significant resources and responsibilities. More than one-third o f public
spending i s n o w executed by sub-national Governments. This level o f expenditure requires an adequate
regulatory framework, together with sufficient P F M capacity, at the sub-national level if it i s to be fully
effective. In order to address this need, in 2005 the central Government passed comprehensive legislation
on PFM reforms at the sub-national level, with the aim o f mirroring reforms already being implemented at
the center. However, most regions lack the technical and human resources t o implement the reforms. For
example, sub-national Governments are obliged by law to report certain fiscal and financial information to
the central Government within a clear timeframe. In reality, many sub-national Governments struggle to
meet their deadlines for submission.

Current budget allocation and disbursement practices contribute to fiscal discipline at the central level but
they constrain successful financial management at the local level. Allocations to individual sub-national
Government units are formally issued after the passage o f the central Government budget in October prior to
the fiscal year in question and commence through derivative regulations (e.g., ministerial decrees). While
this may appear to allow sufficient time for sub-national Governments to finalize their budgets, in reality
budget allocations o f shared non-tax revenue (oil and gas) are frequently issued close t o the beginning, or
first few months, o f the fiscal year. The notional allocations are frequently underestimated by the central
Government and disbursements from the center to the regions are back-loaded towards the end o f the fiscal
year. This i s because the transfers in the final quarter are based on actual revenues, which are usually higher
than projected revenues.

Sub-national Governments are struggling to spend their increasing budgets and have built up reserves in
recent years for three main reasons. First, sub-national Governments often underestimate their revenues due
to lack o f revenue-management capacity, compounded by the under-budgeting o f transfers by the central
Government. Second, delays in the budget approval process and lack o f implementation capacity result in
under-spending o f budgeted programs by sub-national Governments. Third, sub-national Governments may
not have the capacity to spend the resources at their disposal when these resources increase significantly and
abruptly. This was the case with the 64 percent increase in DAU from 2005 to 2006, which led t o a
significant increase in sub-national Government reserves.

Some challenges in addressing the constraints in P F M at the sub-national level include: (i)providing timely
estimates from the sectoral ministries o f revenue-sharing transfers; (ii)
                                                                         building the capacity o f sub-national
Governments t o manage accumulated reserves; and (iii)      improving and streamlining the budget approval
process. The need to streamline the budget approval process in particular affects L a w No. 33/2004, which
stipulates that provincial and district budgets must be approved by the Ministry o f Home Affairs and the
provincial Government, respectively. This process may be one factor contributing towards lengthening the
budget approval process.

In order to address the financial management issues and challenges at the sub-national level, the central
Government has implemented several reforms.

                                                      95
    1. `Burden-sharing policy'
       Introduced in 2009, this policy incorporates major subsidies, including fuel subsidies, into the DAU
       pool allocation. The policy will increase the accuracy o f transfer projections during the full fiscal
       year, given that there i s a disincentive for the central Government to under-estimate the o i l price
       assumption, especially in relation t o regional transfers. A more rational o i l price assumption will
       lead to better estimates o f revenue-sharing which, in turn, will help sub-national Governments to
       better plan their budgets.

        T w o actions are needed to optimize this policy. First, the central Government should establish a
        system to regularly monitor quarterly revenue-sharing transfers, as stipulated in L a w No. 33/2004.
        Second, the central Government, through the relevant agencies, needs to improve production
        estimates for revenue-sharing, especially if back-loaded transfers at the end o f the fiscal year persist.

    2. Late budget approval sanctions
       Starting from 2008, the Government has applied sanctions t o improve the budget approval process.
       Based on Government Regulation (PP) No. 56/2005 and M o F Decree No. 46/2006 regarding the
       Regional Financial Information System, provincial and district Governments have to submit their
       annual budgets t o the M o F before January 31 o f the corresponding fiscal year. Sub-national
       Governments that miss the deadline by at least one month will receive a warning letter to submit
       their budget before March 3 1. If a sub-national Government has not submitted i t s budget by that
       date, the MoF, in coordination with the M o H A , can sanction the sub-national Government by
       postponing the transfer o f i t s DAU (25 percent o f monthly transfers) until the budget i s submitted.

        This sanction initiative has resulted in more timely budget submissions. Between 2006 and 2007, 54
        percent o f sub-national Governments approved their budgets after March. In 2008, the year the
        sanction was introduced, 87 percent o f sub-national Government submitted their budgets t o the M o F
        before March. If the Government wishes to ensure the effectiveness o f this reform, the M o F should
        continue t o monitor sub-national Government budget submissions.

The M o F can also assist sub-national Governments in managing reserves and surpluses by strengthening the
regulatory framework for sub-national financial management. In doing so, the M o F should develop
guidelines for regions on the accumulation and use o f reserves. These guidelines should help sub-national
Governments use their reserves more effectively, for example, by (i) settling outstanding arrears, given that
about 85 percent o f sub-national Governments in arrears could clear these arrears by drawing on reserves;
and (ii)where sub-national Governments have constant or increasingly high levels o f reserves, using them to
increase investment in public infrastructure.

    7. Governance and Anti Corruption

Public confidence towards the Government's efforts in combating corruption has been slowly restored over
the past few years, as reflected in a number o f opinion polls and corruption perception surveys, but concerns
are mounting as to whether the fight against corruption will continue as vigorously or as effectively in the
next five years, given what appear to be strengthening efforts to reduce the effectiveness o f the well-
regarded Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK).

The Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK) continues to take the lead in investigating and prosecuting
high profile corruption cases. At an unprecedented level, politicians and high ranking Government officials
are under investigation or have been convicted o f corruption, including members o f the parliament, the
former national police chief, former governors and officials o f the central bank and heads o f local
Government. Even after the recent arrest for a murder charge o f the chair o f the Commission, KPK has been
proving i t s effectiveness as an independent law enforcement agency. The Attorney General's Office has also
created an Anti Corruption Task Force to improve effectiveness o f corruption investigation and prosecution.

                                                       96
Recently, however, there have been attempts to weaken the K P K . Since in 2006, the Constitutional Court
declared that the Anti Corruption Court - a special court currently trying cases investigated and prosecuted
by KPK - was unconstitutional and extended i t s life to 3 years, the law governing both the K P K and the
Court has to be revised soon. The administration i s taking this opportunity to propose the Corrupt Crimes
Court Bill which, if passed, will shrink the proportion o f ad hocjudges from a majority to a minority o f
judicial panels thereby rendering the Corrupt Crimes Court very similar to the regular courts that have
largely failed to punish corruption. The bill would also end KPK's power to prosecute cases itself requiring
instead that the problematic Attorney General's Office conduct all prosecutions.

Apart from stronger enforcement against corrupt practices, c i v i l service and judicial reform remains at the
heart o f the Government's strategy in the fight against corruption. The Government plans to expand the
ongoing civil service reform to other agencies under the leadership o f MenPAN. While the Ministry s t i l l
lacks the necessary capability to monitor, evaluate and drive bureaucracy reform, Government agencies are
undertaking significant reforms. The Supreme Court, in particular, has undergone significant restructuring
and as a result, the quality o f i t s decisions has improved. The majority o f Supreme Court decisions has been
in favor of, or has reinforced a strong stance against corruption.

While civil service reform i s taking a steady pace, the future o f KPK and the mainstream efforts to
investigate and prosecute corruption will depend on the outcome o f the revision o f the law on Anti
                                                                                n
Corruption, the law governing KPK, and the law on the Anti Corruption Court. I addition, it also will
depend on the budget allocated for KPK (in the previous budget year the Parliament cut down the budget
proposed by the KPK).

Management o f Foreign Exchange

The foreign exchange control environment i s assessed to be generally satisfactory. The country i s no longer
subject to the Extended Arrangement from the IMF. Bank Indonesia (BI) was last subject to the transitional
procedures under the Fund's safeguards assessment policy in 2002. That assessment recommended
remedial action to address a number of vulnerabilities in the audit arrangements o f BI. The main
recommendations have been implemented, including the establishment o f an independent audit committee at
B I and the publication of BI's audited financial statements. Audited financial statements for B I for 2008
have been reviewed and the audit report issued by the BPK contained an unqualified opinion.

Iv.             Conclusion

Indonesia has established a sound legal and administrative framework for modern PFM. Changes in the
legal and regulatory architecture are n o w largely complete and the momentum has shifted towards
implementation o f new P F M practices.

K n o w n weaknesses in financial management and accountability continue to be gradually addressed through
the P F M reform program discussed above. The development partners are actively engaged in this process.
K e y elements o f the reforms are supported by the DPL triggers, as well as the G F M R A P project and
initiatives supported by development partners. However, much remains to be done, and it will take time to
realize the full impact o f these reforms.

Although the pace o f implementing the planned reforms has been somewhat slow over the past year, the
trajectory o f reform i s in the right direction and the current Government continues to demonstrate a
commitment to continue the task o f completing the planned reforms in PFM. Taking these into
consideration, the Bank assessment team does not propose putting in place any additional fiduciary
arrangements for this operation.
