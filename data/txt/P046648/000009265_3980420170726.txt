1. INTRODUCTION
1.1   The Organisation pour la Mise en Valeur du Fleuve Senegal (OMVS ) was created in 1972 by
Mali, Mauritania, and Senegal to promote the economic development of the Senegal River Basin for
purposes of irrigation, power supply and navigation. A member of the initially called Organisation des
Etats Riverains du Senegal (OERS), Guinea has now a status of observer in OMVS. The Senegal River,
1,800 km long, is one of the largest rivers in Africa. It begins in Guinea, flows north across the western
part of Mali, then turns north east, marking the border between Senegal and Mauritania until it reaches
the Atlantic Ocean at St. Louis (Map 28332). The total surface of the Senegal River Basin is about
290,000 km and supports a population of about two million people, or about 10% of the population of
the three countries. It consists of three main regions: the upper basin, the valley and the delta. It is the
heavy rainfall in the upper basin that provides almost the whole of the flow of the Senegal River which
brings water and life downstream to a vast stretch of land in desert and dry regions of Mauritania and
Senegal. These rains fall from April to October, causing an annual flood in the river for about two
months every year. Agriculture in the valley has traditionally depended on the annual flood. The flood
also allows the raising of livestock, the recharging of shallow acquifers that are tapped for village water
supply, the regeneration of vegetation cover and the reproduction of fish. In the past few decades,
however, all these activities were severely hampered due to drought, and malnutrition forced people to
migrate out of the valley.
1.2   It is partly in response to the severe drought and famine of the early 1970s that creation of
OMVS was accelerated. The original plans for OMVS called for the development of controlled
irrigation to permit double cropping of about 375.000 ha. The integrated development plan also
envisaged (a) barge traffic from Saint-Louis to Kayes; and (b) power supply to centers that could be
economically and technically served from hydropower sites. In 1981, with financing of about US$620
million from 12 donors, construction began on two dams - one at Diama, on the river delta, to prevent
intrusion of salt water into the lower valley and a second at Manantali in western Mali, for water storage,
river flow regulation and power generation. Both dams have been in operation since 1988. The barrier
dam at Diama has made irrigated agriculture possible throughout the year and allowed the supply of
water for populations and cattle upstream from Diama and for the city of Dakar. Storage of the annual
flood by the Manantali dam has allowed OMVS to maintain the Diama reservoir at full and stable level,
and to raise levels of two lakes (Guiers and Rkiz) considerably reducing costs for pumped irrigation
systems around these water bodies. Because of these improvements, irrigated agriculture has become a
major economic force in the valley. As of 1996, about 100,000 ha of controlled irrigation have been
developed. In addition, OMVS has tried to maintain an artificial flood for the population dependent on
flood recession agriculture with a target area of 50,000 ha, that can be cultivated in all but the driest
years.
1.3    While the navigation components of the integrated development plan for the valley show little
prospect of being justified in the medium-term and may have to be deferred, there is significant demand
for power in the capital cities of Bamako, Nouakchott, and Dakar and in the Senegal Valley itself, to
justify the development of power generation facilities at Manantali. The power facilities would help
minimizing the long-term cost of electricity supply to the three countries and improve the financial
situation of local utilities. Without the power component, no significant revenues would be generated
from the dams, revenues that could be used to reduce outstanding debt incurred in constructing the two
dams. Development of the power facilities fits well with the country assistance strategies of the three
countries which seek to lower domestic factor costs, including energy costs, the development of
agriculture and infrastructure and the promotion of private sectors. The power project will also help
mitigate the environmental and health problems that were caused by the building of the two dams.

2. THE ENERGY SECTORS
2
2.1    Together, Mali, Mauritania and Senegal have a large surface area of about 2.5 million km , but
the countries have limited natural resources. As a result, the combined population of 20 million are
among the world's poorest. The economies are largely dependent on agriculture and fisheries which are
the primary source of income for most their population and account for the largest share of product
exports. Energy consumption in Mali, Mauritania and Senegal is dominated by fuelwood which
accounts for between 60% and 90% of the energy used. All three countries rely almost entirely on
imports to meet all their petroleum needs, with the exception of limited gas and oil production in
Senegal. Power generation is mainly thermal-based, with the exception of small hydroelectric plants in
Mali. The Manantali dam, also located in Mali, on the Senegal River is the first major attempt to use the
region's domestic resources to meet the demand for commercial energy in the three countries.
2.2   Some of the major issues in the energy sector in Mali are the high cost of energy (petroleum and
power), the limited access to power supply (especially in Mali and Mauritania), the unreliability and poor
quality of power supply (load shedding in Mali and Senegal), excessive Government involvement in the
power sector, the sector's adverse impact on public finances due to poor financial performance of
Government-owned utilities and large investment needs and the lack of private investment in the power
sector. In the household energy sector, the uncontrolled "mining" of wood has led to deforestation
around major cities and to an increase in CO2 emissions.
Mali
2
2.3    Background: Of the three countries, Mali is the largest, with an area of 1.24 million km . The
population was estimated at 10 million in 1995, with 80% living in rural areas. Its per capita income was
estimated at US$250 in 1995. Of the eight main cities in Mali, only Bamako, the capital, with a
population of about 800,000 inhabitants, is a major urban center. During the last six years, the
Government of Mali has undertaken major economic reforms, with the underlying objective of
improving the competitiveness of the economy and laying the foundation for sustained long-term growth
needed to help raise the standard of living and alleviate poverty. These reforms have removed
bottlenecks to private sector participation, allowing market forces to play a more important role in the
determination of economic outcomes. The impact of these reforms, including the change in parity of the
CFA Franc in January, 1994, is shown by the improvement in economic performance over the
adjustment period. Real GDP grew at a rate of 6% in 1995 compared to 2.4% in 1994. Inflation was
contained at about 12.5% compared to 24.8% in 1994. Budgetary revenues increased by 27.5% and
export revenues by 27%.
2.4    Energy Resources and Consumption: Mali's domestic energy resources consist of biomass
(fuelwood, charcoal, agricultural and agro-industrial residues), hydropower, and solar energy. Presently,
only biomass for household energy and hydropower are exploited on a significant scale. The country's
hydropower potential, based on the Senegal and Niger river systems, is estimated at about 1,000 MW
capable of producing 5,000 GWh in an average year. Of this potential, only about 50 MW (220 GWh)



3
have been developed so far, at the Selingue and Sotuba plants on the Niger River and the Felou plant on
the Senegal river. Mali has no significant known fossil fuels and petroleum product consumption is
entirely dependent on imports (about 200,000 tons annually). Final energy consumption is low and was
estimated in 1992 at 1.83 million tons of oil equivalent (toe) which corresponds to a consumption of 200
kg of oil equivalent per capita per year. Fuelwood and charcoal represent more than 88% of the
consumption.
2.5    Energy Issues and Government Strategy: Major issues in the sector include the high cost of
power and petroleum, the limited access to and poor quality of power supply and accelerated
deforestation in areas supplying woodfuels to urban centers. To address these issues, the Government of
Mali has contracted the management of the power/water utility (EDM) to a private operator for a period
of four years, is making efforts to reduce the fragmented responsibility for investment planning and
system operation, and is investing in repairs of its hydropower facilities and expansion of thermal power
plants. Further private involvement is being contemplated, possibly under a concession contract or
through privatization of EDM, and/or independent power producers schemes (IPPs) to increase
productivity of the utility. In the petroleum sector, the Government has: (a) negotiated with the
refineries in Senegal (SAR) and C6te d'Ivoire (SIR) a lower price for their petroleum products, which
has saved the economy over US$10 million a year; (b) fully liberalized the procurement and distribution
of petroleum products; and (c) adopted a simplified system of tax collection at customs, incorporating
into a single tax all of the previous many taxation components. These measures have resulted in
increased competition and lower prices for petroleum products. Actions are being taken by the
Government to tackle the problem in the household energy sector, both on the supply and demand sides,
including the transfer of responsibility for the control and management of the natural forests from the
State to the local rural communities, the revision of the woodfuel tax system, and the promotion of inter-
fuel substitution and improved cooking stoves.
Mauritania
2.6    Background: Mauritania is also a vast country with 1.1 million kM2, but with a population of
only about 2 million people. The rate of urbanization is high, however, with over 60% living in towns.
The capital city of Nouakchott, established only three decades ago, now has about 730,000 inhabitants,
or nearly a third of the country's population. This is partly because of the impact of desertification,
which has been complicated by Mauritania's economic management performance which has seen a
checkered history. A foreign-led boom of investment and growth in the 1960s was followed by
stagnation and economic crisis in the 70s. Concerted efforts to focus on economic reform essentially
date from 1985, when the economic adjustment program now underway was launched. Efforts were
interrupted in 1989 by social turbulence. Since 1992, however, Mauritania's implementation of
structural policies have generally been satisfactory and hopes for a better future for this poorly endowed
country rest on mining and fisheries, which if well managed, offer good development potential. Major
infrastructure investments in the Senegal Valley have been made or are envisaged, including power,
which now offers a much richer potential for agricultural development. Mauritania has also made
important strides in human resource development, while the existence of a dynamic private sector offers
the potential of significant economic growth.
2.7    Energy Resources and Consumption: Mauritania's known energy resource base is small and
essentially consists of a fragile forest cover and potentially wind and solar energy. Energy consumption
is relatively low and is estimated at 0.55 million tons of oil equivalent (toe) in 1995. It is entirely
dependent on the importation of petroleum products to meet its commercial energy needs. Petroleum
products are supplied by a Government-owned refinery with a capacity of one million tons per year,



4
located in the north of the country at Nouadhibou. The refinery imports crude oil from Algeria as well as
refined products at international prices. The annual consumption of petroleum products total some
315,000 tons/year. Household energy needs are met from domestic woodfuel resources, which are
rapidly dwindling. It is estimated that the production potential of forest resources in Mauritania is
100,000 tons a year, while consumption is estimated at 500,000 tons or five times the estimated rate of
regeneration. Electric power is supplied by isolated systems based on diesel generators. Less than half
of the generation capacity is publicly owned and managed by the local utility, SONELEC, and the rest by
the refinery and mining industry (SNIM mainly).
2.8    Energy Issues and Government Strategy: The energy sector in Mauritania suffers a number of
problems: (a) petroleum imports continue to be a burden on the balance of payments; (b) the
infrastructure facilities at both Nouakchott and Nouadhibou are inadequate to handle unloading and
storage of petroleum products, increasing costs and, in the case of Nouakchott could lead to a sudden
rupture in supplies; (c) until recently the reliability and efficiency of power supply was low; (d) the cost
of power generation remains relatively high, particularly for the population in the interior of the country
and the Senegal River valley; and (e) the adverse climatic conditions and the growth in the demand for
household fuels have put an enormous pressure on the natural vegetation cover and has led to severe
depletion in the country's meager forest resources.
2.9    Beginning in 1987, the Government of Mauritania pursued a well designed strategy that sought
to (a) attract private oil companies to explore for petroleum in the country; (b) liberalize the procurement
and distribution of petroleum products; (c) promote the conservation of woodfuel resources by
promoting improved wood and charcoal stoves and interfuel substitution; and (d) allow SONELEC more
autonomy to manage and operate the power sector. As a result, several oil companies signed contracts to
explore for petroleum off-shore where the prospects were the brightest. Secondly, there was a large
increase in the consumption of LPG by households and some experimentation with solar and wind
energy, which reduced the pressures on the local remaining woodfuel resources. Thirdly, SONELEC
was able to reliably supply power to an increasing number of consumers without being a burden on the
public budget.

2.10   Background: Senegal is the smallest of the three countries with an area of 197,000 km .
Senegal's GNP of US$608 per capita in 1995 places it among the lower-middle income economies, with
a population of 9.2 million growing at a high but declining rate of about 2.7% a year. This is greater than
the rate of growth of GDP which was 1.9% from 1985 through 1995. The level of urbanization is 42%
and growing, placing it well above the sub-Saharan Africa average of 28%. The country's social
indicators are low and stagnant. The adult literacy rate is about 33%; primary school enrollment is at
56%. Modern sector activities are concentrated around the capital city of Dakar which has a population
of about 2.5 million and along the coast. Foreign exchange reserves are earned principally from
groundnut, phosphate, chemical and fish exports and from tourism. Agriculture accounts for about 20%
of GDP but employs 60% of the population. Groundnuts are the most important crop, accounting for
75% of farm cash income. Since the beginning of the 1990s, the economy experienced a serious decline,
with a contraction of output in key sectors, including food and fish processing, phosphates and textiles.
In an attempt to reverse this economic deterioration the Government adopted a series of adjustment
measures in 1993, including cuts in public sector wages. After the devaluation of the currency in 1994,
the Government started to implement a medium-term strategy for the development of the private sector.
As a result, GDP is estimated to have grown by 4.8% in 1995, with substantial growth in exports of fish,
groundnut products, phosphates and tourism. Senegal's privileged geographic location and good



5
communication infrastructure give it the potential to become a gateway service center in the region and
to benefit from its proximity to European markets.
2.11   Energy Resources and Consumption: Senegal has diversified energy resources, but under
present conditions their use is associated with either environmental risks (forest-based fuels) or
substantial investment outlays for research (hydrocarbons) and mobilization (hydroelectricity, solar and
wind power). Energy consumption is dominated by woodfuels, which account for 53% of energy used.
Available forest stock information indicates that Senegal could reasonably expect to meet much of its
demand for household fuels, provided the forest stock is properly managed. In reality, however, supply
of wood to the urban and peri-urban areas is based on geographically concentrated and non-sustainable
forest management practices that have led to a gradual loss of forest cover and degradation of the rural
environment. The country's hydroelectric potential, based on the Senegal and Gambia rivers is estimated
at 1,000 MW which has still to be tapped. Fossil fuels in the form of heavy petroleum was discovered in
the 1950s offshore at Dome Flore (100 million tons), but it has yet to be produced. Small amounts of
natural gas and light petroleum, however, were discovered and produced onshore near Dakar (Diam
Nadio). Between 1987 and 1992, these fields produced 61,000 barrels of crude oil and 31 million cubic
meters of natural gas which was used to produce electricity. Additional gas reserves were discovered
recently in the same area (amount estimated at 10 billion m3). Most of Senegal's commercial energy
needs are met, however, by imported petroleum products which totaled 900,000 tons in 1994 and
claimed 16% of export earnings.
2.12   Energy Issues and Government Strategy:  Senegal's energy sector is dominated by public
sector agencies, the performance of which has been hampered inter alia by a lack of real management
autonomy, lack of transparency and competition, and lack of incentives to run efficiently the sector. The
cost of petroleum products are high to the economy because the local refinery (Societe Africaine de
Raffinage - SAR) has the monopoly to procure all products and refines crude at an uneconomic cost, and
has been granted a supplement of US$ 2.30/barrel that has not led to improvements in operational
efficiency. Similarly, the local utility, SENELEC, has the monopoly for the production and distribution
of electricity, but has been mismanaged, resulting in poor service, deteriorating generating facilities and
low level of connections, despite improvements resulting from the action plan implemented as of 1993
(see para. 3.44). In the context of its structural adjustment program, the Government has been taking
some important measures in most sectors, except energy. Beginning this year, however, it is determined
to implement reforms in the energy sector that are expected to increase the availability of energy, reduce
costs and minimize environmental problems. The Government has articulated its policy to deal with
household fuels without the loss of forest cover by devolving responsibilities for forest management to
local rural communities. In its Letter of Sector Development Policy (February 1997), the Government
has also stated its intention of abolishing the monopoly of SAR, liberalizing petroleum procurement and
pricing and privatizing the power sector. These measures should lead to lower prices for petroleum
products as well as power and better and more efficient service.

3.1    The three national utilities will be supplied with the energy to be produced at Manantali. The
largest is SENELEC (Senegal) with a peak load of 276 MW in 1995 ; Energie du Mali (Mali) has a peak

MALI (EDM)
A.    Power Sector Organization
3.2    Established in 1960, Energie du Mali (EDM) is a mixed capital company with the Government
holding the majority (97.2%) of the shares and Electricite de France (EdF), the remainder (2.8%). EDM
is administrated by a Board with nine members, of which seven are appointed by the Government, one
by EdF and one by the staff of EDM. EDM has about 1,300 employees of which 88 are classified as
professional staff. Although the total number of staff is high for a utility company of this size, there is
still a shortage of suitable qualified staff. EDM has its own training center, the Centre de
Perfectionnement du Personnel (CPP), and also sends some staff for training at the Ecole Superieure
Interafricaine d'Electricite (ESIE) and the Centre des Metiers de l 'Electricite (CME) in C6te d'Ivoire.
3.3    Sector regulation, policy and coordination are ensured by the Ministry of Mines, Energy and
Hydraulics (MMEH). The Ministry of Finance and Commerce (MFC) also participates in tariff
approval.
B.    Existing Power Facilities
3.4    The existing supply system in Mali comprises of an interconnected power system with a capacity
of about 92 MW and 10 isolated centers totaling 13 MW. Detailed characteristics of existing facilities are
provided in Annex 1. The Selingue hydroelectric plant is Mali's largest plant and the principal source of
electric energy. It has an installed capacity of 44 MW and an average potential output of about 180 GWh.
EDM also operates two other plants in Bamako, the Sotuba hydroelectric plant with an installed capacity
of 5.4 MW and an average annual output of 39 GWh, and the Dar Salam diesel plant with an installed
capacity of 20 MW. The Balingue diesel plant has been completed in 1997 with an installed capacity of
23 MW (in units of 1 to 2.25 MW). The isolated centers are supplied by diesel units varying in capacity
from 35 kVA to 1375 kVA, except for the 500 kW Felou hydroelectric plant which meets part of the
demand of the town of Kayes. Mali's principal transmission grid interconnected system comprises 350
km of 150 kV lines (130 km from Selingu6 to Bamako, 220 km from Bamako to Segou), 127 km of 66
kV lines (Selingud to Kalano) and 75 km of 30 kV lines forming a loop around Bamako and supplying
the town of Koulikoro. The 150 kV and 66 kV systems are reasonably well maintained. The distribution
system (15 kV and low voltage lines and substations) in Bamako as well as in the isolated centers is



7
being rehabilitated and expanded, so that reliability of service will be improved, high energy losses
(21%) reduced and access to power expanded.
C.    Access to Power Service and Consumption
3.5   In 1995, EDM had about 66,200 customers of which about 80% were served by the Bamako
system. About 50,000 customers were classified as domestic and about 7% of the Malian population had
access to electricity. Even in Bamako, with about 51,000 customers, the rate of electrification is only
about 40%. From 1987 to 1995 EDM's electricity sales grew from 135 GWh to 216 GWh, an average
annual increase of 6% compared to the rate of about 13% from 1981 to 1987, mainly due to supply
limitations (generation capacity and distribution network expansion). Demand studies demonstrate that
there is a considerable potential for increased electricity use. To address this supply constraint and
improve efficiency of the system, EDM has designed a least-cost development program for the Mali
power system, that includes the following: (a) completion of the Selingue Rehabilitation project (Cr.
2850-ML) and rehabilitation and extension of distribution network in Bamako and isolated centers, now
being undertaken and financed by the Power II project (Cr.1998-ML); (b) completion of the
rehabilitation program and extension of generation systems in isolated systems also underway and
funded by CFD and KfW; (c) reinforcement of the thermal capacity by construction of the Balingue
plant; and (d) in the medium/long-term (2000/2001), development of the Manantali power development
facilities and expansion of the transmission system (Manantali-Bamako; Segou-Sikasso-Koutiala-
Bougouni) and possibly the interconnection with the Cote d'Ivoire transmission system.
D.    EDM's Financial Performance
3.6   In the absence of a Malian Chart of Accounts, EDM's accounting system is based on accrual
accounting principles using the 1982 French Chart of Accounts. These principles have not been properly
followed because of EDM's technical and management weaknesses which are being addressed through a
four-year management contract that started in 1995 (para 3.9). Since 1983, EDM's accounts have been
audited by independent auditors acceptable to the Bank. Because of the weaknesses indicated above, the
result has always been a disclaimer of opinion. One of the immediate objectives of the ongoing
management contract (see para. 3.9) is to address this problem and have the fiscal year (FY) 1996
accounts certified without significant qualification.

3.8    EDM charges uniform electricity tariffs throughout the country. The average tariff level in 1995
was CFAF 72/kWh (US$ 0.14/kWh). Financial indicators show that EDM's financial performance
improved steadily until 1993. In 1994, however, in spite of a 19% electricity tariff increase which
resulted in increased revenues and operating income, EDM sustained important losses (CFAF 3.3
billion). Two combined factors explain this result: the 1994 devaluation of the CFA Franc and the
constitution by EDM of important provisions for doubtful receivables which were previously
underestimated. The January 1994 devaluation resulted in a 45% increase of fuel costs and the doubling
of the debt service (for which EDM bears the foreign exchange risk). The amount of provisions made in
1994 for doubtful receivables was CFAF 3.8 billion compared to CFAF 0.4 billion in 1993. Although
EDM's current ratio appears reasonable, its net working capital is tied up in customer receivables (CFAF
17.5 billion at the end of 1995 representing about 8 months of billing). This has been one of the main
reasons for EDM's persistent liquidity problem.
E.    Sector Reforms
3.9    Until recently, the power sector in Mali was poorly organized, with responsibilities divided
among several public sector agencies. Under reforms supported by IDA and other donors, planning and
operation of all power facilities (with the exception of regional and multi-purpose projects), as well as
the responsibility for the supply and distribution of electricity was consolidated in EDM. Despite the
persistent efforts at training and technical assistance programs funded by donors, EDM management
capacity and performance has shown little improvement in the last ten years. As a result, the Government
decided on a more drastic line of action and contracted out all of EDM's management to a private
professional partner in 1995 for a period of four years. In the medium-term, the role of the State in the
power sector will be revisited and the institutional framework and regulation system revised to promote a



9
rapid and sustainable development of the sector and allow private participation and investment. Studies
will be carried out under the recently IDA-approved Selingue Power Rehabilitation Project (Cr.2850-
ML) that would serve as a basis for further reforms. The updated content and timetable of activities for
continuing the reform of Mali's power sector was agreed during credit negotiations (para. 8.1) and is
summarized in Annex 3.
3.10   To address the deteriorating financial situation, a restructuring plan was adopted in October 1995
which includes the following: (a) some of EDM's debt to the GOM was written off (US$30.0 million);
(b) EDM's investments for the 1995-1999 period were exonerated of taxes and duties; and (c) electricity
tariff levels are to be increased by 9% by January 1998. This plan also takes into account substantial
improvement in EDM's management performance under the current management contract. These
improvements include in particular a decrease in EDM's receivables from 246 days in 1994 to 70 days in
1998 and a reduction in network losses from 21% to 16% in 1998. In the water sector, in the context of
the Urban Development and Decentralization Project (IF-004) which covers most investments needed
over the next four years for the Bamako area, an agreement was reached with the Government to increase
prices, over the 1998-2002 period, in order to meet marginal development costs. Water prices would
thus increase from 195 CFAF per m3 in 1997 to 404 CFAF per m3 in 2002. As a result, EDM's financial
future position appears reasonable beyond 1998. However, in the short term (1997-1998), because of the
higher than expected electricity demand which resulted in a more costly thermal/hydro generation mix,
the Government needs to take measures to balance EDM's operating statement, in parallel with expected
cost reductions and commercial performance improvements in EDM's operations resulting from the
management contract. These measures will form an action plan (which objectives are summarized in
Annex 3) for restoring the financial viability of EDM over the period 1997-1999. The finalization of an
action plan, satisfactory to IDA, to improve the financial situation of EDM, is a condition of credit
effectiveness (para. 8.2).

MAURITANIA (SONELEC)
A.    Power Sector Organization
3.18   Since its creation in 1975, SONELEC, a government-owned company, has the responsibility for
the production and distribution of water and electricity for the country as a whole. It is governed by an
eight member Board of Directors and has a staff of 710 persons, of which two-thirds deal with electricity
and one-third with water-supply. SONELEC provides electricity to seven urban centers, water service to
ten and sewerage services to three centers. In addition, a program has been completed to electrify 13
additional small centers, with populations of above 10,000. Today, SONELEC has greatly improved its
service coverage, efficiency and reliability. Clarification of the respective obligations and responsibilities
of SONELEC and Government was established through the preparation of the first contrat-programme in
1989 which has been renewed twice. Sector regulation, policy and coordination are ensured by the
Ministry of Hydraulics and Energy (MHE). The Ministry of Finance also participates in tariff approval.
B.    Existing Power Facilities
3.19   Because of the large distances involved, there is no interconnected electricity system. Moreover
all of its generation is thermal based. Total generating capacity in Mauritania is about 145 MW of which
about 65 MW is owned and operated by SONELEC, including a 28 MW plant (4X7 MW) in the capital
city of Nouakchott, a 20 MW plant in the northern port city of Nouadhibou and about 15 MW in small
diesel plants in secondary urban centers. In addition, the iron ore company, SNIM is responsible for a 56
MW plant at the mining town of Zouerate and a 17 MW plant at the Nouadhibou harbor. The refinery at
Nouadhibou operates its own 6.8 MW unit.
C.    Access to Power Service and Consumption
3.20  In 1995, SONELEC had 29,000 customers, of which about two-thirds were in Nouakchott, 17%
in Nouadhibou and 5% in Rosso in the South. More than 99% of customers are classified as domestic. In
Nouakchott, the rate of electrification is about 20% and in Nouadhibou 17%. From 1984 to 1995,
SONELEC's electricity sales more than doubled from 69 GWh in 1984 to 162 GWh in 1995, an average
annual increase of nearly 9% per year. Studies indicate that demand is expected to increase at about the
same rate (10%) for the next 3 years and then to taper off to 5.5% per year.
D.    SONELEC's Financial Performance
3.21   Approximately two-thirds of SONELEC's revenues are derived from the sale of electricity, and
the remaining one-third from the sale of water. The key financial indicators of SONELEC's activities
(electricity and water) are shown in the table below. From 1991 to 1995, energy sales grew by 35% in
both Nouakchott and Nouadhibou, while sales of water remained nearly unchanged, partly because of
water production problems. Except for 1992, SONELEC made a net profit every year. Its net internal
cash generation amounted to over UM 1.1 billion in 1995, its rate of return on rate-base was around 4%
and the self-financing ratio reached 97%. Tariffs, however, have been raised regularly, partly to
compensate for the devaluation of the Ouguiya (UM), which increased the costs of imported materials.
Average electricity tariffs in 1995 were 20.8 UM/kWh or US$ 0.17/kWh and average water tariffs were
120.4 UM/m3 or nearly US$ 1.0 per cubic meter, which is very high even by West African standards.

E.      Sector Reforms
3.24    In the early eighties, the situation at SONELEC was chaotic and bleak. Statistics on production
and distribution were non-existent and financial records not maintained. In Nouakchott, only one diesel
group out of 15 was in working order, and the availability of electricity practically nil. Beginning in
1984, SONELEC benefited from a large amount of technical assistance, supported primarily by the
French and IDA, both at the management and technical levels. This assistance has been greatly reduced
in recent years and functions taken over by qualified Mauritanians. In addition, SONELEC and the

SENEGAL (SENELEC)
A.    Power Sector Organization
3.32   Until December 1971, electricity supply was in the hands of private companies operating under
concessions granted by the Government. In January 1972, the Government terminated the concessions of
the most important private company and transferred its functions to the public sector. It was, however,
only in 1983 that SENELEC in its present form was created to be responsible for the generation,
transmission and distribution of electricity in all of Senegal. Unlike its counterparts in Mali and
Mauritania, SENELEC is not involved in supplying water and sewerage services. SENELEC is governed
by a Board composed mainly of public sector officials, and management is delegated to a Director
General. SENELEC has a large number of employees (2,230), of which about 12% are classified as
professionals, all Senegalese nationals.
3.33   The number of connections per employee is about 137. The total number of staff has been
slightly reduced in the past five years, but it remains high for a company of this size. Nevertheless, many
professional posts are still unfilled. Training is the responsibility of a department attached to the
Directorate of Human Resources.
3.34   Sector regulation, policy and coordination are ensured by the Ministry of Energy, Mines and
Industry (MEMI). The Ministry of Economy, Finance and Planning (MEFP) also participates in tariff
approval.
B.    Existing Power Facilities
3.35   The existing supply system in Senegal comprises of an interconnected power system with a
capacity of about 280 MW, and some 24 isolated centers totaling 50 MW, including Saint- Louis (11
MW), Kahone (14 MW), Boutoute (10 MW), and Tambacounda (5 MW). The largest plant is located in
the outskirts of the capital of Dakar (Cap des Biches) with a capacity of 188 MW. All of the plants are
thermal-based, and the annual consumption of petroleum products amount to 300,000 tons. SENELEC
also purchases excess energy (4 GWh in 1994), from independent producers of electricity who have an
installed capacity of around 90 MW. Senegal's principal transmission grid comprises of 60 km of
220 kV, 295 km of 90 kV and 2,000 km of 30 kV lines. It has in addition 650 km of 6.6 kV and 2,500 km
of low voltage lines. A BOOT contract is in under negotiation, by which the utility would purchase
300 GWh per year on a take-or-pay basis, with an additional 50 GWh purchased if needed. The 50 MW
plant is expected to begin operations in two steps: a 35 MW gas turbine in January 1998 and a 15 MW
combined cycle unit in January 1999.
C.    Access to Power Service and Consumption
3.36  In 1995, SENELEC had more than 280,000 customers, of which 3 industrial consumers (TAIBA,
SOCOCIM, ICS) accounted for 20% of the energy sold. Of this total, 170,000 customers are in the Dakar
area and the remainder (110,000) in the interior. The rate of electrification is about 25% (50% in the
urban centers and 5% in the rural areas).

D.      SENELEC's Financial Performance
3.37    In recent years, SENELEC's accounts have been audited by independent auditors. Because of
serious and recurrent weaknesses in book-keeping practices, as well as in management practices, the
result were always a disclaimer of opinion until fiscal year 1994. After a first refusal to certify 1994
accounts, SENELEC and the auditor worked together at the beginning of 1996 in order to address
problems identified and carry out all necessary corrections. A second report published in March 1996,
concluded on the certification of the company's 1994 corrected accounts with reserves that are, however,
of a very serious nature, mainly concerning the reconciliation of customer accounts..
3.38    The key financial indicators of SENELEC's activity for the period 1991 to 1995 are shown in the
table below. In spite of an increase in energy sales, SENELEC's financial performance has been
chronically weak in the past few years. Recurrent high losses due to a low productivity and high non-
technical costs led to a contraction of the company's equity. From 1991 to 1995, energy sales grew at an
average annual rate of 5.1% and reached 900 GWh by 1995. This improvement was due to a substantial
increase in domestic consumption over the whole period and a sharp rise in industrial demand after the
1994 devaluation. As there has been no expansion of generation facilities, the increase in demand was
met by a more intense use of existing generators and improvements in operating standards: the global
efficiency of power transmission and distribution recorded a 3% increase from 1991 to 1995.
3.39    Despite this improvement, the company sustained high losses of CFAF 7.0 billion in 1994 and
CFAF 7.2 billion in 1995 (after provisions for equipment renewal), or nearly 11% of revenues. The last
tariff increase was granted in 1994, and prior to that, in 1989. The tariff level had been stable since 1986,
because the Government did not pass on to SENELEC the decrease in the cost of fuel which it enjoyed
due to the lower prices of crude and the weakness of the US dollar. The 28% increase in the tariff
allowed in 1994 compensated the impact of the CFA devaluation on operating costs (+24%) and the
increase in some taxes. The present average tariff is 70.6 CFAF/kWh or US$ 0.14/kWh which is high,
even for a system of this size. Furthermore, the tariff structure is complex and has become inadequate.
Although since 1976, SENELEC has been implementing a tariff structure based on long-term marginal
costs basis, this includes a steep degressivity for higher levels of consumption, which is not relevant any
longer as SENELEC now faces frequent capacities shortages.

due to a stabilization of the number of employees and a strict wage control after the devaluation.
3.41   From 1991 to 1994, the gross internal cash generated amounted to an average of CFAF 11.5

fell under the 1.0 level, so that SENELEC's revenues were too low to meet debt service. As a result,
SENELEC's capacity to invest decreased sharply and the self-financing ratio also declined from 55% in
1993 to a negative 17% in 1994 and 1995.
3.42   Although SENELEC's current ratio appears acceptable, its net working capital is tied up in
customers receivables (CFAF 36 billion by the end of FY 1995, representing about 6.5 months of
billing). The breakdown of these figures shows rather good performance regarding private consumers
and chronically bad results for administration and public companies (especially SONEES, the water
company). If not solved, this situation will generate acute liquidity problems for SENELEC in the future.
3.43   SENELEC's accounting system includes a special principle which has a significant impact on
the company's financial statements. SENELEC is allowed to enter in its accounts a deductible provision
for replacement and maintenance of company's fixed assets. (5 % of the amount of gross fixed assets in
service at the beginning of the year, except if the annual net income is already negative without this
provision). Even though this is not really a reserve, it is normally imputed on a special reserve account
at the end of the year. As this provision is added to the common depreciation of assets, this practice
allowed SENELEC to constitute an apparently comfortable CFAF 42 billion net reserve at the end of
1995. However, in real terms, the reserve is more than balanced by the cumulated retained losses (CFAF
59 billion, at the end of 1995), and it should therefore be converted into equity.
E.    Sector Reforms
3.44   The main issues in the sector are the high cost of power, the low level of connections, falling
level of investments leading to a deteriorating of its production facilities, shortage of generation capacity,
inefficient management and deteriorating financial performance. The Government and SENELEC
initiated a number of measures to address the problems facing the company. Beginning in 1993, a Plan
d'Urgence was adopted, which resulted in a net profit (before special reserve) of 1,937 million FCFA (in
contrast to a loss of 3,218 million FCFA in 1993). Public sector cross-debts were settled, which resulted
in a single exceptional payment of 1,900 million FCFA to SENELEC in October 1995. In February 1995,
SENELEC produced a management document " Plan de redressement durable - periode 1995-1997 ",
which presents the long term objectives of the company and has been used as a basis by each department
of SENELEC to define medium- and short-term objectives and management indicators. More recently,
therefore, the Government in the context of its agreements with the Bank and the IMF has committed
itself to the privatization of SENELEC, the modification of the regulatory framework to encourage
private sector investment in generation, and reducing the petroleum costs of SENELEC through the

4. BANK GROUP STRATEGY AND RATIONALE FOR
INVOLVEMENT
4.1    One of the main CAS objectives in all three countries] is to achieve sustained broad-based
growth by among other things, the promotion of the private sector and the development of infrastructure.
Another CAS objective is addressing environmental issues. The project would contribute to achieving
these objectives by providing a reliable supply of electric power at lower cost, contributing to increasing
the access to electricity of the population in the Senegal river valley and to improving the efficiency of
the electric power sectors in the three countries, and helping tackle environmental and health issues
related to the existing dam of Manantali, and the construction of the power plant and transmission lines.
The project grid will interconnect the major production system of the three countries and make possible
exchanges of electricity between the three utilities, and will allow in the long-term interconnection of the
three countries with the regional power transmission grid in Western Africa.
4.2   The Bank's involvement in developing the Senegal River Basin dates back to the 1960's. In
1979, it disagreed with OMVS on the rationale for and potential impact of the construction of the Diama
and Manantali's dams and withdrew its support to OMVS. Ten years later in 1989, the donors and the
three Governments invited the Bank to participate in the financing of the power component and the
irrigation schemes in the Senegal valley. This was largely because the OMVS program directly affected
policies and strategies in agriculture, health, environment, power and transportation sectors which are
vital to economies of the countries concerned and in which the Bank was involved. These complex
issues required coordination between three countries under structural adjustment and the three national
utilities that already receive Bank support.
4.3   The largest contribution of the Bank (which is providing only about 9% of the financing of the
project), has been in ensuring the preparation of a viable project, with the most economic and technically
sound design (in particular, concerning the route and design of the Western transmission line), and
adequate arrangements for economic management of the Manantali reservoir (through the reservoir
management optimization study) and mitigation of environment and health impacts. The project has
been determined as the least-cost option for the expansion of the interconnected systems of Mali,
Mauritania and Senegal, through the comparison of an all-thermal scenario with a mixed hydro/thermal
scenario. Although, prospects for private financing have been reviewed and found not viable, the Bank
was instrumental in OMVS decision to subcontract project operations to a private operator under a 15-
year concession contract and future generation projects that could be connected to the project
transmission grid are likely to attract private financing.

5. THE PROJECT
A.        Background
5.1    The Manantali dam, commissioned in 1988, is located on the Bafing River, a tributary of the
Senegal river some 1,200 km upstream from the mouth of the river at Saint-Louis. Its construction was
financed by Arab donors, IDB, Italy, ADB, CFD, KfW, ACDI and the European Union for about
US$500 million. The structure consists of a 1,460 meter long rock-filled dike, flanking a central
concrete structure 493 meters long and 65 meters high . The spillway crest is at 208 meters above sea
level and the average hydrostatic head on the turbines will be 40 meters. The altitude at the top of the
concrete structure is 212 meters above sea level. The reservoir created by the dam has a shoreline of
about 150 km. The volume of water retained at elevation 208 meters is 11.3 billion m3, with a lake
2
surface of 477 km . At the minimum operating level of the lake of 187 meters, the volume of stored
water is reduced to 3.4 billion m3 with a surface of 275 km2.
B.    Project Objectives
5.2   The project's main objectives are to: (a) reduce the long-term cost of electricity supply to the
three countries; (b) contribute to meeting debt service associated with the building of the Manantali dam;
(c) contribute to increasing the efficiency and reliability of power systems in the three countries; (d)
establish an effective organization to construct and operate the project facilities and to mitigate
environment and health impacts of the project and the Manantali dam; (e) promote competitive private
sector participation in project exploitation as well as in financing of future generation projects in the
valley; and (f) support the traditional agricultural sector downstream through the rational management of
the Manantali reservoir. By tying with the national power systems of OMVS three member states, the
power supply from Manantali would also encourage cooperation and coordination between these
member countries and allow electricity exchanges between the power utilities. The project transmission
grid could also be interconnected at a later stage to the Western African System.
C.        Project Description
5.3    The Regional Hydropower project would finance the installation of 200 MW  of power
generation capacity at the Manantali dam, that would provide about 800 GWh in an average hydrology
year, to be economically dispatched to Mali, Mauritania and Senegal through transmission lines totaling
about 1,300 km. In order to provide electricity to the population living along the Senegal Valley and to
the population in eastern Mali, the project would also include a 87 km long 90 kV line from Matam to
Kaedi and several substations located at Sakal, Dagana, Rosso, Matam, Kaedi, Kayes, Kita, and
Kodialani. The general layout of both systems is shown on Map IBRD 28332, while a single-line

F.        Project Preparation and Implementation
5.12   The project has been under preparation since the 1980s with the assistance of German, French,
Canadian, Belgian and English consultants. Alternatives that were reviewed related to technical and
financial aspects, as well as making trade-offs in water use for irrigation and flood recession agriculture,
power generation, water supply and navigation. When initially designed, the project was to serve only
the areas surrounding the Manantali dam. However, with the oil crisis of the early 80s, the objective was
changed so that power could be supplied to the capital cities, which are the largest consumers of
electricity. Several transmission routes to Dakar were evaluated from an economic point of view, which
led to many years of debate among the donors, OMVS and the three countries. The route currently
adopted is economically and technically sound. There was a similar debate with regards to the design of
the transmission line, as to whether it should be a single-circuit or double-circuit line. It was finally
agreed to opt for the single circuit design, which was confirmed to be technically feasible and reduced
project costs by more than US$80 million. Given the length of the transmission lines, it was of utmost
importance to be fully sure that the transmission system would be stable and that the designed
transmission capability could actually be achieved. For these reasons, engineering designs have been
reviewed by an internationally-known expert in this discipline and his recommendations have been
incorporated in the final engineering designs.
5.13   There were similar discussions as to when Nouakchott should be connected and the most recent
studies confirmed that the corresponding transmission line should be built at the same time as the rest of
the Western line. The project has been determined as the least-cost option for the expansion of the
interconnected systems of Mali, Mauritania and Senegal, through the comparison of an all-thermal
scenario with a mixed hydro/thermal scenario. Prospects for private financing have also been reviewed
and found not feasible. Lack of interest in the project financing by the private sector was further
evidenced through informal contacts made by the Bank with promoters of private power projects and can
be explained partly because of the complexity of the project, but largely because of the political and
sovereign risks covering three countries, two of which have been at war a few years ago and one country
that did not belong to the CFA Franc zone. However, because of increasing demand for power in the
three countries and the need under the proposed project for a rational exchange of power between the
utilities, there is likely to be increased interest by the private sector to invest in future power generation
projects, particularly in thermal generation in Senegal and possibly hydroelectric sites along the Senegal
river, and to wheel part of the energy produced to Mali and Mauritania through the project transmission



28
grid. Also, the reforms envisaged by the three governments in the power sectors would lead to increased
private participation in the future in particular in commercial operations.
5.14   Detailed engineering studies have now been completed for all proposed facilities and bids called
for have been received and already evaluated for both civil works and the electromechanical equipment
of the power station. In the case of civil works, bids were received on May 5, 1997 and are in the

Acquisition of right-of-way for the transmission lines is underway as well as the task of physically
spotting towers along the corridors. Location of substations has been decided and land acquisition has
been initiated.
5.15   The complexity of the project, due to its technical features, the significant financing required and
the involvement of three countries and twelve potential donors, required extensive coordination and
cooperation. The project implies energy exchanges between utilities, sound technical scope, economic
management of water resources, an autonomous private operator, an efficient tariff system and healthy
utilities (the three customers). All these elements have been assessed through studies and from
experience in other countries and have been taken into account in project design and conditionalities.
The involvement of three countries, three utilities and an entity without previous Bank lending
experience (OMYS) has resulted in a long preparation process, in particular for aspects relating to the
environment, reservoir management, private sector involvement, tariffs and cost recovery, and project
economics and risk mitigation. Experience with regional power projects in neighboring countries (Togo,
Benin) shows that such projects can work well, but it has also shown that the issues to address can be
complex and that preparatory work can be time-consuming. Although the Diama and Manantali dams
have been in operation since 1985 and 1988 respectively, it took almost 10 years to come to closure on
the Manantali power component.
5.16   There is total ownership of the project on the part of OMVS and the three Governments. The
preparation of the project was handled exclusively by OMVS with financing from various donors and the
commitment is evidenced from the number of complex and sensitive agreements that have been reached
on routing, privatization of management, water resources management, health and environment issues,
tariffs, organization, etc. Delays in project preparation can almost exclusively be linked to project
complexity, rather than weak commitment on the part of OMVS and the Governments.
5.17   Adequate donors coordination was ensured by the Caisse Franqaise de Developpement and has
been a key element in establishing the project scope and justification and in securing financing from
twelve donors. Several donors meetings and join preparation missions have taken place during the last
three years. A workshop was organized by OMVS in November 1995 to review experiences in reservoir
management and define the detailed terms of reference for the program optimizing the management of
the reservoirs of Manantali and Diama. A workshop was organized in June 1996 with participation of
the Governments and the three utilities, to finalize the institutional framework of the project and discuss
tariff issues. Another workshop took place in March 1997 for finalizing the agreements to be reached on
tariff mechanisms and principles between the utilities, the Governments and SOGEM. Extensive
consultation of stakeholders took place during the preparation of the environment assessment and during
the finalization of the environment mitigation and monitoring plan from January to March 1997 and will
also occur during project implementation.

J.          Environmental, Social and Health Impacts
5.31    Two Environmental Assessment (EA) studies were conducted by OMVS in the last few years
with support of internationally renowned firms, and in close coordination with the preparation of bidding
documents for project components, in particular transmission lines and civil works. OMVS has prepared
a synthesis of the EA studies, as well as a comprehensive environmental impact mitigation and



34
monitoring plan (PASIE) including detailed costs and financing plan, with the assistance of
internationally experienced environmental management consultants. The EA, the EA Summary and the
PASIE have been disclosed in the three countries. The Canadian Government executed an independent
evaluation of the environmental impacts and mitigation plan of the project. The results of this
independent evaluation are in line with the results of the EAs conducted by OMVS. The EA studies
foresee only very limited impacts of the project on the environment, caused mainly by the 30 meter
corridor to accommodate power transmission lines and maintenance roads.
5.32   The main environmental impacts are not caused by the proposed Regional Hydropower
Development Project, but rather by the Manantali and Diama dams on the Senegal River, which have
already been constructed. However, it is impossible to mitigate the impacts of the hydropower project
only, without addressing the problems created by the management of the dams themselves. Therefore,
the mitigation plan of the proposed hydropower development project will take into account the partial
mitigation of some of the most serious environmental and health impacts of the two dams.
5.33   With respect to resettlement and related issues, the major social impacts of the proposed regional
hydropower development project are the partial/permanent loss of property and land use rights.
Partial/permanent loss of agricultural land will be primarily the result of land taken in order to establish
the transmission towers and substations, access roads and construction work areas. The routing of the
transmission lines has been chosen to avoid as much as possible sensitive ecosystems and protected areas
such as forest areas, cultural sites and natural habitats for migratory species. For the major part the
transmission lines pass through open plains with scattered trees and light bushes, the typical Sahelian
landscape.
5.34   The main potential impacts of transmission lines will be in the more densely populated areas,
such as around Bamako and at the Senegal river crossing at Dagana. Impacts will be limited in the areas
outside the Senegal River floodplain and in the higher river basin.
5.35   The PASIE has been finalized by OMVS in May 1997. It includes five components to be
implemented under the supervision of OMVS High Commission and SOGEM: (i) mitigation of impacts
during construction; (ii) involuntary resettlement and compensation program; (iii) optimization of
Manantali reservoir management; (iv) health component; and (v) environment monitoring during project
operations.  The PASIE contains adequate dispositions for resettlement and land acquisition, in
accordance with OD 4.30. On the basis of the land tenure legislation of each of the three countries,
detailed land acquisition plans and compensation mechanisms for loss of property and land use rights
have been prepared for the three different transmission lines. Conditions for the implementation of the
relevant procedures are specified, including a fair compensation scheme and available means of redress.
Detailed actions and budget to carry out resettlement and land acquisition in the framework of the PASIE
were agreed during credit Negotiations (para. 8. 1).
5.36   The Manantali Reservoir will be operated as a multi-purpose reservoir in accordance with a
Reservoir Management Agreement between the three Member-States. This agreement and the related
reservoir management manual will be prepared in the framework of the Reservoir Management
Optimization Program, financed  under the project (see Annex 10 and Annex.11). Final terms of
reference for preparing the Manantali Reservoir Management Agreement were agreed during credit
negotiations (para. 8. 1), as part of the PASIE. This program is an essential part of the overall mitigation
plan. The installation and operation of the hydropower plant includes the creation of an optimized
artificial flood and will cause limited changes in the hydrological regime of the Senegal river
downstream of the dam, as compared to the existing situation without the hydropower plant.



35
5.37   It is expected that the artificial flood, except in the driest years, will re-establish for a
considerable part the traditional floodplain functions impacted by the Manantali Dam, which include
considerable increases in flood recession agriculture, dry season pastures and crop residues, an increase
in fish production, which is linearly related to the area covered by the flood and its duration and a
possible regeneration of forest resources, which are also strongly dependent on improved management.
There is also a possible improved recharge of nearby shallow aquifers, which are used for village water
supply systems. To monitor the impacts of the artificial flood a comprehensive monitoring system will
be put in place, including field evaluation, which will allow timely detection of negative and positive
trends.
5.38   The creation of the Manantali Reservoir has increased the incidence rates of bilharzia, including
the intestinal form of bilharzia. The project will investigate measures to reduce incidence rates to
acceptable levels. At present bilharzia snail vector and prevalence rate baseline data are being established
and a monitoring program has started. Patients will be treated and a health education program will be
initiated by the Schistosomiasis Program of the Republic of Mali.
5.39   After the creation of the Diama Reservoir, with its constant water level, there was and still is an
explosion of intestinal bilharzia. The most affected areas are Canal de Taouey and Lake Guiers and to a
lesser extent around the Diama Reservoir in Senegal. Although the proposed regional hydropower
development project does not involve the Diama dam, the project will nevertheless establish several pilot
projects to investigate practical and least-cost measures, including artificial water level fluctuations in
the Diama Reservoir, to reduce the man-snail vector contact and to reduce in this manner the
transmission and incidence rate of intestinal bilharzia on both the Senegalese and the Mauritanian side.
The EU financed ESPOIR project executes the bilharzia control and treatment activities, while the health
education component will be financed under the upcoming IDA financed Bank Senegal Endemic Disease
Control Project. Other projects which would address the remaining health impacts, such as community
based water supply around Richard Toll, the bilharzia snail vector habitat elimination in the CSS sugar
cane area are listed in Annex 11.
5.40   Local populations, key stakeholders and beneficiaries have been consulted during the preparation
of the EA and PASIE and they will participate in the implementation of the PASIE through the local
coordination committees that will be created with the financial support of the Bank. The Environmental
Management Plan includes the preliminary identification of further consultation needed at the level of
local communities, during and after construction.
5.41   The implementation of the project will be coupled with the strengthening of OMVS'
environmental management capacity. In this context, it is to be noted that the French govemment is
financing the establishment of an Environmental Monitoring Office within OMVS (Observatoire de
1'Environnement). This office will develop environmental indicators and establish an adequate and
demand driven environmental monitoring system.

5.42   Dam Safety. An inspection of the dam and electromechanical equipment conducted by a
reputable independent group of engineers in June-July 1996 revealed no major flaws and confirmed that

K.    Poverty Impact
5.43   The project will allow increasing the supply of electricity to low income rural communities and
existing and potential productive activities in the Senegal River Valley at a significantly lower cost than
the energy sources presently used (small diesel generators, kerosene lamps, candles, etc.). The supply of
electricity to the three capital cities will also be increased at least cost which will benefit in particular
low income households that are currently not connected to the distribution network.
5.44   By addressing some of the key impacts of the existing dam, the project will also improve the
situation of low income rural communities in the river valley and around the dam, with regard to health
and agricultural aspects. The reservoir management optimization program, prepared under the project,
will contribute to improve the artificial flood regime for purposes of traditional agriculture, which is vital
for low income rural households. The project includes a component to mitigate man-vector transmission
of bilharzia, the prevalence of which will be addressed in the reservoir management program (through
fluctuation of reservoir levels).

6. INSTITUTIONAL ASPECTS AND FINANCIAL ANALYSIS
A.    Institutional Framework
6.1    Background. The Organization for the Development of the Senegal River (OMVS) was created
in 1992 to promote the economic development of the Senegal River Basin for purposes of irrigation,
power supply and navigation. OMVS is headed by a High Commissioner who is appointed for a period
of four years. He reports to the Council of Ministers which consists of a Minister of each country
responsible for OMVS. The headquarters of OMVS is Dakar, Senegal but it has offices in Saint-Louis,
Rosso and at the sites of the Diama and Manantali dams. It has a staff of about 245, of which 105 are in
Dakar. OMVS's budget for 1994 was 1.5 billion FCFA (US$530,000 at an exchange rate of US$1 = 280
FCFA).
6.2    OMVS main investments to date have been (a) the construction of the Diama dam at the mouth
of the Senegal River which was initiated in 1981 and completed in 1986; (b) the construction of the
Manantali dam, about 1,000 km inland, on the Bafing River, a tributary of the Senegal River, which was
initiated in 1982 and completed in 1988; it took four years, however, for the reservoir to fill up to the 208
m level; (iii) the construction of a 76 km long dike on the right bank of the Senegal River and other
works which were initiated in 1988 and completed in 1994; (iv) the rehabilitation and elevation of a 50
km dike on the left bank of the Senegal River which was completed in 1994. These four projects cost a
total of FCFA 204 billion (US$728 million at an exchange rate of FCFA 280 = US$1) as follows:

6.5    More than two-thirds of OMVS budget is financed by the three States. Operational costs are
shared equally, while investment costs are divided on the basis of determined shares (cle generale de
repartition) which are periodically revised (currently: Mali 35.3%, Mauritania 22.6% and Senegal
42.1%).
6.6    There are serious problems with the way OMVS is currently structured, staffed and financed. It
has little autonomy, and less than a quarter of the staff are listed as professional, generally consisting of
civil servants from each of the three countries. Its budget is financed by contributions from each of the
three Member States and from donors, as well as by water-user fees. None of these sources of funding
are predictable and arrears are common. For example in 1991, revenues from water-users fees was
projected at 172 million FCFA, but actual receipts amounted to only 16 million FCFA. Moreover, about
55% of the expenditures are on personnel and not enough is spent on maintenance of the dam and other
structures. Approval and payment procedures are cumbersome for the departments in charge of dam
maintenance (particularly for unscheduled maintenance), which budget is chiefly affected by recurrent
cash flow difficulties of the organization. As currently structured, OMVS is not a financially autonomous
organization and it has a budgetary type of accounting system.
6.7    As a condition for proceeding with the proposed project, a major change in the institutional and
organizational setup has been agreed to with OMVS and the three countries. The major objectives of the
new institutional framework was (a) to clearly define responsibilities, separating the regulatory and
management functions; (b) to provide financial and managerial autonomy of the entity charged of project
construction; and (c) to allow for efficient operation and management of the facilities already built and to
be built under the proposed project Under the new arrangements, the following was agreed:
(a) that the Member-States (Mali, Mauritania, and Senegal), would keep ownership of project assets and
facilities and be responsible for debt service on the Manantali and Diama dams;
(b) that the High Commission of OMVS (Haut-Commissariat) would be restructured and its
responsibilities limited to coordination, regulation and policy matters relating to water management,
environment and health impact mitigation and monitoring, etc.;
(c) that a new asset holding company, the Societe de Gestion de l'Energie de Manantali (SOGEM),
would be created which will be delegated the right to use the power facilities and be responsible for
the renewal of the hydropower generation facilities as well as transmission lines;



39
(d) that a private, professional company, the Societe d'Exploitation de Manantali (SEM), would be
recruited and be responsible for operating and managing the power facilities, under a 15-year
concession contract, renewable at the end of the period; and
(e) that a separate agency be created, under OMVS supervision, to be responsible for managing and
operating the Diama dam and related water supply facilities.
6.8   In the future, the Member-States will continue to remain undivided owners of the dams, the dikes
and facilities to be built under the proposed project. The Governments will also give in concession to
SOGEM the Manantali dam and related equipment. However, SOGEM will be responsible for only
43.8% of the debt service incurred in the construction of the Manantali damn, with the other 56.2%
attributed to other activities including irrigation and navigation. Energy generated at Manantali will
begin to be dispatched to the three national power utilities (EDM, SONELEC and SENELEC) following
the share in the project which is as follows: Mali 52%, Mauritania 15%, and Senegal 33%. However,
actual repartition and dispatching will vary on a daily basis depending on the demand and costs of each
utility.
6.9    Financial arrangements. SOGEM will be responsible for the renewal of major equipment, and
will make appropriate provisions for this. In its agreement with the private operator (SEM), SOGEM
will require SEM not only to operate and maintain the power facilities and the dam, according to
specified performance indicators, but also to carry out and finance all repairs and replacement of
equipment within the timeframe of its contract. Within this framework, the financial flows would be as
follows:
(a) SEM will bill the national utilities in accordance with the energy sold to each utility and
agreed upon tariffs;
(b) SEM passes on the revenues to SOGEM, minus its fees and any other contractual expense;
(c) with revenues received, SOGEM will:
(i) service the project debt on behalf of the Member-States and for the loans it contracts
directly;
(ii) pay its operating expenses;
(iii)constitute adequate provisions for addressing hydrology-related financial risks and for
renewal of major equipment;
(iv) pay a fee to the Member-States (as per tariff agreement) to reimburse part of the
advances made by the States for dam construction and project expenses;
(d) any funds remaining over and above those used by SEM and SOGEM will be shared equally
by the three States; and
(e) The States will continue to finance directly the operations of OMVS.
B.    SOGEM's Financial Analysis
6.10   Power tariffs to be charged under the project have already been agreed to and are based on
numerous studies and the result of difficult negotiations. The main objectives of the tariff structure are:
(a) to achieve SOGEM's financial equilibrium, after debt service and the cost of SEM's contract for
operation and maintenance; (b) to ensure that the tariff level will be acceptable to the three utilities; and
(c) to guarantee sufficient revenues to the Member States, reimbursing them for part of past investments
and advances.



40
6.11   The financial equilibrium of SOGEM will be affected by three main factors: (a) the average
level of tariff, (b) the level past and future debt service on the dam borne by SOGEM (total annuities of
FCFA 89 billion paid from 1982 to 1996; and remaining capital of FCFA 124 billion at the beginning of
1997), and (c) the level of energy output from Manantali, which is dependent on hydrological factors.
Two scenarios were initially developed to evaluate the relative impact of these factors on SOGEM's
equilibrium (the detailed description of these scenarios has been placed in Project Files).
6.12   The base case scenario (Scenario A) uses the 1950-1994 hydrological series (average energy
output of 804 GWh per year), and only assumes the part of the debt service (future annuities) that OMVS
has allocated on an economic basis to the power component of the dam (43.8% of the total cost of the
dam), and also the reimbursement of past annuities (payments made by the three Member States from
1982 to 2001).
6.13   The second scenario (Scenario B) uses the same hydrological series, but assumes all of the debt
(100%) related to the dam. Under these assumptions, SOGEM would bear the complete future debt
service (FCFA 123.9 billion) as well as the global reimbursement of past annuities (FCFA 75.4 billion by
the end of 1996). Based on the results of simulations and on the potential impact of scenario B on the
tariff of the Manantali energy, OMVS has decided not to seek additional revenues from the project
beyond the share already allocated to the energy on an economic basis (43.8%) and allow irrigation
development and other users to bear the rest of the debt burden. Although this implies an unusual
accounting treatment in SOGEM's balance sheet of the assets constituted by the dam and associated
equipment, it is an acceptable solution. Scenario A is thus used in the financial analysis of SOGEM.
6.14   The impact of low hydrology occurrence on the equilibrium tariff was also assessed for the base
case (scenario A) in the following situations: (i) average energy output reduced to 565 GWh, based on
the 1970-1994 series (scenario C); (ii) occurrence of driest years from the start of operations in 2001
(based on the 1978-1991 series with an average of 450 GWh and average output of 600 GWh thereafter,
scenario D).

* total revenues from energy sales must equal SOGEM's incompressible applications in dry years

* total revenues must also cover all other applications, including sufficient endowment of the
hydrology fund, in an average hydrology year,

6.40   Addressing the main financial risks. The main financial risks borne by the project result
from: (a) the evolution of the revenues required from SOGEM; (b) the uncertainty of Manantali energy
output, resulting from hydrology; (c) the currency convertibility risk ; and (d) the risk of non-payment of
electricity bills by the national power utilities.
6.41   Flexibility of revenues. In Scenario A, SOGEM can easily honor both the debt service of the
proposed project as well as the part of the debt service on the dam that refers to the energy component.
The redevance (which represents the debt service on the dam) decreases throughout the period, as well as
the debt service of the proposed project after 2007. Furthermore, the amount of SOGEM's total
applications varies significantly from one specific year to another, as a result of the normal evolution of
the debt service on the many different loans involved in the project. Thus, at the beginning of project
operations, it will be necessary to postpone in part the provision of the renewal fund and the payment of
the dam debt service, so as to allow a smoother evolution of the average level of tariff.  The
reimbursement of the Member States advances (the part of 1982 to 2001 annuities of the debt service on
the dam, that will be transferred to SOGEM), as well as the redevance if necessary, would be partially or
completely postponed in case of low energy output.
6.42   Allocating hydrology risk. As the annual output of Manantali may vary significantly with
hydrological parameters, there is a need for regulation mechanisms to reduce the impact of the hydrology
risk and to optimize the allocation of the remaining part of the risk. Indeed, a stable tariff level, whatever
the output, would lead in bad hydrology years to sharp cash shortfalls for SOGEM, whereas giving the
priority to SOGEM cash flows will result in an erratic evolution of the tariff level. In order to avoid
either problems, the hydrology risk can be greatly reduced by creating a special hydrology fund, which
would be provisioned during the best years and used as complementary resource in the driest years. The
provisions for fund is considered as a priority as soon as operations begin. In fact, as debt service on the
project remains at a low level until 2005, an acceptable level of tariff will generate cash surpluses that
could be easily transferred to the hydrology fund.
6.43   But the proposed fund will not fully address the hydrology risk. Therefore, it is necessary to
envisage allocating the remaining risk between the project participants. It seems difficult to allocate the
risks to the private operator (SEM), which will be mostly concerned with operations and maintenance
expenditures for which there is no possible postponement. Secondly, SOGEM can only bear the risk
through the hydrology fund. Consequently, the remaining risk can only be borne by the national utilities
and the Member States. The forner would assume part of the risk through a tariff formula that allows



48
for increases if hydrology is insufficient. The members States will have to bear the remaining part
through the variable rate of payment of both redevance and past debt service on the dam.
6.44   Currency convertibility and exchange rate risk. During credit negotiations, it was agreed
that the exchange rate risk will be borne by SOGEM (para 8.1). The company assumes the debt service,
and most loans are contracted in hard currencies. Though operation and maintenance costs are SEM's
responsibility, the management contract should include a revision formula based on SEM real costs
structure, which will finally transfer the risk to SOGEM. However, a significant and durable depreciation
of the CFAF is not likely, as inflation is already low in all three countries. Moreover, if this depreciation
should occur, it would be perceptible on thermal generation costs as well, and an eventual adjustment of
the tariff level would not compromise the sale of Manantali energy. Currency convertibility issues are
only related to payments to be made to SEM in Ougiyas, which however would concern a very limited
part of sales (less than 10%) and would be repaid in part by SOGEM to the government of Mauritania for
debt service. During credit negotiations, it was agreed that payment of electricity purchased by the
utilities will be made in the currency of their respective country, with satisfactory dispositions to be
established concerning the conversion of Ougiyas and the bearing of corresponding transaction costs by
SOGEM (para 8. 1).
6.45   Mitigating the risk of non-payment. Because of the chronically weak financial situation of all
three utilities, it is necessary to address the risk of non-payment of Manantali's electricity bills. Even
though sector reforms have recently been implemented or are planned during the period of construction,
the risk that one (or more) utility would face sharp cash shortfalls cannot be ruled out. The following
options have been reviewed and are to be considered in the final dispositions that will be implemented
before the start of project operations (2001):
*  offsetting by SOGEM of its debt service to the respective governments against unpaid bills: such
a disposition tends to involve government into the management of the sector, which is precisely
contrary to the general objectives of the project. This option would be used only if the utility
faces difficulties due to unpaid public sector bills.
*  ensuring that national budgets reflect adequate provisions for Government electricity/water
consumption bills.
*  paying directly part of Government bills for electricity/water consumption into an escrow account
managed by SOGEM, rather than to the utility itself, if the utility accumulates unpaid bills
towards SEM.
*  direct billing and collection by SEM with large industrial customers of the three utilities: this
option would necessitate an adaptation of the national power institutional and legal framework to
address the problem of distribution monopoly of the utilities.
6.46   It must be noted, however, that the average price of Manantali energy will be attractive to the
utilities in comparison with other generation alternatives, which will strongly mitigate the non-payment
risk. In case of non-payment, as included in the provisions of the concession contract, SEM would
interrupt or reduce energy supply to non-paying utilities, and/or sell the corresponding surplus to another
utility.
6.47   The principles and procedures for establishing special funds and the corresponding bank
accounts, to be opened by SOGEM, in order to constitute adequate provisions (starting in 2001) for
hydrology risk and plant renewal and for project debt service were agreed upon during credit
negotiations (para 8.1). A special account (compte domiciliataire) for payment of debt service could be
directly provisioned by SEM from the proceeds of energy sales.

7. PROJECT JUSTIFICATION AND RISKS
A.       Project Justification
7.1   The electricity supply systems of the three OMVS states are predominantly thermal; only Mali
has about 50 MW of hydro generation capacity, while the other two countries have none. Mali also
suffers from the handicap of its landlocked location and the poor transport infrastructure to bring fuel
from the Atlantic ports of its neighboring countries, which adds over US$100/ton to the cost of fuel. All
three power systems are small in size, with a total combined installed capacity of less than 400 MW and
do not benefit from economies of scale in power generation. Hence electricity is a high-cost product in
the three countries, even at current (low) world oil prices.
7.2    Yet the regulation of the Senegal River in the 1980s, by the construction of two dams, Diama
near the mouth of the river, and Manantali, about 1,200 km upstream, has given rise to an opportunity to
tap the river's hydroelectric potential. The creation of a reservoir at Manantali with a storage capacity of
11.3 billion cu. m offers the potential of generating 200 MW of power, equivalent to about half of the
present combined generating capacity in the three OMVS countries. In economic terms, the existence of
the dam and reservoir are unused physical assets which give a hydroelectric generation project an
overwhelming advantage over any thermal alternative.
7.3    However, Manantali is in a remote location far from the main electricity markets of Dakar (1,100
km) and Bamako (300 km), and its development for power requires lengthy transmission lines in
addition to the power plant. Sluggish economic growth over the 1980s, with a consequent impact on
power demand, falling real oil prices and difficulties in reaching agreement on the scope and phasing of
the energy project all contributed to the delay in proceeding with it.
B.        Demand Forecast
7.4    Demand for electricity is growing in all three countries, but at differing rates. Over the past five
years, it has increased at an annual average rate of 7.2% in Mali, 7.8% in Mauritania, and 5.1% in
Senegal. Details of the demand forecasts are contained in Annex 1 (Table 1.3). These are considered to
be realistic in the light of historical trends and projected GDP growth. Mali already suffers from acute
power shortages in the dry season and urgently requires new generation capacity. Load shedding
amounting to almost 50% of peak demand took place during the two months preceding the 1996 rainy
season. Due to the delay in proceeding with the proposed project, Mali is investing in about 20 MW of
high-cost, new thermal plant to meet dry season demand during the Manantali construction period.
Existing capacity is sufficient to meet peak demand in the other two states (Senegal and Mauritania) till
the end of the decade, but they too will face shortages if new plants are not brought on stream within the
next five years. However, much of the hydro output from Manantali will meet base-load demand,



52
enabling a significant reduction in the use of high-cost thermal plants. This renders the proposed project
largely immune from fluctuations in the level of total demand.
C.        Least-cost analysis for system expansion
7.5    The alternative supply solution for each country would have been to proceed independently with
increments to their thermal plants. Integrated system planning studies have demonstrated that the least-
cost solution is to develop Manantali to meet their combined power needs as estimated by the load
forecasts for the three states. OMVS' consultants compared an all-thermal, system expansion scenario
with a mixed hydro/thermal scenario in which the three power systems would be interconnected. Details
are given in Annex 9. The equalizing discount rate was about 22%, demonstrating the significant cost
savings arising from the development of Manantali. These savings arise from (a) deferral of thermal
investments, (b) economies in fuel consumption and maintenance costs, and (c) opportunities for
exchanges of power between the three utilities to minimize overall system costs, by maximizing use of
the lowest cost generation plant, regardless of its location.
D.        Benefits
7.6    The main project benefits consist of the production of electricity, which has been estimated at an
annual average of 804 GWh, based on 1950-94 hydrological data. Given the high variability in rainfall,
the annual output from the project is subject to a considerable degree of uncertainty. The use of a shorter
hydrological time series (1970-94), has a very significant impact on the estimated energy output from
Manantali, which drops to 547 GWh, because of the succession of very dry years in the late 1970s/early
1980s. The base case economic analysis of the project has been based on the average output of the 1950-
94 hydrological series, but this has been subjected to a full risk analysis (Annex 9), in the light of the
high variability of rainfall.
7.7    The average net electricity output of the project has been conservatively valued at the current
weighted average high voltage tariff of the three companies (CFAF 63/US$0.12 per kWh). This is
considered to be the minimum economic value of the plant output. Present, low voltage tariffs are
significantly higher (CFAF 82/US$0.16 per kWh), and consumer willingness-to-pay for reliable power
supply is deemed to be significantly higher than the value used to estimate the benefits of the project.
7.8    Simulations carried out by OMVS' consultants show that the transmission lines to be built to
bring power from Manantali to the three capital cities will also be used to reverse the direction of power
flows when justified by fuel savings. For example, the heavy fuel oil units in Nouakchott produce
electricity at a lower cost than some of the units in Dakar and thus power from Nouakchott would flow to
Senegal at certain times. Similarly, there are expected to be periods when power would flow eastwards
from Dakar to Mali. The interconnection will thus allow system-wide fuel savings by optimizing the use
of generation plant. Such benefits have not been included in the estimate of the project's internal rate of
return.
E.    Allocation of water between irrigation, artificial flooding and energy
7.9    Both of the estimates of energy output from Manantali (para 7.6) take into account the
requirement to release sufficient water from the reservoir to produce an artificial flood of about 5.5
billion m3 in all but driest years. A flood of this size would permit the cultivation of sorghum on at least
50,000 ha. of flood plain by traditional methods, as well as a number of environment benefits which are
outlined in Annex 10. However, such a support to the natural flood is not possible during the driest years



53
(I out of 7 in average). Under the 1950-94 hydrological series, the average energy output from
Manantali would be about 7% higher without the constraint to provide an artificial flood, but there would
then be an unacceptable reduction in the frequency of occurrence of the flood. The proposed reservoir
management strategy is thus a compromise that strikes a good balance between these competing uses for
the water. Providing a larger flood than 5.5 billion cu. m would further reduce the electricity output
while only marginally increasing the area actually cultivated. On the other hand, reducing or eliminating
the flood entirely to maximize electricity output would be too costly for a multitude of economic, social,
political and environmental reasons, as explained in Annex IO.
7.10   The agreed reservoir management strategy for the proposed project is thus a compromise
between energy and flood-recession agriculture. At present, the water requirements for pumped
irrigation are not a constraint for energy production. An artificial flood can also be produced in years of
average rainfall while still holding back sufficient water in the reservoir to meet irrigation needs and
generate about 800 GWh of electricity. As the area of irrigated land increases, the higher dry season
flow needed for agriculture can still be passed through the turbines to first generate power. Nevertheless,
there is a risk that in the long term, once the area under irrigation largely exceeds 100,000 ha., the release
of water for an artificial flood may come into conflict with both irrigation and power in dry years.
However, this may take another 20 years as the current pace of development is only about 2000 ha. per
year in gross terms. In addition, improved returns to irrigated agriculture would help to reduce
dependence on flood-recession cultivation and thus diminish the need for an artificial flood. In the past,
prior to the construction of the dams, rural populations were adapted to the risk of occasional dry years
when no flood-recession cultivation was possible. The construction of the dans allows improved timing
of flood water releases and interannual water storage, thereby enabling more frequent 'artificial' floods
than would occur if the river had remained unregulated. However, in the driest years it would be
impossible to create a flood because releases from Manantali only represent about half the flows needed
for the Senegal to overflow its banks.
F.        Economic Rate of Return
7.11   The base case internal rate of return (IRR) on the project was estimated to be 16%. Details of
the calculations are contained in Annex 9. The investment already made in the dam was excluded from
the analysis. However, the loss in agricultural output arising from a reduction in the average area of
flood-recession cultivation was considered as a cost and included in the analysis. Additional benefits to
the energy sector from interconnection, such as fuel savings from bilateral energy trading were excluded.
G.        Sensitivity Analysis
7.12   The IRR falls to 11% if the annual average gross energy output over the 1970-94 period is used
(547 GWh). The impact of a 10% cost over-run is to lower the base-case IRR to 14%. However the
risks of such an overrun are low, as bids have already been received for project components that account
for about half of project costs. The effect of a one-year delay in completing the project is a reduction in
the base case IRR to 15%. The risk of such an occurrence is considered low because the construction of
a transmission line over flat terrain does not present any particular technical difficulties and the process
of land and rights-of-way acquisition has already begun.
7.13   The IRR is not sensitive to changes in the final demand for electricity because the plant would
mainly serve for base load, thereby allowing the national power companies to reduce the operations of



54
higher-cost thermal plant. The impact of lower than expected demand growth would therefore only be
felt in the utilization rate of thermal plant.
H.        Probabilistic risk analysis
7.14   A risk analysis was carried out to assess the impact on the project's IRR of the uncertainty in key
variables, particularly hydrology and energy output, project costs, construction time and the loss of
traditional agricultural output from reduced flood-plain cultivation. A spreadsheet based software
(Crystal Ball) was used to that effect. The risk analysis showed that the base case IRR varies from
11.6% to 17.2% with an 90% probability of being more than 13.1%. In the low-hydrology variant, the
IRR ranges between 6.3% and 12.4% and has a 90% probability of being above 8.2%. Details are
contained in Annex 9.

PROJECT INSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK
Rationale for selected options
The project institutional framework aims at:
(a) identifying clearly the scope of the Project. It consists of the already built Manantali dam and
of the hydropower plant to be build (the hydropower station, the dispatching center, the lines
and the transformer stations). The irrigation facilities and the Diama dam, downstream
Manantali, are not in the scope of the project.
(b) providing the financial autonomy of the Project in order to cover the operating costs and
guarantee the debt service. It implies the constitution of a new company entrusted in
managing the facilities of the Project.
(c) obtaining an efficient operation of the facilities that will allow the best quality of service and
maximize the duration of the plants. This necessitates the involvement of a professional
private firm.
Entities and responsibilities
2.    The following entities are involved in the institutional framework of the project:
(a)    The Member-States: they are undivided owners of the works and are responsible for the
debt service of the works already built.
(b)    The Haut-Commissariat: as before, its role is to be a development agency for the River
Senegal. Regarding the Energy project, it will essentially work as a coordinator and a
regulation agent between the different customers.
(c)    An assets management company: the Societt de Gestion de 1 'Energie de Manantali,
named SOGEM. It will be granted of the right of use of the facilities of the Energy
project. It finances, realizes and takes possession of the works. It subcontracts the
operation to a professional private firm. It is in charge of the renewal of the hydropower
generation facilities as well as the lines and the part of the dam attributed to the Energy
project.
(d)    An operation company: the Societe d 'Exploitation de Manantali, named SEM. It runs the
Energy project facilities. It has the widest responsibility regarding operation and
maintenance (inclusive regular upkeep and (< important repairs )>), under 15 year
renewable operating contract and cahier des charges.

dam. It should be designed to provide a real autonomy to the water sector, and especially

(f)    Coordination should be established between the entities in charge of the Energy project

REFORM OF THE POWER SECTORS IN MALI, MAURITANIA AND SENEGAL
Action Plan - Mali
A. Long-term Institutional Framework of the Electricity Sector
Objectives and Justification
The objective is to set up a new institutional framework conducive to the rapid and sustainable
development of the power sector in Mali. The GOM has set up a steering committee (CEDISE) in charge
of: (a) organizing a wide consultation among all the stakeholders during 1997; (b) analyzing questions,
needs, and propositions raised during this first phase; and (c) proposing, during the first half of 1998,
decisions to be taken by the Government.
Duration
The total duration for this process is estimated at two years, starting with the nomination of the manager
of a small steering committee in charge of coordinating the process.
Organization and Management
The steering committee has the responsibility to investigate, organize debates and/or launch studies to
formulate a comprehensive strategic proposal for the development of the sector mainly from the
institutional, financial, and human resource perspectives.
This structure is managed by a leader with extensive experience within the sector, and who will have the
capacity to work with the current or potential stakeholders: managerial staff of the involved ministries
(Energy, Finance, Territorial Administration, etc.), and also communities, consumers, the banking sector,
operators involved in the electricity industry, autoproducers, trade unions, etc. This list is not exhaustive
and other people/resources should be consulted for specific contribution.
The Ministry of Mining, Energy and Hydraulics is in charge of the follow-up and the implementation of
the proposed options.
Implementation Schedule
March 1997 - November 1997

* Consultation/debates with the current or potential stakeholders of the sector;
* Formulation of a report on the existing situation from the perspective of different actors and
identification of main topics to be analyzed for the long-term development of the sector.

B.    Restoring the financial viability of EDM
The following objectives were established by the Government immediately following credit negotiations,
in order to restore the financial viability of EDM in 1998: (i) deficit of less than CFAF 1 billion in 1997;
(ii) balanced operating statement in 1998 and beyond (without further Government support as of 1999);
and (iii) satisfactory cash flow position.
An action plan will be prepared by the Government before September 1997 to reach these objectives and
to take into account most recent data on EDM position/perspectives. The implementation of the action
plan will be monitored thereafter in the framework of this project and other IDA financed projects in the
power sector in Mali.
The Bank has indicated that a satisfactory cash flow position would imply that EDM: (i) maintains in
permanence its suppliers' credit to less than three months, as of January 1, 1998; (ii) is current with
payment of its debt service, as of January 1, 1998; (iii) maintains its cash flow situation to not less than 2
months of working capital needs, as of July 1, 1998. A satisfactory action plan will also be based on
adequate asset depreciation and provisions for asset renewal.

Action Plan - Senegal
1.    The power sector in Senegal faces many difficulties such as an insufficient access of populations
to electricity, both in urban and rural areas, a continuing deterioration of SENELEC's performances and
an insufficient competition in the petroleum sector. In 1996, in order to address this situation, the
Government of Senegal prepared a Letter of Sector Development Policy which was signed in February
1997. In this letter, the Government presents the decisions made regarding the evolution of the sector.
These decisions are fully in compliance with macroeconomic orientations discussed between the
Government of Senegal and the Bank, whose conclusions are contained in the country's Policy
Framework Paper.
2.    Regarding the power sector, the main decision was to privatize and restructure SENELEC in the
short run and to create the conditions for allowing competition. In order to reach these objectives a
profound modification of the legal and regulatory framework is necessary. The Government of Senegal
will recruit a specialized consulting firm to prepare this framework and to assist the Government in
preparing and conducting SENELEC privatization process. The following schedule has been adopted:
- Recruitment of the consulting firm to be completed before August 1, 1997;
* First draft on the future institutional framework (Power Sector Law) by November 15, 1997;
-  Proposition of the new Power Sector Law by November 30, 1997;
* Adoption of the new Law by December 31, 1997;
* Evaluation of SENELEC assets by February, 28 1998;
* Selection of a private strategic partner by June, 30 1998;
*  Transfer of the majority of SENELEC's shares to the strategic partner, the Senegalese private sector
and SENELEC's personnel, as well as transfer of full control of SENELEC's management and
operations to the strategic partner, by September 1998.
3.    In addition, the Government will take the necessary actions to initiate the audit of SENELEC's
1996 accounts before September 1, 1997, and to obtain a final audit report before December 31, 1997.

REGIONAL HYDROPOWER DEVELOPMENT PROJECT
Detailed Project Description
1.    The Regional Hydropower Project would develop an extensive power system which would reach
the urban areas of Mali, Mauritania and Senegal, fed with electric energy generated at a power station to
be established within the Project scope at the toe of the Manantali Dam. The Manantali Dam was
constructed in 1980s by OMVS across the Bafing River, a tributary of the Senegal River. Its reservoir
capacity is 11,300 million m3. The proposed power station would a have 200 MW installed capacity in
five 40 MW units. Average energy output is estimated at 804 GWh (based on hydrology data for the
period 1950-1994).
A. Investment Component
A-l. Power Station
2.    This component includes: (a) civil works for the power station and dispatching center at
Manantali; (b) electromechanical facilities (5 units of 40 MW each); (c) a step-up substation; and (d)
reinforcement works on the Manantali dam.
3.    Lot 3 (civil works) cofinanced by IDA, 1DB and BOAD more specifically includes the following
items:
-      Powerhouse
-     Water supply piping system
-     Concrete tollgate channel
-     step up transformer and substation building
-     Cable gallery
-     Dispatch center building
-     New office building and reform of existing buildings
-     Reinforcing the grout curtain
-     Potable/industrial water supply system
-     Batardeau
-     Vent pipes
-     Repair of amortizing basins
-      Services for contractors of other project equipment/works (electromechanical, electronic)
A-2. Transmission and Load Dispatching System
4.    The transmission system from Manantali would consist of an East System towards Bamako and a
West System towards Dakar. The general location of both systems is shown in Figure 1. attached, while
a single-line diagram appears in figure 2. The East System comprises a 306 Km long single-circuit 225
kW transmission line from Manantali to Kodialani (Bamako) with an en-route supply at Kita. A 150 kV
line 20 km long would be built between Kodialani and Sirakoro where the East System would
interconnect with Electricite du Mali (EDM) system. The West System comprises a 945 km long single-
circuit 225 kV transmission line from Manantali to Tobene. At Sakal it interconnects with the Dakar
system of SENELEC. The Sakal to Tobene 225 kV transmission line already exists. En route, supplies

I.    INTRODUCTION/BACKGROUND (To be completed)
Description of the new institutional framework, the various players, and their respective functions.
Justification for hiring a private operator: project sustainability, efficient operation (best service at
lowest cost), assumption of investments in maintenance and renewals during the period of the concession,
etc. Adequate incentives required. Objectives to be achieved. Risk sharing. Payment procedures.
Description of the principal technical characteristics of the project.
Mention of all of the studies and legal documents prepared within the project framework, and of the
results of the published announcement seeking expressions of interest from prospective bidders.
Purpose of the consultants' assignment: to assist the client (SOGEM and the technical committee,
see below) in the process of selection of a competitive private operator offering the necessary transparency
to avoid any investigations at a later date, with a view to identifying sufficiently qualified bidders and
ensuring that bids received are as advantageous as possible from the Government's point of view.
II.    TASKS TO BE PERFORMED BY THE CONSULTANTS
1.    Document review
The consultants will be required to review, and suggest any necessary changes to, the documents
that have been prepared, in particular the model operating contract between SOGEM and the private
operator (SEM) and the relevant specifications.
2.     Expressions of interest
In the event of a sparse response to the request for expressions of interest, the consultants may then
have to identify other prospective bidders likely to be interested and organize information meetings at
which to describe the project to them and obtain any additional statements of interest.

3.     Prequalification
The consultants will have to prepare a prequalification package containing:
* relatively succinct information on sector reforms undertaken, the institutional framework, the technical
characteristics of the project, and the functions to be performed by the operator (Note: some of the
principal studies or legal documents prepared within the context of the reforms may be included in the
package);
* all of the relevant prequalification criteria, such as experience of technically and geographically similar
projects, minimum size of the prospective bidder's company (capitalization, number of hydroelectric
kWh produced, and/or number of employees, etc.), credit standing, financial results, etc.;
* the method of bid evaluation at the prequalification stage: for example, the award of a maximum
number of points for each criterion and the setting of a minimum number of points to be obtained by
each bidder. The establishment of a minimum threshold for individual key criteria may also be
considered;
* bid submission forms;
* the sum to be paid by prospective bidders to obtain the prequalification package.
* The consultants will be responsible for transmitting the prequalification packages to prospective
bidders.
They will send a written report to the technical committee on evaluation of the bids received at the
prequalification stage, and will assist the committee in identifying those bidders that prequalify.
4.     Preparation of the bidding documents
The consultants will prepare the bidding documents. These will comprise a package of information
for the bidders' use. The consultants will be required to prepare a document providing detailed information
on the project. This will include information on reforms undertaken in the sector, on the existing
institutional framework, on the sector's finances (SOGEM, OMVS), on the technical, hydrological and
environmental characteristics of the project, on the procedures for transferring SOGEM staff to the private
operator, on pricing mechanisms, on the obligations of the private operator, on the procedures for energy
exchanges among the different national electricity companies (SNEs), on the financial and technical
situation of the different SNEs, etc. The consultants will be responsible for listing, and if necessary
finalizing, all of the pertinent studies and other documentation, which they will attach to the bidding
documents. These will include: (i) the agreements establishing SOGEM and SOGED; (ii) SOGEM's
statutes; (iii) the draft SOGEM-SEM operating contract (including conditions of payment,
penalties/incentives, and supervision mechanisms) and attached specifications (nature of the installations,
operating conditions, characteristics of the service, performance indicators, etc.); (iv) the water use charter;
(v) the pricing protocol; (vi) hydrological and reservoir management studies; (vii) draft contracts between
the SNEs and SEM for the purchase of electricity; (viii) financial guarantee mechanisms established by the
SNEs; (ix) mechanisms for addressing water-related risks. etc.

7.     Evaluation of Bids
The consultants will assist the technical committee in evaluating bids. Candidates will be
required to submit bids in accordance with a two-envelope system, one envelope containing the technical
terms and the other the financial terms. The technical terms will be evaluated first, and assigned to one
of the following three categories: (i) acceptable; (ii) rejected; (iii) possibly acceptable, subject to
subsequent clarification. The only envelopes of financial terms to be opened will be those of candidates
submitting technical terms that are deemed acceptable, either immediately or after satisfactory
clarifications have been properly provided. The successful candidate will be the one offering the lowest
financial terms. This process will minimize the need for negotiations once the successful bidder has
been selected (since the latter will already have submitted acceptable technical terms and will have been
selected on the basis of the financial terms, and thus neither of these elements should require further
negotiation).
First, the consultants will examine the bids in order to ascertain whether any should be
automatically rejected as failing to meet the basic terms specified in the bidding documents. If they
consider this to be the case, they will send a written report to the technical committee explaining their
views, and will assist it in reaching a decision on the matter.
The consultants will then evaluate the technical terms of the bids, on the basis of criteria
identified in the bidding documents. They will forward a written report to the technical committee
presenting their reasons for placing each of the various bids in one of the above three categories. The
report will also specify what clarifications the consultants believe should be called for in the case of
those bids that may be judged acceptable once further details have been provided. They will assist the
committee in its decision-making on these various points.
The consultants will examine the additional information provided and determine whether it is
acceptable or not. They will then send a written report to the technical committee explaining their views
and will assist the committee in its decision-making on this point.

Economic Analysis of the project
A. Least-cost solution for system expansion
1.    The alternative supply solution for each country would have been to proceed independently with
increments to their existing thermal plants. Integrated system planning studies have examined this
alternative and demonstrated that the least-cost solution is to develop Manantali to meet the combined
power needs of the three countries as projected by the load forecasts summarized in Annex 1, Table 1-3.
2.    Demand for electricity is growing in all three countries, but at differing rates. Over the past five
years, it has increased at an annual average rate of 7.8% in Mauritania, 7.2% in Mali and 5.1% in Senegal.
The load forecast shown above is considered to be realistic in the light of historical trends and projected
annual GDP growth of 5.6% in Senegal, 6.8% in Mauritania and 9.1% in Mali.
3.    OMVS's consultants (TRACTEBEL), compared an all-thermal, system expansion scenario with a
mixed hydro/thermal scenario in which the three power systems would be interconnected. The necessary
investments in generation capacity to meet demand over the next 20 years both with and without the
Manantali project are shown in Annex 1, Tables 1-6 and 1-7

7.     In order to assess the economic benefits of interconnecting Nouakchott to the main OMVS

B. Project Benefits
10.   The average net electricity output of the project has been conservatively valued at the current
weighted average high voltage tariff of the three companies (CFAF 63/US$0.12 per kWh). This is
considered to be the minimum economic value of the plant output. Present low voltage tariffs are
significantly higher (CFAF 82/US$0.16 per kWh on average), and demonstrate that there is a consumer
willingness-to-pay considerably more than the value used to estimate the benefits of the project.
11.    Simulations carried out by OMVS' consultants show that the transmission lines to be built to bring
power from Manantali to the three capital cities will also be used to reverse the direction of power flows
when justified by fuel savings. For example, the heavy fuel oil units in Nouakchott produce electricity at a
lower cost than some of the units in Dakar and thus power from Nouakchott would flow to Senegal at
certain times. Similarly, there are expected to be periods when power would flow eastwards from Dakar to
Mali. The interconnection will thus allow system-wide fuel savings by optimizing the use of generation
plant. Such benefits have not been included in the estimate of the project's internal rate of return, although
they were part of the EDR calculation.
C. Economic Rate of Return
12.   The base case intemal rate of return (IRR) on the project was estimated to be 16%. Details of the
calculations are contained in Table 10-4 The investment already made in the dam was treated as a sunk cost
and excluded from the analysis. However, the loss in agricultural output arising from a 14,000 ha. reduction
in the average area of flood-recession cultivation was considered as a cost and included in the analysis.
Annex 10 on Reservoir management and Artificial Flooding explains the methodology used to derive these
estimates of lost agricultural benefits. Additional benefits to the energy sector from interconnection, such as
fuel savings from bilateral energy trading were excluded from the rate of return calculation.
D. Sensitivity Analysis
13.   The IRR falls to 11% (Table 10-5) if the annual average gross energy output over the 1970-94
period is used (547 GWh). The impact of a 10% cost over-run is to lower the base-case IRR to 10.6%.
However the risks of such an overrun are low, as bids have already been received for a significant portion of
the project. The effect of a one-year delay in completing the project is to reduce the base case IRR to 14%.
The risk of such an occurrence is considered low since the construction of a transmission line over flat
terrain does not present any particular technical difficulties and the process of land and rights-of-way
acquisition has already begun.
14.   The IRR is not sensitive to changes in the final demand for electricity because the plant would
mainly serve for base load, thereby allowing the national power companies to reduce the operations of
higher-cost thermal plant. The impact of lower than expected demand growth would therefore only be felt
in the utilization rate of thermal plant.
E. Probabilistic risk analysis
15.   A risk analysis was carried out to assess the impact on the project's IRR of the uncertainty in key
variables, particularly hydrology and energy output, project costs, construction time and the reduced
agricultural output from reduced flood-plain cultivation. A spreadsheet based software (Crystal Ball) was
used to that effect. In the simulations, probabilities of cost under/over runs were attached to each major

Reservoir Management: Artificially Augmented Flooding and Energy Production
1.      The multi-purpose nature of the Manantali project means that its maximum potential energy output
is constrained by the need to take into account other uses for the water stored in the reservoir. In the long
term, irrigation will require a constant flow in the dry season of at least 100 cubic meters per second
(cusecs) at Bakel, while the future needs of shipping3 would raise this to 200 cusecs. These flows would not
conflict with power generation, as the water would first pass through the turbines. In theory; it would
therefore be possible to operate the reservoir so as to maximize electricity production. On the other hand,
the OMVS member states have concluded that there are strong economic, social, political and
environmental reasons (paras. 2-6) to perpetuate the traditional flooding of the flat plain along the Senegal
river between Bakel and Dagana. This gives rise to an obligation to release sufficient water from the
Manantali reservoir to create an 'artificial' flood4 at the end of the rainy season. The volumes required for
the flood mean that some water may be spilled without being used for power generation, which in turn
imposes a cost in terms of foregone energy generation in the ensuing dry season.
Rationale for an artificial flood
2.      The flood has numerous beneficial features5 which justify perpetuating it artificially at least for
another twenty years, the time required for it to be progressively replaced by irrigated agriculture. The main
benefits are environmental and agricultural. The latter are easy to quantify as the flood permits the
cultivation of sorghum on the flood plain. It is a significant source of complementary income (para. 3) and
food security for over one hundred thousand households in the Senegal Valley. Flood-recession agriculture
is an economic activity that is unlikely to be abandoned voluntarily by peasants, because it requires no cash
inputs and much less labor than irrigated agriculture and is almost risk-free. Socio-anthropological research
shows that the majority of the population of the Senegal Valley relies on three sources of income: irrigated
agriculture, recession cultivation and external remittances. Elimination of flood-recession cultivation would
thus further increase the pressure to migrate to urban areas, given the generally poor performance of
irrigated agriculture, which has failed to become a source of adequate revenues for most rural households .

3.     Average yields from flood-recession cultivation are about 600 kg. of sorghum per hectare. At

another benefit from flood-recession agriculture. This has been estimated at over 50,000 CFAF/ha. . Thus,
directly quantifiable benefits are about CFAF 114,000 (US$ 228)/ha. The average reduction in the
cultivated area of about 20,000 ha. due to the electricity project would therefore result in an annual average
loss of about US$ 4.5 million of agricultural output.
4.     Researchers have also established that the existence of a flood was crucial to the reproduction of
fish. However, as the fish population of the river had been decimated by the severe droughts of the late
1970s/early 1980s, the perpetuation of the flood will require accompanying measures to reconstitute fish
stocks and revitalize river fishing as an income-generating activity. This issue will be examined as part of
the project's environmental mitigation measures.
5.     It has been found that Acacia forests depended for their survival on periodic inundation of their
roots that occurred during floods. However, due to continuing severe deforestation, (mainly for charcoal
production) in the Senegal Valley, the perpetuation of a flood will contribute little to the survival of the
remaining trees. They are much more vulnerable to charcoal producers than to lack of flood water.
6.     There is also a link between the flood and the recharge of shallow acquifers that are tapped for
village water supply in the Senegal Valley, although this has not been conclusively established. Eliminating
the flood might therefore jeopardize village water supply, and require costly deep boreholes as an alternative
water source for villages.
Size and nature of the artificial flood
7.     The area flooded varies widely from year to year, depending on rainfall in the catchment area,
hundreds of kilometers upstream. Historically, over 300,000 ha were flooded in the wettest years and as
little as 20,000 ha. in the driest ones. However, during the drought years of the 1970s and 1980s, there was
no flood at all. The construction of the Manantali dam permits OMVS to provide a flood more often than
would occur naturally if the river had remained unregulated, provided that this does not conflict with the
requirement to provide a constant dry-season flow for pumped irrigation. The proposed addition of a power
generation project puts further constraints on the size of the artificial flood.
8.     Considerable uncertainty prevails regarding the appropriate area of the artificial flood and the
volume of water that would have to be released from Manantali to achieve it. The relationships between
volume and duration of water flows in the river and the extent of the areas inundated are not well quantified.

Environmental, Social and Health Aspects
Background
1.    Two Environmental Assessment (EA) studies were conducted by OMVS in 1993-96 with
support of internationally renowned firms, in close coordination with the preparation of bidding
documents for project components, in particular transmission lines and civil works. OMVS has prepared
a synthesis of the Environmental Assessment (EA) studies, as well as a comprehensive environmental
mitigation and monitoring plan (PASIE) with detailed costs and financing plan, with the support of
internationally experienced environmental management consultants. The EA, the EA Summary and the
PASIE have been disclosed in the three countries and shared with local NGOs. The Canadian
Government executed an independent evaluation of the environmental impacts and mitigation plan of the
project. The results of this independent evaluation are in line with the results of the EAs conducted by
OMVS.
2.    The main environmental impacts are not caused by the proposed Regional Hydropower
Development Project, but rather by the existing dams of Manantali and Diama on the Senegal river.
However, it is impossible to mitigate the impacts of the hydropower project only, without addressing the
problems created by the management of the dams themselves. Therefore, the mitigation plan of the
Hydropower Project will take into account the partial mitigation of some of the most serious
environmental and health impacts of the two dams.
3.    This annex presents successively: (a) impacts due to the power plant and the electric
transmission lines and the mitigation of these impacts, (b) impacts due to the management of the dams,
(c) mitigation measures to limit the impacts due to the management of the dams; and (d) the institutional
organization to implement mitigation measures and monitor environment impacts.
DIRECT IMPACTS OF THE PROJECT AND THEIR MITIGATION
Impacts of Transmission Towers and Lines, Substations, Access Roads, Construction Camps and
Labor Force
4.    With respect to resettlement and related issues, the major social impacts of the Regional
Hydropower Development Project are the partial/permanent loss of property and land use rights.
Partialpermanent loss of agricultural land will be primarily the result of land taken in order to establish
the transmission towers and substations, access roads and construction work areas. The routing of the
transmission lines has been chosen to avoid as much as possible sensitive ecosystems and protected areas
such as forest areas, cultural sites and natural habitats for migratory species. Attention has been given to
the environmental regulatory framework requirements in the three countries. Other sensitive areas that
were avoided were the area West and Northwest of Richard Toll and the area of Richard Toll itself where
a rapid urbanization is expected. For the major part the transmission lines pass through open plains with
scattered trees and light bushes, the typical Sahelian landscape.

Annex 11

5.    The main potential impacts of transmission lines will be in the more densely populated areas,
such as around Bamako and at the Senegal river crossing at Dagana. Impacts will be limited in the areas
outside the Senegal River floodplain and in parts of the higher river basin. Losses of land would be both
permanent and temporary in nature and variety of mitigation measures will be deployed to address these
issues, including proper routing/placement of transmission infrastructure and construction work sites to
avoid agricultural land where possible.
6.     Land expropriation will take place around Bamako. It is mainly caused by the 30-50 meters
wide corridor needed for Right of Way (ROW) for the transmission lines. Agricultural activities will be
allowed under the transmission lines, apart from a 3-meter wide strip needed for access. This reduces
considerably the loss of agricultural and agro-pastoral lands and reduces compensation costs.
7.     A Resettlement Action Plan has been prepared in accordance with OD 4.30. On the basis of the
land tenure legislation of each of the three countries, detailed land acquisition plans and compensation
mechanisms for loss of property and land use rights have been prepared for the three different
transmission lines. Conditions for the implementation of the relevant procedures are specified, including
a fair compensation scheme and available means of redress.
8.    Another important point is the crossing of protected forests, which might affect wildlife and
biodiversity. Forest specialists have proposed to protect equivalent areas in the neighborhood.
9.     The implementation of the mitigation plan will be carefully monitored. The environmental unit
in OMVS will be strengthened for this purpose. Maximization of benefits of the project will also be
achieved notably by providing local employment.

IMPACTS DUE TO THE MANAGEMENT OF EXISTING MANANTALI AND DIAMA DAMS
Impacts of the Manantali Dam
12.   Originally, the main objective of the Manantali dam was to respond to the drought and famine
of the 1970s and 80s, which had devastating impacts on flood recession agriculture, floodplain fisheries,
floodplain dry season pastures, and recharge of the aquifers. The dam, which was not financed by the
World Bank, was finished in 1988 and its objectives were hydropower, irrigation development and
navigation. The major environmental and social impacts of the Manantali dam come from the
attenuation of the yearly floods and its impacts on downstrearn users. Special emphasis was placed on
the following impacts of the Manantali dam (thus not the proposed regional hydropower development
project) mainly in the middle valley of the Senegal river basin: (a) loss of flood recession agriculture; (b)
impacts on the area of dry season pastures, which are traditional grazing lands for agro-pastoralists; (c)
impacts on fisheries resources, which lead to reduced catches; (d) impacts on the Gonakie forests, which
need regular flooding (heavy human pressure has decimated these forests further); (e) possible negative
impact on the recharge of shallow aquifers in and near the floodplain (this last impact is not confirmed).
It should, however, be noted that many of these impacts were already occurring because of the drought
years. In the Manantali reservoir itself, there was an outbreak of bilharzia or schistosomiasis, especially
of Schistosoma mansoni, the cause of intestinal bilharzia which did not occur before in the Manantali
area.
Impact of the Diama Dam
13    Basically, the Manantali and Diama dams can be operated completely independently of each
other. The main objective of the Diama dam is to allow development of irrigated agriculture in the dry
season by eliminating the saline intrusion of seawater, which could reach up to more than 200 km
upstream. The Diama dam has created an invaluable freshwater body, which included Lake Guiers-Bas
Ferlo, the main drinking water source for the city of Dakar. The major health impact caused by the
constant water level of Diama reservoir and lake Guiers is the outbreak of a serious epidemic of the
intestinal form of bilharzia (Schistosoma mansoni), which has Biomphalaria pfeifferi as its snail vector.
This snail species cannot bury itself in the mud and therefore cannot survive under relatively rapid water
level fluctuations, which provide a mechanism for controlling these snail populations. Another impact of
the Diama dam is the cut-off of migratory routes for aquatic organisms such as important commercial
fish species.
MITIGATION OF THE IMPACTS OF THE MANANTALI AND DIAMA DAMS
Reservoir Management Plan and Artificial Flood
14.   The Manantali Reservoir will be operated as a multi-purpose reservoir in agreement with a
Reservoir Management Manual, which is under preparation as part of the Reservoir Management
Optimization Program. This program is an essential part of the overall mitigation plan. The installation
and operation of the hydropower plant, if the operation includes the creation of an optimized artificial
flood, will cause positive changes in the hydrological regime of the Senegal river downstream of the
dam, as compared to the existing situation without the hydropower plant (i.e. without optimization of
artificial flood in timing and volume).



106
Annex 11
Page 4 of 7
15.   It is expected that the optimized artificial flood will re-establish in large part, except in the driest
years, the traditional floodplain functions impacted by the Manantali Dam, including significant
increases in flood recession agriculture, dry season pastures and crop residues, and increase in fish
production. The latter is linearly related to the flooded area and flood duration. There would also be
regeneration of forest resources, although this is strongly dependent on improved management of these
resources. There is also a possible improved recharge of nearby shallow aquifers, which are used for
village water supply. To monitor the impacts of the artificial floods a comprehensive monitoring system
will be put in place, including field evaluation, which will allow timely detection of negative and positive
effects of the river flow manipulation.
16.   The minimum target area for flood recession agriculture has been taken as 50,000 ha (about two
thirds in Senegal and one third in Mauritania). This is based on a study prepared by an international team
of experts in 1987, which defined a minimal flood of 2,500 m3/h in one week (called Gibb A), to meet
recession agriculture needs. During the rest of the year the Manantali dam will release an adequate and
regulated flow to allow irrigated agriculture. OMVS has tried to maintain an artificial flood since the
construction of the Manantali dam, although there were problems with the timing of the flood, because of
the lack of adequate flood prediction equipment and flood management methods. Such equipment will
be installed under the project to optimize the artificial flood. A communication system will be installed
to warn riparian populations about flood arrival. The exact scope and timing of the artificial flood will
be defined through comprehensive studies for optimizing the management of water resources from the
Manantali reservoir, which should allow exceeding the above mentioned target area for flood recession
agriculture. The optimization program will be finalized before project completion and be formalized
through an agreement (Charte) between the three Governments, OMVS and SOGEM on a reservoir
management program and the corresponding procedures (Manuel de Gestion). The terms of reference of
these studies, which will be financed by the French Government, have been defined by international
experts (see outline in Appendix). To achieve joint decision making and operation of the reservoir
management, local communities will continuously participate in the studies through local coordination
committees. This process will be financed by the Bank and managed by OMVS High Commission, in
coordination with SOGEM.
17.   A preliminary hydrology study conducted in 1996 by ORSTOM for project preparation indicates
that there would be on average a reduction in flood recession agriculture from 45,000 to 30,000 ha (based
on the 1979-1993 series) or from 85,000 to 73,000 ha (based on the series 1950-1993), as compared to
the situation without the power plant. However, it should be noted that these results assume an optimum
management of the Manantali dam, which requires accurate forecasts of the flows. This is not possible
today without the forecast system that will be implemented during the Reservoir Management
Optimization Program that is part of the project.
Manantali Reservoir Health Aspects
18.   The creation of the Manantali Reservoir has increased the incidence rates of bilharzia, including
the intestinal form of bilharzia. The project will investigate measures to reduce these incidence rates to
acceptable levels. At present bilharzia snail vector and prevalence rate baseline data are being established
and a monitoring program has started (see Appendix). Patients will be treated and a health education
program will be initiated by the Schistosomiasis Program of the Republic of Mali.

Diama Reservoir and Lake Guiers Health Aspects
19.   After the creation of the Diama Reservoir in 1986, with its constant water level, there was and
still is an explosion of intestinal bilharzia. The problem is serious, specially since there is drug
resistance of the intestinal bilharzia parasite against the preferred drug. If no adequate action is taken,
infected people would suffer severe disability and death. The most affected areas are Canal de Taouey
and Lac Guiers and to a lesser extent around the Diama Reservoir in Senegal. Although this impact is not
caused by the proposed regional hydropower development project, the project will establish some pilot
projects to investigate practical and least-cost measures, including artificial water level fluctuations in
the Diama Reservoir, to reduce the man-snail vector contact and to reduce in this manner the
transmission and incidence rate of intestinal bilharzia at the Senegalese side (no intestinal bilharzia has
been observed on the Mauritania side).
20.   In several heavily bilharzia infested villages, pilot projects will be implemented. These pilot
projects will include shore improvements in the form of cemented banks with easy access, removal of
aquatic weeks (mainly Typha spec.), and a monitoring program of bilharzia snail vector population
densities and infection rates. At the same time, human populations in the area will be screened and
treated for intestinal bilharzia through the ESPOIR program (created in 1991 by the Government of
Senegal to coordinate schistosomiasis-related research and monitoring activities, with the financial and
technical support of the EU, the FAC, the Pasteur Institute and several European Universities). A similar
approach will be followed to investigate the effects of water level fluctuations in the Diama reservoir in
Senegal. The ESPOIR program would execute the bilharzia control and treatment activities, while the
health education component would be financed under the upcoming World Bank Senegal Endemic
Disease Control Project (to be launched in July 1997). It is expected that this approach will reduce the
intestinal bilharzia problem. There is no plan to introduce snail eating exotic fish species in the Diama
reservoir, since the introduction of exotic fish species might cause more problems than they solve. The
Senegal Long-term Water Sector Project also includes a study on the bilharzia snail in the light of the
resistance of the intestinal bilharzia parasite against the preferred drug, alternative control methods
should be developed, e.g., the use or competitor and predator snails to control the intestinal bilharzia
snail vector populations. These activities would be financed under health-related projects, under the
supervision of the Ministry of Health.

Other Risks
22.   Hydrological risks. Hydrological risks, in the form of decreased rainfall, exacerbated by climatic
changes induced by global warming are unknown at present. No long-term predictions exist for West
Africa. It is unknown if the trend of the last 25 years of decreasing rainfall will persist, get worse or
improve.
22.   Dam safety. A five-year inspection of the Manantali dam and its electromechanical equipment
conducted by a reputable independent engineering group in June-July 1996 revealed no major flaws and
confirmed that adequate staffing, procedures and instrumentation were in place for monitoring and
maintenance of the existing facilities. Civil works for the power substation already include all remedial
work identified during the inspection. Additional information has been requested from the inspector
concerning possible seepage under the dam and uplift in the pelite rock area, and has been reviewed
during appraisal and found satisfactory.

Brief Outline of Reservoir Management Studies
1.1    The Reservoir Management Studies includes a hydrological part which will start in the rainy
season of 1997 and will be executed by ORSTOM under OMVS supervision, and financed by FAC. For
this purpose, temporary flood prediction equipment will be installed in Guinea, upstream of the
Manantali dam. The studies involve the analysis of past reservoir management (1987-1995), real time
forecast of flows, optimization of the flood hydrograph, elaboration of a mathematical model and the
preparation of a reservoir management manual (technical specifications). The hydrological part of the
studies is combined with remote sensing activities (the Bank will finance satellite images and processing
of these images) to determine the extent of the flood in a selected part of the floodplain (test zones will
be in the Podor and Matam areas) and field observations to monitor and evaluate the extent and impacts
of floods to calibrate and optimize the flood hydrograph, through an iterative process. However, the
optimization of the flood hydrograph will cover the entire flooded area in the floodplain. A groundwater
baseline survey has been finished and the results will be used in the optimization of the flood
hydrograph. Hydrobiological and fisheries monitoring activities in the upper and middle valley, based
on field surveys and analysis of satellite imagery, will be executed in 1998 and 1999, with financing
from Canada. The years 1997 and 1998 will be used to calibrate the mathematical model. It is expected
that the optimization of the flood hydrograph will be achieved after the artificial flood of 1999.
1.2    The Reservoir Management Program will be based on the simulation of multi-use reservoir
management scenarios and the cost-benefit analysis of the different scenarios and will include the
preparation of a reservoir management guidelines and procedures that will form part of bidding
documents for recruiting the private operator and will then be included in the operator's contract. A
Charter for Water Resource Management will be prepared and subscribed by the three Governments. It
will specify modalities for its revision, arrangements for driest years and dispositions for management of
possible conflicts between water users.
1.3    The status of the Reservoir Management Program will be reviewed during the project launching
workshop in October 1997 and a detailed action plan will be prepared at that time.
Brief Outline of Health Impact Mitigation in the Manantali Reservoir
1.4    Until present the health data collection was limited to the following activities:
a.    Inventory of the different bilharzia snail vector populations: Bulinus species and Biomphalaria
species;
b.    Monitoring of the presence of these snail populations on the banks of the reservoir;
c.    Microscopic analysis of the state of the snails (e.g. if they are dead or alive).
1.5    Another activity will be added to provide a clearer picture of the current situation:
d.    Based on available data from the Mali Schistosomiasis Program, a baseline will be established in
relation to the location of the human settlements and the prevalence levels of bilharzia in these
populations. A separation would be made between urinary bilharzia and intestinal bilharzia. On
the basis of these data, pilot villages will be chosen for a monthly monitoring program.

1.6    During the implementation of the artificial flood at the end of the 1997 rainy season, the water
level in the Manantali Reservoir will fall rapidly. To obtain an insight in the impacts of this rapid water
level fluctuation on the bilharzia snail vector populations a study will be undertaken during a period of
two years by the Manantali Limnology Unit, which will be strengthened for this purpose, and the Mali
Schistosomiasis Program with the following objectives:
a.     To obtain an insight in the population dynamics of the bilharzia snail vector populations, which
are actually present on the banks of the Manantali Reservoir;
b.     Evaluation of the impacts of the reservoir water level variations on these snail  vector
populations. For this purpose the speed, the magnitude, the duration and the frequency of the
reservoir level fluctuations will be monitored and specific variations will also be tested.
1.7    The study includes the following components:
a.    Identification of the contact points between the human population and the snail vectors and the
different snail habitats;
b.     Site selection for the monthly monitoring program;
c.     Establishing for each selected site the contact time man-snail vector;
d.     Laboratory analysis to investigate if snails are infected, after which snails are returned to their
original sites.
e.     Habitat changes at each site, e.g. vegetation type, quality of the substrate, water velocity, water
turbidity and water level;
f.     Evaluation of the impact of the water level fluctuations on the snail vector population, e.g.
determination of the mortality rate through for instance desiccation;
g.     Registration of climatic data: temperature, humidity and rainfall.
1.8    The staff of the Manantali Limnology Unit will be trained in bilharzia research methods by the
staff of the Mali Schistosomiasis Program. Patients will be treated through the Mali Schistosomiasis
Program, which will also identify and implement specific measures to reduce the incidence of bilharzia
around the Manantali reservoir.
