A. STRATEGIC CONTEXT AND RATIONALE
Country and Sector Issues

1.    Strong economic growth is expected to continue. Panama's economy emerged from a
recession in 2003 and has been booming ever since, averaging over 7.5 percent annual growth
during 2004-07, or twice the average growth rate achieved during the previous decade, This
economic boom appears to have been triggered by rapid improvements in the external
environment, coupled with an improved fiscal stance and a strengthened banking system, both
of which raised investor confidence and facilitated the transmission of positive external shocks
onto the domestic economy. An important challenge facing Panama's policymakers i s h o w to
sustain rapid economic growth once the impulse currently being provided by an exceptionally
favorable external environment recedes. In this regard, the recently started Canal expansion
promises to remove an important infrastructure constraint to growth, as the existing Canal
infrastructure is about to reach its maximum traffic capacity.


2.    Growth has helped Panama improve social indicators, but poverty and inequality
remain a challenge. Increased social spending has helped improve social indicators such as
infant and child survival and education enrollment rates at all levels, however, in 2003, over a
third of Panamanians still lived in poverty and more than one-sixth lived inextreme poverty.
Income inequality -with a Gini Index of 0.56 as of 2003- is one of the highest in the world and
higher than the average for countries with similar per capita income levels. Poverty rates are
highest among people with the lowest levels of educational attainment, hence unequal
education opportunities translate into more social and economic disparities (World Bank, 2006),

3.    Rural and indigenous communities are the most affected by resilient poverty and
growing inequality. Poverty affects close to 60 percent of the population in rural areas and
virtually all people living inindigenous areas, where the poverty rate reaches 98 percent (World
Bank, 2006). Nearly 10 percent of Panama's 3.5 million people belong to indigenous groups, the
three largest of which have obtained semi-autonomous comu~cm.ZAll three are far from cities
and roads, adding lack of infrastructure to the challenges of delivering services. The migration
of indigenous people to cities insearch for better economic opportunities has led to risingurban
poverty rates. The percentage of poor people livingincities doubled to 16 percent between 1997
and 2003 (World Bank, 2006).


4.    Furthermore, Panama`s lack of progress in reducing poverty and inequality cannot be
attributed to low levels of social spending. The country spends more than 18 percent of its
GDP in the social sectors, which is substantially higher than the average in Latin America (14
percent) and matches Costa Rica (18 percent), the country with the highest investment in social
programs in the region. Therefore, improving the targeting of social programs and efficiency of
spending must take priority to redress the entrenched inequalities insocial sector outcomes.

Key issues in the Education Sector
5.    The education sector has reached important milestones. More than 18 percent of
government social spending was dedicated to education in2005, up from less than 15 percent in
2000. This increase inspending supported Panama's efforts to broaden access to education in all
areas of the country. From 2000 to 2005, preschool enrollment for children aged 4 and 5
increased from 36 to 62 percent while at primary level enrollment reached universality. Between
1996 and 2005, coverage at secondary level rose by 18 percent and most recently, the
professional and vocational training system entered a phase of rapid modernization and it n o w
offers an increasing number of courses covering the immediate demand for skills in key sectors
of the economy. Inaddition, the proportion of the populationwith completed tertiary education
-9.9 percent in 2000- is higher than the average among Latin American countries -4.9 percent-
and OECD member countries -9.5 percent. With a university gross enrollment rate of 31 percent,
Panama i s the country with the region's second largest proportion of students enrolled in
tertiary education in the region, second only to Argentina. Furthermore, Panama has met the
Millennium Development Goals (MDG) targets of gender parity at all education levels (World
Bank, 2007~).


6.    However, national averages mask deep social, regional and ethnic disparities ineducation
outcomes. To this point, the 2007 joint report from the Programa de Promocidn de la Reforma
Educativa (PREAL) and the Consejo del Sector Privado para la Asistencia Educacional (CoSPAE), as
well as the Primer Informe a1 Serior Presidente de la Reptiblica issued by the Consejo Nacional la
Educacidn (CONACED) in 2006 and the Plan de Concertacidn Nacional para el Desarrollo identified
key challenges for the sector. Key among these are the need for programs to improve quality,
equity, internal efficiency and governance in education and the development of appropriate
systems to measure outcomes and longer term impacts. These are discussed next.


7.    One of the main problems inthe sector concerns the low quality of education produced
by the system. The results from three national assessment tests indicatethat most studentshave
significant problems inapplying essential skills and competencies inbasic academic subjects. In
2000, at least 40 percent of the students showed learning deficiencies in all grades and subjects
tested, particularly inMathematics and Social Sciences (PRODE / ME-BID, 2000). In2001, results
from the Comprender exam confirmed these deficiencies when participating students at all
grades were able to respond less than 50 percent correct answers ineach subject. Moreover, the
2005 SINECA2 results provided a gloomier picture: more than 50 percent of 3rdand 6thgraders
scored "deficient" in all subjects; among 9* and 12thgraders, this proportion rose to 70 percent
inSpanish and85 percentinMathematics.What ismore, in2006, more thanhalf of the students
attempting to enter the University of Panama failed the university entry exam (PREAL-
CoSPAE, 2007).



 The Sistema Nacional de Evaluacih de l a Calidad de 10s Aprendizajes (SINECA) administered its first national
achievement test inNovember 2005. Students ingrades 3, 6 and 9 were tested inSpanish, Mathematics, Social
Sciences and Natural sciences, those ingrade 12 were tested inSpanish, English and Mathematics only.

8.    In addition, inequities are deeply ingrained in the system. Measures of enrollment,
retention, completion and learning achievement show significant disparities between education
levels in urban, rural and indigenous localities. In 2005, net enrollment rates at pre-school,
lower and upper secondary levels reached 55.3 percent (ages 4 and 5), 64.5 percent (ages 12 to
14), and 44 percent (ages 15-17), respectively, compared to universality at primary education.
National net enrollment rates vary widely across regions: for example, secondary net
enrollment rates in the relatively urbanized province of Coldn reach 71 percent compared to 25
percent inthe Ngiibe Bugle' and Kuna Yala comarcus and only 3 percent inthe Embera' comarcus. In
terms of completion rates, only 35 percent of the students living in rural localities and 10
percent living in indigenous communities complete secondary education compared to 60
percent among those living inurban areas. Also, indigenous sixth graders scored well below the
2005 SIENCA national averages, attaining 23.2 inSpanish and 35.9 inMathematics, compared to
44.8 and 46.8 for rural, and 50.9 and 48.6 for urban students, respectively (MEDUCA, 2006).
Learning achievement results were similar among students ingrades 3,9 and 12.

9.    Also, internal efficiency i s low overall and it perpetuates inequalities. Highoverage and
l o w completion rates constitute a sign of persistent internal inefficiency. According to
MEDUCA, the rate of overage students i s 32.2 percent in primary and almost 50 percent in
secondary education (MEDUCA, 2005). As for drop-out and completion rates, cohort analyses
using 2005 data on primary and secondary levels showed that for every 1,000 students who
began primary school, only 817 completed the full six-year cycle and fewer than 600 did so
without repeating a grade. Similarly, at the secondary level, for every 1,000 students entering
grade 7, fewer than half went on to complete grade 12 and only 357 did so without repeating a
grade. Problems of internal efficiency as the ones mentioned above increase operational costs
and reduce spending efficiency. The 2007 PREAL-CoSPAE report estimated that the cost of
grade repetition at all pre-university levels averaged US$17 million per year between 2000 and
2004.

Government Strategy
12.   The GoP's development policy agenda, articulated in its "Strategic Vision of Economic
and Employment Development Toward 2009"                  report,  emphasizes the importance of
government investments in programs aimed at reducing poverty and income inequality,
promoting economic growth and job creation, fostering more transparent public finances,
developing h u m a n capital, and promoting the reform and modernization of government. These
4 pillars go hand in hand with the results emerging from the recently approved Concertacidn
Nacional para el Desarrollo 2007, a participatory process set forth to define the strategic principles
that should guide development policy inthe country.


13. In line with this vision, the Ministry of Education (MEDUCA) articulated its 2005-2009
Strategic Plan, a set of specific sector priorities for the further development of education at all
pre-university levels. These policies and priorities were validated by the National Education
Council (CONACED)3 in its 2006 Report to President Torrijos on the State of Education in Panama.
The 2005-2009 Strategic Plan outlines 4 areas of intervention: (i) quality of education and
learning processes, (ii) education inequality among groups and across regions, (iii)strong
commitment to education from all significant stakeholders, and (iv) strengthening of the

14.    These areas institute strategic lines for action; key among these are the: (i) expansion of
access to pre-school and junior secondary levels; (ii)       improvement of quality inservice delivery;
(iii) evaluation of student learning outcomes alongside teacher education programs; and (iv)
development of intercultural, bilingual education programs to raise educational attainment in
indigenous areas, among others.


15.    Consistent with the GoP's commitment set forth in its 2005-2009 strategic plan, MEDUCA
set in place non-formal education programs to reach poor and disadvantaged communities in
remote locations. The expansion of junior secondary education was mainly supported by its
mandatory status resulting from incorporation of primary and junior secondary to create the
basic education level (grades 1through 9). Inaddition, Panama made important efforts aimed at
measuring education quality in the system. At the national level, the National Decree No. 433
allowed the creation of the National Directorate for Education Evaluation, home to the Sistema
Nacional de Evaluacidn de la Calidad de los Aprendizajes (SINECA), the national student assessment
system responsible for conducting triennial standardize tests to measure student academic
achievement in grades 3, 6, 9 and 12. Internationally, the country participated in the 2006
UNESCO-LLECE4 examination and is scheduled to participate inthe 2009 edition of the OECD's
PISA exam.



Rationale for Bank Involvement

16.   The Country Partnership Strategy (CPS) with Panama approved in 2007 emphasizes
human capital accumulation (Pillar IV) and equity of educational opportunities as key
interventions to reduce poverty and inequality (Pillar I).

17.   Inaddition, the GoP and the Bank have developed a strong base of analytical studies on
Panama's economic growth prospects and social development, including the 2006 Poverty
Assessment,      the 2005 Public Expenditure Review and the 2007 Country Economic
Memorandum. These studies identify the challenges and opportunities facing education,
training and innovation systems in the country and inform policy decision-making to guide
future sector investments, including the proposed project, inline with the Government's policy
agenda.

18.   At the sector level, the Bank has a 10-year technical dialogue and investment partnership
with MEDUCA, supporting operations in initial and basic education (World Bank, 1996 and
2003) with satisfactory outcomes. Currently, the Bank i s financing a Second Basic Education
Project with a Loan of 35 million (Ln. 70320) approved in2000 and an Additional Finance Loan
of US$5 million (Ln. 74830) approved inAugust 2007. The ongoing project is a continuation of
the 1996-2002 First Basic Education Project completed in 2002 and rated successfully in the

Project Development Objective and Key Indicators

23.   The Project Development Objective (PDO) seeks to support the Government of Panama to
improve: (i) the quality of non-formal initial, basic (grades 1-9) and secondary (10-12) education
programs; (ii) the coverage and internal efficiency of initial and basic education; and (iii) the
strategic planning and institutional performance of the Ministry of Education at the central,
regional and local levels. Improvingequity is considered a transversal principle and therefore
embedded ineach component of the Project.

24.   Although the focus of the Project centers on strengthening programs in pre-school (i.e.,
initial education) and basic (grades 1-9) education levels, selected activities at secondary
education (grades 10-12) were incorporated into the Project's design in order to promote a
better integration between basic and secondary education as w e l l as to contribute to reduce the
technology gap in the region by fostering IT literacy skills among young students and
secondary school graduates.

25.   Progress towards PDO accomplishment will be measured against the following Project
Outcome Indicators (POIs) improvements on: (i) the difference inlearning outcomes between a
sample of schools participating in the focalized National Plan for Learning Quality
Improvement compared to the national averages of SINECA exams for grades 3, 6, and 9 in
Spanish and Mathematics; (ii) net enrollment rates for children in early education programs
(age 4 and 5) and basic education (grades 1-9); (iii) the survival rate at 5th grade; (iv) repetition

Lessons learned and reflected inthe project design

43.    Important lessons from the Panama Second Basic Education Project and other similar
Bank financed education projects in Latin America have been incorporated in the design of
the Project. Key among these are: (i) mainstreaming project implementation into ministries'
administrating structures has proven crucial to maximizing ownership and increase project
sustainability; (ii)   defining clear objectives, measurable results and outcome indicators is
important to avoid potential problems of attribution; (iii) maintaining simplicity in the project's
design to maintain some level of control and appropriate monitoring on the number of activities
associated to each component and subcomponents; (iv) embedding impact evaluations on
project design from the outset to gather rigorous evidence that m a y serve to guide future sector
policy and decision making; (v) ensuring quality at entry to avoid delays in project
implementation; (vi) providing technical assistance on a timely basis well before project launch
in order to ensure implementation readiness at the Ministry's Directorates responsible for
fiduciary and procurement responsibilities; and (vii) postponing interventions that require
legislative changes or protracted negotiations with teacher unions, as they m a y not materialize
thus risking the successful completion of the project. In addition, other specific lessons from
existing literature and international experiences are described below.

44.    Interventions targeting the most disadvantaged are associated with important
equalizing effects. Students from lower income families usually have no access to quality
education programs or the necessary school materials, which compromises their academic
performance in school. Carefully targeted interventions contribute to restoring equity in access
to quality education to those students in most need of assistance. The proposed project
interventions are being planned in coordination with the interventions supported by
conditional cash transfer program financed by the World Bank and the IDB.

45.    Early education interventions have demonstrated to improve children's readiness for
reading and writing, and they reduce repetition and drop-out rates in primary school.
Moreover, empirical evidence also show that intensive early education interventions targeted
specifically to disadvantaged children lead to significant equalizing benefits for these children
(Barnett, 1993; Barnett, 1995; Currie and Thomas, 1995; Reynolds, 1998; Karoly et al., 1998;
Danzinger and Waldfogel, 2000; Currie, 2001, Garces et al. 2002, Blau and Currie 2004, and
Schweinhart 2005). Specifically, expansion of non-formal approaches make it possible to
provide early educational services to children of rural and poor urban families and encourage



                                                 14

parental involvement inunderstanding better child rearing practices (MEDUCA, 1999).

46.   Projects providing IBE materials have proven successful in increasing the efficiency
and learning outcomes among indigenous groups. World Bank experience from projects in
Mexico, Guatemala, Bolivia and other Latin American countries supports the case for these
interventions on the basis of their cost-effectiveness as means to reduce repetition and drop-out
rates and increase academic achievement among indigenous populations. What is more, current
efforts to produce bilingual education materials in Panama have yielded valuable information
o n specific teacher IBE training, production and distribution of materials and communication
with the community, which have been taken into consideration during the design of the
Indigenous People Plan for this Project and the overall design of the Project.

47.   Alternative pedagogical methods in multigrade schools have proven successful in
providing quality education in rural and remote areas. Lessons learned from projects
providing education services for rural, dispersed communities in countries like Colombia
(Escuela Nueva), Guatemala (NEU), Brazil (Escola Nova), Bolivia, Togo, France, N e w Zealand and
Pakistan, among others, show that appropriate execution of pedagogically managed multigrade
classrooms can serve as efficient vehicles increase student achievement due in part to the
flexibility of the curriculum and its tailoring to the individual needs of the students.

48.   Distance learning can improve access to secondary levels for students in rural and
remote areas. Evidence from most developing countries show that students completing primary
school in rural multigrade classes are often deprived of the opportunity to attend lower-
secondary school. In particular, evidence from Mexico and Colombia show that distance
education methods via information and communication technology can effectively provide
quality secondary education to students in remote, isolated rural communities. In Panama,
current operation of the program has rendered significant increases in coverage at the lower-
secondary level.

49.   Development of Management Information Systems (MIS) allow the processing of
timely informationessential inpolicy decision-making. The Bank's wide experience on design
and implementation of MIS has demonstrated that effective monitoring of progress in service
delivery requires that managers at the regional and national levels become acquainted and
proficient in the use of automated systems capable of producing reliable information on actual
provision of services in real time. MEDUCA's incipient exposure to MIS allowed the latest
national teacher nomination and selection process to be more expedite and transparent since it
was fully conducted through the Ministry's Human Resource Management System (SIAREH).
Moreover, reliance o n SIAREH has resulted in almost total elimination of duplicate personnel
resource records.

Alternatives considered and reasonsfor rejection

50.   Non-formal pre-school vs. traditional pre-school programs. The alternative of extending
coverage of traditional pre-school education to all children aged 4 and 5 in Panama was
consideredbut rejected because of the highunit cost involved inbuildingadditional classrooms



                                                   15

(particularly in isolated rural communities), hiring and training professional pre-school
teachers, staffing rural, isolated schools whose enrollments are below the threshold for a full
time teacher, and other operational expenses; but also because of the growing body of
supporting international evidence on the quality of non-formal, initial education programs to
achieve, due to early family involvement, a significant impact on the children's academic
achievement and retention inschool.

51.   Massive vs. gradual expansion of non-formal preschool. Massive expansion of the less
expensive non-formal model with community participation was rejected because of the need to
consolidate program institutionalization at the central and regional levels, while building
institutional capacity at MEDUCA to evaluate, follow up and promote the program among
dispersed rural and indigenous populations.

52.   Distance education (Teleba'sica) vs. traditional secondary schools. An alternative to
extend coverage of secondary education to the rural areas through conventional secondary
schools was considered impractical because dispersed populations make it close to impossible
to assemble enough students and teachers to operate a school in small localities. Experience in
other countries, notably Mexico and Colombia, has shown that rural secondary students can be
effectively reached with distance education methods via television. Moreover, lessons learned
from the introduction of Tekbdsica programs inrural communities provide solid evidence for its
nationwide expansion.

53.   Project implementation by a PIU vs. institutionalization at MEDUCA. Project
implementation was decided to be placed within the structure of MEDUCA rather than going
through the traditional PIU scheme in order to maximize the technical and financial
sustainability of the project and promote the progressive institutionalization of programs
supported by this Project. This is inline with operational guidelines adopted by the World Bank
to maximize the risk of sustainability in the longer run and increase institutional capacity in
Borrower's countries.

54.   Establishment of competency-based standards vs. focalized pedagogical training.
Establishing minimum competency-based profiles and standards for supervisors and school
principals would have required changing a n existing Decree, requiring protracted discussions
and negotiations      with teacher    unions thus lowering the probability        of successful
implementation given the country's current political economy. Instead, the alternative was to
concentrate efforts in providingtargeted training for teachers and selected school personnel to
address effectively the learning gaps identified by the results of the 2005 and 2008 SINECA
exams.

55.   Creation of a competitive quality improvement fund vs. targeted quality education
interventions. The design initially contemplated financing a competitive quality improvement
fund to strengthen school-based plans currently in operation. It was decided that at the
moment, MEDUCA does not account for the institutional and technical capacity required to
establish such effort, thus rendering other interventions on teacher training, curricular design



                                                16

and improvement and the provision of technological and didactical aids better attune with the
existing operation mechanisms at the Ministry.

56.   Strengthening measures on the quality and access to initial, primary and junior
secondary vs. system-wide interventions.The design focused on strengthening ECD, primary
and lower-secondary to improve foundational knowledge and skills before moving to upper-
secondary levels. However, quality improvement on a targeted need for IT inupper-secondary
was indeed selected in order to provide IT literacy and employability capacity for secondary
school graduates.

57.   Sector-wide institutional capacity strengthening vs. creation of MIS. Institutional
strengthening is focused on buildingMEDUCA's M I S since the Ministry already accounts for a
system for human resources management that can b e upgraded into a fully articulated MIS.
Also, improvement of MEDUCA's capacity to produce and disseminate timely education
statistics as well as to conduct and process data from national examinations were chosen given
the importance of these two for immediate policy-decision making.

 Sustainability

 84.   The sustainability of project investments is primarily dependent on government ability to
 finance incremental recurrent costs of n e w and expanded educational programs, such as non-
 formal pre-school education and TelebGisicaprograms. A careful first assessment of the financial
 impact of project and sector budget has been made during pre-appraisal and specific
 government commitments have been negotiated to ensure continued financing of incremental
 recurrent costs, especially those associated to the expansion of the CEFACEI and initial
 education programs, and the Telebasica program. The financial sustainability of the Project is
 likely given that the increment inexpenditure is relatively small.

 85.   Sustainability is also dependent on community ownership and willingness to participate
 in preventive maintenance of school buildings and overall management of school operations.
 Social assessments studies produced in support of the Second Basic Education Project helped
 identify relevant stakeholder groups and modes of their involvement inproject preparation and
 implementation. The project will help strengthen regional MEDUCA offices and will provide
 technical assistance and training to ensure that they gradually and competently assume a

  D. APPRAISAL SUMMARY

  EconomicandFinancialAnalyses

  88.   Panama's economy emerged from a recession in 2003 and has been booming ever since,
  averaging over 7.5 percent annual growth during 2004-07, or twice the average growth rate
  achieved during the previous decade. This economic boom appears to have been triggered by
  rapid improvements inthe external environment, coupled with an improved fiscal stance and a
  strengthened banking system, both of which raised investor confidence and facilitated the
  transmission of positive external shocks onto the domestic economy. An important challenge
  facing Panama's policymakers is h o w to sustain rapid economic growth once the impulse
  currently being provided by an exceptionally favorable external environment recedes. In this
  regard, the recently started Canal expansion promises to remove an important infrastructure
  constraint to growth, as the existing Canal infrastructure i s about to reach its maximum traffic
  capacity


  89.   The decision to invest in primary and secondary education is justified by high private
  economic rates of return (9 and 18 percent respectively, in 2003) as estimated by using
  household surveys (wage regressions). A cost-benefit analysis was also done. The Internal Rate
  of Return (IRR) i s estimated at 22 percent, which is a much higher return for an individual than



                                                  28

any average financial market instrument. The Net Present Value of the Project (NPV) is
US$378.4 million for a total project cost of US$42 million, which confirms that the investment is
worthwhile. The different interventionswere selected after runninga cost-effectiveness analysis
to make sure that they provided high impact at a low cost. The fiscal impact of the project's
incremental recurrent costs on the education budget (between 1 and 2 percent) and the
government (0.15 percent) are considered minimal and hence canbe absorbed (see Annex 9).


Technical

90.   International experience shows that provision of access to quality ECD and secondary
education comprise effective mechanisms for reducing inequalities in the distribution of
education and cognitive skills, and promote human capital development in the long run. In
particular, efforts to expose students in secondary levels to technology strengthens their
technological abilities and increases their changes for success should they decide to enter the
labor force at an early age.


91.   In addition, international experience on the relevance of IBE programs for indigenous
peoples has proved to be efficient at reducing the education disparities between this group and
the rest of the population. Moreover, the provision and implementation of IBE programs
contributes to empowering citizens by supporting basic rights granted in national legislation.
The specific content of each component was designed taking into account international
experience and Panamanianrealities drawing on significant World Bank experience.


Fiduciary

92.   Overall project coordination and administration will fall under the Ministry of Education
(MEDUCA) for the proposed project. Therefore, MEDUCA, through its National Directorate of
Finance and Institutional Development (DNFDI) will be directly in charge of financial
management (FM) tasks. These will basically include: (i) budget formulation and monitoring,
(ii) cash flow management (including processing payments and submitting loan withdrawal
applications to the Bank), (iii) maintenance of accounting records, (iv) preparation of in-year
and year-end financial reports, (v) administration of underlying information systems, and (vi)
arranging for execution of external audit. A World Bank Financial Management Specialist may
perform a supervision mission prior to effectiveness to verify the implementation of the unit
and the FM system. After effectiveness, the FM Specialist must review the annual audit and
operational reports, should review the financial sections of the semi-annual IFRs, and should
perform at least one supervision mission per year.

Social
96.   Approximately 10.1 percent of the population of Panama are indigenous peoples
belonging to seven groups: Ngobe (59.3 percent), K u n a (21.6 percent), Embera (7.9 percent),
Bugle (6.2 percent), Wounaan (2.4 percent), Naso (or Teribe), and Bri-Bri (or Bokota) account for
the remaining 2.7 percent. Most groups are bilingual Spanish-autochthonous language(s). The
large majority of indigenous (82 percent) live in rural areas. Some 91 percent of Ngobe live
mainly in rural areas of the provinces of Chiriqui and Bocas del Tor0 and particularly in the
Comarca of Ngobe-Bugle, although some have migrated to East Panama in the last decade.
About 63 percent of the Kuna live in the Comarcas of Madungandi and Kuna Yala in the
provinces of Panama and Darien; the rest have migrated mainly to Panama City and other
urban areas. The Embera and Wounaan live mostly inthe Comarca Embera-Wounaan.

97.   Despite efforts made by MEDUCA in the past, the difference ineducation levels between
non-indigenous and indigenous peoples is striking. Average schooling across generations
nationwide has increased dramatically, however, that of C O ~ ~ Z Y C Ushows a m u c h slower
                                                                           S
increase, lagging w a y behind. Not surprisingly, the indigenous continue to b e the largest group
in extreme poverty. While illiteracy for the non-indigenous is at 5.9 percent for the non-
indigenous, the average is 38 percent for the indigenous of Comarcas, with 49 percent for the



                                                30

Kuna Comarcas. While 60 percent of urban and 35 percent of rural adults have completed
secondary school in Panama, only lopercent of the indigenous in the same cohort have similar
level of schooling. The Panama Poverty Assessment (World Bank, 2006) states that educational
programs targeted to the indigenous areas will be needed if schooling levels of indigenous
peoples are to converge to the national average and extreme poverty is to be reduced in
Panama.


98.   MEDUCA carried out national consultation Forums in 2007 in four Indigenous regions
(Ngabe-Bugle, Kuna Yala, Embera-Wounaan, K u n a of Madungandi) with the Indigenous
Congresses authorities and regional bilingual teachers,            who recommended support to
intercultural bilingual education. Likewise, the Acuerdos de la Concertacion Nacional para el
Desarrollo (October, 2007) recommended the creation of a plurilingual intercultural education
system, and more specifically, the contextualizationof the national curriculum to respond to the
needs of indigenous peoples of Panama in a culturally pertinent manner. Thus, in 2007, Decree
324 created the National Directorate of Intercultural Bilingual Education (DNEIB) at MEDUCA.
The Directorate is being staffed at the central and regional level. It aims at guaranteeing access
to education to all indigenous peoples inPanama, where bilingual education is to be conceived
as an asset, not a liability. Initial steps towards a linguistic and a n intercultural bilingual policy
have been taken. Meanwhile, the present World Bank project will support MEDUCA in
curriculum adaptation from preschool to 4th grade, and the adaptation of bilingual teaching
and learning methods and materials, to respond to the needs of the different groups, plus the
necessary training of MEDUCA staff at central and regional levels a n d pertinent teacher
training to teach IBE.

                                Annex 1: Country and Sector Background
                        Panama: Basic Education Quality Improvement Project

    1. Strong economic growth is expected to continue. Panama's economy grew at a robust
pace of 5 percent per year from 1997 through 2006 and a n even faster 7.5 percent per year from
2004 through 2006, a mild recession in 2004 notwithstanding (World Bank, 2007a)6. Current
estimates suggest growth will continue at almost 9 percent or more in coming years as a
Government-led project to expand the Panama Canal gives a n added boost to the economy
(World Bank, 200%). Real per capita GDP rose 2.7 percent per year since 2000, placing Panama
among the world's upper-middle-income nations with a per capita income of 7,900 (PPP 2006).
Although unemployment is relatively low at 4 percent of the labor force (World Bank, 2006),
Panama's strong growth is heavily concentrated in the export and service sectors, which have
generated relatively few jobs inthe formal economy (Galiani, 2007).


    2. Growth has helped Panama improve social indicators, but poverty and inequality
remain a challenge. Increased social spending has helped improve social indicators such as
infant and child survival and education enrollment rates at all levels, however, in 2003, over a
third of Panamanians still lived in poverty and more than one-sixth lived inextreme poverty.
Income inequality -with a Gini Index of 0.56 as of 2003- is one of the highest in the world and
higher than the average for countries with similar per capita income levels. Poverty rates are
highest among people with the lowest levels of educational attainment, hence unequal
education opportunities translate into more social and economic disparities (World Bank, 2006).


    3. Rural and indigenous communities are the most affected by resilient poverty and
growing inequality. Poverty affects close to 60 percent of the population in rural areas and
virtually all people living inindigenous areas, where the poverty rate reaches 98 percent (World
Bank, 2006). Nearly 10 percent of Panama's 3.5 million people belong to indigenous groups, the
three largest of which have obtained semi-autonomous ~ornur~us.7                    All three are far from cities
and roads, adding lack of infrastructure to the challenges of delivering services. The migration
of indigenous people to cities insearch for better economic opportunities has led to rising urban
poverty rates. The percentage of poor people livingincities doubled to 16 percent between 1997
and 2003 (World Bank, 2006).


    4. Furthermore, Panama's lack of progress in reducingpoverty and inequality cannot be
attributed to low levels of social spending. The country spends more than 18 percent of its
GDP in the social sectors, which is substantially higher than the average in Latin America
(14percent) and matches Costa Rica (lgpercent), the country with the highest investment in
social programs in the region. Infact, if the amount currently spent on social programs were to

    8. Average schooling has increased dramatically inPanama across generations. As seen in
Figure 2.4, while adults born inthe 1930s exhibit in average 5 years of schooling, those born in
the 1980s and entering the labor force today have accumulated twice as much schooling in
average (10.5 years). For urban dwellers, the average years of schooling has more than doubled
between the 1930s and 1980s cohorts. Young adults inurban areas today have in average close
to 12 years of schooling. That is, the average young adult born in the 1980s has completed
secondary school.

    9. While rural adults still have significantly less schooling than their urban peers, they
seem to be slowly catching up.For the 1930s rural cohort, average years of schooling is less than
half of their in urban peers. But for younger adults, born in the 1980s, average schooling has
become only 24 percent lower inrural areas.

    10. The average level of education for adults living in indigenous cornurcus has also be
increasing, but at a significantly lower pace. In fact, as seen in Figure 2.4, while the average
schooling of adults in rural and urban areas have converged closer to the national average,
average schooling for the indigenous seems to be lagging more and more behind. N o t
surprisingly, the indigenous continue to be the largest group inextreme poverty. It is clear that
educational programs targeted to the indigenous areas will be needed if schooling levels of
indigenous peoples are to converge to the national average, and extreme poverty is to be
significantly reduced inPanama.

                             Internal Efficiency: Repetition and Dropout

    27. In addition to enrollment rates, the internal efficiency of the school system has also
improved significantly in the past ten years. In spite of the increase in enrollment in primary
school, both repetition and drop out rates have dropped between 1996 and 2005. A s shown in
Table 2.5, the number of students repeating a grade, as a share of initial enrollment, has fallen
by 25 percent between 1998 and 2005. The number of drop outs, as a share of total enrollment
has also decreased by a similar degree.

    28. For secondary students, dropout rates among secondary students have not declined
much. Interms of repetition, for the two years for which data exist, 2001 and 2004, w e observe
no change in repetition rates. For both primary and secondary school, the extent to which the
system can bring down or at least maintain repetition rates inthe face of expanding enrollment
is important for efficiency of resource allocation.

    29. Analysis based on the 1997 and 2003 LSMS data also suggest that there has been a
substantial decline in repetition rates for both primary and secondary students (see Table 2.6).
For dropout rates at the primary level, the household survey data is also consistent with the
administrative data supplied by the Ministry of Education which suggest a fall in dropout.
However, the household data provides no evidence that secondary school drop out rates fell.
The data actually show a n increase in secondary dropout rates, but this difference from 1997 is
not statistically significant.

    30. For repetition rates at the primary level the evidence is mixed. A s can be seen in the

Arrangements for Results Monitoring

1. The results monitoring for this project will require a high level of coordination between
National Directorates at MEDUCA. M a n y reports necessary for the monitoring progress have to
be developed a compiled by more than one Directorate, and this requires that these units work
jointly from the beginning. MEDUCA has indicated great commitment to achieve this level of
coordination. Output reports of the project will require strengthened capacity several
Directorates in MEDUCA, especially from the National Directorate of Education Planning
(DNPE) which will be responsible for producing and or collecting the several reports and the
information required to monitor POIs.



Information Sources
2. A s part of the proposed Project, the Bank will support MEDUCA to design, put inplace and
execute M&E mechanisms to evaluate Project operation regularly and decide whether or not
adjustments to such mechanisms are needed. To this end, a series of tools and information
sources will be developed to follow closely the extent to which the program delivers efficient
target services as intended. The information sources for this assessment include a
comprehensive management information system, which will provide the Operation Task Team
(OTT) at MEDUCA with tools to follow up physical and financial progress, in addition to
supervise program's processes and operations. The newly proposed M I S will evolve from the
existing data sets in SIAREH and will be complemented with tools to assess the quality of the
information gathered, regular audits (technical, financial, social), and impact evaluations to
selected interventions proposed in the Project. This will contribute to even out differences
between administrative and school data bases. If needed, baseline surveys for the interventions
whose impact will be evaluated will be aligned to the n e w l y proposed MIS.

3. More specifically, information sources and systems will encompass:

   other Directorates, Regional Coordination Units, as w e l l as administrative and procurement
   units in the Ministry to streamline the flow of information between participant units and
   kept he information in the Project scoreboard updated. A series of quality controls will be

   will gather and provide information to manage and support all stages of the program cycle.

    1. Project alignment: Interventions outlined inthis project aim to expand and strengthen
selected education programs inorder to strengthen the overall education supply of the country
and create development synergies between MEDUCA and other international organizations, in
particular, the Inter-American Development Bank and the Spanish Agency for International
Cooperation (AECI).

    2. Development of World Bank support: The proposed Project builds upon 10 years of
dialogue and technical cooperation between the Bank and the Government of Panama. During
this period -covering the First and Second Basic Education Programs- technical assistance and
financing of key education programs have progressively evolved to become interventions
ambitiously address both design and implementation of programs, thus reinforcing ownership
from both areas. This project is fundamentally different from past project experiences inat least
to ways. On the one hand, it scales up previous interventions (e.g., from provision of bilingual
books to the operationalization of intercultural bilingual education programs) building on
development synergies created by paired projects from other international financing
institutions. And on the other, as the project falls under total MEDUCA design and execution, it

aims to strengthen MEDUCA's incipient culture for strategic planning, which will later prove
advantageous in the Ministry's efforts to secure international financing for later education
projects.

    3. Sector issues. This project addresses three sector problems: (i) low quality of learning
outcomes in basic education, (iii) inequality in access to education opportunities for people
living in rural and indigenous communities, and (iii) low institutional administrative and
managerial capacity inthe education sector.

    4. Project Development Objectives (PDOs). The Project Development Objective (PDO)
seeks to support the Government of Panama to improve: (i)         the quality of non-formal initial
education, basic (grades 1-9) and secondary (9-12) education programs; (ii)        the coverage and
internal efficiency of initial and basic education; and (iii)
                                                            the strategic planning and institutional
performance of the Ministry of Education at the central, regional and local levels. Improving
equity is considered a transversal principle and therefore it is embedded ineach component of
the Project.

    5. Project Outcome Indicators (POIs). Progress towards PDO accomplishment will be
measured against the following Project Outcome Indicators (POIs) improvements on: (i)
learning outcomes among a selected sample of schools participating in the focalized National
Plan for Learning Quality Improvement, as compared to the national averages of SINECA
exams for grades 3,6, and 9 inSpanish and Mathematics; (ii) net enrollment rates for children in
early education programs (age 4 and 5) and basic education (grades 1-9); (iii) the rate of
students at the 5th grade; (iv) repetition rate in basic education levels (grades 1-6); (v)
graduation rates from grade 9; and (vi) the percentage of total annual student report cards that
will be available for on-line consultation by teachers, students, parents, school personnel and
other education authorities. Since equity is the transversal principle, POIs will reflect gender,
ethnicity, region, pedagogical model of instruction and school-type differences, in addition to
income decile differences w h e n applicable.

    6. Project Implementation Arrangements. MEDUCA will take overall responsibility for
managing the Project within its regular institutional structure, w h i c h will facilitate compliance
with technical, financial, and administrative procedures supporting project progress towards
PDO accomplishment. Overall responsibility for the successful completion of the Project will
fall under the authority of the Director General for Education (Director General de Educacidn),
responsible also for the oversight of each of the three components. National Directorates will b e
responsible for implementing programs and activities outlined in their corresponding sub-
component and will be held accountable for the periodical oversight and successful completion
of their respective interventions. MEDUCA's Regional Coordinating Units will be responsible
for coordinating activities at the regional and local levels, mainly, those related to expansion of
existing and the implementation of n e w programs. Day-to-day operations will be monitored by
a n Operation Task Team embedded in MEDUCA's institutional structure. M&E mechanisms,
outlined in the Project Operation Manual, will provide the basis to ensure vertical integration
and horizontal collaboration between these units.

at MEDUCA will be responsible for conducting the national and international bidding
processes resulting from the activities proposed in this project. Procurement management tasks
include the execution and timely report of progress implementation inthe projects Procurement
Plan, as well as annual audits with emphasis on physical inspections plus annual procurement
post-reviews as a means to mitigate risk of highly decentralized delivery of goods (Le., various

implementation (Le., Operational Task Team and the Project Steering Committee) to provide

procurement reports for the Bank. Similarly, the Finance Unit (Direccidn de Finanzas) at

                       Annex 9: Economic and Financial Analysis
                    PANAMA: BasicEducationQuality Improvement Project

                             Economic Analysis of the project

I. MacroeconomicContexP

1. Panama's economy emerged from a recession in 2003 and has been booming ever
since, averaging over 7.5 percent annual growth during 2004-07, or twice the average
growth rate achieved during the previous decade. This economic boom appears to have
been triggered by rapid improvements in the external environment, coupled with an
improved fiscal stance and a strengthened banking system, both of which raised
investor confidence and facilitated the transmission of positive external shocks onto the
domestic economy. An important challenge facing Panama's policymakers is h o w to
sustain rapid economic growth once the impulse currently being provided by an
exceptionally favorable external environment recedes.           In this regard, the recently
started Canal expansion promises to remove an important infrastructure constraint to
growth, as the existing Canal infrastructure is about to reach its maximum traffic
capacity.

2. Inrelying on the Canal expansion to generate a new growth impulse, however, there
is a potential danger that Panama could settle back into the same patterns of duality that
have characterized its development until now. That pattern has been marked by rapid
growth inthe open, internationally competitive services sectors, with little spillover into
the agriculture and industrial sectors, which had been left to stagnate behind protective
barriers and excessive regulatory rigidities. Of particular concern i s the very inequitable
distribution of income that was engenderedby this dual development model. Panama's
Gini index is among the highest in the world, and considerably higher than the average
for countries with similar per-capita income levels. This unequal distribution of incomes
also is accompanied by a wide dispersion of social indicators across urban-rural, socio-
economic and indigenous/ethnic divides. In spite of Panama's upper middle-income
development level, a surprisingly large share of the population (37percent in2003) lives
below the national poverty line, with 17percent livinginextreme poverty.

3. In order to leverage growth prospects from the Canal expansion and associated
investments, the Government of Panama (GoP) has recognized and the central tole of
deepening investments inhuman capital. These are seen as essential to increase the skill
and productivity level of the labor force, while at the same time reducing the high level

11.     Methodological approach

1.      Macroeconomic conditions and the economic outlook inPanama warrant further
investments in human capital in broad terms and in education more specifically tpo
leverage growth and promote social development. However, it is necessary to determine
where to invest, the worthiness of the investment, and the eflectiveness of the interventions
vis-&vis their cost. The analysis here relies on three established methodologies to answer
these questions and guide decision making. The first method explores the economic
rates of return (ERR) to determine the worthiness of the public investment. The second
method investigates the internal rate of return (IRR) of the project to provide
information on the overall return to the project investment, meaning the pay-off per
dollar investment. Finally, cost-effectiveness analysis is used to assess the different
interventions in terms of their level of impact per dollar invested. It should b e noted that
assumptions are made in all three methodologies and therefore the findings should be
interpreted with caution and in conjunction with criteria that is not merely economic or
that rather m a y not be quantifiable ineconomic terms.

111.    Economic Rates of Return

2.      H u m a n capital accumulation has been long recognized as a critical factor both
from a macroeconomic perspective (economic growth, GDP per capita) and a
microeconomic perspective (jobs and wages). While the first approach concentrated in
cross-country regressions that try to find correlations between a certain macroeconomic
variable and enrollment rates or average number of education, the second looked to the
economic returns of education to the individual by regressing education levels on wages
(as dependent variable). In essence, both perspectives are looking at the same
phenomenon. From a macroeconomic perspective, education improves economic
growth and competitiveness by having a more skilled labor force, while from a
microeconomic perspective the individual reaps the benefits of its higher productivity in
terms of better wages.

3.      M o r e recently, and in the wake of mixed empirical results derived from cross-
country regressions, and the econometric challenge of some of the analysis in terms of
their robustness, a n e w literature (Pritchett, 2004, Hanushek & Wobmann, 2007) is
focusing not just in the quantity of education, measured by variables such as enrolment
rates or years of education, as a determinant of economic growth and GDP per capita,
but also in the quality of education, measured by assessing learning outcomes using



                                            97

mainly results form standardized tests). The results show that school attainment appears
to be correlated with higher economic growth and individual earnings, but also with
lower income inequality.

4.     According to the 2006 Panama Poverty Assessment, these rates of return have
been increasing steadily since 1991. They are high, have been increasing over time, and

IV.     InternalRates of Return
9.      Inaddition to the economic rates of return (also called education sector rates of
return), it is worth looking into the Internal Rate of Return of the project. This
methodology assesses the future income flows of those directly benefiting from the
project under a set of assumptions.

10.     The project includes several components and subcomponents that will produce
economic benefits: (i) Improvements inlearning outcomes, (ii) Higher enrollment inpre-
primary, (iii)  Reduced repetition and drop-outs in primary education, and (iv) Higher
enrollment in upper primary. This section estimates the benefit stream from these
components.

11.     The combination of these components is expected to have a number of effects:

    P   Increases in school enrollment and reduced dropout and repetition, resulting in
        higher levels of educational attainment and increased productivity and earnings
        over the long term.
    P   Improvements in the quality of primary and secondary education, resulting in
        higher returns to education.

12.     The m a i n conduit for quantifiable economic benefits is the increased levels of
education attainment, which will lead to higher incomes. This conclusion is supported
by a number of previous analyses in Panama, summarized in the World Bank Panama
Country Economic Memorandum (2007) and the Panama Poverty Assessment (2006). The
intergenerational transmission of poverty and inequality through the educational
system is clear.

13.     While the potential benefits of increased education are numerous, m a n y are
difficult to quantify. The effects of greater quantity and quality of education on the
quality of life, citizen participation, and parenting skills, along with economic spillover
effects, cannot be easily measured or predicted. Consequently, this analysis focuses on
the economic effects interms of private economic benefits, measured as higher expected
earnings, for individuals directly participating in any of the project's programs. These
higher expected earnings are driven by increased educational attainment for individuals
benefiting from the project. Because it is very likely that the other effects are likely to b e




                                            99

positive and maybe substantial, the quantified economic effects should b e taken as a
lower boundon the total benefits of the project.

14.      This methodology estimates the effects of these components and subcomponents
on completed years-of-schooling for benefiting children. These years-of-schooling are
then summed up to calculate a total increase in years of schooling for all program
participants. A stream of lifetime income is then simulated for a n individual with one
additional year of schooling, which is adjusted by the probability of not enrolling and/or
droppingout, and the estimated rate of teacher absenteeism. The marginalincome gains
from one additional year of schooling are multiplied by the total increase in years of
schooling across all participants to simulate the benefit stream. These figures are then
combined with the project costs figures (for the three components) to estimate an
internal rate-of-return for the program14.

15.      The key inputs for the estimate of economic benefits are the number of children
participating in each component, the average additional education attainment (years of
schooling) achieved by each child, the private returns to education, and the base income.

V.      Sensitivity Analysis

20.     The assumptions used for the analysis are conservative. Inparticular, w e assume
no wage growth other than that created by the increased education levels due to the
program. If wages grow in the economy overall, and the rate of return to education in
percentage terms remains at the same level, the long-term gain in absolute terms from
increased education and from the program would b e higher.

21.     The largest source of uncertainty inthe estimates of economic gains is tied to the
estimates inthe gains inyears of schooling from the program, particularly the additional
effect of improvements inquality. To test this element for sensitivity, the estimated gains
from each component were cut in half. Under these much reduced assumed gains, the
internal rate of return drops to 18 percent and the net present value to US$173.0 million,
which represent nonetheless sizable returns per dollar spent.

4. Legal Framework
10. Law # 47 (1946 Constitution), decreed the homogenization of the education system,
contents, methods and models for all Panamanian citizens, thus undermining
indigenous cultures and languages. A Special Program for indigenous peoples was
created under an Education Reform in the 1960s which was voided thereafter. But in
1995, Law 47 was replaced by Law # 34: "education for indigenous peoples must be
framed within principles of national education, but it must be developed according to
intercultural and bilingual characteristics, objectives and methodologies".

11.In1971Panamaratified ILOAgreement 107(1957).

12. The Constitution of Panama, adopted in 1972 and reformed in 1978,1983,1993,1994
and 2004 makes reference to the rights of Indigenous Peoples to education in Title 111,
Title IV, Art 84: decrees that Indigenous languages will be object of study, preservation
and dissemination; the State will promote bilingual education in Indigenous
Communities. Art 86 states: The State recognizes and respects the ethnic identity of the
Indigenous Communities; it will carry out programs to develop material, social and
spiritual values characteristic of each culture.

13. Chapter V, on Education, Art 104: the State will develop education and promotion
programs for Indigenous groups as they have their own cultural patterns, to aim at their
active participation insociety.

14. In1998: Reglamento Organico de la Comisicin de Asuntos Indigenas

15. In2000 Executive Decree No.1 creates the Consejo Nacional de Desavrollo lndigena. In
that same year, Law No. 20 protects intellectual property of collective rights of
indigenous peoples for the protection and defense of their cultural identity and
traditional knowledge. ExecutiveDecree No. 12: creates the regulation.

16. In September 2007, Panama signed the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of
Indigenous Peoples.

18. The information systems do not include the "ethnic group" and "native language"

 7. Demographic & Ethno-linguistic Profile
19. In demographic terms, indigenous groups have high population growth rates. The
average fertility rate is 6.6 children per woman, in contrast with 2.9 for non-indigenous
women. Household over-crowding (8 people) is common, compared to 5.8 in poor
households. Life expectancy (64-66 years) is significantly lower than the national
average of 75.6 years.

20. Some 10.1 percent of the population are Indigenous: Ngabe (59.3 percent), Kuna
(21.6 percent), Embed (7.9 percent), Bugle (6.2 percent), Wounaan (2.3 percent), and
Naso (or Teribe), and Bri-bri (or Bokota) account for the remaining 2.7 percent. Only 57
percent of the indigenous live in Comarcas and many live in Panama City. Seven
languages other than Spanish are spoken in Panama: Kuna, Ngabere, BuglQe, Embera,
Wounaan, Teribe and Bri Bri.

21. Although the native language of the Afro-Caribbean population is not Spanish but

8. Official and Traditional Entities overseeing IndigenousAffairs inEducation
22. The Kunas, Emberas, Wounaan, Ngobe, Bugle, and Naso are represented by
Congresses. At the community level, all decisions (including education) are made under
the consent of the Sahila or Cacique, who in turn consult with their respective Comarca
Congress.

23. The political arms of the Congresses are the Comarca Governors. The Teribe are
represented by a King. At the national level, Indigenous Congresses are represented by
the Coordinadora Nacional de Pueblos Indigenas de Panama (COONAPIP). Two other official
organizations are the Committee of Indigenous Affairs, to present appeals to the
National Congress, and the Directorate of Indigenous Policy in the Ministry of Interior
and Justice overseeing issues of land tenure, large infrastructure and human rights.

9. Migration
24. Migration to Costa Rica (Northwest) and immigration from Colombia (Southeast)
present challenges to student permanence in the classroom. Many indigenous, mostly
Ngabe and Bri-bri (Guaymi and Bri-bri in Costa Rica), migrate twice a year particularly
for seasonal agriculture labor. The Colombian border, particularly the provinces of
Darien and Comarca Kuna Yala, receive hundreds of displaced people fleeing the
military conflict in Colombia for many years21, adding an extra challenge to education
services.


10. Illiteracy, schooling, gender and those still outside the education system
25. Literacy in indigenous regions improves slower than in non-indigenous, widening
further the gap between those who have and those who do not have educational
opportunities. While illiteracy is 7.8 percent at the national level (2002), it is of 39.7
percent for Comarca Embera, 39 percent for Kuna Yala and 53.6 percent for Ngabe-Bugle,
but the number of illiterate women almost doubles that of men. Likewise, the national
average years of schooling for the population between 25 and 59 years of age is 8.6
grades, but there was a large difference between the population inthe first quintile, with
4.1 grades, and the fifth quintile with 12.1 grades. The indigenous population had on
average 2.9 grades22. For the Comarca Ngabe-Bug16 it is 2.7 years, while it is of 3.9 years
for the Ember6 and 4.8 for Kuna-Yala.
