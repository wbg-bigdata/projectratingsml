                                I.   INTRODUCTION

1.     The proposed Third Economic Reform Support Grant (ERSG 111) i s the
second in a programmatic series, which was designed to assist the Government of
Burundi (GOB) to transform Burundi's economy from a post-conflict context and to
design and implement policies to accelerate growth and promote human development,
including through private sector development. The ERSG series (ERSG I1 and 1 1 builds
                                                                                1)
on the Emergency Economic Recovery Credit (EERC, 2000), the Economic
Rehabilitation Credit (ERC, 2002), and ERSG I  (2006) which was not part o f the series.

2.      The proposed ERSG I11 operation directly follows on ERSG I1 and will
support the government to continue: (i)reforms in the public finance management
system to improve accountability and transparency in public spending; (ii)reforms
in the business, legal, and institutional enyironment to foster private sector-led
growth; and (iii) reforms in the coffee and domestic petroleum subsectors. It
proposes to: (i) consolidate reforms already underway in public finance management (as
identified in the recent joint 2008 PEMFAR); and to (ii) reenergize the reform process in
private sector development (specifically through the legal, and regulatory framework for
the general business environment, and the coffee and the domestic petroleum reform
process). In addition, it proposes to continue diagnostic studies and to support the
development o f a strategic approach in areas where action is needed but knowledge i s too
limited (such as public enterprises).

3.     The program structure aims to maintain a balance between the need for
essential reforms and the simplicity dictated by the weak institutions and
implementation capacity. This balance i s maintained with a particular bias toward
deepening the reform process in areas already supported by predecessor operations,
where government's buy-in has already been established. Overall, the program continues
to support the establishment o f new legal and institutional frameworks to implement
essential reforms. Given the current limits o f fiscal space, it was also designed to help the
government to maintain adequate financial resources through: (i)        economic growth, (ii)
                                    i idebt
external financial assistance, and ( i ) relief.

4.      The ERSG I11 operation would be disbursed in a single SDR 16 million (US$25
million equivalent) tranche, which would be released upon effectiveness in fall 2009,
provided that macroeconomic performance continues to be satisfactory. Areas where
progress will be monitored for future operations (e.g., ERSG I V and V programmatic
series) are suggested. These areas will be evaluated later with the authorities in light o f
the forthcoming preparation o f a new PRSP, and the next Consultative Group (CG)
meeting, two events which follow Burundi's HIPC Completion Point reached in January
2009. Progress in the overall reform program will be tracked over the l i f e o f the ERSG
grant series through these prior actions and other related measures in the policy matrix.



                                             8
5.      While the ERSG operation series are risky operations, they are needed to
support this politically fragile and institutionally weak country. Burundi i s highly
dependent on budget support to transition out o f a post-conflict economic environment to
one that i s focused on accelerating growth and human development. The risks o f the
operation are counter balanced by potential rewards o f macroeconomic stabilization,
which would be sustained through external support o f the budget and substantial
fiduciary facilitation o f core policy reforms. The fiduciary section o f this document
outlines the measures that will be taken to mitigate the risks attached to these operations.

6.      The ERSG series attempts to mitigate r i s k s through maintaining continuity
in the reform process and by leveraging other on-going technical assistance and
capacity building programs. The design o f the ERSG series as a two-operation
programmatic series, which maintains continuity in the policy dialogue and reform
agenda, i s one major way o f mitigating the risks associated with these budget support
operations. This effort i s further bolstered by technical assistance and capacity building
from the Economic Management Support Project (EMSP), in partnership with IFC PEP-
AFRICA program, which contribute to strengthen institutional capacity and support
crucial reforms. The alignment o f ERSG series with the budget cycle should be
completed by the eventual follow-up grant (ERSG IV), expected in August 2010. The
release o f the proposed single-tranche operation will coincide with the new budget cycle,
whose implementation has been delayed. It i s now expected to be adopted by the
authorities (July 2010 to June 2011) as part o f their recent membership in the East
African Community.

7.     At the conclusion o f the proposed ERSG 11-111 programmatic series, tangible
progress with respect to each component o f the proposed reform program i s
expected, including: (i)measurable improvements in public finance management, (ii)
measurable improvements in the environment for private sector development, and (iii)
progress in the execution o f the reform program in the coffee and domestic petroleum
subsectors.

                              11.   COUNTRY CONTEXT

                         A.     POLITICAL   AND   SOCIAL CONTEXT

8.      I n the time following the Arusha Peace Agreement o f 2000, Burundi has
experienced both great strides and major setbacks on the road to lasting peace.
Following 13 years o f civil war, 39 politico-ethnic groups signed a comprehensive peace
and reconciliation agreement in Arusha (Tanzania) in August 2000. This significant
political turning point paved the way for the establishment o f a transitional government in
2002, and the approval o f a new constitution in February 2005 providing for appropriate
ethnic checks and balances. With the establishment o f this basic constitutional
framework, presidential and parliamentary elections were held in August 2005, and the
new government took office in September 2005. In September 2006, a cease-fire accord
was reached with the last hold-out rebel movement, the FNL-PALIPEHUTU faction.




                                             9
9.      Following the remarkable progress made in 2005 and 2006, the political and
security situations deteriorated somewhat over the course o f 2007 and early 2008. In
early 2007, following the removal o f the old leadership o f the ruling party (CNDD-FDD),
the political tensions increased as the government lost i t s parliamentary majority, leading
to a political deadlock. The security situation deteriorated somewhat following the last
rebel group's (FNL) withdrawal from the peace negotiations in July 2007. Nonetheless,
an agreement with the major opposition political parties was reached in November 2007,
resolving the political deadlock. The cabinet was reshuffled to bring in representatives of
the two major opposition parties. The Parliament, whose activity had been blocked by the
political impasse, resumed i t s activity. At the beginning o f 2008, a new political impasse
emerged related to the dismissal o f the first vice president (due to political discord within
her own party) and the national assembly did not meet until mid-2008. This situation was
fbrther complicated by the degradation in the security situation, when the FNL-
PALIPEHUTU, which had declared i t s willingness to restart peace negotiations, broke
the cease-fire in April 2008, leading to repeated armed skirmishes with government
troops and some 100 deaths.

10.    A turning point came in M a y 2008, when FNL-PALIPEHUTU leadership
again declared their willingness to reach a negotiated peace. In June 2008, following
pressures from the international community and intervention by regional powers such as
South Africa and Tanzania, the FNL leadership returned to Bujumbura, including
Agathon Rwasa, the movement's leader. They rejoined the Joint Agreement o f
Verification and Control Mechanism (JAVC) after the government reiterated the
immunity granted to the FNL leadership. In 2008, the President o f the Republic and the
FNL leader agreed to meet on a bi-weekly basis for peace talks, and to restore public
confidence. In late July 2008, following a contested Constitutional Court decision, 22
parliamentarians who had rebelled against the ruling party were replaced by other
members o f the same party in the Parliament, thereby allowing the ruling party to recover
the majority in this legislative body. A new cease-fire was signed, which facilitates the
consolidation o f the peace process and further demobilization and reintegration o f the ex-
combatants into civilian life.

 11.     Since negotiations began again in June 2008, progress has been steady and
the security situation has improved somewhat. A major breakthrough came on
December 4, 2008, when the government and the last rebel group, the FNL-
PALIPEHUTU, signed a power sharing agreement. In January 2009, the two sides were
able to resolve two o f the major outstanding issues in the peace process: (i) rebel
                                                                                   the
group removed the ethnic connotation o f i t s name - PALIPEHUTU - shortening it to
FNL and therefore making the group constitutionally eligible to register as a political
party; and (ii) government on i t s side freed FNL political prisoners and prisoners o f
               the
war. In addition, preparation for the demobilization o f FNL combatants i s underway with
the two parties committing to start disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration no
later than January 30, 2009 (see below). On April 21, 2009, FNL became Burundi's 42"d
political party. Furthermore, important issues o f the peace process such as the
reunification o f FNL child soldiers with their families and the integration o f some of the
FNL forces and leadership into the Burundi armed forces and national institutions,
including the government are being addressed.


                                             10
 12.      Over the course o f the recent disturbances, prospects have remained positive
for the integration o f all armed factions into new security and defense forces, and
the demobilization and reintegration o f former combatants not being enlisted in
these forces. The national Demobilization, Reinsertion, and Reintegration program
(DRR), launched in 2004, has benefited from the support o f the Bank and Multi-country
Demobilization and Reintegration Program for the greater Great Lakes (MDRP).' The
presence o f the UN peacekeeping operation (UNOB) played a major role in facilitating
the implementation o f the DRR program and the accompanying security sector reform
             The
programm2 peace process has attracted considerable attention. In June 2006, the U N ' s
Peace Building Commission (PBC) decided to create a country-specific group for
Burundi, one o f only three countries benefiting from this status. In response to a request
f i o m the Government o f Burundi, a new phase o f support for DRR was prepared by the
government, the World Bank, and interested donors. Focusing on the dismantling o f the
irregular forces3, this new program will be supported by the World Bank through an IDA
grant and a single-purpose, multi-donor trust fund administered by the World Bank. In
June 2009, the World Bank's Board approved a US$15 million IDA grant to help the
Government o f Burundi facilitate peace building, to preserve and restore human social
capital, particularly related to conflict. The governments o f the Netherlands, Norway,
Sweden and the European Commission have made pledges to the program and other
donors are exploring additional contributions. The new operation liaises closely with the
United Nations Mission to Burundi and the U N D P for an integrated and consolidated
response to DRR.

13,     Overall, notwithstanding the general progress toward the consolidation o f
peace, the underlying economic forces leading to recurrent conflicts will need to be
addressed through economic and legal reforms, as well as economic diversification
and growth to achieve lasting peace and stability. Burundi i s a small country with a
high population density. While the country i s agriculturally rich, there i s strong
competition for resources, especially for land in rural areas where 90 percent o f the
population lives. The thirteen years o f recurring conflict has had a devastating effect on
Burundi's economy. With a population o f 8.5 million, Burundi's per capita income fell
by about 40 percent, from US$l80 in 1993 to US$110 in 2007. Among children less than
five years old, undernourishment reached 57 percent in 2000, but has improved in recent
years. This i s exacerbated by a growing population and at times ethnic tensions.
Furthermore, the peace era i s seeing the return o f large numbers o f internal and external

                                  D.          SUSTAINABILITY
                                           DEBT                            ANALYSIS
45.      With the attainment o f HIPC Completion Point in January 2009, Burundi's
external debt situation has improved, but remains vulnerable to debt distress due to
its narrow export base. Prior to HIPC Completion Point, Burundi was one o f the most
heavily indebted countries in Africa with public debt representing about 174.1 percent o f
GDP in 2007. The country was granted access to debt r e l i e f under the enhanced HIPC
Initiative in August 2005. In January 2009, as a result o f sustained prudent economic
management, Burundi reached HIPC Completion Point, at which time debt r e l i e f under
the Enhanced HIPC Initiative from all o f Burundi's creditors was revised upward to
US$833 million in net present value (NPV) terms from US$826 million estimated at the
time o f the Decision Point document. HIPC debt relief includes US$425 million from
IDA and US$28 million from the IMF (NPV terms). By reaching the HIPC Completion
Point, Burundi also became eligible for additional debt r e l i e f from the IMF, IDA, and the
African Development Fund (AfDF) under the Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative (MDRI).
MDRI r e l i e f net o f HIPC assistance would lead to a nominal reduction o f debt owed to
IDA and the IMF by US$90 million and SDR9 million (US$15 million) respectively. In
the context o f the HIPC CP, a debt sustainability analysis was conducted. I t s findings
and conclusions are summarized in B o x 1.
                                    Box 1: Debt Sustainability Analysis
The latest debt sustainability analysis (DSA), prepared in the context of the HIPC CP, assesses Burundi's
external public debt dynamics using the forward-looking debt sustainability framework for low-income
countries (LIC). This DSA indicated that, prior to HIPC Completion Point, Burundi was one of the heavily indebted
countries in Africa, with public debt representing in 2007 about 174.1 percent o f GDP.
The baseline scenario assumes continued interim HIPC debt relief through the medium term. An alternative scenario
o f the DSA models the HIPC Completion Point and the delivery of additional MDRI debt relief in September 2008.
Most debt burden indicators substantially exceed their policy-based thresholds under the baseline scenario. Under the
alternative scenario, the risk of debt distress i s significantly reduced but remains elevated.
Burundi is currently in debt distress. Even after HIPC relief and MDRI assistance, Burundi's external public debt
burden indicators are expected to remain high over the medium term, leaving Burundi highly vulnerable to shocks that
could lead to lower exports and economic growth. The NPV of debt-to-exports ratio for Burundi i s expected to have
declined from 967 percent at end-2007 to 96 percent at end-2008. I t would subsequently rise to 113 percent by the end
2018, before continuing on a slow downward trend, reaching 69 percent by the end o f the projection period.
For Burundi to lower its high debt stock ratios with respect to exports and to maintain the other.debt indicators
below their respective thresholds, particular emphasis needs to be placed on four aspects of future economic
performance. First, the authorities need to implement a strong and sustained reform effort, especially to develop a
diversified export base, in order to support robust growth in exports and GDP. Second, the authorities need to
implement prudent fiscal and monetary policies to ensure fiscal discipline and macroeconomic stability. Third, together
with prudent debt management, future financing needs to be in the form o f grants and highly concessional loans in the
short and medium term. Fourth, attention should be given to strengthening debt management policies and institutions."

Similarly, the Sources o f Rural Growth recommends transforming the use o f Burundi's
rural assets, notably coffee, to enable this sector to become a better contributor to shared
growth in the short term and to provide the state with sufficient revenues to assist in the
long term diversification o f the economy. The reinforcement o f export crops'
contribution to growth will require a sound privatization o f the coffee and tea sub-sectors
as a means o f improving the sector's competitiveness and o f contributing to shared
growth. The study on divestiture options for the assets o f the coffeejliire provided the
authorities with concrete advice o n how to proceed o n the privatization o f the sector.

65.     Finally, the World Bank provided, at the request o f the government, a TA for the
revision o f the Burundian downstream petroleum sector. I t s report revealed, among other
recommendations, the need to reiterate the authorities' position o n the principle o f a
quasi-automatic adjustment o f local fuel prices to changes in international prices, and to
stabilize operational margins, customs duties and taxes de transaction (transaction taxes)
for greater transparency and predictability o f government revenue.23

       a. Following almost thirteen years o f c i v i l war, which devastated i t s infrastructure

68.     As a follow-up o f ERSG 11, ERSG I11 will be focused on the same issues,
which includes: (i) improving public finance management; and (ii) promoting
private sector development. As in ERSG 1 , the PSD component o f ERSG I11 has two
                                              1
main sub-components: (a) improving the business environment; and (b) restructuring the
export crop sector. In addition, ERSG I11 will address issues affecting the supply and
price o f petroleum products, which are critical to support activities o f private enterprises.

   a. Establishing a transparent public finance management. Improving public
      finance management procedures and practices i s essential to make the budget an
      effective instrument o f growth and poverty reduction, and to reassure the donor
      community o f the commitment o f the government to continue and intensify the
      fight against corruption. Substantial results have been achieved in P F M in the
      context o f ERSG Iand 1 . Additional efforts are needed to improve the strategic
                                 1
      content o f the budget planning process and the transparency o f Burundi's public
      finance management system. Better budget preparation, execution and
      monitoring, procurement, and improved controls will, therefore, remain at the
      center o f the policy and institutional reforms supported by ERSG 1 1
                                                                         1.
   b. Improving business environment. A conducive investment climate i s crucial to
      creating a private sector-led growth for poverty reduction. The Burundian private
       sector i s underdeveloped. It includes a large number o f small enterprises, most of
       which operate in the informal sector. The development o f the formal sector and
       the privatization o f public enterprises are hampered by the small size o f the
       domestic market, the landlocked position o f the country, an uninviting business
       climate, and the lack o f an effective dialogue between the government and private
       enterprises. The ERSG I11 program will include a series o f measures already



                                             32
          initiated but only partly implemented under ERSG I and I1 to improve the
          business environment, modernize the legislative and regulatory framework,
          improve the dialogue between the public and the private sectors, and provide tax
          incentives24for small and medium-sized enterprises.
      c. Restructuring the coffee sector. Burundi i s primarily an agrarian economy.
         Coffee i s the main export o f the country and the main source o f cash income for
         close to 600,000 poor rural families. Although output declined sharply over the
         past ten years, Burundian coffee has good potential for sales on specialty markets.
         Therefore, improving production, productivity, and prices, i s an essential
         component o f a growth and poverty reduction strategy and i s one o f the main
         themes o f the private sector development objectives o f the ERSG program. So far,
         progress in this area has been slow and subject to reversals, but recent decisions
         seem to indicate that the government i s now determined to address more
         forcefully the politically sensitive issue o f coffee sector privatization. With
         funding from the Economic Management Support Project, international
         consultants have been recruited to help define the most appropriate regulatory and
         organizational framework and identify options for privatizing coffee processing
         facilities. Following a detailed review o f the report, the government has now
         chosen one o f the strategic options proposed by the consultants, prepared an
         action plan and initiated a communication campaign aimed at explaining its
         decision and engaging all the stakeholders in the reform process. As a part o f this
         strategy, a decree setting up a new regulation agency was promulgated by the
         President o f the Republic. Bidding documents for the sale o f washing stations
         were published and a conference o f potential investors was organized. ERSG I11
         will accord the highest priority to supporting the proposed reform, monitoring
         implementation and evaluating results.
      d. Reforming the domestic downstream petroleum sector. Petroleum products are
         the main import o f the country and a reliable delivery system i s essential to the
          development o f private sector activities. Recent government price fixing policies
          and taxation practices have contributed to a disorganized petroleum sector and
          caused a brief interruption o f supplies at the end o f 2008. The future o f the sector
          i s a new but important theme o f the policy dialogue between the government and
          the Bank. ERSG I11 reforms in this sector will be focused solely o n the pricing
          issue. The government has put in place a quasi-automatic mechanism for
      '
          adjusting local prices to changes in the international market. To mitigate the
          impact o f these adjustments on local prices, the government i s ready to stabilize
          customs duties, taxes, and operational margins.

80.    At the beginning o f the 2000s, the execution phase o f the chain o f expenditure
used to follow a dual system. As redundant controls and cumbersome spending
procedures slowed down budget execution, government officials tended to use
exceptional procedures that bypassed controls, thus creating material risks o f inefficient
spending and misappropriation o f public funds. In addition, recurrent tensions over cash
availability increased the scope for discretionary intervention regarding actual payment o f
government commitments and created perverse incentives.

8 1.   Several important measures have been taken under ERSG Iand I1to improve the
execution phase o f the budget cycle.

   a. The new Organic Budget L a w clarified and streamlined the steps involved in
       regular expenditure processes. Duplicative internal controls have been eliminated
       (the Central Bank will no longer duplicate controls carried out by the Accounting
       Department), and the scope and purpose o f other internal controls (by financial
       controllers and accountants) have been clarified.



                                            45
   b. The rules regarding exceptional commitment procedures have been tightened and
      their use restricted to a limited number o f well-defined situations (ministerial
       ordinance on expenditures without prior authorization for payment (ddpenses sans
       ordonnancement pre'alable)). Irregular off-budget accounts, which received
       earmarked income from taxes and service fees and were managed at the discretion
       o f the Ministry o f Finance and the Presidency, have been closed, with the.
       exception o f one account. Corresponding balances and financial flows have been
       integrated into the 2009 budget. This i s an important break with past practices,
       which needs to be completed by the closure o f the last remaining off-budget
       account.

   c. Cash management also improved. The number o f government bank accounts was
      reduced from 300 at the end o f 2006 to 10 at the end o f 2008. The five line
      ministries with the biggest budgets (defense, education, health, public works, and
      interior) prepared rolling quarterly cash flow plans from the second quarter o f
      2008. This, combined with improved data from tax, customs, and debt
      directorates, enabled the Ministry o f Finance and the Central Bank to prepare cash
      flow projections that are updated daily and used as a basis for payment decisions
      by weekly treasury committee meetings chaired by the Director General for
      Accounting, Budget, and Treasury.

                            11.   Private Sector Development
86.      Background. Burundi's private sector includes a large number o f small
enterprises most o f which operate in the informal sector. The formal private sector
includes about 3,000 registered enterprises, employs some 3 7,000 workers and produces
mainly for the local market. Construction, agricultural processing, brewing, energy, and
communications are the main activities o f a very small industrial sector. The service
sector i s more important and accounts for close to 37 percent o f GDP, but it i s dominated
by a growing public sector which accounts for 67 percent o f the tertiary sector. Private
sector growth i s hampered by the small size o f the domestic market, the landlocked
position o f the country, other structural constraints and an uninviting business
environment. The privatization strategy o f the government i s hampered by the poor
financial performance o f many public enterprises and by the lack o f capacity o f the
institutions responsible for planning and monitoring the program.

87.     A 2007 study found that the government had shares in 48 entities (40 enterprises
and 8 financial institutions). Several public enterprises are faced with a difficult financial
situation. They are burdened by the governance problems typically encountered in many
countries, including political interference from the State. Their indebtedness increases
faster than their turnover, and debt obligations often exceed cash flow. By the end o f
2006, the overall debt o f the 21 largest public enterprises amounted to FBu 128 billion
(about 14 percent o f GDP), including debt to suppliers, the public sector, foreign lenders
and local banks. The Service Charge` des Entreprises Publiques (SCEP), a special entity
created in the 1990s to oversee public enterprises, does not have the capacity and the
resources necessary to bring about substantial improvements in the performance o f the
sector and to plan and monitor the privatization program envisaged by the government.

88.     An effective private sector development strategy will require actions o n many
fronts, including investments in infrastructure (transport, energy and communications),
that will be supported by investment projects. The reforms being targeted within the
ERSG I11 framework are: (i)     improving the business environment; (ii)   reforming and
privatizing the coffee sector; and (iii)
                                       improving the supply o f petroleum products. This
section will focus o n the measures needed to improve the business environment. The
following sections will discuss the measures envisaged for restructuring the coffee JiZiBre
and reforming the petroleum sector.

B. Restructuring the Coffee Sector
98.                   --
        Background Performance and previous reforms o f the coffee sector. Coffee
accounts for more than 60 percent o f Burundi's export earnings and i s the main source o f
cash income for close to 600,000 rural families that are among the poorest in the country.
The Burundian coffee sector i s increasingly inefficient and suffers from l o w and
declining productivity. This i s due to the combined impact o f multiple causes: aging
plantations, high intermediary costs, and an inadequate incentive framework. Yields are
approximately half those in neighboring countries (250/250 grams per tree, versus
450/500 grams in Kenya). Production follows a cyclical pattern and declines significantly
from year to year. From 42,000 tons in the early 2000s, production dropped to 6,500 tons
in 2007-08, recovered in 2008-09 to reach about 25,400 tons, but i s expected to decline
again to 12,000 tons or less in 2009-10. These large variations in coffee output are one o f
the factors that explain recent fluctuations in the country's GDP growth rates.

99.     Reforming the coffee sector i s of high priority. The country's ecological
conditions are favorable to the production o f high quality coffee that could sell at
premium prices on specialty markets. However, the marketing o f Burundi's coffee i s
flawed. Despite a market structure that allows direct contracting with international coffee
buyers, most o f the production i s sold on the commodity market at relatively l o w prices
due to an ill-adapted processing strategy that does not foster specialty coffee. As a result,
fully-washed coffee, which commands premium prices, accounts for only 60 percent o f
the production versus more than 75 percent when the washing stations were built in the
nineties. Declining and volatile production also affects buyers' perceptions on the
reliability o f Burundi as a supplier. Inefficient intermediation under the present
ownership structure and marketing rules further increases costs and reduces returns to
farmers, which are among the lowest in the sub-region.

100. Since 1992, a series o f reforms have been initiated by the government in order to
improve the structure and the performance o f the coffee sector and give Burundian coffee
a higher profile on international and niche markets. However, l i t t l e practical change has
been accomplished so far. The government s t i l l owns most o f the sector's assets (133
washing stations and two large dry mill factories), which are operated by local
entrepreneurs (SOGESTAL and SODECO) under a lease agreement with the
government, which was only recently amended (see below).

101. Reforms resumed in 2005, after a long hiatus related to the civil war. A
Presidential Decree liberalized price setting and marketing activities, reduced taxes on
orchards, and removed state guarantees for financing the sector. Key studies were
undertaken and the government created a Coffee Reform Committee in March 2006 and

adopted an action plan in November 2006, which included the divestiture o f the washing
stations. However, no progress was made in implementing the action plan. A major
setback was the decision, in 2007, to reestablish a system o f guaranteed price and to
contract the marketing o f fully washed coffee to a single foreign buyer. That decision was
rescinded in 2008 and the new regulation - a prior action o f ERSG I1 - i s viewed as a
step forward in liberalizing the sector.

 106. Background. As a landlocked country located at the end o f the supply line from

Principle 1: Reinforce country ownership
The Government of Burundi i s strongly committed to the reforms supported under the operation. The grant supports the
government's PRSP, which was developed in a participatory manner involving consultation of a wide range of stakeholders
and including 17 provinces and sectors. The process was led by the Permanent Secretariat for Monitoring Economic and
Social Reforms, with the support of international partners and local non-governmental organization (NGOs), quantitative
and qualitative surveys were undertaken in all the provinces and more than 145 grassroots civil society groups were
consulted. The Bank has actively supported the government in this process.

Principle 2: Agree up front with the government and other financial partners on a coordinated accountability
framework
The government and the budget support donors have agreed on a common framework "Cadre de Partenariat" and the
government i s developing a PFM action plan based on the PEMFAR (2008) around which donors can consolidate their
support.

Principle 3: Customize the accountability framework and modalities of Bank support to country circumstances
The ERSG series i s fully aligned with the PRSP and has been developed through extensive consultations with the
government, the private sector, and development partners. The ERSG series should be seen as the overall umbrella for Bank
support and policy dialogue, which i s complemented by specific projects providing focused investment assistance. Budget
support i s seen as necessary instrument to address the budgetary needs of Burundi while strengthening the government's
systems. The series i s designed to give the necessary space to develop critical, but sensitive, reforms in coffee and public
enterprise reform as well as reviewing progress made in the petroleum sector, where fiscal and pricing reforms have been
introduced simultaneously to sustain the domestic government revenues. T h i s approach i s fully consistent with the
government objectives, and in some areas broadens the setting of the policy dialogue to multiple stakeholders, beyond
government policy declarations.

Principle 4: Choose only actions critical for achieving results as conditions for disbursement
The ERSG series focuses on a few selected prior actions and triggers that are based on the PRSP. The prior actions and
indicative triggers were chosen based on extensive discussions with the authorities and the private sector to identify the
actions that would be critical steps for implementing the government's program within a realistic timeframe. They would
provide meaningful outcomes and help loosen major constraints or facilitate further reforms. A higher number of measures
are included in the policy matrix (annex 9 ) as measures of progress to monitor the overall progress of the reform program.

Principle 5: Conduct transparent progress reviews conducive to predictable and performance-based financial
support
A full and close supervision of the program progress review will take place in line with the agreed Partnership Framework
between the Government of Burundi and the budget support donors.

Annex 5: Burundi ERSG I11 Impact o f Food and Petroleum Prices and Bank's Response

Background: Burundi i s one o f the hardest hit countries in Africa by recent food and fuel price increases. The IMF
listed Burundi among the 18 countries in the world most vulnerable to balance o f payments (BOP) shocks arising
bom food and o i l price increases. Burundi's food crop sector has performed very poorly over many years for a
lumber o f reasons, including low productivity, drought, political instability, and the like. I t i s a net importer o f food
2nd o i l products, and its position as a landlocked country raises significantly import costs. In fact, the food price
increase in Burundi mainly reflected higher petroleum and transport costs. Before the recent decrease in food and
fuel prices, food and fuel imports represented an estimated 43 percent o f total imports. Because o f rising food prices,
inflation rose sharply starting in mid-2007, reaching 27.2 percent year-on-year in April 2008. From December 2007
to April 2008, prices o f basic staples rose on average by 23 percent. The food and fuel prices have subsided in the
past several months, easing the crisis.
Government's response and recent developments: Faced with the combined impacts o f the poor rainy season, the
D i l price shock, and the food crisis, the Burundian authorities have taken a number o f measures in three broad
:ategories:
           Alleviating the fiscal burden. The government reinstated a measure suspending, from February to
           December 2008, duties and domestic transaction taxes on 13 basic food products, including beans, cassava,
           potatoes, banana, maize, rice, and milk. The majority o f these products are mostly consumed by the poor.
           Moreover, since April 2008 tariffs on diesel imports were suspended. At the same time, tariffs on gasoline
           imports were raised from 12 percent to 16 percent to curb consumption and to preserve overall tax
           collection levels. The above measure abolishing temporarily levies on basic products was reiterated in the
           context o f the 2009 budget. However, the revenue target in the 2009 budget assumes a phase out of the
           temporary suspension o f levies on oil products, while considering the social impact on the poor. The
           government recently requested a technical assistance from the IMF's Fiscal Affairs Department on the use
           o f fiscal policies to better protect the poor, within the context o f an automatic price adjustment mechanism,
           which i s being fmalized with IDA'Ssupport. A Fund's TA mission i s currently in Burundi.
         Strengthening targeted programs. The authorities have also sought to mitigate the impact o f higher food
         and o i l prices on the poor by enhancing social safety nets (e.g., food security programs and school feeding
         programs). Moreover, in line with their PRSP, the authorities are planning in their 2009 budget to allocate
         additional resources to targeted programs. Consequently, the revised 2008 budget includes a budgetary line
         o f about US$3 million to finance the expansion o f the school feeding and nutrition programs. The 2009
         budget increases (doubles) the credits allocated to primary school feeding programs to about US$6 million.
         I t also doubled by a similar amount the allowance for returnees, displaced persons and victims.
         Designing supply side measures to increase agricultural production. The supply side constraints have to be
         addressed by increasing investments in agriculture, especially in food crops. An improvement in
         agricultural production and productivity i s essential and can be addressed by higher investments in
         agricultural research, extension, infrastructure, market access and overall trade facilitation, with the aim of
         making Burundi a net exporter over the medium term. The government highlighted a number o f measures
         for agriculture supply to be financed under the revised 2008 budget. These include, inter alia: (i)   creating a
         strategic stock o f agricultural fertilizers, (ii) rehabilitating the irrigation system in the Imbo plain, (iii)
         increasing food output, and (iv) contributing to rural agricultural microcredits. Furthermore, the 2009
         budget continues to support several o f the above activities aimed at improving food supply.
Donor's support: While AfDB, Netherlands, and Norway provided additional financing to cover the increased cost
o f fuel and food to the budget (secondary boarding schools, university, prisons, and security forces), the Bank
supported Burundi with a U S $ l O million grant from the Food Price Crisis Response TF facility in July 2008. The
grant supported the revised 2008 budget that included a number o f remedial measures to the crisis such as school
feedings and provision o f inputs to improve food production (together with WFP and FAO). In the spring o f 2008, a
US$15 million additional financing was approved for the on-going PRASAB project that seeks to increase crop and
livestock production and productivity, diversify farm income, support farmers' organizations, and promote
sustainable land use. This additional financing was provided to help Burundi scale up activities. Finally, under the
CAS (2009-12), a follow-on agricultural project i s programmed to promote high-value export crops as well as to
further improve production o f food crops.

I.     INTRODUCTION

 1.      To support its program o f economic reforms for the 2009-2010 period, the Government o f
Burundi i s requesting support from IDA. This program aims at macroeconomic adjustment and
stabilization as well as the implementation o f structural reforms for poverty reduction, by consolidating
the progress already made under the ERSG I1 in the area o f public finance management and promotion
o f a friendly business environment. The present Policy Letter summarizes the social context and the
recent economic developments. I t describes the policies that the government commits to pursue in the
following areas: (i) national economic program within the context o f the second Poverty Reduction
                     the
Strategy Paper (PRSP) (ii)      reforms aimed at improving public finance management; and ( i )         ii
implementation o f policies for private sector development, and especially development o f the coffee
and petroleum sectors.

2.      The government's program as reflected in the PRSP aims at assuring the transition from a post-
conflict economy to a situation o f economic growth recovery and development. Therefore, the PRSP
has as the objectives o f reinforcing political stability, consolidating peace, and reducing poverty
through an accelerated economic growth. The main strategic orientations o f the PRSP are the
following: (i)  improving governance and security; (ii)   promoting a lasting and equitable economic
growth; (iii)developing human resources; and (iv) fighting against HIV/AIDS. All these four domains
have registered tangible results. The first implementation Progress Report o f the PRSP was adopted by
the government and the Parliament after it had been approved by all the technical and financial
partners, including the World Bank. The second Progress Report i s about to be finalized. Sectoral
participatory consultations for the second progress Report are already finalized, while consultations at
the community level are being completed. The final progress report will be submitted to the authorities
for adoption at the end o f September 2009. With regard to the Second PRSP, a note o f orientation i s
expected to be elaborated and discussed with various partners before the end o f 2009 or at the
beginning o f the 20 10 at the latest.

3.      The Government o f Burundi has achieved significant progress in implementing i t s economic
reform program that was begun during the period o f transition; this has helped register encouraging
results on macroeconomic and financial stability. The government i s committed to consolidate these
results in order to accelerate economic growth and ensure irreversibility o f the policies already
implemented. In recognition o f the government determination to carry out deepened reforms, IDA and
IMF Boards o f Directors approved in January 2009 the accession o f Burundi to the Completion Point
for debt relief under the "enhanced HIPC' Initiative".

4.      Despite these encouraging results, the government remains aware o f the various challenges that
Burundi economy has to face. The government also remains confident that there are important
opportunities that Burundi should seize in order to improve i t s economic environment, modernize i t s
productive infrastructure and enhance i t s access to international market. Presently, the sources o f
economic growth are s t i l l dominated by agricultural sector, especially the traditional exportation
channels such as Coffee, Cotton, and Tea. The objective o f the authorities in implementing reforms in
agriculture i s to increase productivity in these sectors with a view to allow: (i) more contribution to
economic growth, and (ii)   more income to agricultural producers, notably in the rural area.

reform program. For this purpose, Burundi will need a reference framework for harmonised policy
with donors. In this respect, the government wishes to get increased coordination with IDA and all
other financial partners through Burundi's PRSP process, o f which the first Implementation Progress
Report was presented to IDA and IMF Boards o f Directors in January 2009, which adopted at the same
time the Completion Point under the "enhanced HIPC' initiative".

6.       This Letter o f Development Policy reflects the government's economic reform program
extending over the period o f 2009-2010. It also presents the performance indicators which will
facilitate evaluation o f the progress and quality o f i t s implementation. Aware o f the difficulties
inherent to economic reforms and taking advantage o f past experience, the Burundian authorities will
continue to judicially involve all stakeholders in order to build consensus and enhance ownership of
any reform which has sensitive socio-economic implications with a view to maximise success o f the
program.

11.     RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS

7       Burundi's economic performance has generally improved over the past five years, even though
the annual GDP Growth average has remained weak (around 3.8%) over the 2004-2008 period, due to
cyclical fluctuations in agricultural production and frequent unfavorable weather conditions. During
that period, the government pursued i t s efforts to improve the quality o f public expenditure by
increasing the budget allocations to priority sectors o f education, health and socio-economic
infrastructures. However, in order to embark the economy on strong and lasting growth, and
significantly reduce poverty, the government i s determined to attack the multiple challenges identified.
These include the necessity to: (i)   improve the business climate; (ii) increase investment in human
                         i i reduce bottlenecks and upgrade infrastructure in support o f productive
capital development; ( i )
sectors; and (iv) increase agricultural production and productivity.

8,      Recent statistics (2004-2008) indicate that the productive structure o f Burundi economy
remains dominated by agriculture (almost 45% o f the GDP), followed by services (nearly 38% of the
GDP) and industry (17% o f the GDP). The role o f agriculture in remains prominent not only for its
high contribution to GDP growth and exports receipts (more than 70% on average over the last three
years, 2006-2008), but also in terms o f the number o f jobs that its generates. However, it i s important
to note that, over the past years, the agricultural sector's contribution to GDP has trended downward
because o f weak productivity, the effects o f frequent unfavorable weather conditions, and some
episodes o f prolonged conflicts. O n the other hand, the contribution o f the services sector has
continued to increase during that period. Also, following a recovery o f activity in housing and public
infrastructure reconstruction, the contribution o f the manufacturing sector to GDP has slightly
increased.

9.       The recent progress in security restoration and peace consolidation process, which has reached
a decisive step with the recent transformation o f the last armed movement into a political party,
foretells o f good perspectives for national reconstruction and economic activity recovery. Thus, it i s
easy to notice that since the year 2001, Burundi has progressively made up for i t s negative economic
growth (-1.8% in average) from the decade 1993-2003 o f the socio-political conflict, and has
positioned itself at a positive growth rate o f 4.5% in 2008. The GDP growth for 2009 was initially
estimated at 4.8% but has, unfortunately been revised downward at 3.2% since then following the
negative impact o f the global financial crisis and recession. Even though the growth level o f the last
years proves satisfactory, it remains largely dependent on the high volatility o f agricultural production,
                                                   103
as witnessed by the strong depression o f the year 2005 (with a growth rate o f 0.9%) due to a long
drought and to the weak growth (3.6%) in 2007 also linked to a shortage o f agricultural crops, despite
increased capital expenditures mainly supported by the donors.

10.     Burundi had succeeded in maintaining inflation levels to a single digit until the mid-2007.
However, following the dizzy rise o f the world prices for o i l and food commodities, inflation increased
again since the last months and reached 14.7% at the end o f 2007, and 24.4% at the end of 2008,
exceeding by far the authorities' revised objectives (fixed at around 14%). The government i s
committed to pursue i t s objective o f progressively bringing back the inflation to a single digit in the
medium-term, by applying prudent monetary and budgetary policies, especially by constraining the
resort to bank credits to finance public expenditures. Consumer price indexes are calculated from a
basket o f consumer goods based on prices exercised on the central market o f Bujumbura. This basis of
calculation o f inflation has not changed since 1991. However, a new household expenditure survey
envisaged will help update the basket.

11. The authorities pursued their effort o f budgetary discipline and, therefore, the fiscal outturn
remains satisfactory in 2007 and 2008. The fiscal primary deficit has been lower than expected,
because domestic revenue collection has remained strong, whereas the non-priority expenditures
decreased for about 2.6% o f GDP in 2007 and 1.2% in 2008. In compliance with their PRSP objectives
and the MDGs, the authorities have had the merit o f constantly increasing poverty-reducing spending,
from 6.3% of GDP in 2005, to about 9.3% in 2006, 11.2% in 2007 and 12.1% in 2008.

 12.   The main indicators o f the 2009 budget promulgated in December 2008 are compliant with
macroeconomic objectives agreed upon with the IMF within the PRGF supported program. The 2009
budget objectives are to fbrther increase spending allocated to poverty reduction to about 14.8% o f the
GDP, and to accommodate more space to the most priority socioeconomic expenditures, in general.
These expenses should be financed in part by resources resulting from debt cancellation received from
the HIPC completion point and the "MDRI". The revised 2009 budget remains in line with the PRGF
supported program objectives despite the negative effects on Burundi economy o f the international
financial crisis and the world economic recession.

13.     After a weakening o f the external position in 2007 following the deterioration o f terms o f trade
by 23.4%, a relative improvement has been observed in 2008 with a recuperation on terms o f trade to
3.5% in conjunction with an improvement o f the value o f coffee exports (+9%) due mainly to an
increase o f international market prices for this product. The government has continued to meet i t s
official foreign reserves objectives, which increased from 3.6 months o f import cover in 2006 to 3.9
months in 2007 and 2008. The Burundi Franc remained relatively stable and the real effective
exchange rate (REER) depreciated by 5.7% in 2007 while it has appreciated by 16% in 2008 due to the
surge in food and oil prices.

14.      In 2009, the external position i s expected to be negatively affected by the decrease o f
international market prices o f commodities owing to the world financial crisis and recession. Burundi
external position i s also expected to be affected by the deep fall o f coffee production consecutive to the
shrinkage o f the coffee production cycle for which it i s observed an alternation o f good and bad
harvest each year, instead o f the 3 to 5 years before, in conjunction with the ageing o f coffee tree
plantations. On the other hand, the projected reduction o f food and o i l prices on the world market will
lower the cost o f Burundi's imports. In total, however, the external current account deficit (including
official transfers) should narrow slightly, settling at 9.5% o f GDP, according to the IMF estimates. The
gross official foreign reserves should be limited at about 5.5 months o f import cover.
                                                    104
15.     In order to cope with the surge in world prices o f food commodities that hit the country toughly
over the last years, the government implemented number o f measures in order to help the most
vulnerable layers o f the population resist the food crisis, while encouraging local production.
Temporary and targeted duties reductions have been applied o n imported food and o i l products often
consumed by the poor. Moreover, the government has reinforced social safety nets, including food
security programs, school canteens, transfers to farmers, and agricultural micro- credits in rural area, as
well as food aid to refugees and war displaced. T o increase agricultural production, the government
proceeded finally to providing seeds and manure to farming peasants, launching rehabilitation o f
irrigation system o f the Imbo plain, and rehabilitation o f basic infrastructures. The government
expresses i t s gratefulness to all donors who financially supported these initiatives, especially the World
Bank, the Netherlands, Norway, the European Union, the African Development Bank, and many
others. The government has translated these initiatives into programs, and wish to keep them running
over the future period o f i t s reform program given the perseverance o f the crisis.

16.     The determination to reach its development objectives as outlined in the PRSP led the
government to initiating a vast program o f reforms articulated o n the following pillars: reinforcing
macroeconomic stabilization, deepening structural reforms aimed at improving public finance
management (PFM) system, monetary and exchange rate policies, as well as efficiency in productive

17.     It i s important to notice that the ERSG I1 has effectively facilitated the acceleration o f the
Government's progress in implementing i t s reform program which started with the support of previous
IDA operations and other cooperating partners. Consequently, Burundi continued to achieve
encouraging economic growth results in spite o f the internal difficult post-conflict situation, bad
weather conditions, and the unfavorable world economic and financial situations. Following i t s reform
effort, Burundi reached the completion point for debt relief under the "enhanced HIPC' Initiative" in
January 2009. The main focus o f the ERSG I1 included reforms to improve transparency in public
finance management, the business climate, and efficiency in productive sectors, especially through
reforms in coffee sector.

 18.    As regards implementation o f public finance management reforms, substantial results have
been recorded. The Parliament has adopted a new Organic L a w for Public Finances which takes into
account modern principles o f management and transparency, and specifies concepts, principles and
responsibilities applicable to agents o f the Treasury. Moreover, the L a w establishing the Value-Added
Tax (VAT) has been promulgated o n February 17, 2009 to replace the Transactions Tax system. Also,
the elimination o f all exemptions o n indirect fiscal taxation has been initiated and incorporated in the
new "Tax Code" which i s under preparation. The government has also finalized the census o f the
public sector c i v i l servants, as well as police and army. Personal magnetic identification cards have

and facilitate identification o f future reforms for improved public sector management.

more generally implementation o f i t s global plan o f privatization o f state owned enterprises. A

amendments repealing the former management conventions between the government and managers of
the Coffee Washing Stations (SOGESTALS) were signed by both parties involved on a consensus
basis.

3.2.   Challenges

24.       Political and economic challenges to be tackled are s t i l l daunting in order for Burundi to meet
i t s objectives o f sustainable and equitable growth.

25.     The main political challenge remains the need to ensure political stability through peace and
security consolidation. Actions already conducted by Burundi concerning political dialogue and
consultations, with the support o f the sub-region and the international community, have already
produced convincing results and have showed the' capacity o f the country to get through the challenge
o f establishing a stable political system. This capacity can be illustrated with the following recent
important political actions carried out:

         during the period o f transition, the integration o f former armed movements into the defence
         and security forces, as well as the demobilization and socioeconomic reinsertion o f ex-
         combatants were carried out o n a consensual basis, following signed peace agreements;

         the government resulting from democratic elections at the end o f the transitional period
         continued political dialogue and consultations with all others protagonists, and as soon as it
         proved necessary, a government o f national union was set up on November 15,2007;

         in spite o f many disruptions in the implementation o f a definitive cease-fire with the
         remaining fighting movement, the FNL-Palipehutu, dialogue continued and has recently
         reached decisive results with the transformation o f this movement in a political party
         compliant with the country's law, the integration o f a part o f i t s former fighters in the national
         defence and security forces, and the demobilization and socioeconomic reinsertion o f the rest
         o f i t s ex-combatants into civil life;

         Following an assiduous process o f national dialogue and consultations associating political
         parties and the civil society, an Independent National Electoral Commission (CENI) in charge
         o f preparing, organizing and conducting the next political elections expected in mid-2010 was
         also recently created o n a consensual basis.




                                                    108
26.     Currently, all political forces in Burundi have given up the use o f weapons to embark on a
serene political debate thanks to mutual understanding o f the different protagonists on the need to
follow the way o f dialogue and in favor o f a political consensus building process. The Government i s
convinced that security recovered throughout the country will help focus national effort on the
implementation o f development programs in all sectors. This will make it possible to significantly
reduce poverty and hence to set up lasting peace and political stability.

27.        The major economic and social challenges include the need to put on track the economy with a
diversified, high and sustainable growth, able to significantly reduce poverty and accelerate
meaningful progress towards reaching the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs). With a level o f
widespread poverty exceeding 65 percent on average, together with the country's vulnerability to
undernourishment, malnutrition and epidemic illnesses, as well as the high prevalence o f the
HIV/AIDS, the Government i s aware that Burundi i s classified among the least developed countries of
the world. The Government i s determined to reverse these unfavourable tendencies and will articulate
i t s strategy on the following axis:

      0   Reinforcing fiscal sustainability in order to avoid debt build-up and prevent a return o f the
          country into debt distress;

      0   Pursuing relentlessly the effort o f macroeconomic stabilization through prudent budgetary and
          monetary policies, in order to bring back the inflation rate in the l o w single digits ranges. The
          government i s confident that it will succeed in reducing progressively inflation in the medium-
          term. Indeed, the government had succeeded in the past in maintaining l o w inflation rates until
          very recently, when the international market prices o f o i l and food commodities escalated.

      0   Investing in infrastructure in support o f productive sectors, diversifying the sources o f
          production, and enhancing institutional capacities and good governance. The Government i s
          conscious o f the need to deepen implementation structural reforms necessary for: promoting a
          dynamic and efficient public administration; reinforcing principles o f the "Rule o f the Law",
          promoting respect o f property rights, promoting a friendly business climate; reinforcing good
          governance, including the introduction o f standards and ethic rules concerning good
          governance, and accountability at all levels o f the national public life.

      0   Pursuing resolutely and thoughtfully the process o f regional integration. Burundi can benefit
          from larger market opportunities thanks to i t s belonging to the regional and sub-regional
          economic communities such as the East African Community (EAC). The government i s
          however conscious o f the need to develop a global strategy aimed at (i)     bringing in line i t s
          administrative institutions with those o f other country members, and (ii)  negotiating the most
          suitable terms for a mutually profitable adherence to these regional economic spaces. To this
          effect, technical and financial support from cooperating partners s t i l l remains urgent and
          indispensable.

           Annex 7: Burundi ERSG I11 Prospects for Achieving the MDGs by 2015

Goal 1: Eradicate extreme poverty and hunger
Unlikely. The internationally agreed baseline year of 1990 for the MDGs coincides with the beginning of a decade
of political turmoil and civil war during which GDP fell by almost 20 percent. The national poverty headcount
increased from 36 in 1990 to 81 percent in 1998. However, according to a 2006 household survey, about 67 percent
of the population s t i l l lives below the national poverty line (the MDG target i s 18 percent).
Meeting the hunger target i s unlikely. Malnutrition rates were very high even before the crisis, and in 2000 Burundi
had the highest under-five malnutrition rates in Afiica with 57 percent. However, according to national nutrition
surveys, malnutrition rates decreased to 53 percent in 2005 and 46 percent in 2007 (the MDG target i s 23 percent).
Goal 2: Achieve universal primary education
Likely. Burundi will potentially meet the target for ensuring that all boys and girls complete a full course of primary
schooling. Since free universal primary education was introduced in September 2005, gross enrollment reached
close to 100 percent. But completion rates remain low at only 37 percent.
Goal 3: Promote gender equality and empower women
Likely. The target on gender equality in primary education will very likely be met with the ratio of girls to boys
estimated at 0.8 in 2000, 0.9 in 2005, and 0.95 in 2008. The target on women participation in parliament has been
met. However, women's lack of control over resources will remain a key challenge.
Goal 4: Reduce child mortality
Highly unlikely. Child and infant mortality rates remain high at 190 and 114 (per 1,000 live births) but show
improvements from their 2000 peak rates of 291 and 156, respectively. Communicable diseases, such as malaria,
diarrhea, respiratory infections, and the compounding effect of malnutrition are the major causes of mortality and
morbidity especially among children.
Goal 5: Improve maternal health
Highly unlikely. Maternal mortality remains very high at 1,000 (per 100,000 live births). At the same time, the
proportion of births attended to by skilled health personnel i s only 31 percent, well below the MDG goal of 100
percent. The key challenges to enhance utilization of health services are: (i) cost to households; and (ii)
                                                                                                          lack of
trained people.
Goal 6: Combat HIV/AIDS, malaria and other diseases
Likely. Burundi will potentially meet the HIV/AIDS target. Overall HIV prevalence of 2.9 percent i s low. However,
Burundi's rate of 12 percent HIV affections among pregnant women i s quite high compared with neighboring
countries (Rwanda 9.8 percent, Uganda 8.5 percent, and Tanzania 7.5 percent). ,
Goal 7: Ensure environmental sustainability
Unlikely. About 71 percent of the population has access to improved water resources. Among these, 75 percent live
in urban areas and only 45 percent in rural areas.
