                                       1. INTRODUCTION

 1.1     This program document proposesa SecondRe-engagementand ReformSupport Program
Grant (RRSP 11) to the Republic of Liberia in the amount of SDR 2.7 million (US$4 million
equivalent). This one-tranche Development Policy Operation supports the Government's efforts
to pursuethe implementation of reforms to sustain pro-poor growth that were initiated under the
first RRSP approved by the Board in December 2007. The grant i s an integral part of IDA'S
Country Assistance Strategy for the period FY09-12 presentedto the Board inApril 2009 and is
closely coordinated with assistance provided to support the program of Government's reforms
through the ongoing Economic Governance and Institutional Reform Project (US$ll million),
the Enhanced HIPC Initiative, the AfDB budget support operation and the IMF PRGF. Since
Liberia i s at high risk of debt distress, IDA resources are provided on grant terms, per the
stipulations of the grant allocation system agreedwith IDA donors for the IDA 15 period. Liberia
i s also eligible for annual exceptional post-conflict allocations from IDA.

1.2         The grant would assist the Republic of Liberia at a critical stage of its transition from
post conflict recovery to long-term sustained growth and development. This transition will be
guided by the vision and major strategies articulated in the government's Poverty Reduction
Strategy being implemented from April 1, 2008 to June 30, 2011. Specifically, the RRSP I1
focuses on continuing and deepening government-owned policy reforms initiated by the first
RRSP to rebuild core state functions and institutions and support private sector development by:
(i)improving the efficiency of budget preparation and execution and increasing the
professionalism and improving the human resource management o f the civil service; and (ii)
improving the investment climate through the reform of the revenue and private investment
regimes.

1.3         Liberia has made substantial progress on many fronts since the end of the civil war in
2003.     Free and fair elections in 2005 led to the installation of a new government in January
2006, more than 81,000 ex-combatantshave beendemobilized and reintegrated, and most of the
displaced peoples have returned and are being reintegrated into society. Efforts are also being
made to improve governance, includingthe empowerment of local government. The economy is
recovering rapidly, infrastructure is being rebuilt and the delivery of basic services inhealth and
education is improving. Notwithstanding the progress, the current socio-economic environment,
which is characterized by widespread poverty, significant urban-rural divide and high
unemployment and underemployment, presents a very fragile country with non-trivial risks to
maintaining the peace and security that is itself so critical to creating the space for reforms
necessaryto sustain the transition to long-term development.


            2. LIBERIA'S DEVELOPMENT CHALLENGES AND PROSPECTS

                      A.      POLITICALAND SOCIOECONOMIC BACKGROUND

2.1        Poor governance and nearly a decade and ahalf of conflict have left Liberia's economy,
institutions, and human capacity completely devastated. Approximately 270,000 people were


                                                - 4 -

killed and about a third o f the total population was displaced by the fighting. The conflict left
the once wealthy country in economic ruin, with its endowment in roads, railroads, electricity
generation and transmission, potable water and sewage utterly destroyed. The origins o f the
conflict lay in the exclusion and marginalization o f much o f its population, which have
characterized Liberia for most o f its existence as a country. Although Liberia's economy posted
strong sustained growth inthe 1960sbased on the exploitation o f its rich natural resources which
include iron ore, rubber, timber, and diamonds, the benefits o f growth were unevenly shared and
many have remained in poverty. Ifthe exclusion and marginalization o f the indigenous people
sowed the seed o f the conflict, it was watered by the economic collapse that started in the early
1970s with the first oil shock. By the second oil shock in the early 1980s the economy was in
free fall. The social discontent, which had been brewing, largely as a result o f the inequitable
distribution o f the benefits from growth, erupted into a brutal civil war in 1989. A 1995 peace
agreement led to the election o f Charles Taylor as president in 1997, but did not last long as
fighting broke out again in 1999.

2.2        The 2003 Accra Comprehensive Peace Accords and deployment o f a 15,000 strong UN
peacekeeping force have provided much-needed space to lay a solid foundation for recovery,
including reestablishing a public financial management system, rebuilding key public sector
institutions, delivering essential public services, and laying the foundations o f a local
government system. World Bank engagement since 2003 has focused on providing emergency
stabilization support to begin the process o f rebuilding state institutions and restarting public
service delivery. During this period, the World Bank's programs focused on basic economic
governance reforms, community-driven development for service delivery, and emergency
infrastructure rehabilitation.

2.3        The election o f a new democratic government on January 16, 2006 following a period
o f ineffective rule by a National Transitional Government (NTGL) has brought renewed hope
and new dynamism to the recovery efforts in Liberia. The country i s now at an inflection point,
moving from the transitional post-conflict recovery phase to laying the foundations for long-term
development. The Government's main focus i s on sustaining economic growth-getting             major
transport corridors functioning to open up trade and commerce, revitalizing agriculture, and
getting energy infrastructure up and running. Employment generation i s also important,
particularly inthe short term, to ensure that citizens experience the tangible benefits o f peace and
reform. On the policy side, the government i s focusing on consolidating economic governance
reforms, moving beyond the transitional Governance and Economic Management Assisted
Program (GEMAP) to a fully-owned and functioning system o f public financial management.

2.4        Notwithstanding recent substantial progress, the situation in Liberia remains fragile
with significant political and socioeconomic risks.        Current per-capita GDP i s estimated at
US$190,' and an estimated 63.8 percent o f the population (or about 1.7 million Liberians) live
below the national poverty line, with 47.9 percent o f the population (or 1.3 million) living in
extreme poverty.2 Poverty manifests itself inboth low income and poor access to social services,
resulting inpoor health and education status o fthe population. There is also a strong ruralhrban
divide in the incidence o f poverty with more than two-thirds o f the rural population being poor
compared with 55 percent for the urban population. Unemployment and under-employment are
highand many o fthe unemployedhave little or no marketable skills, not having hadthe benefits

concrete peace dividends remain very high, two years into President Johnson Sirleaf's tenure.
The challenges presented by an opposition-controlled Parliament has complicated passing of

                           B.       RECENTECONOMICDEVELOPMENTS

2.5        Since the peace accord in 2003, the economy has posted a strong recovery and the
authorities have made significant progress with macroeconomic stabilization and fiscal
management reforms.        The postwar recovery continued in 2008 and, despite a difficult
international environment,       economic   performance    remained broadly      satisfactory   and
macroeconomic stability was maintained. As a result, the IMF Board approved the completion
o f the first reviewunder the PRGF-supported arrangement inDecember 2008.

2.6        Economic Growth and Inflation. Although the quality of economic data is poor and
current GDP figures are widely regarded to be underestimated, trends indicate that the economy
has been performing strongly since the end of the war. After a sharp contraction of more than 30
percent in2003, real GDP expanded by 5.2 percent on average during 2004-2006 and further by
9.5 percent in 2007. Over the past four years growth has been spurred by agriculture (mainly
rubber), retail trade, communications, transport and the construction sub-sectors. Inflation has
remained stable inthe single digit range between 2004 and 2006, anchored on a relatively stable
exchange rate. However, there was an uptick in the rate o f inflation to 11.7 percent at end 2007
largely as a result o f increases in food and fuel prices.       The postwar economic recovery
continued in 2008 (with real GDP growth estimated at 7.1 percent) supported by continued
growth in agriculture and services, and in forestry, returning to the level o f real GDP towards
pre-war levels. However, Liberia was significantly affected by the increase in world food and
fuel prices in the first half o f the year. Year-on-year inflation peaked at almost 27 percent in
August, and is estimated at 17.5 percent for the year (see Table 2.1). The World Bank has
provided support to Liberia to help mitigate the impact o f higher food prices. This support,
delivered through the Emergency Food Crisis Response Program, provided US$10 million to
help finance: (i)the implementation o f social safety net measures to maintain access to food
among vulnerable households, and (ii)expedite a supply response o f agricultural production
through the implementation o f measures to raise the yield and consequently the production of
staples.

2.7        Trade and Balanceof Payments. Export growth recoveredstrongly in2005 and 2006
following the contraction in2004, mainly driven by the recovery in rubber exports. At the same
time, strong import demand driven by the reconstruction efforts as well as the large donor
presence on the ground resulted in a widening o f the trade balance from 25.2 percent o f GDP in
2004 to an estimated 40 percent of GDP in 2007. The current account deficit (includinggrants)
also widened from 19.4 percent o f GDP in 2004 to 31.7 percent in 2007. For 2008 the current
account deficit i s estimated to have widened further to 40.7 percent o f GDP as imports grew
faster than exports, fuelled by the reconstruction needs as well as reflecting the higher prices for
food and fuel and as remittances inflows decreased as a result o f the global slowdown.

2.8       Fiscal Balance. Revenue mobilization declined precipitously during the conflict with
the collapse in economic activity coupled with the prevalence o f rent-seeking behavior in the
customs and revenue administration. Total revenues and grants that amounted to US$232.7
million inFY87/88, a year before the war, fell to only US$56.6 million inFY02/03. Inthat year,
total revenues and grants were inadequate to cover total expenditures and the Government
accumulated more domestic arrears, primarily to civil servants and suppliers. However, tax
revenues began to improve in FY04/05 largely as a result o f a three-fold increase in income tax
which pushedtotal revenues to US$80.3 million. In FY05/06, there was some improvement in
revenue mobilization-largely           from corporations-and           an increase in grants, which increased
the total revenues to US$84.7 million while expenditures were kept incheck at US$73.2 million
down from US$77 million the year before. With the improvement inrevenues the budget shifted
from a deficit o f 2.6 percent o f GDP inFY02/03 to a surplus o f 2.1 percent o f GDP inFY05/06.

2.9       Since 2006, the authorities have pursueda balanced budget and have made progress in
fiscal management. Revenue collection increased by 73.3 percent in FY06/07. This increase
was due to strong economic activity and improvedtax administration. Expenditureshave largely
been kept in line with revenues although the Government struggled with managing the intra-
quarterly expenditures with spending significantly higher in the last quarter. This i s in part

2.10       Under the PRGF agreed with the IMF inMarch2008, the new Government committed
to a focus on rebuilding fiscal institutions to further enhance revenue collection and strengthen
public expenditure management. The Government has maintained fiscal prudence in FY07/08.
Preliminary fiscal data for the year show that revenue collection has remained strong (25.6
percent o f GDP) as a result o f continued improvementintax administration as well as continued
strong economic growth. The pace of expenditure has also picked up to better match revenue
flows. The fiscal balance for FY07/08 showed a surplus o f 1.2 percent o f GDP.

2.11      External and Domestic Debt. Liberia's external debt burden rose from US$750
million in 1979 to US$1.4 billion in 1985.         Inability to fully service the debt led to the
accumulation o f substantial arrears so that by June 2007, total external debt, including
capitalized interest and penalties, amounted to an estimated US$4.7 billion. O f this amount,
about US$1.6 billion was owed to multilateral creditors including the IMF, the World Bank and
the African Development Bank, approximately US$1.5 billion was owed to bilateral creditors,
mostly Paris Club Creditors, and an estimated US$1.6 billion was owed to commercial creditors.

2.12      Liberia cleared its arrears to the World Bank and AfDB in December 2007 and to the
IMF in March 2008. As a result, it reached the Decision Point under the Enhanced HIPC
initiative in March 2008. This will enable the government to benefit from interim relief under
the HIPC initiative. The government also reached an agreement with the Paris Club creditors on
April 17, 2008. The agreement, concluded under "Cologne terms", resulted in the immediate
cancellation o f US$254 million o f debt. In addition, given Liberia's limited capacity for debt
repayment, Paris Club creditors also agreed to no payments between March 1, 2008 and
December 31, 2010 provided that Liberia continues to implement satisfactorily an IMF-
supported program. In mid-April 2009 the government concluded the buy-back o f US$1.2
billion o f its commercial debt, following two years o f good-faith negotiations with its
commercial creditors. This effort was supportedby a technical assistance grant under the Bank's
Debt Reduction Facility (DRF) and support from Germany, Norway, United Kingdom and the
United States. While interimrelief will reduce the amount due to multilateral and other creditors,
Liberia's debt service has nonetheless increased significantly from its position o f only token
payments previously. The additional debt service obligations have considerably constrained
Liberia's limited fiscal space. Liberia therefore expects to finance debt service due during the
interim period mainly through grants. It i s expected that all preferred creditors will provide
positive flows to Liberia during the interim period. Furthermore, Liberia will receive additional
debt relief from IDA, the African Development Fund and the IMF on MDRIterms once Liberia
reaches the HIPC Completion Point, which i s currently targeted by the Government for early
2010. The Government has made good progress towards meeting the HIPC Completion Point
triggers (see Annex 8 for details o fprogress on each o fthe triggers).

2.13      In addition to its substantial external debt, it is estimated that the Government of
Liberia has domestic debt o f about 39 percent o f GDP in2008. The debt to the Central Bank o f
Liberia accounts for about 84 percent o f the total domestic debt, while the majority of other
claims constitute salary arrears to government employees and arrears on goods and services. The
Government has recently completed a comprehensive debt management strategy, which will
guide the debt resolutionprocess and the establishment of routine debt management practices.

2.14      Debt Management. The Debt Management Unit o f the Ministry o f Finance (MoF) is

2.16      As Liberia makes the transition from post-conflict recovery to laying the foundations
for long-term development, a stable macroeconomic environment will be critical to the
achievement of rapid, inclusive and sustainable growth. The Government's macroeconomic
framework would be anchored on sound fiscal, monetary, trade and exchange policies to foster
competition, maintain price stability and encourage private sector investment to provide a
foundation for rapidand sustained growth to increase employment andreduce poverty.

2.17      Growth and Inflation. The Government's macroeconomic framework projects GDP
growth to average 7.6 percent per annum for the 2009-2011 period down from the average o f
14.3 percent per annum projected prior to the global economic slowdown. Growth i s expected to
be driven largely by new investments in key sectors including mining, rubber (substantial
replanting) and infrastructure (rebuilding o f roads). The global economic slowdown has resulted
in lower primary commodity prices with adverse effects on planned investments in related
sectors. The lower price for logs may also result in delay in some of the start-up from the
concessions that the government has recently signed. Overall investment in 2009 i s therefore
expected to be lower than planned, contributing to slower growth. The growth projection for
2009 i s 4.9 percent, down from 7.1 percent in 2008 and significantly below the original
projection o f 12.7 percent. As primary commodity prices improve and the production from the
forestry and iron ore industry increases and export agriculture improves, GDP growth i s expected
to rise to about 7.5 percent in 2010 and further to 10.3 percent in 2011 subject to a recovery o f
the global economy. The authorities intend to reduce inflation from nearly 12 percent in2007 to
single digit in 2009-201 1. Inflation (end o f period), which was 9.4 percent in 2008 as a result o f
higher food and fuel prices, is expected to moderate to about 4 percent in2009 largely as a result
o f the sharp decrease in food and fuel prices. The inflationrate i s expected to moderate at around
5 percent in 2010 and 2011. A protracted global economic slowdown beyond 2010 with
attendant lower primary commodity prices, further fall-off in remittances and a possible
slowdown in donor support could have substantial adverse consequences for Liberia. A low case

scenario could see GDP growth falling below 4-5 percent for 2009 and 2010 with adverse fiscal
and balance o f payments consequences.

2.18       Fiscal Policy. Macroeconomic stability i s expected to be supported by prudent fiscal
policy. The Government i s committed to maintaining a balanced cash-based budget for most o f
the PRS period in order to maintain macroeconomic stability. On the revenue side, the
Government will continue its efforts to broaden the tax base through tax policy reforms and
modernization o f domestic tax and customs administration. The Government i s also making
efforts to maximize access to grants and to encourage donors (including through initiatives to
improve PFM) to increasingly channel grants through the government's budget. The Government
therefore expects to raise revenues as a share o f GDP from an estimated 25.6 percent inFY07/08
to 28.1 percent in FY08/09 and to 29.3 percent by FYlO/11. O n the expenditure side, the
Government intends to improve the efficiency, quality and transparency o f public expenditure
while keeping spending to the limits of revenues. The improvement on the expenditure side
should come from, inter alia, new PFM legislation, improvement in budget preparation,
executing, reporting and auditing as well as improvement in procurement planning and
implementation. The fiscal policy framework envisages a fiscal deficit o f 2.3 percent o f GDP (on
a cash basis after grants) in FY08/09 largely reflecting the lower revenues than expected as a
result o f the external shock to the economy as well as a result of the resumption o f debt service.
The fiscal balance is expected to revert to a small surplus o f 0.3 percent in FY09/10. A small
deficit o f less than 1 percent of GDP i s projected for FY10/11. A reduction in fiscal revenues
from the slower growth below 4-5 percent could force the Government to eliminate its already
anemic capital spending and cut the already meager social spending on health and education,
givenits inability to borrowto sustain such expenditures.

2.19       The FY08/09 budget, which was approved by the Parliament on August 5, 2008,
continues the vein o f a cash-balanced budget with zero borrowing. The "core budget" targets
spendingo f US$265.3 million (excluding amortization) to be financed by projected revenues o f
US$249.2 million leaving a gap to be finance by a drawdown of the Government's accumulated
balances at the central bank. It i s estimated that about 20 percent o f the revenues will come from
payments from the mining and forestry concessions as well as from the surplus o f state-owned
enterprises.    The budget also consists o f a "contingent budget" o f US$27.9 million, to be
financed from revenues, which the government considers less certain, including payments from
concessions which are currently being tendered.

2.22       Insummary, Liberia's macroeconomic framework, which forms the basis for its PRGF

3.17      The Government also intends to prepare a multi-year budgeting strategy that would
place the annual budgets in the context o f the medium-term fiscal framework for the PRSP. At
present, a one-year budget is prepared. As a component of the PRS, the government undertook a
three year costing exercise, yielding costing estimates by fiscal year and sector requiredto meet
the PRS objectives. It also estimated the anticipated budgetaryresources that will be devoted to
achieving the PRS objectives, and in doing so provided a first step towards a multi-year budget.
The costing exercise also illuminated the multi-year financing gap that will affect the
government's ability to rebuild the economic and social infrastructure needed to underpin
economic revitalization and combat poverty. Currently all appropriations lapse at the end o f the
fiscal year, except for certain sensitive appropriations such as the County Development Fund
(which is transferred into an escrow account if unallocated in the current fiscal year). Thus, the
multi-year nature o f investments i s not taken into account. A multi-year budgeting strategy i s
also expected to help reduce the variance between budget and actual out-turns through the
discipline it will bringto the overall budgetprocess.

3.18      Budget execution procedures have also been regularized. A strict cash management
and commitment control system has been established within the Ministry of Finance and
approves all payments. Spending agencies are required to submit monthly cash plans, which
determine their allotments, and can subsequentlyraise purchase orders based on these allotments.
All payments are reviewed by the Cash Management Committee prior to approval and checks are
issued by the Ministry of Finance. All revenues are collected and expenditures made from a
single consolidated account at the Central Bank of Liberia. While these systems have helpedto
re-impose fiscal discipline on the previously weak budget execution system, they may needto be
reformed in the medium-term as capacity develops and central controls can be relaxed and
streamlinedwith processes decentralized to budget owners inthe ministries and agencies.

3.19      Although budget controls are improving, the accounting system i s currently weak and
does not meet internationally acceptable standards. In most cases, it i s not based on a double

                                               - 17-

entry method o f book keeping, does not maintain a general ledger and does not include sufficient
reports to permit reporting based on generally accepted accounting principles. It also omits debt
and contingent liabilities. In 2007, an Expenditure Control and Accounting Program (LECAP)
were made operational in the Ministry o f Finance and the Bureau o f the Budget. This was a
transitional step to create a basic system to meet the specific requirements o f the Ministry o f
Finance for budget monitoring, listing o f payment vouchers for Cash Management Committee
approval, printing o f checks and preparation o f monthly expenditure statements. This is being
replaced with the interim accounting system currently being piloted within the Ministry o f
Finance. Double entry accounting software has recently been introduced through the interim
accounting system, but i s primarily used for reporting purposes and captures data from the cash
management committee only after payments have been approved and paid.                     Different
departments along the budget execution chain utilize stand-alone spreadsheets and databases.
With the recently introduced interimaccounting system, the Ministryof Finance intendsto move
towards monthly bank reconciliations. Plans are underway to introduce an Integrated Financial
Management Information System (IFMIS), to be operational in 2010, which will address many
o f the current shortcomings in the accounting structure. Fiscal reporting has improved with the
production o f timely quarterly outturn reports and annual fiscal reports, but the quality o f the
reporting could be further strengthened. The Government also intends to adopt International
Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS-cash basis).

3.20      The Revenue Code requires that the Government keeps its books on a double entry and
accrual basis o f accounting. However, the Government now does not have the financial and
technical capacity to move to an accrual accounting system. The Government i s planning to
transition from the current cash-based budget to accrual accounting at a later stage o f its wider
budget system reform process (post HIPC completion point). The process for transitioning to
accrual accounting is still in its early phase, involving enhancement of the cash accounting
system; ensuring a double entry general ledger is adequately kept; and improving fiscal
reporting. The introduction o f the IFMIS (as discussed above) will mark the next important
phasetoward accrual accounting.

Internal and external audits

3.21      While both the internal and external audit functions are currently being revived, they
remain hampered by capacity constraints. Only a few ministries and agencies have functioning
internal audit units, and these staffs are poorly trained. As a first step to improving the internal
audit function, an internal audit strategy was completed and approved in June 2008. Further
efforts will be needed to implement the strategy and build the requisite human capacity. The
external audit function is undertakenby the General Auditing Commission (GAC), established in
2005 and headed by the Auditor General. In order to increase professionalism, all staffs at the
GAC were made to reapply for their jobs in 2007. Applicants were tested, interviewed and
screened before the new set o f auditors was appointed. In order to conduct external audits in
2008, the GAC contracted experienced auditors from neighboring countries to lead teams o f
Liberian auditors and thereby facilitate knowledge transfer. The GAC has recently completed its
first set o f audits meeting international standards and audit reports were submitted to the
President in October 2008. While this marks an important milestone, further capacity building
support and external assistance will be required over the medium term to improve auditing
quality and guarantee the integrity o f the audit function.

Debt Management

3.25      The debt management department within the Ministry o f Finance i s poorly resourced.
Liberia's complex debt resolution process i s handled by senior government officials, and little
institutional capacity exists at lower levels to support a return to more regularized debt
management practices.       A debt management strategy was developed in 2008 to guide this
process forward, and a domestic debt strategy (2007) is under implementation. The government
intends to introduce software tools for debt management(currently data is stored in excel files)
and continue capacity building efforts to boost capacity for recording and analysis.

Aid coordination

3.26      Donor aid coordination and policy dialogue i s managed through the Liberia
Reconstruction and Development Committee, chaired by the President, which oversees the PRS

Principle 1: Reinforce Ownership

The formulation of the comprehensive PRS and the increasing alignment of the Government's budget, beginning with the

                            6. OPERATION IMPLEMENTATION


                                  A.       COUNTRYOWNERSHIP

6.1     The proposed operation is fully supported and owned by the authorities as indicated in
the Government's Letter o f Development Policy. The specific reforms it supports are critical
elements o f the government strategy as articulated inthe PRSP and have been strongly promoted
by both the President and Minister of Finance inthe donor's forum inBerlin, Germany as well as
inbilateral meetings. The PRSP is the result of comprehensive consultations with all levels of
society that were unprecedented in Liberia's history. It included detailed discussions with
Liberians at the district and county level, civil society organizations, the private sector, the
Parliament and international partners. Consultations confirmed the four pillars underlying the
Government's poverty reduction strategy with high priority being accorded to securing lasting
peace, strengthening the judiciary and combating corruption, job creation, and restoring Liberia's
infrastructure.


                                 B.       FIDUCIARY   ASSESSMENT

6.2       A Public Expenditure Management and Financial Accountability Review (PEMFAR)
has recently been concluded which includes an analysis o f Liberia's PFM strengths and
weaknesses. The findings from the PEMFAR show that the Government has taken considerable
actions to improve public financial management since 2006.               As detailed in section 3,
government revenues have increased several folds since FY02/03, and expenditure controls have
been strengthened through the establishment of the cash management committee and the interim
commitment control system. However, the Government still needs to address weaknesses in its
financial management systems, specifically within the areas o f the legal and regulatory
framework; internal and external audit functions; procurement and concessions; budgetplanning,
formulation and execution; accounting, recording and reporting; human resources and payroll
management; cash and debt management and aid management. This i s a central objective o f the
public financial management component o f the operation, the ongoing Economic Governance
and Institutional Reform Project and complementary ongoing and planned IDA and other
donors' interventions (see Section 4).

6.3       Both physical and bureaucratic infrastructure was decimated by the war, including an
exodus o f most of the skilled personnel. Thus, the human capital in the area o f financial
management i s decidedly low, affecting both the private and public sectors. A decision was
made early in the re-engagement process to develop a centralized project financial management
unit, the PFMUinthe MoF, to create a reservoir of skilled personnel that would be available for
all donor projects. It is this unit that this operation will also tap into, to avoid the obvious FM
capacity constraints. Fiduciary risks for this operation are mitigated by the fact that the PFMU
of the Ministry o f Finances will be in charge o f financial management reforms, while an
international firm specialized inprocurement has been contracted with the support o f the EGIRP
to support procurement as well as working to build national procurement capacity in both the
MoF and other ministries.

6.4       In2008, the IMF staff conducted an updatedsafeguard assessmentof the Central Bank
o f Liberia (CBL) following the interim assessment conducted in 2007. The updated assessment
found that while the C B L has largely addressed measures to increase transparency (as

                                                - 36 -

 recommended in the interim assessment), significant risks exist in the control framework o f the
 Bank. An area o f concern was the weak internal audit capacity, including the lack o f effective
 oversight o f CBL financial reporting, audit and control systems by the Audit Committee. The
 IMF staff noted that a subsequent special audit conducted in August 2008 indicated that the
 monetary data submitted by the CBL are accurate and that progress has beenmade on improving
 some aspects o f the general control environment. The CBL has also made progress in
 implementing other recommendations, including adoption o f investment guidelines and
 segregating the duties o f the Banking and Financial Departments regarding placement o f CBL
 resources. Areas where further progress i s needed include: (i)further strengthening o f internal
 audit and control; (ii) developing an action planto implement recommended improvements; and
 (iii)adopting an internal audit charter.

                       c.      GRANT   ADMINISTRATION AND DISBURSEMENT

 6.5       The proposed operation would consist of a grant of SDR 2.7 million (US$4 million
*equivalent) to be available upon effectiveness and disbursed on the basis o f a withdrawal
 application. The operation will be disbursed according to the Bank's disbursement procedures
 for developmentpolicy operations. The grant will be disbursed inone tranche upon effectiveness
 and i s not tied to any specific purchases and no special procurement conditions will be required.
 Once the grant becomes effective, the funds will be deposited into an account designated by the
 Government at a Bank acceptable to the Association. The equivalent Local Currency amount
 will immediately be transferred to the Consolidated Treasury account o f the Government that is
 used to finance budgeted expenditures and an equivalent amount recorded in the government's

6.16      Inthe current environment of a country emergingfrom conflict, the proposed operation

6.17      Security risk. The security situation in Liberia remains fragile, despite the presence o f

Liberia continued to make progress on reconstruction and development during the third year of
President Ellen Johnson Sirleaf`s administration.        Progress occurred despite many new
challenges, with the food, fuel and financial crises complicating Liberia's already difficult
recovery. A particularly significant achievement in 2008 was the completion of Liberia's
Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper, which will be the guiding framework for Liberia's policy

1. Economic Context

In the previous Letter of Development Policy, we noted the scale of the damage and negative
consequences o f the conflict. Now, three years into President Sirleaf`s administration, it i s
possible to gain some perspective on the progress that has been made.

In2005, the government budget was a paltry US$80 million. Civil servants salaries were as low
as US$15 per month. Exports valued US$131 million. Sanctions on diamond and timber exports
were still in place. Our roads, not maintained for many years, had countless pot-holes, some
virtually impassable, resulting into extremely high repair and maintenance costs on vehicles.
Large foreign and domestic arrears were accumulating alongside significant revenue leakages.
The Central Bank's international reserves stood at just a little over US$5 million.Reflecting the
underlying concerns about the management o f the economy, net outflows o f remittances were
US$133 million, foreign direct investment was virtually non-existent, and Liberia's massive
external debt had accumulated to over US$4 billion, equivalent to US$1,500 for each Liberian
citizen.

Since that time, the economic outlook has improved markedly, although there are still many
challenges. Economic growth increased from 5.3 percent in2005, to 9.5 percent in 2007 with an
expected decline to 7.1 percent in 2008 on account o f lags in investment starts and adverse
shocks. The ban on diamond and timber exports has been removed, the Kimberly Certification
Process has been adopted, new forestry law and regulations established, and timber exports are
expected to restart in the next few weeks. The level o f exports rose to US$260 million in 2008.
The government's budget has increased to nearly US$300 million. The lowest monthly civil
servants salaries is now US$70 and civil servants salaries are no longer in arrears. The
government's external and domestic debts are being addressed, and the Central Bank o f Liberia
(CBL) international reserves now stands at approximately US$50 million. Additionally, the


                                              - 45 -

capital and liquidity positions o f banks operating inLiberia have significantly improved, and the
exchange rate has been broadly stable, thereby containing inflation, before the shocks o f the last
year. The government has made substantial progress on the management o f its external debt,
returning to good standing with the major international financial institutions and reaching the
Decision Point inthe Enhanced Highly Indebted Poor Country (HIPC) initiative in March 2008,
and a generous agreement with the Paris Club reached soon after.

Nevertheless, this does not downplay the enormity o f the economic and social challenges that
remain. Basic infrastructure remains in a state o f disrepair after years o f neglect and damage
during the conflict. Many roads are impassable, which seriously constrains peace building
efforts; contributes significantly to weak economic activity in agriculture, timber, and mining;
and undermines the delivery o f basic health and education services. Consolidating the peace,
revitalizing the economy, and reducing poverty will be next to impossible without a significant
improvementin roads. There was no electricity or piped water in the country for 15 years until
the new Government turned on some water and electricity in Monrovia in July 2006. Schools,
hospitals, and clinics are badly damaged, and most government buildings are dilapidated. O f the
325 health facilities operating before the war, about 95 percent were partially or wholly
destroyed, and there were at war's end only about 50 Liberian physicians to cover the nation's
public health needs - one for every 60,000 Liberians. Over half o f Liberian children and youth
are estimated to be out o f school. The war created a generation with very limited exposure to
formal education, with the destruction o f about 70 percent o fthe school buildings.

The decimation of the economy has led to very high levels o f unemployment and
underemployment. With the collapse o f so many sectors, ex-combatants and returning refugees
and internally displaced persons are struggling to find work. One o f the most important
challenges facing the government i s to create opportunities for employment as quickly as
possible for as many people as possible through a combination o f the revitalization o f
agriculture, rebuilding o f infrastructure, community-based revitalization projects, and the
resurgence of natural resource-based economic activities.

About half o f the Liberian population lives in rural areas, and over 80 percent o f those are
subsistence farmers with little or no cash income. However, many o f those farmers were
displaced during the conflict, and are now returning after many years to situations o f uncertain
land tenure and a lack o f seeds, fertilizers, and tools. Based on data inthe Core Welfare Indicator
Questionnaire (CWIQ), 64% of the population remains below the poverty line and 48% i s in
extreme poverty. 55% o f urban residents live in poverty compared to 68% o f rural dwellers.
The situation is similar in small and medium-sized towns that once relied on rubber and mining
activities. InMonrovia the situation i s slightly better, with about 50 percent of households living
inpoverty and22 percent insevere poverty.

Furthermore, our progress has been threatened during the last year by a trinity o f crises - the
food, fuel, and financial crises, presenting untold challenges for the government and the people
o f Liberia. The rise in food prices was particularly difficult for our population, as it comprises a
major component of household expenditure. Over half o f urban incomes are spent on food,
while ruralhouseholds spend two-thirds of any cash they earn on food. Rice is an important part
o f the Liberian diet, and the surge in international prices-around       120 percent in the year to
September 2008-presented      a severe risk to food security as two thirds o f rice consumption in
Liberia is imported. Inflation reached a high o f 26% on a year on year basis, portending the


                                                 - 46 -

levels o f stress that much o f the Liberian population felt. Finally, with the financial crisis now
translating into a global economic slowdown, the forecast 2009 GDP growth rate for Liberia
have been revised sharply downwards, from 12.7% in November 2008, to 6.2% in February
2009, and to 4.9% inMarch 2009.

Therefore, while we have made progress in the last three years, substantial difficulties remain.
We are particular mindful of the global slowdown resulting from the financial crisis, and have
already begunto take actions to mitigate possible negative effects on the Liberian economy over
the next year.



2. Our Vision for a New Liberia

The government's key objectives are to builda new Liberian nation that is peaceful, secure, and
prosperous -to work, inthe words o f President Sirleaf, so that "Every Liberian, no matter where
they live, no matter who they are, should have the foundation and opportunity necessary to
achieve their goals and live long productive lives." Achieving our goals will require policies
aimed at both economic recovery and political stability that are mutually reinforcing and serve to
ensure that our future growth does not recreate the pattern o f the past, achieving instead greater
distributional equity.

Development cannot proceed without basic peace and security. The drive to human development
inthe new Liberia must be based on the total elimination o f insecurity, restoration of peace and
reconciliation as well as the protection o f human rights. Additionally, it will require the building
of a new security and police force that i s sufficiently strong to keep the peace while resting
firmly under the democratic civiliancontrol.

A key element to securing these goals is restoring and strengthening basic human rights and
further empowering the Liberian people. The new Liberia will do away with the divisions,
marginalizationand exclusion of the past. Political power will be decentralized and shiftedto the
counties and communities, which will be empowered to effectively participate in decision
making and to take control o f local issues and development process. The goal i s to usher in an
inclusive and highly participatory democracy in which rights are respected; people are engaged
inthe governance process; nationalresources are used for the benefit o fthe people; and effective
institutions o f governance are built. These will require human and institutional capacity building
to ensure good governance, an effective state, and proper checks and balances among the three
branches o f government.

These goals are reflected in our recently-completed Poverty Reduction Strategy (PRS) for 2008
to 2011,which was finalized inApril 2008. The PRS has four main pillars:

    1. Expanding peace and security,

    2. Revitalizing economic activity,

    3. Strengthening governance and the rule o f law, and

    4. Rebuildinginfrastructure and providing basic services.



                                               - 47 -

Actions in each o f the four pillars are mutually reinforcing. National security i s a pre-requisite
for real economic progress with substantial impact on poverty. At the same time, peace and
national security will be severely threatened if chronic poverty continues to rise. Sustainable
peace will largely dependupon the ability to deliver basic social services throughout the country.
Similarly, without basic infrastructure the private investments needed to fuel growth will not be
forthcoming. Governance and the rule o f law provide the institutional base for strong economic
performance and poverty alleviation, and the justice that i s needed to ensure that grievances are
settled through dialogue within the political system, as opposed to violence.

These four pillars are underpinnedby a growth strategy with three basic prongs.

First, we must rebuildour infrastructure, particularly roads. The PRS process revealed that across
the country, Liberians' number one priority is better roads, which Liberians see as essential for
creating jobs and new economic opportunities, revitalizing agriculture, reducing prices,
strengthening local governance, facilitating access to health and education services, increasing
the effectiveness ofthe police and other security forces, and helpingto maintainpeace.

Second, we must quickly revive our traditional sources o f economic growth - rubber, timber,
mining and cash crops - and ensure that the benefits accrue to all Liberians in a sustainable
manner. Concession contracts aim to balance the need to generate competitive returns for
investors with the need for robust and transparent financial flows. The Government i s working
hard to revitalize agriculture as the bedrock o f the economy, as it provides livelihoods for the
majority o f Liberians, and this has become even more important inthe context o f the global food
crisis. A vibrant agricultural sector i s central to reducing poverty, providing food security, and
ensuring progress toward the Millennium Development Goals.

Third, the Government is beginning to take steps to diversify the economy over the mediumterm
into the competitive production o f labor-intensive downstream products, manufactured goods,
and services. It aims to create an open economy with a strong business environment with low
tariff and non-tariff barriers, strong linkages to international markets, minimal government
intervention except where necessary to address market failure, and low levels o f red tape and
unproductive regulation.

Inall ofthese activities, the private sector will bethe maindriver of growth. O fcourse,
economic growth alone will not ensure poverty reduction. But taken together with actions across
the pillars--creating a peaceful and secure environment, building strong institutions o f
governance, and delivering effective health and education services-these    actions will provide
the foundation for rapid, sustainable and equitable growth.

For the three-year period 2008/09-2010/11, we estimate the total cost o f implementing the PRS
at US$1.6 billion. We expect to be able to finance about a third o f this with our own resources,
which leaves a funding gap o f approximately US$l.l billion, or about US$370 million per year.
While the precise level o f donor support is uncertain-improving              data collection and
strengthening aid management i s one o f our key priorities-present          donor support, while
extremely important, does not fill the funding gap, and is not always fully aligned with the PRS.
Inparticular, our highestpriority inthe PRS, infrastructure, continues to needfunding. Owingto
the near absence o f donor support through the budget, we are actively working on a mechanism
to better coordinate and manage aid inline with the PRS priorities.

3. Key Actions to Date & Priorities for Future Action

Expanding Peace and Security

We have made good progress in deactivating former security personnel, demobilizing ex-
combatants, and rebuildingnew security forces. We have fully completed the process o f making
severance and retirement payments to former armed forces personnel. We also deactivated a
large number o f the almost 1,000-strong Special Security Services (SSS) personnel. The
government places very high priority on peacefully re-integrating ex-combatants, and we have
successfully disarmed, demobilized, and reintegrated many thousands o f these individuals. We
have facilitated the return o f refugees from neighboring countries, worked to reintegrate tens o f
thousands o f internally displaced people, and provided support to the returnees through health,
water, training, and employment programs. Inthe last year, repatriation from Sierra Leone and
Guinea has been completed, while support to returnees from Ghana continues.

A key challenge going forward is to create new, professional, and accountable security forces.
We have begunthe process o f rebuilding and strengthening the Liberian National Police (LNP)

qualified personnel, re-equipping and logistical support to the LNP, and restructuring and
reform. The LNP's Emergency Response Unit became fully operational, and i s assisting the
Police Support Unit and regular police combat crime. With 139 o f an anticipated 500 personnel
already graduated, it i s anticipatedthat the ERUwill reach full strengthby the end o f 2009.

Moving forward, and as we prepare for the eventual withdrawal o f the United Nations
peacekeepers, we plan to maintain an effective security system that i s capable o f defending and
protecting Liberia and promoting domestic and sub-regional peace and security. We wish to
create a secure environment conducive to personal security, business activity, economic growth,
and sustainable development. A National Security Sector Reform Strategy has been completed,
and our target for the new Armed Forces o f Liberia has been met with the current strength o f
2,057 personnel. This includes 773 formed into military police units, an engineering company,

It is important to ensuring the peace that our national security agencies are present throughout
the country.    Representatives o f the security institutions are increasingly being deployed

We are placing special emphasis on the protection o f women and children, and encouraging

conducting specialized training for juvenile officers and those dealing with gender-based
violence. Women will be encouraged to form part o f every security institution with the goal o f
having at least 20% women's participation inall national security institutions.

Economic Revitalization

Our strategy for economic revitalization has two basic prongs: strengthening the overall
management o f government finances, and establishing the basis for a strong rebound in
productive economic activities. The Government's short-term objectives have been to stabilize
the economy, createjobs, and restore the Government's credibility inusing the country's scarce
resources efficiently and effectively.

To guide its actions, the Government finalized and embarked on a Staff-Monitored Program
(SMP) with the IMF in February 2006 inorder to strengthen economic management. Following
strong performance on this program, in March 2008 the Government entered into a Poverty
Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF) and Extended Fund Facility (EFF) arrangements. In
December 2008, the IMF completed the first review o f Liberia's performance under the program,
and found it satisfactory.

We aim to promote accelerated and shared economic growth that i s underpinned by a stable
macroeconomic environment. To that end we will ensure that the evolving policy framework
increases productivity and promotes growth and development in the key productive sectors
including forestry, agriculture, and mining and implement growth-supportive infrastructure
policies and programs for roads, energy, telecommunications, and water and sanitation. We will

We also continue to make progress on our large and onerous debt burden. After clearing our
arrears with the World Bank and African Development Bank in late 2007, in March 2008 we
returned to good standingwith the IMF and reached Decision Point inthe Heavily IndebtedPoor
Countries (HIPC) Initiative. In April 2008, we met with the Paris Club, the group o f official
bilateral creditors, for the first time in over 25 years, successfully negotiating the most generous
terms ever afforded by the Paris Club to a HIPC country at Decision Point. The agreement
provided immediate forgiveness o f over US$250 million in debt, and paves the way for 97%
percent forgiveness o f our bilateral debts at Completion Point, with several Paris Club creditors
agreeingto go beyond this requirement. Up front 100 percent debt forgiveness has already been
provided through full cancellation agreements already signed with the United States (US$425
million), Germany (US$410 million), Italy ($79 million) and Denmark (US$29 million), among
others. InApril 2009 we successfully concluded-after        two years o f negotiation-the  buy-back
o f US$1.2 billion o f outstanding government debt to commercial creditors. This buy-back was
made possible through support from Germany, Norway, the United Kingdom and the United
States. In addition, support was provided under the World Bank-administered Debt Reduction
Facility.

Since taking office, we have implemented a number o f policies to support economic
reconstruction and development. Supported by a recovery in rubber production, construction
activities, and the impact o f a large donor presence, real GDP growth is estimated to have risen
from 2.6 percent in 2004 to about 9.5 percent in 2007, after having declined by over 30 percent
in 2003.    Growth is estimated have dipped slightly to 7.1 percent in 2008 due to lags in
investmentand adverse external shocks.

Governance & Rule of Law

Poor governance was a root cause o f the multiple crises that besieged the Liberian nation over
the last quarter century from war, mismanagement, and human rights abuse to the deepening o f
poverty. The current government i s taking a range o f measures to strengthen key institutions to
promote good governance and the rule o f law. To begin with, all Ministers publicly declared
their assets and publishedthem inthe local newspapers. We have also developed a new code of
conduct for all public officials, which is currently awaiting legislative approval. Court
proceedings have been undertaken against those accused o f corruption and malfeasance, a
practice that we will seek to continue. The government i s implementing its new anti-corruption
strategy, with legislation passed in August establishing an independent anti-corruption
commission.      The Liberia Anti-Corruption Commission (LACC) became operational in
December 2008 after the passage o f the law creating the Commission and the appointment o f
Commissioners.

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) was established to account for atrocities
committed from April 1979 to October 2003 and to foster reconciliation within and between
Liberia's polarized communities. It continues to hold thematic hearings and in December 2008,
issued volume one o f its comprehensive final report. The Governance Commission (GC) is
working to restore and reform institutions o f government and to ensure democratic self-
governance that i s inclusive, participatory, andjust; to that end the GRC has produced studies on
Judicial and Land Reforms. The Independent National Commission on Human Rights has been
established to fight the culture o f impunity and protect and promote human rights, including
economic, social and cultural rights. The University o f Liberia has established a Center for
Conflict Transformation (CCT) which aims to undertake research, offer courses and give policy
advice. Additionally, the capacity o f Liberia's civil society in this area i s noticeably increasing;
there are at least 20 national NGOs that focus on peace-building initiatives and conflict sensitive
development issues across the country, as well as many faith-based organizations and
community-based organizations that undertake peace building and conflict resolution in a variety
o f forms.

The destruction o f public institutions is a critical constraint on which the Government will be
focusing attention. Ongoing reform efforts will be deepened to ensure that the public
administration i s transformed into an agent for change, development and socio-economic
transformation. We have developed a comprehensive civil service reform strategy in order to
strengtheninstitutions and build capacity. This strategy, which lays out the broad framework for
civil service reform was adopted in June 2008. Pay and compensation reform i s also being done,
inline with the strategy. Civil servant wages were increased to a minimumo f US$70 a month in
2008/09. The senior executive service (SES) program was also started in order to attract high
quality talent to government service, with over 60 positions recruited to date. The Government

meanwhile continues its efforts to vet the payroll to remove irregularities (including ghost
employees) and eliminate redundant staff.

Building civil service capacity i s critical to improved governance. The Resource Management
Unit(RMU)withinthe Ministry of Finance is implementingtraining programs for financial staff
o f government ministries and agencies and training two groups of students over a two-year
period. The first batch o f 29 students graduated in February 2009. These graduates will evolve

Looking forward, we plan to progressively decentralize governance, devolving decision making

The administration o f the justice and prosecution system will be strengthened, with the goal of

 A law reform commission will be established to review existing statutory and customary law

Infrastructure and Basic Services

The government inheritedan infrastructure that was ina total state o f disrepair as a result o f the
war and years o f neglect. Roads, telecommunications, the power system, water and sanitation,
schools, and health care facilities were mostly destroyed. The task before the Government i s to
rehabilitate the infrastructure and social services in order to create the conditions needed to
achieve broad-based growth and poverty reduction. Health, education, and other services must
have a particular focus on youth and women, who too often are marginalized in the access to


                                                 - 56 -

such services. Health activities will need to focus on malaria, HIV/AIDS, diarrhea, and other
major diseases that kill so many Liberians. Increasing school enrollment, offering more and
higher quality training opportunities, and providing more recreation and sports activities will
help improve the security situation, strengthenthe foundation for growth and poverty reduction,
and train Liberia's next generation o f leaders.

Rebuilding the road network i s central to achieving our goals o f enhancing security and
revitalizing the economy. Towards that end, we have built bridges and reconditioned a number o f
roads, as well as the international airport. This infrastructure redevelopment will continue into
the future with the help o f the World Bank-administered Liberia Reconstruction Trust Fund, a
multi-donor trust fund established in the last year. Over the next few years we plan to construct
and rehabilitate 1,000 miles o f primary roads and reconstruct and rehabilitate 300 miles of feeder
roads to assist farmers with delivery o f their products to the market and boost economic
empowerment and growth. We will also work to improve public transport both within Monrovia
andbetweencities, and have recently obtained 19 buses towards this end.

It is crucial that we improve facilities and processes at our ports to facilitate trade. This year will
see the `one-stop shop' at the Freeport o f Monrovia become fully operational, with a large
amount o f progress already made last year, which will expedite the payment o f import duties and
clearance o f goods. We are currently implementing a Port Sector Reform Program, with the
objective o f concessioning operations at the Freeport o f Monrovia and operating the port under a
landlord model. The concession process i s already underway, and could be awarded by mid-
2009. We have continued to undertake emergency repairs to port infrastructure, and have
consulted with development partners on how to both keep the port functioning and how to
improve its operations. The marginal wharf remains a major concern, and a key focus o f our
efforts and discussions with partners.

We have turned on power and water to parts o f Monrovia for the first time in 15 years, and will
continue to expand electricity in the months ahead. In 2008, we significantly expanded the
electrical grid in Monrovia and we will continue with this process in 2009. A concession for a
renewable energy plant i s also expected to be completed inthe early months o f 2009, which will
significantly increase our generation capacity. Inthe coming years we will continue to explore
our hydropower potential. Inthat context we have undertaken a feasibility study for the Mount
Coffee Hydroelectric Dam, with US Trade Development Authority support. We plan to increase
access to safe drinking water, doubling the number o f Liberians who have access to safe water to
50% in 2011 and increase access to sanitary human waste collection and disposal facilities. We
also aim to promote the creation o f local Water & Sanitation (Watsan) committees at the local
level nationwide.

Education i s central to our reconstruction and development program, and plays a central role in
human resource development, improved health awareness, awareness o f human rights, greater
participation in civic responsibilities, improved gender balance, and productive capacity. The
Government plans to improve educational access for as many children as possible, especially
those from poor families, and to provide skills development for adolescents and adults. We will
give special attention to implementing the new Girl's Education Policy and ensuring equal
opportunities for girls. The government needs to increase access by providing new schools where
none exist in population centers, reconstructing those damaged during the war and making
available school materials to ensure that teachers do not levy unofficial fees from parents.

Towards that end, we have started the long process o f rebuildingschools and bringingthem back

The provision o f basic health and nutrition services i s a major priority for the Government. With

We are working to strengthen the protection and promotion o f human rights, including the
establishment of an effective and operational Independent National Commission for Ilunian
Rights (INCHR) that will provide technical support to lead ministries in their obligations to
treaty reporting and involving civil society in the process of treaty body implcmentation,
monitoring and rcporting.

Infirastruelure and Basic Services

The government inherited an infrastructure that was in a total state of disrepair as a result of
the war and years of neglect. Roads, telecommunications, the power system, water and
sanitation, schools, and health care facilities were mostly destroyed The task before the
Government is to rehabilitate the infrastructure and social services in order to create the
conditions needed to achieve broad-based growth and poverty reduction. Health, education,
and other services must have a particular focus on youth and women, who too often ale
marginalized in the access to such services. Health activities will need to focus on malaria,
HIV/AIDS, diarrhea, and other major diseases that kill so many Liberians. Increasing school
enrollment, offering inore and higher quality training opportunities, and providing morc

  foundationfor growth and poverty reduction, andtrain Liberia's next generationof leaders.

  Rebuilding thc road network i s central to achieving our goals of enhancing security and
  revitalizing the economy. Towards that end, we have built bridges and reconditioned a
  number of roads, as well as the international airport. This infrastructure redevelopmentwill
 continue into thc future with the help ofthe World Bank-administeredLibcria Reconstruction
 Trust Fund, a multi-donor trust fund established in the last year. Over the next few years we
 plan to construct and rehabilitate 1,000 miles of primary roads and reconstruct and
 rehabilitate 300 miles of fecder roads to assist farmers with dclivery oftheir products to thc
 market and boost economic empowerment and growth. We will also work to improve public
 transport both within Monrovia and between cities, and have recently obtained 19 buses
 towards this end.

 It is crucial that we improve facilities and processes at our ports to facilitate trade. This year
 will see the `one-stop shop' at the Freeport of Monrovia bccome fully operational, with a
 large amount of progress already made last year, which will expedite the payment of import
 duties and clearance of goods.        We are currently implementing a Port Sector Reform
 Program, with the objective of concessioning operations at the Freeport of Monrovia and
 operatingthe port under a landlord model. The concessionprocess i s already underway, and

 We havc turned on power and water to parts of Monrovia for the first time in 15 ycars, and

continue to explore our hydropower potential. In that context wc have undertaken a

Education is ccntral to our reconstruction and development program, and plays a central role
in human resource development, improved health awareness, awarencss of human rights,
greater participation in civic responsibilities, improved gender balance, and productive
capacity. The Government plans to improve educational access for as many children as
possible, especially those from poor families, and to provide skills development for
adolescents and adults. We will give special attention to implementing the new Girl's
Education Policy and ensuring equal opportunities for girls. The government needs to
increase access by providing new schools where none exist in population centers,
reconstructingthose damaged during the war and making available school materials to ensure
