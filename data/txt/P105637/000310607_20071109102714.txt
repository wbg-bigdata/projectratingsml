                                      I. INTRODUCTION

1.      Ten years ago, Indonesiawent through an extreme economic crisis and politicalturmoil,
followed by a prolonged and painful adjustment period.                     However, very successful
macroeconomic management, sweeping decentralization, and bold policy decisions aided by a
favorable external environment have strengthened Indonesia's economic foundation.              In 2006,
Indonesia's average GNI per capita reached US$1,410. In 2007, poverty has fallen to 16.6 percent
and growth is on track to surpass 6 percent by the end of the year, the highest level since 1996.

2.      Now that Indonesia's post-crisis period is over the current challenge is no longer fiscal
consolidation but instead to ensure that the available resources are spent effectively.
Government debt fell from 80 percent of GDP in 2000 to an estimated 37 percent of GDP by mid-
2007. As a result of the sharp reduction in fuel subsidies in 2005, as well as declining debt service
payments and increasing revenues, Indonesia has created additional "fiscal space" of US$15 billion
annually (out of a total national budget of some U S 8 0 billion in 2007). Equivalent to around
4percent of GDP, this is the largest increase in additional fiscal resources since the oil revenue
windfall inthe 1970s.

3.      However, Indonesia still faces many challenges and is performing below its potential,
particularly with respect to the investment climate and public services. The Government is
committed to tackle these difficult microeconomic and institutional reforms, which are at the core of
the DPL program.        Indonesia has been improving the investment climate, public financial
management/governance and service delivery. However, the country started from a low base and
many of Indonesia's competitors are making even more rapid gains.

4.      With reforms progressing and with increased fiscal space, why does Indonesiawant this
Development Policy Loan? Policy-based loans have become the centerpiece of a more mature
partnership between international partners and Indonesia. The DPL program has been effective in
three ways:
    0     Advancing reforms. The DPL program has provided an impetus for reform-minded
          decision-makers to operationalize the Government's medium-term development strategy
          (RPJM) by translating broad objectives into priority actions.
          Predictable low-cost financing. Indonesia can borrow on the domestic and international
          bond markets, albeit at higher rates than program loans. While deficits are low, Indonesia
          faces very high gross borrowing needs (in excess of U S 1 6 billion annually), due to high
          levels of foreign and domestic debt principle repayments. Thus, program loans from
          development partners provide an important and preferred contribution to Indonesia's
          overall financing needs. The authorities are looking to the Bank and other development
          partners as a source o f reliable financing in meeting these needs.
          Cementing development partner coordination.               The DPL program has improved
          harmonization and reinforced support for reforms among the country's main development
          partners: the World Bank, the Asian Development Bank (ADB), and Japan. The ADB and
          Japan have been providing parallel financing to the DPL program. The overall DPL 4
          program could amount to as much as US$1 billion, with the World Bank committing
          US$600 million, the Asian Development Bank US$200 million, and the Government of
          Japan yet to confirm the sum of its parallel financing of US$lOO-US$200 million.

5.      The proposedloan to Indonesia for US$600 million is the fourth operation in a program
of annual, single-tranche loans. This significant policy-basedprogram lending to Indonesia has
developed into the centerpiece of the Bank's assistance and attests to Indonesia's own reemergence.
Launched in 2004, the DPL programwas the first major World Bank budget support program since
the East Asia crisis.     At its inception, the rationale reflected the opportunities arising from the
inaugurationof the first directly elected President in Indonesia's history. Not only did the newly
elected President have a decisive mandatebut his policy pronouncements indicateda strong desire for
reform. The country was also transitioning from economic stabilization and post-crisis recovery
towards longer-termmicroeconomic and institutionalreforms. The DPL program created a platform
for sustainedBank engagement duringthese criticalpoliticaland economic transitions.

6.       In 2007 Indonesia has experienced another strong year of reforms. This fourth
Development Policy Loan (DPL 4), together with an InfrastructureDevelopment Policy Loan (I-
DPL), supports the Government in its reform efforts. The DPL 4 builds on the past operations'
support to the Government in the core areas of: (i)the investment climate; (ii)enhanced public
financial management and governance; and (iii)service delivery to the poor. In 2007, the most
important reforms have included landmark tax administration and investment laws, a major VAT
refund of accumulatedarrears to the private sector worth US$1.2 billion, implementationof Ministry
of Financerestructuring(particularly intax, customs, andtreasury) and a substantiallyrevamped2008
budget. The DPL program has supported these reforms. Out of a total of 18 indicative triggers,'
Indonesiahas made importantgains in all areas: 14 triggers have been met or exceeded, while four
triggershaveseensubstantialimprovementsbut not complete implementation(see Chapter V).

7.      Indonesia is entering a pre-election year with an ambitious reform program. The year
2008 is the last full year before the 2009 parliamentary and presidentialelections. The Government
has presented a solid budget with a strong emphasis on increasing public investment, particularlyin
infrastructure, which also reflects the importance of improving public services. The investment
climate agenda is also substantial and will focus on implementationof the investment and tax laws
and further regulatoryreform, while public financial managementreforms will place additionalfocus
on improvedbudget executionand addressingfiduciary weaknesses.

8.      DPL 4 paves the way for a further series of Development Policy Loans. The current
World Bank CAS was preparedwhen Indonesia's economy and governance environment were more
fragile. With a new government in 2004 and an acceleration of reforms, DPLs have played a more
importantrole than anticipated. With its strong focus on Governmentownershipand strategic support
on selected critical reforms, the IndonesiaDPL series has respondedto an increasinglyself-confident
andwell-performingmiddle-incomecountry (MIC).

9.      The Government has indicated its interest in maintaininga strong DPL programwith
an increased lendingenvelope. The World Bank is currentlypreparingits new Country Partnership
Strategy (CPS) for FY09-FY12 and will address the Government's request in extending the current
DPL series. With the new CPS, Indonesiawill graduate from IDA and be positionedto become one
of the World Bank'smainIBRD-onlymiddle-incomeclient countries.

           11.      COUNTRYCONTEXTANDDEVELOPMENTCHALLENGES

10.      Ten years after the East Asian financial crisis, Indonesiahas emerged as a very different
country. It has embraced political pluralism and participation, and is in a strong position to resume
high growth and accelerate poverty reduction.               Prudent fiscal and macroeconomic policies,
particularly low budget deficits, have been instrumental in Indonesia's recovery, with the most
dramatic improvement in the form o f reduced levels o f Government debt -from 80 percent o f GDP
in 2000 to a forecast 34.5 percent at the end o f 2007.2 Despite several shocks during the past three
years (e.g., natural disasters, terrorist strikes in Jakarta and Bali, and global fuel price increases), the
economy has performed well and has now overcome the impact o f the slowdown at the end o f 2005
that resulted from the large fuel price increases (and resultant inflation and high interest rates).

11.      The World Bank projects growth to surpass 6 percent in 2007, adding a boost to the
Government's poverty reduction efforts. While continuing prudent macroeconomic policies, the
Government is pushing forward on broad structural and institutional reforms. These include efforts to
improve governance and address endemic corruption. Given its improved fiscal position, Indonesia
has the opportunity to enhance the quality o f education, expand healthcare, and close critical
infrastructure gaps to augment the investment climate, reduce poverty, and build a sustainable and
globally competitive economy.

                 A. POLITICAL AND SOCIAL CONTEXT
12.    Democracy in Indonesia is taking root and creating a more stable political environment.
The country has demonstrated a new found political stability over the past couple o f years as the
democratic process has deepened and achieved wider acceptance. This political stability and the
broader embrace o f the democratic process are two o f the main components supporting Indonesia's
economic growth.

13.    I n a major step towards democratic governance, Indonesiaconducted its first ever direct
presidential election in mid-2004. A former army general, Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono won a
commanding 61 percent o f the votes in a second-round run-off on a platform based on promises to
fight corruption, jumpstart the economy, and ensure social cohesion and stability. Since then the
Government has undertaken some key structural reforms and consolidated macroeconomic stability.
These efforts have contributed to increasing Indonesia's growth rate, which this year is projected to
reach its highest level since the 1997 financial crisis. Despite the fact that the President's own
Democrat Party is in the minority in Parliament, the Government has managed to preserve a broad
coalition of parties and has presided over a stable political environment.

14.    Direct local elections have also created a new framework of accountability. Constitutional
reforms securing direct elections have created a new accountability framework at all levels o f the
political system. Direct elections have been introduced on a rolling basis in provinces and districts.
Local elections in 2006 resulted in a strong backlash against incumbents, implyingthat the electorate
i s holding leaders accountable for their past performance.

15.    Political stability has also served to calm separatist sentiments and regional conflicts.
Another important consequence of growing political stability, together with greater decentralized
governance and regional development, is that regional separatism has declined and regional conflicts

for corruption. The main driver o f the anti-corruption effort has been a set o f powerful independent
oversight and prosecutorial institutions such as the Anti-Corruption Commission (KPK), Anti-
Corruption Court, Judicial Commission, Police Commission, and the Prosecutorial Commission.
Mostly established under the previous administration, these new institutions have begun to deliver
tangible results with strong support from President Yudhoyono. In addition, existing institutions such
as the State Audit Agency (BPK), the Financial Transaction and Analysis Center (PPATK), and the
Attorney General's Office are more active. High profile corruption investigations have been launched
across Government and also among some state-owned enterprises. These have led to a number o f
successful prosecutions.

19.   Nevertheless, despite the active prosecution of some offenders, the Government has been
slow to implement a proactive strategy to prevent corruption. Reform o f the judicial system has
stalled and there has been some backtracking as a result o f Supreme Court and Constitutional Court
decisions to curb checks and balances onjudicial power through newly created institutions such as the
Judicial Commission and the Anti-Corruption Court. More broadly, corruption prevention efforts
lack a clear focal point in the Government, weakening overall progress in implementing a preventive
anti-corruption strategy.

20.   The Government has also been slow to address civil service reform, but gradual progress
is now being made. Recently, the Government has begun to take some significant first steps to
develop and pilot models for civil service reform targeting high-level public officials and specific
ministries, in particular the Ministry o f Finance, the State Ministryfor State Apparatus and the BPK.
The President has called for wider civil service reform with the aim o f improving performance and
good governance. In September 2007 the Minister for State Apparatus presented to Parliament a

series o f programs for bureaucracy-wide reform. However, civil service rules and procedures remain
overly rigid and the procedures for recruitment, promotion, and compensation do not provide a
foundation for enhancing public sector performance.

21.     Indonesiahas experiencedan increasing number of natural disasters. More than 25 major
disasters each inflicting over 50 casualties have struck Indonesia the past 10 years, including the Aceh
tsunami (December 2004) and the Yogyakarta earthquake (June 2006), which caused widespread
fatalities and suffering among the population. Overall, Indonesia has made good progress in the
reconstruction o f Aceh and the islands o f Nias. The most remarkable reconstruction experience has
been in Yogyakarta. There, less than one year after the devastating earthquake in May 2006, almost
150,000 houses (more than half the total needed) were rehabilitated, most at low cost and with strong
community participation. The Government also implemented a new Disaster Management Law in
March 2007 to prepare and respond more effectively to disasters (see also Annex 9 on Disaster
Management and Reconstruction).

22.     Indonesiahas become the epicenter of Avian and Human Influenza (AHI) risks. Since it
first began ravaging Asian poultry stocks in 2003, the H5N1 virus has killed at least 203 people
worldwide. As o f October 26, 2007, Indonesia accounted for 89 o f those deaths. Worryingly, the
fatality rate among those infected is rising. The more lethal fatality rate i s partly due to ignorance as
people refuse to believe the virus poses a serious threat. It can also take up to five days for patients in
rural areas to be diagnosed and sent to a bird flu-reference hospital, by which time it is often too late
to save them. The Government's public awareness campaign appears to be reaching the bulk o f the
country's 240 million people and a recent consensus emerged between the Government and
international specialists on a detailed plan o f action.

23.     Indonesia is entering a pre-election year and uncertaintiesabout the future direction of
the Governmentwill increase. The next Presidential and legislativeelections are to be held in2009.
There is a risk that election politics will increase political tensions and slow reform momentum (see
the risk section in Chapter VI). However, historically, most legislation has been passed towards the
end o f each government cycle. There is also an opportunity that, with elections nearing, pressure will
increase on the Yudhoyono administration to deliver demonstrable results, especially in the priority
areas o f infrastructure development, governance reform, poverty reduction and the investment
climate.

43.    A large percentage of the population remains vulnerable to poverty. Measurement of
poverty in Indonesiais relatively volatile becauseof the large number of householdsclusteredaround
the poverty line. For example, raisingthe poverty line 20 percent from roughly US$1.50Cjust below
the Government's poverty line) to US$1.80 per capitaper day would increasethe povertyrate from 17

effects on the proportionof households classifiedas poor.

44.    Large gaps remain between the poorer east and the more prosperous west. Poverty
incidence is highest in the eastern islands, including Papua, Maluku, and Nusa Tenggara, at 41, 26,
and 23 percent, respectively. In contrast, poverty rates are 16.5 percent on Java and Sumatra.
However, Java and Sumatra are the most populous regions and contain over three quarters of the
country's poor. There is also a continuing disparity between urban and rural regions, with a poverty

45.    Performance with regard to the MDGs has been mixed.                  Of the eight Millennium
DevelopmentGoals (MDGs) listed in Table 2.4 on the following page, the majority are on track: in
2006, the percentage of people living on less than US$1 a day was substantially below the MDG
target of 10.3 percent; net enrolment in primary schools remains high - also when assessing the
gender balance; under-five child mortality is falling steadily; and access to improvedwater facilities
has increased dramatically. Other indicators are less positive, however. Reductionsin malnutrition
have stagnated since 2002, undermining the progress made in the other indicator in the poverty
alleviation goal, and maternal mortality fell by only 20 percent between 1993 and 2005, probably
insufficient to meet the MDG target by 2015. Finally, despite recent progress, access to improved
sanitationalso remains significantly short of the MDGtarget for the environmentgoal.

46.    The Government is scaling up spending on poverty through a National Community
Empowerment Program (PNPM). The PNPM program is expanded to over half of the country's
sub-districts in 2007, with full coverage planned for 2009. The community empowerment program
builds upon Indonesia's accumulated experience with community driven development programs, in
particularthe World Bank funded KecamatanDevelopmentProject (KDP) and UrbanPovertyProject
(UPP). These programs focus on improving local governance and service delivery at the sub-district
and village level by encouraging participatory planning and providing block grants. Funds are
generally used for rural roads, or education- and health-relatedprojects. Previous evaluations show
that projects provide internal rates of return of 39 to 68 percent and are completed at substantially
lower cost than comparablepublicly funded projects.

47.    Indonesia is also piloting two major conditional cash transfer (CCT) programs, one
directed at households and the other at communities. The householdCCT is modeled after similar
programs in Latin America, as well as the 2005-06 targeted cash transfer program. The household
CCT provides poor households with an average transfer of about US$140 per year, conditional upon
utilizing a basic set of health and education services. In the community CCT, which is an important
component of the PNPM, communities receive a grant that is aimed at increasingutilization rates of
the same basic services that are targeted by the household CCT. Facilitators guide the process of
decision-makingand provide technical advice on how to improve health and education outcomes.
Conditionality is introducedinthe form of financial incentives, by makinga portion of the block grant
inthe secondand subsequentyears dependent on exceedingcommunity-specifictargets establishedin
the initial year.

                    111.   THE REFORMPROGRAMAND PRIORITIES

48.    The Government's medium-term strategy (Rencana Pembangunan Jangka Menegah, or
RPJM) represents the country's development vision for 2004-09. This lays out three broad and
multi-pronged goals: (i)a safe and peaceful Indonesia; (ii)ajust and democratic Indonesia; and (iii)a
prosperous Indonesia. It also incorporates the eight MDGs in order to frame medium-term targets as
part o f international commitments to be achieved by 2015. Each o f these three broad goals contains
more specific objectives in a number o f sectoral or thematic policy areas. For the past three years, the
Government has further elaborated on the RPJM through the Annual Work Plans (RKP).               Through
the RKPs, the Cabinet identifies priority areas and associated targets among the 33 policy areas found
in the RPJM. The priorities o f the annual RKP provide an umbrella guide to aggregate budget
allocations in the state budget in order to respond to emerging challenges and to align spending,
programs, policies, and activities to current development needs.

49.    In 2008, the Governmentwill continue with its reform program. The 2008 budget proposal
submitted to Parliament in August proposes a substantial reallocation o f spending to priority areas,
including infrastructure, education and social assistance. This represents a recent break from a long
tradition o f incremental budgetingwherein allocations typically resembled those of the previous year
and only new proposals were subjected to scrutiny and analysis. The development theme for the 2008
RKP is "acceleration of economic growth to reduce poverty and unemployment".

50.    The 2008 Work Plan (RKI') delineates eight national development priorities. The eight
national development priorities are: (1) increased investment, export and employment opportunities;
(2) revitalized agriculture, fishery, forestry, and rural development; (3) accelerated infrastructure
development and energy management; (4) improved access and quality o f education and healthcare;
(5) improved effectiveness in poverty alleviation; (6) corruption eradication and acceleration o f
bureaucratic reforms; (7) strengthened defense capability and stabilization o f domestic security; and
(8) disaster management and mitigation, and improved treatment o f avian flu. The 2008 executive
budget proposal reflects these priorities, with significant increases in capital and social assistance
spending. Capital expenditures are increasingly focused on areas with expected higher economic
returns, such as the construction o f roads, bridges, irrigation tracks, and transportation infrastructure
and facilities. Underpinningthe high priority placed on economic infrastructure and investment-led
growth, significant spending increases are proposed for the Ministry o f Public Works (33.1 percent in
real terms) and the Ministryo f Transportation (54.8 percent inreal terms).

                 IV.      THE DPLPROGRAM: STRATEGYAND RESULTS
56.    The CAS for FY04-07, discussed by the Board in November2003; envisaged the DPL 1in
2004 as the first in a series of policy-based budget support loans to the Government of
Indonesia. A DPL was seen as the most appropriate instrument at a time of transition. In policy
terms, it was designed to support the transition from post-crisis stabilization to longer-termstrategic
development -best symbolizedby Indonesia's successful completionof the IMF-supportedprogram
andthe issuanceof the Megawatiadministration's White Paper in August 2003. The latter entaileda
one-year, time-bound package of policy reforms intended to help consolidate recent macroeconomic
and structural achievements, and to reaffirm a commitment to prudent economic management. The
DPL 1 also sought to sustain the reform momentum across administrations as President Yudhoyono
took office under the mantle of reform.

57.    In recognition of policy actions linked to the White Paper and the new Government's
"100-day plan", the Bank Board approved US%300million for DPL 1 in December 2004. The
DPL was expected to provide a foundation for continued policy engagement in the areas of
macroeconomic stability, the investment climate, and public financial management and governance.
Subsequent DPLs would broaden the reform agenda to encompass a wider range of policy areas -
includingpublic service delivery. As a result of a requestby the Government and its continuedstrong
performance, the World Bank has provided incrementally larger financing volumes in DPL 2
(US$400 million) and DPL 3 (US$600 million) in partnership with the Government of Japan and the
Asian Development Bank (see also Chapter VI on donor coordination). Four years on, the DPL
programhas succeededin providingpredictableand harmonizedbudget support reflectiveof the good
progress made in implementingreformsand achievingdevelopment results.

             A. DESIGN,PROCESS,AND SUBSTANCE
58.    The DPL program comprises four annual, single-tranche operations within a
programmatic framework that reflects good practices and lessons learned in budget support
and development effectiveness. The reform program supported by these DPL operations has been
underpinned by analytic and diagnostic work, policy engagement by Bank staff and policy
collaborationwith development partners - involving both budget support and non-budget support
partners (Figure4.1). The programcomplements, and is reinforcedby, Bank sectoral investments and
a large trust fund portfolio (see Annex 40.

59.    The DPL program has benefited from and, at the same time, reinforced strong country
ownership. DPL reform measures have been taken directly from Government planning policy
packages (e.g., the White Paper, medium-term and annual action plans, and policy packages in
investment climate, financial sector, infrastructure, etc.), built upon existing legislation (e.g.,
implementingregulationsand decrees on the Finance, Treasury, and Audit Laws), and have provided
blue-prints for, or helped to enable, high-level initiatives (e.g., civil service reform at the MoF,
nationwidecommunity-drivendevelopment programs). Inview ofthis, the inclusionof policy actions
in the DPL is not viewed by the Government as conditionality, but rather as support for an integral
part of the Government's own program. It has helped to raise the profile of key issues at the
ministerial level, accelerated the timetable for a number of the policy actions to be taken, addressed
administrative and bureaucratic bottlenecks, and provided an impetus for decision-makers to further
coordinate across economic and line ministries.

Principle I: Reinforce Ownership

The policy matrix represents an agreed framework by Government and the country's development partners supporting the budget,
Le., the Bank, the ADB, and the Japanese Government. All development partners base their disbursementdecisions in consultation
with one another and on the Government's fiilfillment of the same set of triggers. The policy matrix also sets out medium-term
outcome indicators with targets to be achieved by the end of the program. These indicators and targets were developedjointly with
the Government (and in the case of macroeconomic triggers, with the IMF) and have been adjusted as such as the program has
evolved.
Principle 3: Customizethe accountabilityframework and modalities of Bank support to country circumstances
The policy matrix entails a subset of key reform measuresthat are part of the Government's broader development program. It does
not pretendto capture the universe of reforms being implemented, nor to raise issues that are key to growth and poverty alleviation
but where, for various reasons, this may be counterproductive as part of a high profile Bank program, such as the DPL. Hence,
although the Bank is engaged in dialogue across a number of sensitivepolicy issues, many policy actions are left outside the scope
of the DPL to take into account country circumstances andthe politicaleconomy.
Principle 4: Chooseonly actions criticalfor achievingresults as conditionsfor disbursement
Country ownership and the criticality of policy issues to stated objectives are the foremost criteria in selecting DPL policy actions.
In some cases, "criticality" extends to key process measuresthat have a strong likelihood of initiating or accelerating medium- to
long-term reform implementation where vested interests or bureaucratic hurdles could block or slow the pace of reform. Both the
phasedwithdrawal of blanket guarantees and the consolidation of over 18,000 cash operations into a treasury single account (TSA)

under DPL 4. The pilot for comprehensive civil service reform and the development of an assessment framework for service

Principle 5: Conduct transparentprogress reviews conducive topredictableand performance-basedfinancialsupport
Developmentpartners agree to notional commitment amounts andpolicy triggers duringthe prior year's DPL. Developmentpartner
commitments are further refined, based on Government confirmationof program lendingneeds by the first quarter of the calendar
year. During this time, Government and development partners agree to specific criteria on assessing triggers. Given the

           B. RESULTSACHIEVED INMACROECONOMICSTABILITY AND CREDITWORTHINESS
64.   Prudent macroeconomic management and ongoing structural reforms have made
Indonesia a very different country now from the one it was just a few years ago, and have
boosted market confidence. In early 2004, at the inception o f this DPL series, the economy was
growing less than 5 percent, the Government's debt-to-GDP ratio was over 60 percent, investment
was less than 20 percent o f GDP, and the markets remained wary o f the country's long climb back to
a pre-crisis environment o f sustained high levels o f investment and growth. Given continued sound
economic management and progress on structural reforms on several fronts, momentum has shifted,
particularly in the past two years. Despite occasional bouts o f volatility, the economy is now on a
much stronger footing. Recent growth rates (5.6 percent in 2005, 5.5 in 2006, and a projected 6.3
percent for 2007) are among the highest in the region. Indonesia's international credit ratings have
seen dramatic improvements over the past four years, with S&P upgrading Indonesia's credit rating
from CCC+ in 2003 to BB- in 2007      -reflecting  a five notch improvement infive years.

65.   After several years of prudent post-crisis economic management, Indonesia has achieved
a level of fiscal consolidation in line with the Government's projections. Smaller budget deficits
and non-debt financing (e.g., BRA asset sales), along with steady economic growth, have brought the
debt-to-GDP ratio down rapidly. By end-2005, debt-to-GDP had dropped to 45 percent from 54
percent in 2004 and 80 percent in 2000, well below the 60 percent target specified in the State
Finance Law. For 2007, the debt-to-GDP ratio is expected to fall to below 35 percent o f GDP,
achieving the expected outcome on debt reduction envisioned at the outset o f the D P L program. Even
with expected increases in public investment to the education, health, and infrastructure sectors, fiscal
sustainability will continue and the debt-to-GDP ratio i s projected to fall below 30 percent in 2010.
To bolster the Government's efforts, the Bank and AusAID are supporting the development o f sound
debt management practices. These include governance, risk management, and staff capacity-building,
as well as providing policy guidance.

66.   The Government has also achieved significant improvement in non-oil and gas tax
revenues, mainly through the modernization of tax offices. While the increase in revenues over
the past four years is mostly attributable to higher oil and gas revenues, non-oil and gas revenues have
also increased, from 10.4 percent o f GDP in 2003 to a projected 11.4 percent for 2007.              The
Government's overriding objective o f its tax agenda is to encourage increased and more predictable
revenues through improved compliance and a broader tax base, while reducing rates to improve



                                                   22

investment sentiment. Over the past few years, the Government has increased taxpayer registration
and tax return filings by new and existing taxpayers, and has improved recovery o f tax arrears and
expanded audit coverage. In December 2004, e-registration o f taxpayers was introduced, as was e-
filing in January 2005. This was accomplished, in part, through the establishment and expansion o f
modern tax offices, including to medium and small taxpayers in 2006 and 2007, who are now
responsible for by far the largest share o f Indonesian taxes.        The GFMRAP is supporting the
modernization o f these tax offices, including the upgrading o f facilities and IT systems, staff
reorganization by function, increased remuneration, and the institution o f a taxpayers' bill o f rights
and a tax official code o f conduct. In 2007, the Government continued its program o f extending
modern tax offices with a major expansion o f medium-sized tax offices in Indonesia and through the
beginning o f a reorganization o fthe central tax office to support the longer-term tax reform agenda.

67.     Despite progress on extending the modern tax office system, many problemsremain. The
tax system i s still characterized by relatively low revenue generation, poorly defined and overly
complicated taxes (Le., creating excessive compliance costs), and poor and inconsistent enforcement
(i-e., creating uncertainty and abuse). In part, the problem requires better oversight o f tax officials,
and in part it stems from a systematic lack o f information and capacity for tax and customs analysis
outside o f the respective tax and customs offices. The lack o f capacity has led to: an inability to
accurately assess and forecast the implications o f the proposed tax and customs changes; reduced
checks and balances; and the inability to balance taxpayer and Government requirements. As part o f a
second phase o f the institutional reorganization in 2006-07, the Ministry o f Finance has established a
Fiscal Policy Office (Badan Kebijakan Fiskal, or BKF). The BKF replaces Bapekki (Agency for
Economic and Financial Sector Research and International Cooperation), itself only operational in
2005, with the aim o f separating formulation and implementation o f fiscal policy within the Ministry
o f Finance and to address the weaknesses described above.

68.     One of the most defining reforms supported by the DPL program was the dramatic
reduction in fuel subsidies during DPL 2, which reduced the overall level of subsidies from 4
percent of GDP in 2004 to 2.7 percent in 2007. Despites some initial missteps in 2005, the
Government and Bank Indonesia managed the transition to higher domestic fuel prices and rising
global interest rates. The fuel subsidy reduction was underpinned by on-time analytical work and was
accompanied by an unprecedented and effective communications campaign.                 The result was a
substantial reduction in regressive fuel subsidies that stabilized the macroeconomy and vastly
improved Indonesia's fiscal position. More broadly speaking, with lower subsidies, the central and
regional governments were able to use the additional US$10 billion a year in reallocated funds for
social protection programs that helped to mitigate the social impacts o f the fuel price hike on the poor
and to focus more effectively on human development and other priorities, such as increased
infrastructure.

69.     Despite these successes, there is scope for further reductions in subsidies. While the 2005
fuel price hikes were a bold move, Indonesia's retail prices for gasoline and kerosene still remain
fixed and well below international prices. With international oil prices currently at close to record
high levels, fuel subsidies will remain a large part of Indonesia's budget. The Government also faces
a critical choice on electricity subsidies. The increases in fuel prices have increased the subsidies
required by P L N (the state-owned electricity company) as they pass into production costs. In 2006,
the central government spent Rp 35 trillion (US$3.8 billion) on electricity subsidies, which have
increased significantly after the fuel price hikes - resulting in an additional subsidy o f Rp 20
trillion. As i s the case for fuel, electricity subsidies distort consumer and producer incentives and
constrain higher value spending. Most o f the electricity subsidy is absorbed through a regressive



                                                    23

subsidy to consumers (66 percent), followed by industry (29 percent), and business (5 percent) in
2006. Reducing inefficient spending with due consideration for growth outcomes and the impact on
the poor will continue to be an important topic of analytical work and policy engagement between
development partners andthe Government.

            c.  RESULTSACHIEVED INTHEINVESTMENT CLIMATE
70.    Improving the investmentclimate is critical to Indonesia'sgrowth and povertyalleviation
prospects. After the crisis, investment fell from 30 percent to 20 percent of GDP. Such low levels of
investment have prevented Indonesia from growing at the same rate as China and Vietnam, which
benefit from a high investment ratio of 45 percent and 32 percent of GDP, respectively. The
weaknesses in the investmentclimate were clearly identified at the outset of President Yudhoyono's
administration, with the new Government's medium-term plan detailing the major constraints and
proposed solutions. The Indonesian Chamber of Commerce (Kadin) provided a roadmap for
improving the business environment, and the Government and development partners outlined high
priority policy areas, including: investment policy, taxation, customs administration, infrastructure,
and SME policy. The Government issued three economic policy packages in 2006: one on the
investment climate, one on infrastructureand one on financial sector reform. Then, in June 2007, a
comprehensivefollow-up packagewas issueddetailing 168 specificreform measuresto be carriedout
over the following 12-18 months. Many ofthe key reformareas spearheadedby the Governmenthave
beensupportedbythe DPLprogram.

71.    In general, these reforms have shown good results in improvingthe environment for high
quality investments. This is evident in the strong contribution by investments to economic
expansion, which is projected to account for about one-quarter of total GDP growth in 2007.
Business confidencehas strengthened over the past few years, with the rupiah amongthe top six best-
performingcurrencies tracked by Bloombergthis year and with the Indonesianequity market one of
the best performers in 2007. Business perceptions about the investmentclimate have also improved
significantly between2003 and 2007. These positive investment trends are due in large measureto a
favorable global economic environment and Indonesia's macroeconomic situation, as described
above, with inflation sharply lower, interest rates declining, the Government's debt-to-GDP ratio
falling, strong export growth and a relatively stable exchange rate. However, the Government's
recent reformshave also helpedto improveIndonesia'sbusiness environment.

DPL agenda this year, these have been all benefited from the Government's commitment to
make improvements in these areas. According to manufacturingfirms surveyed in July 2007,

Indicators also show an improvement in perceptions over customs and trade regulations as a
constraint to doingbusiness, from 51 percent of respondents in 2003 to 32 percent in 2007. Despite
improved perceptions and reforms undertaken in recent years, businesses and investors continue to
suffer from an overall poor business climate. For example, while import clearance has improved30

investmentclimatepolicy package.

75.   The recent gains in improving the investment climate took place from a low base.
Indonesiacontinues to rank poorly in internationalsurveys. The World Bank's Doing Business 2007
report ranked Indonesia 135th out of 175 economies on ease of doing business, and IMD's World
Competitiveness Yearbook ranked Indonesia 54th out of 55 countries, well behind the Philippines,
Indiaand Thailand. However, there were signs of progress over the past year with Indonesia's Doing
Business ranking improving from 133 to 123, and with other indicators such as the Heritage
Foundation's Index of Economic Freedom also showing improvement. But despite this progress,
businessesin Indonesia still face a difficult legaland regulatoryenvironment. Strict labor regulations
continue to create a disincentive to employment expansion and infrastructure weaknesses raise the
cost and uncertaintyof doing business. Businesses face a complex regulatory system consisting of
numerousoverlapping, redundant and unnecessary licenses, each with its own prerequisites and often
involvingmultiplereportingrequirements and inspections. The result is a slow, costlybusiness start-
up process and a difficult operating environment in which arbitrary decisions from officials create
high levels of risk and uncertainty. The Government has committed publicly to addressing these
problems and in September 2007 announcedseveral new decrees aimedat cuttingthe time to establish
and register a limited liability company. Finally, much remainsto be done to improvethe regulatory
and institutionalframework for public-privatepartnerships (PPP).

            D. RESULTSACHIEVED INPUBLICFINANCIAL           MANAGEMENTAND GOVERNANCE
76.   Public financial management (PFM) systems in Indonesia were left essentially untouched
by reformsand modernizationuntil the beginningof 2000. The systems were heavilycentralized,
input-based, and largelyrelianton manual processingof transactions. PFMwas basedon an outdated
chart of accounts and regulatory framework. Based on the recommendations provided in the CFAA
andthe GovernmentWhite Paper on ReformofPublic FinancialManagement Systems-bothissued
in 2001 - there has been an ongoing period of reform to modernize the regulatory framework,
reorganize the Ministry of Finance, improve budget documentation, and streamline the budget
preparationprocess.

77.    The seminal changes to the legal and regulatory framework on PFM came with the
approval by Parliament of a troika of laws on State Finance, State Treasury and State Audit in
2003 and 2004. In addition, the presidential decree on procurement in 2003 set out the basic
principles of procurement: transparency, open and fair competition, economy, and efficiency. It
covers all areas of procurement that use public funds and is supposed to cover contractingentities at
all levels of government. These laws together providethe framework for PFMmodernizationin line
with good internationalpractice.

78.    The DPL program has supported a subset of the ambitious PFM modernizationagenda.
It has supported many of the implementingregulations to put into force the new legal and regulatory
framework. The DPL program has also helped move forward reforms to re-engineer business
processes across the budget cycle, including making the national budget compatible with the
international standard GFS classification, consolidatingGovernment accounts into a Treasury Single
Account (TSA), improving fiscal reporting, and establishing forward-looking budgeting. The DPL
program has also supported institutional reforms aimed at modernizing the Ministry of Finance.
Overall, Indonesia has taken important steps in recent years to establish a sound legal and
administrative framework for modern financial management. Changes in the legal and regulatory
architecture are now largely complete and the momentumhas shiftedtowards implementationof new
financialmanagementpractices.

79.    In terms of budget reforms,the DPL programhas supported implementingregulationsto
the State Finance Law that have defined fiscal relationships between various parts of
Government. The provision of the new regulations is ongoing. The budget classificationis largely
consistent with contemporary internationalstandards. The MoF has taken first steps in developing a
Medium-TermExpenditureFramework (MTEF) to guide budget policy. The 2008 budget includes
for the first time a Medium-TermBudget Framework based on aggregate macro-fiscalforecasts of
both revenues and expenditures for two out-years. However, budget plans continue to be preparedon
a strictly annual basis and medium-termpublic investment needs are notyet addressedin a systematic
framework that reconciles medium-term budgetary costs with aggregate resources. Integrationof
planning and budgeting processes, a critical element in the move towards performance-based
budgeting, is also progressing. However, despite this progress, ministries and agencies still lack a
goodunderstandingof the objectives of the work planand the budget document. Implementationof a
more performance-orientedbudget will take moretime and a clearer strategy.




                                                 26

80.    On budget execution, Indonesia still spends 50 percent of its total capital expenditure in
the final quarter of the year.' For the past five years, spending has always started slowly and then
accelerated at the end o f the year. This spending pattern raises concerns, as project implementation is
disrupted by an adverse cycle. Moreover, under-spending in capital expenditures constrains increases
in infrastructure investments. Slow and back-loaded disbursements are symptoms of more severe
challenges that are encountered at each stage of the public expenditure management cycle. With
expanding fiscal space, public investment is increasing, putting additional pressure on PFM systems.
Planning and executing public investment is inherently more demanding than current or mandatory
expenditure. As part o f the indicative DPL 5 triggers, the Government has committed to important
measures aimed at accelerating public spending early in the fiscal year (see Chapter V).

81.    Treasury and cash management reforms have largely focused on the conversion of
operationalbank accounts into zero balance transit accounts and the installationof a Treasury
Single Account. Pilot runs to test zero-balance arrangements with commercial banks have been
successful. It is planned to complete the consolidation of all Government cash balances into the TSA
by the end o f 2007. The cash-based accounting system generates timely records o f revenue and
expenditure transactions.       Transaction records are electronically transmitted to the Directorate
General o f Accounts at treasury headquarters for consolidation and generation o f periodic reports on
budget execution. The cash-based treasury system does not track payment arrears on a monthly basis,
although DG Treasury's plan to move towards an accrual recording system would make this possible.
Treasury cash management and payment modernization will be supported by the planned
computerized Treasury Information Management System (SPAN) under the World Bank GFMRAP
project which is under implementation.

82.    The past few years have witnessed incrementalimprovements in the public procurement
environment. Presidential Decree No. 80/2003 (Keppres No. 80/2003) provided a national public
procurement regulation that meets most o f what is generally regarded as accepted international
practice including basic principles: transparency, open and fair competition, economy, and efficiency.
This decree also requires that a national public procurement office (NPPO) be established as a
regulatory body for public procurement. With the introduction o f Keppres No. 80/2003, a previous
certification ("pre-qualification") process for suppliers/providers o f goods and services was, in most
instances, abandoned by the Government. This had led to segmentation o f the market, especially at
the provincial level. Keppres No. 80/2003 also established a basis for sanctions, complaints handling
and requirements for certification o f users.          However, the public procurement system still has
significant deficiencies in its regulatory and, more importantly, implementation aspects. Although
delays in establishing a strong regulatory body are now close to being overcome, there continues to be
slow progress in developing a procurement law, and standard bidding documents and users manuals.
There i s also continuing weakness in procurement capacity in implementing agencies, especially at
the provincial and district levels, which results in collusive and corrupt practices in the bidding
process and concerns over the efficiency o f anti-corruption measures and sanctions.

83.    In terms of budget reporting,the aggregate Government annual financialstatements have
been prepared in a timely manner for three years now (2004, 2005 and 2006).                     This is a
remarkable achievement. These comply with the new Government Accounting Standards that are
described by the authorities as "cash towards accrual basis".               The aggregate annual financial
statements present revenues, expenditures and transfers, surplus/deficit, and financing aggregates, as
compared with budget provisions. A balance sheet and a cashflow statement are also presented, along

with a full statement o f government accounting policy. Despite these improvements, there continue to
be serious challenges in making the government financial statements reliable, as reported by external
auditors in their annual audit report for 2006. This reflects the immense challenges that lie ahead in
an environment where accounting capacities at the grassroots are weak and accounting processes are
not yet integrated or fully automated.

84.    The internal audit framework of the Government is extensive but scope and organization
do not match needs. Ironically, even with (or perhaps because of) multiple internal audit agencies in
existence, the internal audit function continues to be a weak link in governance. The internal audit
function i s carried out at the central government level by three institutions: Inspectorates General
(IGs) in each spending ministry; the State Development Audit Agency (BPKP), reporting to the
President's office, with government-wide jurisdiction; and the Bawasda at each local government.
This institutional structure is complex and the mandates and division o f labor between the various
internal audit institutions are unclear. The IG represents the principal internal audit apparatus o f a
ministry. The scope o f IG audit activities is generally limited to technical audit. The IGs therefore
operate as individual institutions corresponding to the number o f ministries.

            E. RESULTSACHIEVED INMAKINGSERVICESWORK FORTHEPOOR
85.    Savings of an estimated US$10 billion from the 2005 fuel subsidy reductions represent a
significantopportunity to improve the delivery of public servicesto the poor. After the economic
crisis, Indonesia failed to invest sufficiently in its economy, particularly in public service delivery.
Public development spending became one o f the lowest among middle-income countries, declining
sharply from 6.5 percent o f GDP in 1996 to about 4 percent in 2000. With an estimated US$lO
billion to reallocate from the regressive fuel subsidies and an additional US$5 billion available from
increased revenues and declining debt service, the Government has started to realize its ambitious
poverty reduction agenda linked to the achievement o f the MDGs. This agenda was added on to the
D P L umbrella last year, as part o f the Bank's on-going analytic, diagnostic, and policy support to the
Government's fuel compensation schemes. This year, under DPL 4, education and community driven
development have been added as new policy areas.           Given their recent addition, assessing the
improvements in service delivery in these policy areas will be difficult and consequently assessment
will be limited to reviewing the results o fthe fuel compensation programs.

86.    With funding now available for service delivery improvements, and despite Indonesia's
mixed human developmentoutcomes, change has already taken root in certain areas. Since the
fuel price hike, there has been an evolving change in how social protection programs are
administered, implemented, monitored, and evaluated. Prior to the 2005 fuel price rises, Indonesia's
social protection system was characterized primarily by (i)crisis-era safety net programs; and (ii)
large commodity price subsidies and transfers, particularly through fuel products. The crisis-era
social safety net programs were temporary short-term interventions, rather than long-term social
protection systems, and aimed to ensure continued access for the poor to affordable food, health and
education services. While meant to be temporary, many o f these programs became entrenched social
policy. The continued safety net programs address a diversity o f issues, but are low in coverage,
institutionally fragmented, and are not managed as one umbrella system. In2003, budgets allocated
to the three principal targeted programs, namely subsidized rice for the poor, educational scholarships
and health cards, totaled about Rp 8.4 trillion, accounting for only 2.2 percent o f total government
expenditure. By comparison, in 2005 about Rp 103 trillion was spent on fuel subsidies, o f which only
an estimated Rp 5 trillion benefited the poorest quintile.

87.    The fuel compensation programs enacted shortly after the second wave of fuel price
increases in October 2005 represented a break from traditional safety net programs. The
Government opted for a cash transfer scheme wherein 19.2 million households would receive
Rp 300,000 each quarter. This representedthe largest ever cashtransferprograminterms of coverage
and total volume transferred. The UnconditionalCash Transfer (UCT) program was designed as a
temporary, one-year cash transfer program aimed at mitigating the impacts of the fuel subsidy. For
poor recipients, the cash transfer more than compensated for the losses incurreddue to the fuel price
hike. Initial assessments during the first tranche noted several problems, particularly in targeting,
promptingthe Governmentto make adjustments inthe implementationofthe UCT.

88.    Lessonslearnedfrom assessmen'tsof the UCT programs-which have beensupported by
the DPL program-have led to the pilotingof ConditionalCash Transfers (CCT). Conditional
cash transfers can be used to stimulate demand for services by the poor and give the poor more of a
voice in frontline service provision. Programs that transfer resources directly to poor families have
provedeffective in several other countries at raisingschool enrolment and attendance, and increasing
utilization of healthfacilities. Community-basedapproachesthat aim at increasingutilization are also
relevant in the Indonesian context. The President announced that the Government would pilot both
`conventional' CCT to households and a community-based alternative CCT in mid-2007. The first
pilot in the province of Gorontalo started in July 2007. These approaches are led by Bappenas, and
involvethe Ministriesof Social Affairs, Education, Health,Public Works and Home Affairs.

                                       F. LESSONSLEARNED
89.    Over the course of the DPL program, several important lessons have been learned. These
lessons stem from the nature and evolution of the relationship betweenthe Bank andthe Government,
and also from the characteristics of the DPL program as a means of offering policy support, together
with the changing policy environment on the ground. Reflecting on three years of Development
Policy Lending in Indonesia, four lessons stand out: (i)DPLs only work with strong Government
ownership; (ii)DLPs needto strike a balancebetween breadthand focus; (iii)DPL-supportedreforms
should focus on implementation, but not exclusively; and (iv) DPL-supported reforms are most
successful ifunderpinned and complemented by a strongAAA and TA program.

90.    Lessons 1: DPLs only work with strong Government ownership. For the DPL program to
succeed and effectively build on previous operations, it is crucial that the program reinforce the
Government's reform agenda. As a growing middle-incomecountry, Indonesiacan access domestic
and international capital markets, thus offering it a range of financing choices. While the Bank's
financing is attractive, the driving reasonfor the Government's engagement with the Bankthroughthe
DPL program is the utility of the instrument in bolstering, locking in, and accelerating critical
reforms. To succeed, DPLs not only need Government commitment at the highest levelbut also mid-
level officials responsible for implementingthe reforms and following progress on a daily basis. On
sensitive policy issues, such as subsidy reductions, ownership may also need to extend to Parliament
and other decision-makers, as well as key elements of the broader civil society, e.g., academia, trade
unions, etc. In areas where broad consensus is still evolving, such as procurement reform and the
PPP-infrastructure agenda, the pace of implementation may be slower than desired but their very
inclusion in the DPL-supported agenda maintains a focus on the policy issues, prompts on-going
deliberation of policy options, strengthensthe resolve of reform champions, and may eventually lead
to reformbreakthroughs.




                                                   29

91.   Lesson2: DPLs need to strike a balance betweenthe breadth and the focus of the reform
program. Giventhe healthypolicy dialogue across a broad range of areas, it was naturalthat as the
DPL program rpogressed the scope of the reform program would expand. As experienced in other
countries with DPL programs, a tension typically arises between supporting a broad program and
maintaininga small set of key policy measures as triggers, in accordanceto Bank goodpractice. Over
the course of the program the number of triggers has crept up slightly but still been maintained at
below 20 overall. This is a little more than usual for DPL lendingbut manageablegiven the tenor of
the Government's reform program, breadth of engagement of internationalpartners and efforts to
monitor and assist reform implementation. Inthe case of DPL 4, after implementationof more than
half of the reforms by mid-year, the dialogue focused on a small number of key remainingreforms.
As a result, these received crucial senior-level Government attention, allowing obstacles to be
addressed(e.g. Trigger 6: Reduction intime neededto set up a new business).

92.   Lesson 3: Allow sufficient flexibility for modifications and increased complexity as the
reform program evolves. While DPL reforms should ultimately focus on implementation, in order
to start the reformprocessthis requiresan initial focus on other types of policy action. Reforms have
a natural trajectory consisting of planning, formalizing and implementing. For instance, prior to
implementinga key reform, the cycle may need to start with a planningphase, followed in turn by
formalizing in the form of a law, then implementationwith a pilot phase and, finally, roll-out.
Furthermore, this reformcycle will start at different stages in a DPL series. This DPL 4 has a strong
focus on implementation, particularly in the investment climate and public financial management.
This is because these two pillars have been part of the DPL series since the beginningand, as the
program has matured, reforms in these areas have advanced and often reached implementation.
Meanwhile, once reform areas "graduate" (for example, the macro-economic pillar after DPL 2), the
DPL programsupports new reform agendas, which are newly designed. A good DPL program needs
to balance strong implementation with reforms supporting regulations and, in some areas, the
analyticaland operational underpinningto suchregulations.

93.   Lesson 4: DPL-supported reforms are most successful if they are underpinned and
complemented by a strong AAA and TA program. This DPL program spans a periodof time that
has witnessedtransformativeeconomic and political developments in Indonesia. An overallstrategic
focus on the key dimensions identified macroeconomic consolidation, the business climate and
governance as essential. However, given the complexity of the reform process and the challenges
facingthe DPLprogramin engaging inthe process, successhas only beenpossiblebecauseof support
from a strong programof AAA. The programhasbenefitedsignificantlyfrom AAA that has included
a PovertyAssessment, a PublicExpenditureReview, a DPR, an InvestmentClimate Survey, a CFAA,
and a CPAR, all of which have been vital in strengthening the analytical underpinnings of the
program. The Bank's engagement is also grounded in Technical Assistance to the Government
associated with these AAA products and with ongoing lending operations, such as the Government
FinancialManagementandRevenueAdministration(GFMRAP)project.

education and CDD). In doing so, the DPL program aims to apply international good practice on
policy basedlending.

104. Breakthrough areas, such as civil service reform, have gained political momentum over
the past year. Following a stalemate in comprehensive civil service reform for many years, an
important initial breakthrough in this area was made by the MoF in piloting a comprehensive human
resources and pay plan. This plan includes the development of new job descriptions and job
evaluations, a new job grading system, a new bonus structure linked to job grades and performance,
clear standard operating procedures and service standards for every department, and right-sizing of
the bureaucracy in response to streamlined business processes. Discussions are underway to expand
the MoF pilot to other ministries.

105. Despite many achievements, significant challenges remain in all of the three pillars,
particularly in PFM. Despite strong improvements in budget allocations and budget processes,
budget implementationremains aweakness. Investment projectsare oftennot started before mid-year
and officials' time is consumed in revisions of detailed budget documents. The civil service reform
agenda, although promising, has only just begun and has mainly centered on the MoF. The situation
is similar with respect to off-budget accounts, where the MoF has started to address unregistered
accounts, and implement more transparent governance arrangements for two of the most important
(RDVRDA), but muchmore is needed.

109. Average processing time for VAT refunds was reported to reach more than one year in
2003." This ties up working capital for export firms and reduces the competitiveness of Indonesian

maximum time limits for processing refunds. In addition, the Government exceeded the trigger

Rp 10trillion by July 2007. Morethan 99 percent ofthe backlogwas settled by this deadline. VAT
refund payments doubled from roughly Rp 8 trillion during January-August 2006 to roughly Rp 16

Trigger 2: Issue MoF decree on tax audit procedures that allows taxpayers to request details of
             audit findings and a review in case of disputes after closing conference but before

110. In late 2006, the Ministry of Finance and the Directorate General for Taxes issued a
number of regulations that affect tax audit procedures in response to concerns raised by the
businesscommunity. MinisterialDecree No. 123/PMK.03/2006revised previous provisionsrelated
to tax audit procedures and taxpayers' rights. The decree is intended to strike a better balance
between the rights and obligations of taxpayers and the powers of tax officials. The decree allows
taxpayers to appeal tax assessmentdauditswithout havingto pay 100 percent of the disputed amount
in advance. Taxpayers are also given the right to be present during audit hearings and to receive a
copy of the results. Article 15 of the decree supports transparent tax audit procedures and
strengthened taxpayer rights in disputing audit findings. It requires the tax auditor to inform
taxpayers in writing of the results of the audit and closing conference, includingdetails on the audit
findings andthe legalbasis for audit decisions. Inaddition, the DGTax regulations on desk auditand
on field audit procedures, (PER-173/PJ/2006 and PER-l76/PJ/2006) stipulate taxpayers' rights to
bring an audit decision to -and request a review by -a higher level authority in case of disputes.
The Tax AdministrationLaw passedby the Parliament in July cementedre-balancedrights and duties
of taxpayers andtax department officials.

Trigger 3: Roll out a National Single Window with on-line clearance of merchandise. (Status:

111. The National Single Window is currently the most important trade facilitation initiative
underway in Indonesia. Under a single window, exporters and importers will no longer be required
to apply individually to multiple Government agencies, such as customs, the Ministry of Trade, the
food and drug agency, and quarantine. Indonesia has signed two international agreements with
ASEAN to establish an ASEAN Single Window by 2008. A national team to implement the single
window, chairedby the Ministerof Finance, was establishedin March2006. A pilot test of the single
window was conducted in Batam in December 2006 and a detailed blueprint for the single window
was releasedin August 2007. A larger pilot is under preparation for Indonesia's largest port(Tanjung
Priok) inDecember2007. Selectionof an operator for the single window is scheduledfor April 2008,
followed by a nationwide launch and integration into an ASEAN-6 Single Window by the end of
2008.

Trigger 4: Enhanced procedure for tariff-setting through implementing better methodology,

112. Import tariffs are generally low in Indonesia, but import licensesand non-tariff barriers
(NTBs) are becoming popular instruments to restrict trade.I2 NTBs and tariff escalation on

budget and lack of career structure, which reduces the incentive to improve performance. The

113. Indonesia's Parliament passed a new Investment Law in March 2007. Two implementing
regulations were subsequently issued in July 2007, one outlining the criteria for formulating the

         D. POLICYAREA11: IMPROVED PUBLICFINANCIAL              MANAGEMENTAND GOVERNANCE
 117. Indonesia continues to make improvements to the management of its public finances.
These efforts are part o f a broader agenda to improve governance and enhance service delivery, with
the ultimate goal o f sustained economic growth and poverty alleviation. Based on the Government
White Paper on Public Financial Management Reforms issued in 2001, the Government presentedto,
and received approval from, Parliament for a troika o f laws (on State Finance, State Treasury and
State Audit) in 2003 and 2004 that provide the framework for P F M modernization in line with
international good practice. Most implementing regulations for the new regulatory framework have
been passed and consecutive rounds o f organizational restructuring, including separation o f budget
and treasury functions have been implemented. Presidential Decree No. 80/2003 on procurement sets
out the basic principles o f procurement: transparency, open and fair competition, economy and
efficiency.   It covers all areas o f procurement that use public funds and is supposed to cover
contracting entities at all levels o f government.

 118. While progress has been made in putting in place a modernized regulatory framework,
substantial challenges remain with regard to re-engineering of business processes across the
 budget cycle to ensure high quality and efficient public spending.           The overall financial
management framework continues to be heavily centralized, input-based and largely reliant on manual
processing o f transactions. Key challenges now pertain to actual implementation o f the modernized
regulatory framework, integration o f planning and budgeting, automation o f manual treasury
processes, financial reporting, accounting and audit, and the full consolidation o f Government
accounts with operating balances. Full implementation o f these complex institutional changes will by
nature require a medium- to long-term time horizon.

 119. The DPL program has been supportingthe implementingdecrees for several of the above
 mentioned laws. Notable progress includes making the national budget compatible with the
 international GFS classification and beginning the consolidation o f government accounts into a
Treasury Single Account (TSA) regime inter alia in preparation for a new automated treasury system
 (SPAN).    These reforms are intended to make important contributions towards increasing the
efficiency and transparency, and reducing corruption in Indonesia's PFM. Strategically chosen, the
DPL 4 actions underpin the broader governance agenda in Indonesia. Under the PFM and governance
heading, DPL 4 supports the introduction of a medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF), the
consolidation o f Government accounts, the establishment o f an independent national procurement
office, increased accountability for RDA and RDI accounts, and the establishment o f a remuneration
commission and a civil service reform plan for the Ministry o f Finance. These actions focus on
 strategic aspects o f the PFM budget cycle including budget preparation, execution and ex-post
accountability. In addition, through the focus on pay and civil service reform, the DPL seeks to
address the underlying reasons for corruption.

120. The budget reform process is at an important stage in its evolution. Progress has been
demonstrated in moving towards a policy-based budget by further integrating the planning and
budgetingprocesses into the 2008 budget preparation cycle. This has resulted in significant budget
re-allocations to priority sectors, particularly infrastructure, education and health, more closely
reflectingGovernment policy priorities as outlinedin the Government's annual work plan(RKP). In
addition, while budget submissions in the past were strictly annual and only included out-turns for
two previous fiscal years, the 2008 budget that was submittedto Parliament in August 2007 included
for the first time aggregate revenue and expenditure forward estimates based on macroeconomic
forecasts. This constitutes a significant step forward in the implementation of a full MTEF.
However, bottom up programmed expenditures for out-years based on multi-year commitments and
foreseeable future implicationsof present spending and/or planned programtargets for out-years, are

because of a lack of drive from the Ministry of Finance. Despite the fact that all line ministries
prepare fixed five-year strategic plans, expenditure forecasts do not include a functional breakdown.

Trigger 10: Continue to consolidatecoregovernment bank accounts (Status: Exceeded)

121. Implementation of a Treasury Single Account regime (TSA) as foreseen by State
Treasury Law No. 1/2004 is ongoing. Zero-balance banking arrangements with commercial
banks have been successfully piloted in 50 selected regionaltreasury offices(KPPN). The tender
for administrationof operational accounts to commercial banks is already completedin all 29 regions
where there are regionaltreasury offices. The roll-outof zero balance arrangementsto expenditure
accounts in all 178 KPPNs occurred on October 1,2007. This consolidates morethan 1,000 balance-
bearing treasury accounts into one balance bearing TSA and associatedzero-balance bank accounts.
Onthe revenue side, currently each KPPNmaintainsaccounts inmore than one of 70 or so authorized
commercial banks that collect revenues. These accounts are scheduled to become zero-balancedby
the end of 2007. Zero balancingof line ministries' Imprest accounts (uangpersediaan) is pending.
Meanwhile, substantial state funds continue to be kept in bank accounts held by both spending units
and state officials at commercial banks. Accordingto the BPK Audit Report 2005, this includesabout
Rp 8.5 trillion held in some 1,300 current and savings accounts that are not recorded in the treasury
system. These off-the-booksfunds not only distortthe consolidatedGovernment cashbalance, but are
also highly vulnerable to embezzlement and corrupt activities. The adoption of the Government
regulation on cash management enacted in July 2007 provides legal backing for a census of all

Trigger I 7: Completethe regulatoryframework for issuance of sub-nationalgovernment bonds.

131. Sub-national borrowing could become an important tool in closing Indonesia's
infrastructure gap, notwithstanding the substantial resources currently available to local
governments. Historically, Indonesian sub-national governments and enterprises have not, however,
borrowed significant amounts for public capital development.         Consequently, borrowing remains
below estimated public infrastructure investment needs required to sustain 6 percent economic
growth.    This suggests that sub-national governments may need to borrow more to finance the
creation o f infrastructure for local public service delivery.       In order to do so, sub-national
governments will need to employ a broader array o f financing mechanisms, including bonds. Sub-
national governments are provided broad cover for issuing bonds by Law No. 33/20004 on Fiscal
Balance and by Government Regulation No. 54/2005 on Regional Borrowing. In addition, the MoF
has already issued further details on the regulatory framework for sub-national government bonds via
Minister o f Finance Decree No. 147/2006, and the MoF's Capital Markets Regulatory Agency
(Bapepam) has released six decrees on various technical aspects related to the issuance o f sub-
national bonds (PBAPEPAM VIII.G.14, VIII.G.15, VII1.G.16, IX.C. 12, IX.C.13 & IX.C. 14). Finally,
the Directorate General o f Fiscal Balance has recently finalized the drafting o f a set o f guidelines for
issuing bonds (Buku Panduan Obligasi Daerah) and placed the guidelines on its website for easy
access by all sub-national governments.

The trigger addressestwo key constraints to these publicly funded CDD programs. The first relates to

               F. TOWARDS      FUTURE  DPLs
133. A continuation of the DPL series offers support to a reform-oriented Government in the
second half of its term, and the potentialto support the transitionto the next Government. The
first series of DPL operations began during a last political transition based on actions spelled out in
the previousGovernment's White Paper. Agreed on actions were accomplishedand a commitment to
the reformprocess was undertaken by the current Government in 2004. The financing predictability
and reform support embodied in the subsequent DPL operations have been instrumental to their
effectiveness. Ifapproved, DPL 5 couldbe concludedwith the existinggovernment in 2008, focusing
on reform progress in the currently identified areas (see below). A DPL 6 would occur during a
political transition andhaveto take this into considerationduring its formulation.

134. The next series of DPLs will fall under the new Country Partnership Strategy (CPS)
covering FY09-FY12. A critical element of the new CPS will be the total lending envelope, the
volume and appropriate mix of projectand program lending, andthe strategic options availablefor the
former and latter. The Government has expresseda desire to continue, and possibly expand, program
lendingto support its medium-termgrowth and social development objectives and to meet its gross
financing needs through a reliable source of finance. While not wanting to prejudge the outcome of
this process, it is likely that program lending will continue to constitute an important business line
under the new CPS.

135. In anticipation of a continued DPL engagement and to provide continuity in our
engagement, a policy program for future operations has been discussed with the Government.
The policy and reformfocus has shiftedfrom macroeconomic reformsto reforms designedto improve
regulations, increase transparency and deepen institutions as requiredto sustain medium-termgrowth
and social development. The investment climate, public financial management and governance,
service deliveryto the poor, and infrastructure(covered by the I-DPL) remainthe highest Government
priorities. The Government continues to request Bank and other partner support through such DPLs
to strategicallyreinforceits own reformagenda.

136. Within the broad and comprehensivereform programa core set of 19 policy actionswere
selected to anchor the policy engagement of a possibleDPL 5 operation. The selection of policy
actions was guided by a desire to continue the reform momentum in given areas and, in some cases,
expand into new but related areas where momentum towards reformwas increasing. The objectives



                                                   51

represent Government policy commitmentswith specific actions formulatedin a dialogue betweenthe
Government and development partners. In important areas the indicative policy actions for DPL 5
deepen policy actions supported under previous DPL operations, for example, moving beyond the
National Single Window pilot into full operation. Inother areas, policy actions aim to strategically
broaden the focus of the policy dialogue into new areas in response to openings and accelerations in
the Government's evolving agenda, for example, working with the Central StatisticsAgency (BPS) to
begin the difficult task of improvingthe targeting of the poor. Consequently, while some areas are
well-advanced and beginningto have an impact on desired outcomes, others are focused on inputs
(such as action plans, laws, or organizationalchanges) and may take longer to translate into progress
on outputs and outcomes (see also section on LessonsLearned).

137. Improving the investment climate remains high on the Government's agenda and the
economic policy package (Inpres No. 6/2007) provides a source of specific policy measures. In
particular, DPL 5 indicative triggers will focus on improvingentry conditionsby following up on the
new Investment Law and negative investment list, as well as improving the ability of the private
sector to voice its concerns. Improving private sector operations will focus on actions designed to
follow up on the new Tax Administration Law, the NationalSingle Window andtariff harmonization.
These objectiveswill be facilitated by improvingthe transparency of entry and service provision. In
addition, DPL 5 will continueto focus on improvingthe operations of financial markets by supporting
improvedinstitutionalstability and strengtheningthe operations of insurancemarkets, a key non-bank
market needed to spread risk and increase the availability of longer-term finance. Finally, the
Government is greatly interested in the economic healthand vitality of small and mediumenterprises
(SMEs) andthe focus here will be on continuingto improvetheir access to credit.

138. Financial management and governance are becoming ever more important in light of
increased fiscal space. Continuity in the next DPL series would involve focusing on actions
designed to maintainmomentum on financial management. Inparticular, this should include a focus
on the Treasury Single Account (underpinningthe new budget andtreasury systemthat should roll out
in 2009), extendingbudgetingreformsto improvingperformance measures, improvingthe profile of
disbursements through measures designed to anticipate multi-year spending, and improving overall
feedback through support for improved accounting by the line ministries. In addition, procurement
remains a critical area where improvements are needed and it would be important to assist the
development and institutionalization of the National Public Procurement Office (NPPO), while
simplifying proceduresthat have also been delayingdisbursements. Finally, it would be beneficialto
push ahead with civil service reforms by extending the pilot at the Ministry of Finance and adding
additionalagencies.

139. Future DPLs series could also continue to develop an increasedfocus on improvementsto
publicservice delivery that are criticalto povertyreductionand other MDG goals. Three broad
areas are under consideration: improving the effectiveness and targeting of pro-poor programs; the
operations of Indonesia's premier Community-DrivenDevelopment (CDD) program; and reinforcing
the teacher-based education reforms introducedin DPL 4. Three activities are proposed inthe area of
improved pro-poor targeting: (i)continue to institutionalize program assessments; (ii)develop an
improved methodology and database for poverty targeting; and (iii)develop better mechanisms to
involve local governments in poverty programs. Improving the operations of CDD would be done
through improvements to the system of community block grants. Education reform would be
continuedthrough improvedmonitoringofteacher performance standards.

          C.        ENVIRONMENTAL,      POVERTY,AND SOCIALIMPACTS
149. Environmental,poverty and social considerationsare particularlyimportant for a loan of
this size. OP 8.60 (Development Policy Lending) mandates the Bank to determine whether policies
supported by the operation are likely to cause significant effects on the country's environment, forests
and other natural resources, as well as incur adverse poverty and social impacts. This program
document provides an in-depth review o f Indonesia's poverty and social conditions (Chapter 11,
Section C), a review o f the poverty and social impacts o f D P L 4-supported reforms (Annex 5),
together with a review o f key environmental issues and the possible impacts o f DPL 4-supported
reforms (Annex 6).

150. A social impactand poverty assessment (Annex 5) found that the proposedDPL 4 triggers
are unlikelyto harm the poor, while several triggers have the potentialto reduce poverty in the
medium to long term. The majority o f the triggers concern policy areas that do not directly affect
the household budgets o f the poor. The links between poverty reduction and areas such as tax and
customs administration, investment procedures, financial sector improvements, and increased
transparency are indirect, through increased economic growth. Bureaucratic barriers to establishing
new businesses, increasing access to finance, and strengthening transparency are all areas that are
constraints to growth in the medium term.

151. Three DPL 4 triggers could affect the poor more directly. Three triggers in the DPL 4
policy matrix are likely to affect the poor in a more direct manner: (i) Changes intariff rates can alter
the purchasing power o f poor households. Tariff reductions in goods that make up a significant share
o f poor households' budgets will benefit the poor by increasing their purchasing power. To mitigate
the risks o f adverse effects in low-income groups, the additional research capacity mandated by the
trigger should enable policy-makers to use empirical estimates o f the impact o f tariff adjustments on
poor households as an input into the policy process. (ii)An effective assessment framework for
selected service delivery can also reduce poverty in the medium to long term.                  The poor
disproportionately suffer from low quality services and low levels o f access to health, education and
infrastructure. For example, only 50 percent o f the bottom quintile complete grade 7 education
compared with 90 percent o f the richest quintile. Similar large inequalities are present in health
outcomes and water access. (iii)Teacher certification also has great potential to improve the quality
o f schools in poor areas, since only 55 percent and 73 percent o f primary and secondary teachers,
respectively, currently meet the minimum qualifications required by the Ministry o f National
Education.

152. An environment impact assessment (Annex 6) of the DPL 4 policy matrix concluded that
of the 18 triggers contained in the DPL 4 policy matrix most have negligible or unlikely
environmentalimpacts. Three items might affect the environment more directly. First, improved
trade policy has the potential to affect the export o f natural resource-based products (which may or
may not be produced sustainably). Tariffs can also affect the affordability or incentives associated
with pollution control equipment or other environment-improving innovations.            To improve the
potential for positive outcomes, the operation could ensure that natural resource and environmental
issues associated with tariff policies are explicitly evaluated, particularly for high-value commodity
crops that affect land use, community livelihoods, and rural environmental conditions, in particular oil
palm. Second, improved investment procedures and reduced business start-up times, while unlikely to
have direct and short-term impacts, may affect certain industries resulting in environmental and equity
impacts, in particular for natural resource industries, extractive industries, and capital intensive firms
vs. labor-absorbing ones.      Third, strengthened small businesses may raise some environmental

           F.       R I S K S

157. Despite a stronger-than-anticipated performance at the beginning of the DPL series,
Indonesia faces a number of risks particularlyas the country moves closer to nationalelections
in 2009. Three major categories of risk can be identified: governance, politicaland economic. All of
these risks could have potential impacts on the DPL program, includingon the reforms achieved in
DPL1-4 and also on any futureDPL series.

Governance risks
158. Budget implementation and fiduciary weaknesses. Although the Government has made
progress in improving governance and combating corruption, fiduciary risks remain significant. A
new set of anti-corruptioninstitutions is actively functioning and several high profile prosecutions
have resulted. Public financial management (PFM) reforms focusing on the budget, treasury and
taxes, and associated institutionalreorganization, have continued with assistance from the Bank and
other development partner programs. However, stricter procedures have created new problems and
increased delays in key processes as officials adjust to a tighter fiduciary environment. Three
principalgovernance and implementationriskscouldimpactthe DPLprogram:
 0   Poor budget implementation.             The Government continues to experience problems in
     implementingits budget effectively. In particular, there remain problems regardingthe pace of
     Government spending, with more than half of the Government's capital and operational budget
     still spent inthe last quarter of each fiscal year.

 0   Delays in progress on procurement. Delays in reform of the procurement process within the
     Government are particularly problematic and affect the Government's ability to deliver on its
     investment program. Prolonged delays in operationalizing and capacity building in a new
     procurement institution, and passinga public procurement law would have an adverse impact on
     the DPLprogram.
     Continuing fiduciary weakness. Although the DPL has supported significant progress in
     strengthening the fiduciary framework, a substantial unfinishedagenda remains   -one   that will
     require continued attention in future DPL operations. In particular, failure to make progress in
     areas such as off-budgetaccounts, procurement and auditing, increasingthe risksofmalfeasance,
     could adverselyimpactthe DPL program.

159. This operation continues its focus on PFMand broadergovernance reforms. Built around
the fiduciary reforms in the Government FinancialManagementand Revenue AdministrationProject
(GFMRAP), extensive Analytical, Advisory Assistance ( M A ) and Technical Assistance (TA)
projects, the DPL program has supported policy-makers evaluate the impact of reforms, assess
remaining bottlenecks and focus on a selected crucial reforms. In DPL 4, the Government has
achieved more efficient budget implementation. DPL 5 is expected to continue this process and
furthermitigaterisks.

Political risks
160. The coming 2009 elections. The 2009 legislative and presidential elections will put pressure
on the administration to deliver demonstrable results, especially in its priority areas o f infrastructure
development, governance reform, poverty reduction and the investment climate.              However, the
elections also give incentives to the coalition partners inthe current six-party coalition government to
strengthen their political appeal by distinguishing themselves from the Government, making policy
implementation more difficult. The elections pose the following risksto the DPL program:
     Infrastructure investments fail to maintain fiduciary standards and investment climate
     improvements. The pressure for infrastructure investments could lead the Government to move
     to quickly and without the systems necessary to sustain the improving fiduciary and investment
     climate.
     Pre-election breakdown of Government coalition. Centrifugal forces within the coalition
     could derail implementation o f the Government's program, especially in those areas that require
     legislative approval. For example, the departure o f a major coalition partner, such as Golkar, to
     field its own presidential candidate could produce a political stalemate in the pre-election period.
     Politicians focus only on the short term.           Elections often reduce the time horizons o f
     politicians, leading them to avoid new policy initiatives in the pre-election period and shifting
     their focus to short-term deliverables. This could make it difficult to agree on the longer-term
     agenda for the D P L series.
     Anti-debt sentiment. Concerns remain over the use o f foreign loans to support the Government
     budget. This has generated opposition to foreign loans, in particular from, but not limited to,
     civil society groups. Anti-debt sentiment is also found in the legislature, where despite having
     explicitly agreed to program lending, including the DPL, several key political parties hold
     positions designed to limit foreign debt exposure.       Such sentiment raises potential risks for
     budget support operations such as the DPL, as the legislature seeks to play a stronger role in
     overseeing foreign borrowing.


161. However, there are potentially positive aspects of the forthcoming election. A number o f
politicians and bureaucrats may push to complete established reform agendas to position themselves
for roles in the next government. The likelihood o f continuity in Government also provides a positive
incentive to push ahead with reforms.

Economic risks
162. External vulnerabilities and international oil prices.                With improvements in its
macroeconomic indicators, Indonesia's resilience to external shocks has increased. However,
economic risks remain. The following economic risks could impact the DPL program:
 0  Global growth and trade slow-down. Inthe past two years, Indonesia's growth has been driven
    by its export performance. A global growth and trade slow-down, triggered by a sub-prime led
    recession in the US, or high international oil prices, would translate into lower Indonesian growth
    as direct exports to affected markets fall, indirect exports (commodities) to the region fall and
    commodity prices will weaken (reducing the terms o f trade gain). Depending on the magnitude
    and duration o f the slow-down, this would also slowjob creation in Indonesia.
    Poor policy choices in response to global slowdown. Poor policy choices in this environment,
    especially those that derailed macroeconomic stability and reform momentum, could compound
    the situation.




                                                    61

 0  Major outflow of assets from Indonesia. Another potential risk in this situation comes from a
    sudden reversal o f financial assets held in the Indonesian stock and bond markets. Such an event
    would feed into the exchange rate fluctuations and inflation, raising domestic tensions.

 0  High international oil prices unaccompanied by domestic fuel price rises. Indonesia raised
    domestic fuel prices by more than 150 percent in 2005 and significantly reduced its vulnerability
    to international oil prices. Nevertheless, large subsidies remain, especially in fuel and electricity.
    At present, marginal changes inworld prices are neutral to the budget deficit with higher revenues
     off-setting subsidies.         However, significantly higher sustained international oil prices not
     accompanied by domestic fuel price increases would generate a substantial increase in subsidies
     and raise fiscal concerns as fuel substitution and smuggling mount.


163. The Government is aware of these economic risks and in a better position to address them
than in the past. Indonesia's economy is more robust now than at any time since the Asian financial
crisis, with higher reserves, less foreign exchange denominated debt, and a stronger banking system.
Nevertheless, while the Government is cognizant o f the economic risks faced by the country, more
could be done to reduce the impact o f external economic shocks, such as operationalizing the
Financial Sector Stability Forum.

                          Recent Developments and Short-Term Prospects

Economic growth reached 5% percent in 2006 as the dip in activity following the late 2005 fuel
price and interest rate hikes proved shallow. Strong exports compensated for weak domestic
demand. Notwithstanding heightened risks to the outlook from a possible slowing of US. growth,
staff still projects GDP growth at around 6 percent in 2007 with some shift from externalto domestic
demand. Inflation has remained broadly stable around 6 percent in 2007-the       middle of the 2007
target range of 5-7 percent-allowing Bank Indonesia (BI) to lower its policy rate further to 8%
percent. The external current account surplus is projected to narrow from 2% percent of GDP to
around 1% percent in 2007 as lower global growth and commodity prices reduce exports.The central
government deficit increased modestly to 1.0 percent of GDP in 2006. For 2007, staff projects a
somewhat higher deficit of around 1.6-1.8 percent of GDP.

Along with other emerging markets, Indonesia's equity and bond markets were hit by the
recent turmoil in global financial markets. The extent of the sell-off was relatively high in a
regional comparison, but it followed a prolonged period of increases, driven by the abundant global
risk appetite as well as domestic factors such as declining interest rates and perceptions of reduced
vulnerability and improving economic prospects. Portfolio capital flows mirroredthese developments
as foreign investors increased their positions in Indonesian securities during most of 2007, then
reduced exposure since midJuly. After appreciating in May vis-a-vis the US dollar in response to
portfolio inflows, the rupiahhas subsequently fallen by around 7 percent (3 % percent since the Article
N consultationinmid-July), butremainswithin arangeconsistent withexternalcompetitivenessand
macroeconomic stability.

Banking sector indicators have strengthened, although the impact on balance sheets from the
recent market turmoil has yet to be fully assessed. Over the past year, bank profitability has
improved.The bankingsystem is well capitalizedand nonperformingloans are low for private banks.
State banks' non-performing loans have fallen significantly but still remain higher. Overall, with the
improving indicators, the banking system appears to be in a relatively good position to weather the
recent global financial market turmoil. Direct exposure to collateralized debt obligations (CDOs) or
other structured credit products appears minimal and Indonesian financial markets have so far
remained sufficiently liquid duringperiodsof marketpressure.

Similarly, external vulnerability indicators have improved over the past year, helping the
economy to weather recent volatility. Fiscal deficits are modest and public debt is declining, the
external current account is in surplus, and the exchange rate is broadly marketdetermined, with some
symmetric market intervention to smooth volatility and automatic purchases of the government's
foreign exchange receipts. The ratio of total external debt to GDP is expected to decline to less than
20 percent of GDP by 2012, from 35 percent in 2006, while government debt (including domestic
debt) is projectedto decline from 39 percent of GDP to 26 percent over the same period. A steady
build-up of reserves and lower external borrowing have increased the ratio of reserves to short term
debt to morethan 150 percent.




                                                  80

Nonetheless, downside risks remain to the positive outlook, in particular related to the global
financial market turmoil and its impact on global growth. While reducedvulnerabilitieshave put
the Indonesian economy in a good positionto weather moderate external shocks (see adverse macro
scenarios in the 2007 Article IV staff report), a sharper than expected slowdown in global economic
growth and a sustained reduction in risk appetite could have a significant negative impact on the
Indonesian economy. The main risks arise from potentially lower export demand and commodity
prices, reducedaccess to financing and higher financingcosts, and possible balance sheet impacts of
lower asset prices and a depreciatedrupiah.

                               Macroeconomicand Structural Policies

The Government continues to implement its comprehensive reform agenda to boost growth
over the medium term. Key elements of the strategy include policies to strengthen financial
intermediation, improve the business climate, and increase infrastructure investment. While
substantial progress has been achieved, includingpassing of a new investment law and several fiscal
reforms, other measures,particularlywith respectto the labor market, haveencounteredresistance.

    With respect to monetary policy, Bank Indonesia (BI) has responded flexibly to shocks to
    inflation and output, while building the credibility of its newly established inflation
    targeting framework. The last two policy meetingssaw a pause in the current rate-cuttingcycle,
    in response to the recent weakening in the rupiah, a slight increase in headline inflation, and
    uncertaintyover the fall-out from the global financial turmoil. Staffjudge that room for further
    rate cuts will remainlimited, particularly since the full effect of recent rate cuts i s likely still to be
    felt andthe currency has shown some renewedweakness inrecentmonths.

    In the staff's view, the recent relaxation of prudential regulations to encourage lending is
    not likely to have much impact on lending growth. At the same time, the steps represent a
    departure from internationalstandards and risk havingan adverse signalingeffect on supervisors
    and banks alike. With bank lending already increasing, in the staffs view BI should reconsider
    the appropriatenessof the measuresas soon as possible.Moralsuasionto encouragebanksto lend
    to specific sectors could result in credit misallocationand should also be avoided. Instead, staff
    hasencouragedthe authoritiesto strengthen efforts to promote the developmentof capitalmarkets
    and instruments of longer-termfinancing and to develop a strategy on the future role of state
    banks.

    The authorities' projected fiscal stance is appropriate.The modest relaxationin deficit targets
    for 2007 and 2008 provides some room for additional development spending while ensuring
    further decliningdebt ratios.Further improvements intax administration,some newtax measures,
    and the streamliningof expenditures, includingthrough greater flexibility of energy prices, could
    create additional space for priority spending, such as infrastructure, health, and education.
    Enhancingthe implementationcapacity of localgovernmentwould also contributeto this effort.

    The authorities have made good progress in fiscal reform. Recent achievements include the
    restructuringofthe tax agency andthe adoption of a keytax reformbill. The plannedintroduction
    of a fiscal risk statement andthe medium-termfiscal framework inthe 2008 budget shouldhelpto
    further strengthenfiscal management.

                         Annex 5     Poverty and SocialImpactAssessment

                                             Introduction

Overallthe operation is unlikely to have any immediate distributionalimpacts. Both the Povertyand
Social ImpactAssessment and the EnvironmentalImpactAssessment (see Annex 6) measure impacts
based on the two standards of "do no harm" and "do more good".                When viewed from this
perspective, this operation is unlikely to have any significant adverse impact(do no harm) and is also
unlikely to produce any immediate distributional impacts (do more good). The main focus of the
operation is threefold: improvingIndonesia's investment climate though a variety of institutionaland
process reforms (e.g. Tim Tariff and PEPI); raising the standard of public financial management
through the implementationof technical improvements to the budget systems; and developingmore
effectiveassessmentmethods with the ultimateaim of improvingservice deliveryto the poor.
However, several measures will have positive medium-termimpacts on poverty. While the overall
short-term impact of the operation is neutral, there are nonetheless several policy actions that have the
potential to deliver positive impacts on poverty over the medium term. For example, enhancing the
procedures for tariff-setting should create an opportunity to make existing tariffs more progressive.
With the implementationof the new Investment Law, the hope is that by encouraging higher levels of
investment this in turn will support economic growth, job creation and positive poverty outcomes.
Similarly, the simplification of the business licensing procedures will promote competition, once
again supportinggrowth with positive poverty outcomes. The implementationof the Government's
policy package as it pertains to SMEs also has the potential to reduce poverty incidence by
encouragingeconomic growththrough improvingSMEs' access to finance. The consolidationof core
Government bank accounts could potentially reduce corruption and increase local government
outlays, indirectly reducingpoverty. In the area of service delivery, the teacher certification program
should improve educational outcomes for the poor, while reform of PDAMs could have positive
poverty outcomes if these result in an expansion of water delivery services. Also, enabling local
governments to issue bonds could allow greater investment in high-quality infrastructure projects,
helping to connect the poor to markets and reducing the costs of transportation. Finally, allowing
CCD programs to spend over multiyear periods will allow for greater flexibility in the selection and
design of CDD projects, which largely benefit poor households. In general, the reallocation of the
budget towards poverty and infrastructure and poverty programs should register positive poverty
outcomes in the medium term. The impact matrix on the following page provides a more detailed
analysis ofthese impacts.

Going forward, the World Bank and its partners will continue to engage in analysis of the impacts of
the DPL reform agenda on poverty and development. These include the 2006 Indonesia Poverty
Assessment (Making the New Indonesia Workfor the Poor) and recent researchon the rates of return
of education spending (Investing in Indonesia`sEducation).

                         Annex 6      EnvironmentalImpactAssessment

           1.Environmentand NaturalResourcesManagement:Context and Issues

Natural resources are an important contributor to Indonesia's GDP and the Government budget.
As Indonesia returns to political stability and robust economic growth, the time is right to begin
considering ways to improve the quality o f growth by addressing environmental and equity issues
in the broader context of improved transparency and accountability of governance. Agriculture,
forestry, and mining contribute about 25 percent o f Indonesia's GDP and about 30 percent o f
overall Government budget revenue. Yet, Indonesia's macroeconomic policies appear to favor
resource depletion over sustainable use as they reward district governments based on resource
revenue as opposed to performance or stewardship.

Energy policy, forest sector practices and climate change issues are intricately linked in
Indonesia.    Fossil fuels dominate energy consumption in both rural and urban areas, and

the second-largest Green House Gas emitter among developing countries in the region - it
Indonesia is gradually increasing the proportion o f energy produced from coal. Indonesia i s also

generates nearly one-tenth o f the world's greenhouse gases, mostly from logging and
foresthwamp fires. National energy policies propose to increase reliance on renewable energy

reduce Indonesia's dependence on oil imports - a move that could lead to significant negative
sources, but at the same time the Government also plans a large scale-up in the use o f coal to

environmental impacts.

Poor environmental management is hurting both the poor and the economy.               The costs o f
pollution and inadequate waste management to the Indonesian economy are high: total economic
losses attributable to limited access to safe water and sanitation are conservatively estimated at 2
percent o f GDP annually. Annual costs o f air pollution to the Indonesia economy have been
calculated at around US$400 million a year. The economic cost o f the health effects o f pollution
in Jakarta alone is estimated at US$700 million a year. These costs are typically borne

5. Longer-TermEnvironmentalChallenges
While there is recognition among development partners and in Government o f the importance o f a
broad spectrum o f environmental challenges, recent progress is clearly skewed towards some sectors
rather than others, in particular the `green' and `blue' agendas. The advances that have been made in
these sectors must be consolidated and built upon, but there i s a need also to redress the balance
towards the equally important issues o f pollution control, urban environmental management, and
environmental health and risk mitigation. Furthermore, community based management approaches
need to be supported by a revamping o f Indonesia's public environmental management system,
including strengthening the implementation o f environmental regulations and adapting regulatory and
administrative procedures to decentralization.
Current interest in, and Government attention towards, climate change is important; it should be seen
as an opportunity to continue mainstreaming key environmental issues such as: (i)  an improvement o f
the legal and institutional framework for environmental management, which remains ineffective and
inconsistently applied, largely because its objectives remain undervalued; (ii)establishment o f
reasonable and achievable goals to manage Indonesia's forest resources as prospects for positive
change are significantly better now than in the past; (iii)drafting o f regulations and support for
artisinal and small-scale mining, which is leading to soil erosion, sedimentation o f water bodies, and
increasing human health impacts associated with mercury and cyanide pollution; and (iv) increase
support to public awareness campaigns (formal and informal, national and local), to help stimulate
interest and concern among the general public and key decision-makers about key environmental and
NRM issues.       As in the health and education sectors, informed and aware environmental
constituencies can put political pressure on local and national governments for improved
environmental performance and service delivery. This is an opportunity for broad-based engagement
and national level progress that should not be overlooked.

                             5. Experience in Implementing Budget Reforms
The fiscal roles o f the executive and legislative branches are now defined in law and evolving in
practice. Fiscal relationships between the executive and legislature are defined in the State Finance
Law No.17/2003. The House o f Representatives (DPR) is the principal legislative body and it plays a
significant role in shaping the budget and fiscal policy. The budget is passed in agreement between
the President and the DPR. The process involves an examination by the DPR o f the macroeconomic
framework, macro-fiscal policies and the detailed budget allocations.               The relative roles o f the
executive and legislative branches inthis area are evolving.

The laws governing budget management clearly specify the responsibilities o f the Minister o f Finance
and comprehensive regulations have been developed.                   While the President, as head o f the
Government, has overall authority to exercise national fiscal management, Law No. 17/2003 clearly
delegates the responsibility o f overall fiscal management o f central government finances to the
Minister o f Finance as chief financial officer. The law also assigns responsibilities to individual
ministers, governors and other local authorities for financial management and accountability in their
jurisdictions.

Provisions o f the new regulations are being implemented.              The budget classification is largely
consistent with contemporary international standards.          The MoF is developing plans for a medium-
term expenditure framework (MTEF) to guide budget policy.                Forward estimates at the aggregate
level have been introduced for 2009 and 2010 with the budget for 2008. However, much more work
needs to be done to fully operationalize the MTEFapproach.

                                      6. Reforming Budget Execution
Indonesia still spends 50 percent o f its total capital expenditure in the final quarter o f the year.24 For
the past five years, spending has always started slowly and then accelerated towards the end o f each
year. This spending pattern i s o f concern because project implementation is disrupted by an adverse
cycle. Moreover, under-spending on capital expenditure constrains increases in infrastructure
investments. Slow and back-loaded disbursements are symptoms o f more severe challenges that are
encountered at each stage o f the public expenditure management cycle.

The Government maintains a comparatively rigid budget execution process. Detailed input controls
aim to ensure that the composition o f the budget complies with political priorities and that the budget
is not altered duringexecution. Spending warrants (DIPAs), while now issued at the start of the fiscal
year, contain excessive amounts o f detail leaving little flexibility for adjustments in the composition
o f inputs needed to carry out a given activity. Re-allocations across DIPAs from delayed programs to
better performing ones that could enhance satisfactory implementation o f the overall expenditure
program require lengthy revision, sometimes involving Parliament.                   Such inflexibility creates
practical difficulties and inefficiencies.

With expanding fiscal space, public investment is increasing, putting additional pressure on PFM
systems. Planning and executing public investment is inherently more demanding than current or
mandatory expenditure.

                                        8. ProcurementReforms
The past few years have witnessed improvements in the public procurement environment.
Presidential Decree (Keppres) No. 80/2003 provided a national public procurement regulation that

meets most o f what is generally regarded as accepted international practice, including basic
principles: transparency, open and fair competition, economy, and efficiency.       This decree also
requires that a national public procurement office (NPPO) be established as a regulatory body for
public procurement. With the introduction o f Keppres No. 80/2003, a previous certification ("pre-
qualification") process for suppliers/ providers o f goods and services was, in most instances,
abandoned by the Government. This has previously led to segmentation o f the market, especially at
the provincial level.    Keppres No. 80/2003 also established the basis for sanctions, complaint-
handling and requirements for certification o f users.

However, the public procurement system still has significant deficiencies in its regulatory and, more
importantly, implementation aspects. These are mainly the delays in establishing a strong regulatory
body; slow progress in the development o f procurement regulation anchored by a law; slow progress
in the development of standard tools in terms of bidding documents and users manuals; weakness in
procurement capacity in implementing agencies, especially at the provincial and district levels; and
collusion and corruption practices in the bidding process and concerns over the efficiency o f anti-
corruption and sanction measures.

A new presidential decree has been drafted that, when issued, will establish a fully independent
Goods/Services Procurement Policy Development Institution (Lembaga Pengembangan Kebijakan
Pengadaan Pemerintah, or LPKPP). The planned institution will be chaired by an appointee from the
civil service (non-political), who may be assisted by a committee comprising procurement experts
sourced from both inside and outside government. The establishment o f the LPKPP, in itself, should
not be regarded as reforming public sector procurement. Rather, it should be perceived as the first
crucial steppingstone towards enabling a focus on a wider public procurement reform program.

Laws enacted since 2000 impact, or make reference to, public procurement cover construction
(governing civil works), state finance, treasury, audit, and small-scale business.      The pace o f
Indonesia's commendable decentralization reforms has impacted public sector procurement at all
levels o f government, with ministers, governors, and even mayors able to issue decrees, regulations,
and instructions. The plethora o f regulations is often inconsistent and many regulations do not meet
accepted international practice. Consequently, national procurement reform and the need for national
procurement policies and standards have become urgent.

Indonesia's legal framework for public sector procurement can best be strengthened by anchoring it
with an overarching consolidated and comprehensive national public sector procurement law at the
highest level that (i)establishes the fundamental principles and procedures applicable to all public
sector procurement, (ii)allows for the imposition o f sanctions where the principles and procedures,
particularly those relating to good governance and ethics, are not met, (iii)amends other laws that
refer to public sector procurement, and (iv) ensures that such a law has the necessary authority in a
decentralized environment. Drafts o f a new high-level law and associated subordinate regulations are
beingfinalized and should be ready by the end o f 2007.

                          9. Accountingand Reportingon Budget Execution
The aggregate Government Annual Financial Statements have been prepared in a timely manner for
three years now (2004, 2005 and 2006). Although the timeliness is a significant achievement, serious
concerns remain over their reliability. The BPK has for three years issued a "disclaimer" opinion on
these financial statements due, among various factors, to serious shortcomings in the control
environment in which these statements were prepared and due to scope limitations.

The aggregate annual financial statements present revenues, expenditures                 and transfers,
surplus/deficit, and financing aggregates, as compared with budget provisions. A balance sheet and a
cashflow statement are also presented, along with a full statement o f government accounting policy.

The recent development o f Government Accounting Standards is a step forward in improving
financial reporting, although the requirement o f the State Treasury Law o f the complete introduction
o f accrual accounting by 2008 is very ambitious and may not be realistic. The Accounting Standards
Committee (KSAP) has been in operation for a few years now duringwhich time it has put into place
a due process for development o f Government accounting standards that are well aligned with
comparable international standards (IPSAs). Technical capacity constraints limit the pace at which
these accounting standards can be implemented at all levels o f government, including line ministries
and sub-national governments.

Monthly and quarterly reports on budget realization are produced by each ministry on a monthly basis
and sent to the MoF for consolidation.              A mid-year un-audited report on budget realization is
presented to the DPR inJuly and is available to civil society.

There are no systematic and publicly available reports o f the Government's contingent liabilities, tax
expenditures or quasi-fiscal activities. Data on central government debt are regularly published, but
there are time-lags in reporting total debt and concerns over the reliability o f the data on external
public debt. Information on government financial assets is published annually.

Although commendable action has been taken to improve government financial reporting, there
continue to be serious challenges in making government financial statements reliable, as reported by
the BPK in its annual audit report for 2006. This reflects the immense challenges that lie ahead in an
environment where accounting capacity at the grassroots is weak and accounting processes are not yet
integrated or fully automated.

external state audit agency, granting it extensive rights o f audit over public finance, generally in line
with international standards (Lima Declaration). Inpractice, however, some restrictions continue to

                                 11. Internal Controls and InternalAudit
The internal audit framework o f the Government is extensive but scope and organization do not match
needs. Ironically, even with (or perhaps because of) multiple internal audit agencies in existence, the
internal audit function continues to be a weak link in governance. The internal audit function is
carried out at central government by three institutions: the Inspectorate General (IG) in each spending
ministry; the government-wide internal auditor BPKP (Badan Pengawasan Keuangan dan
Pengembangan), reporting to the President's office, with government-wide jurisdiction; and the
Bawasda at the local government level.

This institutional structure is complex and the mandates and division o f labor between the various
internal audit institutions are unclear. The IG represents the principal internal audit apparatus o f a
ministry. The scope o f IGaudit activities is generally limited to technical compliance audits. The IGs
therefore operate as individual institutions corresponding to the number o f ministries. Professional
backgrounds of IG staff therefore normally include technical qualifications and not accounting or
auditing. Audits by IGs are not conducted on a risk-based methodology. Local governments maintain
their own internal audit units (the Bawasda).

BPKP's staffing levels do not reflect its reduced mandate. Despite this reduced mandate, it maintains
fully staffed decentralized offices in 26 provinces. The BPKP no longer has a formal role in internal
audit, except to provide audit services to line ministriesupon request.

Each line ministry plays the role o f program executing agency and is therefore principally responsible
for maintaining internal controls. It i s responsible for performing and achieving the objectives stated
in budget allotment documents. In order to strengthen responsibility and accountability over the
appropriated budget by the line ministries, the function o f payment order issuance has been
transferred to the line ministries, resulting in a better segregation o f the functions for verifying

                       12. ReformingRegionalPublic FinancialManagement
Decentralization has provided local governments with significant resources and authority. This
requires an adequate regulatory framework and PFM capacity at the local level. The PFM reforms
stipulated in the decentralization and subsequent finance and treasury legislation aims to modernize
the regional PFM system and improve accountability, if efficiently implemented, but these reforms
are vast in scope and the concepts are sophisticated.

The regulatory framework for regional PFM reforms is largely in place and includes mechanisms for
fiscal transfers to local governments.      After decentralization, the central government passed
comprehensive legislation for PFM reforms at the regional level to mirror the changes being made at
the centre.

However, most regions lack adequate technical and human resources to implement the reforms and
the central government has not yet provided the required support. The unclear division o f tasks
between the MoF and M o H A has resulted in inconsistent and contradictory legislation with regards to
regional financial management and this has caused confusion among most local governments. While
sub-national governments are obliged by law to report certain fiscal and financial information to the
central government, many do not (the data may be missingor simply withheld voluntarily).

                                     13. State-Owned Enterprises
Indonesia has a sound legal framework for corporate governance and monitoring SOE operations.
First, the SOE Law No. 19/2003 sets a clear ownership policy as the state is not allowed to be
involved in the day-to-day management o f SOEs and must allow them full operational autonomy.
Second, a 2002 ministerial decree clearly details corporate governance responsibilities, including the
need to: (i) submit each quarter the SOEs' financial statements to the State Ministry for State-Owned
Enterprises and to the appropriate line ministry; (ii)prepare annual financial statements that must be
audited internally and by an independent auditor; and (iii)publish SOE annual reports in a timely
manner.

Existing laws and regulations governing SOEs are not effectively implemented. Long delays still
occur in auditing and publishing SOE financial statements. SOE annual reports are usually only
regularly and systematically made public in cases where companies have accessed the local capital
markets with listed instruments.

The planned use o f PPPs to accelerate investment will create fiscal risks that merit disclosure. The
Government has taken steps to mitigate fiscal risks from PPPs. Presidential Regulation No. 67/2005
emphasizes careful project preparation and the selection o f bidders in an open, competitive tender and
gives the Minister o f Finance the rightto approve or reject requests for Government support.

                                        14. Debt Management
Two major initiatives in debt management have been launched. Despite recent improvements in debt
indicators, risks to the Government's budget remain substantial and further improvements in debt
management are essential to avoid future debt distress. The MoF has made good progress in this

regard. Two specific examples are the development and publication of a comprehensive debt
management strategy in September 2005 and the decision to create a DGfor Public DebtManagement
(DGDM). The Government is now workingon a more formal debt sustainabilityanalysis.

                               15. Governance and Anti-Corruption
The Anti-Corruption Commission (KPK) is fully operational and performs its duties independently,
free from any influence.      It coordinates with other entities combating corruption, conducts
investigationsand prosecutions against corrupt acts, performs preventative actions against corruption,
and monitors state governance. It has initiated and signed memoranda of understandingwith some
provincial and local governments to establish and support good governance at the sub-national level.
As its capacity to carry out prosecutions is limited to a few cases per year, its focus tends to be on
high-levellarge-scale corruption.

Following initial years of analyticalwork, Indonesiahastaken important steps inrecent years to build

ConcIusion
Two important results have been achieved by the Government in improving the investment climate.
First, the goal o f attaining an investment-to-GDP ratio o f between 23 and 25 percent has been
achieved, with a ratio o f 23.75 percent in the second quarter o f 2007. Second, the goal o f growing
non-banking financial institutions' (NBFI) assets to be 5-10 percentage points higher than the 15
percent level o f assets in the financial sector estimated in 2003 has also been achieved, with NBFI
assets comprising 22.3 percent o f total financial sector assets. However, the target o f reducing the
start-up time for new businesses down to 30 days, always a very demanding goal, as not been
achieved.

Despite this unachieved target, the DPL has nonetheless been effective in supporting the
Government's own economic policy packages, which have placed substantial emphasis on
streamlining company formation and business licensing. The DPL has helped the Government focus
attention on efforts to improve the business environment and has been an effective instrument for
promoting inter-ministerial agreement to push for difficult reforms opposed at lower levels o f the
bureaucracy.

Going forward towards a new DPL series, continued efforts are needed to streamline investment
procedures, including a comprehensive regulatory reform program. Further simplification o f tax
administration will be required, including a shift o f tax audit resources away from their almost
exclusive focus on refund claims towards more productive uses. Port and customs reform are needed
to further reduce import clearance time and curb corruption.

                                           A. INDONESIA'S           VULNERABILITY

Indonesia i s one o f the world's most disaster-prone countries. The September 2007 8.4 magnitude
earthquake and aftershocks that hit West Sumatra were a reminder o f Indonesia's vulnerability.
Located at the conjunction o f three tectonic plates, Indonesia has a very high exposure to seismic
activity. The 2006 "hotspot study" (jointly undertaken by the World Bank and Columbia University)
identified Indonesia as beingamong the most vulnerable countries in the world, with 40 percent o f the
population facing a high mortality risk from multiple hazards. With close to 200 volcanoes, 70 o f
which are categorized as "very active", the country has the highest number o f active volcanoes in the
world. Furthermore, Indonesia regularly experiences mudslides, floods and earthquakes. Flooding
presents a particular risk and tends to have the highest impact on GDP and mortality. Wildfires are
also a considerable risk, as demonstrated inthe 1998 fires that occurred duringthe ElNiiio event and
were the major source o f greenhouse gas emissions that year. Java faces the highest risk in terms o f
casualties; Sumatra and Java are the islands that suffer the greatest negative economic impact.

Over the past ten years, there has been at least one major disaster each year. Since 1997, Indonesia
has experienced 25 natural disasters with death tolls in excess o f 50 people. Of the four major
categories o f natural disaster, earthquakes and tsunamis account for the largest number o f casualties.
The most extreme events were the December 2004 tsunami in Aceh and the May 2006 earthquake in
Yogyakarta and Central Java. Flooding and landslides followed, with disaster locations spread evenly
across Indonesia, from western Indonesia to the eastern extremities, and from urban to the rural areas.
The economic cost ofjust the Aceh and Yogyakarta events amounted to US$7.6 billion.

For many Indonesians, disasters have become a regular feature o f their daily lives and the country will
be even more exposed to multi-hazards in the future. With the climate trend pointing towards more
extreme conditions and increased climate variability, Indonesia will become ever more vulnerable to
climate-related disaster risks. The Government o f Indonesiahas taken the first steps in addressing the
issue by preparing a national action plan for disaster reduction and passing the Law on Disaster
Management. Going forward, this first step needs to be carried further with the full empowerment
and implementation of the action plan to streamline the risks. This could also help towards
minimizing the cost o f disaster response, which can be huge. For example, it is estimated that about
US$2.5 billion was allocated from the central government budget in 2006 and 2007 in response to the
disasters in Aceh and Yogyakarta.

Inlate 2006, the Government issueda national action plan for disaster risk reduction to address the
country's perennial challenge o f managing natural disasters and to go beyond an emergency response
mode. This was followed in March 2007, when Parliament adopted long-anticipated legislation on
disaster management, including measures first discussed more than two years ago after the December
2004 tsunami. The new law establishes a National Disaster Management Agency to coordinate efforts
to reduce disaster risks in advance and to provide leadership during an emergency. The agency will
report directly to the President and will determine what constitutes a disaster. In such an event, it will
be given extraordinary powers, including the right to tap resources, financial and material, from any
other government ministry. Meanwhile, Indonesia has installed 90 tsunami early warning systems
across the country with 70 more tsunami detecting devices to be installed by 2008. The Government
had also installed seven weather radars to serve as an early weather monitoring system in several
areas such as Jakarta, Medan, Surabaya and Palembang. Twenty-two more radar units are needed to
cover the entire country.



                                         Law on Disaster Management
The Indonesian Parliament has achieved a major breakthrough by passing Law on Disaster Management in
March 2007 after a long process of consultation and review. Under the new law Indonesia will establish a
National DisasterManagementAgency to engage in comprehensive disaster risk reduction and responsethrough
both routine sectoral programs and specially earmarked finds in both national and local budgets. The law is
based on the premise that protection from disaster hazards is a basic rights of the population, and confirms the
role of the Government in ensuringsuch protection throughthe integrationof disaster risk reduction into national
development programming, includingthe allocation of sufficient funds for this purpose.
The development o f the bill and implementation of the law is in line with Indonesia's broader commitment
towards realizing the 2005 Hyogo Framework for mainstreaming risk reduction into development planning.
Having signed the framework, the Government launched the Strategic National Action Plan on Disaster Risk
Reductionin January 2007. Based on the Action Planthe Government has now included disaster risk reduction
to be one of eight national development priorities for 2008 and beyond, which has also been used by an
increasingnumber of local governments as abasis for the development of local plans.
Having taken the significant step of passing the bill, the implementationof the law is now the key challenge.
Presidentialand governmentalregulations are being finalized for approval; the Agency need to be drafted and
approved; the Agency and its provincial level equivalents need to be created and empowered; and much work
remains to be done to ensure that the provisions of the law are widely disseminated to responsible government
officials and the general public. So far, the implementationof the law is proceedingahead of schedule, i.e. the
Agency is expectedto become operationalbefore the deadline.

                       B.RECONSTRUCTIONINACEH,NIASANDYOGYAKARTA

The December 2004 tsunami devastated Aceh and was followed by an earthquake in March 2005 in
the Nias islands, together leaving a total o f 170,000 dead and the economy and infrastructure o f the
two regions in tatters. The reconstruction program that followed was the largest ever seen in the
developing world. At the end o f 2006, half o f the US$5.8 billion in funds allocated to projects had
been disbursed. The health and education sectors recovered rapidly to pre-tsunami levels, while the
more challenging housing sector experienced a somewhat later acceleration with an estimated 60,000
houses rebuilt by the end o f December 2006. The recovery o f the economy and livelihoods o f those
affected i s proving more challenging and receiving renewed attention midway into the reconstruction
effort.

The devastation from the tsunami, coupled with the changing attitudes o f both the central government
and the separatist movement, is largely credited with bringing an end to the conflict in Aceh. The
President received international praise for signing a peace deal in 2005 with separatist rebels, which
has held firm to date. The peace deal officially ended a brutal 29-year conflict between the central
government and the Free Aceh Movement (GAM), and opened the way to a consolidation o f the peace
process.

Reconstruction programs are driving economic growth in Aceh, which was still relatively modest in
2006, at 2.4 percent (including the declining oil and gas sector). Other usually dominant sectors
(agriculture, manufacturing and mining) have yet to recover from the combined effects o f the tsunami
and conflict, and these sectors must expand their outputs to create sustainable growth and
employment moving forward. Domestic investment has improved significantly (to 14 percent o f
provincial GDP). However, this is still significantly lower than the average national investment level
o f 22 percent. Unemployment remains one o f the greatest challenges in Aceh, estimated at over 10
percent, although this declined slightly in the first two quarters o f 2007. Inflation has continued to
decline, falling to 8.5 percent by June 2007 (year-on-year). However, the two years o f high inflation
post-tsunami have resulted in a higher consumer price index and higher nominal wages than inthe rest
o f Indonesia, putting pressure on the competitiveness o f the local economy. Given the pace o f
reconstruction, it is likely that this will continue to be the driving force behind economic growth in
Aceh in the near term.

Despite a slight increase in poverty levels in the aftermath o f the tsunami, poverty fell significantly in
Aceh in 2006 to 26.5 percent, below the pre-tsunami level o f 28.4 percent in 2004, but this level
remains substantially higher than in Indonesia as a whole. Poverty in Aceh is predominantly a rural
phenomenon, with about 30 percent o f rural households living below the poverty line compared with
less than 15 percent o f households in urban areas. By 2006, poverty in tsunami and non-tsunami
affected areas seemed to be equal, but households in conflict-affected areas are still more likely to be
poor than households in areas not affected by the conflict.

While Aceh's record in recovering from the disaster is strong, the Yogyakarta reconstruction effort
has set a new standard. From the available funding o f close to US$900 million, almost 150,000
houses (more than half o f the requirement) were rehabilitated less than one year after the devastating
Yogyakarta earthquake in May 2006        -at    a lower cost than in Aceh and with strong community
participation.

More than two years after disasters struck the islands o f Nias, and despite a slow start, modest
progress in reconstruction has been made. By the end o f 2006, about 5,400 new and 350 non-
permanent houses, together with more than 300km o f roads and 37 bridges, had been built or repaired.
However, disbursements have been relatively slow in the affected districts o f Nias and South Nias.
By the end o f December 2006, only 35 percent of allocated funds had been realized due to numerous
challenges faced given the island's remoteness. These challenges include hightransportation costs in
ferrying materials from Sumatra, poor transportation networks within the island group, a lack o f
skilled labor, and a lack o f construction materials. The disbursement rates o f the two districts inNias
islands are lower than those in Aceh, where half o f all allocated funds had been spent by the end o f
December 2006.      While Nias district has made relatively better progress, with 43 percent o f its
allocated funds disbursed by the end o f December 2006, the lack o f progress in South Nias district is
alarming: by the end o f December 2006 only 18 percent o f funds had been spent.
