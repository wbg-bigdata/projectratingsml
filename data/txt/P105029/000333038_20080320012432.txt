                                       I. INTRODUCTION


  1.       Improvingthe country's economic competitiveness has been a centraltheme of the
' Governmentof Colombia (GoC) overall policy programand thus has also beena key pillar
  of the Bank support strategy. On this basis, the Government requested the preparation of a
  Programmatic Development Policy Loan (DPL) series to support implementation of its
  competitiveness-enhancing policies, particularly with regard to five key areas considered
  bottlenecks. This series was included as part of the Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) Progress
  Report discussed by the Board in September 2005, which extended the CAS period through the
  end of the Bank's FY07. This third loan is included in the Country Partnership Strategy (CPS)
  which i s being submitted to the Bank's Executive Board concurrently with this operation. As
  these loans focus on the microeconomic rather than the macroeconomic aspects of
  competitiveness, they are named the Business Productivity andEfficiency DPL series.'
  2.       The proposed operation for US$550 MM discussed in this Program Document
  would be the third loan in the series, continuing with the same broad themes and the same
  development objectives. This program was designed since its inception in late 2005 to be
  implemented over a period of three years covering five policy areas (Figure 1). Initiation of the
  preparation of this loan reflected the continued implementation of the program, including
  advancing toward the completion of the triggers identified during preparation of the previous
  loan. In addition to the areas of support originally envisaged, this loan would also expand its
  support to policies to strengthen the country's fiscal management, an essential pillar of overall
  competitiveness. The development objectives of this programremains unchanged: it i s to support
  sustainable growth andthe alleviation of poverty by:
       (a)      Facilitating the operation of businesses and promoting investment, to boost
                productivity and employment levels; and
       (b)      Consolidating the financial sector and capital markets as pillars of economic growth
                to address the needs of individuals and the productive sector.

3.      In supporting the GoC's implementation of this program during the last three
years, the Bank's engagement has followed the principles that today constitute the Middle
Income Countries (MIC) Strategy. The GoC's policy planning and execution capabilities are
strong and the country has access to international and domestic capital markets. Thus the World
Bank Group has offered a full array of products and services to respond to the needs of this
sophisticated client, including:

        (a) Flexibility in response to evolving challenges: The programmatic approach allows
            the Bank to respond in a flexible manner. The GoC has full ownership of the policy
            reform agenda. This program extends over two GoC's National Development Plans
            and, as the priorities of the Government have evolved, this DPL program has been
            able to support the Government's policy implementation;

        (b) Innovative Financial products: The Government has requested the Bank to use the
            DeferredDraw-Down Option (DDO) for disbursement of this loan; and

        (c) Knowledge services: The delivery of Bank analytical support to the GoC as part of
            this DPL series has been essential to the adequate implementation of the reform
            program. This has taken the shape of either broad reviews of certain sectors which
            then have been used as input for policy design by the Government (e.g., the Quality
            and Logistics Infrastructure for Competitiveness in Colombia Economic and Sector
            Work) or specific technical notes that the GoC has requested to fine-tune certain
            aspects of reforms already under design (e.g., five technical notes requested as
            background for a conference with stakeholders that led to the design of the Financial
            Sector Reformlaw currently being finalized).

4.      The World Bank Group engagement in Colombia, particularly with respect to
policy areas supported by this DPL, has emphasized IBRDLFC synergies. The central
vehicle for IBRD support to the GoC's competitiveness agenda has been this DPL series which
has contributed to establish the necessary legal and regulatory enablingenvironment for increased
growth and competitiveness. Concurrently, the International Finance Corporation (IFC) has
contributed to the private sector's ability to take advantage of such an enhanced environment.
For example, IFC's Bogota-based pilot to reform the inspections systems i s expected to be
expanded to the municipalities of Cartagena, Cali, Medellin, Bucaramanga and Santa Marta
(through a multi-donor initiative). IBRD reports on infrastructure, logistics and regional
competitiveness have been complemented by the recent regionalDoing Business report, prepared
in collaboration with the US Agency for International Development (USAID). IFC plans to
undertake studies in areas such as Public Private Partnerships, simplification of business
processes, and managementof petroleumroyalties.

RECENTECONOMICDEVELOPMENTS INCOLOMBIA

5.      The near stagnation of the economy during the early 2000s has given way to
sustained economic growth and a strengthening of consumer and investor confidence. The
turnaround was influenced by an improved global economic environment in which demand for
Colombia's primary exports has risen along with prices, while the cost of internationalborrowing

has fallen.2 Sound macroeconomic management has played a major part in the recovery as well
as improvements ininternal security.

6.      Real GDP growth accelerated from 1.9 percent in 2002 to 6.8 percent in 2006, with
private investment rising from 7.7 percent of GDP in 2002 to over 16 percent in 2006.
Unemployment has fallen from over 17 percent in 2002 to below 11 percent currently, while
inflation declined from 7 percent in 2002 to below 4.5 percent in 2006; however, progress in
reducingunemployment stalled during2007.3

9.      Due to budget and legal rigidities, expenditures have continued to grow in two
areas: transfers to sub-national governments and the pensions system. The congressional
approval of an Acto Legislutivo' last year limited the growth rate of transfers to sub-national
governments to 4 percent inreal terms in 2008-2009, 3.5 percent in 2010, and 3 percent in 2011-
16. From 2016 onward the growth rate of transfers will be based on the weighted average of the
previous four years. In addition, sub-national governments will receive additional resources
earmarked for education (to meet national coverage and quality goals). Without this reform
transfers would have increased by about 20 percent of GDP in 2009. Transfers to the main state
pension system will continue to expand over the next decade since payouts will greatly exceed
new contributions; a 2005 constitutional reform, however, reduced the net present value of
pension liabilities by 19 percentage points of GDP, from 162 percent of GDP to about 143
percent.

10.      Colombia, like the rest of the Region, has remained relatively isolated from the
recent sub primemortgage-triggered credit crunch inthe US. Latin American EMBIspreads
increased duringthe summer and though these have not returned to their pre-sub prime mortgage
crisis level, volatility has decreased in the past few months and spreads have narrowed. A
temporary depreciation of the Colombian peso took place also duringthis period, but this has also
reversed recently.

around 5 percent of GDP over the next few years, as domestic demand has been growing
faster than GDP, inflation has accelerated and the current account deficit has widened
substantially. The main external risk to the economy would be a slowdown in the global
economy, particularly if key trading partners like the United States or Venezuela were affected.
Adverse commodity price shocks represent a related risk. And if bilateral relations with

 111.     THE GOVERNMENTPROGRAMAND PARTICIPATORYPROCESSES

13.       Competitiveness enhancement has been a priority of the current Government from
the outset. Such policies are at the heart of the 2002-2006 National Development Plan (NDP)'
which was used to design the overall thrust of the program since its inception and sought to
"support sustainablegrowth and employment-generating activities ina context of macroeconomic
stability, debt sustainability and good access to international markets". Efforts concentrated on
improving overall competitiveness through reducing obstacles to entrepreneurial activity,
promoting bilateral and regional free trade agreements, fostering technological innovation, and
improving infrastructure.          The GoC requested Bank support through this DPL series and
accompanying analytical work to implement the competitiveness agenda. Annex 2 presents the
key issues identified as obstacles to businessproductivity and efficiency that this series, as well as
other Bank instruments, is seeking to help address. Annex 3 summarizes some of the analytical
work to identify key determinants of growth in Colombia prepared by the Bank to support the
GoC's programdesign.

Development Plan 2006-2010 and the Visidn Colombia 2019. The National Development Plan
2006-2010: Estado Comunitario: Desarrollo para Todos lays out a comprehensive set of
programs for the next four years and provides guidance for monitoring results and outcomes. The
new NDP was formally adopted through Law 1151of July 2007. Table 1presentsthe five pillars
of the NDP. The shaded areas are supported by the reforms of this DPL series. Inanticipation of
Colombia's independence anniversary, the National Planning Department (DNP) launched an
initiative to develop a longer term vision of the country and a strategy to reach it. The process
involved the development of a proposal, Visidn Colombia II Centenario: 2019- Propuestapara
Discusidn, followed by rounds of discussions with citizens and sub-national governments to

RELATIONSHIP TO OTHERWORLD BANK GROUPOPERATIONS

27.      This Loan reinforces and complementsother Bank operations inColombia. Several
Bank projects in Colombia contribute to business productivity and efficiency. Some of these
projects address policy areas that are not covered by this operation and others include the same
policy areas while focusing on aspects that are absent in the proposed loan. Finally, other Bank
operations are not directly related to business productivity and efficiency but strengthen the
GoC's capacity to implement and monitor the policy program.

28.      This DPL series was originally designed building upon the achievements of two
previous Bank programmatic financial sector adjustment loans (FSALs), approved in April
2003 and September 2004. The first FSAL supported advances in the process of restoring
soundness to the banking system after the crisis that started in 1998. It sought to strengthen the
Government's capacity to manage and mitigate weaknesses in the financial system as well as
fortifying and diversifying the mortgage market, improving access to finance for the micro sector
and strengthening non-bank financial services and securities institutions. The second FSAL was
aimed at consolidating earlier reforms in the banking sector and ensuring future stability,
capitalization and capacity for savings and credit intermediation through the implementation of a
new legal framework for managing baking risks. Building on increased financial stability, the
current operation fosters access to financial services for the poor through several reforms,
including: (i)liberalizing the interest rate regime so that financial institutions can make
financially viable loans to riskier and underserved segments; (ii)    reducing the costs of basic
savings accounts for the poor by exempting them from the financial transactions tax; and (iii)
clarifying know-your-customers rules so that they do not constitute an undue barrier for access to
financial services (particularly savings accounts for lower income individuals) among others (see
Table 3 for more details).

29.      The series of Fiscal and Institutional Adjustment (FIAL) DPLs contributed to
improved fiscal management. The FIAL series included three Loans (the first two approved in
2003 and the third one in 2005) that provided support to the GoC's fiscal and institutional reform
agenda to: address fiscal rigidities, improve the provision of public services and establish the
institutional basis for higher efficiency and accountability in public expenditures. The reform
effort has improved the fiscal situation, creating fiscal room for new priorities and reducing

crowding out of the private sector. Thus, the series has contributedto macroeconomic stability, a
necessary requirement for financial sector development and business competitiveness.

30.     The Bank also completed a Programmatic Labor Reform and Social DPL series
(PLaRSSAL), which tackled business environment factors that are not being addressed by
the current loan. The series included three loans approved in September 2003, November 2004,
and July 2006 respectively.      These operations provided support to a number of reforms to
improve two key aspects of the business environment that are not a direct focus of the program
under discussion: the labor market and human capital. The reforms supported by PLaRSSAL
were aimed at stimulating employment and expanding training by reducing the wedge between
employers' hiringand training costs and acceptable wages, while improving the social protection
of vulnerable workers. In addition, the modernization of the National Training Service (SENA)
has increased its efficiency and effectiveness. In particular, the approval of the Labor Reform
Law (Law 789) in 2002 decreasedlabor rigidities by reducing payroll taxes and overtime pay and
extendingthe working day.

31.     Several Bank projects have supported improvements of infrastructure, a policy area
that is also included in this loan. Inadequate supply of infrastructure i s currently considered
among the top constraints for doing business in C~lombia.'~In the urban sector, the Bogota
Urban Services Project (approved in 2003) i s assisting the Municipality of Bogota to increase
access, coverage and quality in the provision of transport, water, sanitation and related basic
services (particularly in low income areas) through investments intransport, water, sanitation and

sanitation, drainage, and access infrastructure.      In the transport sector, the Bogota Urban

conditions in Bogota by promoting the use of public transport and non-motorized transport
models, improving major transport corridors, facilitating the access of public transport to areas of
low income populations and extending the life of road infrastructure. This included support for
the Trunsrnilenio project, a low-cost and very efficient bus-operated public transport system,
regarded as best practice regionally and internationally.

LESSONS LEARNED

37.      The design of the proposed programmatic DPL takes into account the lessons
learned from the overall program with Colombia and in general from Bank work with
other middle-income countries in multi-sector competitivenessloans. Inparticular, lessons
learned were incorporated from the Brazil Programmatic Loan Series for Sustainable and
Equitable Growth (Ln. 7218-BR and Ln. 7386-BR), the First Mexico Programmatic
Competitiveness DPL (Ln. 7367-MX), the First Mexico Finance and Growth DPL (Ln. 7363-
MX) and the first loan of this Business Productivity and Efficiency DPL series (Ln. 7334-CO).
The most important lessons from these operations are the following:

38.      Competitiveness operations should be focused and avoid large and complex policy
matrices. Competitiveness touches a broad range of policy areas. Rather than seeking to cover



                                                 16

all of them in a single complex operation, the design of this DPLhas sought to concentrate on key
areas that can be more effectively addressedas a single package while leaving other areas to be
covered through other Bank operations. Thus, for example, while human capital issues such as
vocational training are directly related to competitiveness, the Bank has been able to collaborate
more effectively with the GoC through different operations such as the completed PLaRSSAL
program.

39.      The importance of flexibility to respond to changing circumstances. In order to
implement a lending program that best addresses Colombia's needs as a creditworthy MIC, the
Bank needs the flexibility to be able to respond quickly to changing circumstances. This i s
particularly important when supporting a program, as i s the case in this DPL series that has
spanned across two presidential administrations. The programmatic approach, as opposed to a
multi-tranche operation, provides the necessary flexibility to respond to new needs and priorities
that can emerge during the implementation process while ensuring a long-term approach to a
reformtheme.

40.      The value of prior strong analytical work. In preparing private sector development
and financial sector policy-based operations, the Bank should lead with its comparative advantage
which i s bringing to bear strong technical capacity and ample cross-country experience. The
Borrower must be confident that its dialogue with the Bank will add value to its own efforts. The
Bank has maintaineda very fruitful dialogue with the GoC duringthe current CAS period leading
to the preparation of analytical work that has assisted Colombia in the definition of the reform
programsupported by this DPLprogram.

41.      The need to take into account the political economy of reform. Indesigning a policy-
based operation, political factors and the legislative needs of the country need to be understood
and included inthe dialogue with the client. The broad consultations that have been carried out by
the Government in the context of the negotiations of the FTA have facilitated reaching the
consensusnecessary to enact reforms, particularly inthe five areas covered by this operation.

42.      The commitment of the Government is central in ensuring the success of the
program. Previous policy-based and investment operations all point to the important role of
strong government leadership in their success. The proposed operation effectively responds to
the GoC's own priorities and commitment as reflected in the actions already taken as well as the
fact that the policy thrust supported by this operation lies directly along the key policy axis of the
Government's NDP.

43.      Coordination with other international financial institutions, such as the IMF and
the IADB, is critical to ensure a consistent policy approach towards borrowers. This
proposed operation buildson close collaboration with the IMFon overall economic policy advice
and the Financial Sector Assessment Program (FSAP) as well as with the IADB, which has been
conducting dialogue with the GoC in the area of competitiveness. Furthermore, USAID has also
been providing substantial support to the GoC in all areas related to the implementation of the
FTA.

52.      Colombia has two budget and legal rigidities that have kept expenditures growing:
transfers to sub-national governments and the pension system. Transfers to the main state
pension system will continue to expand over the next decade since payouts will greatly exceed
new contributions due to the transition period of pension reform. A 2005 constitutional reform,
however, reduced the net present value of pension liabilities by 19 percentage points of GDP,
from 162 percent of GDP to about 143 percent. Last year Congress approved a new legislation to
limit the growth rate of transfers to sub-national governments.

53.      Between 1987 and 2004, transfers to sub-national governments increased four times
as a share of GDP. By the end of the 1990's Colombia had already developed into one of the
most decentralized unitary countries in the Latin America region, providing nearly 45 percent of
current revenue to sub-national governments in the form of transfers. This limited the center's
efforts at adjustment-precisely          at a time when the central government deficit increased above 7
percent of GDP in 1999 (Figure 4). At the same time, sub-national governments engaged in
higher borrowing with limited regulation.                   There was no effective ex-ante control of cash
advances from banks, and sub-national debt with the banking sector rose from 2.6 percent of
GDPin 1991to 4.6 percent of GDPin 1997.

FinancialSector Policy Actions

58.      The banking system has fully recovered from the crisis of the late 1990s.
Specifically, private credit (the claims of the banking sector on the domestic private non-financial
sector) to GDP has increasedfrom 22 percent in 2004 to 27 percent in 2007. While this i s still far
away from the levels of the mid-to late 90s, when it reached 35 percent, it constitutes a significant
recovery from the crisis. At the same time, the banking system has become more stable, with
capital ratios being well above the 9 percent (itself above the Base1minimumof 8 percent) for all
banks and non-performing loans (NPLs) having decreased to below 2 percent. Improvements in
the contractual framework, including the reform of the insolvency framework, have strengthened
confidence in financial contracts. Specifically, the possibility of judicial validation of out-of-
court reorganization agreements, the simplification of procedures for an accelerated recognition
of controversial claims in insolvency proceedings, the establishment of provisions to streamline
and encourage actions seeking personal liability against directors and officers of the insolvent
company, the adoption of modem rules for the treatment of the international aspects of
insolvencies with cross-border impact and the suppression of "consensual priorities" and of
"internal creditors" have strengthened creditor rights. The consolidation of credit bureaus, which
allow the exchange of both positive and negative information, has helped deepen and broaden the
financial system. The increased confidence in the banking system i s also reflected in the
disproportional increaseintime deposits.

59.      However, the authorities realize that financial intermediation is still below that of
comparable countries and have prepared further reforms. Compared with other countries in
the region and around the world, Colombia shows a relatively low level of financial development.
The results of a benchmark exercise, that takes into account variables like country size, level of
economic development and population density show that Colombia fares below the predicted
value for most financial development indicators (Figure 11). To deepen and broaden the financial
system further, the authorities have prepared a financial sector reform package, soon to be

60.      The proposed DPL would support the improvement of the lending interest rate
regime by adjusting risk-adjusted interest rate caps. Up to 2007, the usury interest rate was
uniformly set at 150 percent of the average lending rate. MHCP has issued a decree that
establishes separate interest rate caps of 150 percent of the respective average lending rate for
"regular" and for micro-credit loans, where the latter i s defined as loans up to 40 times a monthly
minimum salary or approximately US$ 8,000. While in the case of regular credit, this cap is
certified every three months, it i s certified every year for micro-credit. Currently, these caps
stand at 32.75 percent for "regular" loans and 33.93 percent for micro-loans. The higher micro-
finance interest rate gap reflects the higher costs and higher riskinvolved with borrowers of small
loan amounts and is in line with international experience. While it i s hard to establish causality
without more rigorous analysis, the increase in micro-loans over the past year seems an indication
that this initial process to more explicitly tie interest rate caps to risk has hadpositive effects.

61.      The GoC has recently issued a decree that exempts transactions on saving accounts
up to COP 7,340,900 (US$ 3,899) per client and per month from the financial transaction
tax (4por mil, 0.04 percent). While the transaction tax is an important impediment to efficient
financial intermediation, this measure i s an important step towards reducing distortions
introducedby the tax and fostering access to formal financial services by low-income segments of
the population who are hardest hit by this tax. This measure should encourage the use of formal
banking services for savings and payment services by segments of the population that are
currently either at the margin or outside the formal banking system.

62.      The proposed DPL would support the issue of a Circular by the SF to clarify the
minimum regulatory information requirements of new account holders. This Circular
clarifies that financial institutions are required to collect only the mandatory information, while
additional information indicated by the SF i s to be decided by the financial institutions. Further,
if some of the mandatory information is not available, this should be indicatedas such, but does
not have to constitute a barrier to open an account. This clarification should reduce the regulatory
burden on financial institutions and the documentation requirements for new customers, and thus
help bring segments of the population into the banking sector that are currently outside as they do
not have many of the necessarydocuments requiredto open an account. This approach is aligned
with the recommendations from the Financial Action Task Force on Money Laundering, which
support a risk-based approach to ensure that anti-money laundering provisions concentrate on
transactions, customers and financial instruments that are likelier to engage or be used for money
launderingpurposes.

A)      Strengthening the quality institutional framework

66.     Important steps have been taken to address the weaknesses of Colombia's National
Quality System. The GoC identified a number of weaknesses of the national quality system in
the National Quality Planpublished in 2005 and proposed specific measuresto address them in a
technical report prepared by DNP, MICT and the Industry and Commerce Superintendency (SIC).
The technical report was discussed by the National Council for Economic and Social Policy
(CONPES), which approved a policy document (CONPES 3446) in October 2006.                     This
CONPES elaborates the strategies for a National Quality Policy aimed at increasing the
competitiveness of Colombian enterprises and improving the activities of regulation, control and
vigilance of the State. The recommendations of CONPES 3446 have been incorporated in the
NDPenactedinmid-2007.

67.      The National Development Plan 2006-2010 establishes Government strategies to
strengthen and reorganize the National Quality System. Based on CONPES 3446, the NDP
establishes eight basic strategies for the reorganization and strengthening of the National
Normalization, Certification and Metrology System (SNNCM): (a) institutional and regulatory
adjustments to incorporate the National Quality System to the National Competitiveness System;
(b) organization and coordination of technical regulations; (c) strengthening of normalization and
effective and timely participation of the country ininternationalnormalization events; (d) creation
of the National Accreditation Organism with international recognition; (e) implementation of a
policy for the designation of evaluation of conformity organisms; (f) creation of the National
Institute of Metrology; (g) strengthening of legal metrology; and (h) coordination of the National
Quality Subsystem and the Mandatory Health Attention Quality Guarantee System of the
Ministry of Social Protection. Several of these strategies are currently under implementation.
The most advancedis the creation of the National Accreditation Organism(see below).

68.      The authorities are working on the institutionalreorganizationof the SNNCM. The
framework decree that organized the SNNCM (Decree No. 2269) was enacted in 1993. The
authorities are working on a revision of this Decree that would reorganize the SNNCM to address
current problems of articulation among activities and entities. The denomination and objective of
the SNNCM would be changed, converting it into the National Quality Subsystem (SNCA),


                                                28

which would be a component of the National Competitiveness Administrative System (SNC).
The new SNCA would coordinate the activities of public and private institutions related to
normalization, elaboration and expedition of technical regulations, accreditation, designation,
evaluation of conformity and metrology.         As a component of the SNC, it would have the
objective of promoting the security, quality, confidence, productivity and competitiveness of
goods and services, and protect consumers' interests.         An Intersectoral Quality Committee
composed of representatives of relevant public and private organizations would coordinate the
activities of these entities within the guidelines of the SNCA. The Committee would propose
quality policies, prepare bills of law, decrees and resolutions to strengthenthe SNCA, support the
competent organizations to obtain international recognition, and ensure that the technical
regulations meet the Agreement on Technical Obstacles (OTC) of the World Trade Organization
(WTO).

69.      There are advances in strengthening the organization and coordination of technical
regulations. InColombia, a number of public entities at the national and local level are incharge
of issuing mandatory requirements for goods, services and processes. Some entities have been
issuingtechnical regulations without regard the requiredprocess of notification to the WTO. To
improve the issuing of technical regulations the authorities are working on a unified model for
developing technical regulations that comply with WTO technical norms. The expectation i s that
this model will be implemented through an administrative act, and the coordination will be
carried out through the MCIT.

70.      An electronic `Toint of Contact" has been created to inform both the public and the
WTO about technical regulations. An electronic information system has been established to
provide transparency in the area of technical regulations to importers, exporters, regulatory
entities, the MCIT and the WTO. The system provides information on existing national technical
regulations as well as technical regulations under preparation. It also provides information on
sanitary measures, phytosanitary norms, and procedures for the evaluation of conformity. In
compliance with the WTO technical norms on transparency, the system provides information to
other WTO members on technicalregulations adopted or to be adopted. The system also provides
exporters with centralized access to information on national and international notifications and a
follow-up to their information requirements. For regulatory entities, the system provides a
centralized database on notifications, andthe classification andfollow-up.of the notifications.

provide accreditation to conformity assessment organisms for mandatory and non-
mandatory standards. Untilnow, the accreditation entity in the SNNCM has been the SIC and

recognition. To address this problem, the ONAC was created in November 2007 as a mixed

90.     As Colombia progresses substantially in accumulating endowments of productive
infrastructure, a remaining agenda is to improve the soft components of infrastructure
~ervices.2~ The logistics for competitiveness agenda i s not constrained to transport infrastructure
and services, but also includes trade facilitation procedures. Different studies in Colombia have
pointed out that inspection processes at several governmental entities are not efficient and hamper
trade competitiveness. One of the key reasons for this occurrence is the lack of coordination
among those entities, particularly those responsible for customs, phytosanitary control, public
health and drugs control. Currently, 25 percent of Colombia's export containers go through the
"red channel", mostly at the request of the customs agency (DIAN); about 35 percent of export
containers are subject to control. The lack of coordination of this large number of inspections
leads to multiple container movements and openings and increased trade logistics costs. In
addition, the way in which the inspections are performed i s usually intrusive, due to the lack of
modem equipment and procedures. Container scanners in the main gateways (SPRs, Eldorado
airport and the main border crossing with Venezuela) are critical to overcoming this weakness.
With regards to ports, Cartagena already has container scanners, and the recent MoUs have
includedthe obligation of the other three SPRs to incorporate scanners, as well as adequate areas
for controlled activities.

91.     The GoC is implementing CONPES Document No. 3469 of 2007 (Lineamientos para
el Control de la Mercancia y la Seguridad en 10s Nodos de Transferencia del Comercio
Exterior) to improve inspections and controls related with international trade.                   In
undertaking the recommendations of the document, a Unified Window for International Trade
was enacted. The GoC i s currently implementing this policy document by establishing caps for
inspections elapsed time, mandating the unification of inspection procedures, non-intrusive
inspection practices, harmonizationof service hours, guidelines for the establishment of adequate
control areas, and guidelines for the unification of the information systems to support a common
riskprofile analysis.

92.     Recent analyses stressed the need for a more cohesive institutional structure and
regulatory framework for the trucking indu~try.2~         Trucks move 90 percent of all freight in
Colombia and their activity i s growing at high rates (around 12 percent in the last two years).
The industry has historically been highly regulated, with high entry restrictions, fares established
by the public sector and severe constraints for fleet renewal. As a result, performance indicators
show poor results (Le., most trucks runningabout 60,000 kmper year, about half the distance run
in peer countries). For the trucking industry, international experience shows no clear consensus
on the appropriate regulatory scheme. The balance i s between extremes of strong regulation to
entry and a complete deregulation. Neither extreme has proved to be effective in assuring a level
of cost recovery and profitability consistent with the needs for value added logistics services that
competitiveness requires.

FIDUCIARY ASPECTS

110.      In general, Colombia's fiduciary environment for DPL operations is considered
adequate. Important progress has been recently made in its Public Financial Management (PFM)
systems, as summarized in the Box below.

                                 Box2: Public FinancialManagement
Colombia has made important progress towards improving transparency and enhancing accountability inits
public financial management (PFM) systems. Key developments include the adoption, in 2002, of a
presidential anticorruption plan, the enactment of a Fiscal Transparency and Responsibility Law in 2003,
and the rollout of an e-government approach with the objective to increase the provision of reliable and
timely information, as well as the efficiency in the services provided to citizens.

Worth highlighting i s the implementation of a second version of the Integrated Financial Management
System (SIIF). SIIF-which     includes the whole budget process, treasury and accounting modules-records
and incorporates timely and reliable information on the expenditure process. In early 2006, the GoC fully
implemented direct payments to suppliers improving the management of cash on hand and in banks, and
increasing transparency of the system. The Government has also clarified responsibilities for managing
expenditures among different levels o f Government, introduced modern accounting concepts and
procedures, and began implementing a program to strengthen and integrate the financial information
systems for local governments (Programa para el Fortalecimiento del Sistema de Informacidn Financiera
Territorial, FOSIT). These activities are financed by the Public Financial Management Project MAPFII.

Fiscal control is vested in the external and internal control systems. The Office of the Comptroller General
of the Republic (CGR) and the regional comptrollers are in charge of external audits, and each public entity
has an internal control unit. The CGR has improved its performance in recent years, achieving IS0 9000
certification in 2003.

There are still, however, a number of areas of opportunity. Budget earmarking i s still significant, making
the budget extremely rigid. In terms of information systems, despite progress with SIIF, there are still
multiple systems in use that are not fully integrated, resulting in an incomplete picture of management o f



                                                      43

public finances and introducinginefficienciesin public administration. On the procurement side, current
legislation is still to be reformed, procurement planning is limited, information is not integrated with the
budget execution modules, there are multiple access points for public procurement information, and the
Government does not have a website that centralizes procurement related policies, documents and
regulations, procurement plans, invitationsto bid, or purchaseorders. Implementationof a transparent and
efficient e-procurement systemis still inprogress.

Looking ahead, country diagnostics could be updated through the use of the internationally-accepted
indicators for public financial management (PEFA) & procurement (OECDDAC). Application of these
methodologieswill be of relevance to Colombia. At the nationallevel, it would set a strong analyticalbasis
to evaluate the outcomes of PFM reforms, remaining areas of opportunity, and future modernization
programs. Additionally, and given the unevenperformanceof departmentsand municipalities, application
of an action-orientedsubnational diagnostic tool based upon the cited international methodologies could
help identify (andproposemeasuresto deal with) how PFMperformanceaffects the seen resultsinterms of
efficiency, managementand fiscal viability.



DISBURSEMENTAND AUDITING

111.      The proposed operation will follow the Bank's disbursement procedures for
development policy lending, using the Deferred Drawdown Option, which the government
has requested.           The untied financial support will be disbursed against satisfactory
implementation of the development policy lending program and not tied to any specific
purchases.

112.      The Bank will disburse the loan proceeds into an account of the Central Bank
(Bunco de la Repliblicu, BdR) denominated in US dollars. BdR will immediately credit the
disbursed amounts to the MHCP's Treasury Account, thus becoming available to finance
budgeted expenditures. Upon request from the Bank,MHCPwill provide written confirmation of
the mentioned transaction. An IMF central bank safeguards assessment of the BdR found no
systematic vulnerabilities in the safeguards of the BdR as of 2003. Nothing further came to the
attention of the DPL preparation team that caused it to believe that the control environment into
which the loanproceeds will flow i s other than adequate.

RISKSAND RISK MITIGATION

 113.     As was the case for the first and second operations, the proposed loan is subject to
moderate risk. Prior actions needed for this loan have been achieved and all policy reforms
previously supported remain in place and are actively being implemented. Even so, progress of
the overall program i s subject to moderate risks primarily due to the challenges that may derive
from the inter-institutional arrangements that need to continue to exist for multi-sector reforms
such as those associated with overall competitiveness and evolving conditions with respect to
Colombia's main trading partners.

 114.     There is the risk that effective implementationof mechanisms for inter-institutional
collaboration in support of entrepreneurial activities will be hampered by lack of
coordination among agencies. This risk i s mitigated by the strong commitment of the
Government to the implementation of the competitiveness and enhancement policies supported
by this program, as demonstrated by steps already taken to streamline exports and improve the
businessenvironment andthe creation of aNationalCompetitiveness Commission, a coordination
mechanism that also includes the private sector. Also, under the proposed operation, the GoC




                                                    44

would commit to prepare semi-annual reports on the progress of streamlining bureaucratic
procedures.

115.     There is a risk that Colombia's economy may slow down due to evolving conditions
with respect to the main trading partners. The economic slow down in the US as well as a
possible negative evolution of recent tensions with Venezuela and other trading partners will
exert negative pressure on the Colombian economy. Although Venezuela and the United States
are important trade partners of Colombia, the GOC has signed several FTAs with other Latin-
American and European countries. Over the medium-term, these efforts will help mitigate the
impact in case of a closure of borders. Also, the aim of this DPL series i s precisely to lower the
possibility of this risk materializing by contributing to improve the economy's competitiveness
and thus facilitate the finding of new markets for Colombian products.

116.     And finally, there is the risk of a crisis preceded by large external imbalances and a
sharply appreciating currency. Current account deficits on the order of 5 percent of GDP
prevailed duringthe mid 1990s, and the real effective exchange rate appreciated sharply over this
period. In response, a sharp demand adjustment took place in 1999 along with a sharp currency
depreciation. Inrecent years, the current account deficit has widened (to about 4 percent of GDP)
and the currency has appreciated in real terms; however, so far neither variable has reached the
critical levels of the mid 1990s. Much of the recent current account deficits have been largely
financed by FDI and portfolio flows. As a result, external indebtedness remains manageable at
about 27 percent of GDP at the end of last year (see separate annex on public and external debt
sustainability). The appreciation has also continued to be a challenge for export-oriented firms,
but they have effectively managedthis risk duringthe period of appreciation (which began about
three years ago) and as presentedbefore, have expanded non-traditional exports.

ExternalDebt

121.      Analyzing the sustainability of the external debt requires a model that incorporates a
different set of key macroeconomic variables, such as Colombia's current account, and allows us
to model another set of different shocks, for example, a reduction in net international capital
inflows. External Debt has fallen from a high of about 52.3 percent of GDP in 2002 to more
manageablelevels of around 27 percent of GDP in 2007.

122.     Despite a sharp increase in the current account deficit this year, Colombia's total external
debt profile (public and private) is likely to be sustainable, given that the recent external
imbalances have been financed largely by non-debt flows. Total external debt i s expected to
decline to about 27 percent of GDP under our baseline scenario. A potential risk associatedwith
external debt sustainability i s a sharp exchange rate depreciation. A depreciation of the real
exchange rate in excess of 15 percent within one year would increase the total external debt to
GDP ratio from about 27 percent at end-2007, to over 30 percent of GDP, which is manageable
level. In a worst case scenario, where the current account deteriorates to more than 5 percent of
GDP while the non-debt flows decline would put considerable pressureon external indebtedness.
Under this scenario, external debt will jump from about 27 percent of GDP up to 35.2 percent of
GDP. Although this representsa sharp increase, it is not likely to generatea crisis.

                             Annex 3: Determinantsof ProductivityinColombia

126.      The reforms supported by this DPL series have potentially significant impact on economic
growth in Colombia. The reforms address microeconomic distortions and obstacles -in factor markets,
business regulation and access to infrastructure-           that hamper the growth of firms and creation of jobs.
These areas form what i s often called the investment climate of a country - the economic incentives shaped
by macroeconomic and regulatory policies and public administrative procedures, and embodied in
institutional arrangements, such as security of property rights and rule of law and governance stability.

127.      Evidence on the extent to which these areas are important to firm level productivity-and               thus
to long run economic growth in Colombia-is
Bank   -the                                                 given by a recently completed survey by the World
                 Investment Climate Survey (2006)?* In order to assess the impact of investment climate on
productivity, the variables were grouped into four categories (besides control variables): a) infrastructure; b)
redtape, crime, and corruption; c) finance and corporate governance; d) quality, innovation, andlabor skills.
These four areas correspond, by and large, to the areas supported by the DPL series.

128.      Econometric analyses performed showed that variables related to all categories were
statistically related to total factor productivity of Colombian fim1s.3~ Variables related to red tape,
corruption and crime and to infrastructure present the higher relative impacts on productivity. Labor
informality and import delays play significant role in determining aggregate productivity. Measured in
terms of "workforce reported to taxes", labor informality plays the most important role in determining
productivity, corresponding to 55 percent of the red tape group and 28 percent of the total factor
productivity. As far as infrastructure i s concerned, almost half of the impact i s due to "time to clear customs
to import". The statistically significant negative coefficient reinforces the perception that import delays
represent a major setback for productivity in Colombia (Figure 9). Power outages and shipment losses also
affect productivity. Finally, variables related to quality, innovation and skills and to finance and corporate
governance aspects exert important influence on productivity.

129.      In order to give a sense of relative importance of the determinants of productivity in
Colombia, the results were compared to those of Brazil and Chile, countries where the same
methodology was applied (see Figure 10: Share of Groups of Variables in Total Factor
Productivity).a In a comparative perspective, factors associatedto business regulation and to infrastructure
seem to have higher impact on productivity in Colombia than in countries like Brazil and Chile. The
variables related to the two groups are responsible for 84 percent of the productivity of firms, whereas less
than two-thirds of productivity in Brazil i s influenced by those two groups of variables. Redtape, corruption
and crime related variables in particular exert higher impact on productivity, accounting for half of total
factor productivity, higher than in Brazil, where it reaches 36 percent. Possibly reflecting more solid
institutional development, Chile shows different results, with factors related to quality, innovation, and skills
being the major determinants of productivity, corresponding to more than 50 percent of non-control
variables.

        Annex 4: Impact of Key Reforms Supported by Previous Operations of the Series

130.     This Annex reviews the main reforms supported by the First and Second operations in the
series and their impact up to date. The paragraphsbelow describe key reforms within the different policy
areas included in the program and highlight some of their main effects, to provide a general picture of the
impact of the program.

A) Reforms to improve the business environment and promote trade

131.     Streamlining of administrative procedures to enhance business efficiency. The first operation in
the series included two key pieces of regulation to: streamline administrative procedures (particularly those
associated with business activities), reduce transaction costs and, increase the efficiency of entrepreneurial
activities. The first piece of regulation was CONPES Policy Document 3292 (June 28, 2004), which
established the strategy to improve the competitiveness of Colombian enterprises and specified relevant
future steps to: support the restructuring and modernization of the productive apparatus, promote
investments and optimize public business support programs. Importantly, this Policy Document specified
strategies to rationalize bureaucratic procedures, including: (a) eliminating formalities that have no legal
justification and generate additional costs to entrepreneurs and citizens; (b) rationalizing formalities that
adversely impact the efficiency and management of the entity or agency; and (c) making automatic
formalities that can be completed, fully or partially, by electronic means. This Policy Document constituted
the basis for Law No. 962 of 2005 to rationalizebureaucratic procedures, the secondkey piece of regulation
supported by DPLIto streamline administrative procedures.

132.      From July 2005 until November 2007, 330 administrative procedures have been rationalized.
Rationalization has implied eliminating steps and requirements as well as the reducing time and costs of
 procedure^.^^ Examples of rationalized procedures include those related with the creation of enterprises,
environmental licenses and electronic invoices. The rationalization of the administrative procedures to
create a business has led to the establishment of a one-stop shop (Ventanilla Unica), with a decline of
procedures (from 20 to l),   requirements (from 45 to l), (from more than 50 days to a maximum of 2

reducing time from 2 years to 4 months and replacing manual procedures for automatic procedures to allow

facilitated enterprises' tax declarations and the reduction of costs related with invoices' emission,
distribution and conservation.

IMF Executive Board Concludes 2007 Article I V Consultation with Colombia
Public Information Notice (PIN) No. 08/07
January 28, 2008

   On January 16, 2008, the Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund (IMF)
   concluded the Article IV consultation with Colombia.'
   Background
   Colombia's economic performance in recent years has been strong, aided by sound
   economic policies. The combination of wide-ranging structural reforms and prudent
   macroeconomic policy management has contributed to increased private investment,
   higher economic growth, and lower inflation. At the same time, both the public finances
   and the financial system have been strengthened.
   The rapid economic growth of recent years, however, has led to a rise in inflation and a
   widening of the external current account deficit. Economic growth in 2005-06 exceeded the
   Latin American average and, at close to 7 percent in 2006 and the first half of 2007,
   equaled its fastest pace since the late 1970s. Growth is projected at 63/4 percent for the
   year as a whole. Driven by excess demand pressures and a surge in food prices, inflation
   rose in 2007 and closed the year at 5.7 percent, above the official target of 31/2-41/2
   percent. The current account deficit is projected to double to 4 percent of GDP in 2007,
   reflecting rapid import growth.
   The authorities have been tightening monetary policy, with the aim of reducing inflation.
   Since April 2006, the Banco de la Repljblica has raised its policy interest rate by 350 basis
   points to 9% percent. Combined with the introduction of an unremunerated reserve
   requirement on new deposits in May 2007, this has led to a substantial increase in lending
   rates and reduced the rate of credit growth, although the latter remains high.
   The peso has appreciated over the last year, driven by strong capital inflows, especially
   foreign direct investment. The nominal exchange rate vis-a-vis the U.S. dollar and the real
   effective rate appreciated sharply in the first half of 2007. The authorities attempted to
   alleviate these pressures through sterilized intervention in the foreign exchange market
   and later through capital controls. The Banco de la Republica stopped intervening in May,
   as continued intervention would have compromised achieving its inflation objectives. The
   capital controls remain in place, however, although they were relaxed somewhat in mid-
   December.
   Fiscal policy was broadly neutral in 2007. The combined public sector deficit is expected to
   remain roughly unchanged at    314percentage point of GDP, with strong increases in
   revenues offsetting a rise in investment expenditures. The public debt ratio is expected to
   fall by almost 5 percentage points of GDP to 38 percent of GDP, reflecting strong economic
   growth.
   Aided by its flexible exchange rate regime, Colombia has weathered well the recent
   turbulence in global markets. Equity prices and spreads have moved broadly in line with
   the rest of the region, and there have been no disruptions to debt markets.
   Colombia's financial sector indicators remain strong. Despite rapid credit growth, credit-to-
   GDP ratios are below their peak in the late 1990s and close to the emerging market
   average, Liquidity has declined, but remains sufficient to cover short-term maturity
   mismatches. Nonperforming loans remain a small share of total credit.
   Executive Board Assessment
   The Executive Directors commended the Colombian authorities for their implementation of
   significant economic reforms and prudent macroeconomic policies, which, in the context of

a favorable global environment, have increased private investment, strengthened economic
growth and reduced unemployment, lowered inflation, reduced the public debt ratio, and
increased international reserves.
Directors shared the authorities' concern that the rapid economic growth of 2006-07 has
led to overheating pressures, with inflation exceeding the 2007 end-year target and the
external current account deficit rising. Key policy challenges facing the authorities are to
achieve sustainable economic growth with price stability through an appropriate
macroeconomic policy mix, while further enhancing productivity through the ongoing
structural reforms. I n this context, Directors endorsed the authorities' tightening of the
monetary policy stance, and welcomed the strengthening of the fiscal position and the
authorities' commitment to long-term fiscal sustainability. Given the prospect of an
expansionary fiscal stance in 2008, many Directors urged the authorities to stand ready to
tighten fiscal policy to help reduce excess demand pressures. Some Directors considered
that further fiscal steps should await an assessment of the impact on domestic demand of
the recent monetary policy tightening.
Directors noted that the current inflation targeting framework has served Colombia well,
and generally agreed that it would be premature at this juncture to adapt the framework
by moving to a longer-term inflation target on a rolling basis. They agreed that the flexible
exchange rate regime is consistent with external stability. They noted that the value of the
peso remains consistent with fundamentals, with the recent real appreciation of the peso
being driven largely by increased confidence and stronger economic fundamentals. Most
Directors stressed that continued flexibility of the exchange rate is important to underpin
the inflation targeting framework and allow the economy to respond rapidly to changes in
external conditions, while some Directors saw merit in the automatic intervention rule
aimed at controlling exchange rate volatility. They viewed the rising current account deficit
as reflecting the cyclical phase of the economy. Directors welcomed the relaxation of
capital controls in late 2007, and endorsed the authorities' view that capital controls are
ineffective in the long run. A number of Directors emphasized the importance of phasing
out capital controls, while some others supported them as a short-term measure.
Directors welcomed the continued progress on structural fiscal reform, including tax
reform, intergovernmental transfers, and the commercialization of the state oil company.
Many Directors saw merit in continued reporting on the state oil company's operations in
budget documents, and a few Directors suggested that the authorities also publish a
measure of the fiscal balance that includes this enterprise. Directors urged careful
administration of the recent decree on special tax zones to minimize negative revenue
effects. They also encouraged the authorities to deepen the agenda of fiscal reform. On the
revenue side, Directors emphasized the importance of a comprehensive tax reform,
including a reduction of the number of value added tax rates and tax exemptions, and
encouraged the authorities to build the necessary political support for such a reform. On
the expenditure side, there is scope for steps to reduce revenue earmarking and other
budget rigidities. Some Directors encouraged the authorities to move forward with the
privatization of two national electricity companies.
Directors welcomed the finding that the banking sector remains well capitalized, with a low
level of non-performing loans and a high level of loan loss provisioning. They urged
continued vigilance in view of the increase in nonperforming loans for consumer credit. I n
this regard, they commended the ongoing efforts to further strengthen the financial
system, including the introduction of counter-cyclical provisioning in the banking system,
reinforcement of risk-based supervision, improvement of the regulatory framework for the
supervision of derivatives markets, and further liberalization of the insurance sector. They
also emphasized the importance of increasing the independence of the Financial
Superintendent,
A few Directors encouraged the authorities to remove the exchange restriction arising from
the special regime for the hydrocarbons sector.

highest level in 14 years. The fact that this recovery of banks' profitability comes at the same time as long

demands range from erasing negative information from the credit bureaus over demands for always positive
returns on pension funds to strong consumer protection. The authorities have undertaken considerable
efforts to reach consensus among stakeholders about financial sector reforms. This dialogue has to continue
to help the public distinguishbetween genuine concerns and reform proposals, demands by specific lobby
groups and calls for policies that have failed inthe past.

155.     Credit growth has somewhat decreasedin recent months, following interest rate tightening by
the Central Bank, accompanied by an increase in the interest rate spread. Credit growth has been
strong across all categories of credit, including housing credit, which was particularly hard hit during the
crisis of the 1990s (Figure 12). Real credit growth accelerated to 22.6 percent in June 2007, driven by
consumer credit, which now constitutes almost 30 percent of total credit to the private sector. The recent
interest rate hikes as well as an increase in marginal reserve requirements, however, have slowed down the
rapid credit expansion. While the recent increase in the relative share of time deposits among total deposits
has alleviated maturity mismatch, it has also increased the cost of funding, which - if translated into higher
lendingrates- can support the deceleration incredit growth. Since deposit interest rates have not reactedto
the tightening of monetary policy, the increase in lendingrates has increased the interest rate spreads to 6.7
percent inJune 2007.

156.     The single financial supervisor created in early 2006 is functioning well, but the merger seems
more like an on-going process than a one-time event. The recent merger of banking, insurance and
capital market supervisory agencies into an integrated financial sector supervisory agency has been
proceeding smoothly on the administrative and technical level. The merger seems to have allowed the new
entity to attract higher-qualified staff, reduce inter-agency coordination costs and strengthen overall financial
regulation and supervision. A major challenge remains the strong focus on bankingsector stability, possibly
at the expenseof the insurance andpension sectors.

157.     An important component of regulatory strengthening hasbeen the introduction of anti-cyclical
provisioning requirements. In years where the overall credit portfolio is improving, banks are required to
establish an additional provision for each borrower, computed as difference between default probability in
"bad years" (overall credit portfolio deteriorating) and default probability in "good years" (overall credit
portfolio improving).    In "bad years", the additional counter-cyclical provision can be applied to thus
cushion the impact of credit portfolio deterioration on additional provision requirements. This model has
been introducedfor corporate loans inJuly 2007 and will be gradually introduced in the following years for
consumer, mortgage and micro-credit loans.

158.     Given turbulences in the international market, with potential contagion effects on Colombia,
as well as regional political tensions with possible economic implications, it is important to test the
efficiency of the financial safety net. While the financial system appears strong and resilient, turbulences
in international markets and the regional political tensions can change this panorama rapidly. Further, the
rapid increase in consumer credit and the recent (though only limited) increase in NPLs are reason for
caution. It will therefore be important to test the capacity of the financial safety net, including the inter-
institutional cooperation between Banco de la Repu'blica, SF and Fogafin (deposit insurance fund), to deal
with troubled banks, both on the individual level as well as systemic stress. Inthis context, legal protection
for supervisors and supervisory independence could be further strengthenedby providing for a fixed term for
the Financial Superintendent and delineating a narrow set of conditions under which he could be dismissed,
as currently planned inthe context of the FinancialReformpackage. The Banki s also currently developing a
program with the financial authorities to run crisis simulations to ensure that the necessary coordination
among institutions i s inplace.

greatly outnumbers the number of beneficiaries. The return on the capital, however, has been decreasing,
given that almost half of the assets are invested in government paper (Figure 14), and the real return on these
assets has been decreasingwith progress inmonetary stability.

160.      There is an increasing awareness that the current investment structure of the pension fund
industry is in need of urgent revision. Currently, all resources of a pension fund are invested in the same
portfolio and subject to a minimumreturn requirement imposed by the supervisory authority ex-post. This
structure, however, does not take into account the different investment horizons and risk aversion profiles of
contributors of different ages.      Regulatory restrictions, such as the minimum return requirement and
solvency margin result in an asset concentration on domestic assets that are highly correlated with each other
and underinvestment in equity securities and international assets, resulting in a diversification loss. The
minimum return requirement has resulted in herding behavior by pension funds with consequent
macroeconomic risks. The high asset concentration has also led to an increasing volatility in asset returns
over time. Overall, the current resource allocation of the pension fund industries i s far from the efficiency
frontier.

161.    While the introductionof multiple investment funds is being facilitated in the FinancialSector
Reform Law, urgent changes to benchmark and commission structure are needed, which can be
introduced by decree.       The financial reform package discussed above includes provisions to introduce
different portfolios depending on the age and risk profile of contributors, other reforms such as the
benchmark to measure the minimum return can be introduced via decree. Also, the current commission
structure - as a function of invested resources rather than return - i s incentive-incompatible with the
contributors' interest to maximize return subject to a certain risk. The Bank i s providing technical assistance
in this area to the Financial Regulation Directorate within MHCP. The reform of the pension system will
require an intensive public awareness campaign. The recent turmoil in national and international markets
has resulted in a (temporary) loss in capital of the pension funds. These losses and the increasing volatility
will raise concerns among the contributors, especially as the reforms imply greater investments in assets that
are riskier inthe short-term.

162.     A processof private-public cooperation to expand outreach of the financial system (Bancade
Zas Oportunidades)has been initiated. After several years of public debate, a trust fund was established
per Decree 3424 on September 8, 2006, to foster access to financial services in public-private cooperation
and in a market-friendly and sustainable way. The trust fund, administered by Bancoldex, received COP
1,200 billion (approximately US$ 621 million) inJanuary 2007 as an initialbudget allocation, is coordinated
and supervised by inter-ministerial committee, and has started a battery of initiatives, in the area of
regulation, financial education and subsidy schemes to expand access to financial services.                 Box 1
summarizes some of these initiatives. While it i s too early to judge the success of these initiatives, the
approach of an independent trust fund that engages in dialogue rather than prescription and that tries to work
with the market rather than to replace it, seems very reasonable. This i s line with efforts in other countries
such as Brazil, Chile, and Mexico where development finance institutions have taken initiatives to help
jumpstart markets and overcome coordination problems, without directly providing financial services.55

163.     International experiencehas shown that itis important to focus on savingslpayment services as
much as on credit services. While the original idea of micro-credit was to enable micro-entrepreneurs to
overcome financing constraints, a large share of micro-credit clients borrows for consumption purposes,
which might indicate the absence of appropriate savings products. Further, access to cheap and effective
payments services i s a prerequisite for participation in a modem market economy. The success of the low-
income transaction accounts in South Africa (Mzansi account) and payment services via cell phone in Kenya
(MPesa) shows the potential for such products.

 164.    The differentiation of the interest rate ceilings is an important step towards expanding access
to credit to riskier client groups. The introduction of a differentiated interest rate ceiling (usury interest
rate) for micro-credit loans and the permission to charge an additional commission of 7 percent have allowed
expansion of such loans. Further efforts for legislative changes to allow a previously contracted interest rate
to stay effective even if the usury interest rate later gets adjusted below this rate, i s a welcome initiative that
will help lengthen loan maturities in this market segment and will be important to strengthen the
securitization market.

non-bank correspondent outlets and Decree No. 3965 of 2006 extended this possibility to cooperatives.
Several banks have used the opportunity offered by the correspondent banking model and started such
agreements with different non-financial corporations. As of September 30, 2007, there were 3,547 non-
branch bank outlets, with 50 municipalities having gained physical access to formal financial services. In

170.     The IBR is being initially computed for overnight and one month maturities (through daily

private and public sector specialists hope this can become an interbank short-term reference rate in Colombia

lack of a credible money market reference rate has been frequently stated as the main bottleneck for the
development of these types of derivatives transactions in Colombia. The creation of the Il3R i s yet another
measure for the development of money markets and the development of hedging tools that can alleviate
volatility and the impact of shocks in Colombianbond markets.

 World Bank

 Dear Dr.Zoellick:

 The second administration o f President Alvaro Uribe VClez remains committed to the
 implementation of the 2002-2006 program o f structural reforms, as well as the reforms set forth in
 the 2006-2010 National Development Plan.            All of this is part of the ongoing quest for
  macroeconomic stability and integration into the global economy, to which the Government of
 Colombia has openly committed itself, and includes the launch and continuation o f a series o f
 measuresthat have also been endorsed by the World Bank.

  In order to ensure the sustainability of this reform package, the Government of Colombia is
 requesting a third loan from the World Bank, as part o f a programmatic series known as the
 Programmatic Business Productivity and Efficiency Loan, which seeks to strengthen and
 complement the processes undertaken by the Government primarily in the following areas: (i)
  strengthening o f the financial system and the capital market; (ii)
                                                                    improvement o f quality standards
 and promotion o f technology innovation; and (iii)      strengthening of infrastructure and logistics.
 With support from multilateral financial institutions, and in particular, through the technical
. assistance provided by the World Bank, these actions will strengthen the financial sector and
 promote enterprise development, with a view to expanding efficient access to capital by productive
  sectors and, in so doing, improving their competitiveness on international markets.

  This letter of policy therefore sets out the commitment of the Government of Colombia to the
 reform processes o f the elements for enterprise development and strengthening o f the financial
  sector, thus demonstrating the coherence between the program and the Government's strategies,
  and indicating the measures that will be promoted under the program.

 The ongoing efforts o f the Government o f Colombia to enhance the economy's competitiveness
 were evident in the 2003-2006 National Development Plan (NDP), approved under Law 812 of
 June 26, 2003, and are reaffirmed in the 2006-2010 National Development Plan, which was
 recently approved by Congress under Law 1151 of 2007. Within the context o f the cross-cutting
  strategy for productive development, this latest NDP targets growth sustainability and increased
 productivity, through actions set forth in the regional production initiatives and competitive sector
  strategies of the National Competitiveness and Productivity Agenda [Agenda Znterna para la
 Productividad y la Competitividad], which include enterprise development, innovation, and
  technological development, through which it seeks to implement a comprehensive enterprise
  development policy to face the challenges o f the domestic market and take advantage o f trade
  opportunities generated in external markets. It therefore seeks to face these challenges through the
 following six strategies: (i) promote innovation for competitiveness; (ii)  promote competitiveness


                                                   81

related to business productivity; (iii) the adequate functioning of the domestic market; (iv)
                                         ensure
facilitate trade and international integration; (v) establish specific strategic policies regarding
productivity and competitiveness for the MSMEs; and (vi) promote tourism.

Inorder to ensurethat the reforms set forthinthisprogramwill improvebusinessproductivity and
efficiency, it i s essential to have a stable macroeconomic environment that serves as the basis for
their implementation. Inthis regard, the Government of Colombia has, inrecent years, successfully
devised and implemented an economic program aimed at strengthening public finances, the first
fruits o f which were seen in 2004, and which have continued to strengthen steadily. The main
objectives of this economic program have been the gradual stabilization o f public debt and the
reduction of the fiscal debt to ensure that public finances are structurally sustainable by focusing on
reducing current and future expenditure and increasing fiscal revenue. The fiscal situation has
improved significantly, as seen in figures for recent years. For example, the consolidated public
sector deficit fell from 1.3 percent o f GDP in 2004 to 0.8 percent o f GDP in 2006. Inthese cases,
performance well exceeded the targets agreed upon with the International Monetary Fund.

In 2006, Congress enacted a number of laws that have helped bolster the Government's economic
program. The main approved initiatives include: (i)     the reform o f Law 100 through Law 1122 of
2007, pertaining to health, which will allow the country to secure the resources to finance full
health coverage by 2010, thus entailing a half percentage point increase in employer contributions,
as well as contributions from the State and the General Participation System [Sistema General de
Participaciones]; (ii)   the capitalization o f ECOPETROL; and (iii)    between 2006 and 2007, the
Legislative Act pertaining to the reform o f transfers was approved, with a view to adopting a
system to ensure fiscal sustainability and help increase coverage of health and education services in
local governments.

Colombia's economic growth has generally been positive, owing to the sound performance of all
sectors o f the economy, and based on a significant increase in domestic demand, particularly
investment and private consumption, in a context o f increased security o f private property, which in
turn has generated increased confidence in the sustainability of medium-term growth of the
Colombian economy and the reduction o f fiscal risks.

The Government therefore proposed working to establish an environment that paves the way for all
Colombians, in particular the poorest and most vulnerable, to have access to quality jobs and thus
earn sustainable incomes, which will depend on factor accumulation, increased productivity and
savings, macroeconomic stability, and a series o f reforms designed to strengthen fiscal stability and
generate consistency between labor and capital markets and the taxation structure.


Consequently, and in keeping with the firm objective o f strengthening the financial sector and the
capital market, and establishing clear and secure regulations for the various market stakeholders,
the Government has proposed working on a number o f strategies, including:

     (i)     Expand bancarization, with support from Banca de Oportunidades, whose policy i s set
             forth in CONPES (National Council on Social and Economic Policy) 3424 of 2006.
             This strategy has promoted coverage at the national level, with a view to promoting
             access by households to financial services through formal channels and, in turn, access

    (ii)   Improve the financial system structure to enable banks to provide other types of

    (iii)  Increase the use o f electronic payment methods; and
    (iv)   Improve the credit information system.

The results of this strategy are reflected in the following regulatory instruments: (i) 1111 o f

Specifically, the goal was to point out that the minimumconditions established in Chapter Eleven
o f Title Iof the Basic Legal Circular o f the Finance Superintendence o f Colombia entitled "Rules
on the Prevention and Control o f Money Laundering" do not constitute a barrier or prevent lower-
income citizens from opening savings accounts.

Similarly, the work done to prepare and disseminate the draft law on the financial system cannot be
overlooked. The objective of this draft law will be to increase the depth and efficiency o f the
system, in order to generate the tools and structures required for the suitable channeling o f savings
into productive investments, simplification of transactions between agents, and the appropriate
monitoring o f risks.

With respect to the money market, following a major effort by the national government, money
market regulations were amended and short-term securities began to be issued. With regard to
regulatory amendments, Decree 343 o f 2007 was enacted, setting forth the provisions to standardize
the prudential regulation o f transactions described in Decree 4432 o f 2006, which are conducted by
entities subject to inspection and oversight by the Finance Superintendence o f Colombia and
controlled mutual investment funds. This decree also amends the investment system for the
technical reserves of insurance companies andinvestment companies.

These developments, as well as other economic processes being promoted by the Government,
provide an environment conducive to executing the Third Programmatic Business Productivity and
Efficiency Loan, which attests to the Government's commitment to continue these policies.

Against this backdrop and with respect to the National Agenda, the Government has promoted
actions to strengthen the strategy for competitiveness and productivity, for which needs and
requirements, as well as commercial and productive priorities, have been identified. Inthis regard,
a process was initiated to enhance the quality o f the institutional framework, for which the 2006-
2010 NDP stipulates that the promotion o f quality i s an indispensable requirement for commercial
development at the national and international level. The National Standardization, Certification, and
Metrology System (SNNCM) has been working on this area, and has presented, among others, the

Some of these challenges have materialized in a number o f areas such as the strengthening of

and the substantial progress in the Ministry o f Commerce, Industry, and Tourism in the design of

health standards.

Similarly, the national government considers it imperative to support and promote the progress of
micro, small, and medium-sized enterprises, which are strategic stakeholders for economic growth
and the improvement of the country's competitive position, as they contribute to reduced poverty
and inequality. Within the development o f this area, the CONPES issued document 3484, which
defines policy guidelines so that these enterprises reach the productive transformation in a
sustainable and competitive manner at the national and international level, and become a growing
source o f income andjob creation.

Competitiveness and productivity are complementedby policies designed to promote scientific and
technology innovation, on which the Government has placed an emphasis, to create a culture o f
innovation and financing for projects under favorable conditions, with a view to promoting
technology transfer, and providing ongoing support for technology innovation processes that
generate social, institutional, and business changes. These activities have become evident through
the resources allocated to the Colombian Institute for the Development o f Science and Technology
[Instituto Colombiano para el Desarrollo de la Ciencia y Tecnologia "Francisco Jose` de
Calderas" Colciencias], with a view to creating the conditions and infrastructure to generate
scientific knowledge and technological development.

As part of the increase inproductivity, the Government seeks to generate conditions to facilitate the
flow o f foreign trade and promote competitiveness, and has paid particular attention to certain
factors that have a direct impact on enterprise development, such as infrastructure strengthening
and the adoption o f better practices on logistics and transportation. In this regard, the Government
has been working on strategies seeking an important structural reform o f road haulage rules, the
rationalization and optimization o f commerce procedures, and the promotion of logistics standards
at the enterprise level and along the supply chain among others.

On the other hand, important advances in sectors like transport and energy have been achieved
through the formulation o f policies and plans for the development, expansion, maintenance, and
improvement of strategic infrastructure capacity. For this reason there i s currently a policy to
expand road, port, and airport capacity, which i s complemented with the progressive development
of infrastructure related services, interms of opportunity, efficacy and efficiency, as it i s set forth in
