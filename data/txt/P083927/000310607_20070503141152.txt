Risks              The risks associated with the PRIDPL Iare considered low to
                   moderat;.  There are no risks o f non-compliance with the tax,
                   bankruptcy, information transparency and disclosure, and
                   social protection prior actions as they have already been
                   carried out by government.

                   There are important sustainability risks, as some o f the
                   PRIDPL I-supported actions in the capital markets sector
                   represent the starting point o f reforms that will require
                   continuation over time to be effective. These sustainability
                   risks can be classified as political, economic, and managerial.
                   Political risks relate to the potential for a reversal o f the
                   current prudent fiscal policy in the event o f a substantial
                   external shock. These risks are expected to be mitigated by an
                   expectation o f continued strong commitment to fiscal prudence
                   and sustained policy reforms by the government. Inthe area o f
                   capital markets reforms, the risks are more susbstantial as
                   reforms are less developed and the timetable for the reform
                   program i s less clearly defined.

                   Managerial risks relate to the possible difficulties inmanaging
                   new programs, particularly implementation o f the tax reform,
                   and systems because o f the potential shortage o f trained human
                   resources. This risk is mitigated by efforts o f the government

                                    I.INTRODUCTION

1.      The proposedProgrammaticReformImplementationDevelopment Policy Loan
(PRIDPL I)to the Oriental Republic of Uruguay for US$lOO million is the first of two
operationsto support implementationof priority economic and social sector reforms as
determinedby the government.The loan is the second DPL under the FY05-FY10 Country
Assistance Strategy (Report No. 31804-UY). The first DPL was the Social Program
Development Policy Loan (SPDPL) approved inJune 2005 with the CAS. The amount o f the
Loan is to be disbursedina single tranche upon effectiveness. Inline with priorities set by the
government, the PRIDPL Iwould support the implementation o f reforms in three key areas:
(i)tax reform; (ii)thebusinessclimate andcapitalmarkets; and(iii) socialprotection
                                                                         the
system where the loan builds on the achievements o f the SPDPL.

2.      The proposed PRIDPL program is underpinned by (i) Uruguay's strong
macroeconomicperformance since the 2002 crisis and (ii) the government's established
track record of implementingstructural reforms. The key objective o f the loan and an
important value-added o f the Bank is to provide the government with a well-specified,
monitorable framework for the implementation o f the reforms. The reform areas supportedby
the operation feature clearly definedupfront actions, well-specified interimbenchmarks, and
monitorable medium-termoutcome indicators. The PRIDPLprogram represents the latest step
inthe Bank's supportfor Uruguay'smacroeconomic stabilization and structural reform efforts
following the crisis. The program currently i s designed as a sequence o f two operations.
However, there is a possibility that the program could be eventually expanded to encompass a
third DPL as foreseen in the FYOS-FY10 CAS, if the government were to request further
Bank support for its reform agenda.

                                 11. COUNTRY CONTEXT

                                11.1.POLITICALCONTEXT

3.      PresidentTabarC Vazquez took office on March 1, 2005, following the victory of
the Frente Amplio-Encuentro Progresistu-Nuevu Muyoria (FA-EP-NM) coalition in the
October 2004 elections. This ended 170 years of dominance o f Uruguayan politics by the
two traditional parties, the Partido Colorado and the Partido Nacional. The Frente Amplio
reached agreement with the two traditional parties as well as independents on an overall
framework for the conduct of economic policy, foreign policy and education policies. To date,
the administration has targeted macroeconomic stabilization-most    notably by exerting strong
fiscal discipline-and  initiated an ambitious structural reform program. As an integral part o f
the reform effort, the administration has pursued social justice through enhancing social
protection, including the initiation o f an emergency social assistance program, Plan de
Asistencia Nacional a la Emergencia Social (PANES), and reforms aimed at increasing equity
o f opportunity.

                         11.2. RECENTECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS

4.       Uruguay has largely recovered from the 2002 crisis. In2002, a serious crisis hit the
economy, triggered partly by events inneighboringArgentina, leading to an 11percent drop
inGDP in2002 and an increase inpoverty to an unprecedented peak of 32 percent in2003.
The economic recovery has been stronger than initially expected. By end-2005, real GDP
regained its pre-crisis peak level o f 1998 (Figure 1). Unemployment has fallen, and inflation
has been brought under control, falling from double-digit figures in 2003 to 6.6 percent in
2006. Public debt declined from 104 percent o f GDP at the end of 2003 to an estimated 69
percent of GDP by the end of 2005. Financial indicators have improved. However,
macroeconomic vulnerabilities remain, in particular the exchange rate risk associated with
public sector debt, high levels o f dollarization throughout the economy, and significant
contingent liabilities inthe infrastructure and financial sectors.

5.      Uruguay's growth and fiscal outcomes have outperformedthose projectedin the
2005 CAS. The economy grew 6.6 percent in 2005 and an estimated 6.8 percent in 2006
(Table l),   compared to 6 percent and 4 percent projected in the CAS, respectively. The
primary fiscal balance moved fiom a position o f large deficits during 1998-2001 to surpluses
close to the 4 percent o f GDP medium-termtarget. The 2005 primarybalance was 3.9 percent
o f GDP, 0.4 percentage points higher than targeted. Preliminary estimates are that the primary
surplus in 2006 equaled 3.7 percent of GDP, as projected in the CAS. Fiscal consolidation
was mainly due to expenditure restraint. Public sector wages, social security benefits and
capital spending have contracted in real terms. Stronger tax revenue and social security
contributions have offset lower surpluses of the public enterprise sector, thereby keeping
overall fiscal revenues broadly stable. The fall in the public enterprise surplus has been due
mainly to rising costs for the electricity sector caused by higher oil prices and a large fall in
hydroelectric power generation due to the recent severe drought.

6.      The current account has remainedclose to balance since the crisis, with a large
growth in exports volume. Inflows of FDI increased substantially in 2006 due to large
investments from Botnia and ENCE (Empresa Nacional de Celulosa Espaiia) related to
construction o f the pulp mills projects. Excluding these investments, the current account
deficit was equivalent to 1.1percent of GDP in 2006. This underlying current account deficit
was to a large extent caused by the rise inimports due to highoil prices.

7.     Uruguay's strong recovery owes much to the prompt and purposefulresponse of
the authorities. Negotiations with creditors led to the successful conclusion o f a sovereign
debt exchange in May 2003, raising the average maturity o f bonds by over 5 years without
increasing interest rates and without a principal haircut. Policy achievements include a
substantial fiscal adjustment that resulted ina primary surplus o f about 3.5 percent o f GDP on
average over 2003-06 (a major departure from primary deficits averaging 1.6 percent o f GDP
through 1999-2001). The government adopted a flexible exchange rate regime in mid-2002.
The resulting currency devaluation increased the competitiveness o f Uruguayan exports,
particularly o f agricultural products, in world markets. This i s a temporary boost, however,
and longer-term competitiveness will hinge on fundamental structural changes. Prudent
monetary management enabled the inflationary impact o f the 2002 devaluation to be
contained. Adoption o f specific inflation targets by the central bank, pursued through
management of broad monetary aggregates, has kept inflation and inflation expectations in
check. Inflation was brought down to single figures in2004, and is currently within the target
range o f the central bank.

8.     The privatesector has respondedwell to the combinationof good macroeconomic
policies and favorable conditions. Strong macroeconomic performance owes much to the
buoyant export response to favorable terms o f trade and a very positive response o f domestic
aggregate demand-particularly         investment-to   sound macroeconomic policies. While it has
appreciated since the crisis-induced devaluation, the real exchange rate remains well below its
pre-crisis level. Foreign investment i s playing an increasingly important role. Particularly
important are large-scale projects in the wood pulp sector which have begun to materialize.
Foreign investment i s also playing a dynamizing effect in the rural, forestry and tourism
sectors.

However, there i s considerable scope for medium-tern growth to remain well above the 3

11.      The current account deficit is projected to remain modest in 2007 and 2008.
When the substantial imports relatedto the construction o f the pulp millprojects are excluded,
the deficit i s expected to remain in the range o f 1.5 percent o f GDP in 2007 and 2008.
Uruguay has moved somewhat toward mitigating these current account risks, by reducing
public debt and diversifying exports. But risks to growth remain from potential current
account shocks, particularly those related to international interest rates and/or adverse
regional developments.

12.      Under the baseline macroeconomic scenario, the public sector debt ratio falls
from 64 percent of GDP in 2006 to 50 percent of GDP in 2010. This positive outcome is
contingent on maintaining the primary surplus. The fiscal policy o f the government i s aimed
at achieving a 4 percent primary surplus target from 2007 onwards. The government's track
record o f maintaining prudent macroeconomic stabilization policies and their strong fiscal
performance lends credence to this target. Both revenues and primary expenditures are
projected to increase in line with GDP growth, with the additional spending targeted on
health, education and social security benefits. If real GDP growth and the primary balance
were instead to equal historical averages, then the public sector debt ratio would grow to 69
percent o f GDP by 2010. Sensitivity analysis highlightsthe critical importance o f the primary
surplus to a continued decline in the public sector debt ratio: a primary surplus o f 4 percent
would lead to a falling public sector debt ratio even with a lower growth rate equal to the
historical average.

13.      Economic and financial risks remain, however, due to the high level of public
sector debt and guaranteesand the still-high dollarizationin the economy. Public sector
debt continues to represent a significant fiscal risk. The government's debt servicing
capacity remains dependent on a combination o f factors including continued fiscal discipline,
moderate world interest rates, low sovereign risk premiums, sustained economic growth, and
active debt management. Muchof the public sector debt-over 90 percent at end-June 2006-
remains denominated in foreign currency. These large foreign currency obligations expose the
public sector debt to exchange rate risk (Figure 3). The government also has large contingent
liabilities arising from explicit and implicit guarantees to the state banks and non-banking
financial institutions, and the large public sector pension system. Further contingent liabilities
are inherent in the large public enterprises which dominate Uruguay's public services and
infrastructure.

14.      Financialsector vulnerabilities have been substantially diminishedbut currency
mismatches remainimportant.Financial sector resilience has been enhanced since the 2002
crisis: Banks have improved risk management, and strengthened their liquidity and capital
adequacy, thereby increasing their capacity to withstand shocks. Bank regulation and
supervision have improved, and measures have been taken to encourage de-dollarization and
reduce credit risk. To a certain extent, these policies have been successful with dollarization
declining, the creditability o f the peso increasing and capital returning to Uruguay.
Nonresident deposits were down from 50 percent o f total deposits at the end o f 2001 to 23

dollar deposits comprised 85 percent o f deposits and dollar, credits 71 percent o f credit at the

15.      An external shock such as an adverse movement in global interest rates or
regional exchange rates would cause stress to the financial sector in Uruguay (FSSA,
2006). Issuance o f more peso-indexed private and public sector debt would promote de-
dollarization and reduce the vulnerability to external shocks. However, this will be a long-
term process, requiring a sustained period o f macroeconomic stability. Strong macroeconomic

policies, further reforms o f state financial institutions, and further strengthening o f bank
supervision and resolution mechanisms are critical to the medium-term resiliency of the
financial system.

16.      The structural reform program of the administration has been subject to delay as
further time was required to put together the necessary political and social consensus
for the reforms, in particular the tax reform. The passing o f the tax reform through

been adjusted, including the approval o f the financial sector law-aimed        at increasing central

restructuring o fthe housingbank (BHU) to increase its financial viability i s also pending.

                                     11.4. GROWTHCHALLENGE

17.      The recovery phase is now over and the challenge is to achieve sustained growth.
The growth strategy proposed inthe 2005 Uruguay Sources of Growth study has three pillars,
corresponding broadly with policies and reforms for consolidation o f macroeconomic stability
(Pillar I),improvement inthe business climate and the allocative efficiency of the factors o f
production (Pillar 2), and private sector led growth drivenby innovation (Pillar 3).

18.      Tax reform will facilitate fiscal consolidation and thereby contribute to medium-
term macroeconomic stability. Uruguay has made substantial progress with the first pillar
since the crisis, introducing a number o f reforms in the area o f fiscal and financial stability
that are facilitating the transition from recovery to sustained growth. In December 2006,
parliament passed a tax reform bill that i s due to take effect on July 1, 2007. The tax reform
bill is intendedto reduce inefficiencies caused by the current structure o f the tax system and
increase equity. As such, the reform will underpin the medium-term buoyancy o f fiscal
revenues. Rapid progress in tax administration will be a necessary condition to successfully
implementing the tax reform and meeting revenue targets. The PRIDPL operations and
accompanying Institutions Building Technical Assistance Loan (IBTAL) are intended to
assist the government with the implementation o f the new tax system.

                                11.5. SOCIAL SECTORCHALLENGES

22.      During the 2002 crisis, unemployment and poverty increased to levels never
previously experienced in Uruguay. The economic slumps inthe late 1990s and early 2000s
affected the labor market and social conditions, increasing unemployment, poverty and
inequality to unprecedented levels. From 1998 to 2002, GDP declined by 18.6 percent.
Unemployment began to grow in 1999 and, by 2001, was over 15 percent o f the labor force,
while inequalityalso beganto worsen, most probably as a response to growingunemployment
and informality. Poverty and extreme poverty remained stable during the early period o f the
crisis. However, an 11 percent fall in GDP in 2002 impacted significantly on households.
Poverty and extreme poverty showed rapid increases, even after the economy began to
recover, so that by 2003 poverty had almost doubled pre-crisis levels. As policies
implemented by the government began to have an impact and the overall economic situation
improved, unemployment and poverty incidence began to decline. As o f mid-2006, moderate
poverty has fallen to around 28 cent and extreme poverty was at 3 percent, below the record 4
percent during2005, but more than twice its value five years earlier (Table 5).

23.      A key challenge is to strengthen the social protection system, ensuring broad and
sustainable coverage. Uruguay has a large social protection system, which includes cash and
in-kindtransfers, food, housing, training andother programs. The pension system is by far the
dominant program in terms o f budget and beneficiaries, but other smaller programs, such as
family allowances have a significant role (see Table 6 and Table 7). The unemployment
insurance program has always been small; it covered less than 7 percent o f the unemployedat
the worst of the crisis. In addition to the programs funded by the Social Security Agency
(Banco de Prevision Social, BPS), the PANES emergency program to fight poverty provides a
monetary transfer (known as Ingreso Ciudadano). In 2006, expenditure on the income
component for PANES was approximately US$lOO million dollars or 0.5 percent the GDP.

due to its high coverage. Nearly seven out o f ten elderly Uruguayans receive a pension
benefit from the government, a proportion only matchedby Brazil (at a similar fiscal cost) in
the region. While the pension system is very effective inproviding protection to the elderly,
its high cost tends to crowd out other programs, targeted to different age groups. Expenditures
on pensions reached as much as 15 percent of GDP (11percent at BPS and 4 percent at other
occupational pensionfunds) inrecent years, but it declined to around 11percent as a result o f
the decline inreal benefits.

25.      While pension expenditures are unlikely to further decline, improvements in
management and regulation would result in both higher coverage and revenue
collection.As o fearly 2006, 65 percent o fthe occupied labor force was makingcontributions

government to improve efficiency incollection resultedina substantial improvement, and the
number o f contributors to BPS grew by almost 20 percent since late 2004. Some additional
actions should result in even higher coverage and collection, which would increase the
financial sustainability of the pension system. On the other hand, the proportion o f elderly
receiving a pensionbenefit has been stable at slightlybelow 70 percent.

26.      The family allowances program has grown in importance in recent years.
Originally designed as a small supplemental salary for formal workers with children, in 2000
the government passed a law extending its coverage to families o f informal or unemployed
workers, with a strict targeting mechanism. In2004, inthe aftermath o f the crisis, a new law

the number of beneficiaries under this program grew to more than 200,000 children in 2006.
Due to this policy, coverage of family allowances (contributive and non-contributory) o f

households with children in the poorest quintile o f the income distribution went from 26
percent in2001 to 61.5 percent in2006.

27.     In additionto the programs already in place, shortly after taking officein March
2005 the Vasquez administration announced the creation of a new Social Emergency
Program, called PANES.The objective o f the planwas to attend to the needs o f individuals
below, or at high risk of falling below, the extreme poverty line-estimated     to include some
200,000 individuals, or 6 percent o f the population. PANES features a set o f eight,
coordinated programs (basic food, health emergency, basic income, education for critical
areas, workfare, two housing programs, and housing for the homeless). The basic income
program (Ingreso Ciudadano) rapidly became an important contribution to poor households'
budgets. However, the emergency plan was explicitly designed to be temporary in nature,
thereby providing time for a more permanent solution to be found through strengthening o f
the institutionalized social protection system. President Vazquez announced inmid-2006 that
the new program would be calledPlan de Equidad, and technical teams have beenworking in
defining its components and financing since then.

28.      One of the main challenges for Uruguay'ssocial protectionpolicies is to include
poor workers and families that do not participate in the formal labor market. Earlier
efforts inthis direction were made in 2000 and 2004, when laws 17139 and 17758 expanded
family allowances coverage to these families. As the government works on the design o f the
new Plan de Equidad, it seems clear that a core element o f the new policy will be an income
transfer program. While a new scheme could be introduced, the family allowances program
represents an important opportunity and could be easily incorporated in the new policy, since
it is already operating at a high level o f coverage and performance efficiency, and collects
information on household and children that could be used to target and condition additional
benefits. Also, the fact that children outside the poorest and most vulnerable groups also
receive this benefit could be considered a positive aspect, as it would reduce the risk o f

                                          IV.5.LESSONSLEARNED

53.       The Bank has learned important lessons from its recent experience in Uruguay.
First, the `experience underscores the consensual nature o f Uruguay's policy-making
environment. In Uruguay, reforms generally take a long time to mature, but then tend to be
sustained once implemented. In several high profile cases in the recent past involving
privatization and market liberalization, relatively less attention was paid to political consensus
in an effort to speed reform preparation. However, this led to policy reversals. Second,
country ownership o f the reform program i s critical, with the role o f the Bank focused on
suggesting policy options and, where appropriate, supporting reform implementation in
priority areas as determined by the government. Third, while Uruguay has made substantial
progress since the 2002 crisis, it continues to be vulnerable to external shocks. The 2002 crisis
showed how painstaking development progress can be brutally set back in Uruguay due to


                                                        23

external shocks. Prior to the crisis, the Bank had envisaged a scaling down o f IBRD funding
activities inUruguay, but the subsequent crisis fundamentally altered this assessment.

54.     The design of the PRIDPL program reflects these lessons learned. First, the
programmatic structure takes into account the time needed for reforms to come to h i t i o n ,
and the focus i s largely on the implementation o f measures for which the government has
either already obtained approval or at least submitted legislation to the parliament. Second,
the PRIDPL program focuses on areas that the government has itself deemed as priority,

                   COMPONENT I.REFORMINGTHE TAX SYSTEM

59.     The aim of the government's tax reform is to create a more efficient and
equitabletax structure, andto promoteproductiveinvestment.The elimination o fvarious
low-yield distortive taxes and a number o f tax exemptions (see Box 3 for details o f the tax
reform) will lead to a substantial simplification o f the tax systemwith beneficial implications
for both tax collection efficiency and economic (resource allocation) efficiency. The
simplification o f the tax system i s expected to reduce tax evasion, thereby contributing to
revenue buoyancy and by implication macroeconomic stability. A reduction in the corporate
tax rate from 30 percent to 25 percent will support private investment and growth. The
generalization o f the corporate income tax (DUE) to cover income from any combination o f
labor and capital will improve horizontal equity, and enhance tax neutrality across factors o f
production.

60.     The introduction of a dual personal income tax (IRPF) combines a progressive
tax schedule for labor incomewith a low flat tax rate on capitalincome.Horizontal equity
i s improved by the new personal income tax as it replaces the current system whereby only
labor income i s taxed. The reform i s also likely to lead to an improvement in vertical equity,
as the labor income tax i s progressive with six marginal income tax rates ranging from zero in
the first bracket to 25 percent in the 6th bracket. A further improvement invertical equity is
likely to occur due to a reduction in the revenue share o f indirect taxes. The tax on industrial
goods used to finance social security contributions will be eliminated and there will be a small
reduction inVAT rates. The proportionally higher reduction in VAT rates for basic consumer
goods also will contribute to equity.

61.      The tax reform is designed to be broadly revenue neutral. The losses from the

                                        CORPORATE INSOLVENCY

 76.     The government's objective is to amend legislation regulating corporate
 insolvency to provide a dynamic reorganization mechanism facilitating the recovery of
 distressed viable companies, or an efficient liquidation process for non-viable insolvent
 companies. Currently, the insolvency mechanism i s hardly used. This i s a long-standing
 problem, which must be mainly ascribed to the lack o f timely updating o f the insolvency
 legislation. Duringthe 2002 crisis insolvency mechanisms were implementedmore frequently
 but users o f the system point out important limitations regarding the needs o f the corporate
 and financial sectors. In Uruguay there is no unique law on corporate insolvency, but a
 complicated medley o f legal dispositions o f difficult integration. Besides being fragmented
 and complex, the legal system in force provides an insolvency regime for corporations,
 different from the regime applicable to the other legally organized companies. The applicable
 regime for corporations i s currently the Concordat0 Preventivo Regime Law of 1900. The

                                                          33

banks, AFAPs, and insurance companies are excluded from this regime. State-owned
enterprises are also excluded because they cannot undergo reorganization or liquidation
without changing their Charters.

77.     The Executive presented to Parliament a Bill on Insolvency and Corporate
Reorganization in August 2006. The Bill contains a detailed regulation on reorganization
and liquidation proceedings that would replace every law, fragmentarily governing such
matters at present. The Bill,was subject to public discussions with representatives o f the
different sectors involved. The Bill is expected to be discussed by the House o f Deputies
Legislative Commission and approved by mid-2007. Passage o f the new Bankruptcy Law will
be facilitated by the wide recognition in Uruguay that the current insolvency legislation i s
obsolete and needs to be brought in line with current international standards.

78.     The PFUDPL program would support the government's efforts to reform the
legal, regulatory and institutional framework for corporate insolvency. Reforming the
obsolete legal and institutional framework for corporate insolvency i s necessary to induce
corporations to disclose problems at an early stage, facilitate the recovery o f distressed
businesses, and allow an efficient liquidation for non-viable businesses. In this context, the
Bank already has undertaken an assessment o f the draft Bill against the Insolvency and
Creditor's Rights Principles and provided comments to the Executive. The staff assessment i s
that the Billwould facilitate the reorganization o f viable companies and enable the liquidation
o fnon-viable ones to be carried out more efficiently.

79.     Implementation of the future insolvency law may present some challenges,
particularly the training and education of all the stakeholders in the corporate,
financial, labor, legal and judicial sectors. The implementation o f the new legislation will

            INFORMATIONTRANSPARENCYAND DISCLOSUREREFORM

 81.     The government is seeking to improve its accounting, financial reporting and
 auditing practices and to align them with internationalstandards. Improved corporate
 accounting and auditing practices would foster investor confidence by providing investors,
 creditors and other relevant parties with timely and accurate information regarding their
 investment and other business decisions. As well as promoting greater economic integration
 in regional and international markets by aligning with internationally accepted standards,
 increasing the quality and reliability o f corporate financial reporting will create opportunities
 for domestic pension fund managers to diversify their investments. In this way, improved
 information transparency and disclosure would foster the conditions necessary for a revival o f
 Uruguay's securities market.

 82.     The alignment of Uruguay's information and disclosure practices with
 internationalstandardshas already advanced,but there are, however,several measures
 that the government wishes to take to strengthen further financial information
 transparency. International accounting norms (IAS) began to be implemented in 1991, and
 the application o f the IAS/IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards) became
 mandatory in 2004. Now, the challenge for the government i s to put a system inplace so that
 the updated versions o f the IFRS are continually adopted and complied with by the full range
 o f corporate and financial entities.

 83.     In Uruguay, there are, differing accounting norms for banks, insurance
 companies, pension funds, SOEs, listed companies, meatpacking companies,
 cooperatives, agro-companies, offshore, other non-listed companies. The regulatory
 bodies also differ according to the type o f enterprise. The supervision of listed companies i s a
 shared responsibility o f the National Audit Office (Auditoria Interna de la Nacidn or AIN)
 and BCU. The accounting and financial reporting obligations o f listed companies, banks,
 insurance companies and pension funds are set inspecific statutes and inregulations issuedby

                                                         35

the BCU. State-owned enterprises (SOE) are audited by the Court o f Accounts (Tribunal de
Cuentas de la Republica or TCR) and, in most cases, by a private audit firm on a voluntary
basis. Not all types o f enterprises are required, and there i s little incentive, to publish the
financial statements in the Registrar o f Financial Statements (REC). Improving information
transparency will then involve bringing together these different bodies to agree on and
implement a strategy to enact and ensure compliance with new accounting and financial
information disclosure regulations.

84.     The PFUDPLprogramwould support government efforts to improve adoption of
and compliance with the latest international accounting standards as set out by the
IFRS.Strengthening the accounting standard-setting commission, the Permanent Commission
o f Accounting Norms (CPNCA) will be key to improving adherence to the IFRS. The
CPNCA assists the Government in drafting decrees dealing with accounting. Currently, the
CPNCA has no specific resources, does not have clear terms o f reference definingthe process
by which standards are to be developed or adopted, and does not operate under a regular
working schedule. The government intends to improve this situation by granting
administrative autonomy to the CPNCA and increasing its resources. The main benefit
associated with an adequately resourced, specialized body with administrative autonomy i s
that it can rely on its resources and not just on voluntary, pro bono contributions by
stakeholder groups, to conduct its standard-setting activities. The CPNCA could then take a
more substantial role in implementation o f the IFRS, including increased dissemination and
guidance activities.

85.     Adoptingthe latestinternationalaccountingstandardsis not sufficient on itsown
and the government intends to put in place a system to ensure compliance with these
standards. Key to this aim is the establishment of a system o f independent oversight for all
statutory audits involving BCU, the professional body for auditors (El Colegio de Contadores
y Economistas del Uruguay or CCEA), AIN and other key stakeholders. Independent
oversight i s important to ensure that statutory auditors not only have adequate capabilities and
professional competence but also strictly comply with auditing and ethical standards, to
adequately fulfill their functions. Reliance on the assurance that auditors provide on the
corporate financial information i s an essential component o f investor confidence. Many
countries have introduced such an oversight system since 2000 and the IFAC, the accounting
profession's international body, has established one within its organization. The oversight
system should fulfill the following main functions: license qualified statutory auditors;
monitor the process for adopting ISA and the IFAC code o f ethics; and ensure quality control
and enforcement o fprofessional standards.

86.     The PFUDPL program would also back the government's aim of defining and
implementing a strategy to improve information disclosure for SOEs, including the
publishingof audit reports and financial statements by all public companies.While the
overall presentation o f financial statements by the SOEs i s good inUruguay, there i s room for
furthering compliance with international accounting standards and ensuring external auditing
practices are put in place. SOEs play a major role in Uruguay's economy. In that regard,
reinforcing SOE accounting and auditing practices i s critical to hrthering compliance with
international standards for the enterprise sector and to equalizing the standards required by
different types o f entities inUruguay. It also is an important component o f the public sector's
financial management agenda. SOEs are subject to an accounting regimethat i s essentially the
same as for the private sector, including the requirement to report according to the 2003

                                                36

version o f IFRS. Updating this should not be problematic. The challenge lies in ensuring full
compliance with the IFRS. SOEs are audited by the TCR and, most o f the times, by a private
audit firm, on a voluntary basis. Inherent in this strategy would be a system o f oversight to
ensure full compliance with IFRS standards.

87.     The PFUDPL program would support an improvement in disclosure of
information about ownership and related party transactions for all enterprises. The
government i s continuing with its plans for the National Registry o f Companies (RNC) to
computerize all company registration records (for all business entities, including non-listed
companies). Inthe mediumterm, all registration information should be made easily accessible
through an on-line companies' registry as per best practice. Achieving this may require a
strengthening of RNC's effective enforcement powers, such as the ability to de-register
companies with dormant /inactive filing status.

88.     The government has produced an implementation plan for improving
informationtransparency. This is a key step in moving toward the application o f common
standards consistent with international laws for accounting, auditing and financial reporting
across all entities. The implementation plan drawn up by MEF, in collaboration with BCU,
AIN, TCR and the CPNCA, includes benchmarks and a chronogram of actions for the
following:

        Adoption o f the 2006 version o f IFRS as common standard;
    0   Improvement of the functioning, autonomy and resources o f the CPNCA;

        BCU, CCEA, AIN and other key stakeholders;

89.     The result of the policy measures is expected to be improved availability and
quality of audit reports and financial information for all entities, be they listed

                              VI.l. POVERTY AND SOCIAL IMPACT

97.     Overall, the policies supported by the PRIDPL program are expected to have a
significant, if modest, positive social impact, includingfor the poorer groups in society.
The program will have a positive impact by increasing equity inthe tax system, encouraging
investment and growth, and enhancing social protection for low-income households. The tax
reform and social policies supported by this operation are expected to have direct, positive
distributional effects. These policies also are expected to be beneficial for social cohesion.

98.     The general conclusion of a number of studies is that the overall distributional
impact of the tax reform will be relatively progressive, resultingin an improvement in
vertical equity in Uruguay. By design, the reform should result in greater horizontal equity
for individual income earners by taxing treating all domestic labor income equally. Several
studies analyze the direct effect o f a change in the tax structure on the income distribution.
The majority use household income survey data from various years o f the Encuesta Nacional
de Hogares Ampliada (ECH) and household expenditure data from Encuesta de Gastos e
Ingresos de Zos Hogares (EGIH) o f 1994/95.19The focus o f the analysis i s on analyzing the
direct distributive impact o f the main changes in the taxation o f consumption (elimination o f
COFIS, reduction o f the basic VAT rates and removal o f VAT exemptions) and the advent o f
a general income tax (IRPF), and the elimination o f the wage tax (IRP).

99.      The replacement of the wage tax (IRP) with the personal income tax (IRPF) is
found to be progressive,shiftingthe tax burdento the higher deciles. Inaddition, findings
suggest that the changes to the consumption tax regimemay benefit those at the lower end o f
the distribution. The analysis was carried out prior to the modifications inthe draft legislation
that were made in the final weeks before its presentation to parliament. One o f the key
changes was the retention o f exemptions for agricultural, health services and passenger
transportation. These changes may have made the consumption tax more progressive. One
investigation has beenmade into the impact on labor demand o f the unification o f the rate o f
employer contributions and social security (CINVE, 2005): the findings suggest that the
reforms would result in a slight increase inthe demand for labor.20

100.    A poverty and social impact assessment (PSIA) on the distributiveimpact of the
tax reformis beingcarried out by the Bank. This consists of a detailed analysis that makes
it possible to quantify with greater precision the magnitude o f the impact o f the tax reform as
well as its incidence on different groups o f households and people. A subject that deserves
particular attention, and that has not been treated to date, i s the impact o f the reform on
poverty. In Annex 3, the existing analysis on the distributional impact o f the tax reform i s
outlined and a detailed description o f the on-going PSIA study on the impact o f tax reform i s

                                       ANNEX 1. TAX REFORM22

124.     The aim of the tax reform program is to create a more efficient and equitable tax
structure, and to promote productive investment. In addition, the simplification o f the tax
system is expected to improve compliance, thereby improving the budget process and
contributing to macroeconomic stability. The tax reform i s projected to be revenue neutral in
the short to mediumterm. The tax reform i s to be put ineffect on July 1,2007.

125.     The pre-reform tax system in Uruguay is inefficient in that it is characterized by a
large number of distorting and low-revenue generating taxes, and a large number of tax
exemptions. Uruguay's current tax system is characterized by a proliferation of small taxes.
Duringthe last twenty years a customary practice to sustain revenue collection under adverse
conditions has been to incorporate new taxes or to increase the rates o f some already existing
taxes. This has generated a sizable number o f taxes, many o f which have turned out to be
difficult to administer and generate low levels o f revenue. There are 12 different direct taxes
and 18 indirect taxes, with 20 taxes each accounting for less than 1 percent of revenue. Just
five taxes, three consumption taxes (VAT, IMESI and COFIS) and two income taxes-the
wage tax (IRP) and the income tax rate for corporations and individuals (1RIC)-provide                     over
85 percent o f tax revenues.

126.     Uruguay's pre-reform tax system distorts resource allocation through specific
exemptions and/or differential tax rates. Manufacturing and construction firms, for example,
are exempt from paying social security contributions; instead, retail and services pay a 12.5
percent rate. Similarly there are many exemptions in the V A T and COFIS taxes which imply
that some firms have to absorb the tax on their inputswithout being able to transferring it to its
clients. For example, wholesale sales are subject to the COFIS tax whereas retail sales are not.
In 2000, DGI gave a figure o f 22 percent for the amount o f forsaken collection due to
exemptions (77 percent inthe case of VAT). The result i s high statutory tax rates, for example
that o f VAT.

127.     The pre-reform tax system is somewhat inequitable both in terms of horizontal
(treat equally those of equal income) and vertical (treat unequally those of unequal
income) equity due to the excessive importance of consumption taxes, consumption taxes
account for almost 70 percent o f total tax revenue, and the partial taxation o f income. With
regard to vertical equity, the taxes that revenue collection mostly relies upon-VAT,                   IMESI,
and IRP-are     regressive, if analyzed by the average tax rate imposed by decile and through
indicators o f income inequality (see Annex 3). The current system also does not respect the
basic criteria for horizontal equity. Direct taxes on individual income are derived almost
exclusively from labor income and pensions, while personal income from capital sources, such
as financial assets and property, are tax exempt, irrespective o f whether it i s generated
domestically or abroad.

Impact of tax reform

131.     Evaluating the overall impact of tax reform on investment, efficiency, equity,
neutrality and fiscal sustainability would require a general equilibriumframework and
estimates of multiple demand and supply elasticities. With this caveat, and taking as a
counterfactual the current tax law, the preliminary conclusions that follow stem from partial
equilibrium impact analysis of specific changes proposed in the tax reform. The overall
conclusion i s that the reform will contribute to economic and tax collection efficiency, to
improved horizontal and vertical equity and to greater private investment in the economy; in
addition, the reform i s expected to be revenue-neutral over the short andmediumterm.

132.     The tax reformis expected to be revenue neutralin the short term (Table 13), and
potentially to lead to a rise in tax revenue in the medium-term due to a broadenedtax
base and a rise in compliance.While the impact o f the tax reform on effective tax collection
has not been analyzed, various authors-e.g.,      Barreix-Roca (2006), IMF (2006)-coincide that
if anything revenue is bound to increase at least following the implementation phase of the
reform. Inthis regard, the tax administration authorities have been extremely active as reflected
in: the prior and projected close coordination between DGI and all the collection agencies,
particularly BPS; substantial adjustments to operational and IT systems o f both DGI and BPS;
the strengthening o f the control, audit and collection fhctions; investment in IT
modernization, communication and training. Some o f these changes have already been
introduced and others are scheduled for 2007. Increased compliance i s expected to be the result
of: (i)the strengthening o f the tax administration including DGI and BPS, (ii)a lower
corporate tax rate, (iii)the reduction o f the V A T tax rate and the elimination o f the COFIS tax,
and (iv) the simplification of the tax system. Since the government does not wish to increase
overall tax pressure, it i s likely to (further) reduce V A T tax rates if and when revenue targets
are met with excess.

133.     The elimination of various low-yield distortive taxes (ICOM, IRA, IMESSA,
IMPEQUE, COFIS, ICOSIFI, IMABA, etc.) will lead to a substantial simplification of
the tax systemwith beneficialimplicationsfor both tax collectionefficiency and economic
(resourceallocation) efficiency (Figure 6). The generalization o f the corporate income tax to
cover income from any combination o f labor and capital (the current tax only taxes
combinations o f labor and capital) will improve horizontal equity, enhance tax neutrality
(across factors o f production) and, since it lowers the corporate tax rate from 30 percent to 25
percent, will contribute to private investment and growth.

134.    The introduction of a dual personal income tax-taxing            income from labor and
from capital-is expected to contributeto equity. Horizontal equity should improve as the
pre-reform system that only taxes dependent labor income i s to be replaced by a
comprehensive personal income tax. Also, it is likely to lead to an improvement in vertical
equity as the labor income tax to be introduced will be progressive with marginal income rates
from zero in the first bracket to 25 percent in the 6th bracket. In spite o f the overall beneficial
impact on equity o f the reform, the fact that capital income i s to be taxed at a lower rate than
labor income (above certain levels o f labor income) implies that neither horizontal nor vertical
equity are beingmaximized. Justification for the adoption o f a lower tax rate on capital income
sources o f individuals than on labor sources i s that, being capital a highly mobile factor, it
mitigates arbitrage towards (untaxed) foreign capital instruments.

135.    A further improvement in vertical equity is likely to occur due to a reduction in
the revenueshare of indirecttaxes. The elimination o fthe COFIS tax (3 percent) and a small
reduction in the VAT rates (from 23 to 22 percent) along with the introduction o f a personal
income tax will reduce the share o f indirect taxes in total revenue. This will tend to improve
vertical equity-as        indirect taxes are proportional and direct taxes are progressive. The
proportionally higher reduction inthe VAT rate for popular consumer goods (from 17 percent
to 10percent) will also contribute to equity.

136.    The reform increases the neutrality of the tax system. The unification o f the
employer's social security contribution at a 7.5 percent rate creates a more level playing field
between manufacturing and construction on one hand (which were exempt from social security
contributions) and retail and services (which paid 12.5 percent contributions) on the other.
Similarly, the elimination o f various VAT exonerations creates a more level playing field
across sectors. By taxing income from all factors (labor, capital, or any combination o f the
two) the reform creates less perverse incentives interms o f the business structure adopted. For
example, professionals which were untaxed under the previous regime faced disincentives to
be incorporated. Finally, the elimination o f COFIS implies that there i s no longer an
asymmetry between firms that were allowed to collect this tax on their sales (thereby taking




                                                 54

credit for what they paid on their inputs) and those that were not. Under the pre-reform regime,
the latter still had to pay COFIS on their inputs and so faced a larger net tax burden.

137.    The implication of having a more neutral tax system is that there are some
"winners" and "losers" in the sense that the relative tax burden of certain economic units
will shift. At one end o f the spectrum among the main losers are the professionals which
income was virtually untaxed prior to the reform. Similarly, industry and construction are no
longer exempt from social security contributions. Among the winners are banks for which
taxes on assets has been eliminated, as well as the services and retail sectors, for which social
security contributions have been reduced. The retail sector also benefits with the elimination o f
COFIS paid on their inputs,which before they couldn't pass on to their clients.

138.    The tax reform does not create special incentives for domestic capital market
development, with the possible exception of listed securities. Interest on listed corporate
bonds (Obligaciones Negociables) i s taxed at the minimum tax rate (3 percent) and the
deductibility o f interest payments for borrowers has been increased from the BROU interest
rate on dollar deposits (around 2 percent) to 12/25 o f the effective interest rate. Such
advantages do not apply to unlisted securities. While the tax treatment o f investment trusts has
not been revised under the reform, parliament has recently approved a special regime for
financial trusts structured according to the Multisectorial III (an IADB funded facility that
provides medium- and long-term finance to the private). Such trusts will be exempt from the
corporate income tax and the net worth tax.

139.    Taxation of income from financial instruments will be overall moderate.
Differential tax rates applied to different financial instruments stems from additional
government objectives such as promotion o f peso or UI denominated instruments and o f
securities in the stock market. In the case o f corporations, the impact o f the reform promotes
investment in financial instruments because the corporate income tax rate is reduced from 30
percent to 25 percent. For, previously untaxed individuals, the least taxed instruments-aside
from investments outside o f Uruguay which remain untaxed-are               listed corporate bonds,
inflation indexed deposits and peso-denominated deposits (a 3 percent tax rate). The flat tax
rate i s higher for other instrument, including less than 1 year peso deposits (5 percent),
dividends (7 percent), and dollar deposits, investment funds and trusts, unlisted securities and
income property (12 percent). In view o f the high rate dispersion, the reform does not
contribute to tax neutrality across different instruments.

140.    The wedge in favor of investing in (untaxed) foreign assets may be offset (from an
investor's perspective) in the case of domestic bank deposits by lower overall taxation
levels applied to banks. For investors with sufficient financial culture and/or resources, the tax
reform creates an incentive for de-intermediation from domestic capital markets. However, the
elimination o f IMABA and IMESI taxes, as well as the reduction in social security
contributions and the corporate tax rate applied to banks paves the way for banks to pay higher
interest rates on deposits thereby potentially offering higher net o f tax returns on their deposits.

141.    Taxation of domestic investor's savings and foreign financing instruments in
general tend to raise corporate finance costs. However, the reduced tax burden on bank's




                                                 55

intermediation (due to the elimination o f the IMABA 2 percent tax and the ICOSIFI 0.36
percent tax on assets) could more than offset this effect in the case o f bank financing,
particularly ifbank competition induces banks to pass on the tax saving to borrowers.

142.    The tax reformprivilegesfirm financingthroughdomestic public issues, bank and
suppliers' credit vis-his foreign securitiedcreditors or unlisted bonds. The advantage
stems primarily from greater interest deductibility for corporate profit tax in the case o f
domestic bank financing, suppliers' credit and listed securities compared to foreign financing
and unlisted securities. Also, the elimination o f debt deductibility from the net worth tax in the
case o f foreign financing places this mode o f financing in an unfavorable situation; unlisted
domestic debt remains non-deductible.

143.    The reform exacerbatesthe bias in favor of debt and against equity financing of
investment. The reduction in the corporate tax rate under the reform tends to reduce the tax
shield factor, i.e. the after tax increase inprofits that results from interest deductibility. To that
extent, the bias in favor o f debt and against equity financing is lower than in the pre-reform
scenario. However, the new tax on dividends (7 percent) more than offsets this factor.

Policy options

144.    In spite of the overall favorable impactof the tax reformon efficiency, equity and
neutrality, severaldesign issues couldbe enhanced. Personal income taxation is based solely
on income from domestic sources, with foreign-sourced income exempt, reducing the potential
equity gains as higher-income individuals usually have better access to untaxed international
financial investments compared to the average citizen. There may be scope for further
reductions in the still high VAT rate, with potentially positive implications for equity. The
wealthhet worth tax (Impuesto a1Patrimonio), together with the personal income tax, implies
a double taxation o f the stock o f wealth and the flow o f income that it generates. The
government does have plans to eliminate the wealtWnet worth tax for individuals, albeit
gradually, but initially the tax i s to be used as a control instrument.

145.    The tax reform does not create significant incentives or obstacles for domestic
capital market development. Taxation o f income from financial instruments is overall
moderate. Tax rate dispersion applied to different financial instrumentsshows that rather than
seeking neutrality across different instruments, the government has privileged promotion o f
peso or UI denominated instruments and o f securities in the stock market. The tax reform
privileges firm financing through domestic public issues, bank and suppliers' credit vis-a-vis
foreign securitiedcreditors or unlisted bonds. Taxation o f domestic investor's savings and
foreign financing instruments in general tend to raise corporate finance costs. However, the
reduced tax burden on bank's intermediation could more than offset this effect in the case o f
bank financing, particularly if bank competition induces banks to pass on the tax saving to
borrowers. The bias against equity financing could be reduced by allowing the deductibility o f
the cost o f own funds from the corporate income tax. Neutrality between domestic and foreign
financing could be deepened by allowing foreign debt to be deducted from the net worth tax.
Improved tax treatment o f trusts could be brought into law. The new capital markets law being

prepared may possibly include additional tax measures to promote capital markets

            CAPITAL MARKETS: IMPACT ON STABILITY AND GROWTH

146.    The Uruguayanbankingsystemhas recoveredfrom the severe crisis of 2002, both
from the prudentialandprofitability standpoints, but the bankingsystemis smaller than
before the crisis with reduced lending portfolios (FSA, 2006). Bank regulation and
supervision have improved significantly, and banks' performance has also improved. Banks
have increased their liquidity (36 percent of total assets at-end 2005 comparedwith 14 percent
at-end 2001), reduced duration mismatches, and they are now adequately capitalized: equity
capital accounted for 15 percent o f risk-weightedassets at end 2005, compared to a reported 9
percent at end-2001. Asset quality has improved with non-performing loans (NPLs) accounting
for about 3 percent o f total loans and provisions about 150 percent o f NPLs. However,
commercial banks' assets are growing only slightly after falling from about US$21 billion at
end 2001 to about US$13 billion at end-2005. Loans to the private sector declined to 26
percent o f GDP at end-2005 from about 75 percent prior to the crisis, reflecting the decline in
deposits, the rise indemandfor liquid assets and the write-off o fbad assets.

147.    The experience in other countries in the region that have recoveredfrom banking
crisis, is that bank lending takes a long time to increase; currency and maturity
mismatches along with improved prudential regulations limit the scope for bank's
financing of new investment. With the bulk o f banking deposits concentrated in short term
deposits, banks are ill equipped to provide credit to finance long-term investment needs -
unlike banks, capital markets are not constrained maturity mismatches. Similarly, given that
close to 90 percent o f bank deposits are dollar-denominated, and that banking regulations limit
dollar lending to borrowers with legitimate dollar incomes, banks are not in a position to
satisfy most potential borrower's needs in view o f currency mismatches. Capital markets are
not constrained by such combination o f currency mismatches and prudential regulations.

148.    Capitalmarkets are thus called to play an important role in mobilizing resources
towards expanding firms and activities; the development of the capital markets is a
priority for the government to bring about higher productive investment and
employment generation. A challenge for the government is to take short and long term policy
actions to help ensure that enterprises, including small and medium sized enterprises, have
access to financial resources, debt or equity, to make the investments necessary to improve
their productivity and efficiency. The government sees the development o f the capital markets
as a channel between the growing supply o f long-term savings by the private pension fund
administrators (AFAPs) and the demand for long term financing by private and public sector
investments. Inaddition, the development o f a market o f financial instruments (even in a small
economy) would contribute to improvements in the enterprises' risk management and
competitiveness. Capital markets may specifically serve to meet Uruguay's large infrastructure
needs and-in     that process-it  can both help to foster good corporate governance in state-
owned firms and encourage new public-private initiatives inthe sector.

149.    Capital market development can enhance macroeconomic and financial stability.
In spite of recent good performance; Uruguay is still a heavily indebted and dollarized




                                                58

economy with significant contingent liabilities inits financial sector (FSSA, 2006). The hrther
development o f currency and commodity futures markets could serve the hedging needs o f
firms and agricultural firms thereby allowing firms to specialize on their commercial niche by
providing tools to manage relative price and financial volatility. Securitization can also help
banks to get rid o f their non-performing loans while the strengthening o f the collateral
execution and bankruptcy procedures may help to speed up firm restructuring and reduce
capital destruction through bankruptcy. The importance o f deep and efficient capital markets
cannot be over-emphasized due also to their potentially stabilizing role in the presence o f a
chronically unstable financial system still dominated by large state-owned banks (BROU and
BHU) and the large state-owned insurance company (BSE). In the specific case of BHU,
capital markets can play an active role in the securitization o f its mortgage portfolio thereby
supporting ongoing restructuring efforts.

150.    The intrinsic flexibility of capital markets can provide timely opportunities for
both firms and investors-particularly institutional investors. Capital markets have a
significant potential for the development o f innovative products tailored to the needs and risks
that different firms or projects may necessitate. Even during the 2002-03 crisis, a number o f
new products have emerged, e.g., investment certificates for dairy farmers, non-performing
loan sales, and banking certificates of deposit. For example, there i s scope for employingnew
instruments-like     the trust or fideicomiso-to          finance sizable investment needs in
infrastructure, forestry, agriculture or tourism. Government incentives may even help to foster
the development o f venture capital funds to help financing Uruguay's incipient innovation
sector.

151.    Institutional investors in Uruguay are accumulating funds, but their investment
opportunitiesremainlimited; the development of a pipeline of new financialinstruments
and the review of portfolio investment regulations could help to at once help to improve
returns and provide better portfolio and risk diversification. Pension funds depend on
capital markets for the investment o f the funds that their members save for retirement and the
savings o f those who have already retired and opt for a system of programmed withdrawals.
Insurance companies offer retirement insurance for those whose only asset i s the accumulated
saving inthe social security system duringtheir working lives, which inturn is invested inthe
market. In this situation, in view o f the social responsibility implicit in a mandatory saving
system for specific ends, it i s crucial to develop an efficient and reliable capital market.

                 CONSTRAINTSTO CAPITAL MARKET DEVELOPMENT

155.     There is general consensus on the main obstacles to capital markets development
in Uruguay. A number o f studies have shed light on the main obstacles to capital markets
development in Uruguay. More recently, the 2006 FSSA and the Governance, Insolvency,
Accounting and Auditing and Payments and Securities Settlement Systems ROSCs conducted
during 2005 and 2006, provide detailed analysis o f the institutional, legal and regulatory, and
infrastructure constraints to capital markets development, as well as recommendations on how
to overcome some o f these constraints. This section summarizes some o f the main findings o f
these studies.

156.     The lack of development of capital markets in Uruguay is the result of a large
numberof factors. The market's limitedsize reflects (i) country's small size, which limits
                                                               the
liquidity; (ii) easy access to and the ability to roll-over loans from state banks for firms in
                the
the main industry, livestock, and in construction; and (iii) full state-ownership o f major
                                                                  the
utilities that typically would issue stock (FSA, 2006). Inaddition, confidence inthe market was
hurt by the 1999 fraud inthe commercial paper market by a major issuer, Granja Moro, and
more recently the 2002 financial and currency crisis. Other issues include: macroeconomic
volatility, lack o f professionalism o f issuers and intermediaries, high issuance costs, weak
institutional development o f the stock markets, inefficient capital market's infrastructure,
portfolio investment regulations o f institutional investors, public scrutiny and governance, and
tax treatment, particularly o f securitized instruments in the local market. Some o f these
obstacles are discussed insome detail below.

157.     Slow economic growth and chronic financial volatility have provided an adverse
environmentfor capitalmarket development.Slow average growth has reduced the needfor
investment financing and hence the need for firms to rely on capital markets. Likewise,
recurrent balance o f payments and banking crises have exacerbated the financial volatility o f
firms thereby increasing uncertainty to potential investors (De Brun et al, 2006). Reflecting the
adverse macroeconomic environment, Uruguayans have held a large share o f their financial
assets abroad thereby limitingthe potential intermediary role o f domestic capital markets.

158.     The lack of professionalism of intermediaries and issuers has limited the
credibility and the scope of capital markets. In the case o f market intermediaries, the
broker's industry i s still dominated by the traditional small family business, with small
amounts o f capital committed, primarily based on a brokerage activity targeted to a small
group o f retail individuals with limited investment banking and capital markets capacity; with
low levels o fprofessional skills o f their staff; and very informal systems o f internal control. All
these factors have limited the credibility o f investors. In the case o f the issuers, the problem
also lies on the family dominated structure o f most o f the firms, with limited professional
management, unsophisticated financial management (lack o f risk management, diversification
strategies) and limited good governance practices. These factors combined with the smaller
size o f the firms have limitedthe scope for bringingnew companies to the market.

159.     Public ownership of major companies limits the number of issuers in the market.
The major corporations-power,       water, electricity, telephony, refining, banks, insurance and




                                                   62

pensions-are     state-owned. Most analysts agree that state-owned firms' issues and issues by
public private partnerships are the best options, in the short term, to bring a new supply o f
financial instrumentsto the market. Large private firms in livestock, the main industry, and in
construction, have been able to get loans from foreign and public sector banks and restructure
their debt incase o fproblems, making equity andbondissues unattractiveto them.

160.    The Exchanges are not perceived as capable and professional institutions;
particularly BVM seems to be weak in terms of governance and with no strategic vision
on the reforms needed to move forward. They are not performing any self-regulation role
(both on the members and on the market regulation) or any active promotion activity. BEVSA
i s perceived as having more technical capacity but given its ownership, other market players do
not expect it to have a major role as a market promoter.

161.    The capital market's regulatory/supervisory capacities at the central bank (BCU)
are perceived to be weak. Weaknesses stem from insufficient resources (quantity and quality)
allocated to this activity, combined with a regulatory framework that limits their capacity to
introduce changes and promote better standards in the market. The lack o f independence o f
regulators and the limited enforcement powers restrict the potential for development o f
securities markets: Subordination o f capital market regulators to B C U gives rise to potential
conflicts o f interest-given  the role o f the B C U as both an issuer o f securities and regulator.
The capacity o f supervisors to apply the regulations is in general low. Examples o f the
difficulties that exist include delays in the provision and publication o f information, and the
absence o f sanctions or difficulties in enforcing them. In particular, physical and legal
resources assigned to inspection work are limited. Legislation does not grant special powers o f
supervision, such as the ability to require changes or corrections to accounting entries, inspect
installations and records, appoint external auditors or initiate proceedings in the ordinary
courts.

162.    Capital market development is hindered as well by weak investor's protection.
Uruguay lies behind both developed countries and developing countries interms o f shareholder
and creditors' rights. The overall investor's protection index lies behind that o f countries,
which are comparable in size in other regions (Estonia and New Zealand) and also behind
some o f the more advanced countries in the region (Chile). The low level o f the disclosure
index stands out. The capital markets' legal framework does not provide specific guidelines on
disclosure o f information by the issuers and reporting standards by the intermediaries. The
legal treatment o f economic groups i s weak, which reduces control o f insider trading; there i s
lack o f protection o f minority investors, and norms o f corporate governance are inadequate.
The current bankruptcy framework does not protect firms under financial distress who are
engaging in effective economic restructuring. The government, however, has send to
parliament a draft law that would enhance the scope o f restructuring before economic value o f
firms i s lost; this would help to strengthen creditor's rights. Judicial processes to enforce
contracts-including    financial contracts-are   extremely lengthyinUruguay (Table 17).

                     TAX REFORM: IMPACT ON CAPITALMARKETS

167.      The tax reformwill notpromote-but will neither constitutea major obstacle to-
capitalmarket development.Inspite o f the dispersion of tax rates, the overall average rate is
relatively moderate and to that extent the overall impact on investor's preferences i s likely to
be moderate too. Since the higher tax rates are on income from property, unlisted bonds and




                                                           64

dollar deposits, capital markets as such are less likely to be affected by the reform. Some
arbitrage away from unlisted bonds, property and equity towards foreign and government'
securities, and to a lesser extent listed bonds, i s likely. The reform privileges o f financing
through domestic public issues, bank and suppliers' credit vis-a-vis foreign securitiedcreditors
or unlistedbonds.

168.    The net effect on domestic capitalmarkets is boundto be moderateboth because
tax changes are minor and there exist offsetting factors. For example, greater trading in
government securities may offset reduced trading inprivate securities. And the higher tax cost
to investors, which could discourage financing through capital markets, may be offset by the
higher fiscal cost o f foreign borrowing by firms. Banks are bound to be better off fiscally and
thus could exert greater competition on capitalmarkets.

169.    The tax reform does not address issues concerningthe potentially more dynamic
instruments;a separate legalinitiativeunderway,however,is boundto reducethe impact
of this omission. The tax reform package concedes a similar level o f tax deductibility across
different financing instruments, obligaciones, fideicomisos or bank loans. However, the tax
reform does not address over-taxation offideicomisos-such         trusts are taxed as an independent
corporation (unless they are a financial guarantee trust) or are tax exempt by government
decree. Financial trusts are widely regarded as a potentially dynamic instrument to finance both
working capital and fixed investments o f corporations. The government, however, has
separately from the tax reform, sent to parliament legislation that if approved would provide
tax exemptions to investment vehicles channeled through public offerings which would include
financial trusts (Programa Global de Financiamiento MultisectorialIII).

                                   GOVERNMENT STRATEGY

170.    The government is taking a multi-facetedapproach to address the constraints to
capital markets' development through a framework incorporatingbroader measures to
improve the business climate and stimulate investment. The government has indicated its
interest in collaborating closely with strategically important stakeholders like the stock
markets, the regulator and issuers. The government has already taken the initiative to provide
better context conditions for capital market development through the Competition Law, the
draft Multisectorial law, changes to the central bank charter to grant greater autonomy to the
financial supervisory authority (draft law i s in parliament) and tax reform legislation sent to
parliament. It is also focusing on three broadly definedpolicy areas which are likely to have a
more direct impact on capital market development: (i)reform o f the institutional, legal and
regulatory, and infrastructure framework for capital markets, including the enactment o f a new
Capital Markets Law (which may also cover pendingtax issues) and institutional reform o f the
regulatory agency, measures to improve the professionalism o f key market players and
infrastructure improvements; (ii)    reform o f the insolvency legislation, including the enactment
o f a new Bankruptcy Law and reform o f reorganization and liquidation procedures (the draft
law has already been sent to parliament); and (iii)     reform o f the information transparency and
disclosure regulations and registration. In addition, the government may encourage greater
participation o f public utilities (energy, oil, gas, and telecommunications) inthe supply o f new
instrumentsto the market.

(i)ReformoftheInstitutional,Legal,RegulatoryandInfrastructureFrameworkfor
Capital Markets

171.    The country's securities market regulator is the DMV, a division of BCU. In the
current legal environment, the DMV i s not able to impose explicit fines on issuers or revoke a
license from a brokeddealer, whose conduct under the current law i s monitored by the self-
regulated stock exchange. The DMV priorities related to the institutional framework include
upgrading o f the licensing requirements, certification o f professional skills;         improving
governance rules and internal controls, and strengthening risk management skills. The DMV
also supervises securities' settlement. The DMV i s coordinating with the Operations Division
o f B C U regarding a comprehensive reform o f the payments and securities settlement systems
in Uruguay that will allow for a "Delivery versus Payment" environment in securities
settlement. The securities settlement oversight i s also done in a limited way by the stock
exchange in its self-regulating capacity. The DMV has not only legal limitations in its
regulatory and supervisory capacity; but also limitations with respect to the quantity and
quality o f the human resources allocated to carry out its work. Its budget depends on the
decisions o f BCU's Board.

172.    The government authorities face several major challenges with respect to the
reform of the institutional, legal and regulatory, and infrastructure framework for
capital markets. One major challenge i s to achieve the adequate level o f professionalism o f
key market players and infrastructure improvements in a small market. Another major
challenge is the decision with respect to the institutional arrangements for the DMV.
Currently, there is the proposal o f merging the DMV with the other two financial supervisors
(bank and insurance) into a single unit in the BCU, under the leadership o f the banking
supervisor. This could dilute the authority o f the securities' supervisor, although it would solve
the problem o f lack o f representation o f the DMV in BCU's Board compared to the other two
financial supervisors. The strong links that exist between banks, AFAPs and stock market
traders are the main reason behind the need to coordinate and eventually consolidate
supervising agencies. Another challenge is the level o f authority o f DMV over the stock
exchanges, and what amount o f self-regulating capabilities should the exchanges keep.

173.    The government authorities are working on a draft Capital Markets Law, which
would give more powers to the regulator by allowing it to supervise the stock exchanges
and reducing the self-regulating capacity of the stock exchanges. The new law would also
strengthen corporate governance o f issuing companies with regulations concerning the
composition o f board o f directors, the inclusion o f independent directors and the protection o f
minority shareholders. The reform proposal i s in an early stage, and consensus has to be
reached with regards to the reforms sequence and priorities. The B C U in coordination with
other authorities and stakeholders is taking the leadership on a comprehensive payments and
securities settlement systems reform.

 (ii)ReformoftheLegalandInstitutionalFrameworkfor CorporateInsolvency

174.    The current legal framework regulating corporate insolvency does not provide a
dynamic reorganization mechanism facilitating the recovery of distressed viable




                                                 66

companies, or an efficient liquidation process for non-viable insolvent companies; as a
result, it is hardly used. This i s a long-standing problem, which must be mainly ascribed to
the lack o f timely updating o f the insolvency legislation. During the 2002 crisis insolvency
mechanisms were implemented more frequently but users o f the system point out important
limitations regarding the needs o f the corporate and financial sectors. InUruguay, there i s no
unique law on corporate insolvency, but a complicated medley o f legal dispositions o f difficult
integration. Besides being fragmented and complex, the legal system in force provides an
insolvency regime for corporations, different from the regime applicable to the other legally
organized companies. The applicable regime for corporations i s currently the Concordato
Preventivo Regime Law of1900. The banks, AFAPs, and insurance companies are excluded
from this regime. State-owned enterprises are also excluded because they cannot undergo
reorganization or liquidation without changing their Charters. Some o f the main problems o f
the current legislation are the following:

    0   One o f the criteria for entering insolvency procedures is the debtor's cessation o f
        payments. Reorganization can be petitioned by the debtor exclusively and the judicial
        liquidation or bankruptcy can be petitioned by the debtor, a creditor, and exceptionally,
        it can be filled ex ofJicio. Creditors tend not to petition judicial liquidation because
        bankruptcy dividends are usually very low or non-existent.
    0   The law does not regulate, or state clearly, the effect o f the commencement o f an
        insolvency proceeding over the current numerous contractual legal relations between
        the debtor and the creditors o f thirdparties.
    0   Injudicial liquidations, the provisions to revoke actions by debtors to defraud creditors
        carried out prior to the bankruptcy are complex and rarely used.
    0   Mechanisms to attempt the reorganization o f companies unable to pay their creditors
        are ineffective for three reasons: (i)  strong requirements regarding the approval o f the
        reorganization plan; (ii)  lack o f flexibility o f the reorganization proposal which has to
        give perfect equality among common creditors; (iii) number o f preferred creditors,
                                                                    a
        mortgages, pledgees, labor and fiscal creditors, are authorized to enforce the debtor's
        assets and their actions cannot be stayed.
    0   There are no regulatory agencies or supervisors for administrators o f insolvency
        proceedings. The National Insolvency Office could undertake the supervisory role, but
        i s not yet inoperation.

175.    There is wide recognition in Uruguay that the current insolvency legislation is
obsolete and needs changing. However, implementation o f the future insolvency law may
present some challenges, particularly the training and education o f all the stakeholders in the
corporate, financial, labor, legal andjudicial sectors. The implementationo f the new legislation
will require careful planning and consistency.

176.    The Executive has recently presented to parliament a Bill on Insolvency and
Corporate Reorganization. The Bill contains a detailed regulation on reorganization and
liquidation proceedings that would replace every law, fragmentarily governing such matters at
present. The Billwas subject to public discussions with representatives o f the different sectors

involved. The Bill i s due to be discussed by the House o f Deputies Legislative Commission in
2007.

(iii)InformationTransparencyandDisclosureReform

                              KEY MODIFICATIONSTO THE TAX SYSTEM

192.      The most critical element of the reform in terms of equity should be the
introduction of the personal income tax (IRPF) that will be placed on income from
activities carried out, located or on goods utilized in Uruguay. Other modifications to the
tax system of particular importance for lower-income taxpayers are likely to be the




                                                                   74

establishment o f a tax on the expropriation o f agricultural property (IMEBA), the single self-
employment tax, and variations to the V A T regime. In the case o f the IMEBA the base i s
expanded, reaching income' in the sector that is currently tax exempt. Also, the reform
increases the scope o f the self-employment tax for those inmicro enterprises, depending on the
economic capacity o f contributors. The objective i s to expand the formalization o f the work,
generating substantial exemptions and a more flexible social security system. The elimination
o f COFIS and the reduction o f the maximumV A T rate to 22 percent and the minimum VAT
rate to 10 percent may also have an impact on the equity. A number o f exemptions have been
maintained or generated, inparticular for livestock activity, research and innovation activities,
and deductions allowed for the medical care o f children.

             EXISTING STUDIES ONTHE IMPACT OF THE TAX REFORM

193.    Prior to the presentationof the tax reformto parliamenta number of studies as to
its likely impact on equity were carried out, with some of this analysis was used as an
inputintothe preparationprocess of the reform(Grau and Lagomarsino 2002; Perazzo et a1
2002, Barreix andRoca 2003,2005). A number o f these studies were elaborated on the basis of
the tax reform proposalput forward at end-2005 by ExecutiveBranch.

194.    Before the 2005 proposal for tax reform, analysis had been done showing by
simulationthe possibilityof obtaininga moreequitabletax-relatedstructurein Uruguay.
The assumed modifications used in these studies do on the whole capture the essence o f the
actual tax reform enacted inUruguay: a reduction in VAT and the establishment o f a personal
income tax. The most comprehensive analysis i s that o f Grau and Lagomarsino (2002)
analysis, which simulates the distributive impact o f a tax modification on the basis o f the
household survey (ECH) and the expenditure survey (EGM for 1994-1995). They assume a
unifiedVAT rate of 14percent and the replacement o fthe IRPwith apersonalincome tax with
five marginal rates. Their findings show that the inequality indicators could be improved
through such a change to the tax structure. Perazzo, Robaina and Vigna (2002) starting from
the basis o f the tax structure in2001,simulate the introduction o f an income tax on individuals,
a reformed VAT structure, and the elimination o f the IRP and the IMESI. The authors show
that the introduction o f the personal income tax implies the greatest improvement in the
distribution o f income. Indeed, it i s only with the introduction o f the income tax that the
inequality indicators improve and the change in the tax system i s statistically significant. The
alterations to the VAT regime simulated do not alone have the ability to impact significantly on
inequality.

195.    The existing analysis of the 2005 proposal for tax reform have found its
distributionalimpact to be progressive.Roca and Barreix (2006) present calculations o f the
degree o f progressivity o f the modifications to the income tax o f the individuals and the
generalization and unification o f the V A T regime. With regardto VAT, Roca and Barreix find
that the proposal presented by the government at end-2005 to generalize VAT to a rate of 17
percent conserves the regressive character o f the VAT tax, both if local indicators o f
progressivity (progression of the average rate o f imposition) or global indicators (e.g. the
Kakwani index) are considered. With regardto the changes indirect taxation, they findthat the
introduction o f the IRPFtogether with the elimination o f the IRPpresents a clearly progressive




                                               75

change to the tax system. Thus, if the VAT and IRPF changes are analyzed jointly, a
progressive effect prevails. Deciles 1 to 7 would be "net" winners, since the tax pressure on
these deciles' income would be reduced. Nevertheless, the authors point out that the effect on
the distribution o f the income o fthe income tax i s modest. The Institute o fthe Economy (2006)
also analyzes the distributive impact o f the tax reform proposal released by the Executive
Branch. The findings suggest that the deciles most favored by the VAT modification are the
first two and the last two. In analyzing the distributive impact o f the elimination IRP and the
incorporation o f the IRPF, the study concludes that the first seven deciles would obtain "net
gains" through this modification, to the effect that the tax imposition rate on its income would
be less than in the pre-reform scenario. The Ministry o f Economy and Finance (2006) also
have estimated the distributive impact o f the tax reform impact due to the introduction o f the

Following i s a summary o f the Economic and Social Program o f the government and the policy reforms

1. Summary ofthe Government's EconomicandSocial ProgramSummary

i) Theprogram

The Economic and Social Program o f the government has been proposed to improve the quality o f life
o f Uruguayans, assisting urgently those who are suffering the most, but also striving sooner rather than
later-although   not immediately-to      create the necessary conditions, based on increasing equity in the
access to social opportunities, so the members o f our society can aspire to fulfill themselves completely
as human beings. It would be a serious error to expect a rapid achievement o f these objectives. Inthe
short term, the country i s not in a position to realize either the level or the composition o f public
expenditures required to assure them. If the important constraints that derive from the high level o f
public indebtness were ignored, Uruguay would face a great fmstration. While trying to help those who
are suffering the most, the first national priority consists of improving the quantity and quality o f
employment, in order to start reversing as soon as possible the perverse functioning o f the transmission
mechanism o f poverty and inequity, and establish a mechanism to create jobs and improve living
conditions for a growing proportion o f the society.

There i s only one genuine path toward that priority: a substantial increase in productive investment,
which has historically been at structurally insufficient levels. Furthermore, investment had been on a
declining trend duringthe last few years.

For these fundamental reasons, the stage where Uruguay i s today, understood within the context o f a
medium and long term process that establishes very ambitious objectives (as mentioned above), has to
be characterized by the necessity o f improving confidence and stability within the Uruguayan economy
and society, an indispensable prerequisite to achieve a substantial expansion o f investment. A narrow
perspective o f the definition o f the concept of confidence i s that which refers to credit: Uruguay, a small
vulnerable country, cannot aspire to develop its economy without this fundamental tool.

But there is a broader perspective regardingconfidence. This which refers to the rules o f the game and
to the commitment o f the government to maintain them, in order to attract domestic and foreign
investors. Uruguay needs more people that have confidence to invest here, generating good quality
employment and a better quality o f life for so many Uruguayans that are inneed.

Concerning the concept o f stability, the goal i s to maintain high levels o f employment and lower
inflation than those observed in the Uruguayan economy in recent years. On the other hand, the
objective i s for economic activity to grow sustainably at a higher rate than in the past. For that to be
possible, it i s indispensable that the fiscal program include an important recovery o f public investment.
If the current infrastructure deficiencies are not addressed, not only would economic growth be
seriously compromised, but also it would contribute to the deterioration o f the quality o f the public
services usedby both citizens and firms.

From another point o f view, but not less important, it i s noteworthy that in order to ensure the required
stability it i s necessary to address the difficult situation in which the most vulnerable sectors o f the
population find themselves. Therefore, the commitment to the implementation o fthe Plan de Asistencia
a la Ernergencia Social and the Plan Equidad i s a fundamental aspect o f the government's program.

One o f the fundamental criteria o f the government's program i s fiscal responsibility, which-above
all-demands that the government's financial program be consistent with fiscal responsibility.
Precisely, consistency with fiscal responsibility contributes to economic stability and provides a
credible and predictable framework for the orientation o f economic policy. So, in reference to the
concept o f confidence mentioned above, it can be affirmed that there i s not better guarantee for the
maintenance o f the rules o f the game than a coherent fiscal program that assures budget financing and
the fulfillment o f the obligations assumed during the period o f government. One o f the most important
assets that Uruguay possesses i s its compliance with commitments it makes, which i s respected around
the world and that i s necessary to be maintained.

On this last aspect, it i s important to point out that the five-year budget has forecast the necessary
resources to finance the total expenditure o f the public sector agencies during the government period.
Thus, for example, it is important to point out that the commitments related to debt service are on an
equal footing to the corresponding obligations for each and every item o f current spending and
investment.

As regards the main priorities that orient the government's program, those can be defined from two

emergency, health, education, public security and infrastructure access, are the priority activities. With

salaries and public assets-particularly those that have fallen the most-as well as the improvement o f the
relationship between operating expenses and investments in favor o f the latter, are the most important

A tax scheme like that proposed, with reduced rates for the taxes in question, with greater simplicity,

Investment, production and employment will be stimulated by a series o f changes: the broadeningo f the

substantial generators o f employment, like services and trade. The reduction o f employers'
contributions in the non-financial state-owned enterprises results in better provision o f public services
and more competitiveness for national production. The differential imposition on interest according to
the terms and the currency, promotes long-term saving and contributes to de-dollarization o f the
economy, which together reduce uncertainties and vulnerabilities. The corporate income tax i s reduced
considerably, particularly inthose sectors that apply technology innovation and generate employment.

3.2 Corporate Insolvency and Reorganization

1)       Obiective

This component's objective is to provide companies in financial difficulties with an instrument; which

11)      Mainreforms and impact

The central element o f the reform i s the approval o f a new legal framework for bankruptcy, considering
that the current regime establishes an inefficient and perverse system, based on outdated concepts that
cannot achieve the objectives mentioned above. The existing bankruptcy execution procedures
(bankruptcy, judicial, liquidation) have been shown to be unfavorable to the debtor's weakened
patrimony. Many times, productive and viable corporate units disappear. It also engenders the presence
inthe marketo fdebtors that progressively decapitalize unloyally, thereby competingwith the rest ofthe
operators. This panorama translates into credit retraction, increased cost o f credit and excessive appeal
to real guarantees, which provokes hightransaction costs and immobilization o f assets.

The reforms are supported on nine fundamental pillars, detailed as follows:
(a) To simplify the procedures: the current multiplicity o f procedures must be simplified and replaced
    by a uniform procedure for all companies, detailed and predictable (but acknowledging the
    particularities o f small enterprises);
(b) To establish a unique procedure: the solutions to the financing and economic difficulties can only
    be determined under a unified procedure that assesses the company's viability takmg into account
    all the possible options throughout the three stages: bankruptcy, sale in block and liquidation in
    parts;
(c) To facilitate the access to procedures for debtors, creditors, workers and other relevant actors;
(d) To provide a flexible framework for the parties to reach agreement, increasing the available options
    for them, with the understanding that only the interested parties can find the path to solve their
    difficulties;
(e) To improve the collective decision procedures o f the creditors;
(0 To reduce the costs of proceedings,     interms o f shortening periods as well as reducing the costs of
    trusteeship, publications, and others;
(g) To strengthenthe specialized courts;

The general objective o f the Plan de Equidad i s to assure the full exercise o f the citizen rights by all
inhabitants o f the national territory, especially those socially vulnerable, by equalizing access to
opportunities with regard to: universal social services, income through dignified work, and basic social
benefits.

The strategic objectives are:

     To improve inter-generational and gender equity, which implies the consolidationand deepening o f
     solidarity between age groups, as well as the consideration o f persistent inequalities associated with
     gender discrimination;
     To develop and expand a social assistance net that takes into account the new and multiple social
     risksand situations o f vulnerability, acting as a safety net to avoidthe consolidationor introduction
     o f poverty and extreme poverty situations; and
     To articulate this safety net with the transformations already announced in social sector policies, as
     well with the group o f traditional benefits that the country has, in order to design an integrated
     protection system.

The structural components o f the social protection matrix are identified as follows:

a) Tax reform;
b) Health policy;
c) Employment policies;
d) Housingpolicy;
e) Educationpolicy;
f) Social Security; and
g) Equityo f opportunities andrightsplan.

Regarding the social safety net components o f the social protection matrix the following elements are
being definedz4:

(a) Income transfers
-    Reformulationof the family allowance benefits system; and

5. Conclusions

Based on the information given above, we present the request for the Programmatic Reform
Implementation Development Policy Loan (PRIDPL), a programmatic loan with a first operation inthe
amount of US$lOO million and a second operation in the amount of US$50 million, in accordance with
the Country Assistance Strategy.

The PRIDPL program aims to provide the government with a framework for the implementation o f the
reforms identified as central to obtain the ultimate objective o f sustainable growth at greater than
historic rates, while at the same time fighting poverty and exclusion, and assuring equitable access to
opportunities.

The government o f the Oriental Republic o f Uruguay commits to develop comprehensively the
PRIDPL program, including the adoption, ifnecessary, o f additional measures to achieve the objectives
o f the program.
