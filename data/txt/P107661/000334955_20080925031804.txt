 A. STRATEGIC CONTEXTAND RATIONALE

 1. CountryandSector Issues

 1.        Indonesia i s Southeast Asia's largest country with a population of over 220 million and a
 lower middle-income economy in which GDP per capita reached US$1,920 in 2008. Since the
 economic and political crisis of the late 1990s, Indonesia has undergone remarkable institutional
 transformation with decentralization transferring responsibility for most public service delivery
 to sub-national governments. In recent years, Indonesia has restored its macroeconomic and
 financial stability with prudent economic policies, as well as extremely low budget deficits.
 Economic growth accelerated to a 10-year high of 6.3 percent in 2007 and i s expected to reach 6
 percent in 2008, despite the current slowing of the global economy. In the past year, the growth
 rate was sufficient to reduce poverty from 17.8 to 16.6 percent and reverse the recent trend of
jobless growth, with unemployment falling from 10.3 to 9.1 percent. A strategy of fiscal
 consolidation has resulted in a significant reduction of government debt levels, while inflation
 has largely been kept under control.'

 2.        With over 46 million students and 2.8 million teachers in more than 227,000 schools,
 Indonesia has also the fourth-largest education system in the world, almost similar in size to the
UnitedStates. Two major ministries are responsible for managing the education system, with 82
percent of schools under the Ministry of National Education (MoNE) and 18 percent under the
Ministry of Religious Affairs (MORA).

3.         The risingprice of basic commodities has increased the financial burden on families that
send their children to school. The overall cost of education borne by families has been rising.
School fees are a significant barrier to access to education for the poor: in a survey 34 percent of
households in the poorest quintile with children enrolled in school reported difficulties in
financing school fees at the primary school level and 44 percent reported difficulties at the junior
secondary level.2

4.         Educationsector challenges.Although Indonesia has made a strong economic recovery,
the country continues to under-perform with respect to its neighbors in terms of access to health,
education and other basic services. Ten years after the Asian economic crisis, Indonesia i s now
in a situation where the quality of institutions and public expenditures has taken center stage in
development efforts. The education sector i s an important test case because funding has
increased substantially since 2001. Indonesia's most important education sector challenges
include the following:

       Junior secondary enrollment. Indonesia has almost universal primary enrollment but i s still
       catching up in junior secondary enrollment. Only 55 percent of children from low-income
       families are enrolled in junior secondary schools, despite recent increases since the
       introduction of BOS.
       Cost of education for families. As the price of basic commodities rises, so does the cost of
       education for families. The Government has adopted a policy of free basic education, but
       has not yet been able to fully fund all the operational costs borne by schools. Therefore,

  0   Student learning achievements. Indonesia continues to rank low in international

5.      Education sector reform and achievements. Since 2002, Indonesia has sought to
address structural problems in the legal and legislative framework governing education. Law on
National Education No. 20/2003 and Constitution 1945 Amendment I11 emphasize that all
Indonesian citizens have the right to education; that the Government has an obligation to finance
basic education without charging fees (although the law and regulations were passed in 2003, it
was only with the introduction of BOS in 2005 that schools received sufficient operational funds
to waive or reduce parents' contributions); and that the legislation provides a reaffirmation of the
constitutional target of allocating 20 percent of the Government's budget to education. In 2005,
important changes were introduced to the employment conditions and requirements for
certification of teachers emphasizing quality improvements in teaching and learning (Law No.
14/2005) and establishing a framework for quality assurance in national school examinations
(Regulation No. 19/2005).

6.      Over the past decade, the Government has also introduced elements of school-based
management (SBM) and has formalized first SBM-principles with Decree No. 044/U/2002 on
the Education Board and School Committees. Indonesia embraced S B M by involving school
personnel (principals, teachers and other staff) and parents inthe management of schools in order
to make them more accountable and responsive to consumers. Similar to other countries, the
expectation was that SBM would lead to more efficient use of resources and improved student
achievement (see Annex 10 for a full summary international and Indonesia's experience with
SBM). However, it was not untilthe introduction of the School Operational Assistance (Bantuan
Operasional Sekolah, or BOS program) that school committees have had any discretionary
money to exercise their mandated role. Based on the BOS experience, the Government has taken
another step in cementing SBM and parental involvement with Regulation No. 19/2007, which
further enhances the roles of school committees. Going forward, the Government i s likely to
further expand SBM and allocations to schools in conjunction with a substantial increase in the
education budget in 2009. However, it i s unlikely that school committees will receive full
authority over hiring and firing of non-contractual teachers, as this would entail fully fledged
civil service reform.

7.      The BOS program. The BOS program, which was initiated in 2005, disburses block
grants to all schools throughout Indonesia based on a per-student formula. It i s Indonesia's most
significant policy reform in education financing in two important aspects: (i)     block grants to
schools are based on a per-pupil formula, which provides incentives for headmasters and
teachers to focus on maintaining and increasing enrollment; and (ii)  funds are directly channeled
to the schools, which empowers school managers by allowing them to choose how to best
allocate the BOS grants.

 8.    BOS i s based on lessons learned from a number of Indonesianprograms, most notably the
 Scholarships and Grants Program (SGP) and the School Improvement Grant Program (SIGP)
 which pioneered the provision of block grants to Indonesian schools to mitigate the impact of the
 Asian economic crisis on student enrollment and drop-outs, and on lessons from international
 experience. Based on there lesson and experience, the BOS program introduced SBM for the first
 time on a nationwide scale in Indonesia's education system, reaching all schools whether public,
 private or religious.

 9.      Evidence from a large number of Basic Education Projects (1998-2006) suggests that
 most schools (94 percent) had their School Block Grant Team actively involved in the
 management of the grants, and 93 percent of the 1,284 schools surveyed had their school
 committees actively involved in the monitoring and management of the grant program
 implementation. Furthermore, the Sumatra Basic Education Project, which aimed to stabilize
 enrollment rates through a period of economic crisis, demonstrates the positive impact of grants
 managed by schools: not only did net enrollment rates stay steady intarget districts but they even
 improved in certain districts over a five-year period.

 10.    International partners have supported SBM in Indonesia for more than 10 years. MoNE
 has even established a SBM Secretariat (see chart below) to integrate programs, such as CLCC
 (Creating Learning Communities for Children) led by UNICEF and UNESCO; DBEP
 (Decentralized Basic Education Project) with ADB; REDIP (Regional Education Development
 and Improvement Program) led by JICA; and by the USAID programs, Managing Basic
Education and Decentralized Basic Education. AusAID i s now mid-way through the
implementation of the Australia Indonesia Basic Education Program (AIBEP) and, as the older
programs phase out, UNICEF i s working to 'Mainstream Good Practice' in SBM in a small
number of districts. The work being done by UNICEF will be complemented by ajoint donor
effort under the BEC Trust Fund3to work with the SBM Secretariat to develop a full package for
district-level capacity development support (Figure 1).

11.     International evidence for SBM. The BOS program i s following models from Latin
America (such as Mexico, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras and Nicaragua) that have all led to
greater involvement of communities and parents and, in turn, have had a direct influence on
educational quality (see Annex 10). In particular, it was found that increased autonomy,
accountability and assessment are the three most important changes associated with improvedquality
of education and better learning outcomes. In El Salvador, the SBM program EDUCO reported
significant increases in enrollment over a decade of implementation. Parents of EDUCO students
were reported to be three times more likely to engage in day-to-day classroom activities than parents
in traditional schools. In Nicaragua and Honduras, autonomous schools are more likely to have
curriculum standards and teacher guides, while school conditions and resources tend to be better than
in traditional schools4 (see Annex 4). A review of SBM experience in El Salvador, Guatemala,
Honduras and Nicaragua' suggests that improving management and empowerment at the local level
contributes to pedagogical improvements. This in turn requires improving parental support,
disseminating information on school performance to all education actors, improving transfer
formulas, training directors, and strengthening institutional capacity of coordinating units.

12.     Integrating international experience in BOS-UTA.                        This project i s based on
Indonesian and international experience and will further strengthen SBM. In particular, BOS-
U T A will (i)    increase school autonomy in expenditure choices (within a limited set of eligible
expenditure categories), and (ii)reinforce parental and community involvement in decision-
making and monitoring (see Annex 4 and Annex 10for further details).

2.         Rationale for World Bank Involvement

 13.       On September 11, 2008, the World Bank Board of Directors discussed a new Country
Partnership Strategy (CPS) for Indonesia (FY09-12). The theme of the CPS i s "Investing in
Indonesia's Institutions". Underlying this strategy i s an understanding that successful institutional
change in a large, diverse, dynamic and democratic middle-income country such as Indonesia
needs to be government-led, and may initially have to be limited in scope and to specific areas
(sectors or regions). The focus of the Bank's program will be on core engagements, through
which the World Bank Group will support the Government's own programs. The Government
welcomes the Bank's reorientation towards blending Bank financing more directly in support of
successful ongoing government programs, recognizing that the Bank's financing comes with
global analytical and operational experience that will further enhance the program. Second,
Indonesia borrows from a multitude of sources to meet its gross financing needs of more than
US$15 billion per year. The World Bank remains a preferred lender -even more so after the
recent adjustment inIBRDpricing.

 14.       The World Bank's existing engagement in the education sector. Education i s one of
the seven core engagements in the new CPS. The education engagement aims to support the
Government's transformation of the education sector -a transformation that i s fundamental if
Indonesia i s to compete effectively with its regional peers. The origin of this engagement was the
restructuring of Bank support to basic education in response to the Asian economic crisis, which
broadened in 2004 to help the Government define its comprehensive strategy for the education
sector as shown in the medium-term plan (RENSTRA 2005-09). The Bank has supported
RENSTRA implementation through advisory services, trust-funded activities and investment
projects, which cover the entire spectrum in education -from early childhood education and
development to higher education and teacher upgrading. Bank lending in active and proposed
projects amounts to over US$1.1 billion. Grant co-financing for the active projects i s valued at
US$77.3 million. In addition, the BEC TF and the Dutch Education Support program together
provide over US$72 million in trust funds to support capacity-building and technical assistance
inthe education sector.

15.        Government request. The Government has chosen to borrow for the education sector
based on the value-added of the Bank's global and international experience and to gain access to
best practices worldwide in the sector. The Government hopes to maintain and improve the BOS
program and therefore requested the Bank to be part of this improvement program, particularly
to further increase transparency and accountability (see Table 4 for a list of detailed
improvements). This loan will also help the Government to meet its gross financing needs which
-despitedecliningdebtlevels-willremainaboveUS$20billionperyearuntil2011.6

16.        Engagement with other development partners. BOS-KITA provides an opportunity to
initiate a broader sector-wide program (SISWA), which i s expected to be one cornerstone of the
CPS. This i s an important step towards supporting institutions in Indonesia. BOS-KITA
represents a new type of support that will be key to revitalizing the Bank's investment lending
portfolio in Indonesia and i s a new way of working, starting with an existing, effective,
government-owned program.

 17.      BOS-KITA also prepares the ground for a sector wide approach in education that i s led
 by the Government and supported by the World Bank and other key development partners in
 Indonesia. Starting in 2008, the Bank-managed, European Commission and Netherlands
 Government jointly funded the BEC TF aims to develop capacity at district and school levels in
 financial and information systems management. This will be carried out in 50 districts in order to
 strengthen the decentralized system. TA has already been recruited and will begin work in
 October 2008. The Bank i s working with the Government and a group of development partners
 to prepare a further program of sector reform in basic education called Systems Improvement
 through Sector Wide Approach (SISWA), which will provide performance-based grants to
 districts (those that meet key requirements following the support of BEC-TF or of other
 programs, see summary in Figure 1) and at the same time support the BOS program which goes
 directly to schools.

3.        Higher-Level Objectives to which the Project Contributes

 18.      The BOS program will support the Government's RENSTRA target of increasing the
number of children completing nine years of basic education and improving the quality of the
education that they receive. It will therefore contribute to the CPS aim of developing quality
human resources, ultimately leading to a growth in productivity and the improvement of
Indonesia's competitiveness inthe global economy.


B. PROJECTDESCRIPTION

1. LendingInstrument

19.       In order to ensure adequate and specific focus on implementation and monitoring, the
lending instrument for this project will be a Specific Investment Loan (SIL,). It will comprise
IBRDfinancing with a disbursement period of three years.

2.     Project Development Objective and Key Indicators

20.       The Project Development Objective i s to improve access to quality education for all
children aged 7 to 15 by strengthening SBM and community participation, improving existing
fiduciary arrangements for greater transparency and accountability of the BOS program, and,
consequently bringing about better utilization of BOS funds.

21.       Attainment of this objective will be measuredby a range of indicators and special studies.
BOS-KITA will track both access to, and quality of, education through the following indicators:
the percentage increase in net enrollment and transition rates, and the percentage decrease in net
drop-out rates.7 These indicators will be disaggregated by income quintile to track how the BOS
program i s affecting households in different income groups, particularly the poor. Primary
enrollment, which i s already near universal, will be maintained while junior secondary will
increase by about 1percent (some 84,000 students) per year. Average household expenditure per
child on education by the poorest quintile will also be utilized to measure if school fees have
been reduced for low-income families. Other indicators capture improvements in transparency
and accountability in the use of BOS funds, measured by: (i)                      the percentage increase of schools

3.   Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

33.    Community driven development (CDD) in Indonesia. Successful examples of
community involvement in Indonesian projects, such as the National Program for Community
Empowerment (PNPM), the Urban Poverty Program (UPP) and the Kecamatan Development
Program (KDP), all indicate that social pressure from an informed local community can be a


                                                 9

positive influence on reducing corruption and the misuse of funds. The BOS program design
already closely parallels institutional and implementation arrangements pioneered by these CDD
programs. BOS-KITA will further expand and build on these lessons, enhancing the role of
parents and the local community in planning (and approving) school budgets for BOS funds and
monitoring BOS expenditures. A matrix detailing the specific similarities between BOS-KITA
and PNPM i s contained in Annex 8. The social marketing study will identify the most effective
ways to ensure that parents and communities are aware of the aims of the BOS program, the
eligible and ineligible expenditures under the program, and the mechanism for the flow of funds.

34.       School grants projects in Indonesia. The SGP and SIGP (see paragraph 8) introduced
a new kind of financing mechanism for education in that funds were transferred directly from a
central government source to a post office account held by the recipient schools. Two major
lessons from the SGP and SIGP experience have informed the design of BOS program. First,the
transparency    and application of selection criteria were problematic throughout               the
implementation of SGP and SIGP. In a major step towards greater transparency, equity and
simplicity of design, the BOS program resolves this issue by providing funding on a per-student
basis to all primary andjunior secondary schools. Second, transparency inthe use of block grants
was problematic in SGP and SIGP. At the root of the problem was a lack of participation at the
school level in decisions as to how, and for what, grant funds would be used. To safeguard
against this the BOS program design requires parental and school committee oversight on
planning and the use of funds. To facilitate this participation and broaden transparency the BOS
operations manual requires that records o f funds received and their use be displayed on school
notice boards.

35.     Reviews of SGP (SMERU and C I M U 2002) found this to be superior to past mechanisms
where funds were channeled through various layers of government. While those reviews noted
delays in the flow of funds under SGP the delays were principally attributed to the complexity of
selection and allocation formula, and the finding was that the direct mechanism significantly
reduced the leakage of funds. The BOS design uses the effective financing mechanism
introduced through the SGP, by channeling funds directly to schools' bank accounts. In addition
these reviews of the SGP and SIGP block grants found that where the BOS operations manual
provided explicit guidance, funds were generally used for the purpose intended. The BOS
operations manual builds on this experience by providing a detailed list of eligible and ineligible
expenditures.

4.   Alternatives Considered and Reasons for Rejection

36.     One option was to delay support for BOS until the full SISWA project becomes
operational. This option was rejected, as this would have meant not responding to a government-
identified priority need for additional funding in 2008 and 2009, and a primary aim of BOS-
KITA is to provide lessons to guide the design of SISWA. This in turn would have meant
missing an opportunity to be involved in quality improvements to a significant government
programthat will be an integral part of the Bank's long-term engagement inthe education sector.

37.     A Sector Development Policy Loan (DPL) was considered but rejected because the loan
will finance a determined expenditure program in the education sector and does not seek to
support a set of specific policy actions. The loan will finance actual expenditures made at the
school level, even if disbursement will be made against grant transfers. A DPL instrument would


                                                10

make it difficult to engage in specific issues relating to the implementation of the BOS program.
However, the existing DPL series i s expected to continue addressing selected structural reforms
in the education sector. Since DPL3, the education sector has already been part of the DPL
program supporting specific policy actions with respect to the issuance and implementation of
the new Teacher Law.

 38.     The BOS-KITA project brings forward an element of the SISWA in response to a
government request. The S I L design allows the Bank to respond more quickly and flexibly than
i t could have with an APL. Support for the BOS program i s expected to be ongoing as the
engagement i s part of the Bank's medium- to long-term strategic involvement in the education
 sector. Structuring the project as a S I L will make it easier to eventually integrate the support to
BOS fully into the broader support to the SISWA.


C. IMPLEMENTATION

 1. InstitutionalandImplementationArrangements

39.      Funding for the BOS program comes from the central government budget. The overall
implementation of BOS-KITA i s under the responsibility of Directorate General of Primary and
Secondary Education (DGPSE) of the MoNE.

BOS management teams

40.      BOS management teams were established and have been functioning at the central,
provincial and district level since 2005. The central BOS management team i s located at the
DGPSE and i s responsible for program design; compiling a national aggregate of school data;
setting fund allocations and targets for each province; planning and conducting program
dissemination; training the provincial BOS management team; development and distribution of
the BOS operations manual; planning and conducting national monitoring and evaluation;
complaint-handling and monitoring complaint settlement by the provincial and district BOS
management teams; and consolidating provincial reports of BOS activities into a comprehensive
national report on BOS activities. The provincial BOS team i s locatedinthe provincial education
office, its main responsibilities are: allocations by districts and municipalities; distributing funds
to schools; conducting program dissemination at the provincial level; training the district BOS
management teams; and complaint-handling. The district BOS team i s located in the district
education office. This team i s responsible for: compiling and verifying enrolment data; providing
dissemination and training at the school level; investigating and handling complaints; and
conductingmonitoring and evaluation at the school level.

41.     The school-levelBOS team comprises the school principal, a teacher as treasurer and a
community/parent representative. This team works in conjunction with the school committee to
plan the use of funds, including identifying poor students to be exempted from school fees and/or
to receive support from the funds. The school committee chair i s a joint signatory to the budget
plan; with the implementation of the 2009 version of the BOS operations manual the chair will
also sign-off on the report of the use of funds to be placed on school notice boards. The school-
level team reports to the district team and i s accountable for the use of funds at the school level.




                                                  11

School committees

42.      The MONE Decree No. 044/U/2002 defined the school committee as the community
representative body at the school level with membership comprising parents, community leaders,
education professionals, private sector, education associations, teachers, NGOs and village
 officials. It must comprise a minimum nine members and the chairperson must come from
 outside the school. School committees were first introduced in 1998 under the SGP and SIGP
programs, and tasked with assisting in the selection of scholarships and providing oversight on
 school spending on grants. Since then, according to the findings of a recent (2008) quantitative
and qualitative study, school committees have evolved into effective governing bodies involved
in a much broader range of school activities. The study is based on a random sampling of 520
public primary schools and 1,560 parents of sampled students. Target respondents included head
masters, teachers, members of the school committees, students and parents. Six schools were
then chosen for in-depth qualitative study. Based on preliminary analysis, the majority of the
parents expressed that the school committees: stand up for school policies that are in line with
parent's needs (52 percent); work well with principals (57 percent) and that principals actively
look for parents' inputs and suggestions about the school (53 percent). Furthermore, school
committees have active oversight of financial matters. Specifically, 92 percent of the school
committees reported that they approved the annual budget plan and 91 percent of them received
expenditure reports; signed the contract for school rehabilitation (78 percent) and reviewed the
financial report of school rehabilitation (82 percent).

43.      Education Law No. 20/2003 also defines the District Education Committee as an
independent body representing civil society at the district level with the aim of improving
education service delivery. The committee supports this improvement by: (i)    providing advice to
assist in determining the school program and policy; (ii)     providing support such as financial
support, ideas, and activities for the implementation of the school program; (iii)providing
control over school programs for transparency and accountability; and (iv) providing mediation
and communication between the school and the community. Under the BOS program guidelines
detailed in the operation manual, the District Education Committee must sign-off the district
management team's report on school enrollment data and the allocation of funds (which are
determined by the enrollment data) as well as signing-off on receipt of funds by schools.

Flow of funds

44.      The provincial BOS teams distribute funds to schools quarterly, by transfer of funds to
each school account through the channelling bank. Any excess or shortage of funds received by
a school compared with its verified entitlement will be adjusted in the subsequent transfer of
funds or refundedto the provincial account. B y the end of the fiscal year, provincial accounts are
reconciledand any balance of funds returned to the state treasury account.

45.      This flow of funds follows a mechanism first instituted under the SGP and SIGP
programs - an aspect of these programs that functioned relatively well.           In addition, the
performance of the BOS flow of funds to schools has improved over time. In the first year of
implementation in 2005, BOS funds arrived on average three months late; while the school year
begins in mid-July, the DIPA (budget) was only issued in October and the BOS disbursement
only began in November. B y 2007, funds were only delayed by an average of two weeks for the
second, third and fourth quarters, and by six weeks for the first quarter.

BOS operationsmanual

46.     The BOS operations manual reflects lessons learned from the SGP and SIGP programs.
Since the introduction of the program, the BOS operations manual has been continuously
improved. The first BOS operations manual was issued in June 2005 and has been updated every
year since, with revised versions issued in March 2006 and January 2007. The revisions were
based on inputs from BOS management teams at the district and provincial levels, and a World
Bank-funded study.13 The 2006 revision of the manual included: (i)a more explicit and
consistent statement on the priority of focusing on poor students; (ii)funding BOS management
expenses such as stationery, photocopying, correspondence and report expenditures; (iii)a
statement that leftover BOS funds (after funds have been used for all other BOS components),
can be used to purchase visual aids equipment, study media, and school furniture; (iv) a
statement that BOS funding may not be used for activities that are already funded by central or
regional government sources, such as salaries for contract teachers and fees for overtime; (v)
inclusion of explanations regarding the payment of taxes; (vi) provision for forms for financial
reporting; and (vii) provision for more transparency through posting BOS allocations on school
notice boards.

47.     The BOS program i s currently being implemented under the 2007 operations manual.
The operations manual i s sound in most respects. The strengths among others include: (i)clarity
in the positive and negative lists on the use of funds; (ii) flow of funds mechanism; (iii)

further revise the manual in 2009 and this version will also cover improvements suggested by the

argument put forward. See Annex 9 for more details on the various scenarios with varying

already embedded in the government system.

   D. APPRAISAL SUMMARY

   A.      Economicand FinancialAnalyses

   53.     The economic analysis shows that the BOS program has brought important
   improvements to the education sector (see Annex 9). First, in almost all schools BOS funds have
   substantially increased school revenues. In more than 40 percent of schools included in the
   second Governance and Decentralization Survey (GDS2), the amount of funds received through
   the BOS program exceeded the total funds from other sources. Second, since the introduction of
   BOS, increased enrollment was observed at both primary and junior secondary levels in most
   provinces. The increase in the enrollment rate at the junior secondary school level i s particularly
   significant -evidence that the highcost of education to households was constraining access.

   54.     Certain features of the BOS program make i t particularly well equipped to counter
  corruption. Pre-BOS, a comparison of service level data from 94 schools in 16 districts
  participating in the first Governance and Decentralization Survey module indicates that as much
  as one third of the allocated resources may have failed to reach schools. The BOS program
  provides assurances by using a very simple and transparent formula and providing mechanisms
  for scrutiny both from the top, through the internal audit, and from the bottom, through
  community mobilization and oversight. Under the BOS program, where operational funds are
  received directly by schools and allocated independently, opportunities for greater SBM are
  created.


                                                               15

 55.     In 95 percent of the cases, these schools further claimed to have experienced positive
 benefits from SBM, and mostly observed improvements in students' grades (66 percent of
 schools surveyed), their attendance (29 percent of schools surveyed) and discipline (43 percent
 of schools surveyed).

 56.     These improved sector outcomes and processes brought about b y the BOS program are
 expected to generate important gains in economic terms. The first source of these gains i s
 through increasing lifetime earnings of those children who pursue their education to higher
 levels. It i s estimated that an increase in enrollment of half of a percentage point would generate
 a gain of close to US$14.5 million annually for Indonesia's households (see Annex 9).
 Additional benefits include savings to the education system obtained from lowering repetition
 rates. Using a simple estimate of per-pupil cost of public provision at each level of education, it
 i s estimated that even a modest decrease in repetition rates by half of a percentage point would
 translate into yearly savings of over US$36 million at the primary level and US$20 million at the
junior secondary level. Further savings are realizedthrough reduced leakage and corruption.

 57.     There i s strong justification for public investment in a program such as BOS. The BOS
 program first addresses equity concerns in education. Although education constitutes an
 attractive option for households to invest in, the inability of a large number of households to
 meet the present costs of a complete basic education despite the existence of significant future
 returns provides a strong rationale for a public subsidy. Without appropriate funding, Indonesian
 society as a whole may under-invest in basic education, not only because the poorest households
 face financial constraints but also because private investment decisions fail to take into account
 the social returns and externalities of basic education. These additional social returns, including
 society's value for equity and externalities brought about by the BOS program, cannot be
 measured but nonetheless constitute a significant component of the total benefits of the program.


B.       Technical

 58.     BOS has been under implementation since 2005. This appraisal i s based on empirical
evidence generated by independent studies contracted out by either M o N E or the Bank. The
assessment (2008) comprises a desk review of publications by MoNE, MORA,the World Bank
and other development partners, the Indonesian research institutes (SMERU), and individual
researchers. In addition, the assessment analyzed BOS targeting, coverage and utilization data
from the GDS2, as well as the Susenas14 (2005 and 2006 panel data), produced by the Central
Bureau of Statistics (BPS).Data from the GDS 2 survey include quantitative and qualitative data
from 133 districts in 31 provinces from over 12,500 households, 2,500 village leaders, 1,200
school principals, and 400 district officials. In terms of the quantitative impact assessment of the
BOS program, the assessment draws heavily on previous work conducted by the World Bank's
poverty team (IndoPov) in early 2007. Research findings of the Indonesian BOS are consistent
with international evidence on school grants (see Annex 10).

59.      BOS achievements. After two years of operation, the indications are that the BOS
program has operated efficiently and already had a positive impact. Enrollments in primary and
junior secondary schools have continued to increase, despite the economic shock felt after the

doubling of fuel prices. The BOS program has operated smoothly, experiencing only a few
operational bottlenecks.

60.       Using data from various household surveys and the MoNE's MIS system, a 2008
technical assessment carried out by Bank staff identified a number of positive developments to
which the BOS program contributed during its first years of operation. Most notably, the BOS
program has made a significant contribution in reducing school fees, increasing operational
budgets for schools, and rolling out an almost nationwide introduction of SBM:
    Reduction of school fees. On average, school fees were reduced by 37 percent in primary
    schools and 39 percent in junior secondary schools (in real terms). Data from the second
    Governance and Decentralization Survey, (GDS2) show that out of 1,170 school committees,
    88 percent indicated that schools had released poor students from paying various school fees
    and charges. Moreover, an estimated 76 percent of school committees reported that their
    schools hadexempted all students intheir schools from all school fees and ~harges.'~
    Reduced drop-out and increased enrollment rates. The decline in fees for (poor) students
    appears to have at least partially contributed to higher enrollments and lower drop-out rates.
    While it may still be too early to draw concrete conclusions, increases in enrollments and
    reductions in drop-out rates are observed when analyzing pre-BOS and post-BOS data.
    Furthermore, focus group discussions within the context of basic free education find that the
    BOS program encourages parents to support their children's transition from primary to
    secondary school.
    Increased operational budgetsfor schools. The BOS program has become the largest source

Financial Management

67.     The Bank has carried out a financial management assessment of the program. This
assessment included an evaluation of the BOS operations manual against the COS0 framework,
review of the audit reports of the program for the past three years and some field reviews. The
conclusion of the assessment i s that the controls are working reasonably well, although there i s
room for improvement. The strengths of the BOS program among others include: an operations
manual that i s comprehensive and provides reasonable guidance; transparency in transfer of
funds to schools; good controls at the school level where school committees are active and
oversee expenditures; internal audit; external audit; recording and maintenance of supporting
documentation at schools; and monitoring from the district and provincial teams. The financial
management assessment concluded that the current BOS arrangements satisfy the Bank's
minimumrequirement for financial management arrangements as laidout under OP/BP 10.02.

68.     In order to ensure that schools receive funds in line with their entitlement and that these
funds are used for their intended purpose, schools are required to report on enrollment data,
verified by school supervisors, and report on expenditures by category. The school committee
provides an oversight role on use of the funds at schools. Monitoring rests with the BOS teams at
the district level to ensure that this arrangement i s carried out properly.

69.     Public awareness and information campaign of the BOS program have been carried out
on television and in newspapers. Transparency on the availability of funds i s carried out by
advertising twice yearly the names of schools, number of students and fund allocations in local
newspapers. While there has been some budget reduction for safeguards in 2008, provincial
teams are obliged to continue such advertising, but on a smaller size. With regardto transparency
inthe use of funds, schools are obliged to post budget plans on school notice boards. This will be
further enhanced in the revised 2009 BOS operations manual when the posting of expenditure
reports on school notice boards will also be required.

70.     Complaint-handling and sanctions. The BOS program has an established complaint-
handling mechanism, including a toll-free number and email address for complaints. The role
and responsibilities of each team for recording and following up complaints are set out in the
BOS operations manual. While there i s evidence of follow-up and resolution, more systematic
recording i s needed. The Government plans to strengthen this aspect by adding a cell phone
number for short message text (SMS) and adding dedicated staffkonsultants to the central team
for recording follow up and resolutions. Sanction mechanisms, as stipulated in the BOS
operations manual, also need to be more rigorously monitored and recorded. In addition, the
BOS program will be subject to the Bank's Anti-Corruption Guidelines, which are on MONE's
website and will be included in the 2009 revision to the BOS Operations Manual. The Bank will
retain all of its remedies for suspension, cancellation and acceleration.

schools i s mainly the responsibility of district teams, which regularly visit schools in their

77.     Most of the categories of expenditures to be financed under the project do not constitute
procurement because they are operating expenses for the BOS program, which will be subject to
overall fiduciary controls. The estimated percentage of expenditures for items that may require
procurement i s about 20 percent for purchases of new books, building maintenance and media
and learning materials (categories 2, 7 and 13 of Table 3). The estimated individual contract cost
of items that may be purchased under the above three categories ranges from US$lOO to a

undertake centralized purchasing and distribution system at district level. Additionally, such an
approach would be contrary to the concept of the BOS program in terms of the school managing

the procurement system, especially when preparing the larger Sector-Wide Approach. Such
options will include a review of good practices from countries of comparable context, and would
investigate the use of alternative approaches such as framework contracts and other instruments.

labor from the community where a school i s located. Decisions to conduct purchases are made
by the school team, which i s required to undertake the purchase making the best use of money.
This is usually done through an informal (and undocumented) solicitation for prices. The
community i s involved in deciding which items to purchase and participation will be further
enhanced by the development of purchase plans that will be posted on school notice boards. At

 1.        Country Context and Poverty Strategy

Indonesia i s Southeast Asia's largest country with over 220 million people and a lower middle-
income economy in which GDP per capita reached US$1,920 in 2008. Since the economic and
political crisis of the late 1990s, Indonesia has undergone remarkable institutional
transformations. The first phase, from 1998 to 2003, was a period of radical political and
economic change, typified by democratization and the largest ever decentralization of
government which transferred the responsibility for most public service delivery to the sub-
national governments. The second phase, from 2004 to 2008, has been a period of consolidating
democratic institutions and returning to macroeconomic stability, most notable in Indonesia's
first direct presidential elections in 2004 and its reducing debt levels to below 35 percent of
GDP.

Indonesia has resumed higher levels of growth and, as a result, has now returned to middle-
income status. Over the past few years, economic growth accelerated to a 10-year high of 6.3
percent in 2007 and i s expected to reach 6 percent in 2008, despite the current slowing of the
global economy. In the past year, the growth rate was sufficient to reduce poverty from 17.8 to
16.6 percent and reverse the recent trend towards jobless growth, with unemployment falling
from 10.3 to 9.1 percent. A strategy of fiscal consolidation has resulted in s i nificant reduction
of government debt levels, while inflation has largely been kept under control.F5

Nevertheless, a large number of people remain vulnerable to poverty. Measurement of poverty in
Indonesia i s relatively volatile because of the large number of households clustered around the
poverty line. Small price changes in goods consumed by poor households have a relatively large
effect on the proportion of households classified as poor. Thus, the increase incommodity prices
i s a source of concern, particularly since food commodities account for a significant proportion
of household expenditure among low income groups.

Following two price hikes on fuel products in March and October 2005, the Government reduced
regressively targeted fuel subsidies and reallocated US$1.4 billion in public spending to "pro-
poor" sectoral programs. The Fuel Subsidy Reduction Compensation Programs in these sectors
were intended to alleviate the effects of the fuel subsidy reduction on the poor through sectoral
programs, namely: (i)          the School Operational Grant Program (Bantuan Operasional Sekolah, or
BOS) and the Student Assistance (Scholarship) Program (Bimbingan Konsultasi Mahasiswa, or
BKM) in the education sector, which aimed to reduce or eliminate school fees; (ii) Askeskin               the
program in the health sector, which aimed to increase healthcare access and utilization for the
poor, and (iii)      the rural infrastructure program, which aimed to improve village infrastructure
through a participatory process, while also creating temporary employment. This report assesses
only the coverage, targeting and impacts of the first program for the education sector, the BOS
program, which focused on primary andjunior secondary students.

2.      Education Sector Context

Education i s central to the Government's development agenda. Education spending has increased
significantly in the years since the economic crisis. In real terms, education spending doubled
between 2000 and 2006. In 2007, spending on education was more than for any other sector,
reaching an equivalent of US$14 billion, or more than 16 percent of total government
expenditure. As a share of GDP (3.4 percent) this i s comparable to other similar countries. Over
the initial period of this project, education spending is expected to reach at least US$18 billion
per annum.

The Indonesian school system i s immense and diverse. With over 40 million students and 2.6
million teachers in more than 200,000 schools, it i s the fourth largest education in the world
(behindonly China, India and the United States). Two ministries are responsible for managing
the education system, with 84 percent of schools under the MoNE and the remaining 16 percent
under MORA. Private schools also play an important role. While only 7 percent of primary
schools are private, the shares increase to 56 percent injunior secondary and 67 percent in senior
secondary.

2.1     Key issues: thejunior secondary bottleneck

Inthe academic year 2006/07, about 190,000 public andprivate schools enrolled over 40 million
pupils at the basic education level. As stated inLaw No. 20/2003, formal education in Indonesia
begins with kindergarten comprising two years, followed by primary school comprising the first
six school grades. Graduates from primary school continue with secondary education, which i s
divided into junior and senior secondary levels, each comprising three grades and including two
distinct tracks: general and vocational. Graduates from the senior secondary cycle can pursue a
diploma or graduate program or go on to other types of higher education including university. In
the academic year 2006/07, the distribution of students across these levels of education was: 6
percent in kindergarten; 57 percent in pre-school and primary education; 18 percent in junior
secondary education; 13 percent in senior secondary; and 6 percent in higher education. The
dominance of the primary education cohort reflects both the age structure of the Indonesian
population and the major challenge of ensuring the transition between the primary and secondary
cycles.

Since the 1970s, enrollment rates have increased significantly. Net primary enrollment rates
increased from 72 percent in 1975 to 93.5 percent in 2006 (while the gross rate stood at 110
percent). The net enrollment rate for junior secondary education rose even faster but started from
a low level of 18 percent in the 1970s and only reached 67 percent in 2006 (with a gross
enrollment rate of 81.9 percent). Enrollment in senior secondary has also been rising, although at
a more modest pace.

2.2     The impact of macro conditions: higherfood andfuel prices tighten budget constraints
at the household and national levels

As prices of basic commodities rise, so does the cost of education for families. Although basic
education i s supposed to be provided free at all public schools, household surveys reveal the
widespread persistence of fees collected at the school level. These fees and other education costs
continue to represent a significant obstacle to increasing secondary enrollment - where direct
and opportunity costs rise steeply. The underlying market failures are exacerbated by the high
and rising prices of fuel and foodstuff, which significantly tighten the budget constraint on




                                                    31

 Indonesia's poorer households. Food prices are likely to remain high in the medium term27and
 will absorb a larger share of disposable income of poor households.28

 Rising international fuel prices are putting considerable pressure on the government budget.
 Increased budgetary outlays on energy subsidies (which account for about one fifth of the central
 government budget and about 4 percent of GDP at present) have forced the Government to allow
 fuel prices to rise by an average of 29 percent in late May and put the public expenditure
 program under significant pressure. At the macro level, Indonesia's yearly financing needs are
 estimated to reach US$15 billion.

The BOS program will provide much needed relief at two levels: easing the burden on public
 finances and reducing the cost of education faced by Indonesia's poorer households.

3.         The Demandfor Education:Constrainedby Costs despiteHighReturns

3.1. Basic education remains costlyfor cash-constrainedhouseholds

The BOS program aims to ensure that demand for basic education will be sustained and
expanded. The block grants will reduce the need for schools to charge enrollment and
registration fees and are expected to also translate into higher quality teaching. The cost of
education, which includes direct expenses such as formal and informal school fees, the cost of
transportation, uniforms, books, supplies, etc. and indirect costs from foregone earnings, i s
believed to be limiting demand at the margin for primary education but significantly when it
comes to junior secondary education. The total monthly cost for households of putting one child
through primary and junior secondary comes to about Rp 34,000 and Rp 70,000, respectively.
These costs are substantial, considering that for example the average cost of junior secondary
schooling i s three times the amount currently spent by the poorest Indonesian households on
education.

4.       The BOSProgram

The BOS program i s a demonstration of the Government's commitment to the constitutional
obligation of providing free basic education of adequate quality for students. The Indonesian
Constitution and the Law on the National Education System both emphasize that all Indonesian
citizens have the right to education (Constitution 1945 Amendment 111, Article 31:2) and the
Government has an obligation to finance basic education without charging fees (Law No.
20/2003, Article 34). The BOS program, which provides funding to schools for non-salary
operational expenditures, aims to reduce schools fees, as well as support quality-enhancing
expenditures for all public and private primary and junior secondary schools in Indonesia. Prior
to the BOS program, schools hadvirtually no funds to spendon operational expenses.

The BOS program covers around 40 million students or about 98 percent of the total national
primary andjunior secondary students in 2007.*' The program distributed 73 percent of its funds
to the primary level and the remaining 27 percent to the junior secondary level. The per-pupil
BOS allocation has increased from Rp 235,000 to Rp 276,000 per primary student, and from Rp
324,500 to Rp 376,000 perjunior secondary student per year since 2007.

In 2007, the BOS program spent US$1.3 billion (Rp 11.8 trillion), which represents about 9
percent of total education spending in Indonesia (and 23 percent of central government education
spending). This includes a new BOS sub-program that specifically covers school books. After its

 The following section summarizes the strengths and addresses the weaknesses of the existing
 government M&Eand complaint-handling systems (see also Annex 12for specific timelines).

M&E capacity development: The Government is responsible for tracking the progress of BOS
 implementation, with a focus on what works, what does not work, and why.               Specifically, it is
responsible for: (a) routinely monitoringproject activities, supplementedby ad hoc monitoringbased
 on findings from the routine monitoring; and (b) producing information feedback and dissemination
 to relevant stakeholders and the public. Preliminary analysis of the current BOS M&Esystem reveals
 weaknesses in collecting data related to the indicators listed above. For example, the system for
routine monitoring i s limited in coverage and there i s little harmonization of data collection and
reporting systems across various levels of BOS implementation (Le., district, province, central
 government). This presents challenges in assessing the effectiveness of BOS implementation
nationwide. In light of these weaknesses, the BOS program will receive capacity development
 support for data quality and management, This should enable the use of results in policy-making,
particularly at the district level. This work will be supported by the trust funds in selected districts
with TA already inplace, inNovember 2008 and April 2009, and complementedby similar programs
with the support of various development partners. The capacity building will include strengthening
provincial and national data on the use of BOS resources by expenditure type. The target audience
will be relevant staff (Le., planning and M&E staff) and decision-makers (i.e., program managers) at
district education offices, as well as community representatives. This capacity development work is
intended to ensure local government buy-in to the existing central M&E system and also encourage
local ownership. Procurement of service providers for capacity development will begin in November
2008 usingtrust funds.

Third-party data quality audit through independent monitors. BOS will use an independent third-
party data quality review mechanism to ensure the objectivity and integrity of collected information.
Such periodic reviews will help to improve the collection, processing and dissemination of data.
These reviews will be ongoing throughout the course of BOS. Each of the indicators will be assessed
annually for quality and integrity (including validity, reliability, timeliness, precision, adequacy and
practicality). The audit includes a mix of document and record reviews, random site visits, and key
informant interviews, and will take place at various levels (central, provincial, district and school).
However, emphasis will be at the district and school levels. At the district level, the reporting related
to components -such as program socialization and training, targeting (especially for the poor) and
fund allocation, disbursement and its achievement within a specific time frame, and program
administration -will receive particular scrutiny. At the school level, the focus of the data audit will
be on the utilization of BOS funds and compliance with the BOS operations manual.

The audit will result in a report summarizing the reviews and describing weaknesses found in the
data collection methods, the handling and processing o f the data by responsible entities and the
reporting procedures. The report will make recommendations for overcoming these weaknesses and,
if this is not possible because of practicality issues or cost-effectiveness, the report will recommend
replacement indicators or data sources that are more appropriate or efficient. Findingsfrom the data
audit will be communicated to relevant stakeholders, Le. DGMPSE, BOS managers at the central as
well as local levels, school recipients, development partners and the public. The main purpose of
communicating findings i s to ensure accountability and motivate stakeholders to action, and to
incorporate the findings from the data quality audit into the M&E capacity program mentioned



                                                    39

 above. Audit findings will be presented four times throughout the project (October 2008, February
 2009, August 2009, and February 2010). This work will be supportedby the trust funds.

Final process evaluation: Given that the project implementation i s structured to cover all basic
education students in the entire country in order to avoid identifying a credible comparison group
 through experimental or quasi-experimental methods, the project team concluded that it was not
feasible to conduct a rigorous impact evaluation of the BOS program. Moreover, a three-year time
 span will likely not result inobservable changes in student testing data -the likely primary outcome
 of interest in an impact evaluation. However, a final process evaluation will allow for the summary
 of monitoring data and analysis covering changes that take place over the life of the project. This
will focus on comparisons of indicators before and after the project, what services were provided to
whom and in what way, and whether the project achieved objectives represented by indicators above
and in the BOS operations manual. The evaluation will also look at the incremental contribution of
BOS-KITA to its predecessor, the BOS program. While BOS-KITAi s largely the continuation of an
existing project (Le., it maintains the status quo), some revisions to the BOS operations manual
(recommended in Annex 7) may lead to changes in the indicators above, such as improved funds
allocation. The process evaluation will look at whether the recommended changes were incorporated
into the BOS operations manual, and if the process of implementation was affected by such changes.

Note that this study will be complemented by two others detailed in Annex 13. One will look
qualitatively at factors that contribute to efficacy of SBM, and another will identify what strategies of
public informationare most effective.

Strengthening complaint-handling and resolution: The current complaint-handling mechanisms
established under the BOS program lack a good recording and reporting system that allows for the
efficient tracking of cases. This issue i s compounded by the fact that decentralization has curtailed
the authority of central agencies to track the progress of resolution of alleged corruption cases at the
local level. To some extent, this situation adversely affects the follow-up actions required to resolve
complaints. Thus, BOS-KITA will include support to strengthen fundamental structures related to
receiving and addressing complaints at the local level. This will include better management of
complaints, investigation of alleged malpractice, and the production of reports and tracking of the
progress of complaint resolution. A set of standard operating procedures for complaint-handling and
resolution including recording and reporting system will be established with technical assistance
(TA). This will include the development of a mechanism to disseminate complaint-handling and its
follow-up actions, which will include a web-based system for managing incoming complaints and
disseminating information on resolution. Capacity-building will be provided to strengthen the

 1.      Institutional and ImplementationArrangements

The BOS program funding comes from the central government budget. The overall
implementation of BOS-KITA i s under the responsibility of Directorate General of Primary and
Secondary Education (DGPSE) of the MoNE. At the central level, the central BOS management
team i s responsible for: program design; compiling a national aggregate of school-level data;
setting fund allocations and targets for each province; planning and conducting program
dissemination; training the provincial BOS management team; development and distribution of
the BOS operations manual; planning and conducting national monitoring and evaluation;
complaint-handling and monitoring complaint settlement by the provincial and district BOS
management teams; and consolidating provincial reports of BOS activities into a comprehensive
national report on BOS activities.

BOSmanagementteams

The provincial BOS team i s established under a governor's decree and i s located at the
provincial education office. This team i s responsible for: setting allocations by districts and
municipalities; distributingfunds to schools; conducting program dissemination at the provincial
level; training the district BOS management teams; and complaint-handling. The provincial team
also publishes the names of schools and the magnitude of the fund allocations in local
newspapers.

The district BOS team i s established by the respective district headmayor's decree and located
at the district education office. This team i s responsible for: compiling and verifying enrolment
data; providing dissemination and training at the school level; investigating and handling
complaints; and conducting monitoring and evaluation at the school level.

The school-levelBOS team comprises the school principal, a teacher appointed as treasurer, and
a community/parent representative. This team works in conjunction with the school committee to
plan the use of funds, including identifying poor students to be exempted from school fees andor
to receive supports from the funds. The school committee chair i s ajoint signatory to the budget
plan, and in its enhance role report on use of funds to be placed on school notice boards. The
school-level team reports to the district team and i s accountable for the use of funds at the school
level.

School committees

The school committee i s the community representative body at the school level with membership
comprising parents, community leaders, education professionals, the private sector, education
associations, teachers, NGOs and village officials. It must comprise a minimum nine members
and the chairman must come from outside the school. School committees were first introduced in




                                                 48

 1998 under the SGP and SIGP programs, and tasked with assisting in the selection of
scholarships and providing oversight on school spending on grants.

The formation of school committees i s formalized in MoNE Decree No. 044/U/2002 which
defines their role and responsibility as: (i)advisory, involvement in the determination of the
school program and policy; (ii)     a supporting agency, providing financial support, ideas, and
activities for the implementation of the school program; (iii)      a controlling body, providing
control over the school programs for transparency and accountability; and (iv) a mediator,
providing mediation and communication between the school and the community.

Education Law No.20/2003 also defines the District Education Committee as an independent
body representing civil society at the district level with the aim of improving education service
delivery.   The committee supports this improvement by: (i)providing advice to assist in
determining the school program and policy; (ii)      providing support such as financial support,
ideas, and activities for the implementation of the school program; (iii)   providing a control over
school programs for transparency and accountability; and (iv) providing mediation and
communication between the school and the community. Under the BOS program guidelines
detailed in the operation manual, the District Education Committee must sign-off the district
management team's report on school enrollment data and the allocation of funds (which are
determined by the enrollment data) as well as signing-off on receipt of funds by schools.

Funding allocations and verifications

All public and private elementary and junior high schools in Indonesia are eligible to apply for
BOS funding. Since the allocation of BOS funds i s based on the number of students per school,

allocations can also be made at the school level.

Inorder to ensure that schools receive their full entitlements to andtimely disbursement of funds,

Project Description

BOS i s an evolution of Scholarships and School Improvement Program (SGP/SIGP)38 which
started in 1998/99, and closed five years ago, as a program to mitigate the effects of the Asian
economic crisis. The project provided block grants to more than 130,000 schools and
scholarships to more than six million students. Most of the basic principles that worked in the
SGP/SIGPprograms have been adaptedto the BOS program.

The Bank also financed several education projects that provided block grants for school
rehabilitation and construction, which later became the national model for school rehabilitation
and construction. The main features of the BOS program are: (a) provision of funds to schools
based on a per-pupil formula; (b) funds are directly channeled to the schools; (c) the use of funds
i s defined for a limited number of eligible items prescribed in the BOS operations manual; and
(d) oversight from parents and the community (school committee), school supervisors,
government monitoring and external monitoring.

The BOS program follows a recently approved community-driven development (CDD) project,
the PNPM pr~gram,~'in that it also supports a government-led program that channels funds
directly to local service-providers and ensures the use of funds for the intended purposes through
the provision of manuals, oversight and other fiduciary frameworks. A comparison of features
between the two programs i s summarized in Annex 8. As in the PNPM program, the BOS
program requires oversight on planning and use of funds, which i s sought from the community
through parents and the school committee. Transparency i s reinforced through the placement of
records on school notice boards, regular parent-teacher meetings and the publication of BOS
related details in local newspapers, together with the existence of a complaint-handling system
and sanctions for non-compliance.

While BOS coverage (190,000 schools) i s larger than the number of PNPMvillages (46,000), the
average amount of funds per school in the BOS program i s smaller than the amount per village in
PNPM, and the menu for the use of funds i s simpler and fixed for a limited number of eligible
prescribed items. While the PNPMprogram relies to a great extent on consultants and facilitators
to process sub-projects, the BOS program relies largely on district teams and sub-district school
supervisors andparents through school committees.

Budgeting

BOS program funding comes from the central government budget and is implemented through
the provincial level. The overall implementation of BOS-KITA i s under the responsibility of
DGPSE, MoNE.


Flow of funds

The province management team is responsible for distributing funds to each school. Funds are
released to schools quarterly as described below:
   (a)  Schools submit enrollment data along with the bank account details to the district
        education office.
  (b)   District management teams compile school names, verified numbers of students, eligible
        amounts (based on a per-student formula) and the banking information, and send this
        information to the province education office.
  (c)   Based on the DIPA budget allocated to the province, the provincial management team
        requests the treasury office to transfer funds to the provincial bank account.
  (d)   Through a MOU with the channeling bank, funds are transferred to schools directly into
        their bank accounts.
The provincial team will account for any excesshhortage of transfers to schools based on the
verified number of studentsinthe subsequent transfer.

Inorder to ensure that schools receive the funds to which they are entitled and in good time, the
following measures are being taken: (a) when the province transfers funds to the schools directly,
it publishes a list of schools and the amounts transferred to each school in the local newspaper
for transparency; (b) the District Education Committee holds monthly meetings with
representatives of all schools in the district. The issues relating to the schools, their eligibility to
receive BOS funds and fund usage are discussed at these district meetings; and (c) the District
Education Office assigns school supervisors to visit schools to ensure that the verified numbers
of students claimed by schools are accurate.

 Accounting
 Schools maintain separate bank accounts for BOS funds and are also required to maintain
 accounting books relating to the funds and supporting documents. Field reviews and the review
 of audit reports for past years indicate that, although there i s variance in practices between
 schools, schools are generally doing a good job in maintaining separate bank accounts and
 accounting for BOS funds. In 2009 MoNE i s planning carrying out a training program to
 enhance financial recording and reporting capacity in schools.


 Internal Controls

 At the school level, BOS funds are executed by a school team comprising the principal and a
 teacher acting as a treasurer. School bank accounts require two signatories. Schools maintain
 accounting books of funds and report expenditures on a quarterly basis. The school committee
provides oversight by approving the budget plan, which must be posted on the school notice
board. Regular monitoring of schools i s carried out by the district management teams and school
supervisors. Provincial teams also visit schools from time to time. The release of funds to
schools i s made on a quarterly basis. There i s reasonably good awareness of the program through
information campaigns and the publication in newspapers of participating schools' names, the
number of students attending each school and the amount of funds transferred to each school.

Verification and reconciliation of funds transferred to schools are carried out at the school,
provincial and central levels. Schools reconcile books with their bank statements. The provincial
teams prepare financial statements of funds received and transferred to schools. The system i s
being strengthened through bank account reconciliation reports. The reconciliation i s to be
carried out at quarterly intervals based on the payment order issued by Treasury Office,
remittance to schools and the balance of the provincial account. Reconciliation of provincial
accounts i s made quarterly and the relevant format i s provided. The central management team
reconciles all payment orders with DIPA budgetrealization.

In order to improve internal control, the Government plans to strengthen the role of school
committees, including their approval of reports on the use of funds on a monthly basis. School
committees will also be trained in using financial reports. These reports will also be posted on
school notice boards. While these changes will only take effect once the BOS operations manual
i s revised, the immediate measures will be made through DGPSE reminders to provincial and
district teams to carry out monitoring and supervision at schools.


InternalAudit

Both Inspector General (IG-MoNE) and inspectors of the local governments (Bawasda) conduct
internal audits, but the quality of these audits i s uneven and the audits are not coordinated. As a
result, there i s potential for duplication andor inadequate coverage. Internal auditors focus more
on compliance than internal controls. As agreed during preparation of the project IG-MoNE has
designed a framework for internal audits of the BOS program, which include, development of
BOS internal audit manual, training for both IG MoNE staff and Bawasda on the manual in
coordination with Bawasda in the field. The activities will take place starting January 2009.
Financingwill be provided by the Government.

Reporting and Monitoring

 Schools are required to submit quarterly financial statements to district teams on the use of funds
by category, which the district teams are expected to review. District teams report to provincial
teams on eligible schools and the respective number of students. The reporting from provincial to
central teams includes the amounts transferred, and the number o f schools and students by
district. The central team aggregates the data and produces reports on the number of schools, the
number of students and the amounts transferred to schools by province and by district. Thus,
there i s no aggregation of actual expenditure broken down by categories of expenditure incurred
by schools, as the MoNE considers that data for the entire population are not required. For
evaluation purposes, such data will be collected through surveys of representative samples. This
i s similar to the financial management system in most CDD projects.

The MoNE central management team will submit quarterly financial reports to the Bank,
covering (i)   amounts transferred to schools, the number of schools and students by province and
by district, and (ii) bank reconciliations of provinces, indicating funds received and transferred to
schools. The specific form and the content of the financial reports have been discussed and
agreed during appraisal. The financial report will be used as the basis of audit.

The Balitbang study of the programin2005/643reported that 93 percent of schools sampled were
supervised. The monitoring covers funds allocation, receipt, use of funds, enrollment data,
financial administration and community complaints, and classifies schools into three groups:
excellent, average/good and non-compliant. Schools classified as excellent, about 7 percent of
the total, receive a trophy. Schools classified as non-compliant receive reminders in accordance
to their non-compliance, ranging from light non-compliance recorded as "not good" in the school
visitors' book, to special tutorials at the district office, to written reminders, up to administrative
sanctions. About 11 percent of schools receive reminders for improvements in their school
visitors' books. Due to civil society awareness and the highvisibility of the BOS program, there
has been broad based external monitoring. The BOS manual also urges external monitoring by
NGOs, CSOs, Universities as well as local parliaments. Balitbang study cited above also
indicates that 64 percent of schools were monitored by NGOs and 20 percent of schools were
monitored by universities. In general, this external monitoring focused on progress in the use of
funds andcompliancewith the BOS operations manual.

While the BOS operations manual has clarified the different roles and responsibilities in the
monitoring of schools between the provincial and district teams, some overlap does occur. The
provincial team's responsibility should be to ensure that the district teams are carrying out their
respective roles effectively. Reminder letters from the central team to the provincial teams will
be issued immediately to this effect. Further improvements will also be made in the forthcoming
revision of the BOS operations manual to include better clarification and forms for scheduling of
monitoring and supervision of schools.

 Procurement arrangements
Procurement for the proposed project will be carried out in accordance with the World Bank's
 "Guidelines: Procurement under IBRD Loans and IDA Credits" dated May 2004; revised
 October 2006 and the provisions stipulated in the Legal Agreement. Due to the nature of the
project, which consists of very small value purchases (ranging from a US$lOO to US$2,000)
carried by a large number of schools at the district level, it i s unfeasible for the Borrower to
prepare a procurement plan and, therefore, a procurement plan will not be used at the project
level. In place of a project-level procurement plan, individual schools will prepare their own
purchase plans in a standard format to be defined in the proposed revision of the BOS operations
'manual to plan and control how they conduct procurement under their grants. These school-level
purchase plans will be quality-assured and their implementation monitored by the BOS District
Management Teams in order to ensure compliance with the legal agreement and BOS operations
manual.

Procurement of goods and works: Most of the categories of expenditures to be financed under
the project do not constitute procurement. Rather, those expenditures will be made in respect of
operating expenses for the BOS program, which will be subject to overall fiduciary controls.
The estimated percentage of BOS expenditures with items that may require procurement i s
around 20 percent including purchases of new books, building maintenance and media and
learning materials (categories 2, 7 and 13 of Table 3). All other categories of expenditures to be
financed under the project do not constitute procurement, as they do not involve the acquisition
by schools of goods, civil works or services. Rather, those expenditures will be made in respect
of operating expenses for the BOS program, such as for utilities, subsidies for poor students and
training for teachers and will be subject to the flow of funds, accounting, financial reporting and
auditing arrangements as stipulated in earlier sections of this annex. Furthermore, there will be
no selection of consultants.



                                                 59

 The estimated individual contract cost of items to be purchased under the above three categories
 ranges from US$100 to a maximum of US$2,000. Grouping of contracts at the district level i s
 not feasible because it would involve different schools scattered over the district and there i s no
 organizational system to undertake centralized purchasing and distribution at the district level. In
 addition, such an approach would be contrary to the concept of the BOS program in terms of
 schools managing their own funds.

 Decisions to conduct purchases are made by the school team which i s required to undertake
 purchases by making the best use of the money. This i s usually done through an informal (and
 undocumented) solicitation for prices. The community i s involved in taking decisions on which
 items to purchase thereby providing for a reasonable level of accountability. This will be further
 enhanced by the development of purchase plans that will be made public by posting on school
 notice boards. At the district level, the district management team will monitor and provide
 guidance to schools under their jurisdiction. With the assistance and oversight of these teams, the
 institutional arrangements under the project will be strengthened to ensure that the schools are
 purchasing, at reasonable prices, the prescribed items in their respective purchase plan. The
 documentary records provide the amount of funds used for different items as part of financial
 management procedures.

 As for works, procurement will be conducted usingcommunity participation in accordance with
 para. 3.17 of the Guidelines. Maintenance work consists of small works (less than US$l,OOO
 each) for minor school buildinglfacility repairs. For this purpose, the school will do the repairs
 itself by hiring one or two individual laborers from within the community where the schools are
 located. Although it i s not explicitly mentioned in the existing BOS operations manual, however,
 due to very small works that it involves, the current normal practice i s that the schools contract
 out the works on lump sum basis to laborers (including the material and labor costs). The labor
costs follow the local market rates, which are quite common in Indonesia. The existing BOS
 operations manual will be revisedto reflect this practice in 2009.

Currently, the procurement of goods follows shopping procedures. As part of the improved
process, the School Purchasing Committee will seek to obtain and compare at least three
quotations, wherever feasible. In exceptional circumstances, such as in the case of schools
 situated in remote locations, the School Purchasing Committee may proceed to award the
contract having obtained fewer than three quotations but, in such cases, it shall make a good faith
attempt to obtain three quotations and will record those efforts in the procurement files. Under
shopping procedures, the contract will be awarded to the lowest-priced quotation which meets
the minimumtechnical specifications cited in the Invitation to Quote.

In order to improve the procedures that will be applied to procurement under the BOS program,
the proposed revision of the BOS operations manual will include purchasing guidelines to
achieve economic and efficient use of funds under the responsibility of the School Purchasing
Committee at each school. The enhanced procurement procedures described above will be
reinforced by a robust set of banking, payment, financial reporting and audit requirements, which
will also be defined in the BOS operations manual. At the district level, the District BOS
Management Team will perform a monitoring and guidance role for schools under their
jurisdiction. Procurement ex-post review will be carried on annual basis as part of the overall

a physical verification of goods purchased and rehabilitation works executed.

DisbursementArrangements

The method of disbursement to be used is the reimbursement method. The Bank will disburse
against funds transferred to the schools. However, the actual expenditure incurred at schools will
be monitored closely through school committees at the school level and by the district
monitoring mechanisms. There will also be ex-post controls like internal and external audit.
The schools will report expenditure to the districts on a quarterly basis. However, there will not
be aggregation of the expenditure at the district level.. Each quarter, the BOS central
management team will prepare: (i)financial statements covering summaries of the amounts
transferred to schools, the number of schools and students by province and by district; (ii)
reconciliation of provincial accounts; and (iii)confirmation that the amounts transferred to
schools are determined based on the verified number of students and the standard set up by the
operations manual. These will be submitted to the Bank along with the authorized withdrawal
application for requesting reimbursement. These formats of these statements have been provided
to and agreed with the Bank.

The overall Bank financing of the BOS program i s 23 percent. However, as the Bank will only
reimburse expenditures incurred in a few quarters, the disbursement percentage for the financing



                                                    61

of the program i s set at 60 percent in order to meet the financing plan requested by the
Government. The Bank will reimburse up to US$120 million retroactively against expenditures
from September 1, 2008 to loan signing date. The Bank loan will be allocated into two
disbursement categories: (1) for the 2008 program, and (2) for the 2009 program. Category 1will
be ready for disbursement after effectiveness. Disbursements against Category 2 will be subject
to the receipt by the Bank of acceptable audit reports for FY08 expenditures and a revised BOS
operations manual. In addition, the Bank will only reimburse expenditures incurred for the
quarters after the adoption of the revisedBOS operations manual. Thus the disbursement profile
will be US$250 million in 2008 and US$350 million in 2009.

Use of funds by schools i s governed by the BOS operations manual and for financing of eligible
expenditures as defined in the manual. Supporting documentation evidencing these expenditures
will be kept at the school level. Assurance that the funds to schools were appropriately
determined and have been used for the intended purposes will be sought from audits,
independent monitors and Bank supervision. The Bank will retain its standard remedies for
suspension, cancellation and refund.

As the BOS program covers 200,000 schools there will be cases where funds are misused at the
school level. Cases of misuse may be intentional, where a principal or a teacher abuses their
trust. Misuse of funds may also be unintentional, for example where, because of unfamiliarity
with operational guidelines, funds are used for expenditures that are not eligible for BOS
spending (for example the purchase of computers, ceremonial activities or teacher uniforms). In
either case, the program guidelines detailed in the BOS operation manual require the refund of
such funds to the school so that they may be used for appropriate, eligible BOS activities. Inthe
first instance, where the misuse has been intentional the guilty principal or teacher i s required to
return the money to the school and also face administrative sanctions such as demotion,
relocation or termination of employment. In the latter case where the misuse was unintentional
the school i s obliged to refund the money to the BOS program from other sources of school
income so that it may be used for appropriate, eligible BOS expenditures. The BOS program
guidelines also allow for suspension of the program in the subsequent year for the whole district
or whole province should there be a serious systematic intentional misuse of funds. In addition,
the BOS program will be subject to the Bank's Anti-Corruption Guidelines, which are on
MONE's website and will be included in the 2009 revision to the BOS Operations Manual. The
Bank will retain all of its remedies for suspension, cancellation and acceleration.

1.      Purpose of the Audit

The purpose of the audit is to provide assurance on the financial management arrangements,
procurement arrangements and assurance that funds have been used for intended purposes, for
the BOS grants. This would include policies, procedures, and records established to identify,
assemble, analyze, classify, record and report on transactions and to maintain accountability for
the related assets and liabilities. It is expected that the external audit will go beyond giving
assurance on the accounts to include examination of aspects of compliance with laws and
regulations, internal controls, and detection of fraud and corruption.            Since the funding
allocations to the schools are based on student enrollment, it i s critical that the auditor provides
assurance on the student enrollment data at the schools. In addition the auditor will assess and
report on the achievement indicators of BOS Program objectives.

2.      Background

The BOS program, which was initiated in 2005, disburses block grants to all schools throughout
Indonesia based on a per-student formula. It i s Indonesia's most significant policy reform in
education financing in two important aspects: (i) grants to schools are based on a per-pupil
                                                   block
formula, which provides incentives for headmasters and teachers to focus on maintaining and
increasing enrollment; and (ii)   funds are directly channeled to the schools, which empowers
school managers by allowing them to choose how to best allocate the BOS grants.

BOS i s based on lessons learned from a number of Indonesian programs, most notably the
Scholarships and Grants Program (SGP) and the School Improvement Grant Program (SIGP)
which pioneered the provision of block grants to Indonesian schools to mitigate the impact of the
Asian economic crisis on student enrollment and drop-outs, and on lessons from international
experience. Based on there lesson and experience, the BOS program introduced School-based
Management (SBM) for the first time on a nationwide scale in Indonesia's education system,
reaching all schools whether public, private or religious.

The BOS programs supports school operations through financing essential recurrent expenditure
 items at school level. The Government uses unit-cost estimation to determine the amount of
BOS per student per year. The grant i s currently Rp 276,000 per pupil per year at SDMI level
 and Rp 376,000 at SMPMTs level. The overall objective of the BOS program i s to alleviate the
household financial burden of basic education, while improving the quality of education through
promoting better utilization of resources and school-based management.


 1.           Impact of the BOS Program

Household expenditure on school fees have been uniformly reduced across wealth quintiles
in Indonesia after the introduction of BOS.                                   Poorer households in Indonesia have more
children at primary school age and, since enrolments are almost universal, the benefit incidence
of any spending at the primary school level i s almost always pro-poor (assuming same quality of
services across all income groups). Analyzing primary school level targeting shows that the
percentage of households by decile that report having reduced fees closely follows the
percentage of households that have children enrolled in primary school. Of the households that
had at least one child in primary school and that received BOS at their school, 72.6 percent
reported that their monthly school fees were reduced in the 2004-05 academic year. At the junior
secondary level, 82.6 percent of households that had children enrolled at this level of schooling
and that received the BOS program reported fee reduction^.^^

The BOS program has demonstrated that it can reduce leakage. The BOS program uses a
 very simple and transparent formula (a simple per-capita allocation) and provides mechanisms

school.

3.      Rationale for Public Investment inBOS and Additional Non-measurable Benefits

As one of the largest public investments in education, BOS first of all addresses the equity
concerns in education. Although education constitutes an attractive option for Indonesian
households to invest in, the inability of a large number of Indonesian households to meet the
present costs of a complete basic education despite the existence of significant future returns
provides a strong rationale for a public subsidy. Without a public subsidy, society as a whole
may under-invest in basic education not only because the poorest household face financial
constraints, but also because private investment decisions do not take into account social returns


                                                                 75

and externalities of basic education.              These additional social returns, including Indonesian
society's value for equity, and externalities brought about by BOS, cannot be measured but
constitute a significant part of the total benefits of the program.

The financial sustainability of the BOS-KITA program is examined below through presenting a
number of different scenarios based on different estimates of the BOS program costs and size of
the Indonesia education public expenditure envelope.

BOS program costs are calculated by multiplication of the number of students enrolled at the
primary and junior secondary level each year by the amount of BOS funds to be paid on a per-
pupil basis. The latter costs are likely to change in the future, as in the previous sections of the
PAD it was already pointed out that at present the costs are lower than real unit costs at the
school level. In this financial sustainability analysis, a number o f different unit cost estimates are
used to assess the impact of changes in this variable on the total amount of BOS spending. In
addition, the number of students i s assumedto change due t.o enrollment increases resulting from
the program. Two different scenarios are used, one where the program has very limited impact
and enrollment continues as it did in the past (forecasting enrollments based on past enrollment
growth), and another scenario where these enrollments are higher due to positive impact of the
program on the number of children in schools.

As for the education public resource envelopes, two cases are examined, one high-case
scenario where past expenditure growth trends for the sector were used to project future spending
(with an annual growth rate of about 14 percent), and one lower-case scenario where growth
rates are assumed to be much smaller, due to lower government revenues and lower overall
growth for Indonesia (particularly as oil and food prices increase, and global economic turmoil
continues).

The project is perceived to be financially sustainable as long as the Government only
introduces limited unit-cost increases for the per-pupil funding formula of the BOS
program. The tables below indicate that after the BOS-KITA support ends, the Government will
have to return to BOS spending levels marginally lower as a percent of total public education
spending than was the case before the introduction of the World Bank-supported program
(before BOS-KITA at about 9.5 percent and after the BOS-KITA at about 8 percent for the first
few years).

However, this will only be the case if education expenditures continue to grow at a similar
rate to that seen in the past few years. If expenditures grow at a lower rate due to lower
revenue collection and GDP growth, the Government will have to spend more on BOS as a share
of total education expenditures, but the shares will still be close to the pre-BOS-KITA spending
levels, and are therefore perceived to be sustainable (at about 11percent in the first years after
BOS-UTA).

International Experience

In El Salvador, the SBM program, EDUCO, reported significant increases in enrollment over a
decade of implementation. In Guatemala, the SBM initiative aimed mainly at opening
educational opportunities for 50,000 out-of-school students per year until full primary school
coverage was attained. Today, the initiative provides services to children in virtually all regions
of the country. In a relatively short period of time, the program moved from a small innovative
pilot in 19 rural communities to a nationwide program reaching 3,600 communities. In El
Salvador, SBM school students demonstrate less absenteeism and higher retention rates. In
Mexico, similar SBM initiatives exhibit positive changes in drop-out rates, and failure and
retentionrates.

Interms of community participation, inElSalvador parents of EDUCO students were reportedto
be three times more likely to engage in day-to-day classroom activities than parents in traditional
schools. Parent councils conducted classroom visits four or five times more often than traditional
counterparts. As parental association meetings, 80 and 79 percent of parents' councils discussed

teacher discipline and attendance of school personnel, respectively, while corresponding figures
for traditional schools are only 62 and 38 percent, respectively. In Nicaragua and Honduras,
parents of students in autonomous schools report a greater coverage of issues and better
participation in school processes, respectively.

Impacts on quality have also been observed. In Nicaragua and Honduras, autonomous schools
are more likely to have curriculum standards and teacher guides and school conditions and
resources tend to be better than in traditional schools.50In Honduras, SBM schools tend to have
more learning materials than tradition non-SBM schools, possibly due to the fact that school
committees receive money directly to purchase such materials. InEl Salvador, there i s evidence
that school councils of SBM schools are more likely to discuss teacher attendance and
disciplinary measuresthan parents associations in traditional schools. Furthermore, teachers from
SBM schools inEl Salvador are found to be more likely to visit students' homes if students are
absent. In the Latin American scenario, SBM schools are less frequently closed, SBM teachers
are less likely to be absent and tend to work more hours than traditional school teachers.

As regards to school decision making autonomy, in Guatemala less than 2 percent of parents'
councils (of SBM schools) report dismissinga teacher, not significantly different from non-SBM
schools. However, the SBM schools report nearly three times as many instances of teachers
leaving schools, which may also be due to better salaries, job security and working conditions at
traditional non-SBM schools. In Nicaragua, stakeholders in autonomous schools make 10 out of
25 decisions pertaining to school operations and management at the school level, compared with
seven for traditional public schools.

Evidence on the impact of SBM on student learning achievement i s inconclusive. Data show that
in Guatemala SBM school students score significantly lower on country standardized tests than
their public school counterparts, while in El Salvador and Nicaragua the differences are
insignificant. InHonduras, SBM school students have higher averages in science. It i s found that
despite the fact that SBM schools in El Salvador have fewer days of teacher absenteeism than
traditional schools, student achievement in these schools was no different from that of traditional
schools. According to a World Bank study (2004),51when controlling for the characteristics of
students, teachers, or schools, the results show that the SBM initiative in Guatemala helped
students achieve higher scores in Spanish.

 Indonesian Experience

 The evidence for impacts of SBM in Indonesia on a variety of different indicators of school
 quality has been accumulating since its introduction in 1998. The evidence i s both positive and
 consistent over time and between different basic education projects and independent studies.
 Although by no means conclusive, the consistency of this evidence demonstrates the relevance of
 the SBM approach to Indonesian schools. However, SBM i s not yet universal. A weakness i s
 that the importance of transparency and accountability may not yet be well understood and
 implemented. This i s indicated by the extent to which some schools are not displaying their plans
 and budgets. Nevertheless, there are strong contradictory sources of evidence that schools are, in
 fact, opening-up and becoming more transparent in their work. A key factor i s the extent to
 which parents and communities enthusiastically participate in classes, continuing to build on past
 achievements.

 Evidence from the Basic Education Projects (1998-2006) suggests that most schools (94 percent)
 had their school block grant team actively involved in the management of the grants, and 93
percent o f the 1,284 schools surveyed had their school committees actively involved in the
monitoring and management of the grant program implementation. Ninety-seven percent of
 schools that received block grants reported an increase in the use of teacher aids and active-
learner methods. Evidence from the Sumatra Basic Education project (see Annex 2), which
 aimed to stabilize enrollment rates through a period of economic crisis, confirms that not only
didnet enrollment rates remain steady intarget districts but improved over a five-year period.

There are several ongoing large projects with other development partners supporting the
development of SBM in Indonesia. An early finding, based on field observations and
documented case studies of good practice, from the Decentralized Basic Education Project
(DBEP, Asian Development Bank, 2003), i s that SBM had positive impacts on school
management and community support. These include: communities contributing more to school
development than originally planned; improved quality of community managed rehabilitation;
the very rapid implementation of school plans; and schools being able to demonstrate some
immediate and observable quality outcomes from the implementation of school planning, a key
element of SBM. This i s demonstrated in the purchase and use of new textbooks and teaching
materials, the implementation of special classes and improvements in libraries. The same report
documents two specific case studies of SBM from primary schools in Lombok. Both case studies
illustrate positive outcomes in terms of school leadership, rehabilitation and community
participation.

The Managing Basic Education Project (2003 - 2007, USAID) has also documented in its
monitoring reports the impact of SBM (MBE, 2006). The final monitoring report notes that
implementation of S B M was widespread in the project provinces of East and Central Java and
generally very sound. Apart from positive management developments in schools, MBE records
the continuing growth in parent and community participation in both schools and in classrooms.
As inDBEP, MBEnotes the increased level of community enthusiasm for their schools released
by the introduction of SBM. Project evaluation studies indicate areas of weakness in SBM as
well as positive outcomes. Implementation i s not uniform nor i s it perfect. Nevertheless
important trends can be identified in some monitoring studies. For example, regular parental and

  developed in Indonesia.

 Evaluation of the social marketing and information campaign (SMZC).The overall objective
 of the SMIC i s to support government initiatives to socialize BOS guidelines and regulations in
 order to encourage effective parental involvement. This socialization will occur at the provincial
 and district levels, but will include dissemination of information at the school and community
 levels as the primary focus. The SMIC will investigate the best ways, specific to the Indonesian
 context, of designing and delivering information to parents and local communities so that they
 become aware of their rights and obligations under the BOS program. This information should
 allow them to effectively and efficiently participate in the planning and monitoring of the BOS
 program at their respective schools.

 The research question to be addressedby the SMIC i s whether public information about the BOS
 program has an impact on: (a) increasing enrollment rates, especially for junior secondary
 schools; (b) decreasing drop-out rates; (c) increasing transition rates (from elementary to junior
 high), (d) increasing the satisfaction of stakeholders (schools, parents-school committees,
 parents); and (e) the number of complaintshnquiries filed and resolved. The study will have two
 design elements: first, to confirm that a social marketing and information campaign does have an
 impact on the above variables and, second, to identify the most effective and efficient media and
 strategies to achieve that impact. The study will use randomized experiment where districts will
 be selected randomly to become treatment and control groups. The results of the study will be
 shared with the Government to inform the design and implementation strategies to be undertaken
 inanational campaign of social marketing of the BOS program to be mountedas an integral part
 of the sector-wide approach supported by the SISWA project currently under development.

 Feasibility study considering an independent monitoring system. A feasibility study will be
 conducted to examine the pros and cons of an independent monitoring and complaint-handling
 unit. Three key issues of focus for the feasibility study are: (i)the differentiation of roles
 between an independent monitoring unit and the internal monitoring unit that i s already in place;
 (ii)ensuring the sustainability of this independent monitoring unit while in the meantime
 maintaining its neutrality; and (iii)establishing whether an independent body should be set up in
 order to address sensitive and complicated complaints. On the first issue, lessons will be
 particularly drawn from the experience of the Central Independent Monitoring Unit (CIMU),
 which was established for the SGP program and operated between 1999 and 2003. On the second
 issue, this study will explore several operational modalities for the independent monitoring
 arrangements for BOS. NGOkommunity participatory monitoring will be tested as part of the
'feasibility study for independent monitoring unit. NGOs can be hired to do independent

 monitoring or to train local communities on participatory monitoring and the results evaluated to
 see what works, what does not work, and of those that work, reveal how they work and which
 are feasible for replication nation-wide.

 Qualitative analysis of school committees and SBM. A qualitative study will be undertaken to
 provide insights into: (i)how groups of community representatives (school committees) are
 choosing to use BOS funds; (ii)           whether the uses of funds match community needs; (iii)                      the
 decision-making capabilities of school committees; and (iv) the impact that the projects they
 initiate have on student learning. The case studies will be designed to document the specific
 factors that support or impede smooth functioning of school committees.

 Capacity development of school committees will be conducted in areas including: the
development of integrated and participative school planning; planning-based budgeting; cash,
 asset and resource management systems; procurement procedures and controls; public reporting;
 and the change inmindset, ethic set and code of conduct.
