                                     I. INTRODUCTION

1.     Turkey is implementing an ambitious competitiveness and employment reform
agenda. The program aims to raise export competitiveness and foreign direct investment by
improving the investment climate, enhance the efficiency and depth of the financial sector, and
broaden employment generation, so that growth dividends can be widely shared.

2.     Important reforms under competitiveness and employment reform agenda over the
last year include:

       (i)   the enactment of the new research and development law; the reform of customs
             administration and the amendment to the Land Registry Law; and the satisfactory
             advancement of the privatization program, all measures that will improve the
             investment climate and thus contribute to raising exports and FDI;

       (ii)theapprovalofthenewInsuranceLawandtheenactmentofregulationstoimprove
             corporate governance in the capital markets, which will help to mobilize domestic
             and foreign savings; and

       (iii)the approval ofthe first phase ofthe labor market reform, as part of abroader
             strategy to improve employment outcomes and thus expand job opportunities in
             Turkey.

3.     This Second Competitiveness and Employment Loan (CEDPL 2) supports these
advances in the implementation of the government's competitiveness and employment
agenda (reform program) since mid-2007. While the first in a series of two programmatic
operations (CEDPL 2 and CEDPL 3), it builds on the initial CEDPL (Board Date: June 28,
2007), a stand-aloneloan, that supportedthe early implementation of the program.

4.     Turkey's future competitiveness and employment agenda is expected to include
additional major reforms, such as: the passage of a new Commercial Code, the completion of
the second phase of the labor market reforms, and the approval of a new capital markets law. A
possible follow-up CEDPL 3, the second in a series of two programmatic operations, is
envisaged to support such future reforms.

5.     The government's Competitivenessand Employment program is a central pillar of
Turkey's 9thDevelopmentPlan, and the proposed loanis a cornerstoneof the 2008 Country
PartnershipStrategy (CPS) which fully integrates the Bank's activities for FY08-11into the 9Ih
DeveZopment Plan. Measuresundertaken by the Turkish administration and supportedunder the



                                               1

program are expected to contribute to raise the average annual export growth rate; raise FDI
inflows; and increase the labor force participation, especially o f the young and women.

6.       The Program carries several risks. From the macroeconomic side, given the global
downturn Turkey's continued reliance on external finance creates significant risk. Inparticular,
a sudden capital outflow and disorderly depreciation would have an adverse impact on growth
and employment. Key to mitigating this risk i s government preparedness for potential downside
scenarios for the international financial system and their impacts on Turkey. On the program
side, all CEDPL 2 supported reforms have been implemented, and the risk o f reversal i s low.
Among three key future reforms expected to be supported by the planned CEDPL 3 (second
phase labor market reforms, the enactment o f the new Commercial Code, and the enactment of
the new Capital Market Law), the second-phase labor-market reform presents highrisks because
o f the intrinsic political economy challenges o f introducing regulation to make labor markets
more flexible.

                                           11.        COUNTRYCONTEXT

OVERVIE     w

7.       The external economic environment for developing countries has deteriorated
markedly in 2008. The outlook for 2009 is exceptionally uncertain. World economic growth in
2009 i s expected to be below 2 percent, with the EU, Turkey's main export market, expected to
experience recession or near recession.' Inaddition to slowing demand for exports, capital flows
towards developing countries have fallen dramatically relative to the highs o f 2007 (from
US$472 billion to US$312 billion for January to August of each year). The pricing o f emerging
market risk has altered correspondingly. The spread between the EmergingMarketsBond Index
(EMBI+) and US Treasury bills has risen from 239 basis points at end-2007 to 612 basis points
as o f November 6, 2008. This increase in part reflects current financial volatility but may also
reflect rising risk aversion towards emerging market assets.

8.       Turkey, with high external financing requirements and current account deficits, is
at risk from declining internationalliquidity. Turkey's gross external financing requirement
(current account needs, medium- and long-term amortizations as well as short term
amortizations) in2009 i s expected to be o f the order o f US$130 billion. External vulnerability i s
underlined by consistent real exchange rate appreciation in recent years: comparing to a 1998
baseline (to abstract from movements before and after the 2001 crisis), by the third quarter o f
2008 the REER had appreciated by over 50 percent. Yet the Turkish economy would be much
more resilient to a sudden capital outflow and real depreciation than it was in2001: public sector
debt is lower and better structured, and banks are well capitalized and not significantly exposed
to currency risk. A capital reversal would thus be felt most acutely in the corporate sector, with
effects on growth, employment, and poverty.

9.      Key to reducing these risks will be strong fiscal and monetary management and

macroeconomic management, especially settingrealistic fiscal targets and meeting them; and (iii)
continued structural reforms to drive growth and decrease economic vulnerability.

22.     In May 2008, as part of the preparationof Turkey'smedium-termfiscal framework,
the authorities revised the target for the primary fiscal surplus of the total public sector for

of GDP. The revision reflected three elements: (1) an increase in investment allocations to the
government's flagship South Eastern Anatolian (GAP) program; (2) a decision to increase

contributions as part o f the labor market reforms. Turkey's fiscal performance during January-

23.     Fiscal policy, while still consistent with debt sustainability and macroeconomic
stability, has become more accommodative in recent months. Increased potential budgetary
pressure stem from, inter alia, an August 2008 adjustment in the civil service salary structure,
increased central government transfers to local administrations, and increased deficits from local
administrations and state-owned enterprises. These pressures have appeared in September budget
realizations and projections for the rest of the year. As a result, the authorities now forecast a
primary budget balance of 2.7 percent o f the GDP in 2008-implying           a 0.8 percentage point
deterioration relative to the Medium Term Fiscal Framework (MTFF). At the same time, the
expected central government's overall deficit, forecast at about 1.4 percent o f GDP (versus the
target o f 2.0 percent), will lead to a further fall inthe public debt-to-GDP ratio in 2008 (to below
40 percent from 41.5 percent in2007).

                          OUTLOOK

26.       Given the global economic crisis, Turkey faces lower growth and significant
downside risksin the near term. Bank staffs growth forecast for Turkey in2009 has now been
revised to around 2 percent, with the possibility of a significant economic downturn.
Furthermore, economic activity is subject to a high degree of risk given global uncertainty. The
main question facing Turkey is whether capital inflows can be maintained to avoid a disorderly
unwinding of foreign exchange exposure, which lies mostly in the corporate sector. Were this to
occur, the key mitigation measures would be the rapid adoption of financial-sector measures to
protect liquidity and confidence in the banking sector plus fiscal measures to protect private-
sector access to limited domestic credit and limit the impact on economic activity and
employment.

27.       Analysis by Bank staff indicates the wide range of scenarios that are possible for
2009. The main driver of the growth rate i s the external adjustment required by assuming
different rollover ratios on existing external debt amortizations. Current market reports suggest
that corporations expect to roll over a substantial share of remaining 2008 amortizations. Further
shocks to global liquidity could lower this ratio significantly in the near term, and this potential
variability was captured in the simulations run by staff. These scenarios would involve varying
adjustments to asset allocations (e.g., reserve accumulation, public versus private debt issuance,
and householdholdings of foreign exchange), the exchange rate, inflation, and nominal and real
interest rates.

28.       The relative strength of balance sheets in the financial sector implies that the
valuation effects such downside scenarios imply could be absorbed without a systemic
threat. Price effects from higher nominal interestrates would be felt through the lower value of
government paper on banks' balance sheets, but direct spillovers from the global financial crisis,
through bank ownership structure or exposure to sub-prime assets, are likely to remain limited.
Majority-owned foreign banks operating in Turkey are small, making up 15 percent of total
assets, while foreign banks hold no controlling interest in any large domestic banks,6and the
value of these franchises does not depend on the balance sheets of their foreign shareholders.
Moreover, Turkish banks do not have significant exposure to mortgage-backed securities.

The Turkish banking has undergone a significant transformation since the 2001 crisis and is now very resilient after significant
restructuring and consolidation, but some challenges remain:

The main change relative to 2001 is that the bankingsystem is now much less exposedto market risks and that there is increased
transparency about them:

     The banking system's net foreign currency position is now almost in balance, amounting to about half a percent of capital.
     Moreover, the banking sector itself provides the market with more detailed information about on- and off-balance balance
     sheet currency positions. Finally, there is market recognition that, contrary to 2001, counterparties holding a share of the
     sector's exchange rate risk are more reputable and financially stronger.
     Government securities account for a smaller proportion of assets, and a larger share of them bear floating rates or are foreign
     exchange rate-linked. Gearing ratios have also increased significantly, with the loanideposit ratio increasing from less than
     50 percent in 2001 to about 80 percent currently. Finally, leverage levels are relatively low and the funding base broader and
     mostly in domestic currency, with foreign wholesale funding accounting only for 12 percent of total liabilities.

The bankingsector is now also more competitive and structurally sounder:

     After the 2001 crisis, about 20 banks were closed. These banks were plagued with questionable conflict-of-interest lending
     practices and excessive risk-taking. This process of consolidation opened the door for foreign investment, with foreign
     participation now present in banks accounting for about 50 percent12 of total system. The new ownership structure is
     leading to a more competitive industry, with enhanced transparency and reduced scope for lending to related parties.
     Despite the recent credit expansion, capital levels are high (overall CAR of 17 percent); return on equity and assets in excess
     of 20 and 3 percent, respectively, point to strong profitability, even when adjusted for risk; and asset quality remains strong

94.     The CEDPL series aims to support policies and reformsthat will increaseTurkey's
competitiveness and employment opportunities. The overall goal of the Government's

that translates into job creation and poverty reduction. Consequently, the Program aims to

investment climate; (ii)enhance the efficiency and depth of the financial sector, thereby

95.     The CEDPL 2, the first in a series of two (CEDPL 2 and CEDPL 3) programmatic

the competitiveness and employment program of the Government's gthDevelopment Plan to be
supported by the Bank (see Box 5). Within a context of reduced international liquidity, high and
rising food and energy prices, and a downturn of the EU and US economies (Turkey's major
trade partners), CEDPL 2 is designed to support policies and reforms which, in the long term,
will contribute to promoting domestic savings through more efficient and deeper capital markets
and to increasing productivity and exports through labor market reforms, skill enhancement, and
export focused investment climate reforms (e.g. customs). These reforms are part of Turkey's
agenda over the long term to help reduce its savings-investmentgap and current account deficit.

96.     The CEDPL program contributesin the long run to meetingTurkey's challenge of
maintaining growth in the face of high external financing needs in three key ways. First,

POVERTYAND SOCIAL IMPACTS

106.    The reforms supported under the Competitiveness and Employment Program
are expected to contribute to reduction in poverty in Turkey in the long-term. Overall,
measures to raise export competiveness and foreign direct investments by strengthening the
investment climate are designed to support a virtuous cycle of growth, employment
generation, and productivity gains. A well-functioning financial sector enables the poor to
benefit disproportionally more from economic growth, inducing pro-poor growth. While, on
balance the privatization program is geared to raise productivity and growth potential of the
privatized enterprises, it does affect laid-offworkers and their families negatively in the short-
run. The Government, with Bank support, has designed and is implementing a labor
adjustment program which offers laid-off workersjob-loss compensation as well as retraining
and temporary employment support. The privatization transaction supported under this
operation included the implementation of these social measures to support laid-off workers
and their families (see Table 9 and also Annex 8 for more detail). The short-term adverse
impact on laid-off workers and their families has been mitigated with all workers having
received agreed job-loss compensation and having been offered employment in a temporary
employment program as well as retraining.

107.    The labor market and skill building measures supported under this operation
are expected to have short-term and positive poverty and equality impacts. Measures
supported are expected to raise employment of women and young people which would,
according to a Poverty and Social Impact Assessment, benefit mostly poorer workers and
their families (Table 9 and Annex 8). Similarly, the active labor market programs forming
part of the Employment Package are expected to have an overall progressive impact as
directed primarily to unemployedworkers which have a significantly higher poverty rate than
employed workers (Annex 8).          Further, the fiscal implications of the social security
contribution reductions are not expected to have an impact on the current funding and
coverage levels of the existing social assistance programs. On the contrary, the Action Plan
of the Government includes the creation of a harmonized, inclusive social safety net which
would aim to both expand reachand effectiveness ofTurkey's social assistance programs.

FIDUCIARY    ASPECTS

110.    Modernization of Turkey's system of public financial accountability in line with
international standards was an urgent PEM priority. Enactment of the PFMC Law in
2003 marked a defining moment for the PFM. The PFMC Law defines the new principals,
rules and structures for the Turkish PFM systems including budget formulation and
execution, financial and internal control systems, and internal and external audit structures.
The budgets and indicative proposals for the following two years are prepared according to
the functional, economic and institutional classificationsdefined under the PFMC Law. The
Government Finance Statistics (GFS) analytical budget classification and the accrual based
accounting systems have been expanded to all general government institutions, including
local administrations. The MOF is able to consolidate the budget realizationsfor all general
government institutions. Periodic financial statements are prepared regularly and on time.
GDPA prepares aggregated financial reports for the central government, which are published
as a monthlybulletin. The PFMC Law has transferredthe authorityto incur expenditures and
provide other approvals for expenditures above predetermined thresholds (internal control
over budget expenditures) to the Strategy DevelopmentUnits established within each public
administration. The most important positive feature o f the PFMC Law is that it combines
duties and responsibilitiesin a single spending authority that incurs expenditures and is kept
accountable. The PFMC Law has also introduced a modern internalaudit framework. With
devolution o f authority and responsibility to spending agencies, the need has increased for a
modern internal audit organization that can assure the head o f the spending agency on the
soundnessofthe internalcontrol system. The PFMC Law requires each public administration
to establish an internalaudit unit within its administration.

111.    Accounting. An automated online accounting system has been implemented. The
MOF developed in-house an automated online accounting system, Say2000i system, which
has been up and running since 2002. The system networks and captures receipts and
payments from more than 1,500 sites of the GDPA across Turkey as they are made. The
system can produce periodicfinancial statements and covers all general budgetary institutions
except the Office of the President and accounting office for the State Debt within the
Treasury. The initiative to introduce modified accrual accounting in compliance with GFS
requirements is making progress. Turkey has consciously switched from cash-based to an
accrual basis of accounting. A uniform chart o f accounts that is harmonized with budget
classificationhas been implemented. The GDPA has issueda new framework for the accrual-
based chart of accounts. It is harmonized with the economic classification o f the newly
adopted GFS budget classificationsystem. The new chart of accounts enabled the GDPA to
compile financial statements consistent with the 2001-GFS budget classification. Article 49
of the PFMC Law mandates that accounting be harmonized with the internationalstandards,
and standards be issued by a Government Accounting Standards Board (GASB), which was
established as the general government's sole standard-setting authority, and has been
operatingsince June 2006. The GASB is underthe MOF, and is comprised o f representatives
from the TCA, State PlanningOrganization(SPO), MOF, Treasury, and other agencies.




                                               41

112.    Procurement. Turkey has moved to upgrade its public procurement legislationand
practices in line with international standards. The current Public Procurement Law was
enacted in 2002. The Law is basedon the UnitedNationsCommissionon InternationalTrade
Law (UNCITRAL) Model and moves Turkey in the direction o f compliance with EU
standards. Benchmarkingo f Turkey's public procurement system against an internationally
recognized baseline of best practice in procurement was carried out jointly by the Turkish
PPA and the Bank in February 2006 and updated in October 2007. The assessment showed
the strongest results in the area of legislative and regulatory framework, and in improving
integrity of the national public procurement system. Further improvements in the public
procurement are nevertheless critical for the efficient functioning of the system. Important
areas requiring attention are: (i)lowering of the threshold for publication of contract award
decisions (this is being addressed with the current proposed amendment); (ii) eliminating
restrictions in the form o f thresholds for participation by international bidders; and (iii)
simplifying dispute resolution system. Turkey is one of the countries where the Bank had
consultationsfor the use of countrysystems in procurement in November 2007, and the Bank
received an official request from the Governmentto participateas one ofthe pilot countries in
January 2008. The Government proposed a two-phased approach, whereby the first phase
would address country level procurement assessment, policy analysis and capacity
development, and the second phase would pilot the use with a sector or a project to be
selected by the Government. The Bank Board approved the Board paper titled "Use o f
Country Procurement Systems in Bank-Supported Operations-Proposed Piloting Program"
(UCS) on April 24, 2008. The UCS pilot program is initially targeting eight to ten countries
Bank-widefor pilots from a wider pool of interested countries, with at least one country from
each region to be included. A Bank Team initiated the test program in May 2008 for
assessing Turkey for entry into the piloting program. It is envisaged that this process will
continue over the next six to nine months. Once the pilots are decided, the countries being
selected will participateinthis program over a periodof 12to 18 months.

113,    Auditing. Effective financial accountability requires extensive modernization of
Turkey's public audit system.        The objectives are twofold: (a) clarify institutional
responsibilities, promote improvementsin audit quality in line with international standards
and support the shift from ex-ante controlsto ex-post monitoring in harmony with the efforts
to improve operational performance; and (b) expand the scope o f TCA audits to cover the
entire general government including local administrations, autonomous agencies, social
security institutions, remaining extra-budgetary funds and revolving funds, with the overall
objective of transformingthe TCA into an effective state audit institution. These objectives
have been facilitated by enactment o f the PFMC Law and will hrther be completedthrough
enactment ofthe new TCA law.

geothermal resource-intensive activities are currently not subject to EIA requirements); as
well as better general enforcementofthe current framework.

RISKS AND MITIGATION

119.    Turkey, with high external financing requirements and current account deficits,
is at risk from declining international liquidity.                                  Turkey's gross external financing
requirement (current account needs, medium- and long-term amortizations as well as short
term amortizations) in 2009 is expected to be of the order of US$130 billion. External
vulnerability is underlinedby consistentreal exchange rate appreciationin recentyears.

120.    Yet the Turkish economy would be much more resilient to a sudden capital
outflow and real depreciation than it was in 2001. Since the 2001 crisis, the Turkish
economyhas been transformed. Exports have tripled since 2002, from US$36 billion in 2002
to US$107 billion in 2007. Domesticcredit marketshave deepened from less than 11 percent
of GDP in 2002 to about 28 percent in 2007, while capital adequacy ratios are among the
highest in emerging markets and financial sector regulations have been strengthened. Gross
public debt has nearly halved and exposure to market risk in the public debt portfolio
significantly reduced, while maturitieshave lengthened.

121.    A capital reversal would thus be felt most acutely in the corporate sector, with
effects on growth, employment, and poverty. This follows from the foreign exchange
rollover needs of Turkish corporations and their exposure to foreign exchange risk through
foreign-currency denominatedliabilities. Conversely, vulnerabilities in the public sector and
in the banking sector are more limited. Effects on the public finances would be felt mainly
through nominal interest rates on the government's operating budget and the effect of lower
growth on revenues. Effects on the banking sector would be felt through lower asset values
on banks' balance sheets, but with any systemic threat to solvency unlikely.

 122.   Key to reducingthese risks will be strong fiscal and monetary management,in a
context of overall policy and political stability, as economic growth rates in the world
and in Turkey slow in the near term, and preparedness for more severe downside
scenarios. Maintaining investor confidence in Turkey, key to avoiding capital reversals and
their disorderly resolution, has three main ingredients: (i)continued political stability; (ii)the
real and signaling effects of strong macroeconomic management, especially setting realistic
fiscal targets and meeting them; and (iii) continued structural reforms to drive growth and
decrease economic vulnerability.

 123.   Risks to the Turkish banking sector of a sudden reversal of capital flows appear
to be of an indirect nature. The banking sector's exposure to foreign capital outflows is
mitigated by the fact that, to a large extent, capital inflows havenot come through the banking
system (in contrast to many other countries in the region), but rather through the government
securities market, investmentsat the ISE, and more recently directly to the corporate sector in
Turkey. The effects on the banking sector would thus mostly be indirect as liquidity in the
money market and government securities market would dry up if capital was to leave Turkey
on a large scale. Since 2002, there has been a major shift in banking assets from government
securities to private sector credits. The source of bank profits has shifted from trading and
treasury revenue to profits from net interest revenue in the loan and deposit business. The
tighter fiscal policy, as well as confidence in the ongoing reform process, has made the
speculativetrading business less attractive in both currency and interest rate positioning.

124.    The shift toward domestic credit is expected to have two benefits: the banking
system has become less susceptible to market risks that accompany international debt
crises, mainly rapidly rising real interest rates and falling exchange rates; and financial
deepening improves the prospect for development. However, at the same time, the change
in bank portfolios has increasedthe relative importance of credit risk. The expansion of the
credit portfolio leaves the banking system potentially vulnerable to a substantial increase in
domestic interest rates because borrowers may be unable to meet interest and principal
payments at substantially higher rates. Banks may also become vulnerable to the impact o f
troubles in financial markets outside the banking system during debt crises.

External and Political Risks

128.    External Risks. There are several external risks. First and foremost, although the
resilience of the economy has greatly improved, a key challenge for Turkey will be coping
with potentially volatile capital flows; Turkey remains vulnerable to a sudden reversal in
capital flows. The key to mitigating global financial risk will be for the authorities to move
forcefully on their program of fiscal discipline and structural reforms so as to convince
markets that Turkish fundamentals are sound. Second, sustained high energy prices would
negativelyaffect macroeconomic balances in several ways: larger CAD, higher inflation, and
lower GDP growth. Third, geo-political events in the neighboringregion may affect Turkey
negatively through increased risk aversion by market participants (Le., higher spreads) and
high energy prices- as the country is a net energy importer and the price of energy affects the
current account negatively.

129.    Political Risks. The single-party Government and its strong parliamentary majority
favor political stability and create a positive environment for reforms.            Moreover,
uncertainties created by the recent Constitutional Court case against the Justice and
Development Party (AKP) have passed, with the verdict not to ban the AKP or any of its
members from politics. Nonetheless, political risks to the reform program remain. These
include (i)continuing domestic political differences; (ii) upcoming municipal elections; and
(iii) border tensions and geopoliticalevents in neighboringregions.

time, as the next phaseof reformwill be implemented.

A. Introduction

1.      Raisingtrade integration,export competitiveness and foreign direct investments are
an integral part of the Government's competitiveness and employment program. Deeper
trade integration would encourage specialization, division of labor and thereby productive
efficiency. A sustained flow of foreign direct investments provides a stable source of external
financing. FDI also contribute to technology transfer and thus productivity growth; and foreign
owned companies have on average a higher export propensity than domestic firms. Attracting
FDI in the tradable sectors is thus a central component of a broader strategy to boost export
competiveness. Improving the export performance of Turkish firms would also contribute to
expanding employment, as shown by the 2007 World Bank Investment Climate Assessment
report (the employment elasticity with relation to export propensity was estimatedto be 0.353).
Export-oriented firms in Turkey also hire more skilled workers than non-exporters and pay
relatively higher salaries: expanding exports will not only provide more but also betterjobs.34

2.       This Annex is organized as follows: The next section provides cross-country
comparisons, benchmarking Turkey's competitiveness and investment climate performance and
examines the challenges lying ahead. The second section traces the current status and recent
policy developments in innovation, technology absorption and quality certification. The third
section summarizes main progressand challenges ineasingdoing businessinTurkey.

researchers who reside abroad upon completion of their PhDs. To tackle this problem, TUBITAK

21.     A lack of innovation finance due to the underdevelopment of the venture capital
(VC) and business angel sector is another constraint to the performance of Turkey's NIS.
There are only three Venture Capital Investment Trusts (VCITs) in Turkey, with annual

the financial sector constrain development of these services. In addition, the demand (deal flow)
for VC and business angels' services i s also low, further constraining development of these
industries.

22.     Currently, technology adoption in Turkey is low with respect to comparator
countries. The World Bank Enterprise Survey of 2005 reveals that Turkish firms generally
choose to purchase machinery and equipment, rather than developing and adapting their own
technology. In addition, fewer Turkish firms report to have purchased new machinery in the
preceding year than those in comparator countries. In 2005, 42 percent of firms in Turkey
reported the acquisition of new machinery, which is a lower technology acquisition rate than in
many other emerging economies suchas Brazil (68 percent).

23.     Technology transfer from abroad remains relatively limited as well. The number of
joint ventures established with foreign firms, a major source of technology upgrading for
developing countries, seems to be lower in Turkey than comparator countries. Furthermore,
although FDI inflows have increased significantly in recent years, manufacturing sectors did not
get the largest share of this increaseand levels of investment there seem muchlower than inmost
new EU member states. There is also scope to increase global knowledge transfer to Turkey via
the large TurkishDiaspora.

24.     Both large-scale survey data and case studies show that firm-level innovation is
limited, especially in labor-intensive sectors. The percentages o f firms introducing a new

inconsistent supply o f energy as well as high tax rates, negatively affect the capacity o f
enterprises to innovate and adopt technology, hurtingtheir global competiveness.

26.     In Doing Business 2009, Turkey reached the 59fhrank among 181 countries in the

Introduction

 1.     The Government of Turkey has identified `increasing employment' as one of the
development axes in its Ninth Development Plan, a measure supported under the
Competitiveness and Employment Development Policy Loan (FY08) and outlined its intent to
lower non-wage labor costs, increase flexibility of the labor market, and improve skills of the
work force in various documents, including the Action Plan for the 60thGovernment.

2.      Turkey undertook the first phase of the labor market reform geared to reduce non-
wage labor costs, accompaniedby actions to strengthenthe development of a competency-based
skill-building system and continued reforms of curricula in secondary school that will prepare
students better for the labor market. This operation supports specific actions included in the
Employment Package, comprising the time-bound reduction of employer's social security
contributions for young workers and women which presents a cost-effective way to reach
important employment generating impacts for the most ~ulnerable.~~Further, the operation
supports the lifting of specific hiring quotas for larger firms and the introduction of flexibility in
the provision of specific services by such firms, coupled with the financing the social security
contribution for employees with disabilities from general Treasury funds. The lifting and
flexibilization of such restrictions will lead to additional formal job creation as well as to
additional hiring of workers with disabilities.

3.      The positiveemploymentimpactsof the introducedmeasureswill materializein full
when the second phase of the labor market reformis realized. That second stage, as outlined
in Government policy documents, will center around improving flexibility of the labor market
and improvingthe protection of workers incase of income loss. Protectionreforms will focus on
making the unemployment insurance system an effective tool for workers to manage the risk of
income loss - broadening coverage by revisiting eligibility requirements as well as revisiting
benefit levels. Flexibility reforms would also include possibilities for first-term job seekers as
well as women to enter the work force through temporary employment agencies or on short-term
basis.

D.      Description andAnalysis of the EmploymentPackage

25.     The Government of Turkey has adopted a phased approach to tackle the above
outlined agenda without compromising the integrative and comprehensive nature of the
reforms itself. A first major package of reforms, Law 5763, was passed by parliament in May
2008 and addresses several of the above mentioned policy reforms, especially in the areas of (i)
reducing the non-wage labor costs; as well as (ii)improving skills and reducing labor
mismatches. Below follows a short evaluation of the major parts of the employment package as
passed by parliamenton May 15,2008.

A.      Reduction of HighNon-Wage Labor Costs.

(i)     General reduction of social security contributionsfor employers. The package includes a
        general reduction of social security payments of 5 percent for employers' contributions

                       ANNEX 7: THE PROCESS OF PRIVATIZATION IN2007 200S6'                                -
Introduction

 1.        The Bank is processing a new lending operation for Turkey - CEDPL 2 - which is
intended, inter alia, to support the Government's economic reform program including its
privatization program. For this purpose, it has been agreed by the Government and the Bank that
a joint assessment should be made o f the transparency o f the processes used to consummate
privatization transactions completed during June 2007 - June 2008 and whether or not those
processes were consistent with acceptable competitive practices. This paper contains this joint
assessment. The paper begins with a brief description o f the program to date and then continues
with an assessmento fthe governing legal framework. The results of a similar assessment carried
out in relation to transactions completed in 2005 - 2006 are then presented. Lastly, the findings
inrelation to a sample of 10recently completedtransactions, which represent 99.9 percent o fthe
total volume of transactions, are then analyzed62. The paper concludes by noting that the
processes followed by the Privatization Administration (PA) continue to accord with best
international practice in terms o f transparency and competitiveness. This conclusion i s consistent
with the conclusionreachedon this issue inrelation to CEDPL 1.

Background

2.         Turkey's privatization program was initiated in 1983. Between that date and 2004 many
State Owned Enterprises (SOEs) were formally transferred into the privatization portfolio but
very few transactions were actually carried out. The pace o f activity increased dramatically in
2004 as can be observed in Figure A 7-1, Privatization revenues in that year were seven times
higher than those o f the previous year. This surge inactivity was even more pronouncedover the
following twelve months. In 2005-2006, both the number o f transactions and total revenues
increased significantly. Some 53 SOEs were privatized during this period, generating over $16.4
billion inrevenue.

amendments which, together with supporting regulations, provides a comprehensive legal
framework for privatization. Key features of Law No. 4046 include:

CEDPL 1

10.     For the CEDPL 1, an assessment was carried out to determine whether the privatization
transactions within 2005-2006 had been conducted pursuant to a broadly transparent process
which was consistent with acceptable competitive practices and was environmentally
responsible. For this purpose, the Government and the Bank selected the 20 largest (by value)
out o f the transactions completed in that timeframe for detailed review. These 20 transactions
made up only 34 percent o f the total number but represented over 97 percent o f the data set by
value. The sample was also found to be consistent with the data set in terms o f domestic vs.
foreign components and interms o f industry structure.

11.     Using this sample, the program carried out by the PA was analyzed in terms of; (a) what
was sold, e.g. assets, shares; (b) how these interests were sold, e.g. block sales, asset sales, IPOs;
(c) what processes were used to undertake the sales, e.g, public tenders, negotiated sales; (d) the
transparency, competiveness and efficiency o f such sales; (e) whether best practices were used,
and (0the efficiency and fairness o f the results.

12.     The assessment concluded that the PA showed professionalismand good practices in all
o f the 20 transactions evaluated.     Appropriate sales methods were used; block sales where
strategic buyers were needed, IPOs where broad public participation was the aim, asset sales
where appropriate and concessions where it was considered desirable to keep public assets under
government ownership but where the private sector could be expected to bring greater
efficiencies to the operation o f the assets. Outside financial advisers were usedappropriately, in
line with best practices. The proposed privatizations were publicly announced and both domestic
and foreign buyers were encouraged. Tender processes were open, transparent and competitive
and tender pre-conditions appropriate. Post privatization conditions were not onerous and the
use o f golden shares, while common, did not involve excessive government interference in the
private sector.

13.     The overall efficiency of the process and the fairness of the results were well
demonstrated.     It was concluded that the Government, through the PA, pursued a broadly
transparent process, which was consistent with acceptable competitive practices.

2.      Employment and Skill Policy Reforms: expected direct positive distributional and
poverty reduction impact. The two measures directed at reducing non-wage labor costs,
supported by this operation, would lead to positive net employment effects for groups at high
poverty risk. First, the operation supports the employer's social security contribution subsidy
program to firms hiring young people (18-29 years) and women who have been out o f the formal

I Introduction

1.       Industrial activity has been a major cause o f environmental pollution in Turkey.64
Policy measures to address this problem have increased in recent years and include the
improvement o f its monitoring and inspection system; the amendment o f the environmental
law, laying out more stringent sanctions; the harmonization o f the environmental impact
assessment (EIA) procedures with those o f the EU; and joining the Environmental
Compliance and Enforcement Network for Accession. Important challenges remain and they
include a better permitting system (effectively protecting the environment without creating
unnecessary bureaucratic hurdles for the enterprise sector); revising the regulation related to
environmental impact assessments for the exploration phase (e.g. petroleum, mineral and
geothermal resource-intensive activities are currently not subject to EIA requirements in the
exploration phase); as well as better general enforcement o f the current framework.

2.       The CEDPL 2 supported Program is not expected to have a negative environmental
impact. Rather, as the operation supports measures that contribute to a shift towards a more
export-oriented; skills-intensive and productivity-based growth path, CEDPL 2 i s expected to
contribute to reducing the industrial pollution content o f growth. There are four reasons for
that assessment. First, CEDPL 2 supports measures aimed at deepening Turkey's trade
integration and enterprises oriented to EU and OECD markets are more likely to meet
international standards than firms mainly oriented to local consumers. Second, measures
promoting innovation and skills are expected to contribute indirectly to reduce the pollution
content o f growth by enabling the expansion o f technology-intensive and cleaner sectors (e.g.
automotive) to the detriment o f low-tech and high polluters (e.g. textiles).                     Third, some
CEDPL 2 supported measures to help strengthen Turkey's innovation policy, including
government's support to the development o f clean technologies (as for example in the case o f
Technology Development Foundation o f Turkey), expected to contribute directly to the
reduction o f industrial pollution. Fourth, as state-owned enterprises have shown to be less
compliant with the environmental legislations, by supporting the advancement o f the
privatization program in the June 1007-June 2008 period, CEDPL 2 i s likely to contribute to
lower levels o f contamination inTurkey (for a detailed assessment see Annex 10).

The document puts the cost estimate for compliance by industry at about 14.8 billion for the

o f the acquis as follows: waste management in  9.6 billion, water supply and wastewater
management in 34 billion, protection of natural resources in 264 million.

26.         The 9th DevelopmentPlan for 2007-2013 is another key document that lays out the
main principles for annual budget decisions concerning investments, capacity building and
other activities to be implemented by government agencies in support of environmental
management.

27.         The Government of Turkey now faces the challenge of designing effective policies and
preparing for negotiations to implement the EU acquis related to industrial pollution control,
but is constrained by information shortage. As ENVEST Planners70 noted while preparing a
directive-specific investment plan for the IPPC Directive: ". ..there is hardly any substantive
information on industries and/or individual plants liable to IPPC legislation" (ENVEST,
2005a). TURKSTAT's biennial surveys among enterprises constitute the only information
source on industry and environment presently available to the Government. However, MEF is
inthe processof establishingan inventory of installations liable to the IPPC Directive71.

Monitoring and Inspection Capacities

3 1.     Inspection responsibilities are shared between the MEF GDEM's Department o f
Monitoring and Inspection (DMI), PDEFs, municipal SKIS,and the Specially Protected Area
Administration. Between 2004 and 2006, DMI's Environmental Inspection Division (EID)
carried out 25 planned inspections annually. The Department has gradually expanded the
inspection activities and it i s targeting full coverage by 2010. Presently PDEFs' inspections
are spontaneous and unannounced, and respond to public complaints or requests by Local
Environmental Boards (LEBs). Often they aim at priority polluting sectors or hotspots.

32.      Significant progress has been made in delegating inspection authority to local
institutions. Wastewater inspection is a good example for complete delegation. In the case o f
discharges into sewers, SKIS, municipalities and/or Organized Industrial Zones - Waste Water
Treatment Plant administrations are in charge o f inspection, depending on the location o f the
enterprise. With regard to hazardous wastes, responsibilities are appropriately (given national
implications o f inter-province transfer) shared among central and local institutions.

33.      Enterprises with permits for emissions to air and wastewater discharges are requiredto
carry out regular self-monitoring. With regard to emissions to air, highly polluting facilities
are required to submit an emission report to the MEF every two years to verify that their
emissions are in compliance with emission limit values specified inthe permit. The reporting
period for less polluting facilities is three years. Depending on the type, size, location and
pollution load o f the facility, continuous monitoring may be required. Ambient air quality
monitoring i s also requiredin the impact area o f the facility. Similarly, wastewater discharge
permits specify the frequency o f self-monitoring and reporting. Managers o f waste recycling
facilities are required to submit to MEF regular reports on the amount and type o f wastes
collected and recycled.

34.      MEF is also amending the Bylaw on Environmental Inspection in order to impose the
obligation on enterprises to conduct annual environmental audits. The Bylaw will require the
setting up o f "environmental management units" in large enterprises, and the hiring o f a
person responsible for environmental management in smaller enterprises. Small and medium
size enterprises will be allowed to hire consultancy services to support them inthe preparation
o f environmental audit reports. The reports will be submitted directly to the Ministry o f
Environment and Forestry.

35.      Turkey is making significant progress in improving its monitoring and inspection
system, including officially joining the Compliance and Enforcement Network for Accession.
However, further scope for improvement includes mainly: (i)further clarification o f roles and
responsibilities amongst relevant institutions, (ii)higher number o f MEF inspectors are
needed to monitor and enforce environmental policies, (iii)   a higher number o f inspections is
necessary to cover the industrial spectrum, (iv) capacity buildingand improved skills amongst
inspectors are necessary, (v) improved coordination and data flow between the Central MEF
and PDEFs i s necessary for the preparation o f inspection reports.

EnforcementCapacities

36.      There i s scope for improved enforcement in the regulatory system, and the
Government is already working to strengthen it, starting with a more stringent Law on
Environment. The Amended Law contains tough sanctions against non-compliance, and also
allows Central MEF or Governors to issue sanctions for some serious infractions without
issuinga warning first.

37.      Enhancements to structural and institutional factors are necessary further improve
effective implementation o f the Law, especially with regards to the following: (i)MEF's
ability to enforce environmental requirements appears to be strong in the case of large
enterprises, but needs to be improved for the case o f SMEs and SOEs; (ii)the amended law
requires clear differentiation o f sanctions by type and level o f infraction for effective
implementation, (iii)informing the public about environmental compliance and non-
compliance incidents would increase public's awareness and demand for less industrial
pollution, putting additional pressureon the enforcement system.

Institutional and Legal Framework

38.     In Turkey, the responsibility to privatize SOEs rests with the Privatization
Administration (PA) o f the Prime Ministry. P A activities are regulated by the Law on
Privatization (1994) and overseen by the High Council o f Privatization (HCP). The MEF has
no explicit role inthe privatization process. It i s not a member o f the HCP or o f privatization
project teams. However, under the Law on Public Information it is required to share with
potential investors and their advisors information on legal requirements and compliance status
o f SOEs slated for privatization.

39.     There are gaps in the environmental legislation relevant to SOEs slated for
privatization. Key relevant provisions and gaps in the bylaws on the control o f air, water and
soil pollution, and hazardous waste management, and the Amended Law on Environment are
summarized below:
    0   The Amended Law on Environment (May 2006) and the Bylaw on the Control o f Air
        Pollution from Industrial Sources (July 2006) provide grace periods o f 4 and 2 years
        from the date o f approval o f work plans by the MEF, respectively, to achieve
        compliance with regard to emissions to air and wastewater discharges to water bodies.
        Ineach case, enterprises hadone year from the effectiveness ofthe lawsto submittheir
        work plans to the MEF. The status o f non-compliant SOEs that are privatized past the
        deadlines for the submission o f the above-mentioned work plans remains unclear.
        According to the MEF, in such cases, the "fairness" principle embodied inthe Turkish
        legislation will be evoked. This would allow the MEF to negotiate new deadlines with
        the new owners o f such enterprises.

        With regard to soil pollution, according to the Bylaw on Soil Pollution Control (May
        2005), enterprises causing soil pollution are responsible for improving pollution levels

         to satisfy limit values prescribed in the Bylaw. However, a regulation specifying the
         principles and procedures for identifying and remediating contaminated soils has as yet
         not been put inplace.
         The Bylaw on Control o f Hazardous Wastes (March 2005) defines how such wastes
         are to be stored, transported and disposed off, and lays out respective licensing
         requirements. However, there is a legislative gap with regard to hazardous waste
         accumulations that have emerged over many years, such as mine tailings and sludge
         lagoons near steel mills.

40.      MEF is however preparing a By-Law to cover the issue of legacy pollution with
emphasis on site clean-up and remediation at point sources, and i s also planning to issue the
draft document for the first round o f approvals next March2009.

Recent Progress in Integrating Environmental Liabilities into Privatization Process

41.      The Privatization Administration in Turkey is fully aware o f the importance o f
environmental liabilities for international investors, makes environmental compliance
information available to potential investors and has commissioned audits for the high profile
petrochemical, refining, and iron and steel factories.

42.      As of today, none of the transaction agreements (SPAS) processed by the Privatization
Administration have included remediation plans to ensure that clean-up activities are actually
carried out after the transfer o f the property. Although the PA should not be the institution in
charge o f commissioning site remediation plans or even coordinating this work with other
institutions, the international experience demonstrates that when environmental systems are
weak (e.g. gaps in the legislation, lack o f capacity to inspect and enforce applicable
regulations), the inclusion o f remediation plans in bidding documents increase the likelihood
o f actual remediation. This i s particularly important in Turkey, as there are no regulatory
provisions today covering the remediation o f accumulated pollution in the country's
environmental legislation.

43.      The Bank's ESW report "Towards Less Industrial Pollution in Turkey" has provided
key recommendations based on international best practice to increase both economic and
environmental gains in privatizations in Turkey. These recommendations are being revised by
the Government.

VI1         Conclusions

52.         The proposed DPL operation, which supports the privatization o f a number o f selected
SOEs, does not result or lead to environmental impacts nor contributes to the worsening o f
environmental baselines. On the contrary, the evidence suggests that the privatization o f SOEs
in Turkey may result in improved enterprise environmental performance, for the following
reasons:
      (i)     participation o f international bidders in privatization tenders o f large polluting
              enterprises have prompted the Government (Privatization Administration) not only
              to conduct environmental audits to disclose environmental liabilities during the
              bidding process, but also to implement environmental action plans and
              technology/process modernizations to improve baseline environmental conditions o f
              SOEs in the years previous to privatization, in cases where potential liabilities were
              likely to be significant, as inthe case of PETKIM
      (ii)    Enforcement o f environmental regulations on SOEs has been extremely poor in
              Turkey (mainly due to budgetary constraints and other structural pressures), private
              facilities however -especially large enterprises- are more consistently inspected and
              forced to comply with the law and regulation^^^.
     (iii)Ingeneral,privateinvestors-especiallyinternationalcompanies- arebetterprepared
              to invest in technology modernization and more prone to embark in voluntary
              environmental initiatives (such as responsible care, eco-audits, eco-labeling) than the
              public sector.
      (iv) International private companies are also more concerned (and subject to scrutiny)
              about past and future liabilities (pollution affecting population's health or
              biodiversity) and therefore more likely to take early actions on site clean-up and
              remediation.
