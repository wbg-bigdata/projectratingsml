                                           1. INTRODUCTION

1.1      This program document proposes an Economic Recovery and Governance Grant
(ERGG) to the Republicof Togo in the amount of SDR [103.7] million (US$[170.6]million
equivalent). The ERGG supports the full normalization o f relations between Togo and the
World Bank, and i s also the first Development Policy Operation (DPO) aimed at supporting the
development strategy o f the Government o f Togo inthe context o f a multi-donor re-engagement
process. The proceeds o f the grant will be used for (i)          direct reimbursement o f a bridge loan
extended to clear Togo's arrears to the InternationalDevelopment Association, and (ii)              budgeted
fiscal needs in the period immediately aRer arrears clearance. Interms o f IDA resources, Togo
meets the eligibility criteria that have been discussed with the Executive Directors for
exceptional arrears clearance support from IDA.' The operation would also draw on the regular
IDA allocation for Togo to finance the budget support component. Since Togo is at highrisk o f
debt distress, IDA resources are provided on grant terms, per the stipulations o f the grant
allocation system agreed with IDA donors for the IDA 14period (FY06-FY08).

1.2      Togo is emerging from a socio-political crisis, marked by a period of heightened
tension and instability. Following the installation and subsequent election o f Mr. Faure
GnassingbC in 2005, upon the death o f President EyadCma GnassingbC, concerns emerged about
the overall political situation inTogo. These concerns began to subside as political and economic
reforms started to be implemented, and have abated in the wake o f the inclusive legislative
elections held on October 14, 2007. Although the main opposition party initially contested the
results, Togo's constitutional court subsequently confirmed on October 30, 2007 that the
incumbent partywon a majority o f seats; election observers declared the poll fairly contested.

1.3      Given the complex political environment and governance and transparency issues
facing the government, the proposed grant would assist the country in its economic and
social recovery. The proposed grant supports government-owned reforms to improve
governance, transparency and efficiency in public finance management as well as advance
structural reforms aimed at strengthening governance and transparency inthe key sectors of the
economy (phosphates, cotton, energy, and financial sectors). These reforms would lead over time
to strengthened fiscal sustainability, improved economic governance and enhanced transparency
o f State institutions and processes-critical elements for the country's economic and social
recovery. Based on current projections o f Togo's debt ratios for end-2007, the country would
qualify for debt relief under the Enhanced HIPC Initiative. The operation would then pave the
way for HIPC debt relief under the HIPC Initiative and MDRIwith attainment o f the Decision

              2. TOGO'S DEVELOPMENTCHALLENGESAND PROSPECTS

2.1     Togo, a small-sizecountry with a populationof 6.3 millionin 2006, is a member of
the West African EconomicandMonetaryUnion (WAEMU).Itspopulation is growing at 2.6
percent annually and likely to double in 25 years. There are about 37 ethnic groups in the
country, most o fwhich are closely related to groups inneighboringGhana and Benin.Incomeper
capita (US$350 in 2006 as per the Bank's Atlas methodology) i s low compared to Sub-Saharan
Africa (US$842) and Low Income Countries (US$650) averages. The LomC port is an important
transport hub for the landlockedneighboring countries andhas benefitedfrom diversion o f traffic
from the port o f Abidjan as result of unrest in CBte d'Ivoire in recent years. In the past, the
country played a central role inregional trade and finance. The economy, however, deteriorated
since the 1990s and the banking sector i s currently in a precarious condition due to the
accumulation o f large arrears from public sector entities. Phosphates are Togo's most important
commodity andexport earner; Togo also produces cash crops, mainly cotton andgroundnuts, and
has a considerable agricultural potential due to favorable climate conditions ina large part o f the
country. Despite its natural resources, however, the prolongedpolitical unrest and the withdrawal
of foreign assistance have taken a heavy toll on the economy and social outcomes. As a result,
Togo now ranks 152nd out o f 177 countries in terms of human development, according to the
UNDP's 2007 HumanDevelopmentReport.

                                    RECENT     POLITICAL DEVELOPMENTS

2.2     Following an extended period of political instability, Togo has established an
environment conducive to reforms. The death of President EyadCma GnassingbC in February
2005, which ended his almost 40 year rule, was followed by the election o f Faure GnassingbC as
the new President of Togo in a disputed ballot in April 2005. The election triggered several
weeks o f intense civil unrest, prompting a clampdown by security forces and an exodus o f an
estimated 40,000 refugees3 to neighboring Ghana and Benin. Many donors, particularly the
European Commission (EC), continued to stress the need for democratic reforms in order to
resume assistance that had been cut off inthe early 1990s. Lengthynegotiations among the main
political parties, also mediated by Blaise CompaorC, President o f Burkina Faso, culminated in a
comprehensive political agreement (Accord Politique Global) in August 2006. The agreement
provided for a new, transitional government o f national unity, whose mandate included the
organization of legislative elections by October 2007.

                             IMPACT OF THE POLITICALINSTABILITY

2.6      The prolonged period of political instability, economic and financial
mismanagement andthe relatedwithdrawalof donor support haveresultedin an economic
decline that has reduced living standards for a large segment of the population. Togo's
growth performance has been among the weakest in sub-Saharan Africa, with per capita income
declining by an average o f 1 percent annually since the early 1990s. Poverty has deepened in
rural areas and pockets o f extreme poverty exist in urbanareas, including the capital. Excessive
public debt and weak public finance management have led to large arrears, undermining the
confidence o f suppliers and creditors. Poor management o f state-owned enterprises has caused



                                                  3

large losses, in particular in the cotton and phosphate sectors. As a result, several banks are
burdened by high nonperforming loans, and some are highly undercapitalized. The business
environment is constrained by decades o f underinvestment in infrastructure (notably transport
and energy) and poor governance. Institutional capacity has declined during the long socio-
political crisis and donor disengagement. There is a significant backlog in maintaining health
facilities and schools, while hiring and wage freezes have led to serious bottlenecks in staffing
them.

2.7      More recently, Togo's economy has been hit by a severe energy crisis, the negative
terms of trade impact of high food and energy prices, the Euro appreciation (to which the
CFAF is pegged) vis-his the US dollar, and flooding in certain parts of the country. In

affected by commodity price changes during the period end-2003 to September 2007 (with an

2.9     The persistence of the political crisis, the related withdrawal of donor support and
lack of economic growth weakened the State's finances. The political and economic problems
caused a contraction in the formal economic activities and encouraged the expansion o f the
informal sector, which led to a significant decline in government revenues. Also, treasury
management severely weakened due to lack o f systematic monitoring and recording o f
transactions at an aggregate level. This reduced the transparency of the fiscal situation and
resulted in a breakdown in the use o f expenditure procedures, and by 2006 a large share o f
spendingwas undertaken through exceptional procedures,including without prior authorization.

2.10    To support the Government's financing needs, arrears were accumulated and the
large State-owned companies were tapped to provide advances to the Treasury, thereby
weakening corporate governance. While Togo's primarybalance was positive startingin2001,
this was achieved only by accumulating arrears on domestic and external payment obligations,
since expenditures were undertaken without regard for available fiscal resources. Domestic
arrears, for instance, increased by 0.3 percent o f GDP in 2003, 0.5 percent o f GDP in2004 and
1.9 percent of GDP in 2005. The most important state-owned enterprises, even if experiencing
losses, became subject to the payment of a minimumlump sum for their income tax. This ledto a
decline in the activities and performance o f these enterprises which cover critical economic
sectors (cotton, port, phosphates) and, together with poor management, resulted in large losses
for these enterprises which were unable to renew their investments, thus contributing to the
general degradation o f the economy.

2.11    Poor performance and governance of key public enterprises, notably the phosphate
and cotton companies, were a large factor in Togo's weak economic performance and
resulted in a weakening of the health of the financial sector. The situation was particularly
egregious in Togo's main export sectors, phosphate and cotton. Weak management of the
phosphate sector resulted in a drop in the sector's share in GDP, from 2 percent in 2001 to just
over 1percent in 2006, as well as in its share o f export earnings, from 13 percent in 2001 to 6
percent in2006. More importantly, the sector's losses became a drain on the public treasury, and
its significant non-performing loans constrained the role o f the financial sector in Togo's
economic growth. The cotton sector's output has fallen to a quarter o f the 2001-05 average: its
share o f GDP fell from under 4 percent in 2001 to under 1 percent in 2006, and its share o f
exports fell from 17.5 percent in 2001 to 6.3 percent in 2006. As with the phosphate sector, the
cotton sector's losses are a drain on the public treasury, and its non-performing loans also
constrain the role o f the financial sector in Togo's economic growth. Due in large part to the

2.12    Weak management in other key growth sectors also led to the deterioration of sector

2.13    Weak expenditure management and lack of external financing have squeezed social
spending and public and private investment. In terms of revenues, the wage bill and fiscal
balances, Togo i s closer to the WAEMU convergence criteria than other member countries.
However, expenditures on health, education and public investment are far below regional
averages, largely reflecting low external assistance and poor expenditure prioritization. In
contrast, other expenditures have beenrelatively high, reflecting weak expenditure management.

2003-05.

2.14     As result of the political situation and the weak economic performance, Togo's
Human Development Index, as measured by the UNDP, failed to improve in recent years,
in contrast with a gradual increase experienced in Sub-Saharan Africa (Figure 2.1). The
poor economic performance hampered poverty reduction in Togo. Government budgets were
constrained and favored urbanrather thanrural areas; infrastructure investment inthe rural areas
was very limited and high unemployment has persisted in the urban areas. A household survey
carried out in2006 found that the incidence o fpoverty nationally was 62 percent, with rural areas
accounting for the majority o f the poor (74 percent versus 37 percent inurbanareas). The survey
also revealed that respondents attributed the worsening o f living conditions between 2000 and
2006 mainly to economic difficulties and healthproblems.

2.15    Economic recovery has been underway since 2006, although tempered somewhat by
exogenous shocks. Real GDP grew by about 4 percent in2006 (up from an average 1.8 percent
annually during 2004-5) (see Table 2.1). A buoyant services sector benefiting from growing
regional trade and a moderate increase in phosphate exports (owing to the international
phosphate price boom) offset the dramatic decline (by over 50 percent) in the export o f cotton
fiber. The recovery continued in 2007, thanks to an improved political climate and a modest
rebound in cotton production, but flooding in the North o f the country during the fall o f 2007
hurt local agriculture andelectricity supply shortages constrained overall economic activity (2007
GDP growth slowed to 2 percent). Export growth was sluggish in 2007 partly reflecting the
energy situation, while imports increased due to higher oil prices. Regional policies continued to
determine monetary developments. Broad money grew 22.1 percent in 2006 (compared to 1.4
percent in2005) and by 16.4 percent in 2007, spurredby a surge inremittances andtrade-related
capital inflows in the wake o f the improved political climate. While these inflows boosted net
foreign assets inthe banking system, they translated only marginally into higher domestic credit.
As a result, inflation was subdued, falling from 6.8 percent in2005 to 2.2 percent in2006 andto
1percent in 2007, as food prices moderated due to improvedagricultural supply conditions in
neighboring countries. The external current account deficit (including grants) widened, from 5.3
percent o f GDP in 2005 to 6.0 percent o f GDP in 2006 and 6.4 percent o f GDP in 2007,
reflectinghigher oil prices that outweighed the overall export performance.

2.16    Fiscal policy improved markedly since the second half of 2006. The primary basic
deficit was 1percent o f GDP in 2006, below the budget objective o f 1% percent, and domestic
arrears were reduced for the first time in years (Table 2.2). This outcome was a result of strong
revenue performance (16.9 percent o f GDP in 2006 compared with 15.7 percent in 2005)
reflecting recent reforms intax and customs administration (including the replacement o f the tax
and customs directors and closure o f bank accounts used to divert revenues for off-budget
operations), strong tax arrears recovery, and a large exceptional profit transfer from the central
bank.Externalarrearsremainedsteady at about 29.5 percent o fGDP at end-2006, largelya result
o f the appreciation o f the exchange rate (through the Euro) relative to the U.S. dollar. The strong
revenue performance continued into 2007, with tax revenues estimated at 17 percent.
Expendituremanagement was prudent in2006 as well as in2007 despite pre-election pressures.

                                         OUTLOOK

2.17    The medium-term macroeconomic outlook is based on the assumption of
consolidation of socio-political stability following the elections and a steady economic
recovery.Real GDP growth is projectedto be about 3% percent a year on average during2008-
10, in line with the Government's projection inthe I-PRSP and on par with achievements since
2006 (Table 2.1). The initial economic recovery i s expected to come from a governance-reform-
induced rebound in phosphate and cotton production from the current low capacity utilization
levels, and a resumption o f donor-financed public investment. Economic growth beyond 2008
would be supported by ongoing and planned structural reforms centered on public finance
management and financial sector governance, and measures to address infrastructure bottlenecks
(notably the port and energy) that previously stymied private sector growth. Togo's growth
potentiallargely relies on its comparative advantage as a transit corridor to neighboring countries
and its deep port facilities at Lomk, its past reputation as a strong financial center and its
potential to Wher develop services around these activities (for a more detailed description o f
Togo's main challenges and opportunities inthese areas, see the Interim Strategy Note, paras 36-
42). The regional monetary-exchange arrangement is expected to ensure monetary and price
stability (with inflation at about 3 percent inthe mediumterm).

2.18    The authorities' mediumterm fiscal program aims for a moderate but sustainable
fiscal surplus. The primary balance is assumed to increase progressively from a balanced
position (0 percent o f GDP in 2007) to a surplus o f 1.1 percent o f GDP in 2010 to achieve
broadly sustainable fiscal and debt positions that avoid renewed arrears accumulation. The
Government's macroeconomic framework targets a modest primary fiscal surplus while
reallocating spending toward health, education, energy, roads, and the restructuring o f state-
owned banks and enterprises, supported by scaled-up external assistance. Revenues would
gradually increase, to about 17.8 percent o f GDP by 2010, while domestic spending would be
held broadly constant. The envisaged increase in foreign financing would allow for additional
spending in priority areas. Addressing the energy crisis and the restructuring o f state-owned
banks and enterprises will have significant costs, and external debt service will increase
significantly as the country resumes full debt service to multilateral creditors. To help fill the
gap, the program envisages a drawdown of government deposits at the centralbank (1percent o f
GDP).

2.19    The total external financing needs for 2008-2010 are projected at about US$2.1
billion (75 percent of GDP). About forty percent o f this relates to arrears clearance in2008 by
multilateral and Paris Club creditors (para. 2.25). The macroeconomic framework estimates
project support o f about 5 percent o f GDP a year, broadly in line with the regional average.
Given Togo's high debt, most o f this project-related assistance would be in the form o f grants
and the rest as concessional loans. This leaves an annual financing gap o f about 3 percent o f
GDP, expected to be closed by concessional debt rescheduling (1 percent o f GDP a year) and
budget support (2 percent o f GDP a year), including BCEAO credit unlocked by PRGF
disbursements.Inview of the sizable resources needed in2008, the IMFPRGF, inan amount of
US$108.4 million and approved by the IMF Board in mid-April2008, is slightly frontloaded to
contribute in2008 toward closing the financing gap. This would be accompanied by centralbank
credit inthe amount o fUS$13.8 million unlockedbyPRGF disbursements.

3.21   Togo's financial sector performance has declined sharply since the 1990s' as
economic performance deteriorated. The sector includes ten commercial banks and ten
insurance companies. Difficulties are most severe in three banks: two are under state control-
Union Togolaise de Banque (UTB), Banque Togolaisepour le Commerce et I'Industrie (BTCJ.);
and the third, Banque Internationale pour I'Afrique (BIA), is a private bank whose main
shareholder has indicated his decision to leave Togo. These three banks, accounting for 56


                                                22

percent of the Togolese bankingmarket, have negative equity, fail to comply with the prudential
standards, andhave highnon-performing loans. The latter represented 30 percent oftotal credit at
the end o f 2006, compared to the WAEMU average o f 10 percent. The banks require a
recapitalization, but also a comprehensive restructuring(notably to reform corporate governance,
lower operating costs, strengthen loan procedures and internal controls and develop management
information systems).

3.22     The high exposure of Togo's banks to the public sector still constitutes an unhealthy
concentration of risks and is crowding out financing for the private sector. Inaddition, the
large stock o f domestic arrears o f the Government to the local private sector i s severely
constraining the ability o f private enterprises to service their debts to the banking system. The
Caisse d'Epargne du Togo (CET), which was recapitalizedby a public offering and which has
received a commercial bank license (under the name Bunque Populaire pour Z'Epargne et le
Crbdit), also requires an operational restructuring. Furthermore, the pension branches o f the two
social security institutions have accumulated losses over the years and the civil service pension
system requires significant annual subsidies from the State. With regard to micro-finance
institutions, their range o f service and outreach remains limited and their financial positions
fragile. Weak professional capacity and a lack of supervision and regulation hamper an
appropriate development o fthe micro-finance sector.

The Government's reformprogram

3.23     Restructuring ailing state-owned banks will be critical to macroeconomic stability
and effective financial intermediation. Given the existing situation, the Government's
short-term priority is to restructure the banking sector and formulate a financial sector
development strategy. The Government has already initiated the process by strengthening bank
monitoring and developing a bank restructuring strategy based on the recommendations o f the
WAEMUbankingcommission andtechnical advice provided by the Fundand World Bank. The
Government eliminated a portion o f the negative equity and increased the cash flow of two state
banks by providing them a subsidy to be paid over seven years in exchange for some fully
provisioned loans. The Government carried out a similar operation with the Caissed 'Epargne du
Togo, and in addition proceeded with the closure o f 20 branches and a downsizing by 239
employees. It also entered into negotiations with the private bank's main shareholder to obtain a
contributionto the absorption o f accumulated losseswhich will reduce its negative net worth.

3.24     For the three banks that have negative net worth, the restructuring will have three
components. In the very short-term, the aim is to stop the hemorrhaging. For the two state
owned banks (BTCI and UTB), the Government intends to replace their non performing loans
with government securities, in contribution to the recapitalization o f the banks. To avoid that
these securities create too much rigidity inthe management o fthe banks' assets, the Government
will submit a request to the BCEAO to allow these securities to be refinanced by the central
bank. Concurrently, the Government plans to strengthen the corporate governance o f these two
institutions, in one case (BTCI) by putting in place an interim administration to manage the
implementation o f the restructuring measures, as recommended by the Banking Commission.
The recently appointed interim administrator has been given precise terms o f reference and will
report to the BTCI Monitoring Committee. In the near term, the Government's priority i s to


                                                 23

recapitalize BTCI and UTB by issuing government bonds, includingsome that are tradable. The
Government also intendsto restore UTB's board o f directors.

3.25     A second stage will be to deepen the reforms. At the financial level, the Government
intends to make efforts to reduce its securities inthe banks' balance sheets where they make up
too large a proportion o f the portfolio. Inthe case o f the private bank BIA, the recapitalization
approach will depend on the level o f contribution o f the main shareholder for absorption o f past
losses. All three banks will undergo operational restructuring. In addition, the authorities will
ensure that all banksmeet the regionalprudential requirements.

3.26     In a third stage, the Government and the concerned banks will seek strategic
partnerships, mainly with international commercial banks having adequate financial strength and
a recognized expertise. The banksundergoingrestructuringhave a urgentneedto strengthen their
capacity, which should be provided by bankingpartners. A number o f possibilities exist. Large
Nigerian and Moroccan banks have entered the WAEMU banking market, thus sharply
increasing competition. A number o f these banks have expressed interest in entering the Togo
market and inpartneringwith Togolese banks. The Government's bankrestructuring efforts will
also extend to other banks, such as the Sociktk Inter-Africaine de Banque (SIAB), that suffer
from highoperating costs inrelationto revenues. Efforts to seek strategic partnerships will not be
limitedto the three banks with negative net worth; it will also include the Banque Togolaise de
Dkveloppement (BTD) whose previous efforts at bringing in new investors have been
unsuccessful.

3.27     With respect to the two social securityinstitutions,the Governmentplansto launch
actuarial studies for both the public and private pension systems. Based on these studies,
parametric reforms (for contributions andbenefits) will be implemented after consultations with
all stakeholders. This will be followed by the necessary revisions inthe regulations for these two
institutions.

3.28     With regard to microfinance,the short-term goal will be to strengthen governance of
the institutions andtheir management information systems, encourage them to join networks, and
strengthen their supervision through the dedicated MEF department called Cellule d 'Appui et de
Soutien aux Institutions Mutualistes d 'Epargne et de Crkdit (CAS-IMEC).

Currentissues

3.29     Togo imports 100 percent of its petroleum needs and about 90 percent of the
electricity it consumes. The bulk o f the imported electricity is provided by Communautk
Electrique du Benin (CEB), a joint venture between Benin and Togo. In 2006-07, Togo
experienced an energy crisis marked by long periods of power outages, which hampered
economic growth and caused significant loss inindustrial production. The crisis was caused by a
prolonged period o f drought which depleted the water available for CEB's hydroelectric power
stations in Togo and power stations in Ghana that supply power to Togo, the absence o f
alternative power sources (inadequate thermal production capacity) and rising prices o f
petroleumproducts. Facedwith the failure o fneighboring countries to provide more electricity to



                                                24

Togo and the need to find a rapid solution to the crisis, the Government ordered new diesel
generators o f a capacity for about 20 megawatts (MW), whichwill be inservice bythe end o f the
first quarter o f 2008. These generator groups will help the Compagnie d 'Energie Electrique du
Togo (CEET) to meet demand for electricity duringpeak periods pending its commissioning o f a
100MW IPP plant,possibly in2009. Thereafter, it would be possible to install small generation
units (1to 2 MW) to urbancenters not yet connected to the national electricity grid.

3.30    Increased rainfall in Ghana in 2007 and a significant increase in production in
Ghanaianthermal capacity are expected to facilitate a returnto a balancebetween supply
and demand for electricity in 2008. CEB, Togo's supplier, is currently negotiating a new
contract for the purchase o f electricity with the Volta River Authority (VRA) o f Ghana, for larger
amounts than in the past and at a substantially higher price, given that the power is generated
from thermal energy. The production cost of the electricity for Togo will be mitigated somewhat
by the arrival o f natural gas in LomC in mid-2008 under contract with the West African Gas
Pipeline C~mpany'~,to power CEB's 20 MW plant. However, because CEET does not have a
contract for purchase o f natural gas from the Nigerian supplier, its planned 100 MW IPP plant
may have to operate with diesel fuel, entailing a high production cost. The Government is in
discussions with the NigerianGovernment to negotiate additional supplyofnatural gas for Togo.

3.31    Both the CEET and the CEB are in a difficult financial situation. The current
electricity rates inTogo do not allow CEET to cover its additional costs resulting from a decision
to put an end to the power outages. There i s a needto develop financial scenarios to determine
CEET's budget requirements andwhether an increase inthe electricity rates i s warranted.

3.32    The CEET was accorded sole charge of retaildistribution and sale of electricity in
Togo in2006. The Government established a four member Management CommitteeinCEET for
a transitional six month period, renewable. Upon taking office, the Management Committee had
to cope with the energy and financial crisis. This crisis prevented the Government from taking
the necessary measures to address the longer term issue o f the future o f CEET. As a result, the
Government renewed the mandate o f CEET's Management Committee three times. As the
energy crisis subsides, it is appropriate to put an end to this transitional management situation.

The Government'sreformprogram

managementof CEET,byestablishing a board of directors, appointing a General Manager, and

further accumulation o f arrears in 2009. Furthermore, the Government plans to commission a
strategic review of the future direction of the sector, financed by a recently approved LICUS
Trust Fundgrant.

                                   R I S K S AND RISK MITIGATION

6.16    In the current environment of a country emerging from a socio-political crisis, the

6.17    Political risk. Despite progress in buildingconsensus, a resurgence o f influence o f non-
reformers or o f other political tensions could delay or stall implementation o f the Government's
reform program. A delay could jeopardize the attainment o f program objectives, dampen donor
support, and provoke a resurgence o f political instability. Also, slow progress in addressing the
most pressing social and economic problems could lead to instability in volatile areas and
adversely affect security conditions, the business climate and macroeconomic management.
While mitigation o f the political risk falls outside o f the direct scope o f this operation, it i s
expected that frequent broad-based consultations with stakeholders to strengthen the case for
reforms and the Government's commitment to them would help mitigate this risk. Inthe Letter o f
Development Policy and through the actions it has already implemented, the Government has
expressed its strong commitment to pursue and deepen the reforms already launched. Also, to
reduce vested interests' resistance to reform in critical sectors and to enhance governance, steps
are being taken to set the stage for more transparent and accountable practices inthe phosphates,
energy, cotton and financial sectors. In addition, the proposed prior actions focus on areas in

6.18    Macroeconomic risk to the economic recovery could stem from an inability o f the

I.ThePoliticalandSocio-EconomicContext

2.      The transition from a single-party regime to a multiparty system was particularly p a i n h l for
Togo. From 1990 to 2007, the country was shaken by a political crisis with economic implications which
have been amplified by the sanctions o f the donor community. Indeed, the European Union suspended its

result has been the significant accumulation o f domestic and external arrears.

3.      The death o f President Gnassingbt Eyadtma in February 2005 was followed by the election of
Faure Essozimna Gnassingbt as President o f the Republic o f Togo in April 2005. The election triggered
several weeks o f intense civil unrest, prompting a military intervention and an exodus o f refugees to
neighboring countries (Ghana and Benin). Lengthy negotiations among the main political parties,

4.      The elections were held on October 14, 2007. The President's party, Rassemblement du Peuple
Togolais (WT) won a comfortable majority o f seats at the National Assembly (51 out o f Sl), followed
by the Union des Forces de Changement (UFC), an oppositionparty, with 27 seats out o f 81 and o f the
Comitb d'Action Pour le Renouveau (CAR), another opposition party, with 4 seats. The constitutional
court validated the elections by declaringthem to be fair. The majority o f EUand other foreign observers
also endorsed the results of the election, despite some shortcomings. All the political parties who won
seats during the legislative elections are sitting at the National Assembly. After the elections, the EU

5.       OnDecember 14,2007, the Presidentnamed anew Government, composedof 15 members o fthe

6.       The prolonged period o f political instability and the related withdrawal o f donor support have

FinancialSector Reform

24.     Togo's financial sector performance has deteriorated sharply since the 199Os, due to the
degradation o f the economy's structure. Three banks, two o f which are State owned, account for over
56% of the Togolese banking sector, and have negative net worth. They fail to comply with the
prudential norms, and have a large portfolio o f non-performing loans. These banks require not only a
recapitalization, but also a comprehensive restructuring. The Caisse d 'Epargne of Togo (CET), which
was recapitalized by a public offering and whose application for a license to operate as a commercial
bank operations was approved, also requires restructuring. The pension sector comprising two social
security institutions, the Caisse Nationale de Sbcuritd Sociale (CNSS) and the Caisse de Retraite du
Togo (CRT), have accumulated losses over several years and the civil service pension system requires
significant annual subsidies from the State. This situation requires reforms inthis sector.

25.      Given this situation, the Government's short-term priority i s to restructure the banking sector.
The Government has already initiatedthe process by strengthening the monitoring o f the banks. It intends
to adopt a strategy o f restructuring the banks based on the recommendations o f the WAMU Banking
Commission and on the technical assistance provided inMarch 2007 by the International Monetary Fund
and the World Bank. The Government eliminated a portion o f the negative equity and increased the cash
flow o f the two state banks by providing them a subsidy in exchange for the repurchase o f certain loans
o f public interest which should be totally provisioned for. The Government carried out a similar


                                                     49

operation with the Caisse d'Epargne du Togo. It also entered into negotiations with the private bank's
main shareholder to recapitalize the bank.

26.      For the three banks that do not comply with the prudential norms, the restructuring will have
three components. In the very short-term, the aim i s to stop the financial bleeding. For the two state
owned banks, la Banque Togolaise pour le Commerce et 1'Industrie (BTCI), et 1'Union Togolaise de
Banque (UTB), the Government will replace their non-performing loans with government securities. To
avoid that these securities create too much rigidity in the management o f the banks' assets, the
Government will request the BCEAO to allow these securities to be refinanced, among others, by the
central bank. Concurrently, the Government has strengthened governance by proceeding to change the
management o f one o f the institutions concerned (BTCI) in line with the recommendations o f the
Banking Commission. The new management o f the bank will be given precise terms o f reference and will
report to a monitoring committee. Inthe short term, priority will be given to the recapitalization o f the
B T C I and the UTB through the securitization o f non-performing loans, and some o f these obligations
will be negotiable. At the same time, the Government will accelerate negotiations with the main
shareholder o f the private bank on its recapitalization and restructuring. The Government also intends to
restore UTB's Board o f Directors and identify a strategic partner.

27.      A second stage will be to deepen the reforms. Efforts will be made to reduce the government
securities in the banks' balance sheets where they make up too large a proportion of the portfolio. Inthe
case o f the private bank, Banque International pour 1ilfrique (BIA), the recapitalization approach will
depend on the level o f contribution o f the main shareholder for absorption o f past losses. The
restructuring o f operations will be extended to other financial institutions, including B T C I and UTB. To
improve cash management and create more marketable financial instruments, the Government will
prepare an action plan for auctions o f Treasury bills in Togo, on the basis o f recommendations o f the
recent Financial System Assessment Program (FSAP) o f the WAMU. The Government will consult with
the BCEAO on the evolution o f the monetary situation and possible measures to limit the risks associated
with rapidcredit expansion, including through adjustments o fthe requiredreserve coefficients.

28.      In a third stage, the Government and the banks involved will seek strategic partners, mainly
international commercial banks with solid financial profiles and a recognized expertise. Given the key
role o f the banking sector, the Government gives priority to the financial and operational restructuring o f
the institutions inthis sector.

RecentEconomicDevelopments

Togo has made significant progress ineconomic and political reforms over the past two years,
paving the way for resumptiono f donor support after more than a decade o f interruption.
Performance under a recent Staff-Monitored Program (SMP) was good, with all quantitative
targets comfortably met and significant progress inaddressing governance issues inpublic
finances.

After an upturnin2006, real GDP growth moderated to about 2 percent in2007 partly due to a
severe region-wide energycrisis. The current account deficit increased to an estimated 6%
percent o f GDP. The fiscal position improved substantially in2007 as reforms intax and
customs administrationboosted revenues andbetter fiscal discipline kept spendingbroadly in
line with the budget, allowing a net reduction indomestic arrears.


Programsummary

The main objectives o f the PRGF-supported program are to help Togo revive economic growth
and improve livingconditions within a stable macroeconomic environment. The program will
focus on (i) bringingTogo's excessive public debt to a sustainable level through fiscal
adjustment and comprehensive debt relief under the HIPC andMDR Initiatives, (ii)       facilitating
the reengagement with Togo's development partners, (iii)     increasing resources for health,
education, and infrastructures, (iv) strengthening fiscal governance, (v) restructuring state-
owned banks, and (vi) initiating growth-oriented structural reforms to improve the business
environment and reform state-owned enterprises.

Economic growth i s projected to increase to 3 percent in2008. This initial recovery phase is
