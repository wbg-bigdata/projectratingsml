F. Results Framework Analysis

Program Development Objectives (from Program Document)
The overriding objective of the second PRSO series is to support implementation of the
Government Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy, and in particular
the Flagship pillars on (i) growth for jobs and exports, and (ii) Governance, as outlined
below:
  (i) Growth and Exports Flagship: (a) improving the health status of the population:
(b) enhancing the skills of the population including through a shift away from TVET to
PBE; (c) increasing access to infrastructure services, specifically energy, water and
transport through (d) improving cost-effective and operational efficiency in the energy
sector; (e) developing a framework for integrated and sustainable management of water
resources; (f) increasing rural road access by improving the condition of the classified
district Road network; (g) raising agricultural productivity and production; and (h)
broadening and deepening the financial sector.
  (ii) Governance Flagship: (a) strengthening the public financial system and its
management at the central and local levels; (b) improving capacity and transparency of
procurement methods; (c) implementing budget and fiscal decentralization reforms; (iv)
civil service reforms and incentives to build skills and retention.

1. Program Context, Development Objectives and Design

1.1 Context at Appraisal
1.       Overview of preceding series. The first series of Poverty Reduction Support Financings
(PRSFs)1 consisted of a credit (PRSC-1, 2004) and two grants (PRSG-2 and 3, covering 2005 and
2006 respectively). It supported Rwanda’s first Poverty Reduction Strategic Paper (PRSP-1) in three
priority areas: (i) private sector development; (ii) improvement in access to and quality of socio-
economic services, mainly in education, health, water and energy; and (iii) improvement of economic
and financial governance. The Implementation Completion and Results Report (ICR, 326) rated the
series as satisfactory in every respect, given that achievements (i.e. progress towards development
objectives) far exceeded expectations and plans at the outset of the program with the exception of
procurement. The series was based on a solid analytic foundation and was effectively aligned with the
PRSP and the Bank’s Country Assistance Strategy (CAS). Risk to development outcome was assessed
as moderate given the country’s strong performance with respect to implementation. Bank
performance was considered as satisfactory both as regards to quality at entry and supervision.
Borrower performance was rated highly satisfactory. Coordination with other donors was generally
satisfactory. Supervision was well coordinated with other donors through the twice yearly Joint
Budget Support Reviews (JBSR) and the International Monetary Fund (IMF) reviews of its Poverty
Reduction and Growth Facility (PRGF). Adequate recourse was given to existing Monitoring and
Evaluation (M&E) instruments. The ICR drew attention to three risks (i) conflict flare-ups in the sub-
region, in particular Eastern Democratic Republican of Congo (DRC), which could cause instability
that could interrupt reforms; (ii) reform fatigue domestically; and (iii) the need for increased fiscal
discipline, as more resources flow in the form of budget support. The Independent Evaluation Group
agreed with the ICR ratings.
2.        Borrower performance was rated highly satisfactory overall, with the Government receiving a
rating of highly satisfactory, and the implementing agency – the Ministry of Finance and Economic
Planning (MINECOFIN) – a rating of satisfactory. Prior actions were fully met with two exceptions in
PRSG-2 and PRSG-3, pertaining to (i) private sector growth (privatization of tea factories) and (ii)
transparency and accounting (adoption of new procurement legislation). The ICR also considered that
the first series had contributed to institutional strengthening in many ways, in particular with respect to
MINECOFIN’s capacity to (i) coordinate policy dialogue with donor partners and other stakeholders;
(ii) strengthen relationships with technical ministries; and (iii) engage simultaneously on a range of
issues. Beneficiary surveys and stakeholder workshops were in broad agreement with these findings.
3.       Political context. Since the 1994 genocide, the Government of Rwanda (Government) had
invested in a major effort to promote reconciliation and peace, resettle and reintegrate refugees,
displaced persons and ex-combatants, as well as bring justice to the thousands of people in the
aftermath of the genocide. The key institutions in this regard were the National Unity and
Reconciliation Commission and the system of Gacaca courts, modeled after a traditional approach to
settling disputes, designed to engage citizens in the genocide trials. A new constitution was
promulgated in 2003 that provided the legal framework for managing, controlling and resolving social
conflicts, and for power sharing at the top of the Government. It states that the President is elected for
a renewable seven year term, but he cannot appoint more than 50 percent of the cabinet from his own
party, and the speaker of the parliament must come from the opposition party. Paul Kagame was
elected for his first term in 2003, so that in 2007 –when the second PRSG series began to be prepared-
new elections were three years away.

10.      One of the major factors determining the trends in spatial disparities in poverty and the
increase in rural-urban inequality had been the continued low level of productivity in the agriculture
sector, the principal activity in rural areas, and the frequent unfavorable weather conditions in a
predominantly rain-fed production system. The agricultural sector grew rapidly in the early post
genocide years as idle land and neglected farms were brought back into production by refugees and
displaced persons returning home. Once this process was over however, increases in productivity
became critical for agricultural growth. But this could not occur due to lack of progress in the use of
modern inputs. Measures needed to increase productivity were for that reason identified as key during
the 2007 preparation of the Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS) and the
Strategic Plan for Agricultural Transformation (SPAT).
11.      Progress towards Millennium Development Goals. Rwanda appeared to be making good
progress towards the Millennium Goals. According to the ICR for PRSGs 1-3, three had already been
attained, one was likely to be attained, nine were judged attainable provided that sufficient resources
were allocated to them; four were rated as challenging, and one unlikely (PRSG 1-3 ICR, Table 3
below). One may note in particular that in the education sector, attaining a 100 percent completion rate
would require additional funding of US$4.5 per capita per year; and eliminating the gender gap would
require implementation of existing policies aimed at ensuring 100 percent access to a nine year basic
education. In the health sector, reducing mortality rates for infants and children under five years of age
would require additional funding of US$3 per capita per year for high impact interventions and
vulnerable groups. Similar resources would also be required to ensure that all births are attended by
skilled personnel. Reducing by half the proportion of people without access to safe water would
require investments of US$2.8 per capita per year over ten years.
12.     With regard to goals where attainment was judged likely to be a challenge, halving the
proportion of people living on less than US$1 per day would require strong growth, coupled with
increased agricultural sector productivity. Similarly halving the proportion of malnourished children
would also require strong and sustained economic growth as well as nutrition interventions. Reducing
maternal mortality by three quarters would require a major upgrading of the health system, and tripling
spending on public health.

13.      In summary looking backwards, Rwanda had been a strong performer, with good policy
dialogue with the community of external donors. There was a good rationale for continuing to provide
financial assistance in the form of General Budget Support. The Government had immense financial
needs, their commitment to reform appeared unwavering, other development partners (DPs) had
indicated that they would continue to provide budget and/or balance of payments support as well, and
risks – political economic and financial appeared minimal and manageable were they to materialize.
14.     However, looking forward, it was felt that growth rates of 4-6 percent in the long run would
make it unlikely that the Government’s Vision 2020 and poverty reduction targets could be met.
Moreover, there would need to be more emphasis on infrastructure development while consolidating
the progress made in social sectors during the first PRSF series. Hence, the need to substantially raise
the GDP growth rate which was to be a key objective of the second PRSF series.
1.2 Original Program Development Objectives (PDO) and Key Indicators
15.     The principal objective of the second PRSF series was to support the implementation of
Rwanda’s EDPRS – which was in turn a key objective of the Bank’s Interim Strategy Note3 and 2008
CAS.4 The EDPRS, the Government’s second-generation PRSP, was launched in September 2007 and
elaborates a development plan for 2008-2012. It outlines the strategies and actions to stimulate rapid
growth leading to a reduction of poverty in line with the Government’s national vision document
(Vision 2020) and the MDGs. The EDPRS contains three flagship programs: (i) Sustainable Growth
for Jobs and Exports; (ii) Vision 2020 Umurenge; and (iii) Governance. Monitoring of the program
takes place in the context of the JBSR. In 2008, the Government conducted the first JBSR that was
based on a jointly established Common Performance Assessment Framework (CPAF). This section
reports on overall progress under the three main pillars of the EDPRS as well as on participation,
coordination and monitoring mechanisms for the EDPRS.
16.    The second PRSF series supported two EDPRS pillars: growth for jobs and exports, and
governance, while a companion series of three Development Policy Operations (DPOs) – the
Community Living Standards Grants (CLSG) supported the second pillar, that is community

17.      Under the second PRSF series, support to growth oriented measures was to be scaled up, with
an emphasis on improving the delivery of economic and infrastructure services, critical to growth,
poverty reduction and export development. Various analytical pieces, in particular, the Country
Economic Memorandum (CEM), the Diagnostic Trade Integrated Study (DTIS) and the Investment
Climate Assessment (ICA) identified the constraints to growth, exports and investment, emphasizing
the need to build and maintain the stock of infrastructure and skills. The CEM and other reports
around the time of the launch of the series (Table 6) particularly highlighted the importance of
agriculture in sustaining growth over the medium term and the Agriculture Policy Note (APN)
identified poor input use, water management and soil conservation along with insufficient capacity
support to farmers as the most critical constraints to address in order to raise productivity. Expanding
production capacity was noted as the most critical issue that Rwanda had to address, in order for
exports to be increased. Government also undertook a number of reforms, with World Bank support,
to improve trade facilitation, but while this lead to significant improvements in Rwanda's investment
climate indicators these measures could not by themselves lead to greater exports. The second PRSF
series therefore focused on expanding production capacities and this provided the rationale for
including infrastructure and agriculture, in particular, but also skills.
18.      The areas supported by the PRSFs 4-7 were as follows: (i) support the implementation of the
EDPRS; (ii) maintain a stable macro-economic framework; and (iii) create a strong basis for private
sector led growth driven by agricultural transformation, export promotion, financial sector deepening,
more use of Information and Communication Technology (ICT) and improved economic and financial
governance. The specific supported EDPRS programs were as follows:
x Flagship/pillar Growth and Exports: (i) enhancing the skills of the population including through
  a shift away from Technical and Vocational Education Training (TVET) to Post-Basic Education
  (PBE); (ii) increasing access to infrastructure services, specifically energy, water and transport
  through (a) improving cost-effective and operational efficiency in the energy sector; (b) developing
  a framework for integrated and sustainable management of water resources; (iii) increasing rural
  road access by improving the condition of the classified district Road network; (iv) raising
  agricultural productivity and production; and (v) broadening and deepening the financial sector.
x Flagship/pillar Governance: (i) strengthening the public financial system and its management at
  the central and local levels; (ii) improving the capacity and transparency of procurement methods;
  (iii) pursuing the implementation of budget and fiscal decentralization reforms; (iv) deepening civil
  service reforms and incentives to build skills and retention.
19.     In addition to these development objectives, the second series had very important additional
agenda, albeit implicit, which was to support the establishment and use of a CPAF to be used by all
DPs to assess progress under their individual budget support operations. The CPAF was completed
and adopted in December 2008.
20.     Each PRSF contained a set of expected outcomes with related intermediate performance
indicators, prior actions, identified indicative triggers for the next operation(s). In the case of PRSG-4,
the prior actions and indicative triggers for the prospective PRSGs 5 and 6 were initially drawn from
an operation-specific policy matrix, with a set of measurable outcome indicators for the series as a
whole. But by the time PRSG-5 was approved, the policy matrix used in PRSG-4 was abandoned


                                                    6
because of the adoption of the CPAF – itself a subset of the EDPRS implementation matrix, from
which all prior actions, triggers and performance indicators were supposed to be drawn.
21.     Each operation was expected to support the budget of a specific Rwandan financial year, with
PRSG-4 supporting fiscal year 2008 which corresponded to calendar year 2008. However, in July 1,
2009 the Rwandan Financial Year changed from July to June to harmonize it with the East African
Community budget calendar. Thus, PRSG-5 and PRSG-6 supported a mini-budget from January to
June 2009, and PRSF-7 supported the year from July 1, 2009 to June 30, 2010. PRSG-4 prior actions
were linked to end 2007 EDPRS indicators; PRSG-5 indicative triggers linked to end 2008 EDPRS
indicators, and PRSG-6 indicative triggers to end December 2009 EDPRS indicators. With the
emergence of the CPAF in 2008, the PRSG-6 prior actions were now being tied to the CPAF
indicators. PRSG-6, as approved in March 2010, effectively linked the triggers for PRSG-7 to end
June 2010 CPAF performance indicators, in line with the change of the Rwanda budget year (July-
June) and the new timing of the backward looking JBSR review (October).

23.    In the area of improving economic governance and development of implementation

x   Public Financial Management: Reinforce most of the accounting and internal reporting function

2.2 Major Factors Affecting Implementation:

37.       A number of factors contributed to the broadly successful implementation and outcome of the
second PRSC series: (i) alignment with Government policy instruments; (ii) Government ownership;
(iii) effective donor coordination; (iv) sound PFM arrangements; and (v) a good analytical basis.
38.      Alignment with Government Policy Instruments. The PRSFs were all aligned with the
Government’s poverty reduction strategy, or more explicitly the EDPRS and the first and third of its
key pillars (or flagship programs as they are called in the EDPRS).
39.     Government ownership. Government has been fully committed to complete and generally
timely implementation of the reforms, which it regards as its own. There have been no instances of
policy reversals. Performance under IMF programs has also been satisfactory. The JBSRs have all
concluded that program performance has been technically satisfactory, though some bilateral donors
have expressed unhappiness with political developments.
40.     Effective donor coordination. There are 17 Sector Working Groups (SWGs) organized around
four clusters (Growth, Rural Development, Human Development and Governance), comprising
stakeholders form central and local Government, donors, civil society organizations and the private
sector. Each SWG is chaired by a Lead Government Institution and co-chaired by a Lead Donor.
There are two reviews per year governed by an MoU which was signed in 2008. Donor coordination
seems to work relatively well, although there has been some friction during the introduction of the
CPAF, as the Bank has struggled to ensure that MoU policy actions comply with the prescriptions of
OP 8.60 regarding policy and institutional actions. On occasion as well, the Bank has had recourse to
non-CPAF measures.
41.     Sound PFM arrangements: The Government embarked in 2008 on the implementation of an
ambitious PFM Reform Strategy 2008-2012, which was based on the results of the 2007 PEFA. An
Integrated Personnel and Payroll System (IPPS) was installed and civil servants’ salaries have been
paid through it since February 2009. The alignment with the budget calendar of the EAC was
completed in July 2009, resulting in a switch to the July-June fiscal year from the calendar year. A
MTEF was successfully integrated into the 2009/10 budget process. The Government also started a
process to institutionalize Public Expenditure Reviews (PERs). The strong commitment of the

                                                  13
Government to prudent financial management increased donor confidence, was reflected in the
continued high share of budgetary grants, including the World Bank.
42.      Good analytical basis: The PRSF operations built effectively on a substantial amount of high
quality and very relevant analytical work prior to and during the series. This is well documented in all
of the PDs, PRSG-4 (Box 1) summarizing the structural issues well. It identifies four main areas for
constraints to growth in Rwanda: (i) low productivity in agriculture due to poor water management,
low use of modern inputs such as fertilizers and improved seeds and lack of extension services; (ii) the
poor condition of infrastructure; (iii) the low base of skilled labor, particularly technical and
managerial skills; and (iv) high bureaucratic and administrative costs of doing business. Table 6
provides a listing of the main reports, as well as the links between them and the principal policy
recommendations.

2.3 Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Design, Implementation and Utilization:

44.      Under PRSG-4 monitoring and evaluation arrangements were to continue to rely on
Government arrangements with the aim of strengthening Government capacity and institutions, as
under PRSFs 1-3, on the basis of the PRSG-4 policy matrix. MINECOFIN’s Strategic Planning Unit
was responsible for overall monitoring of the EDPRS. In addition, the Budget Monitoring and
Reporting team reported on spending of the budget and related outputs. The Bank worked closely with
these two units and coordinated with other donors, through the Joint Budget Support Review process
and the Budget Support Harmonization Group, to monitor spending and outputs under the second
series of PRSFs.
45.     As mentioned above, during PRSG-4, it was decided by the Government and DPs to put in
place a Common Policy Assessment Framework, drawn from the EDPRS Results and Policy Matrix,
that was to be used by all stakeholders, domestic and foreign alike, for the monitoring of the EDPRS
in general and budget support operations in particular. At the same time a Joint Governance
Assessment and PFM Action Plan entered into force with PRSG-5. The CPAF drew on the work of the
17 SWGs that support EDPRS implementation. This gave rise to the establishment of the Joint Budget
Support Memorandum of Understanding, signed in September 2008 of which the Bank became a
signatory. This MoU foresaw, either explicitly or implicitly, that (i) all triggers and prior actions for
budget support operations would henceforth be drawn from the CPAF (although they could be
summarized in an operation specific policy matrix as in PRSG-4); and (ii) all monitoring and
evaluation of the PRSFs would occur in the institutional framework of the MoU.
46.      This monitoring framework also foresaw two JBSRs per year, one backward looking and one
forward looking. The backward looking review occurs in October and reviews performance with
respect to agreed policy actions, performance indicators; and budget execution for the preceding year
on the basis of a budget execution report submitted to DPs by the Government. The forward-looking
review occurs in April and, itrevises policy actions, performance indicators targets for the outer CPAF
years and (ii) examines the structure of the Government’s draft budget, for the following fiscal year
prior to its submission to Parliament.
47.      Institutionally, this process appears to have worked quite well and to be capable of
significantly reducing transactions costs for the Rwandan authorities. In the beginning, the Bank
experienced difficulties in respecting the terms of the MoU in that (i) a number of triggers have been
taken from outside the CPAF; and (ii) the timeframe for implementation of triggers has not always
respected the letter and/or the spirit of the CPAF.
48.    The reason why PRSG-6 dropped some indicative triggers and replaced them by other triggers
which had not been included in the CPAF as set forth in PRSG-5 lay in the outcome of the Bank wide
review processes at the time, where the need was voiced to strengthen the quality of actions and to

                                                    15
have non-process oriented actions. The team therefore identified actions that had already been
completed or that the Government had assessed and agreed could be completed in time for the Board
meeting. For PRSG-6 the Bank also insisted on ex-post incorporation into the CPAF of measures
which it found more appropriate, which caused friction with Government and other donors. For the
PRSG-6 it also became necessary to have prior actions be evaluated at a point in time that did not
correspond to that foreseen by the MoU.
49.      For PRSG-4 and 5, the operations were effectively prepared in the fiscal year preceding the
fiscal year being supported, and disbursed in the first or second quarter of said fiscal year in line with
the Government’s desiderata and the MoU. Following the change in Government fiscal year in June
2009, the Bank’s PRSG-6 and 7 became out of sync with the Rwandan budget cycle. The PRSG-6
was approved in March 2010 (i.e. Q3 of the Rwandan Fiscal Year) and disbursed in June 2010, i.e.
very late in Q4. Similarly PRSF-7 was approved by the Bank Board in February 2011 and disbursed
in early April 2011, respectively the third and fourth quarters of the FY.
50.      The PRSG-4 supported the development of a national M&E framework for monitoring the
EDPRS including the establishment of an M&E secretariat in MINECOFIN and the recruitment of
sector M&E staff. In addition, there has been continued progress in the institutionalization of the
citizen report cards with the completion of community score cards in key sectors and institutional
mechanism for linking result to Imihigo contracts are being developed.
51.     The assessment of the poverty and social impact of the measures proposed by the EDPRS and
supported by the PRSF was carried out in the context of Rwanda’s EDPRS monitoring system. Some
environmental issues arose which required compliance with the prescriptions of OP8.60 as regards
environmental questions be validated, in particular regarding removal of taxes and duties on Liquefied
Petroleum Gas (LPG), use of fertilizers and water management.
52.     To improve monitoring systems, the Bank supported the Government’s efforts to build quality
and institutional capacity. Alongside the PRSG, the Bank is implementing grants from the Trust Fund
on Statistical Capacity Building and the Belgian Poverty Reduction Partnership Trust Fund aimed at
enhancing statistical capacity. The Bank’s ongoing investment projects each have an M&E component
that supports both the project and the relevant sectoral ministry. The Bank undertook a policy driven
technical assistance PER to provide capacity building support to Government in expenditure analysis
and monitoring.

2.4 Expected Next Phase/Follow-up Operation:

53.       It is expected that the Bank will continue to provide support to Rwanda through a third series
of PRSFs. This can be clearly justified by (i) financial need; (ii) satisfactory performance to date with
respect to implementation of its EDPRS in terms of progress as measured by (a) the performance
indicators and (b) the implementation of policy actions; and (iii) the satisfactory conclusion of the
Joint Budget Support Reviews; and (iv) the satisfactory conclusion of the 2010 PEFA. There appears
to be little risk of a significant reduction in budget support from other DPs.
54.     The Country team identified the issues to be addressed in a third series, of which some are of a
process nature, others substantive; and the risks.
55.     Process. The main issues to be addressed are; (i) better alignment with the Government’s
budget preparation timetable; and (ii) selection of triggers and prior actions only from the CPAF, as
implied by the terms of the Budget Support MoU signed by the Government and all other providers of
budget support, including the World Bank.
56.     The Bank’s Fiscal Year and the Government’s Financial year are now synchronous, alignment
requires that the PRSF of a specific FY would support the next Rwandan FY. This means that
approval of PRSF should occur ideally in Q4 or the beginning of Q1, with disbursement occurring

                                                   16
shortly after, in Q1 of the next following fiscal year. This implies that the Bank will ideally have
made a credible commitment or pledge so that the Rwandan authorities can incorporate the proceeds
of PRSFs into the new Budget Law, normally at the time of the forward looking JBSR. At the same
time, compliance with the prescriptions of the MoU with respect to policy actions (they come only
from the CPAF) would imply that all of these would have been judged satisfactorily and filled at the
time of the backward looking review in October the year prior.
57.     It will require the three operations of the new series for this alignment to be completely
attained. It was agreed with the Government that each of the following operations would be advanced
by one quarter. It will also require that the Bank will be closely involved in the definition of triggers
on the occasion of the forward looking reviews, which is when triggers are defined for the following
one or two years.
58.      Substance. The proposed series will broadly continue previous reform focus. As Government
decided to extend the EDPRS to the end of 2012/13, rather than December 2012 following the change
in fiscal year, this means the completion of the EDPRS will coincide with the third PRSF cycle,
supporting a broad continuation of the areas covered under the second series. The Bank decided to
follow broadly the Division of Labor (DoL) of 2010 while also taking on relevant cross cutting issues
in regard to sectors of the EDPRS flagships covered with the proposed PRSF series. This is also in
agreement with the spirit of the budget support MoU, that the Bank as provider of general budget
support, is in principle free to take part in any sector dialogue (including providing TA or analytical
work), backed by relevant global experience and track record in achieving sector outcomes.
59.      Supporting the implementation of Rwanda’s reform agenda across two out of three flagships
requires being selective within each of them, but the central themes of economic growth and
governance, would be retained. The broadening or narrowing of scope of sectoral/structural issues was
also influenced by the options to address them in purely sectoral operations or Sector Wide
Approaches. An argument in favor of the preparation of a more focused third PRSF series for Rwanda
was the Government’s DoL. The goal to limit each DP’s activities to three sectors in an effort to
increase aid effectiveness. It is suggested that some prioritization in reform support of a future series
would be advantageous, particularly, since the quality of the CPAF and the PRSF depends crucially on
the participation in sector dialogue through in-country sector staff. The reform program should provide
synergies and opportunities for policy dialogue to other operations in the portfolio.
60.     On the basis of the evidence obtained in the preparation of this ICR, there are issues to be
addressed with respect to PFM and Civil Service Reform. For example, a future series could do more
to shows the results of improved PFM in terms of lower fiduciary risks, as demonstrated by internal
and external audits. In the same vein, the third series could delve more into the structure to public
expenditure to ensure that the public expenditure issues identified at the sector level (for example
adequate funding of the Road Fund) are being effectively addressed, etc.
61.      The success of the previous series in supporting the expansion of production capacity, laid the
foundation for the next series, which should progressively move from a focus on measures to support
production capacity to those that directly support increased exports. As production expands, issues
related to processing, handling, storage, etc. will increasingly require attention. These are areas that the
team plans to ensure that they are reflected in the CPAF.

3.1 Relevance of Objectives, Design and Implementation
63.      All components of the operations –and thus the operations as a whole-- were relevant, both at
the time of conception and in hindsight, to Rwanda’s EDPRS key objectives of (i) promoting growth
for jobs and exports; and (ii) strengthening governance.
64.     In general, design was satisfactory. The operations, all in all, hold together in a credible
manner and produced significant results in terms of (i) reduced constraints to growth and access to
services; (ii) improvements in PFM at the national and district level; (iii) improvements in public
sector capacity; and (iv) a continued shift in the structure of public expenditures towards priority
poverty reducing expenditures – for a detailed discussion see Annex 1.
65.      But there were a number of other areas where the design could have been improved, but not
all were within the scope of the series and task team. First of all the operations were too broad in
scope, covering too many sectors (ten in all), and too many themes (15-16 in all). The result was that
it has been difficult, even in the context of this ICR, to discern what were the most important issues
and whether some other issues, following the example of health, could have been treated just as
effectively in other policy operations or by other donors. Second, the design of the fiscal
decentralization component was unsatisfactory, because it is not clear, ex-post, how the triggers,
policy actions and performance introduced through PRSGs 5 and 6 as well as PRSF-7 indicators
related to the component’s stated objective of fiscal decentralization. Another weakness in the design

                                                              18
of the indicators –and this is a criticism of the CPAF-- is that the outcome indicators should be less
process-oriented, for example, they could be more focused on whether the fiscal decentralization
reforms were effective and were leading to expected results. In the case of the fiscal decentralization
component, the measures and indicators appear ultimately to have related much more to the overall
objectives of improving local Government capacity, and improving local citizen participation.
66.      Strong efforts were made by the Bank team to design a strong operation given a number of
constraining challenges over the life of the series. Despite the two considerable challenges the Bank
team (and Government and other donors) faced during the implementation of the series, the PRSF
series continued to be adapted and delivered on a yearly basis. These two challenges were (1) the
establishment and application of the Common Performance Assessment Framework from 2008 and
during PRSF-5, as part of the aid effectiveness agenda, and (2) the challenging change of Government
fiscal year from calendar year to June-July in mid-2009. Other challenges included the low capacity
and high staff turnover on the Government’s side, the long list of Government’s priorities and its high
pace of change, and the learn-as-you-go coordination of the new Sector Working Groups. In that
regard, it is also important to note that there was strong engagement at the Sector Working Group
levels and that the Bank participated fully at the Joint Budget Support Reviews, which was used as
basis for preparing and monitoring the operation. The Bank also had leveraged the PRSF to identify
additional concessional funding for Rwanda (this included US$10 provided through the Global Food
Crisis Response Program as additional financing for PRSG-5, US$29.8 million from the Crisis
Response Window (CRW) for PRSG-6, and US$24 million from IDA-15 for PRSF-7).
67.     It is noted that the operations had only limited data and information based on which to assess
issues of poverty monitoring and the impact of policies and registered growth in regard to poverty
reduction. Even though the team would have liked to address these issues more prominently in the
discussion of the structure of growth the lack of relevant household data prevented such a discussion.6

3.2 Achievement of Program Development Objectives
68.      Progress towards development objectives is rated overall satisfactory (with four sectors highly
satisfactory and five sectors satisfactory), with the exceptions of transport and fiscal decentralization,
where the rating is moderately satisfactory. The quality of the results framework was generally
moderately satisfactory, albeit that there were frequently only weak links between policy actions and
desired outcomes.
69.      All components of the operations were important given the context of the country and the
program design which was underpinned by a robust suite of analytical pieces. There was an adequate
focus on growth and export growth under the PRSF series, in particular, on expanding production
capacity, including infrastructure, agriculture and skills. Support to input provision, in particular in
agriculture, resulted in Government's ability to sustain production (and even export to neighboring
countries during the crisis) and highlights the importance of increased production for exports. To the
extent to which the growth pillar being supported by the PRSF could be regarded as being inclusive, it
is important to note that inclusive growth cannot be achieved without sustained growth in the
agriculture sector (from which still about 80 percent of the Rwanda’s population derives its livelihood)
and investment in schooling -- which were important areas of focus for the series. A detailed account
of progress towards development objectives and key achievements by component is provided in
Annex 1.
70.     In the case of transport, the rating of moderately satisfactory is primarily on account of the 18
percent shortfall with respect to the outcome target (percentage of districts roads in good condition).

priority spending would increase annually by about 2 percent of GDP over the remainder of the PRSG
period depending on the availability of financing. More resources would be dedicated to public
infrastructure where spending was to be geared towards addressing the issue of limited and insufficient
energy through construction of hydro power dams, generation of power from the Lake Kivu Methane
gas power plant, and rehabilitation of infrastructure. Furthermore, financing of the large investments
needed to close the infrastructure gap and boost agricultural productivity posed a significant challenge,
but would be undertaken if necessary on less than concessional terms, but without compromising
external debt sustainability. At the decentralized level, transfers to districts were projected to increase
as the Government implemented its fiscal decentralization policy, with increases in capitation grants in
education and performance based financing in the health sector, feeder roads financing in the transport
sector and funds to cover district overhead costs.

of US$86 million (about Rwf51.6 billion). However, the international financial crisis had caused a
sharp reduction in exports and fiscal revenues, and this had caused the Government to introduce a
2009/2010 budget with a reduction of 0.4 percent in the expenditure/GDP ratio. In this context, to help

Government to maintain and accelerate poverty reducing spending in education, agriculture, and
infrastructure. In education, the Government accelerated the 9-year basic education program for the

agriculture, the resources helped maintain the Government’s planned activities under the Crop
Intensification Program to improve agriculture productivity with an emphasis on capacity support to
farmers in the use of improved inputs and access to rural finance. The interventions to improve
agriculture productivity and access to markets were instrumental in mitigating the effects of the crisis
and improving Rwanda’s resilience to external shocks. In the infrastructure sector, the Governmen t
emphasized road maintenance, particularly in rural areas.
78.     PRSF-7 provided total support of US$104 million (about Rwf60 billion), of which US$70
million in the form of a grant and the remainder as an IDA Credit). In 2010/2011, expenditures
continued to increase by about 1.6 percent of GDP, but mainly in non-priority expenditure. However,
notwithstanding significantly higher revenues (1.1 percent of GDP), the lower level of foreign grants
in nominal terms and relative to GDP (10.2 percent compared to 13.1 percent in 2009/2010),
additional foreign financing was expected to be needed (project loans amounting 1.8 percent of GDP
compared to 1.0 percent in 2009/20100). Also domestic financing from banking system was expected
to amount 1.9 percent of GDP as against 0.3 percent in 2009/2010.
79.     Thus, overall, the second PRSF series clearly contributed significantly to budget financing in
general and to the protection of priority expenditure in particular though this tapered off in 2010/2011.
But in addition, the series has made a significant contribution to institutional development, aiming to
improve the targeting and efficiency of priority and pro-poor expenditure. A special effort has been
made to improve the institutional framework of the Public Investment Program, with the adoption of a
Public Investment Policy and the issuance of guidelines to improve project selection and safeguard
debt sustainability. The Government has also embarked on the process to institutionalize PERs, with
specific assistance from the World Bank, with the aim of strengthening public expenditure
management and monitoring of spending. In 2010, PERs were completed for the Water and Sanitation,
Agriculture, and Education and Social Protection sectors. Some of them, for example Agriculture and
Transport, provide an excellent link between the structural and public expenditure aspects of sectoral
development.

83.    Sustainability of development outcomes are subject to four main categories of risk: (i) regional
and country risk; (ii) macroeconomic risk; (iii) program risks; and (iv) fiduciary risks. In the
aggregate, these appear low.
84.     Regional and Country risks. Rwanda’s internal and political situation appears stable. In
2010, the country peacefully reelected the incumbent president for his second and last term under the
Constitution. In order to mitigate the risk of a resurgence of internal ethnic unrest due to unequal
access to and distribution of resources, the Authorities focus large parts of public expenditures on
poverty reduction priorities and address remaining disparities with explicit interventions to assist the
poorest groups identified through the VUP. Additionally, the participatory planning and poverty
assessment in use – the Ubudehe program – assesses how the poor are identified and offers
recommendations for reducing poverty.

appear to be (i) slow growth of exports on account of slow uptake in international demand, coupled

under the Policy Support Instrument Facility (PSI). It has also successfully managed the international
crises albeit in part due to the additional financial support of the international community, in particular
through the IDA CRW and the Catalytic Fund for Africa which provided additional funds through the
PRSFs.
86.      Program Risks. The potential risk of a slowdown in implementation of reforms appears
unlikely in the foreseeable future, given the Government’s strong track record. However, there
remains a residual risk due to limited implementation capacity Also, it would be important for the
Government to ensure that growth remains inclusive. Program risks could also result from other
factors: (i) an economic slowdown, resulting from either a decline in export earnings and hence
foreign exchange availability and/or fiscal receipts; and (ii) a slowdown in donor funding, in particular
budget support. The Government intends to mitigate the first risk through an export diversification
strategy, but this has not yet produced the desired results. It is also pursuing its efforts to enhance
revenue collection, but it needs to avoid excesses which might penalize economic activity.
87.      Vulnerability to donor funding could result from a deterioration in the overall world economic
situation, although Rwanda appears to have fared also well over the last two years, notwithstanding a
temporary suspension of economic aid, including budget support, on the part of two bilateral donors
on the grounds of dissatisfaction with Rwanda’s role in the Eastern DRC. This risk could be
exacerbated by increasing bilateral disaffection with Rwanda on account of internal political
developments especially regarding freedom of the press, etc.
88.      An important risk is that implementation could be compromised by lack of capacity. In this
area, the Government is currently seeking to redress through its Strategic Capacity Building Initiative,
which is cross-sectoral in nature and being supported by key DPs including the World Bank Group. In
addition, the Government has a number of thematic capacity building initiatives in the areas of
accounting, procurement, local government.
89.      Fiduciary risks. During the series considerable progress has been made regarding PFM at the
national and sub-national levels, as is reflected in the 2010 PEFA report which reports considerable
improvement since the earlier report for 2007. Further improvements are expected as a result of the
ongoing PFM strategy and the current plans to finalize the IFMIS on the basis of the recommendations
of the international Quality Assurance Group established in the context of the PRSF series.
90.      At the beginning of the series, the main fiduciary risks were considered to concern public
financial management and procurement at the local level. This was attributable to the rapid pace of
fiscal decentralization in a situation of lack of fiduciary controls and capacity at the local level. During
the series, the Government has accelerated efforts to address these risks through (i) seeking to
institute a functioning IFMIS at the district level; (ii) strengthening support to procurement officers at
the district level; and (iii) increasing the number of qualified accountants at both the national and
district level in particular through accelerating the Rwanda Expertise Scheme. Under PRSG-7, the
Government has adopted a comprehensive 5-year capacity building strategy and action plan for local
government, which includes measures to strengthen public financial management. It also developed a
revised fiscal decentralization plan in 2011, which should lead to further improvements.
91.      Finally, it may be added that the Government effectively prosecutes misappropriation of
public funds. In summary, fiduciary risks appear less than at the beginning of the series, though it still
appears likely to be moderate. Given the country’s desire to further decentralize service delivery, it
will be important to pursue the mitigation measures.

6. Lessons Learned
103.   A first key lesson learned from the second PRSF series was that it proved to be effective in
advancing Government-owned reforms. The PRSF series has provided important support to the
Government to operationalize its ambitious medium-term development strategy (i.e., the EDPRS) by

                                                26
translating its objectives into priority actions. As the PRSF series has progressed, so the reform
agenda evolved, but remained overall broad. This is attributable to the breadth of the Bank’s policy
dialogue with Government, but also reflects the recognition by policy-makers of the utility of budget
support, including the PRSF series, in helping to move the reform process forward. While the series
set-out to support the second generation EDPRS, the process of designing the reform program has
evolved in ways that were not foreseen at the outset. A major change over the course of the series was
the finalization of the CPAF in December 2008 and the subsequent alignment of all donor budget
support, including the PRSF series with it.
104.    The PRSF series provided substantial financing to Rwanda’s budget, including during the
2008 crisis. The PRSFs -totaling US$370.2 million8-were a cornerstone of the World Bank’s support
in Rwanda to help implement the EDPRS. Since Rwanda depends on highly concessional financing,
not just to cover an ambitious investment program, but also to cover parts of the recurrent budget
expenditures, budget support grants provide a preferred contribution to Rwanda’s overall financing
needs. The authorities are looking to the World Bank and other DPs as a source of continued financing
in meeting these needs. The Bank was also successful in using the PRSFs to leverage additional
concessional funding for Rwanda, including US$10 million provided through the Global Food Crisis
Response Program, US$29.8 million from the CRW, and US$24 million from the IDA-15.
105.     The second PRSF series was successful in fostering better donor coordination, improved
harmonization and reinforced support for reforms among the country’s budget support donors: the
World Bank, African Development Bank, and bilateral donors —all partners in the Budget Support
Harmonization Group and part of the semi-annual JBSR. Over the course of the series the Bank was
instrumental in supporting the establishment and implementation of the CPAF process. It should be
noted that this process is not an easy one. During the series, the broad scope of the Government’s
reform agenda, reflected in the CPAF, revealed some tensions in its design and strategic use for the
PRSF series. While the CPAF contains a number of outcome indicators, which other donors use as
their condition for budget support disbursement, the policy actions formulated in the CPAF were more
process-oriented. For Government, the goal of these policy actions was to be able to monitor if key
milestones are being achieved, thus, helping the Government to track progress over the years towards
fulfillment of key outcome indicators. The process nature of some prior actions contravened, at times,
World Bank good practice, but should also be seen in the context of strong country ownership of the
reform process.
106.     Another key lesson learned from the successful implementation of the PRSF series is the
importance of capacity building. Given the capacity constraints of a small, but reform-oriented
Government the principles of selectivity, ownership and predictability -applicable across countries—
are emphasized in Rwanda. A complex budget support framework requires capacity support for
program design, elaboration of reforms, identification and measurement of outcome indicators. Strong
capacity support has led to substantial progress in reforms for example, in PFM, the health, education
and agriculture sectors. Another example pertained to the issue of lack of capacity on the side of
Government in fiscal decentralization. That is why part of the supported reform program of the PRSF
series focused here on capacity building, including sub-national levels of Government in planning.
107.    The success of the PRSF series was also largely helped by its ability to base its design on
strong sector dialogue. This was facilitated through engaging the sector specialists early in the process
and ensuring participation in sector dialogue through the SWGs. This has helped to: (i) ensure the
PRSF reflects substantive policy agreements reached at the sector level; (ii) strengthen the capacity of
sector ministries to present their needs and priorities during national level policy discussions and
budget negotiations; and (iii) facilitate coordination of the Bank’s inputs into the CPAF policy

2. Progress by Component
This discussion is based on (i) Annex 4, which reports results on implementation of triggers and prior
actions and (ii) Annex 5, which presents the ex-post Policy matrix for PRSGs4-6 and PRSF-7. The
matrix is ex-post because it reports on the measures as implemented (in terms of wording and timing)
and thus does not capture any changes in wording or delays in implementation. The purpose is to
assess, for each component, its relevance, the adequacy of its design, the quality of implementation,
progress towards development objectives, the extent and nature of achievements, as well as the overall
outcome. This information was then used in the main ICR text to make an overall assessment of the
series, as called for in the ICR Guidelines.
2.1. Health and Education
In the health sector, the PRSG-4 Letter of Development Policy (LDP) stated that the main challenges
in the health sector were to (i) increase access to basic health care services; (ii) reduce the incidence of
communicable diseases, reduce child and maternal mortality; and (iii) increase availability of drugs,
vaccines and other consumables. Inadequate funding remained a major problem. In this context, the
PRSG-4 PD stated a central problem to be that increasing access to and availability of services, in

                                                                                    30
terms of quantity and quality, was constrained by the low level of resources on both the supply and
demand side. The key objectives to be pursued in the PRSG series were therefore: to (i) improve
access to high impact quality services by increasing availability of services at the community level and
in health centers; and (ii) increase the affordability of health services. The primary means to these
ends were to (i) increase budget transfers to service providers, through PBF, while strengthening their
delivery capacity and reinforcing accountability mechanisms; and (ii) establish of health insurance
“mutuelles?, financed in part through budget subsidies at the local level. Expected outcomes were
specified for (i) the percentage of children under five using insecticide treated mosquito nets (PRSF-7
CPAF reference 2.2.2); (ii) the percentage of assisted births in accredited facilities (PRSF-7 CPAF
reference 2.2.3); (iii) per capita allocations to performance based financing for health facilities and
community health operatives (PRSF-7 CPAF reference 2.2.1); and (iv) the percentage of the
population covered by insurance “mutuelles? (no PRSF-7 CPAF reference). These expected outcomes
were supported by policy and institutional actions pertaining to the availability of a basic health
package for integrated management of child illnesses (availability of insecticide treated bed-nets,
antibiotics, etc, in each Umudugudu, the adoption of a law and regulatory framework on mutuelles, a
study on their sustainability, the incorporation of subsidies to mutuelles into district budgets, etc.
The PRGS-5 PD indicates that it was decided to transfer budget support of health and nutrition issues
to a parallel series of three DPOs (CSLGs 1-3). The PD also reports satisfactory progress on the key
policy and institutional milestones identified above, and also a significant increase in service
utilization associated with the PBF system, based on before and after comparisons, with a randomized
impact evaluation underway. The CPAF (PRSG-7 version) indicates that the expected outcomes were
all attained or exceeded through end-June 2010, the latest year for which data are available, which
suggests highly satisfactory performance on the part of the Government. The JBSRs for 2009 and
2010 confirm this, reporting that the targets for all six indicators were attained or exceeded, and 13 out
of 14 policy actions fully achieved. The exception in this regard pertained to the law on health
insurance, whose submission to Parliament (prior action for PRSG-4) was delayed owing to changes
in Rwanda’s constitution. In summary, available evidence suggests that PRSG performance can be
ranked as highly satisfactory with regard to prior actions, triggers and outcomes.
In addition, the health component was clearly relevant, in that its objective mirrored those of the
EDPRS as expressed in the LDP. Its design appears appropriate in that attempted to tackle both
demand and supply side issues. The policy and institutional actions were effectively linked to the
desired outcomes, indicating a credible Results Framework. Overall outcome is satisfactory, given the
relevance of the program, adequacy of design and satisfactory progress toward development objectives.
With regard to education, the PRSG-4 LDP stated in essence that a major issue for Rwanda was to
increase the quality of education in general and in particular concerning PBE and TVET. The latter
needed (i) to be better linked to existing and projected labor market needs in the context of trying to
become a knowledge based lower middle economy by 2020; and (ii) to ensure that Science
Technology and Innovation become embedded in the economy through increased availability and use
in each sector on the basis of its specific needs.
In this context, PRSG-4 specified two development objectives, namely (i) to reduce the teacher pupil
ratio for the 9-year basic and secondary education programs; and (ii) to establish of a TVET policy
responsive to the needs of the private sector. Complementary objectives were the adoption of a
Teacher Development and Management Policy (TDMP) to provide the framework for accelerated
training and recruitment of teachers and ensuring that the TVET policy was accompanied by a demand
driven action plan, with particular emphasis on the participation of girls in the sciences. End of series
targets were set for (i) teacher pupil ratios and the percentage of qualified teachers at the primary level
(CPAF reference 2.6.2); (ii) the transition from basic education to upper secondary (PRGF-7 CPAF
reference 2.6.3); and (iii) the adoption of a TVET policy. Policy and institutional actions were
envisaged concerning (i) the completion of an assessment of teacher supply and demand; (ii) the
finalization and adoption of the TDMP; and (iii) the number of contract teachers being hired until the

                                                    31
adoption of the TDMP. The publication of the TVET policy was a prior action for PRSG-5. PRSG-5
emphasized the importance of using STI as a complement to the development of a skilled labor force
and specifically supported the development of the institutional structures required to implement the
Government’s STI policy and in particular strong links to the PBE strategic framework. Annual targets,
taken from the CPAF, were introduced for (i) the transition percentage from basic education to upper
secondary education; and (ii) the proportion of employers who are satisfied with the performance of
TVET graduates. PRSG-5 also introduced a trigger for PRSG-6 namely the development and costing
of the TVET, but this was dropped when PRSG-6 was processed because it had not been completed in
a timely manner. At the request of the Government PRSG-6 introduced a somewhat broader trigger for
PRSG-7, namely the revision of the long term strategic and costing of PBE programs (so including the
TVET). This became the prior action for PRSF-7 and was implemented without change.
The results with respect to the teacher pupil ratios and the percentage of qualified teachers as set forth
in PRSG-4 were not monitored in policy matrices for PRSGs 5-6 and PRSF-7, but only in the CPAFs.
According to the PRGF-7 version of the CPAF, the pupil/teacher ratio for 2008 (67:1) slightly

2009/10 with an actual of 90.2 percent as compared to a target of 82 percent. On balance, this
indicates satisfactory performance in evaluative terms with respect to outcomes. On the other hand,
data was not available for the percentage of employers satisfied with TVET graduates, which is an
unfortunate and significant shortcoming because it renders evaluation impossible.
A substantial number of policy and institutional actions were implemented during the PRSFs, to
support the realization of the reduction of teacher-pupil ratios, including (i) the completion of an
assessment of schools teachers and students in newly established “troncs commons?; (ii) assessment of
teacher supply and demand so as to inform the implementation of the TDMP policy; (iii) the
finalization of the TDMP; and (iv) the adoption of this policy by the Cabinet. The Results Framework,
as set forth in PRSG-4 appears moderately satisfactory with policy actions linked, albeit weakly, to
outcomes. The Results Framework is however much more sparse from PRSG-5 on.
With regard to TVET related objectives, the most notable achievements have been (i) the publication
of the TVET policy in 2008; (ii) the subsequent ongoing development of a five year action plan for
TVET; and (iii) the development of a properly costed strategy for PBE including a TVET component,

2.2. Infrastructure
In line with the LDP, PRSG-4 stated that lack of infrastructure services (electricity, water and roads)
were a major constraint to doing business and improving welfare in Rwanda. In all subsectors, with
the exception of telecommunications, Rwanda needed to scale up external assistance both for new
investments, as well as rehabilitation and routine maintenance of existing investments. In this context,
PRSG-4 sought to increase access while reducing costs. It spelled out three development objectives
and ten performance indicators. The development objectives were: (i) improve the cost efficiency and
operational efficiency of electricity production; (ii) put in place a framework to ensure effective
management of integrated water resources and increased access to sustainable and safe water
resources; and (iii) improve the strategic framework to support increased transport investments and
reduce maintenance costs. While PRSG-4 spelt out in some detail related policy and institutional
actions in all three areas, it only specified targets for four out of the ten indicators that were proposed,
namely (i) the existence of an improved tariff structure for electricity tariffs; (ii) the establishment of
the Kagera Basin Committee Executive Secretariats; (iii) the number of LWAs created in each district;
and (iv) the percentage of households within 500 meters of an improved water source. These
objectives were to be attained through a variety of prior actions as well as a variety of other supportive
policy actions (PRSG-4, Annex 3).
Following PRSG-5, and the decision to align with the CPAF, the indicative triggers as set forth in
PRSG-4, did not become the prior actions for PRSG-5, although they were in fact met as if they have

the publication on the MINECOFIN website of a monitoring report on the Government Bond market.
In the microfinance domain, policy and institutional actions included (i) the establishment of the

2.5. Improving Public Financial Management
Improving public financial management was one of the top priorities of the Government under PRSP-
1 and remained so under EDPRS, the principal objective being to modernize Rwanda’s public
financial management infrastructure and strengthen related capacity at central and local Government
levels. The adoption of the MTEF by all sector ministries and districts has been a major achievement,
though further progress remains necessary. Other important challenges included the integration of the
recurrent and development budgets, the strengthening of the public investment process, and the
improvement budget monitoring at both central and local Government levels –where the problems are
in the areas of budget execution, accounting and reporting, and fiscal decentralization where
decentralization has vastly increased the scope of the challenge and the capacity constraints appear
more severe.
Thus, prior to PRSG-4, a variety of measures had been taken including (i) the creation of a new
Organic Budget Law in 2006; (ii) the creation of an MTEF to enhance the alignment between budget
allocations and Government policies and strengthen the results orientation of the budget; (iii) the
integration of the recurrent and capital budgets; (iv) the initiation of an IFMIS to enhance budget and
financial monitoring and reporting at the national and sub-national levels, all the more so given the
importance attached to decentralization in general and fiscal decentralization in particular, and (v) the
production of consolidated financial statements for transmission to the Office of the Auditor General.
In this spirit, the Government had, for the first time, produced consolidated financial statements in
2006. In December 2007, Parliament also passed the accountants’ bill requiring all financial
institutions to comply with International Accounting Standards and International Financial Reporting
Standards.
In line with recent decentralization reforms the Government had also begun to put in place monitoring
and appropriate oversight arrangements to ensure local accountability – specifically the Inter-
Governmental Fiscal Department created in 2006 and located in MINECOFIN charged with leading
the oversight function with the Ministry of Local Government (MINALOC). More PFM capacity,
particularly with respect to accounting, needed to be built at the national and sub-national levels to
ensure further progress in reforms. Currently Government was able to monitor only spending financed
by central Government direct transfers to service providers, but not that financed with the districts’
own resources. But lack of robust accounting information constrained the ability of managers to
estimate the true cost of programs and related outputs, as well as the identification of bottlenecks to
better resource use and allocation.
To address these problems, the Government prepared the Multi-Sector Capacity Building Program
(MSCBP), and established the Human Resource and Institutional Capacity Development Agency to
oversee it. The Bank supported the process of reform with analytical work, technical assistance, the
SCBP and the PRSGs. Notwithstanding the progress already made with respect to PFM and the
MTEF in particular, the priority (as set forth in PRSG-4) was to address the weaknesses in monitoring,
accounting and auditing, procurement, fiscal decentralization and technical capacity, in particular at
the level of the newly constituted local authorities. The main focus of PRSG-4 was therefore to
strengthen capacity by (i) complementing the support provided by the SCBP; (ii) strengthening the
accounting function and capacity in general through the adoption of a legal framework for establishing


                                                   43
standards and professional certification in the public accounting profession; (iii) supporting the
adoption of software (BudgetMaster) for the improvement of preparation and management of the
budget at the District level; and (iv) supporting the production of consolidated financial reports by
Ministries on which the OAG relies for its own audit reports. The Prior Action for PRSG-4 was the
application of a software program (i.e. BudgetMaster) in each of the Districts by end-2007, which
would (i) enable improved reporting and monitoring of spending at the district level and (ii) also lay
the foundation for the move to an IFMIS (called SmartGov and being developed by Rwandan experts).
These measures were expected to result in the annual and routine preparation of consolidated financial
statements for submission to the Auditor General’s Office, and accordingly the trigger for PRSG -5
was to be the production by June 2008 of consolidated accounts by MINECOFIN for the budget
period ending December 31, 2007. PRSG-4 specified two indicators for monitoring outcomes: (i) the
preparation of consolidated annual financial statements which had to be submitted to the Office of the
Auditor General within 6 months of the end of the relevant fiscal year, beginning with Fiscal Year
2007; and (ii) the operationalization of an IFMIS integrating budget debt and payroll as of 2009.
During the course of PRSG-4 (calendar year 2008) the consolidated accounts for 2007 were
effectively produced by June 2008, as prescribed by law. District budget reports began to be published
on the Government’s website, as well as guidelines for the preparation of the MTEF as of June 2008.
A revised PFM strategy was published in September 2008, following the 2007 PEFA. A PFM steering
committee, chaired by the Permanent Secretary, MINECOFIN, was reconstituted to oversee PFM
reforms. And, Government and donors signed a MoU for a basket fund to support PFM reforms.
The trigger for PRSG-5, as specified in PRSG-4, that is the production of consolidated accounts by
MINECOFIN for the budget period ending December 31, 2007 by June 2008, was dropped, apparently
because not in the CPAF and also judged without value added in the policy sense because already a
legal requirement, although –as indicated above– it was met in substance. The finalized prior action
for PRSG-5 became to complete the development of the PublicBooks accounting software, and this
was apparently achieved in December 2008. The trigger for PRSG-6, as proposed in PRSG-5, became
to complete, by end 2009, the piloting of PublicBooks and its rollout to all budget agencies including
integration with the Integrated Personnel and Payroll Information System (IPPIS), which was being
developed in parallel. These revisions of the triggers appropriately strengthened the focus of the series
on the finalization of the IFMIS.
However, the strengthening of the budget monitoring and reporting system proceeded more slowly
than forecast, given that the PublicBooks module of SmartGov turned out to have a number of critical
design weaknesses, as revealed by a Quality Assurance Review undertaken in April 2009 at the
suggestion of DPs, in particular the World Bank. This led to the decision to design a new system
(SmartFMS), incorporating both budgeting and accounting modules (as well as modules on
procurement and fixed asset management). The roll out schedule called for the new system
(SmartFMS) to be piloted in key Ministries by 2010, and then rolled out to all central ministries and
integrated with the payroll system by 2011, and finally rolled out to districts by 2012. This represents
a delay of a year or more compared to the original timetable. As a result of this, the trigger for PRSG-6
as specified in PRSG-5 – the completion of PublicBooks piloting and its rollout to all budget agencies
including integration with the IPPIS payroll system – could not be met in a timeframe compatible with
the PRSG-6 processing schedule, and was replaced by a new one calling for the adoption of the
payroll subsystem of the IPPIS in central Government ministries, which was achieved in a timely
manner. The development of the IPPIS, an extremely important tool for payroll and personnel
management, also ran into problems, albeit manageable, according to a review undertaken by the
Adam Smith Institute, which found gaps with regards to the need to document system functionalities
and instructions for use of the system. In addition, the payroll system still contains large data gaps.
The trigger for PRSF-7 became to complete the piloting of the IFMIS (SmartFMS) in central
Government and integrate it with the payroll module of the IPPIS.

The new system does enable the production of consolidated financial statements both at the district
and national level. Nevertheless, Government has continued to use SagePastel, an off-the-shelf
accounting software, to produce consolidated financial statements in parallel, until it becomes clear

PRSF-7 considered that the ongoing challenges remain that of building capacity in public accountancy
and managing the transition from the current budgeting and accounting system to an IFMIS. Efforts in
this area are now being focused on developing a final blue print for IFMIS design and implementation
which focuses on whether (i) to continue with the development of SmartFMS) through enhancing its
core functionalities and designing interfaces with other systems; or (ii) whether to abandon SmartFMS

completion of the piloting of the IFMIS (SmartFMS) in central government and its integration with the

and then the four core modules were rolled out to 94 out of 236 budget agencies. Similarly, the
creation of an interface between the IFMIS and the IPPIS was also accomplished. Once the roll-out of
SmartFMS to all agencies is completed, Government’s intention to phase out Sage Pastel, the
accounting software currently is being used in the absence of PublicBooks.
The performance indicators were also revised in PRSG-5. First, the indicator pertaining to the
operation of an IFMIS integrating budget payroll and debt was modified to omit the reference to debt.
Secondly, the indicator pertaining to the preparation of consolidated financial statements and
submission to the Office of the Auditor General within six months after closing of the fiscal year
appears to have been replaced by an index on the quality and timeliness of annual financial statements
to be measured accorded to the corresponding PEFA index for which the benchmark was the 2007
score of C+, with a target of B- for the 2010 PEFA. The IFMIS was only piloted in key Central
Government ministries as against being operational in all agencies and also for that reason the PEFA
score with respect to the index on timeliness worsened to D+ from C+ in 2007. The Bank
acknowledges the difficult technical challenges around the decision processes on the design of the
supported IFMIS installation in which it was involved. It suggests the implementation of this
component to be rated moderately satisfactory, but given that the IFMIS is only one aspect of the
overall PFM agenda and in light of intermediary progress achieved in this area, to regard the outcome
as satisfactory.
The JBSRs for 2009 and 2010 both reported broadly satisfactory progress in the area of PFM, but they
play down the many difficulties which had been incurred with the development of PublicBooks.
However, they recognized that it was a wise decision not to implement PublicBooks as initially
designed. They also value the review by the Quality Assurance Group which resulted in a better
solution but also engendered a delay relative to the original timetable.
The PEFA is broadly complimentary to the progress made by Rwanda in the area of PFM, as judged
by the progress between the 2007 PEFA and the 2010. In broad terms, IMF reviews of both the PRGF
and PSI programs report significant improvements in PFM over the last few years, but nonetheless
point to the continuing needs for significant improvements, especially with respect to accounting and
reporting. This suggests that performance with respect to triggers was satisfactory, notwithstanding the
six month delay, which can be considered relatively minor, in realization of the triggers pertaining to
the roll out of the IFMIS. In fact, in hindsight, the original timing for the rollout was clearly optimistic.
On the positive side, the resulting implementation delays have been relatively minor, and the Quality
Assurance Review by international experts should in the end contribute to a better IFMIS and the
outcome can be considered satisfactory. The PRSG series has made important contributions in that
regard, in particular the finalization of the national IFMIS (SmartFMS), and the development of the
IPPIS. These were important steps in strengthening the Government’s ability to manage resources
within the public sector. The installation of the Payroll Module (subsystem) of the IPPIS in central
Government ministries, and its integration with the IFMIS, has allowed producing truly consolidated
financial statements including fully capturing wages and salaries in the system. Ensuring that wages

and salaries are captured will improve transparency and eliminate potential errors that could arise
through manual entry or manipulation of data.
Overall, it can be said that the PFM component was relevant, its design satisfactory given the technical
knowledge at the time, but somewhat overoptimistic with respect to the development of the IFMIS.
Implementation has been moderately satisfactory, because slower than forecast and because of lack of
timeliness with respect to submission of financial statements (PEFA rating). The Results Framework

years. Progress towards the development objective was rated satisfactory due to the successful
development of the national IFMIS and the development of the IPPIS. All in all, the overall outcome
is assessed as satisfactory.
2.6. Improving Procurement
When the PRSG series was initiated, a new procurement code had already been adopted (2005), and
the law creating the Rwanda Public Procurement Authority (RPPA), a very important piece of the
architecture of the system, was before Parliament. With regard to making the Procurement Code and
related institutions operational, the Government indicated that it wished to (i) phase the introduction of
the Code over three years; (ii) maintain a certain degree of centralization for three years, until the new
decentralized procurement entities had become fully operational; and (iii) transfer the workload of the
existing National Tender Board (NTB) to the decentralized procurement entities and the RPPA. The
road map to indicate the tasks and responsibilities was prepared with the assistance of the World
Bank’s Public Sector Capacity Building Project and was awaiting final adoption by the Council of
Ministers. The second priority was to adopt and implement a national capacity building strategy in
procurement, structured to ensure the long term sustainability of its training programs by entrusting
the local institutions with the tasks of providing the training, opening up the training programs to
public and private sector agents and developing the professional cadres for procurement.
In this context, support to the process under the PRSF focused on (i) adoption of procurement
regulations and standard bidding documents, (ii) the preparation of procurement plans by all
procurement entities following the adoption of the annual budget; and (iii) training and the
elaboration of needed manuals and procedures, particularly at the decentralized levels. The stated
development objective was to develop the institutional and legal framework to support capacity in
procurement. Expected outcomes were an increase in the percentage of budget agencies in full
compliance with procurement regulations so as to ensure that budget transfers and execution would be
informed by well prepared procurement plans, and public procurement would be carried out in a
transparent and competitive manner.
The prior action for PRSG-4 was the adoption of Regulations and Standard Bidding documents that
are made available on the NTB website. Triggers for PRSG-5 were (i) to make the RPPA operational,
and (ii) that all procuring entities submit a draft procurement plan to MINECOFIN following approval
of the budget. Only one monitoring indicator was proposed, namely the percentage of budget agencies
in full compliance with procurement regulations, but no targets were specified.
These objectives were not revised under PRSGs 5-6 and PRSF-7. However, the indicative trigger for
PRSG-5, as specified in PRSG-4, was not retained as the prior action for PRSG-5, because it was not
in the CPAF. In its place, the PRSG-5 prior action became the publication of the independent panel
addressing appeals by contractors of public procurement decisions, and the trigger proposed for
PRSG-6 was that all procuring agencies should submit procurement plans to MINECOFIN, and that
these procurement plans should be published on the RPPA website. The performance indicator was
also modified so as to align with the CPAF indicator, namely the percentage of procurement tendered
competitively or otherwise justified. The PRSG-4 indicator was dropped.
In the event, the PRSG-6 prior action, as finally agreed, was a slight variation on the trigger as
specified in PRSG-5, stating that at least 80 percent of agencies should have submitted procurement
plans, and RPPA should have published 60 percent of them The trigger for PRSF-7 was to amend the

                                                   46
procurement law to increase the procurement law to increase efficiency and transparency of public
procurement. This was deemed necessary, following the establishment of the RPPA in 2008, to ensure
that the law would be in line with (i) the mandate of the RPPA; (ii) Common Market of Eastern and
Southern Africa (COMESA) requirements in this area. The amendments would also clarify the scope
of the law and update definitions of certain procurement methods, etc.
Performance has been very good. Three of the four prior actions were met without modification (for
PRSGs -4, -5 and PRSF-7), having been introduced as of the operation concerned or corresponding to
the indicative trigger as formulated in the preceding operation. The PRSG-6 prior action –that at least
80 percent of procurement agencies submit procurement plans to MINECOFIN, and that RPPA
publishes at least 60 percent of them– did not correspond to the indicative trigger as formulated in
PRSG-5 which called for submission of procurement plans by all agencies and the publication of all
plans by RPPA. However only 88 percent of procuring entities, as compared with 52 percent in 2008,
had been able to meet this target, thus a shortfall of 12 percent, compared to the 100 percent target
specified in PRSG-5. The slippage can be considered little more than a minor shortcoming.
Considering that performance with respect to the other triggers was highly satisfactory (i.e. no
shortcomings), overall performance with respect to prior actions can be considered as satisfactory (i.e.
objectives realized with minor shortcomings). Performance with respect to outcome indicators was
highly satisfactory, given that 100 percent of procurement contracts were tendered competitively in
2009/2010, as against a target of 87 percent.
The JBSRs share this positive assessment. The JBSR reviews report only on performance with respect
to the one indicator that is monitored, namely the proportion of the value of procurement that is
tendered competitively or justified –which attained 100 percent in 2010. On a number of occasions
however, comments are made about the slowness of procurement procedures both on the part of
Government and donors.
More generally, Rwanda has made very good progress in establishing a modern procurement system
with an appropriate institutional and legal system. A Procurement Law was adopted in April 2007, and
in February 2008 the institutional framework for oversight of procurement was established through the
RPPA, with the aim of establishing an institutional framework for oversight of procurement that is in
line with internationally accepted standards. A new organizational chart of RPPA was approved and
put in place, as well as a draft Code of Conduct specific to procurement staff. This reorganization was
made following a change in mission and responsibility. The new chart will help to staff RPPA in
accordance with the new mandate. A user guide manual was approved by the RPPA board and
published on the RPPA website, which also contains all regulations standards and bidding documents.
In an effort to develop capacity and support for procurement practices RPPA conducted an intensive
capacity building/training program in Government’s institutions.
In order to further improve the efficiency and transparency of public procurement, the Cabinet
approved a bill to amend the Procurement Law in December 2010. The proposed changes in the law
are meant to address some of the gaps identified during the recently undertaken Country Systems
Assessment and Government’s own lessons from experience in the law implementation. These
amendments, when in effect, should reinforce progress made to improve procurement practices and
transparency. Amendment of the law was also necessary to ensure its provisions are in line with: (i)
the revised mandate of RPPA, resulting from the full devolution of the procurement transaction
function to the procuring entities; and (ii) the COMESA framework for procurement. The amendment
also clarifies the scope of the law, the definitions of certain procurement methods, participation of
state owned enterprises in bidding, language(s) used in bidding and application of administrative and
criminal sanctions. It will strengthen appeal procedures, increase publicity of contract awards, will
broaden definitions on fraud and corruption, and, in general, will ensure the consistent use of
terminology. The preparation of the bill to amend the Procurement Law followed a consultative
process, including World Bank input.

Aside from establishing the legal framework and accompanying reforms, the Government has put in
place institutions to promote increased transparency in procurement. Independent review panels have
been set up and made operational at the national and district levels, according to the provision of the
Law (five members, out of which three are from the private sector and civil society organizations).
During the first semester of 2008, twenty six (26) appeals (compared to 29 for the last semester 2007)
were filed, of which 42 percent led to a change in the contract award (compared to 50 percent).
Bidders on public procurement contracts now have access to independent recourse to appeal contract
awards. In line with this achievement, Government is now publishing the independent review of
appeals (which was previously an internal Government document). On Fraud and Corruption cases,
fourteen (14) cases were received in 2008, of which two (2) were convicted of corruption, but ten (10)
are still pending to be tried. In addition, the RPPA website already publishes a list of debarred firms.
Looking forward, the Government intends to continue this process with further support being likely in
the context of the next PRSG series. The next phase of procurement reforms will emphasize: (i)
implementation of the amended Procurement Law, (ii) capacity building and (iii) strengthening M&E,
First, the procurement regulations, manuals and bidding documents will need to be updated to make
them consistent with the revised law. Reforms in this area will also focus on strengthening internal
controls and the set up of an oversight function and to ensure enforcement of its recommendations.
Second, the Government aims to mainstream procurement training in the higher education agenda and
will create a more diversified capacity building program. The final priority is to build mechanism for
information collection, validation and performance monitoring, including studying options for a
Management Information System for procurement. This is essential since decentralization will make it
more challenging to collect information and monitor implementation performance.
The overall evaluation is that the PRSG reforms were highly relevant, program design satisfactory,
and performance highly satisfactory with respect to the implementation and outcomes of the
procurement component of the PRSGs. The Results Framework is satisfactory in that all of the policy
actions have a reasonable link to the stated program development objective. All in all there have been
considerable achievements, and the overall outcome is rated highly satisfactory.
2.7. Fiscal decentralization
Since 2000, under the PRSP and continuing under the EDPRS, Rwanda has made decentralization and
devolution of Government to the local level a major priority with the objective of effective
decentralization of public service delivery. To this end, the National Decentralization Policy was
adopted in May 2000. Territorial reforms were completed in 2005 reducing the number of provinces,
districts, sectors, and cellules, and thus the number of local administrations. In particular the number
of administrations has been reduced from 106 to 30. This set the stage for the second phase of the
reform from 2006 to 2010 which focused on deepening the decentralization process. To this end, the
Government developed the Rwanda Decentralization Strategic Framework in July 2007 that provided
a framework of the coordination of technical and financial resources in support of decentralization.
This was accompanied by a Decentralization Implementation Program , to cover the years 2008-20.
Fiscal decentralization was seen as crucial to the success of the overall decentralization process. Since
both the revenue potential and capacity for collection of revenues of local governments were limited,
transfers from the central government were critical for the local governments to carry out their
mandates. The existing system of intergovernmental transfers was based on (i) block, i.e. non-
earmarked, grants to finance administrative costs of local governments, (ii) earmarked grants for
specific services (education, health), and (iii) development grants from the Common Development
Fund to fund capital projects. In addition to these flows, a significant level of international funding
contributed to local development priorities on either an on- or off-budget basis. However, the
mechanisms for monitoring and recording of both receipts and expenditures, as well as the Results
Frameworks linking expenses to outputs, were considered to be weak and in need of substantial
improvement. Specifically, implementation constraints resulted from low capacity as well as new and

2.8. Adopt Civil Service Reforms to Build Skills, Incentives and Retention
Since the late 1990s, Rwanda had been a very active reformer of its public service, with several waves
of retrenchment, institutional restructuring, elimination of ghost workers and pay reform. Most
recently in line with its Public Service Reform Strategy, 18 adopted in 2004, Rwanda had decentralized
service provision to the district level, reducing central government staff from 2000 to 500 in 2006,
increased pay for the remaining public servants and adopted new institutional organigrams for central
government agencies. However, high staff turnover continued to be a problem and functional
reviews/audits for six ministries had revealed that (i) staffing levels and qualifications were not
commensurate with responsibilities; (ii) capacity in decentralized and delegated entities constrained
their ability to meet public service objectives; (iii) current ministerial structures were not appropriate
for meeting their missions, (iv) fundamental systems and processes for managing a decentralized
public service were inadequate or non-existent. Finally, the audits had pointed to a lack of clarity of
roles between agencies and their parent ministries which hampered the capacity of the latter to manage
agencies and evaluate their performance. A second generation of reforms was therefore required that
focused on (i) building skills in the public sector; (ii) attracting and retaining qualified individuals; (iii)
improving organizational effectiveness; and (iv) improving capacity for core functions such as M&E,
accounting, auditing and procurement; (v) modernizing Human Resource Management (HRM)
including through the establishment of an integrated personnel and payroll management system; and
(vi) establishing a public service commission to implement public service personnel policies.
In this context, the PRSF series, as set forth in PRSG-4, sought to support reforms clustered around
four themes: (i) a strong emphasis on in-career training and capacity building, (ii) improving
organizational effectiveness of central ministries and agencies including use of ICT for greater
efficiency; (iii) putting in place an IPPIS, linked to the planned IFMIS, to help realize efficiency gains
and transparency in line with a lean but effective public sector; and (iv) effectively developing and
implementing a policy for staff retention.
In this context, the development objective of PRSG-4, or rather the expected outcome was specified as
institutional capacity developed to support improved staff incentives and retention. PRSG-4 had as a
prior action that Ministry of Public Service (MIFOTRA) should have completed functional reviews in
6 key Ministries (MINECOFIN, MIFOTRA, Ministry of Education (MINEDUC), MININFRA,
Ministry of Land (MINITERE), and Ministry of Health -MINISANTE). No triggers were specified for
PRSG-5, but a whole series of highly appropriate policy actions were planned (see PRSG-4 policy
matrix for details). Two indicators were proposed: (i) the percentage of vacant or externally or
temporarily staffed positions; and (ii) the staff turnover in Ministries, Departments and Agencies
reduced. No quantitative targets were specified.
The development objective was not revised as of PRSG-5 but the performance indicators identified
under PRSG-4 were replaced, because not in the CPAF, to become the percentage of budget agencies
that achieve at least 80 percent of their target outputs in their annual work program. A trigger was

This ICR serves to express the perceptions on the implementation of World Bank/GoR PRSG/F series
implementation. We note that Rwanda’s economy has recorded strong growth in last 7 years despite
the economic activity slow down in 2009. Statistics show that GDP grew over 6% during the period
and the support from our Development Partners has been cardinal particularly the PRSG resources
from World Bank.

However, the impact of the global financial crisis and tight liquidity conditions was felt in Rwanda
especially in the second half of 2009, as contributions from the industrial and service sectors – worst
hit by the crisis – declined significantly.

The monetary policy measures helped to improve the liquidity conditions. The sharp reduction in
deposits following the liquidity tightening pushed commercial banks to increase deposit rates
significantly, restoring positive real interest rates so as to attract deposits. As a result, the average
commercial bank deposit rate rose from 5.97 percent at the beginning of 2008 to 8.54 percent by the
end of 2009.

The first series of Poverty Reduction Support Operations (PRSOs) consisted of a Credit (PRSC-1,
2004) and two grants (PRSG-2 and -3, covering 2005 and 2006 respectively) supported Rwanda’s first
PRSP (PRSP-1) in three priority areas: (i) private sector development; (ii) improvement in access to
and quality of socio-economic services, mainly in education, health, water and energy; and (iii)
improvement of economic and financial governance.

Thus, PRSG-4 (US$70 million, about RwF42 billion) emphasized that the share of priority spending
would increase.

PRSG-5 (US$80 million or RwF48 billion) focused on reforms to ease human capital and
infrastructure constraints to growth, foster fiscal decentralization and strengthen the results orientation
of the MTEF and the budget process. Continuing capacity support to enable implementation of
reforms in public financial management and procurement was also envisioned, as well as increased
spending efficiency in order to lay the foundation for increased absorption and effectiveness of higher
aid inflows.

PRSG-6, which financed the budget year 2009-2010, had been initially programmed for a total of
US$86 million (about RwF51.6 billion). However, the international financial crisis had caused a sharp
reduction in exports and fiscal revenues, and this had caused the Government to introduce a 2009/2010
budget with a reduction of 0.4 percent in the expenditure/GDP ratio. In this context, to help protect
priority expenditure, it was decided to increase PRSG-6 to US$115.8 million (RwF69.5 billion)
through the Crisis Response Window (CRW), an increase much appreciated by the Government. ,

PRSF-7 provided total support of US$104 million (about RwF60 billion), of which US$70 million in
the form of a grant and the remainder as an IDA Credit). In 2010/2011, expenditures continued to
increase by about 1.6 percent of GDP, but mainly in non-priority expenditure. However,
notwithstanding significantly higher revenues (1.1 percent of GDP), the lower level of foreign grants
in nominal terms and relative to GDP (10.2 percent compared to 13.1 percent in 2009/2010),
additional foreign financing was expected to be needed (project loans amounting 1.8 percent of GDP
compared to 1.0 percent in 2009/20100). Also domestic financing from banking system was expected
to amount 1.9 percent of GDP as against 0.3 percent in 2009/2010.

assessment indicates significant increases in PFM scores in many other areas, reflecting in part
progress from a low base since the 2007 PEFA assessment.

Progress has also been notable in relation to coordinating with Donors and Rwanda stands out in
enhancing Aid effectiveness principles particularly facilitating policy dialogue and regular
assessments on the performance of Government as well as Donors in the commonly agreed
frameworks of CPAF and DPAF arrangements.
