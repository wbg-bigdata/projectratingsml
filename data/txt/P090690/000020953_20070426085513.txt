Borrower:           Government o f Pakistan

ImplementingAgency: MinistryofFinance, Governmento fPakistan

Amount:              SDR 233.2 million (US$ 350 million equivalent)

Terms:              The credit i s being provided in two portions: one portion inthe amount o f
                     SDR 108 million (US$162.1 million equivalent) inhard IDA terms (subject
                    to 4% interest and 0.75% service charge); and a second in the amount o f
                     SDR125.2 million (US$187.9 million equivalent) in regular IDA terms
                     (subject to 0.75% service charge)

Tranching:          The proposed IDA credit, the second in a series o f three operations, in an
                     amount equivalent to US$350 million, will be disbursed in one tranche
                    upon effectiveness.

Description:        Palustan's PRSCs are one-tranche programmatic operations to support the
                     Government o f Pakistan's medium-tern reform program, laid out in the
                     Government's      2003  Poverty    Reduction   Strategy   Paper,  entitled
                     "Accelerating Economic Growth and Reducing Poverty: TheRoad Ahead.''
                     This strategy emphasizes sustained, rapid growth as the main vehicle for
                    povertyreduction and focuses on:
                     (i) acceleratingeconomic growthwhilemaintainingmacroeconomic
                             stability;
                   (ii) improvinggovernanceanddevolution;
                   (iii) investinginhumancapital;and
                    (iv)     targeting the poor.

                    The PRSC IIsupports reforms by the Government o f Palustan that will
                     contribute to economic growth by: maintaining macroeconomic stability;
                    improving the management and effectiveness o f public expenditures;
                     assisting power sector reforms; supporting the privatization program and
                    improving the regulatory framework for competition; and enhancing female
                    labor force participation and labor market flexibility. The PRSC 11will
                     also support     improved governance through reforns in financial
                    management, procurement, and the statistical system.          It will help
                     accelerate progress in human development by creating additional public
                    resources for education and health, strengthening the health and education
                    national sector policies; and piloting innovative programs, focusing on the
                    poor, to enhance demand for these services. The PRSC IIalso supports the
                    poorest and most vulnerable segments o f the population through targeted
                    programs aimed at addressingpoverty and vulnerability directly.

                                         I.INTRODUCTION

1.       The last six years have witnessed a resurgence of Pakistan's economy. After a decade o f
political instability, macroeconomic crisis, and limited economic and social progress, Palustan has
emerged as one o f the fastest-growing economies in Asia, with rising per capita income, and improved
social indicators. Growth has averaged 7% between 2003/04-2005/06, the poverty head count ratio,
which was on the rise throughout the 1990s, has fallen significantly between 2001/02 and 2004105; many
o f the human development indicators have improved markedly. Economic reforms, along with political
stability, are now contributing to increased investor interest from Palustanis and foreign investors alike.
Remittances from Pakistanis abroad have been strong, and after 9/11/2001 have been brought into
official channels. External support has also allowed for a fiscal adjustment, and increased poverty-
related expenditures, most notably ineducation. While there are important challenges to macroeconomic
management emerging in widening current account deficits and a deceleration in export growth, the
Government o f Palustan has been taking measures to address these concerns.

2.       These developments constitute a marked turnaround compared to the decade of the 1990s.
By the end of the 1990s macroeconomic imbalances and debt had increased to unsustainable levels, the
economy was barely growing, and poverty was on the rise.            Many o f Pakistan's fiscal difficulties
originated in the 1980s, during which fiscal deficits - partly financed by grants from abroad - soared. It
proved difficult to reduce these deficits in the 1990s, which remained high- on average 6.6% o f GDP
from 1993 to 1999 - and led to a growing public debt burden, with interest payments consuming more
than half o f total revenue. Growth slowed down to 4% a year on average, translating into only a 1.4%
annual increase in per capita income. The slow growth o f the 1 9 9 0 ~ ~
                                                                        compounded by drought and poor
agricultural performance inthe second half o f the decade, was further exacerbatedby rising international
interest rates and restriction inconcessional aid following nuclear tests in 1998. By 2001, over one third
o f Pakistani households lived below the poverty line. Improvements in health and education indicators
over the 1990s were limited. In fact, even when growth and poverty reduction occurred, there was
limited improvement inhuman development.

3.       The good outcomes of the past six years have been due to a combination of domestic and
external factors. On the domestic front, the Government that took power in 1999 launched an ambitious
program o f economic reforms to reduce the fiscal and external deficits, and attract private sector
investment by reducing the cost o f doing business in Pakistan. This program o f economic reforms and
institutional strengthening was articulated in the 2003 Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP) entitled
"Accelerating Economic Growth and ReducingPoverty: The RoadAhead." The main focus o f the PRSP
i s on second-generation reforms to accelerate growth to over 6%, while maintaining macroeconomic
stability.   Other pillars o f the PRSP include strengthening governance and furthering devolution,
accelerating investments in human capital, and targeting the poor and vulnerable. The PRSP targets are
inline with the progress necessary to meet the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), and the PRSP
projects significantly higher expenditure allocations for education, health, and poverty-related
infrastructure. The Bank-Fund Joint Staff Assessment, discussed by the Board o f the IMF on March 8,
2004 and the Board o f the Bank on March 11, 2004, endorsed the PRSP and indicated that it provides a
good policy framework for the implementation o f a strong reform program, which can translate into the
Government's ambitious development goals and thus accelerate progress towards the MDGs for Palustan.
A subsequent government document, the Planning Commission's 2005 Medium Term Development

Frameworkfor 2005-10, further articulated and expanded the federal government's investment program.
The Government is also currently inthe process o f finalizing a second PRSP.

4.       On the external front, the Government's reform program has been supported by
restructuring of internationaldebt and increasedconcessionaryfinancing. Inparticular, the World
Bank has supported the Government o f Pakistan's structural reforms with two national Structural
Adjustment Credits and a Poverty Reduction Support Credit (PRSC). The Poverty Reduction Support
Credit supported the implementation o f cross-cutting structural and institutional reforms in the
Government's strategy. The first PRSC, in an amount o f US$ 300 million, was approved by the Board
and disbursed in September 2004.            The Implementation Completion Report rated outcomes as
satisfactory. Following the earthquake o f October 8, 2005, the Bank further supported the PRSC reform
program with Supplemental Financing o f US$150 million, which was prepared, approved and disbursed
inNovember 2005, shortly after the earthquake.

5.       The remainder o f this Program Document i s organized as follows: Section 11describes recent
economic and political developments in Palustan; Section IIIsummarizes the Government's reform
program stated in the PRSP; Section IV discusses the World Bank's country assistance strategy; Section
V outlines the progress inthe Government's reforms since PRSC I,       including outcomes o f PRSC Iprior
actions; Section VI describes the proposed operation, and lists prior actions for PRSC 11and indicative
triggers for PRSC III;  and Section VII discusses program implementation arrangements and risks.


                                       11.COUNTRY CONTEXT

A. RecentEconomicDevelopments
6.       Economic growth has been strong and broad based over the past three years. Economic
growth has accelerated in the past three years, with real GDP increasing by 7.5% in 2003/04, 8.6% in
2004/05, and 6.6% in 2005/06, surpassing the targets set out inthe PRSP. This growth has been driven
by solid industrial and services sector performance. Led by the private sector, investment rates have
increasedby more than 3 percentage points o f GDP to 20% inFY 2005/06. The economy has also proved
resilient. It absorbed two significant exogenous shocks in 2005/06, the October 2005 earthquake and the
considerable increase inthe average price o f international crude oil. The 2006/07 GDP growth target of
7% i s reasonable in view o f the steady expansion in agriculture, manufacturing, and banking and
telecommunication services.

7.       The widening current account imbalance however, presents a challenge that needs to be
addressedto sustain growth. Current account deficits have markedly increased from 1.6% o f GDP in
2004/05 to 4.4% in2005/06, driven primarily byhighdomestic demand. While these larger deficits have
been financed by non-debt creating financial flows, specifically foreign direct and portfolio investments
and concessionary flows, the Government has recognized the need for corrective actions to reduce
external deficits.   Monetary policies have been tightened to dampen domestic demand. Increases in
treasury bill rates, discount rates, and liquidityreserve requirements for Banks have led to an appreciable
decline incore inflation rates and a sharp deceleration o f import growth duringthe current fiscal year. On
the other hand, the accompanying deceleration in export growth rates reveals the economy's structural
constraints and vulnerabilities that will need to be addressed through reforms supported by the PRSC
program.

8.       The growth over the past three years has contributed to rising social indicators and a
marked decline in poverty. The 2004/05 Pakistan Social and Living Standards Measurement Survey

(PSLMS) shows a nationwide improvement in key indicators o f human development.                            Significantly,
country-wide primary school enrollment rates have picked up and the gender gap ineducation has fallen,
particularly in Punjab and NWFP. Overall poverty declined sharply between 2001/02 and 2004/05 and
while consumption inequality did increase, there was a considerable reduction inrural poverty.'

9.       Domestic fiscal imbalances have been brought to sustainable levels. The consolidated deficit
o f the Government has been maintained at sustainable levels for the past five years, averaging
approximately 3.6% o f GDP (Table 1). The policy o f using privatization proceeds primarily for debt
retirement, runningprimary surpluses for a number o f years, and real growth rates exceeding real interest
rates have all led to a significant reduction in public debt from over 80% of GDP in 2001/02 to 56% in
2005706.     Having achieved debt sustainability the Government has adopted a more liberal expenditure
stance through a considerable increase indevelopment and pro-poor expenditures over the last two years.
Last year's fiscal outcome exceeded the budget target o f 3.8% primarily on account o f earthquake relief
and reconstruction expenditure; the deficit was 3.4% net o f the impact o f the earthquake.                         The
Government was therefore able to absorb the impact o f the earthquake, which was commendable. The
budget deficit in 2006/07 i s expected to increase to 4.2% (3.6% net o f earthquake related spending) on
account o f higher development expenditures. Deficits are projected to decline in the Government's
medium term plan, consistent with the Government's debt reduction strategy. While the increased losses
o f the Water and Power Development Authority (WAPDA), due to higher fuel prices and stagnant
electricity tariffs, are likely to increase the quasi-fiscal deficit, the Government announced a tariff
increase inFebruary 2007, which will reduce power sector losses, and has taken some significant steps to
improve the management o f the sector.

11.      After declining for several years, tax revenue collections have started to improve, helped by
strong growth in direct taxes. Pakistan's tax revenues remain l o w by international standards and,
despite yearly nominal growth, declined from 10.9% o f GDP in 2001102 to 10.0% in 2004/05. This
decline i s in part transitory due to the structural change from reliance on foreign trade taxes to value
added taxes, and rationalization o f income taxes. The Government recognizes that improving the tax-to-
GDP ratio is central to ensuring macroeconomic stability and to sustaining long term growth, and has
given high priority to the administrative reforms o f the Central Board o f Revenue (CBR). These
measures are starting to y e l d results. For 2005/06, CBR exceeded its collection target o f Rs. 690 billion
with a collection o fRs. 712.5 billion, an increase o f 20.7% over 2004/05. For 2006/07 the tax collection
target i s Rs. 835 billion. As o f December 2006, the total amount o f tax collectedwas Rs.410 billion, an
increase o f 27% over the corresponding period last year. The main contributor to this change are direct
taxes, which increased by 66% over this period. This growth i s a reflection o f the profitability o f the
corporate sector, inparticular banlung, energy and telecommunications, as well as the tax administration
reforms within CBR that have resulted in greater tax effort and encouraged compliance.                   The
performance o f the Large Taxpayer Units (LTUs) has been particularly impressive, with LTU tax
collections growing at a considerably higher rate than overall CBR taxes.              The Government's
macroeconomic framework targets annual tax revenue increases o f 0.3-0.4% o f GDP over the next 4
years, to be brought about by bothtax administration and policy reforms.

12.       Expenditure allocation has also improved.         Development expenditures were significantly
higher, both at the federal and sub-national levels, increasing by 71% from 2004/05 to 2005/06. The
Amendment to the National Finance Commission (NFC) 1997 award issued by the President o f Pakistan
in January, 2006 (Box 1) also implies greater resources for poverty reducing expenditures.               The
Amendment, while maintaining population as the sole criterion for division o f shared taxes among the
provinces, gives the provinces a larger, and increasing, proportion o f these taxes, and makes every
province also eligible for subvention grants. As a result o f these measures, this amendment should
alleviate some o f the fiscal pressures faced by provincial and local governments.

13.       In response to increasing domestic demand and inflation rates, the State Bank has taken
several necessary steps to tighten monetary policy. These include increases inthe Treasury Billrates
(from 1.7% in 2003/04 to over 8% in the current year); raising reserve and liquidity requirements (by
more than 2 percentage points inthe current fiscal year); and an increase inthe discount rate (by 50 basis
points to 9.5%).     As a result broad money supply and especially private sector credit has declined
sharply. While these measures have markedly reduced demand pressures as evident inthe decline incore
(non-food and non-fuel) inflation, increase in food prices have kept overall inflation rates high. The
Consumer Price Index, which increased from 4.6% in 2003104 to 7.9% in 2005106, is projected to be
contained at 7.5% inthe current fiscal year.

uncertaintyaround the size and timing o f capital inflows to finance the current account deficit. Therefore
the Government should be prepared to take all necessary measures to keep the real effective exchange

issues that constrain the growth o f labor productivity and export competitiveness, including regulations
that raise the costs o f doing business by creating rigidities in labor markets and weak contract
enforcement; weak infrastructure, especially unreliable and costly power supply, a poor road network,
and an inadequate transport system.

17.      The PRSC program supports the Government's broad based reforms to strengthen the

 A. The Strategy
 Pillar One: AcceleratingEconomic GrowthWhile MaintainingMacroeconomic Stability

 22.           The PRSP sets the broad policy framework for achieving sustainedhighand broad-based private
 sector-driven growth, focusing particularly on the rural economy, while maintaining macroeconomic
 stability. The PRSP identifies and outlines a good framework for reducing threats to macroeconomic
 stability, namely: (i)           reducingthe overall fiscal deficit; (ii)   improving revenue mobilization; (iii)        reducing
 explicit or implicit liabilities o f the budget; (iv) improving the performance o f key public enterprises, in
 particular the power sector; and (v) reducingthe stock and improving the terms o f public debt. The PRSP
 projects a substantial increase in private sector investment and outlines a set o f structural reforms to
 increase productivity and reduce the cost o f doing business in Pakistan. It also envisages a significant
 increase in public development spending in critical infrastructure, expected to help stimulate private
 investment. Second-generation measures in the PRSP include financial sector reforms; labor reforms to
 increase flexibility in the labor market and to create jobs; deregulation to reduce red tape;
 telecommunications reforms to increase teledensity; privatization to improve efficiency infrastructure



                                                                     9

reforms and investments to address shortages in the transport, irrigation, and energy sectors; and a rural
development strategy focused on water sector investments and reforms to accelerate growth in these
areas.

Pillar Two: ImprovingGovernanceand Devolution

23.      The PRSP recognizes that continuing to improve governance in Pakistan matters critically for
economic growth and the development o f human capital. K e y elements o f the Government's strategy
include accelerating civil service reform, furthering devolution, improving access to justice, police
reforms, accelerating improvements in public procurement and financial management, and additional
measures to accelerate progress on fiscal and financial transparency and reduce opportunities for
corruption.

PillarThree: InvestinginHumanCapital

24.      The PRSP acknowledges that without a considerable improvement in social indicators,
particularly for women, Pakistan will be unable to sustain high rates o f economic growth and poverty
reduction. The PRSP emphasizes the need for investing inhuman capital, through increased expenditures
in education, health, water and sanitation, and rural infrastructure, and more effective delivery o f basic
social services. The Government's national education policy underscores increased enrolments in public
sector schools, eliminating urban-rural and gender imbalances, and improving quality o f education at all
levels. The primary components o f the strategy are: (i)    removing gender and access gaps, (ii) improving
access and equity for quality universal primary education, (iii)    increasing literacy through adult literacy
programs, (iv) supporting technical education in secondary schools, (v) improving quality o f education,
(vi) establishing public-private partnerships to increase access, and (vii) mainstreaming Madrassahs. The
key goals include universal primary education and quality education for all, attaining 86% literacy rate
for both genders, and establishing quality technical and school development programs.

25.      Inhealth, the PRSPrecognizes the needto increasefinancing and enhance efficiency o f spending
through organizational and management reforms. Furthermore, the PRSP focuses on reorganization o f
district health structures, strengthening o f district and tehsil hospitals, improving hospital administration
through greater autonomy, and regulation o f the private sector. On the programmatic front, the strategy
prioritizes control o f communicable diseases, reproductive health, child health and nutrition. It goes
beyond the National Health Policy in the important areas o f reproductive health and nutrition through
development o f community midwifery, improvement o f emergency obstetric care, behavior change and
communication and various nutrition related initiatives.

Pillar Four:Targetingthe Poor andthe Vulnerable

26.      Targeting the poor and vulnerable, to bring the marginalized sections o f the population and
backward regions into the mainstream o f development, and to make markedprogress inreducing existing
inequalities i s an integral pillar o f the PRSP. The strategy explicitly emphasizes the importance o f
strengthening the functioning o f safety nets in Palustan. Existing safety net programs include poverty-
targeted cash transfers, old-age income security, employment-based programs, micro credit and NGO
programs, food-based programs, and untargeted wheat subsidy. The PRSP encompasses the expansion o f
microfinance programs, strengthening public works programs, improving transfer programs and food
support, and reforming pensionsystems for the formal private sector.

B. PRSPI1Preparation
27.     The Government i s currently in the process o f finalizing a second PRSP, and plans to present a

has been discussed with provincial and local governments. The preparation o f the second PRSP has

C. Monitoringof PRSPImplementationand Outcomes
29.      Progress toward PRSP objectives i s being supported by improvements in government systems to
collect, analyze and disseminate data. The PRSP Secretariat inthe Ministryo f Finance is mandated with
coordinating, traclung, monitoring, and evaluating the implementation o f the PRSP. The Secretariat
reports progress on PRSP public expenditures, intermediate social indicators, and final outcomes. The
Secretariat's functions are to collate information from various sources, identify information and data
gaps, and produce progress reports, based on a set of indicators agreed upon by federal and provincial
governments as well as civil society and donors.

30.      Since the PRSP's completion inDecember 2003, the Government has regularly prepared Annual
and Quarterly PRSP Progress reports, which are available on the web. Progress reports track and analyze
pro-poor public expenditures and their sectoral composition and provincial patterns within the context o f
the macroeconomic situation in the country. In addition, the reports also monitor PRSP intermediate
indicators, PRSP and social sector performance, employment, non-budgetary expenditures, and special
programs for poverty reduction. The data available for monitoring PRSP outcomes i s summarized in
Annex E.

C. Lessons LearntfromPreviousOperations
40.      This PRSC builds on the successful implementation of the Government's reform program,
supported under previous operations. The first PRSC was approved in September 2004, and the ICR
judged implementation as satisfactory. Supplemental financing under the PRSC program was approved
in November 2005, to support continuation of reforms while addressing the emergency created by the
October 2005 earthquake.

41.      Prior to the PRSC, the Bank has supported the Government of Pakistan's medium-termreform
program since 1999 with two program support operations, Structural Adjustment Credit I(US350
million equivalent) and Structural Adjustment Credit II(US500 million equivalent), approved in May
2001 and May 2002, respectively.        These operations were the Bank's main vehicles to support
implementationof the I-PRSP.

42.      The SACS and the PRSC effectively supported the achievement o f a number o f important
objectives, and helped lay the foundations for Palustan's achievement of the MDGs. The focus o f these
operations was on policies to engender growth; improve governance; and improve delivery of social
services.   They included measures to accelerate growth, reduce indebtedness and accelerate fiscal
consolidation, prioritized the social sectors and critical infrastructure in public expenditure policy,
improved tax administration, devolved powers to newly established local governments, improved
financial management, and civil service reforms. Growth has now accelerated and i s broad based,
poverty has been reduced, social indicatorshave started to improve.

43.      Notwithstanding these achievements, there were areas where the pace of reform implementation
fell short o f expectations. These includedpower sector reforms, civil service reforms, and improvements

D.Analytical Underpinningsofthe PRSCprogram
46.     The World Bank has continued to support the Government o f Pakistan's reform program with
analytical and advisory work. This work has focused on policies to stabilize the economy, reduce debt,
sustain rapid economic growth, enhance the poverty-reducing impact o f growth, and improve the
transparency and efficiency o f public expenditures. Between 1999 and 2006 the Bank has produced 21
pieces o f formal analytical work, increasingly in collaboration with other development partners that
include the Asian DevelopmentBank, the UK's DFID, the US, and Japan

47.     Knowledge partnership i s a core element o f the Bank`s assistance to Pakistan. The thrust o f the
analytical work on Pakistan i s that the country's economic performance remainedwell below its potential
in the 1990s, and that the recent economic revival could be pushed to a higher growth trajectory. As
Palustan endeavors to further reinforce and deepen its reform and growth agenda, there is recognition
that the country needs to continue maintaining macroeconomic stability while creating an investor-
friendly environment that can enhance export competitiveness and diversification and generate jobs.
Accelerating growth to over 7% per annum, a PRSP target, will depend inlarge part on increasing private
and public investment, and in raising productivity. To this end, stronger efforts to expand the capacity
and quality o f human capital, physical infrastructure, and social services are needed. Palustan also needs
to address the structural bottlenecks that persistently lead government revenues to under-perform, thereby
constraining the level and efficacy o f its public spending. The Bank's most recent analytic works have
been examining these issues in-depth to identify possible reform entry points and make policy
recommendations.

48.     The Pakistan Growth and Export Competitiveness report (2005106) underscored that
macroeconomic stability i s and must remain the strategic foundation for enhanced growth and
competitive prospects. The report highlights the importance o f raising savings rate and fostering higher
investment activity, and stresses that overvaluation o f exchange rate should be avoidednot to undermine
export competitiveness and sustainability o f external balances. The analysis also points out that the
productivity and efficiency o f the private sector has been constrained by lags in the areas o f education,
public infrastructure, and financial depth, and that improving the quality o f the investment environment
inthis subset o f growth engines could lead to an average per capita GDP growth rate o f above 5%. To
overcome the weaknesses inthe investment environment, the report identifies remedial measures that aim
at (i)reducing the cost o f doing business and increasing market competition; (ii)      stepping up factor
productivity through efficiency gains; and (iii)through lowering costs throughout supply chains,
strengthening export competitiveness to diversify and widen Palustan's export base.

49.     The main constraints to a conducive investment climate in Pakistan, consisting o f inadequate
access to finance, burdensome tax and customs administration, insufficient power supply, and corruption,
were identified through an Investment Climate Assessment (2002103), allowing government to take
corrective actions that have led to strong supply responses to investment climate improvements. The


                                                      16

Government lowered barriers to entry in trade and business start-up, privatized significant government
assets in banking, and began improving tax and labor regulations, enabling the economy to respond
positively with rational and predictable market signals and a growing confidence in the business
environment. An Investment Climate Assessment 11,currently ongoing, plans to analyze the impact of
improvements in the investment climate at the firm level in order to better link policy changes to firm
performance. The analysis will be aligned with the Government's private sector strategy and will cover
areas which have been the subject o freform efforts and areas representing future challenges, such as land
and labor markets, and those considered long term efforts such as the provision o f infrastructureservices.

50.      One such constraint has been the state o f Palustan's existing labor regulation, which the Pakistan
Labor Market Study (2006107), currently being finalized, found rigid and not conducive to formal market
development o f the type needed to reduce informality, close the slulls gap, and improve labor
productivity. The report recommends changes in existing labor laws and implementation arrangements,
both at the federal and provincial levels, as entry points for reforms that can lead to higher job creation
rates and greater supply o f and demand for skills. The reforms proposed in such areas as labor regulation,
enforcement and regulatory institutions, social security, and vocational and technical training, are
calibrated against existing government provisions and reforms and lessons learned from good global
practices. For instance, inregard to reform o f labor inspectionregimes at the provincial level to increase
the effectiveness and reduce the cost o f compliance, the report recommends (i)         adoption o f national
guidelines for provincial inspections; (ii) new business process systems to ensure efficient and equitable
implementation; (iii)  a training program for inspectors in the new aspects o f the law, social shlls, and
dispute resolution; and (iv) a centrally or provincially managed third party monitoring and evaluation
systemto ensure that inspections are talung place inthe prescribed manner.

51.      Significant improvements in both the level and quality o f public expenditures are essential to
maximize the development impact o f both public and private investments, especially for overcoming
gaps in the delivery o f public goods, including infrastructure. Improving the efficacy o f public
expenditures requires improvements in budgetary and planning processes, expenditure and financial
management practices, procurement processes and systems, as well as broader policy and institutional
reforms for improved delivery o f services. The ongoing Public Expenditure, Procurement, and Financial
Management Review aims to illuminate the role o f public spending in Palustan's recent development
success and will explore ways to (i)    ensure fiscal sustainability and enlarge fiscal space; (ii)reorient
public expenditure at the sectoral level to enhance allocative efficiency; (iii)     bring about policy and
institutional reforms to increase operational efficiency at the project level; and (iv) improve the usage o f
existing public assets and raise citizens' demand for quality public services.

52.      The primary responsibility for the provision o f critical social services including health,
education, and water supply and sanitation rests with the sub-national governments. With increased
fiscal space, more funds are at the disposal o f sub-national governments to be budgeted and spent
according to local priorities. Comprehensive financial management reforms have been launched, both at
the federal and provincial levels, and the past three years have witnessed significant improvements in
financial management practices in the areas o f accounting, internal controls, and reporting, external
audit,   and legislative oversight. Provincial Public Financial Management and Accountability
Assessments (PFMA) undertaken in 2003104 and 2004105 for NWFP, Punjab, and Sindh were able to
identify weaknesses in provincial financial management systems and accountability arrangements and
recommend actions which have helped further enhance and deepen financial management reforms.
Common weaknesses identified included rigidities in expenditure allocation that are undermining the
development o f performance budgeting, and inadequate internal and external controls that are
insufficient to meet the growing demands o f a tight fiscal environment. A proposed PFMA for
Balochistan, which lags all other provinces in financial management reforms, also plans to provide a

fiduciary risk analysis and an overall framework for dealing with financial management risks through
reform o f public financial management systems and processes. It plans to articulate short, medium, and
long-term actions plans that the government o f Balochistan can develop for strengthening financial
accountability and for undertakmgcapacity buildingand policy reforms.

53.     Pakistan's prospects for accelerating growth through increased public spending hinges on
improvements inrevenue collection. Tax revenue remains l o w against spending needs. Pakistan has over
the last few years embarked on a reform o f its tax system that have included, among other things,
rationalization o f income tax rates and restructuring and modernization o f the federal tax administration
and customs operations. The ongoing Tax Policy Program, which consists o fpolicy papers and technical
assistance to CBR, aims to take a comprehensive look at Pakistan's tax policy reform options for
broadening of the tax base in an efficient and equitable manner. The program intends to provide a
comprehensive assessment o f Pakistan's tax system, share lessons from countries involved in successful
tax policy reforms, lay out options for tax reform in Pakistan, and build government capacity for fiscal
policy analysis.

54.     Strong and sustained economic growth has translated into higher household incomes and lower
poverty. Social protection instruments can complement growth by addressing poverty and inequality and
helping to improve management o f household risks, and the Social Protection in Pakistan report,
currently being finalized, finds that Pakistan's social protection system needs strengthening to adequately
address chronic poverty and vulnerability. The report finds that social protection programs have large
gaps incoverage, are duplicative, lack adequate incentives for exiting poverty, are not flexible enough for
coping with shocks, and need stronger administration and regulation. The report suggests a two pronged
approach for strengthening social protection: development ofproductive safety nets to help individuals
cope with poverty, and incorporating sustainable exit strategies and flexibility to cope with risks; and
strengthening the formal social security system by improving fiscal sustainability, administrative
capacity, and financial management while piloting innovative approaches to extend insurance coverage to
the informal sector.

55.     A significant development challenge i s a persistent gender inequality. Although there has been
progress ina few areas, large gender disparities continue to exist inaccess to human capital development
and participation and remuneration in the labor force, business ownership, land tenure, property
ownership, and family law and inheritance practices. The recently completed Gender Assessment (2006)
identifies two dimensions in which policy must address gender gaps in order to meet Pakistan's
development goals. First, because social and economic constraints limit women's access to education,
health services, and prospects for earning income, policy requires near-term initiatives that work around
these constraints,    increasing female acquisition o f basic services and opportunities. Second,
improvements in gender equality will endure only to the degree that formal institutions reinforce them
and society accepts them. Hence, policy must incorporate long-term measures to create an environment
that enables the reduction o f gender gaps.

57.     The first PRSC was closely aligned with the four reform pillars outlined in the PRSP. As
Palustan's poor growth performance was at the root o f the increase in poverty incidence over the 1990s,
the main thrust of the program was on a set o f policies to sustain rapid growth. Within this broad PRSP

A. MacroeconomicStability and FiscalPolicy
60.     Economic growth has been impressive over the past three years, and i s expected to continue to be
strong, with most sectors o f the economy performing well. Fiscal targets have been broadly met. But
rising demand and inflationary pressures have led to increasing current account deficit (see Table 1)
since 2005/06. As noted earlier, the Government has tightened monetary policy to reduce inflation rates.
As discussed in detail in the next section, the Government i s engaged in significant tax administration
reforms to remove inefficiencies and reduce opportunities for corruption, and i s taking measures to
reformbudget formulation and execution processes to improve the effectiveness o fpublic expenditures.

61.     The Government passed the Fiscal Policy and Debt Limitation Law that will lock in the gains
made in fiscal and debt management over the past four years, while increasing social sector expenditures.
The Law specifies that the revenue deficit be reduced to zero by June 30,2008, and a surplus maintained
thereafter; that total public debt be reduced to 60% o f GDP or lower by June 2013; that PRSP
expenditures be at least 4.5% o f GDP in any year; and that debt guarantees be limited to 2% o f GDP.
The approval o f the Bill by the Cabinet was a PRSC Iprior action, and the Bill was passed into Law,
after approval o f the National Assembly, Senate and the President, in June 2005. An additional
important clause introduced in the Law by the Assembly i s the requirement that education and health
expenditures, as a percentage o f GDP, be doubled over the next ten years. The Law also requires annual
reporting (by the end of January each year) to the Assembly on status o f key fiscal and debt targets, and
ofpolicies that are likely to impact these indicators.

B. Improvingthe InvestmentClimate
62.       Pakistan has made impressive strides in deregulating its economy to increase competition and
reduce the cost o f doing business, has had an extensive privatization program, and is engaged in
important structural reforms, particularly inthe power sector, which has improved the investment climate
and provides good prospects for continued growth. As a result, investor confidence has improved, as
reflected in the considerable increase in foreign direct investment, significant jump in the imports of
machinery, increases in capacity in the textile, automobiles, and electronics sector, and rapid expansion
inthe telecommunications sector.

63.       The impact o f the Government's deregulation policy i s most clearly evident in the
telecommunications sector, which has registered exponential growth, particularly inthe mobile ~egment.~
The Government approved a fixed line policy on January 2004 (a PRSC Iprior action), with the objective
o f increasing customer choice, promoting infrastructure development through increased private
investment and competition, and increasing rural access. As a result o f this policy, 12 new Long
Distance International(LDI), and 35 new Local Loop (LL) service providers have been awarded licenses
by the Pakistan Telecommunications Authority (PTA). The new mobile policy (a PRSC Iprior action)
opened the way for increased competition inthe sector, and under it two additional licenses were issued
to two foreign companies in April 2004, thus increasing the total number o f mobile operators in the
country to six.

64.       As competition has increased, tariffs have been reduced considerably, with free roaming
introduced by many o f the mobile operators, and reduction in activation charges. Overall teledensity
(fixed and mobile) has increased at an exponential rate from 4.3 per 100 in 2002/03 to 26.3 per 100 in
2005/06, and to 35.4 by December 2006. The bulk o f this growth has been in the mobile sector, with
mobile connections increasing from 2.4 million in2002/03 to 48.3 million by December 2006.

65.       Pakistan has made considerable progress in financial sector reforms, transforming a
predominantly state-owned and weak banking sector into a healthier, market-based system. As a result
o f the successes o f the Government's privatizationprogram, roughly 80% o f the country's banking assets
are now in private hands. Privatization has been supported by a strengthened legal and regulatory
environment and significant improvements in transparency, corporate governance, and credit culture,
with a resultant improvement in financial soundness indicators.       The Government i s now focusing on
second generation reforms for strengthening the financial markets infrastructure, inparticular inthe areas
o f insurance, pensions and capital markets development. Insurance penetration is very l o w relative to
other countries at Pakistan's income level, and there i s a need for further consolidation and liberalization
o f the industry. A number o f issues inpensions require the authorities' attention including the financial
sustainability and regulatory oversight of the present schemes. The corporate bond market also remains
underdeveloped. The PRSC program will continue the dialogue with key counterparts, such as the
Securities and Exchange Commission o fPalustan, inthese second generation reforms.

66.       As discussed indetail inthe next section, Pakistan's privatization programhas advanced rapidly
over the past two years, and significant progress has been made inlabor market reforms to improve labor
market flexibility and to reduce the costs o f doing business. The Government has also taken important
steps inthe difficult area ofpower sector reforms, with the aim o f unbundlingand corporatizing WAPDA
to reduce the drain on the budget, improve sector performance and remove a major infrastructure
bottleneckto firm-level productivity.

96.     Tax administration reform is a priority for the Government, and is a prior action for
PRSCII.For macroeconomic consolidationto continue gradually and for additional fiscal space for
poverty reduction expenditures to be created, it i s important that the tax effort be increased.
Pakistan's tax-to-GDP ratio is low in comparison to similar developing countries, due to both
weaknesses in tax policy and intax administration. On the latter, the reform o f the Central Board o f
Revenue (CBR) i s a priority for the Government, and is being supported by the World Bank's Tax
Administration Reform Project (approved in December 2004).                      These reforms include
therestructuring o f CBR along functional lines and integration o f income, sales and excise tax
collections; the strengthening o f the human resource base; reengineering and automating business
processes and workflows; and improving services for the taxpayers. As part o f this strategy, Large
Taxpayer Units (LTUs) have been established in Karachi and Lahore, and Medium Taxpayer Units
(MTUs) have been set up inLahore, Karachi, Peshawar, Quetta, andRawalpindi. The establishment
o f the LTUin Lahore, and the MTUs in Karachi, Peshawar, and Rawalpindi were also PRSC 11prior
actions, and complemented the reform efforts being supported under the Tax Administration Reform
Project. These MTUs will become part o f 13 regional tax offices, three o f which were established in
December 2006 (inRawalpindi, Peshawar, and Abbotabad), which will co-locate sales tax and income
tax, thereby providing for the greater sharing o f information and the removal o f duplication.

stability; (ii) improvements in governance and institutional capacity; (iii) investment in health and
education; and (iv) support for the poor.

150.     The specific country policies proposed in the PRSC 11are unlikely to cause environmental
impact. It i s possible, however, that the indirect effects o f these policies will generate environmental
benefitsby promoting economic diversification and a less resource-intensive economic structure. The
reforms proposed for the power sector are designed to stimulate production efficiency and have n o
direct environmental impact. Concerns over fuel switching in forest areas are unlikely to arise due to
the very limited connectivity and use o f electricity for cooking by households dependent on fuel-
wood. The PRSC recommends the privatization o f a limitednumber o f inefficient public enterprises.
Pakistan's privatizationpolicies have been developed with assistance o f the Asian Development Bank
and include the standard safeguards. A Bank team will assess the implementationo f these policies and
seek ways to strengthening environmental performance. A number o f financial and institutional
reforms are proposed in the PRSC to improve Palustan's fiscal performance. These are expected to

                                                    44

increase the efficiency o f tax collection, lead to more targeted spending and improve accountability.
These initiatives have no obvious environmental impact. The overall conclusioni s that the PRSC IIi s
likely to be environmentallyneutral.


E. Risks
151.    The risks to this operation include i)macroeconomic risks, ii)risks o f exogenous shocks, iii)
implementationcapacity, and iv) political risks:

        i)Riskstothefiscalandexternaloutlook: Thereisariskofincreasedexternalvulnerability
        for Pakistan. Import growth was strong in 2005/06 due to increase in international oil prices,
        and increased domestic demand for a wide range o f consumer and investment goods. While
         import growth has slowed down considerably in the first half o f 2006/07 in significant part
         due to measures taken by the State Bank, rising oil prices could again increase the import bill.
        Export growth has also slowed down, and i s projected at 8% for 2006/07, and as a result the
        current account deficit i s now projected to be above 4% o f GDP in spite of strong growth in
        worker's remittances. It will be important for the Government to maintain real exchange rate
         competitiveness to reduce the current account deficit, as outlined in its macroeconomic
         framework (Table 2). The Bank i s helping mitigate these risks via the PRSC program
         support for stabilization measures to reduce inflation in the short run, and to raise the
         economy's competitiveness in the medium run through structural measures as well as other
         investment, technical assistance and capacity building

         There are also some downside risks on the fiscal front, which may pose challenges to fiscal
         management in Pakistan. Expenditure pressures are evident, especially after the increases in
         the PSDP and the continuing financial difficulties o f the WAPDA companies. The October
         2005 earthquake has also posed additional difficulties on the federal government's finances.
         The Government i s restructuring and reprioritizing expenditures and enhancing revenue
         collection so as to adhere to the Fiscal Responsibility and Debt LimitationLaw.

         ii)Riskofexogenous shocks: Theincreaseintheinternationalpriceofcrudeoilcanpose
         significant risks to the macroeconomic situation and the livelihoods of the poor. The October
         2005 earthquake has also highlighted Palustan's vulnerability to natural disasters. Palustan i s
         subject to earthquakes as well as weather-related hazards including floods, landslides and
         droughts. The Bank has been worlung closely with the Government o f Palustan on the
         response to the present emergency, as well as on a risk mitigation strategy that would help
         reduce the negative impacts o f future natural disasters.

         iii)Imdementationrisks:Goodprogresshasbeenmadeinreducingdelaysinimplementation
         and technical and institutional capacity constraints since PRSC I.Numerous institutions at
         the federal and provincial level have undergone, or are currently going through, major
         restructuring, capacity-building o f their staff, and information technology strengthening.
         These institutions include the State Bank o f Pakistan, the Central Board o f Revenue, the
         Auditor General o f Pakistan, the Controller General o f Accounts, and the Palustan Public
         Procurement Authority. Nonetheless, coordination and capacity weaknesses remain in the
         federal and provincial bureaucracy that could pose implementation challenges. Also, a stalled
         devolution process could negatively affect the achievement o f the Government's human
         development targets. However, as the reform and capacity-building program is extended to
         more and more parts o f the country, weaknesses in implementation capacity and institutional
         coordinationare expected to decline.



                                                    45

         iv) Political and security risks: The reform program faces the risk o f policy reversal due to
         shifting priorities or political changes. The Government has shown itself able to sustain its
         reform program over an extended period. However, the continuing fallout from the regional
         security situation could interfere with the successful implementation o f the reform program.
         In addition, the period leading up to elections in2007 could bringsome uncertainty and slow
         down inreformimplementation.

152.     The elections o f October 2002 showed that the political process can advance without
seriously undermining the reform process. In addition, the increased and more proactive involvement
o f the National Assembly in economic and social reforms has been key to ensure a broad-based
political support for the PRSP reforms. However, increased security risks or political instability
beyond the normal conduct o f elections and political transition could increase the risks premium for
private investors and lenders to Pakistan, and hence undermine the poverty reductionprogram.

153.     While the outlined risks remain relevant, the overall benefits o f supportingPakistan's reform
program outweigh the risks. Demonstrated ownership o f the PRSP's reform program by the
Government and the continuity o f economic and social policies over the past five years have
strengthened the credibility o f the Government as a reformer. The Bank mitigates some o f these risks
by sustaining and deepening its policy dialogue, supporting capacity building o f key government
institutions, and disbursingagainst a set o f prior actions. The proposed operation could help lock in
reformgains and further the reformprocess inPakistan.

           Public InformationNotice (PIN) No. 06/139 December 7,2006
Public Information Notices (PINs) form part o f the IMF's efforts to promote transparency o f the
IMF's views and analysis o f economic developments and policies. With the consent of the country
(orcountries)  concerned, PINs are issued after Executive Board discussions o f Article IV
consultations with member countries, o f its surveillance o f developments at the regional level, o f
post-program monitoring, and o f ex post assessments o f member countries with longer-termprogram
engagements. PINs are also issued after Executive Board discussions o f general policy matters,
unless otherwise decided by the Executive Board in a particular case. The Staff Report for the 2006
Article IV Consultationwith Pakistan i s also available

On November 22, 2006, the Executive Board o f the International Monetary Fund (IMF) concluded
the Article IV consultation with Pakistan.

Background

Pakistan's recent economic performance has been impressive. Growthhas accelerated, improvements
inpublic spending and wide-ranging structural reforms have reducedthe debt burden and increased
efficiency, and pro-poor policies have helped lower poverty rates. The devastating earthquake o f
October 2005 left a heavy toll interms o f human lives and physical and social infrastructure, but had
relatively minor effects on macroeconomic indicators (except for an increase in government
spending) owing mainly to the small share o f the affected areas inthe overall economy.

Economic developments duringthe fiscal year ending inJune 2006 were favorable, with buoyant real
GDP growth and inflation declining to 7.6%. The external current account deficit, however,
increasedto US$5 billion (3.9% o f GDP), from US$1.5 billion (1.4% o f GDP) a year earlier. Record-
high net capital flows (mainly from foreign direct investment-including        privatization) more than
covered the larger deficit and allowed for a build up o f nearly U S $ l billion in official international
reserves. The interbank market exchange rate was broadly unchanged from end-June 2005.

The fiscal deficit exceeded the original budget target for 2005/06 owing to earthquake-related
spending. Excluding the latter, revenues and expenditures rose by roughly the same amounts
compared to the outturn in the previous fiscal year. The government debt-to-GDP ratio fell to 56 %
by the end o f June, below the 60 % ceiling stipulated in the 2005 Fiscal Responsibility Law. The
budget for 2006/07 targets a deficit o f 4.2% o f GDP (excluding grants), unchanged from the
estimated outturn for 2005/06.

The growth o f broad money and bank credit to the private sector decelerated in2005/06, but private
sector credit continued to expand at a relatively strong pace. Bank lending rates became increasingly
positive in real terms as inflation declined; the real return on bank deposits remained negative.
The State Bank o f Pakistan's (SBP) claims on the government rose by more than total government
borrowing from the banking system, as the SBP absorbed treasury bills that commercial banks did
not roll over upon redemption. In July 2006, the SBP tightened monetary conditions by raising
reserve and liquidityrequirements on bank deposits and the discount rate.

Foreign investors' interest in Pakistan increased significantly in 2005/06. Foreign direct investment
inflows, excluding privatization, rose by 70%, and foreign demand for Pakistani bonds was strong.

                                                                                             Annex C
                                                                                           Page 2 of 4
Government divestiture from electricity and telecommunications entities generated large foreign
exchange inflows and revitalized the privatizationprocess.

Progress on structural reforms, other than privatization, was mixed. Reforms to broaden the income
tax base and streamline rates continued, and the legal framework for investor protection was
strengthened. However, implementation o f the schedule o f higher regional electricity tariffs, a key
step for continued reform o f the power sector, remains pending, and progress on trade liberalization
slowed.

Executive BoardAssessment

Executive Directors commended Pakistan's impressive macroeconomic performance since 2001.
They welcomed inparticular the acceleration in output growth, the steady decline in debt ratios, and
the fall in poverty rates. Directors noted that Palustan's strong track record on the macroeconomic
and structural reform fronts had made the country increasingly attractive to foreign investors, as
shown by the record-high inflows o f foreign direct investment (FDI) during 2005/06 and the
favorable terms obtained on recent sovereign bondplacements inthe international capital markets.

Directors noted that during 2005/06 the Pakistani economy had withstood well the impact o f large
negative shocks, including the tragic earthquake o f October 2005, a sharp rise in international oil
prices, and unfavorable weather conditions. Although these shocks had limited the scope for fiscal
maneuver, growth had remained buoyant, inflation had declined slightly and the import coverage o f
reserves hadremainedstable.

Directors nonetheless noted the risks to the outlook, including the continued strength o f domestic
demand, and its adverse effects on the trade and current account deficits as well as on the pace o f
disinflation during 2005/06. They noted also the authorities' view that macroeconomic imbalances
would decline without the need for further changes inthe stance o f policies envisaged for the current
fiscal year, and welcomed the Government's commitment to tighten monetary policy, if warranted.
However, most Directors felt that macroeconomic policies during2006/07 shouldbe more effectively
geared at reducing domestic demand and strengthening the balance o f payments position. To this
effect, many considered that a further tightening o f monetary policy (including by allowing higher
cutoff rates at treasury bill auctions) should not be delayed to help strengthen the external position
and allow the Government to meet its inflation target. Most Directors considered that to be effective,
monetary policy should be supported by exchange rate flexibility and a fiscal policy that keeps this
year's budget deficit (excluding grants and earthquake-related expenditures) at least at the level o f
2005106.

Directors stressed that, beyond 2006/07, Palustan's macroeconomic policies should aim at ensuring
that the external current account deficit-to-GDP ratio remains on a declining path with a steady build
up o freserves. Inthis regard, they encouraged the authorities to adopt a policy stance that maintains
real interest rates at positive levels accompanied by a close monitoring o f credit growth, and a fiscal
consolidation program that lowers the overall fiscal deficit to a sustainable level over the medium
tern.

Directors viewed the favorable prospects for sizable FDI inflows as important for future gains in
productivity and investment, but also as presenting challenges for macroeconomic policy inthe years
ahead. They highlighted that continued reliance on FDI inflows o f uncertain size and timing would
require a large degree o f flexibility in economic policymaking. Inthis connection, Directors stressed
the need to improve the Government's capacity to generate timely policy responses to shortfalls o f
external financing arising from negative balance o f payments shocks. Directors were o f the view that
those shocks should generally require monetary policy and exchange rate responses, but also
envisaged a role for fiscal tightening in cases where the shocks are large, or more permanent in

                                                                                              Annex C
                                                                                            Page 3 of 4
nature. Directors cautioned that the option o f resorting to the use o f international reserves to cover
shortfalls o f external financing (especially those stemmingfrom delays inFDI-related flows) ought to
be used sparingly.

Directors viewed structural reforms conducive to higher saving and investment, an improvedbusiness
climate, and well-targeted poverty-related spending as critical for sustaining growth and poverty
reduction over the medium term. They encouraged the authorities to quickly complete the reform o f
the regulatory and tariff framework for the power sector, and step up efforts to broaden the tax base
and further curtail tax exemptions. Directors also saw scope for improving the Government's debt
management strategy, including by increasing the issuance of long-term marketable securities and
reducing its reliance on treasury bills and the National Savings Schemes (NSS) to finance the fiscal
deficit. Directors welcomed the initiatives underway to modernize the NSS and reform the system o f
broker-financing o f stock trading, but noted that these should be followed quickly with measures that
enable the integrationo f the NSS with local financial markets.

Directors welcomed the authorities' commitment to maintaining a liberal trade regime and their
determination to contribute to the success o f the Doha round o f trade talks. They called on the
authorities to resist pressures to reinstate ad-hoc tariff and nontariff measures and broaden export
subsidy schemes.
Directors encouraged the authorities to further improve the quality and timeliness o f data, including
by reporting fiscal data on an economic classification. They also urgedthe authorities to expedite the
process o f subscribing to the Special Data DisseminationStandard.

                                    Monitoring PRSPOutcomes

                                                Data Sources

         Data for monitoring PRSP outcomes originates from a variety o f sources. The Statistics'
Division within the Ministry o f Economic Affairs and Statistics has three attached departments,
which collect and/or report the following types o f data and information:

         a. Federal Bureau of Statistics (FBS) i s Pakistan's official statistical organization.
         FBS compiles statistics on national accounts, foreign trade, prices, and social indicators.
         Itregularly publishes Statistical Yearbooks, Palustan Statistics Handbook and yearbooks
         inspecialized fields ofstatistics.
         b. PopulationCensus Organization(PCO) is responsible for planning and execution
         o f decennial Population and Housing Census and release o f its data for public and private
         use. PCO disseminates data in the form o f regular census reports and supplementary
         reports. These publications contain general description and broad analysis o f population
         and housingdata on national, provincial and district level, followed by statistical tables.
         c. Agricultural Census Organization (ACO) conducts decennial censuses o f
         agriculture; decennial censuses o f livestock; decennial censuses o f agricultural
         machinery and village censuses before each census on agnculture and livestock. The
         census/survey data are tabulated at the division, district, province and country levels, and
         published in the form o f reports, which include both statistical tables and description
         methodology.


Macroeconomic data:lo

         Macroeconomic statistics in Pakistan are produced by three institutions: (i) Federal the
Bureau o f Statistics (FBS), which i s responsible for the national accoupts, prices, other economic
statistics; (ii)
               the State Bank o f Palustan (SBP), which i s responsible for monetary and balance o f
payments statistics; and (iii)  the Ministryo f Finance (MoF), which is responsible for statistics on
central and general government operations and central government debt.

              National Accounts.       Pakistan National Accounts estimates are prepared broadly
              along the lines o f the System o f National Accounts (SNA) 1993." The source data
              for the national accounts are derived from benchmark censuses, a regular household
              budget survey, and administrative sources. A comprehensive business register i s
              updated on a continuous basis, but regular enterprise surveys are not conducted.12
              The FBS has conducted a comprehensive set o f revision studies as part o f the
              exercise to update the base year and implement the 1993 SNA.

          Consumer Price Index (CPI) and Kholesale Price Index (WPI). Source data include

          sources (for the weights for the WPI). Both price indexes use broad-based price
         collection surveys. Source data are timely.

         Fiscal Accounts. Fiscal accounts include data on central government budgetary
          aggregates (revenue, expenditure, balance, and financing with breakdowns by debt
         holder, instrument, currency, and interest payments) and central government debt
          (domestic and foreign debt, with appropriate breakdowns by debt holder, instrument,
          currency and the Government guaranteed debt). The scope o f GFS is budgetary data,
          covering the bulk o f general government activity, but does not cover the activity o f
          extra-budgetary units in central government. Central and provincial governments are
          distinguished, but local governments are included with the provincial governments.
          Responsibility for preparation o f government accounts lies with the Accountant
          General for Pakistan Revenue (AGPR) for the central (federal) government, and the
          provincial Accountant Generals at the provincial and local government level.

          AGPR compiles GFS revenue and expenditure statistics for the central government
          budget and provides these data to M o F on a monthly basis. GFS domestic financing
          data are compiled by the State Bank o f Palustan (SBP) and provided to M o F
          monthly. External financing data are compiled by the External Affairs Division o f
          MoF. GFS debt data are compiled by the Debt Management Unit (for domestic debt)
          and External Affairs Division (for external debt) o f MoF. The data are broadly in
          line with the internationally accepted definitions, classifications, and scope, and are
          received in a timely manner. GFS are disseminated on the M o F website and in the
          annual M o F publication Economic Survey, produced by the Budget Wing o f the
          Ministry.

          Monetary Statistics. The main source data for monetary statistics are highly
          aggregated weekly statements from the Banking Supervision Department o f the SBP
          that do not provide the required breakdown o f financial instruments and economic
          sectors. Source data are timely, with the exception o f SBP data for the end o f the
          fiscal year (end-June), which are only available with a two to three months lag.

          External Sector. External sector data include balance o f payments, international
          reserves and foreign currency liquidity, merchandise trade, and exchange rate are
          collected by the SBP. The main source data for balance o fpayments statistics are the
          International Transactions Reporting System (ITRS), reports from enterprises, and
          administrative records o f the Government and the SBP. The data are broadly in line
          with the internationally accepted definitions, classifications, and scope and are
          received ina timely manner.

Microeconomic data:

          Household data: Palustan has had a long tradition o f Household Income and
          Expenditure Survey (HIES), with the first such survey conducted in 1963.The HIES
          was supplemented with an integrated household survey (PIHS) from 1990-91, which
          measures social indicators. In 1998, the HIES was integrated with Pakistan
          Integrated Household Survey to link poverty and consumption statistics with other
          indicators o f social development and another round was conducted in 2001-02. The

            above-mentioned surveys have been available in the public domain and have been
            used to track trends inpoverty and other social indicators.

            The Pakistan Social and Living Standard Measurement (PSLM) survey was launched
            in2004 to providemore frequent informationon a variety o f service-delivery related
            indicators, and to be representative at the district level. This survey comprises o f two
            parts: (i) Core Welfare Indicators Questionnaire (CWIQ) component that focuses
                      the
            on living standards and social indicators, representative at the district level; with a
            sample o f 76,529 households; and (ii)    a household income and expenditure survey
            component that collects consumption and income information from households,
            representative at the provincial level. The CWIQ component was designed,
            developed, and pilot tested with technical support from the World Bank. The CWIQ
            component of the PSLM was developed with a specific intent to track access, usage
            and satisfaction o f households with public services at district level. Reports o fresults
            from the CWIQ data at the provincial and district level have been made public by
            FBS.

       0    Labor data. Data on labor markets, employment, and labor characteristics are
            gathered through the Labor Force Survey (LFS) carried out every three years by the
            FBC since 1963. The last LFS was done in 2005-06. The survey covered all urban
            and rural areas o f the four provinces o f Palustan, excluding Federally Administered
            Tribal Areas (FATA), military restricted areas, and protected areas o f NWFP. Since
            2001-02, the LFS has been expanded to include questions on female activities, the
            size and composition o f migration and informal sectors, and occupational safety and
            health related issues. The survey gathers information on the size o f the labor force,
            participation rate, employmentlunemployment by sector, gender, urbadrural divide.
            While the LFS data provide useful intertemporal information, they do not cover
            regular monitoring o f wages. Annual data on wages and earnings are not
            disseminated, and are based on the PMS.

            Demographic data. FBS is responsible for collecting demographic data through the
            Population and Demographic Survey (PDS). The survey provides statistics on births,
            deaths, natural increase in population. Traditional sources for vital statistics are the
            Civil Regstration System (birth and death registration system) and population
            census. Various measures o f fertility and mortality rates are collected every one or
            two years between census years inthe absence o f a vital registration system.

            Inaddition, demographic data are collectedby the Population Census Organization,
            which plans and executes the decennial Population and Housing Census. PCO
            disseminates data inthe form o f Census Reports.
