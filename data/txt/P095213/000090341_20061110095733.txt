                                     I. INTRODUCTION

1.      Improving the country's economic competitiveness has been a central theme of the
Governmentof Colombia's (GoC) overall policy program and thus has also been a key pillar of
the Bank's support strategy. On this basis, the Government requested the preparation of a
Programmatic Development Policy Loan (DPL) series to support implementation of its
competitiveness-enhancing policies particularly with regard to five key areas considered crucial. This
series was includedas part of the Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) ProgressReport reviewed by the
Board in September 2005 and which extended the CAS period through the end of the Bank's Fiscal
Year 2007. As these loans focus on the microeconomic rather than the macroeconomic aspects of
competitiveness, they are namedthe BusinessProductivity and Efficiency DPL series.

2.      The proposedoperation would bethe second of three loansinthe series, continuing with
the same broad themes and objectives as the first.          T h i s Program has been designed to be
implemented over a period of three years (Figure 1). Initiation of the preparation of this loan reflects
the continued implementation of the Program including the key triggers identified during preparation
of the first loan. The development objective of this Program remains unchanged: to support
sustainablegrowth and the alleviation of poverty by:

    (a)     Facilitating the operation of businesses and promoting investment, to boost productivity
            and employment levels; and

    (b)     Consolidating the financial sector and capital markets as pillars of economic growth to
            address the needs of individuals and the productive sector.

                                        11.      COUNTRY CONTEXT

5.        Since a macroeconomic crisis at the end of the nineties, Colombia has been on a path of
economic growth. The country's economy expanded steadily for decades until 1999, when a
combination of domestic and international conditions triggered a severe financial and economic crisis.
By 2002, 58 percent of the population was living below the poverty line and 20 percent were
considered extremely poor. With the return of stable economic growth in the last few years, national
poverty measures have begun to improve again. The nationwide poverty rate dropped to 50 percent
by 2005 and the extreme poverty rate fell to 15 percent. After reaching 10percent in 1999, the share
of the population living on less than US$1a day i s down to 3.1 percent, putting Colombia on track to
reach its Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) for poverty. Similarly, the percentage of people
livingon less than US$2 a day fell from 18 percent in 1999to 7.7 percent six years late?.

6.        The recent orderly congressional and presidential election processes should continue to
foster a more attractive investment climate. During preparation of the first loan of this series,
political uncertainty and a possible deterioration in the internal security situation related to the 2006
elections were identified as potential risks to the Program. These risks did not materialize and the re-
election of President Alvaro Uribe by wide margins, as well as the success of parties supporting him
in the congressional elections, will likely facilitate continuation of the reform process. The
expectation of continued adequate governance, a stabilized security situation, and relatively benign
international conditions has increased local business sentiment. By June 2006, the monthly survey
among Colombian businesses carried out by AND1 (Asociaci6n Nacional de Empresarios de
Colombia, National Association of Colombian Entrepreneurs) reached the highest level of optimism
on record (72.6 percent of business owners perceived their business as "doing well" or better,
compared to 51percent historically).'

RECENTECONOMICDEVELOPMENTS

7.       Colombia's economy has recovered since the start of the current CAS period (2002-2006); the
CAS Progress Report has extended this period through the end of 2007. The near stagnation of the
economy duringthe four years prior to the CAS has since given way to a sustained economic recovery
and a strengthening of consumer confidence. The turnaround i s partly due to the much improved
global economic environment: world growth has accelerated, the cost of international credit has
fallen, and the prices of Colombia's primary exports have risen. Domestic factors, particularly the
improved security situation and stable macroeconomic polices, have also driven the country's
recovery. Improving conditions are reflected by an almost 3 percentage point increase in private
investment as a share of GDP since 2002, and real economic growth that accelerated from 1.6 percent

10.      The Colombian governmentsent a tax reformbill to Congressin July 2006. The reform
intends to simplify the tax code, to encourage companies to invest and to raise the number of people
payingtaxes. The tax bill cuts the corporate tax rate from 38.5 percent to 33 percent in2007 and to 32
percent in 2008. The government proposes to scrap exemptions so that companies pay the same rate.
The government will also reduce the income tax paid by individuals. Additionally, the government
proposes to gradually eliminate the financial transactions tax. The government also proposes to
expand the VAT base.

11.      During the second quarter of 2006, Colombia saw a sudden increase in domestic
markets' volatility which has decreased over the last two months. The currency weakened,
interest rates on government bonds rose and the stock exchange cooled down. The peso has
depreciated almost 8 percent this year. The volatility has been consistent with movements in most
emerging markets as investors are concerned that accelerating inflation will lead the US Federal
Reserve to raise rates, makingrisky assets less attractive than US fixed income assets.

MACROECONOMIC OUTLOOK AND CHALLENGES

12.      Inthe absence of major external shocks,the economy is expectedto continuegrowingat
a pace similar to recent years. Since prospects for significantly improving the factors supporting
domestic demand (internal peace, investment and credit) and external demand (exports and foreign
direct investment) are modest, it is likely that GDP growth will remain at around 4.0-5.0 percent
through 2007. The expected current account deficit for 2006 i s 1.4 percent of GDP, 0.2 percent lower
than in 2005. This decline i s mostly explained by an expected positive result in the trade balance of
US$748 million. The current account deficit is expected to be financed through a capital and
financial account surplus of US$ 1709 millions (1.3 percent of GDP). Incoming years, FDIflows are
expected to increase with the likely US and Colombian congressional approval of the Free Trade
Agreement (FTA) as well as with the enactment of recent business environment reforms.

13.      Gross non financial public sector debt reached about 44 percent of GDP at the end of
2005. This correspondsto 32 percent of GDP, nettingout domestic and foreign financial assets. This
level of debt is sustainable under most scenarios, appearing manageable in the near term even
considering possible adverse economic shocks. The GoC target is to reduce the level of gross debt to
40 percent of GDP by 2010. Even if Colombia faced several combined economic shocks, if the
primary surplus were to remain at the historical level of 2.1 percent of GDP, total debt levels could be
kept at manageable level^.^

14.      Exportmarkets will generate uncertaintyinthe comingmonths. Oil prices will continue
to affect the economy through their major impact on export and fiscal revenues and as a key
determinant of growth in Venezuela, the country's main market after the US for non-traditional
exports. The implementation of the FTA, which could boost trade between the Andean countries and
the US, still depends on approval by the US and the ColombianCongresses and Constitutional Court.

     IV.     KEY ISSUES AFFECTING BUSINESSPRODUCTIVITY AND EFFICIENCY

20.     This section considers the main problems and challenges for doing businesses in
Colombia in the areas supportedby this DPL. Within this policy context, the DPL programputs
emphasis on actions and outcomes that are most important for the achievement of the Government's
objectives of stimulating broad-based economic growth and maximizing the benefits of increased
international integration. The focus of this DPL i s also determined by the GoC's specific priorities
and achievements within its reform agenda, as well as the coverage of complementary programs from
the Bank and other international institutions.

21.     Multiple surveys and analytical works have identified key constraints to business
productivity and efficiency in Colombia, most of which the GoC is addressing with direct or
indirect Bank support. Table 2 lists the main obstacles to doing business identified by survey

surveys and analytical studies conducted in Colombia, including the Colombia Country Economic
Memorandum' (CEM) finalized during the Bank's FY 2006 and monthly surveys by Colombia's
NationalAssociation of Entrepreneurs (Asociaci6n Nacional de Empresarios, ANDI).

la Productividad y Competitividad, AI). The development of the AI has been a bottom-up process to
identify regional and sector competitiveness strengths in order to develop Competitiveness Plans for

BUSINESSENVIRONMENT

24.      The GoC hasmadeimprovingthe businessenvironment a prioritysince2002, aimingto
stimulate business activity and improve competitiveness." The authorities have also sought to
reduce informality by easing the costs and inconvenience associated with formal business activity.
Initial efforts included the establishment of new fiscal incentives for certain types of private
investments (Law 788/2002), increases in labor market flexibility (Law 789/2002), measures to
facilitate foreign direct investment (Decree 1,844/2003), and new incentives to promote credit to the
private sector (Law 795/ 2003).

25.      The National Development Plan established the reduction of bureaucratic
administrative proceduresor "red tape" as a central element of the GoC's driveto improve the
country's businessenvironment. Through the Directorate of Public Administration (Departamento

for such processes. The law also permitted more documentation to be submitted electronically or by

notarized in most bureaucratic procedures.The regulatory Decree 4969/2005, issued subsequentto the
enactment of the law, specified the impact the law would have and established a consultative body,

27.       Further progresscame with the creation of "one-stop shops" to streamline the processes
involved in starting and operating businesses. The GoC's DNP has supported the Colombian
Confederation of Chambers of Commerce (Confechmaras) and local governments in establishing
Centers for Enterprise Assistance (Centros de Atencidn Empresarial, CAEs) in six major cities. The

28.       Almost all formalities for starting a business in Colombia can now be completed in one

30.       The "Legal Stability Law" (Law 963/2005) seeks to address investor concerns about
legal and tax instability. It allows for limited guarantees of legal, regulatory and tax stability for
major investors, both foreign and local, who enter into contracts with the government for an annual

covered will be exempt from any changes to applicable laws and regulations specified in the contract.
Exceptions include labor and social security laws, taxes and charges introduced in a state of
emergency, indirect taxes, and Central Bank (Banco de la Republica, BdR) rules. The contracts only

one such contract has been signed, with food producer Alpina in 2005, and the Ministry of Industry,
Trade, and Tourism (MICT) reports that others are in the pipeline.

31.       The Doing Business reports reflect the improvements made in Colombia's business
environment, particularly in 2003-2004. The 2005 edition rated Colombia the second-fastest
reformer in the world in 2003-2004, after Slovakia, due to the country's significant reforms in the
fields of administrative simplification, contract enforcement, and property registration. The average
time needed to resolve a commercial dispute was cut by 30 percent, from 527 to 363 days. Changes
were introduced in the stages of notification and enforcement, shortening these periods by 46 and 59
percent respectively. Inthe notification process, debtors are now informed of court filings by a private
courier company, as opposed to a court clerk. If the courier fails to reach the debtor, the notice i s
published in a newspaper, and if the debtor does not show up in court, the case continues without him.
For the enforcement of judgments, stricter time limits were introduced for the corresponding
procedures, and professionals other than court officials were allowed to perform them. For instance,
notaries and the chambers of commerce- not only the judge-can organize auctions for the sale of
assets. In the area of property registration, dramatic improvements have put Colombia well ahead of
international averages: it now takes just 23 days to transfer a property title from the seller to the
buyer, compared to 56 days on average in Latin America, and 34 days inthe OECD countries.

FINANCIAL SYSTEMS AND CAPITAL MARKETS

Financial Systems

40.      Institutional investors have become increasingly important over the past ten years,
although banks remain the main source of private sector financing. The relative importance of
institutional investors (Le. pension funds, insurance companies and trust services, jiduciarias) has
increased compared to the banking sector since 1995 (Figure 3). This development, together with the
increased holdings of securities - primarily sovereign bonds - by credit institutions (from 12 percent
of assets in 1998 to 33 percent in 2005), have contributed to the significant expansion of daily market
trading volumes over the same time period, particularly in fixed income (from COP 1trillion in 1995
to COP 12 trillion in 2005). However, the vast majority of institutional investor assets remains
invested in government bonds - in fact, annual debt and equity issuance by the non-financial private
sector between 2001-2005 was only around 1 percent of GDP - so banks continue to represent the
main vehicle for private sector financing.

41.      The pace of the banking sector's recovery has accelerated recently. After a longperiod of
shrinkage due to the crisis of the late 1990s, banking sector size began to recover in 2004 and standard
financial performance indicators - solvency, profitability, efficiency and asset quality - have
significantly improved (Table 7). The recovery in performance has been facilitated by an
accommodating external environment and the resumption of domestic economic growth that increased
loan demand, as well as by banks' significant exposure to government securities that has allowed
them to generate substantial profits from declining interest rates. In fact, it i s estimated that as much
as two-thirds of banking system profits in 2005 stem from valuation gains as a result of the 400 basis
points drop in government securities yields over that period. This trend was briefly and violently
reversed in early 2006 due to the mini-crisis of confidence by foreign investors in emerging markets,
but the subsequent recovery - together with the pick-up in loan growth - has partly protected bank
performance in 2006. However, the exposure of banks to government securities, albeit on a declining
path this year, remains a significant source of market (as well as credit) risk.

42.      The pace of credit growth has accelerated considerably over the past year, helped by
increased competition and lower loan rates. After a long period of stagnation, lending by credit
institutions has accelerated in 2006 across most market segments (Figure 4) and especially in
consumer lending, which had grown to 28 percent of the total portfolio as of June 2006. The
continuation of benign external and strong domestic macroeconomic environments has underpinned
the growth in credit (33 percent on an annualized basis as of June 2006), which has beenbroad-based.
Increased competition among credit institutions has played an important role, aided by the decline in
interest rates over the last year. For example, the 90-day fixed-term deposit rate (Depdsito a Te`rmino
Fijo or DTF), which forms the benchmark reference rate for most loans, has declinedfrom almost 8
percent in early 2005 to 6.50 percent in July 2006. The drop has been even more pronounced in the
`blended' average interest rate used to calculate the usury ceiling (the highest interest rate that can be
legally charged, 1.5 times the blended average interest rate), which has dropped from around 19
percent inearly 2005 to 15 percent inAugust 2006.

43.              Limitations in the legal and institutional framework for creditor rights and insolvency
procedures have contributed to the relatively slower recovery of commercial credit.
Notwithstanding the use of collateral, the risk of loss given default incurred by credit providers i s not
adequately reduced due to inefficient enforcement and collection mechanisms (e.g. limited use of
private enforcement or mechanisms with minimum judicial intervention, long processes and abusive
use of procedural defense mechanisms) and the bias against creditor rights in insolvency proceedings.
The rigidity of the security interest regime, the diminished priority attached to mortgaged debts or

pledges by civil law, and certain weaknesses in the registry systems (lack of electronic
interconnection, geographic fragmentation, and slow procedures) contribute to the low regard in
which collateral i s held18.In fact, many of the problems of the current regime (Law 55011999) arise
from the fact that it was passed in the aftermath of the emergency circumstances of the 1999 crisis,
whereby the focus was primarily to protect debtors in systemic financial distress.

44.      The bankruptcy law recently submitted to Congress is a first positive step towards
addressingthe limitations surrounding creditor rights and insolvency procedures. The proposed
reform to the existing insolvency legislation (bill 207/05) improves certain aspects of the current
regime. Changes include the possibility of judicial validation of out-of-court reorganization
agreements, the simplification of procedures for an accelerated recognition of controversial claims in
insolvency proceedings, the establishment of provisions to streamline and encourage actions seeking
personal liability against directors and officers of the insolvent company, the adoption of modem
rules for the treatment of the international aspects of insolvencies with cross-border impact, and the
suppressionof "consensual priorities" and of "internal creditors". However, the draft law does not yet
adequately respond to certain shortcomings of the existing system or align itself fully to international
best practices". Areas that need improvement include the lack of a clear hierarchy for classes of
creditors, ensuring that the stay of securedcreditors' enforcement actions i s subject to reasonableand
temporal limits, inability of creditors to make an application for commencement of liquidation by
themselves, the lack of preservation of trusts in guarantee and of the credit protection afforded by
them in insolvency proceedings, and the existence of guarantees for the institutional independenceof
the authority withjurisdiction in insolvency proceedings.

45.      Housing finance has shown strong signs of a recovery in recent months (Figure 5). For
the first time since the 1999 crisis, mortgage loans have started to grow in the portfolios of credit
institutions in 2006. As real interest rates have fallen in response to lower systemic risk and improved
macroeconomic performance (Figure 6), the interest rate ceilings imposed by the constitutional court
become increasingly non-binding and allow lenders to profitably expand (particularly non-VIS)
mortgage lending. Such lending has been primarily undertaken by credit institutions; the contribution
of cajas de compensacidnfamiliar, cooperatives and employee funds, with support from FINDETER,
remains limited, although it i s still early to assess the impact of the legal changes that allowed these
institutionsto enter the sector.

46.      Tackling certain institutional constraints would help expand the pace of recovery for
housing finance. Anecdotal evidence suggests that disincentives for lenders to serve the housing
market more broadly include the financial transactions tax, the interest rate ceilings, legal issues
relating to relatively long mortgage foreclosure periods, and product design restrictions such as the
inability to offer floating rate peso mortgages. Even though the ceilings are not currently binding,
there remains the risk that the central bank would further reduce them (it has recently reduced the real
rate for non-VIS loans from 13.9 to 12.7 percent) to reflect changed conditions.20 In addition, the
recent decision by the Banco de la Repliblica (Central Bank, BdR) to apply the non-VIS loan ceiling
retroactively to existing mortgages has effectively converted them into variable rate with
indeterminate and unpredictable variability, thereby introducing an additional element of legal
uncertainty.

Preventionof MoneyLaundering(ML) andFinancingof Terrorism (FT)

63.      Combating money laundering and the financing of terrorism is a key element in
promoting a strong and sound financial system. Money laundering and terrorist financing can
weaken individual financial institutions, as well as representing a threat to overall financial sector
stability. The adverse consequences for institutions are generally described as: i)repututionul; as
high quality clients, that provide a stable deposit base and make reliable borrowers, lose confidence in
an institution connected with money laundering and take their business elsewhere; ii)operational;
where impaired internal processes or relations with other financial institutions impede the institution's
functions or raise its operating and funding costs; and iii)legal; due to the risk of lawsuits, adverse
judgments, unenforceable contracts, fines and penalties.

64.      Colombia's government attaches a high priority to Anti ML and FT (AMWCFT)
actions, to combat drug traffickingand organizedcrime, and promote financial sector stability
and a healthy climate for business. The government policy for preventing and combating the
offense i s described in the President's Democratic Security and Defense Policy and the National
Development Plan (Law 812/2003). This policy stresses the significance of the threat of money
laundering and terrorist financing and enunciated GoC concerns that the laundering of the proceeds of
cocaine and heroin marketing contributes to terrorism.              Moreover, the Colombian government
emphasizes that money laundering distorts the proper functioning of the economy as it disrupts the
foreign exchange market and other financial markets, and promotes the under-invoicing of imports
and exports and the making of fictitious or simulated exports as a suitable mechanism for bringing
illicit money into the country while appearing legal.

65.      The GoC has been addressing over the last year one of the key issues affecting
AMWCFT policies: coordination among multiple agencies. The prevention of ML involves
regulators, legal enforcement agencies and financial sector authorities among others.          A key
challenge, not just in Colombia but in any country in the process of implementing FATF (Financial
Action Task Force on Money Laundering, the international standard setter on AML/CFT policies)

supportedthe reorganization of the Comisidn de Coordinacidn InrerinstitucionalContra el Lavado de

the Colombian AML/CFT regime, as identified by the 2004 GAFISUD AML/CFT Assessment

QUALITY STANDARDS AND TECHNOLOGY AND INNOVATION

Quality Standards

67.      Changes in the nature of global trade caused by new technologies, trade policies, and
corporate strategies have integrated producers in global production networks where quality
and standards play an important role. Standards help to lower transaction costs since they
communicate reliable and consistent information regarding the characteristics and quality of products
and services to consumers. By allowing consumers to differentiate products of distinct quality, they
reduce asymmetric information and promote market competition. Moreover, trade increasingly
involves sub-components and services, making it more complex than the arms length relationships of
the past. Standards increase the efficiency of the development, manufacturing, and supply of such
products and services since they facilitate interfacing of processes and components, allow for
flexibility through component substitution, and bring about economies of scale. Finally, standards
usually embody technology and act as a channel for technology transfer for the institutions and firms
that adopt them, enhancing their productivity and competitiveness.

68.      A country's National Quality System is crucial to increase competitivenessand facilitate
the participation of national products in international markets. Producers that face growing
pressure to meet quality standard requirements must rely on a comprehensive system of interrelated
actors who facilitate the development, adoption, and diffusion of standards and quality in the
economy. The activities used to evaluate whether a product, process, or service fulfils specified
standards or technical requirements - known as "conformity assessment" procedures - are supported
by a multidimensional infrastructure of calibration, metrology, accreditation, and standardization
organizations. Conformity assessment organizations are closely linked. Calibration laboratories ensure
that the measurements performed by testing laboratories and inspection bodies are reliable; but they
themselves must demonstrate the accuracy and precision of their instruments, based on their
traceability to reference measurement standards held at the national metrology institute. The

                                                  23

competence of calibration and testing laboratories, and certification and inspection bodies must be
evaluated by a national accreditation body. All aspects of these activities rely on standards, which
provide the basis for evaluation of all conformity assessment bodies, and define the requirements
against which conformity assessment i s performed. In sum, conformity assessment activities provide
the vital link between standardsand the products, processes, and services themselves.

69.       To minimize technical barriers to international trade, national conformity assessment
procedures should be harmonized across countries and accreditation procedures should be
mutually recognized. Complying with a standard or a technical regulation i s only useful if it can be
demonstrated at reasonable cost. Demonstrating compliance through conformity assessment itself
requires that the testing and certification requirements are similar in the exporting and the importing
countries. If testing or certification in one country i s not recognized abroad, domestic firms requiring
tests on products or quality system and environmental management certification for exporting
purposes needto go through those processes again in each of the importing destination^.^' To promote
the recognition of conformity assessment procedures, many accreditation bodies base their
organizational structure and their accreditation decisions on well-recognized international guides and
standards. Hence, mutual recognition of accreditation procedures decreases transaction costs and
eliminates technical barriers to trade.

70.        In Colombia there are more than 5,000 technical standards in force and close to 130
active normalization committees. More than 35 percent of those standards are basedon international
norms. These figures are high in relation to the region's comparators and reflect the ability of the
Colombian Institute for Normalization (ICONTEC) to involve the most dynamic industrial sectors of
Colombia in the standardization process. But further efforts are needed in standards
internationalization and harmonization. Even though Colombia plays an active role in international
standards development, it is not a member of the InternationalStandardsOrganization (ISO) technical
committees more closely related to its bigger or most dynamic export sectors.31Moreover the free
trade agreement with the US requires intensifying the process of technical standards harmonization
with the Andean countries in priority areas. ICONTEC has started this program(NOREXPORT) with
IADB support but a wider participation of the productive sector is necessary. The Colombian
government provides no financial support to ICONTEC for standard development or participation in
international initiatives. Its funds come from commercial activities, such as certification, metrological
services, consulting, and training, but it sometimes generates conflicts of interest and market
 distortion^.^^

through internal changes in SIC to separate accreditation and metrology functions more clearly from
other activities.

80.      Innovation is a major driver of economic growth and productivity. One of the main
factors explaining large gaps in income and productivity i s resistance to the adoption of new
technologies and to the efficient use of current operating technologies, which in turn are conditioned
by the institutional and policy arrangements a society employs. A coherent, effective and well-funded
science, technology and innovation systemi s vital to gainfully compete in the global economy.

81.      The Colombian National Innovation System (NIS) encompasses public and private not-
for-profit institutions, firms, government programs, and individuals. Key indicators for the
Colombian innovation system are shown in Figure 10 and compared with those corresponding to the
LAC region and G-7 countries.

82.      Even though Colombia has innovative firms in several economic sectors, the country
underperforms on innovation outcomes as measured by the relative number of patents or by
investments in foreign licensing of technologies. Total investment in technology and research and
development (R&D) i s low (amounting to only 0.2 percent of GDP) and unstable over time. This
compares unfavorably with regional competitors, such as Brazil, Chile, Mexico, and Peru. Private
sector capacity and spending in innovation and R&D i s limited, showing low awarenessand adoption
of current innovations, low education levels of employees, and reduced collaboration with suppliers.
Although the indicator of private-public collaboration shows the best figure in LAC, there is still
much room for improvement. Common obstacles are an insufficient scientific and technological base,
a small number of government interventions that actively stimulate collaboration and old practices
and rigid rules in the management of public research institutions. These show lack of emphasis on
commercializing research, a clear policy regarding the division of intellectual property rights, and
incentives to favor mobility between the private and public sectors.

94.     Strengtheningroad infrastructure is key for Colombia's economic growth. A number of

and 65 percent of the country's GDP. Additionally, the expected increase in trade flows will intensify
pressures on the transport infrastructure. Enhancing the quality of the main logistics corridors, where
different and salient bottlenecks arise due to high levels of traffic, would reduce production costs and
improve the country's ability to internationally compete.

95.     Inthe 1990s, Colombia has made significant progress in developingits road network,

transportation modes.

LESSONS LEARNED


                                                   34

112.     The design of the proposedprogrammatic DPL takes into account the lessons learned
from the overall program with Colombia and in general from Bank work with other middle-
income countries in multi-sector competitiveness loans. In particular, lessons learned were
incorporated from the Brazil Programmatic Loan Series for Sustainable and Equitable Growth (Ln.
7218-BR and Ln. 7386-BR), the First Mexico Programmatic Competitiveness DPL (Ln. 7367-MX),
the First Mexico Finance and Growth DPL (Ln. 7363-MX) and the first loan of this Business
Productivity and Efficiency DPL series (Ln. 7334-CO). The most important lessons from these
operations are the following:

113.     Competitiveness operations should be focused and avoid large and complex policy
matrices. Competitiveness touches a broad range of policy areas. Rather than seeking to cover all of
them in a single complex operation, the design of this DPL has sought to concentrate on key areas that
can be more effectively addressedas a single packagewhile leavingother areas to be covered through
other Bank operations. Thus, for example, while human capital issues such as vocational training are
directly related to competitiveness, the Bank can collaborate more effectively with the GoC through
different operations such as the ongoingPLaRSSAL program.

114.     The importanceof flexibilityto respondto changingcircumstances.Inorder to implement
a lending program that best addresses Colombia's needs as a creditworthy middle income country, the
Bank needs the flexibility to be able to respond quickly to changing circumstances. This i s
particularly important when supporting a Program, as i s the case in this DPL series that has spanned
across two presidential administrations. The programmatic approach, as opposed to a multi-tranche
operation, provides the necessary flexibility to respond to new needs and priorities that can emerge
during the implementation process while ensuring a long-term approach to a reform theme.

115.     The value of prior strong analytical work. In preparing private sector development and
financial sector policy-based operations, the Bank should lead with its comparative advantage which
i s bringing to bear strong technical capacity and ample cross-country experience. The Borrower must
be confident that its dialogue with the Bank will add value to its own efforts. The Bank has
maintained a very fruitful dialogue with the GoC during the current CAS period leading to the
preparation of analytical work that has assisted Colombia in the definition of the reform program
supportedby this DPL Program (see paragraph 119below).

116.     The needto take intoaccountthe politicaleconomyof reform.Indesigning apolicy-based
operation, political factors and the legislative needs of the country needto be understood and included
in the dialogue with the client. The broad consultations that have been carried out by the Government
in the context of the negotiations of the FTA have facilitated reaching the consensus necessary to
enact reforms, particularly inthe five areas covered by this operation.

117.     The commitment of the Government is central in ensuringthe success of the program.
Previous policy-based and investment operations all point to the important role of strong government
leadership in their success. The proposed operation effectively responds to the GoC's own priorities
and commitment as reflected in the actions already taken as well as the fact that the policy thrust
supported by this operation lies directly along the key policy axes of the Government's National
Development Plan.

118.     Coordination with other internationalfinancial institutions, such as the IMF and the
IADB, is critical to ensure a consistent policy approach towards borrowers. This proposed
operation builds on close collaboration with the IMF on overall economic policy advice and the
Financial Sector Assessment Program (FSAP) as well as with the IADB, which has been conducting
dialogue with the GoC in the area of competitiveness. Furthermore, USAID,has also been providing
substantial support to the GoC in all areas related to the implementation of the FTA.

127.       Colombia's government has been working to establish a comprehensive and coherent
competitivenesspolicy, along with the institutionalframework to implement it. The issuance of
CONPES policy directive 3439 in August 2006 established the bases for the development of a
productivity and competitiveness policy, includingthe broad outlines of the institutionalframework to

purposes" for obtaining credit reports or credit data from a credit bureau.55On balance, however, the
bill addresses many of the existing problems in the legal and institutional framework and it is
expectedto provide a boost to the activities of the credit reporting industry.

CapitalMarkets

139.    The second DPL supports GoC's advances in issuing important regulations to
implement the new securities markets law, and improve the functioning of its money market.
The new securities markets law provided the framework for significant improvements in the
functioning of the domestic capital markets, by redefining the scope of supervisory powers,
intermediaries regulation, and increased investor protection. The new law sets the basis for
minimizing systemic risks by introducing the notion of finality in transactions settlement and the
central risk counterpart. The impact of the law depends to a significant extent on the way it is
regulated and implementedby the authorities, a process to be accompanied by the Banks operations.

140.    The GoC has adopted new regulations for Public Tender Offers (Ofertus Publicus de
Adquisicion, called hereafter OPAs), with the aim of promoting more active business
acquisitions involving a publicly listed company. Decree 1941-2006 increased the ceiling for
maximum stake prior to formal OPA from 10 percent to 25 percent of the voting shares; the prior
threshold, deemed too low, does not even give the right to a seat in the board of directors. The new
decree also requires more disclosure in the Memorandumof Information, and gives target company's
shareholdersa much longer period of time to decide whether or not to accept the offered terms (10-30
working days instead of one morning session in the exchange). This i s in line with other international
practice (minimum 10 days in France, 20 days in the US). The decree also regulates better the process
for competing offers, and to prevent defensive value-destruction by the target company. Contrary to
some international practice, GoC did not adopt a "mandatory tender offer rule", deemed too




                                                   44

e~pensive~~.Thenew decree does though have a sell-out rule, whereby if one shareholder has more
than 90 percent of existing shares, remaining shareholders have the right to sell their shares at the
same price (but they are not obliged to, Le. there is no squeeze-outrule).

141.     The Colombian government is promoting a significantly wider scope of Self Regulation
in capital markets activities, to all types of financial intermediariesand instruments; this could
reduce substantially regulatory arbitrage risks and lead to higher standards in investor
protection. Only BVC had a SRO (self-regulatory organization) approach so far; but as the recently
approved decree 1565-2006 gets implemented over the coming year or two, it is expected that there
will be a shift to a stand-alone Self Regulation body with a much larger mandate. A FIRST-financed
activity is helping Colombians choose among the different possible models of SROs, and industry as
well as regulators' inclination seems to favor one single entity as opposed to one entity per type of
financial intermediary and per exchange. As it stands, BVC's SRO only regulates brokers and the
exchange. The new SRO will have regulation, inspection, and sanction powers applicable to all FIs
(financial institutions) that sell securities, funds or derivatives to a client base, as well as to all
organized exchanges (except commodities exchanges) and OTC (over-the-counter) platforms. Among
the advantages of this model, harmonized rules, comprehensive coverage of capital markets activities,
and cost efficiency could be achieved. However, weaknesses of this model could include reduced
expertise of the SRO in each specific type of FIor market, and marginalization of independent FIs as
opposedto those conglomerates.

142.     The GoC has introduced the figure of "customer advocate" in capital markets
intermediaries, thus generalizing the coverage of this scheme; its effectiveness will depend on
the quality of implementation by private intermediaries. Decree 4759/2005 complements the
coverage of the "defensor del cliente" function, which was initially introduced in 2003 for all

Preventionof MoneyLaundering(ML) andFinancingof Terrorism(FT)

153.     Adopting a comprehensivelegislation defining and criminalizingacts of terrorism and
the financing of terrorism is a critical step for Colombia in its efforts to combat crime and
fulfill its internationalcommitments. In 2005, the Cornisidn de Coordinacio'n Interinstitucional
pura el Control del Lavado de Activos - CICLA has set up a working group in charge of preparing a
Project Law for the prevention, detection and investigation of the financing of terrorism.64 This
multidisciplinary working group, composed of representatives from the different ministries with a
stake in the financing of terrorism, received support from a Bank technical team that provided
technical assistance on the criminal aspects of the Project Law and in ensuring consistency with
relevant international standards and domestic law in force. The Project Law was presented to the
Congress on December 19, 2005 and is currently going through the different sessions of Congress for
final approval (the last sessionof Congress i s scheduledfor December 2006).

154.     With the adoption of this Project Law, Colombia will have addressed one of the main
weaknesses of its AML/CFT legalframework, as highlightedin the 2004 GAFISUD AML/CFT
Assessment Report, and complied with its international commitments (United Nations
Resolutions and FATF 9 Special Recommendations on the financing of terrorism). National
authorities will be given the legal and operational means to combat efficiently the financing of
terrorism. Indeed, financial institutions and designated non financial businesses and professions will
have to adopt preventive measures and report to the UIAF suspicious activities related to terrorist
financing; the UIAF will have the legal basis to investigate those reports, and where suspicions are
grounded, to disseminate its findings to law enforcement agencies. The Project Law will also greatly
facilitate both national and international cooperation and the exchange of information. National
authorities will be able to provide mutual legal assistance to foreign countries in criminal
investigations, criminal proceedings and extradition proceedings for terrorist financing offenses. In
addition, the new CFT legal framework will allow domestic authorities to sign memorandums of
understanding for the exchange of information with their foreign counterparts.

resourcesand personnelstructure of the UIAFto achieveitsassignedtask. Over the last year, the
GoC has taken significant actions to address this issue and made major improvements to the

RISKS AND RISK MITIGATION
185.       As was the case for the first operation, the proposed loan is subject to moderate risk. In
spite of the fact that all prior actions required for this loan were achieved as originally envisaged and
despite overcoming the risks associated with political uncertainty produced by the general elections
that took place in May 2006, progress of the overall program i s subject to moderate risks, with the
remainingrisks being generally the same as they have been since the beginningof the program.

186.       The proposed operation supports an institutional development effort widely perceived
as necessary and which the Government is already actively implementing. Therefore, there i s a
small risk that the actions requiredfor presentation to the Executive Board are not taken and that the
indicative triggers for the preparation of the next (third) operation are not reached within the twelve
months following the effectiveness of this proposed operation. However, there are still some risks
associated with the effective implementation of the institutional and regulatory reforms supported by
the proposed loan that are essentially the same as they were since the beginningof the program.

187.       There is the risk that effective implementation of mechanisms for inter-institutional
collaboration in support of entrepreneurial activities will be hampered by lack of coordination
among agencies. Given the muti-sector nature of reforms aiming to improve competitiveness, success
requires close coordination among multiple agencies. This risk i s mitigated by the strong commitment

                                                              57

of the Government to the implementation of the competitiveness and enhancement policies supported
by this program, as demonstrated by steps already taken to streamline exporting and improve the
business environment and the creation of the National Competitiveness Commission -a coordination
mechanism among government agencies that also includes the private sector.

188.    There is the risk that the impact of some of the measures supported by the Program
may not be felt duringthe life of the DPL Program. For example, increasing access to financial
services by the poor and MSMEs has proved to be very difficult in most developing countries. The
proposed operation would support some initial actions towards this objective but the full impact of
these reforms may not be felt in the short term. Similarly, there may be some delay before the full
impact of a better business environment is reflectedin increasedeconomic activity.

189.    And finally,there is a riskthat Colombia's currentlevel of public debt couldpose a risk
to the country's macroeconomic stability. On the basis of the developed sustainability analysis
undertaken, levels appear to be manageablein the near term, even when consideringpossible adverse
economic shocks. And the debt level has fallen from a peak of 54 percent of GDP in 2002 to about
43.5 percent of GDP in early 2006. But current levels remain high, imposing a heavy fiscal cost and
exposing the country to financial vulnerabilities, particularly if the favorable external environment
were to deteriorate of if more permanent reforms to address budget rigidities were not to be taken by
the GoC.

                              Translation Letter of Development Policy

Mr.PaulWolfowitz

WORLDBANK

Washington, D.C.


Dear Mr.Wolfowitz:

The Government led by President Alvaro Uribe, cognizant of the needto continue policies and actions
aimed at promotingColombia's full and effective integration into the global economy, i s committed to
pursuing the series of reforms under the Programmatic Business Productivity and Efficiency Loan.
This program loan covers new actions for its second phase, scheduled to be implemented in the
second half of 2006.

Support from multilateral financial institutions, particularly the World Bank, is of great importance to
the nation in opening markets and increasing competitiveness, not simply owing to the resources
channeled toward such initiatives, but also to the technical assistance provided, which allows the
country to benefit from the Bank's experience in other countries undergoingsimilar processes.

The most important actions of this second phase of the program will thus be focused on strengthening
and complementing the processes undertaken by the Government in the following areas: quality, the
domestic competitiveness agenda, financial oversight, and access to financing and innovation policies,
among others. These actions will strengthenenterprise and financial sector development, with a view
to expanding efficient access to capital by productive sectors and, in so doing, improving their
competitiveness on international markets.

The Government of Colombia has shown its commitment to enhancing the economy's
competitiveness inthe National Development Plan (NDP), approved under Law 812 of June 26,2003.
The Plan promotes a competitiveness strategy critical to the country's economy in a globalized world.
The NDPtherefore defines competitiveness as "the ability of a country to create value-added, thereby
increasing national wealth, and maintaining and increasing its participation in international markets
while raising the standardof living of its people." More specifically, the NDPidentifies the following
priority areas:
(i) eliminating bureaucratic procedures and coordinating activities, (ii) state's coordinating role,
                                                                          the
(iii)intellectual property, (iv) the connectivity agenda, (v) biotechnology, (vi) tourism, (vii) market
efficiency, and (viii) access to information and communication technologies.

Under the new administration of re-elected President Uribe, the continuity of the National
Development Plan and its policies i s guaranteed for another governmental term. In this regard, the
Government i s working on developing the new plan, which will include complementary policies and
additional resources to implement actions in those sectors that are most important to the country's
competitiveness and productivity.

It should be emphasized that the country's macroeconomic stability in recent years will be taken into
account in formulating both the new National Development Plan and the policies derivedfrom it. The
Government of Colombia successfully devised and implemented an economic program aimed at
strengtheningpublic finances, the first fruits of which were seen in 2004, before becoming even more
evident in 2005. The program's main objectives are the gradual stabilization of public debt and the


                                                   67

reduction of the fiscal deficit to ensurethat public finances are structurally sustainable by focusing on
reducing current and future expenditure and increasing fiscal revenue. The fiscal situation has
improved significantly, as seen in figures for recent years: the consolidated public sector deficit stood
at 1.3 percent of GDP in 2004, while fiscal equilibrium was achieved in 2005. Inboth instances, this
performance well exceeds the targets agreedupon with the InternationalMonetary Fund.

During the first half of 2005, Congress enacted a number of laws that have helped bolster the
Government's economic program. The result has been higher levels of national and international
confidence, which have ensured macroeconomic stability. The laws approved guarantee both market
transparency and a reduction of potentialfinancial risks for Colombia (Acto Legislativo de Pensiones)
[Pensions Act]. As a result, an environment favorable to both local and foreign investment has been
created, thereby guaranteeing sustained growth of the Colombian economy.           In2005, the domestic
public debt market was characterized by a downward trend in securities interest rates. This trend
continued during the initial months of 2006, when public debt securities attained the lowest ever
recorded negotiated rate (7.11 percent for treasury securities - TES, July 2020). At the moment, after
a brief period of reversal of this trend owing to uncertainty generatedby some areas of performance of
the United States economy in international and local capital markets, rates remain stable and are
consistent with the trend observed in 2005.

The administration has, however, been working in other areas. With the firm objective of fostering
the country's economic growth, negotiations have been concluded for the Free Trade Agreement with
the United States in order to strengthen export activity and improve foreign investment potential in
the country in the years ahead. Implementing all these legislative amendments and developing the
economic plan to strengthen public finances have produced results. The rewards of this labor were
reaped in 2005, with excellent results including GDP growth of 5.2 percent, higher than the average
growth rate of the major economies of the region. Inflation also continues to decline and stood at 4.85
percent in 2005, thereby attainingthe inflation target set by the Banco de la Repu'blica.

Given the 5.2 percent rise in GDP during the first quarter of the year, prospects for growth in 2006
continue to be bright. Moreover, in 2006, greater consumer confidence and the upward trend in
investment (which grew by 25 percent in 2005) provide reason for optimism regarding the positive
impact of domestic demand on GDP growth. Additionally, the favorable economic growth prospects
of our major trading partners will facilitate greater external demand, together with its positive impact
on exports and production growth. Strong performance of real activity has also led to a strengthened
financial sector, which attained cumulative profits of $1,714.5 billion in July 2006. Another of the
most positive aspects reflectingthe revitalization of the financial system i s the sustained growth of the
credit portfolio, which in July 2006 posted an annualized growth of 26.7 percent.




                                                    68

Despite the efforts of recent years, the Government i s aware of certain institutional weaknesses. The
taxation structure is extremely complex, with high levels of taxation on investment that constitute a
disincentive for risk-taking in Colombia. Similarly, institutional factors impede the development of
financial markets and the access to these services by much of the population. These concerns are
shared by many local economic actors and are highlighted in comparative international reports as the
most formidable obstacles to improving the country's competitiveness and growth. In light of the
foregoing, the Government will be taking the following three-pronged proposal to Congress: (i)
taxation reform, (ii) financial legislation reform, and (iii)establishment of a system for transferring
resources to the regions that promotes macroeconomic stability.

As shown in the Fiscal Framework, the Government has a robust and consistent medium - term
financial strategy that targets sustainability in public finances by generating primary balances to
contain the net indebtednessof the nonfinancialpublic sector at 30 percent of GDP by 2015.

The Second Programmatic Business Productivity and Efficiency Loan attests to the Government's
commitment to continue these policies. This continuity has been demonstrated in the World Bank's
participation not only through actions in this program, but also through technical support in the
country's competitiveness strategy by financing important investment projects including: agricultural
transition, port and infrastructure studies, modernization of public financial administration, and higher
education - improving access.        In addition, the Bank financed studies that helped guide the
Government in decision-making and the formulation of competitiveness policies.

The programinquestion focuses on supporting two broadpolicy areas. The first comprises actions to
strengthen the Government's strategy for competitiveness and productivity. The Government's work
in regional formulation and coordination of the national productivity and competitiveness agenda has
been of great importance in defining commercial and productive priorities. In this respect, the
program comprises the drafting and approval of a Consejo Nacional de Politica Econdmica y Social,
CONPES [National Council on Social and Economic Policy] document whose primary aim i s to
define the main policy elements related to competitiveness. Inaddition, issuinga Presidential Decree
establishing the Comisidn Nacional de Competitividad [National Competitiveness Commission] as the
competent institution to coordinate the implementation of the policies outlined in the CONPES
document i s underconsideration.

Establishing the Sistema Nacional de Calidad [National Quality System] through a CONPES
document setting forth the policy guidelines on quality in Colombia is another initiative supported by
this program. Further, establishing an independent accrediting and metrology entity that centralizes
the system's actions will lead to greater international recognition of the quality of national products.
Lastly, the importance attached to the theme of quality has been reflected in the provision of
significant resources to the Office of the Superintendent of Industry and Commerce, whose work on
metrology issues is critical to achieving the Government's commitment of gaining greater access to
internationalmarkets.

Finally, the theme of competitiveness and productivity i s being supported by evaluating current
science and technology innovationinitiatives as a starting point for developing a policy inthis area, to
which the Government attaches vital importance in its bid to enhance the competitiveness of the
productive sectors.

The second major set of policies focuses on actions for preparing and outfitting the financial sector to
implement and support actions related to competitiveness. In recent years, the Colombian financial
sector has provided quality indicators and portfolio coverage and achieved profitability and capital
strength, which are at unprecedented levels and reflect stability and strength, and shown real

                                                   69

prospects for future growth. While this positive trend in financial sector indicators has been
accompanied by increasing profits particularly over the last four years, at end-June 2006 it had
declined, owing in large part to lower earnings associated with investment ratings. However, it is
hoped that this trend will be reversed and that the various groups of financial entities will continue to
generateprofits.

The foregoing is complemented by a series of actions designed to enhance financial oversight. A
comparison of the Colombian financial markets revealed inefficiencies attributable to various factors.
One such factor is the regulatory and oversight system for financial markets which had an adverse
effect on regulatory and supervision arbitrage owing to differentiated treatment of agents who were
performing very similar activities. The second cause relates to the issue of overlapping supervisory
efforts by the State, revealing the pitfalls of overseeing the financial groups as a whole. The final
reason pertains to the merging of almost all the entities overseen by the former Office of the
Superintendent of Banks with the securities market to create issuers of securities that invest and
participate actively in this market as securities intermediaries.

Inlight of the foregoing, it was necessaryto conduct a clear review of both the financial oversight
system and the formulation of actions to integrate oversight of intermediate and non-intermediate
markets, with a view to establishing a consolidated oversight system that offers benefits associated
with economies of scale and scope for intermediaries, and for the State itself with respect to its
oversight of the financial markets.

As a result, the Offices of the Superintendent of Banks and of Securities were merged to form the
current Superintendencia Financiera de Colombia S.A. [Financial Office of the Superintendent of
Colombia]. The Colombian financial market now has a single supervisory entity that i s currently
beingstrengthenedand faces challenges in carrying out consolidated supervision. Cognizant of these
difficulties, the national government i s currently conducting a series of activities designed to
formulate appropriate oversight plans and procedures to counter the risks faced by financial entities,
as well as processes to develop the technical skills of its human resources to meet the needs of the
new entity, and, generally speaking, to develop infrastructure that will enable them to perform
adequately their oversight function.

Moreover, it is essentialthat the Government keep the legal framework of the financial sector updated
and adapted to the changes taking place in the productive sector in the area of liberalization and
market access. This effort, in particular activities involving the financial sector, i s the result of
ongoing work to revise, harmonize, and enhance the tools being advocated for the development of the
intermediate and non-intermediate markets, whether through the implementation and regulation of
standards to promote new operations, the establishment of clear operating rules among the various
market stakeholders, or the alignment of prudential regulation with international standards.

Although a significant number of administrative initiatives within this group of reforms have been
taken in that direction, it is important to single out those measures relating to the transfer of some of
the assets of financial entities, and to establish the special procedure for moving this process forward
and defining the transparency, effectiveness, and efficiency requirements they must meet, as well as
the obligation to guaranteebroad competition and advertising. Along these same lines, and within the
context of the stabilization and reorganization of state-owned banks which established that the State
would have a single credit entity at the helm of the Banco Agrario de Colombia S.A., the plan for
dissolving and liquidating the Banco del Estado S.A.as well as the transfer schedules for shares held
by a number of public entities in GRANBANCO,currently beingprivatized, were drafted.

In the context of financial sector reforms, mention should also be made of another series of policy
actions designed to develop the financial market. These actions are as follows:

                                                     70

First, with respect to the policy regarding access to credit and other financial services, limited
financial deepening, which refers to access to various financial services, i s attributed to operative
costs, inefficient processes, and, generally speaking, uncertainty about the ability of beneficiaries to
repay these loans, and the effectiveness of existing collection mechanisms in the Colombian policy
framework.     Consequently, once the current restrictions for accessing these services have been
defined and in an effort to promote successfully a policy that encouragesaccess to credit and the other
financial services in a manner that is viable, broad-based, and independent of state subsidies, the
"Bank of Opportunities" was established and, with a view to making this project feasible, the
Investment Bank Opportunities program was created.

Generally speaking, the policy elements that will be used to provide incentives for access to financial
services and promote all the instruments used by households and companies to conduct financial and
economic transactions are as follows:

(a) Reform of the regulatory framework in the areas identified as impediments to access to the
financial systemand to financing in general.

(b) Promotion strategies and incentives for the adequate, timely, and appropriate provision of
information pertaining to microfinance issues to provide access to collateral to the population
segments targeted by the project, and promote financial education programs on supply and demand,
among others.

(c) Agreements with entities that carry out microfinancing activities to support and promote wider
coverage, as well as the designand introduction of new financial products for the populationsegments
targetedby this program.

A series of actions that must be carried out in an efficient and effective manner to make this project
operational and enable low-income households, as well as micro, small, and medium-sized businesses
and entrepreneurs to access the formal financial sector has therefore been designed.                The
establishment of banking correspondentsto assist the public with deposit operations, transactions and
payments, and credit disbursementsi s a step inthis direction.

Moreover, and in keeping with the same objective of promoting access to credit, other actions such as
the rules and regulations of Law 920 of 2004 on the support capacity for the establishment of
specialized savings and loan sections within the Cajas de Compensacidn Familiar [Compensation
Associations], the minimum solvency ratio for autonomous funds, decentralized agencies, and
liquidity funds have been carried out.

Lastly, there i s a requirement for the publication of information related to open credit and debit card
payment systems to promote competition among the lending institutions that issue and accept bank
cards, and protect clients and users of the financial system, by providing adequate and timely
information to users of this service, thereby facilitating appropriate comparison of the financial
conditions offered on the market for bank cards.

With regard to access to capital markets and the efficiency of the secondary market, after lengthy
efforts by the national government to obtain a law that regulates activities pertaining to the
management, use, and investment of public resources carried out through securities, Law 964 of 2005,
the Law of the Securities Market, was enacted. This law provides the Colombian securities market
with an appropriate and effective regulatory framework, and mainly, with a clear performance
framework for all the participants in the market, with the legal security necessary to bring together
domestic and foreign investors, and with the flexibility required to facilitate alignment of regulations

                                                   71

with ongoing market innovations. In order to bring it in line with the provisions included in the
market securities law, the regulatory process of this law was conducted and many of its regulatory
decrees were issued.

Given the new requirements of and developments in the financial markets, initiating financial reform
was necessary in order to eliminate barriers that impede further development of the financial markets
and enhance the effectiveness of the system. To that end, a review of the operating rules pertainingto
the financial system structure was deemed necessary, that is, those rules that seek to identify the
classes of financial institutions, the regulations governing their authorized operations, and generally

               ANNEX 5: COLOMBIA -DEBT SUSTAINABILITY ANALYSIS

Debt Sustainability Analysis. Colombia's present level of net of external assets public debt, at
about 42.8 percent of GDP (down from a peak of 54 percent in 2002) remains relatively high.
But it appears manageable in the near term even when considering possible adverse economic
shocks. Many recognize (including the government) that debt needs to decline further as a share
of GDP, due to its high fiscal cost and the financing vulnerabilities to which it exposes the
county. The government's target i s to reduce the level of debt to 38 percent of GDPby 2015, but
given the favorable external environment and the re-scheduling of the debt they could easily
reach a target of 30 percent of GDP. We analyze the debt prospects in four different ways: i)a
baseline projection with historical average values of key parameters; ii)an analysis of policy
responses that would be necessary for the government to meet the debt reduction target of 30
percent of GDP in the face of possible economic shocks; iii)projections of what would happento
debt ratios if economic shocks hit but there were no fiscal policy response, and iv) an analysis of
the sustainability of total public plus private external debt. (A full description of the analytical
work is available from the CMU).

The baseline scenario makes the following key assumptions:

         Historical averagereal GDP growth of 3.2 percent, which i s below last year's growth of
         5.1 percent
    0    A primary balance of 2.1 percent. Last year it was 5 percent, but this includes three
         percentagepoint surplus from subnational governments and public entities, which is not
         be readily available to service the debt
         30 percent depreciation of the real exchange rate, bringingit back to its historical
         average over a five year periodbeginningin 200582
         An average implicit real interest rate on domestic and external debt of 6.3 percent, which
         i s currently 6.4 percent.

Inthis scenario, the government achieves its target of reducingthe debt ratio to 38 percent inten
years.

The second and third sets of scenarios add three adverse shocks to this baseline: an immediate
depreciation of the real exchange rate by 30 percent in 2006 (instead of spread over 5 years), a
reduction of growth to -2.1 percent for 2006 (two standard deviations from the average),
gradually returning to its average by 2010; and a rise in the real interest rate to 8.6 percent in
2006, returning gradually to 6.3 percent by 2010. And there i s also the combination shock,
where all three adverse shocks occur. If the government still wants to meet the target debt ratio
of 30 percent of GDP for 2015, it will have to tighten fiscal policy from the baseline primary
balanceof 2.1 percent, up to 3.3 percent inthe case of the combination shock.




82 Since about half of Colombia's debt is foreign, mostly in US $, the exchange rate has a large and
instantaneousimpact on the debt ratio. In the decline of the debt ratio from its peak of 54 percent of GDP
in 2002 to 47.5 percent at the end of 2004, the 30 percent real appreciation of the exchange rate accounts
for six of the eight percentage points of decline. The exchange rate is unlikely to retain all of this recent
appreciation, so the government should continue with fiscal caution, despite the decline of the debt ratio.

Analyzing the sustainability of the external debt requires a model that incorporates another set of
key macroeconomic variables, such as Colombia's current account, and allows us to model
another set of different shocks, particularly one to inward international capital flows. Total
external debt service (public and private) remains one of the highest levels, as a percentage of
exports of goods, services, and income, in Latin America. It has fallen from a high of above 67
percent in 2002 to more manageable levels of less than 40 percent now, but it i s still just below
Argentina's 42 percent.

Nonetheless, Colombia's total external debt profile (public and private) i s likely to be
sustainable. Total external debt i s expected to decline to 24.2 percent of GDPunder our baseline
scenario. The primary risk associated with external debt sustainability i s exchange rate risk. A
depreciation of the real exchange rate in excess of 30 percent within one year would increasethe
total external debt to GDP ratio from about 28.2 percent currently, to over 41.1 percent but this i s
unlikely to generatea debt crisis,. Total external debt reached nearly 48 percent in 2001 without
a crisis, in an economic environment that was less favorable than today. A transitory peak of
41.1 percent total external debt would put pressure on financing, but is expected to be
manageable.
