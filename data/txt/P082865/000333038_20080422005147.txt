1,Program Context,DevelopmentObjectivesand Design

This Implementation Completion Report (ICR) describes the results of a series of three projects-
PLaRSSAL I, PLaRSSAL 11, and LaRSDPL 111 - together comprising a programmatic lending
operation spanning the four years of the first Uribe Administration. The results of the first two
projects were presented in Simplified ICRs; the results will be briefly reviewed in this document,
as well, in the context of describing the program results. The last project will be described in
more detail in this document as will the full program results.

8.1 Context at Appraisal

Appraisal of the first operation in the series was July 23,2003.

Macroeconomic context

Beginning in 2001, the economy was in recovery after suffering a severe crisis in 1999/2000.
Tine turnaround was partly due to the much improved global economic environment: world
growth accelerated, the cost of international credit fell, and the prices of Colombia's primary
expork rose.        Domestic factors, particularly the improved security situation and stable
macroeconomic polices, also drove the country's recovery. At the time of appraisal, improving
conditions were reflected by an almost 4 percentage point increase in annual private investment
as a share of GDP and real economic growth of 1.9 percent in 2002 and 3.9 percent in 2003. In
spite of these advances, some persistent problems remained. Colombia's unemployment rate was
17 percent in 2002, which was a drop of 3 percentage points from the crisis, but still double the
historic levels of 8 percent. Inflation was 7.0 percent in 2002 and only fell to 6.5% in 2003 (see
Table 1).

Poverty and Inequality

Poverty and inequality continue to be major challenges for Colombia in spite of a long history of
solid growth and stable democratic institutions. Consistei~teconomic growth thou&out the
1980s and early 1990s lifted many out of poverty, but the macroeconomic crisis at the end of the
1390s eradicated more than a decade of progress. By 2002, when GDP growth rates resumed, 58
percent of the population was living below the poverty line' and 20 percent were considered
extreme poor.2 In rural areas, poverty rates reached 75 percent. Colombia also has one of the
most unequal income distributions in the world - the poorest 20 percent of the population
receives just 3 percent of the nation's incomeO3




Social Protection

Colombia's success in avoiding systemic macroeconomic shocks dl-g                              most of its modern
history hindered the creation of an integrated social protection system. Instead, programs were
developed in response to immediate needs of the heterogeneous population, resulting in a
fragmented social protection system with a wide range of institutions at the national, territorial,
and !ocal levels; a hodgepodge of public and private strategies; and an absence of cohesive
policies, clearly defined objectives, adequate targeting, results management, and efficient
financing schemes. Despite significant efforts during the 1990s to increase coverage of social
protection and assistance programs, by 2002, there remained deficits in coverage, access, and
quality of services for the ill, the vulnerable, those who become unemployed or need to improve
their skills for greater success in the job market, and for the poor.

The Government of Colombia's (GoC) long-term vision at the time of program Appraisal (and
continuing through the period of this ICR) is to close the social and regional gaps so that all
Colombians have access to high quality basic services and protection against key social and
economic risks that would alIow them to carry out activities necessary for their personal and
social development.          This would be achieved by building a social protection system that
contributes to strengthening and protecting human capital and household income, giving effective
support in situations of crisis, and helping poor and vulnerable familiesn4

supervision among a diverse portfolio of ECD programs and approaches, with poor targeting,

Health

Until 1993, Colombia had a health system which, like most in Latin America, relied on publicly
operated facilities to provide services to the uninsured population, which encompassed most of
Colombia's poor.6 This system was failing to meet the needs of poor people, as demonstrated by
low health services utilization and access figures by the early 1990s.

In 1993, the GoC initiated a shift toward a system that would utilize public funds to provide
Subsidized Health Insurance to poor people by providing them with an entitlement to a specified
list of services while workers in the formal sector - independent of poverty status - would pay
into a Contributory Health Insurance Regime via payroll taxes, under the hypothesis that
insurance-based entitlements are a more effective means to ensure access to health services,
especially in highly resource-constrained                        The new structure also promoted efficiency
because health care providers must provide services to people in order to receive payment from
insurers. While the new system created the means to ensure access (insurance) and efficiency
(output based reimbursement), impact on utilization, and hence health outcomes, relies on the
breadth of insurance coverage. As of 2002, only 55 percent of the population had health
insurance, a decrease from 61 percent in 1998.8

Immunization coverage, a key public health issue and a fundamental measure to prevent poverty-
inducing illness, has been a challenge for Colombia. In 1995/6, vaccination coverage rates were
above 90 percent for all key vaccines, thus providing "herd immunity" so that if a person did
contract an illness, a sufficient amount of the population was vaccinated to prevent an epidemic.
However, between 1996 and 2001, average coverage rates ranged between 72 and 77 percent,
putting Colombia at a serious public health risk. The dramatic fall in immunization rates was
attributed to inadequate funding and organizational changes associated with decentralization of
the health sector, as well as the shift to an insurance-based system that generated uncertainties
about responsibilities for implementing and overseeing various components of the immunization
program.

Labor and Training

When the Uribe Administration took office in 2002, economic growth was positive, but low, and
the unemployment rate was near 17 percent. Women and youth were hit particularly hard by the
recession-induced unemployment, as labor participation of these groups increased to compensate
for the loss in family income, when household heads lost theirjobs.' Safety nets for unemployed
workers were absent, increasing the hardship of being unemployed.                  Programs to help the
unemployed reintegrate into the labor market were antiquated, inefficient, and insufficient.

In the long run, the vision is to reform legislation to reduce disincentives to employment,
accompanied by a series of social protection schemes to protect the unemployed and to improve
the employability of hard-to-employ groups (youth, women, unskilled). This entails identifying
and modifying those regulations that prevent employers from hiring, building a portfolio of labor-
related social protection schemes, designing and implementing the National System for Job
Training (SNFT, Sistema Nacional de Formacidn para el Trabajo), to increase the quality and
supply of training opportunities, and to expand the apprenticeship program to facilitate labor
force entry for the young.

Education

Despite significant increases in education spending, the implementation of decentralization
policies in the 1990s, and increasing coverage of education, enrollments at tha primary and
secondary school levels are not yet universal. The average educational level of Colombians 15
year and older increased from 6.4 in 1992 to 7.6 in 2003, but significant regional disparities exist:
in regional capitals, the average level of schooling is 8.5 years, but only five years in the rest of
the country. There is gender parity between men and women. The schooling level of the adult
Colombian population continues to lag behind comparator countries in the region.

In terms of school completion, 2003 data revealed that only 61 percent of children who start
primary school finish the fifth grade, while 57 of those who begin secondary school finish ninth
grade and only 35 percent complete eleventh grade. As with other education indicators, large
differences are seen not only between public and private schools, but also across rural and urban
areas.

Despite these statistics, human development remains a high priority for Colombia, as indicated by
the government's strategy to improve people's education and skills formation (and through that,
people's productivity and incomes). The long term vision is that 100 percent of Colombians will
complete primary and secondary education and have the right skills to be internationally
competitive in the labor market and earn a good income.

M~niloring~Evaluation, and Social Oversight in the Social Sectors

To increase efficiency in social expenditures,it is necessary to monitor program expenditures an.d
outputs, carry-out impact evaluation, and publicly debate these findings within the Government
and with civil society. In 1993, the National Planning Department (DNP) established a unit to
focus on monitoring and evaluation (M&E) of public programs, with the objective of collecting
monitoring indicators from all public programs. In 2001 the GoC agreed to invest in a serious
impact evaluation program to determine the effectiveness of three emergency social programs,
thus launching the process to create, understand, and use impact evaluation as a public policy tool.

Citizen's oversight in Colombia can be strengthened to provide policymakers with the public's
feedback in order to better provide the services that their constituents want. In the past, some
Govement processes were open for public discussion, perhaps most importantly, the
Constition of 1991 went through such a process, but this was an exception, rather than a rule.
Little information was produced for public consumption, even though well organized sectorally-
specificcivil society groups have existed for years.

The long term vision is to instill a culture of M&E that will lead to a more efficient and results-
oriented government that responds to the needs of its citizens. This requires the creation of
publicly-shared,easily accessible,and simply presented information about the public institutions
and their objectives, responsibilities, missions, goals, results, and impacts of their programs and
policies. It also requires the creation of an environment and tools where citizensand Government
can share informationand exchangeviews.

Rationale for Bank Assistance

Tine Bank's engagement with the social and labor sectors began in the Pastrana Administration
(1998-2002) and strengthened through the fust Uribe Administration (2002-2006).                The
collaboration was initiated durin the 1998-1999 economic crisis via Bank support to emergency
programs - Farnilfasen Accidn70and Empleo en ~ccidn"- to address income loss caused by
high unemployment (about 20 percent in urban areas in September 1999) and lack of social
protection programs ltbr the unemployed, as well as analytical sector work. A sectoral adjustment
loan (SECK, approved August 1, 2002) was the Bank's fust initiative in the policy side of the
social sectors and served to bridge the Bank's dialogue on social and labor sectors from the
Pastrana to the Uribe Govement.

Shortly after the Uribe Government took office in August 7, 2002, the economy had started to
recover %om the deep recession of 199912000 and the government focused on putting in place
structural reforms in the labor and social sectors to increase employment and improve efficiency
in social spending, particularly health and education. Concurrently, the Bank prepared a two-
operation Programmatic Labor and Social Structural Adjustment Loan (PLaRSSAL I, approved
September 2003 and PLaRSSAL11, approved November 2004) and closely aligned the operation
with the GoC7s goals to support the expansion of social services in a fiscally constrained
environmentby increasingefficiency in social and labor programs and creating a more conducive
policy environment. A Technical Assistance Loan (TAL - approved November 2004) was also
prepared to support the implementation of the PLaRSSAL program, with a focus on regulating
and implementing reforms in the labor, social expenditure, sub-national social protection, social

This component has two overarching sub-themes: institutional reform and increasing access to
social protectio~services (Table 2). The key institutional reforms identified in the program, and
carried out by the clients, were the creation of the Ministry of Social Protection- by the merging
of the Ministries sf Labor and Health - and the creation of information tools to better monitor
delivery of services by the MSP and to increase MSP revenuesthrough a reduction in evasion of
taxes earmarked for the social sectors. The institutional reforms are allowing for a more
comprehensivepackage of social protection by housing in one institution all the key public sector
programs to insure the population against key risks - old age, health, and occupational hazards-
and by simultanesusly monitoring access to insurancefor the entire population, complementedby
a guarantee of hll funding for each. By the close of the program, more than 7 million people
were registered in the beneficiaries tracking system (RUAF) and all firms with more than 30
employees - equaling more than 29 million workers - were paying benefits for their workers
through a web-based payment system (PILA).

which also served as prior actions for the operation. One piece of legislation decreased the cost

2.2 Major Factors Affecting Implementation

The success of the program implementation has been due to the following main factors:

         Strong government commitment to the program from the initial design stage to
         implementation of the Bast operation.            During design, the government actively
         participated with Bank st& in the preparation of the policy matrix, the outcomes to be
         expected and the multi-year targets that the government considered able to achieve.
         During implementation, DNP and NMCP were key coordinating agencies of program
         activities to be implemented by other central government agencies.

         Strong Bank team tbat bad built trust and confidence during previous successful
         operations, such as the SECAL and social safety net investment operations (Familias en
         Accidn and Empkeo en Accidn), and high quality sector work on labor, health, education
         and social protection. Building on this, the Bank team was able to provide high value
         technical advice for better design and implementation of the program operations.

         Close alignment of program targets with targets of the National Development Plan.
         This was critical as the government was highly committed to meeting all the targets in the
         NDP and included necessary hnding for the program in the yearly budget laws.

         Strong commitment and participation of key sectoral agencies such as the Ministry of
         Social Protection, ICBF, SENA, and the Ministry of Education, all of which had a vital

         role in program implementation. Their participation in the design stage produced a more
         realistic program by recognizing the difficulties inherent to institutional change, the
         implementation of new social protection programs, and new processes to expand
         coverage within the tight budget environment.

         Long-termand continued Bank supportthrough sector work and project lending in
         related areas in social protectionand education all helped the successful implementation
         of the PLaRSSAL operation. For instance, a Technical Assistance Loan (TAL) and a
         Japanese Grant (PHRD) helped the MSP to identify critical areas of concern and action,
         and sustain its efforts.    Also, the Social Safety Net loan (Loan No. 73371, which
         supported the Fanzilias en Accidn program, had a component to help the MSP design and
         implement its monitoring and evaluation functions, in close coordination with the
         Evaluation and Public Planning Department @EPP) of D M .

         Strong focus on outcomes and results and information systems to provide timely
         information on progress toward results. This allowed the government and the Bank to
         track progress and quickly identify issues that needed corrective action.

         The main risks identified in the Project Documents for each operation did not
         materialize. The internal violence has reduced as indicated by sharp declines in
         kidnappings, homicides and the large scale demobilization of self-defense groups. The
         external factors have been favorable to the economy which has grown rapidly, especially
         after 2003, resulting in reduced fiscal deficit, additional revenues to support social
         expending and lower unemployment. Regarding the specific risks to labor and social
         reform, the government was able to put together a highly competent team in the different
         institutions carrying out the reform process, supported with technical assistance from the
         Bank, IDB, CAF, and UN which has continued in the second administration of President
         Uribe. Finally, and because of positive impacts of the labor law18and the skillful
         management of the process by the government, the labor reform was not reversed as
         some had feared when it was broughtfor review before Congress at the end of 2004.


2.3 Monitoringand Evaluation (M&E)Design, Implementationand Utilization:

Design

The program's M&E system had a simple design focusing on outcome and output indicators that
were easy to calculate witb reliable and existing information or with information to be collected
by the government's SIGOB system that tracks progress in the goals of the National Development
Plan. In fact, most of the program's indicators could be tracked witb information published by
SIGOB on the D M web page.

The M&E system focused on following a few key indicators in each policy area to facilitate
tracking by both the Government and the Bank and to better identify issues that needed to be
addressed immediately. This design drew on lessons from the previous SECAL program, which
included a large number of indicators, some of which were difficult to track over the life of the
operation.

DNP and MHCP were charged with monitoring and evaluation of the whole program and for

program targets as they were rewarded for good performance with budget appropriations for the

Utilization

Because the Bank's financial assistance was closely aligned to the National Development Plan's
targets for 2002-2006, the GoC used the system to track progress for the PLaRSSALs 1& IIand

achieving targets in vaccination and affiliation of the poor to the subsidized health insurance
program, the MSP has received increased budget allocations for vaccinations and for affiliation to
the health insurance for the poor.

The GoC has used the results of impact evaluations to expand or reduce programs depending on
results. For instance, the highly favorable impact evaluation of Familias en Accion program led
the GoC to expand the program and allocate more funding (counting on two World Bank loans).

3. Assessment of Outcomes

3.1 Relevance of Objectives,Design and Implementation
(to current country and globalpriorities, and Bank assistance strategy)

ICRRating: High. This rating is justified for the following reasons:

The GoC's national development objective in the social and labor sectors was to promote higher
employment, increase human capital formation, and strengthen social protection, especially of the
poor and vulnerable. These objectives were all relevant and aimed at addressing major social
sector issues including: implementing programs to help people cope with the high levels of
unemployment resulting from the 1999 crisis, improving education levels that were lagging
relative to those of similar countries, and providing a better and more effective social protection
system to protect people from declines in employment, ill health and other idiosyncratic or
systemic economic shocks. By improving these investments, it is expected that Colombia will
begin to close the productivity gap between themselves and their international competitors, thus
being more preparedto participate in the global economy.

The above objectives, articulated in the National Development Plan 2002-2006, were to be
achieved through social reforms for more efficient and equitable use of social sector resources
(rather than through increased resources, as social spending has already grown rapidly in
Colombia as a result of decentralization), improved regulatory environment and better
institutional practices.

The implementation was successful due to careful upfront planning by the GoC in the design of
the reforms, a commitment for a change by the implementing agencies, and technical assistance
and funding from international partnersto ensure quality implementation.

Bank support for the program, considered a high priority in the 2002 Country Assistance Strategy
(CAS), and in the update of the CAS presented to the Board in 2005, was an appropriate and
timely response to the GoC's request for assistance to establish more solid conditions for the
expansion of employment, faster human capital accumulation, and better social protection of the
population, particularlyfor the poor and vulnerable.

6. Lessons Learned
(bothoperation-specific and of wide general application)

The lessons learned through the PLaRSSALILaRSDPLprogram operation were as follows:

        Constant engagement with the client is important via a rich technical dialogue and
        variedportfolio. The multi-sectoral PLaRSSAL program team worked with the GoC in a
        wide range of lending and non-lendingactivities, which facilitated the preparation of the
        program in two key ways. First, the GoC and Bank teams developed a good working
        relationship over many years.        Second, the Bank team, through this continued
        relationship was well versed in the objectives, strategies, results-management, and
        outputs of the social sectors, allowingfor a greater focus on strategy and implementation
        in the developmentof the program.

A commitment by the clients to implement a well-designed Government program. The
National Development Plan defined each social institution's strategic objectives for the
Uribe Administration, thereby providing a base upon which to design the Bank's program.
The social institutions were committed to implementing the strategies and meeting the
quantifiable targets and saw the value added of Bank support in the design,
implementation,and evaluation of the strategy.

A strong program of analytical work and dialogue is crucial to underpin the operation.
The PLaRSSALLaRSDPL program focused on policy and strategy, which required
knowledge about Colombia and experiences elsewhere to help operationalize the GoCYs
strategies. The Bank's knowledge was a crucial input to the more innovative strategies
that the Government implemented, particularly in the areas of social protection, labor
reform, training, M&E, health financing, and education service delivery to hard-to-reach
populations.

The results-management focus of the program was possible because of the existence of a
strong monitoring system and culture of results-based management. In 2002, the GoC
set quantifiable targets for the Administration, and a system (the SIGOB) to monitor
progress in reaching those targets, which permitted the Bank and GoC teams to measure
progress and quickly change processes that were not returning the desired results. It also
allowed for the Bank to better assess if the lines of action being supported through the
PLaRSSAL were the correct ones to improve the labor and social situation in Colombia

A multi-sectoral operation is manageable if     the Bank and the client are selective in the
choice of a few key strategies to support. While the PLaRSSALLaRSDPL program
operation covered many policy areas, it only supported a few key lines of action within
each policy area. The output of many of these actions could be quantified and tracked
over time, making the management less burdensome for the government and the Bank.
However, careful attention should be given to make sure that information on outcomes
and output indicators is available or easy to collect without the implementation of new
elaborate systems to collect such information.

A large Bank team with varied skills is necessaiy in a complex operation. The team for
the PLaRSSAL consists of economists, operatio~~sexperts, and sectoral specialists from a
wide range of sectors. This mix of skills allowed for quality work with the clients on
defining and advancing the sectoral strategies, but also for cross-fertilization between
sectors. The results were a more cohesive strategy that was not organized according to
Bank silos and the Bank team's contribution to encouraging partnerships across line
Ministries.

Strong coordinating institution on the Government's side is necessary when many line
ministries are involved in the operation. While the Bank team worked with the line
Ministries in the definition of the strategies to be supported by the PLaRSSAL program,
the DNP and MHCP were constant partners to manage the process on the Government

Annex 4. Summary of Borrower's ICR andlor Comments on Draft ICR

I.      Borrower's Summary of the program

The summary of the COC's ICR for the program operation is as follows (the full report in
Spanish is also attached):


    (i)     Assessment of the operation's objective, design, implementation, and
            operational experience

The Labor Reform and Social Development Policy program (PLaRSSAL I-II and LaRSDPL 111)
proposed to: (a) strengthen Colombia's social protection system and improve the delivery of
social services, (b) raise human capital formation, (c) improve employability, and (d) enhance
monitoring and evaluation systems for better transparency, social oversight, and results
management in the social sectors.

Each one of the objectives was achieved and the tools developed to achieving them have
contributed to attain great benefits to the social policy design and implementation in labor market,
health and education, among others, in Colombia. The program operation that initiated with
PLaRSSAL I-II and continued with LaRSDPL 111, was a key instrument for the implementation
and consolidation of initiatives in the National Development Plan, 2002-2006, as seen below.


    (ii)     Assessment of the outcome of the operation against the agreed objectives

The targets in the policy matrix and the outcomes achieved were as follows:

In social protection the objectives and outcomes achieved were:
        Creation of an effective authority charged with developing an effective social protection
        system to better protect the vulnerable population to social and economic risks. This was
        achieved through the creation of the Ministry of Social Protection (MSP) by merging the
        Ministries of Labor and Health in the context of the PLaRSSAL I and I1operations.
        Development of actions to reform social protection entities such as ICBF, SENA and
        Cajas de Compensacibn to improve targeting of nutrition programs, increase efficiency
        of SENA's training programs and implement social protection programs for the
        unemployed. These objectives were achieved by designing and implementing a targeting
        policy for ICBF and increasing the number of children in Desayunos Infantiles program
        from 78,000 in 2002 to over one million in 2006, more than four fold increase in
        graduates from SEBA, surpassing targets.
        In the context of LaRSDPL 111, the specific objectives included, i) issuing regulations to
        expand the coverage of social security system, ii) improve coordination of social
        programs by creating a single registry of beneficiaries of social programs (RUAF), and
        iii) lowering cost of paying payroll contributions to employers by creating a web-based
        system (PILA) to make such payments through the internet. All these objectives were
        achieved with the issuance of Regulation No. 304 of the CNSS to regulate mobility
        between the contributory and subsidized health insurance program and Presidential
        Decree 3515 of 2005 to regulate and promote group affiliation at lower cost to the social
        security system. By end of May 2007, the RUAF contained nine large social protection
        programs (pensions, health insurance, social assistance, occupational hazards, Cajas,

        among others) while the PILA system was used by about 83,000 firms (over 90% of the
        total formal firms) to make payment contributions.

In social assistance (early childhood) the objectives and outcomes were:

        Improve efficiency and effectiveness of ICBF's nutrition program and increase access to
        poor children by establishing and implementing an explicit targeting policy for ICBF's
        programs. In the context of PLaRSSAL I-II, the targeting policy was established and
        begun to be implemented increasing the number of children in SISBEN 1-2 from 78,000
        in 2002 to over 500,000 in 2004. In the context of LaRSDPL 111,additional expansion to
        1.1 million children was achieved by end of 2006, surpassing targets for the program.
        Also, over 66% of majors signed agreements with ICBF to improve ECD and youth
        programs in their territories and ICBF transferred an increasing share of resources to
        territories with highest incidence of poor, displaced and other vulnerable children.


In health services, the objectives and outcomes achieved were as follows:

        Increase coverage of vaccination program for children 0-1 years of age from 80% in base
        year 2002 to 90-95% in 2006. The objective was achieved after 2003 with coverage rates
        of 90% and over under the PLaRSSAL I-II. Funding for program has been secured with
        IADB project until 2008.
        Increase coverage of poor people affiliated to the subsidized health insurance regime
        from 10.7 million in 2002 to 18.2 million in December 2006, under PLaRSSAL I-II and
        LaRSDPL 111,surpassing targets in each operation.
        Improve the financial sustainability of health system. This objective has been achieved
        by a continuous increase in revenues from liquor taxes and lotteries (collected by States
        and others) between 2001 and 2006, resulting from increased controls and reducing
        evasion.

In labor and training, the objectives and main outcomes achieved were as follows:

        Implement labor reforms to reduce the wedge between employers' costs of labor and
        wages to increase employment and reduce the cost of training. This objective was met by
        passing the labor reform law 789 of 2002 which reduced the cost of labor to employers,
        reduced the cost of apprentice contracts and introduced several social protection
        measures for hard-to-employ workers and the unemployed.              For instance, SENA
        increased apprentice contracts from 33,300 in 2002 to 75,000 annually in 2006, of which
        about 55,000 contracts are with SENA and 20,000 are with other institutions promoted by
        said law.
        Increase productivity of training by SENA by increasing the number of short courses
        provided. SENA has increased the number of graduals from 900,000 in 2002 to 4.1
        million in 2006 largely surpassing the program's targets for the period.
        Increase the share of SENA budget dedicated to train the unemployed from 20% in 2002
        to 27% in 2006. This objective was achieved and in some years such as 2004 the share
        increased to 36% of SENA's funds.
        Improve social protection to the unemployed. This objective was achieved by increasing
        the number of unemployment subsidies (a program created by the labor reform law) from
        0 in 2002 to about 100,000 in 2005, of which 54% were allocated to informal workers, up
        from 36% in 2004. Sixty two percent of subsidies have been allocated to women.

In education, the objectives and main achievements were as follows:

        Increase coverage of primary and secondary education from 7.8 in 2002 to 9.2 million in
        2006. This objective was met.
        Increase the number of children and vulnerable youth in innovative programs from
         100,000 in 2002 to 260,000 in 2006. This target was met as 267,000 children were
         enrolled in innovative modalities at the end of 2006.
         Increase education quality by testing new principals and teachers applying for education
         positions. In 2004-05, 22,094 new principals and teachers who passed the exams were
         appointed. In 2005-2006, additional passing applicants were appointed in the different
         education secretaries.
         Improve education quality by testing students in five major competencies and use the
         results for improvement plans. This objective was met by increasing the number of
         municipalities testing students fiom 35% in 2002 to 95% in 2006 and by implementing
         education improvement plans in over 25% of the lower scoring municipalities.

In transparency and social control, the objectives and main achievements were as follows:

         Improve transparency and social control of social programs through periodic evaluations
         and greater community and citizen participation and oversight. To achieve this, DNP
         implemented several measures including piloting the Bogotd Como Vamos model for
         social control, designed and implemented six impact evaluations in 2006, fiom 2 in 2002,
         and developed the SIGOB system to monitor targets of the National Development Plan
         and provide information to the public.


    (iii)    Evaluation of the borrower's own performance during the preparation and
             implementation of the operation, with special emphasis on lessons learned that
             may be helpful in the future

                 The program contributed to strengthening the capacity of participating
                 government institutions (Ministry of Education, Ministry of Social Protection,
                 ICBF, SENA) as they participated and demonstrated capacity to develop the
                 policy matrix with the Bank team and in monitoring and meeting the conditions
                 of the program.
                 The program has contributed to strengthening the institutional capacity of DNP
                 and MHCP to prepare program operations with multilateral donors such as the
                 IDB and the World Bank, particularly in the social sectors. The experience
                 learned during the first two operations contributed to a more speedy preparation
                 and better coordination and implementation of the third operation in the series.
                 The great value added of this program operation was the constant involvement
                 and coordination of many participating government agencies which resulted in a
                 strengthened government in policy making in general and in social policy.

    (iv)     Evaluation of the performanceof the Bank, any cofinanciers, or of other
             partners during the preparationand implementation of the operation, including
             the effectiveness of their relationships,with special emphasis on lessons learned

                 From the beginning, the World Bank and the preparation team showed good
                 disposition, continued collaboration and technical support, all of which
                 contributed to the adequate preparationand execution of the program.

               The close monitoring and follow up of conditions by the World Bank contributed
               to identifying and addressing early in the process, conditions that were lagging so
               that they could be met. So, the constant interaction between the government and
               the World Bank was key to providing constant feedback on the progress in the
               program so that conditions could be met as established by the program.
               Finally, it is important to highlight that the experience and knowledge provided
               by the World Bank in social sectors and policy contributed greatly to the
               Government's of Colombia formulation and application of better and more
               sustainable social policies applicable to the country.
