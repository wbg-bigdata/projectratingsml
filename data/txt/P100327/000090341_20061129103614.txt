                                        I. INTRODUCTION

1.      The proposed Development Policy Loadcredit (DPL 3) to Indonesia for $600 million
seeks to sustain the Government's reform momentum after a challengingand transformative
year. The proposed operation i s the third in a program of four annual, single tranche loans in the
first series of World Bank budget support since the East Asia crisis.' Each DPL operation has
supported key reform measures with the overall aim of helping the Government achieve its
medium-term objectives in the core policy areas o f (i)macroeconomic stability and
creditworthiness; (ii)  improved investment climate; (iii)         enhanced public financial management and
anti-cormption; and (iv) improved service delivery to the poor. Given the unanticipated financing
requirements for 2006, inpart due to additional natural disasters, the Government has requested the
Bank, ADB, and Government of Japan to consider an increase in overall program lending. For its
part, the Bank proposes an increase in the size of the operation to $600 million from $400 million
for DPL 2. The proposed commitment amount i s also based on the Bank's assessment that the
increase would have little impact on total indebtedness and Bank exposure/risk, both of which have
undergone a steady reductionin recent years and will continue to do so in 2006-7.

2.       Indonesia is a much different country than it was just a few years ago. In early CY
2004, at the inception of this DPL series, the economy was growing less than 5 percent, government
debt to GDP was over 60 percent, investment was less than 20 percent of GDP, and comption was
high. To address these problems, the Government developed a Whitepaper to guide policy during
the transition from the IMF program it was under. Based on the policy actions laid out in this
Whitepaper, the Bank and the Government agreed to a DPL program focused on macroeconomic
stability, the investment climate, and public financial management/governance. These areas were
subsequently augmented by an additional focus on service delivery startingin the policy actions for
DPL 3.

3.       Progress has been substantial. Despite occasional bouts of volatility, the economy i s now
on much stronger footing. Recent growth rates (5.6 percent in 2005, 5.5 expected in 2006) are
among the highest in the region. The debt to GDP ratio i s around 40 percent of GDP and falling
fast while total investment i s now 21.5 percent of GDP. With lower subsidies, the government
(central and regional) has an additional US$10 billion dollars a year to spend on development
priorities, and consolidated government investment i s estimated to be close to pre-crisis levels as a
share of GDP in 2006. After some missteps in 2005, the Government and Central Bank managed
the transitions to higher domestic fuel prices and rising world interest rates with the exchange rate
stabilized, and inflation and interest rates down significantly.                 Financial sector reforms are
continuing: the banlung system has a strong capital position; reform of non-bank financial
institutions i s gaining momentum; and the blanket guarantee on deposits has been withdrawn in line
with the announced schedule. Indonesia's international credit rating improved from B- to BB- and
domestic and international interest premiums have declined. Perceptions of the investment climate
are improving gradually - as indicated in the Investment Climate Monitoring Surveys - although

Finance, Treasury, and Audit laws are in place with a new law on the Supreme Audit Board having
been approved in October 2006 to clarify the role of the Government's external auditor. Reforms at
the Ministry of Finance have been especially impressive. A first and second reorganizationprovides

Nevertheless the time to start a business has been reduced from 151 to 97 days, and breakthrough
investment and tax laws are now under discussion inParliament. Reformi s being institutionalized

4.      Improving governance has been built into the DPL agenda from the inception.
Governance and anti-conuption have been anchored in a broad range of program elements. This i s
most obvious in the Public Financial Management where budgeting, accounting, procurement and
treasury systems have strong governance and anti-corruption implications. However, governance
issues are also central to reforms in the investment climate, including regulatory reform (time to
start a business), the balance between tax payers and tax officials, and the Risk Management Unit at
the Ministry of Finance which i s responsible for determining the nature and amount of government
guarantees. As the macroeconomic picture has improved, the DPL program has placed greater
emphasis on improving governance with the addition of a civil service agenda including the
remunerationof high level government officials and piloting civil service reforms at the Ministry of
Finance.

5.      A nationwide scale up of Community Driven Development Program will assure that
Indonesia meets its Millennium Development Poverty Goals.                 Resources freed up when
subsidies were reduced are being allocated to two new national poverty programs. In the 2007
budget, the Government begins the expansion of the current community driven development (CDD)
programs, based on the World Bank's Kecamatan Development Program (KDP) and Urban Poverty
Project (UPP), from approximately 39,000 villages and neighborhoods to over 70,000 (by 2009).
In addition to this Community Based Program, the Government is also piloting conditional cash
transfer (CCT) systems focused on education and health outcomes to 500,000 households. The
phase-out of the subsidies, the assessment of existing anti-poverty programs, and the incorporation
of best practice (in this case CDD and CCT programs), have been supported by the DPL series.
These anti-poverty programs also reflect, in part, the government's response to an increase in
poverty in 2006, when poverty rose from 16 percent (2005) to 17.8 percent. Based on the Bank's
initial assessment, the Government's unconditional cash transfer program largely compensated the
poor for last year's fuel price increase and 75 percent of the increase in poverty was due to higher
rice prices, which rose by 33 percent between March 2005 and March 2006 (75 percent of the poor
are net-consumers of rice). The rice price increase in Indonesia was due, in part, to a ban on
imports that was relaxed in October 2006. Bank AAA on rice policy (including on how to raise
rural incomes) i s being intensified to facilitate Bank engagement inthis important area.

6.      The Bank will continue to play a key role in supporting Indonesia's reform process
through the DPL program. Inthe past three years, the DPLhas facilitated a coordinatedapproach
to donor policy engagement with the country's main development partners, namely, the World

Bank, the Asian Development Bank (ADB), and the Japanese Government; provided an impetus for
decision makers to debate, coordinate, and prioritize policy and institutional reforms; supported
reform "champions" within the Government by locking in and accelerating key reform measures;
reduced the average cost of borrowing; and extended the government debt maturity. More broadly
speakmg, the DPL has helped the Government operationalize key parts of its 2004-09 RPJM, or

Overall progress has been made commensurate with expectations, both in terms of achieving the

borrowing strategy, with the possibility of a second series of programmatic DPLs to be proposed in
the next full CAS.

     11.      COUNTRY CONTEXTAND DEVELOPMENTCHALLENGES

7.     Indonesia is a vast multi-ethnic, polyglot, and archipelagic nation, extending 5,120
kilometers from east to west and 1,760 kilometers from north to south. It encompasses as
many as 18,000 islands, and with a population of 220 million people, i s the most populous Muslim-
majority nation and the fourth most populous overall. Millions were lifted out of poverty during a
period of tremendous growth from the 1970's until the regional financial crisis in 1997, which
ushered the fall of the New Order regime and the current transition to democratic and economic
reforms. The country has embraced political pluralism and participation, but has struggled with
continuing structural weaknesses and endemic corruption that the crisis exposed. Promises to
undertake broad reforms on both fronts swept President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono to power in
October 2004. Despite several shocks (e.g., the December 2004 tsunami, terrorist strikes in Bali,
global fuel price increases, and most recently, a large earthquake and tsunami in central and west
Java, respectively) and notable risks (e.g., avian flu epidemic, sectarian strife and renewed
separatist violence in Papua), the economy has fared reasonably well, weathering last year's
currency volatility, large fuel price hikes, and resultant inflation, high interest rates, and economic
slowdown. As the economy adjusts, and with an appropriate global environment, the Bank projects
growth to continue at 5.5 percent in 2006 and 6 percent in 2007, adding a push to the Government's
poverty reduction efforts.



8.     The Indonesian economy has shown signs of a resumption of strong growth following
last year's interest and fuel price hikes and consequent economic slowdown. Inparticular, the
Government's fuel subsidy reductions in October 2005 sought to bring Indonesian energy prices
closer to market prices, a vital step to longer-term fiscal sustainability and higher growth. The
immediate impact i s observed in the improvement in net exports in light of decline in oil imports.
The price of fuel rose significantly, registeringa cumulative 143 percent increase for the year. GDP
growth, which had been running over 6 percent early in 2005 - the highest rate in nine years -
slowed to 5.6 percent for the year as interest rates rose and consumers adjusted to the fuel price
increase. Economic contraction bottomed out in the first half of 2006 with GDP growth inthe third
quarter rising to 5.5 percent from 4.9 percent in the first semester (see Figure 1). Falling interest

14.        The governance agenda in Indonesia is being driven by its continued transition to a
more open, democratic society. Constitutional reforms securing direct elections have created a
new accountability framework at all levels. Decentralization i s bringing control over resources
closer to communities, providing new opportunities for participation and monitoring of service
delivery. The opening up of the media and surge of new collective organizations within civil
society are giving rise to voices for good governance. The results of these changes are evident in a
new level of participation, competition, and transparency in public life. But, while governance
indicators show some evidence of improvement, there remains considerable public demand for
broader improvements in public services delivery.

15.        Over the CAS period, GO1 has triggered genuine momentum in the fight against
corruption. The campaign has continued at an impressive pace, with a growing number of high
profile investigations, prosecutions, and convictions for corruption. The main dnver of the anti-
conuption effort has been a set of powerful independent oversight and prosecutorial institutions
such as the Anti-Corruption Commission (KPK), Anti-Conuption Court, Judicial Commission,
Police Commission, and the Prosecutorial Commission. Mostly established under the previous
administration, these new institutions have begun to deliver tangible results with strong support of
President Yudhoyono, who has made governance a key theme of his administration. In addition,
existing institutions like the Supreme Audit Agency (BPK), the Financial Transaction and Analysis


                                                           7

Centre (PPTAK), and the Attorney General's Office are more active. High profile corruption
investigations have been launched across GO1 and even among some state-owned enterprises.
These have led to a number of successful prosecutions.

16.     Nevertheless, active prosecution of offenders has not been accompanied by a proactive
approach to developing and implementing a strategy to prevent corruption. Recently, the
Government has begun to take some significant first steps to develop and pilot models for civil
service reform targeting high level public officials and specific ministries (Ministry of Finance,
Ministry of Trade, and Ministry of National Education), though a more comprehensive reform of
the bureaucracy has not yet been developed. There have been recent encouraging signs with the
establishment of a effectiveness team reporting directly to the President. Reform of the judicial
system has stalled and there has been some backtracking as a result of Supreme Court and
Constitutional Court decisions to curb checks and balances onjudicial power through newly created
institutions such as the Judicial Commission and the Anti-Corruption Court. More broadly, a
National Plan for the Eradication of Corruption (RAN-PK)has been approved and supported by a
special Presidential Instruction (Inpres 5). This plan encompasses a very wide range of measures
proposed by each ministry to be monitored by the National Planning Agency (Bappenas).
However, corruption prevention lacks a clear focal point in the Government, weakening overall
progress in implementing a preventive anti-corruption strategy.

 17.    Indonesia has had its share of tragedies this year between the reconstruction of Aceh
and Nias, the spread of Avian Flu, and the May 2006 earthquake in central Java and July
tsunami in west Java. Aceh and Nias are starting to recover; houses are being built in large
numbers, the school year has started normally, and health services are available in most parts of the
affected areas. Over US$1.5 billion has already been disbursed. And the peace agreementbetween
the Indonesian Government and GAM, which has been implemented without major disruption, i s
cause for optimism. Nevertheless, many challenges remain, particularly in providing infrastructure
and reaching inaccessible regions, conflict-affected areas, and Nias. Rebuilding in the aftermath of
the May 2006 earthquake in Yogyakarta, which claimed close to 6,000 lives, will also require
substantial resources, and coordination across central, regional, and district governments (see Box
 1). And 18 months after the December 2004 tsunami, another deadly tsunami hit west Java on July
29, 2006, killing nearly 600 people and raising questions over serious shortcomings of a promised
Indian Ocean tsunami early warning system to sound an adequate alert. Finally, avian flu has
emerged as a major threat; over the past year, nearly half of all human deaths worldwide have
occurred in Indonesia. Recently, after a slow start, there has been an agreement on a detailed action
plan, and a program of US$250 million per year - jointly funded by the Government and the
international community -i s beingput inplace.

On May 27,2006, an earthquake struck Central Java near the city of Yogyakarta. The earthquake was a reminder of the
natural perils facing Indonesia, an archipelago of more than 18,000 islands lying along the Pacific "ring of fire" of
active volcanoes and tectonic faults. Measuring 6.3 in the Richter Scale, the earthquake was one of the most costly
natural disasters inthe developing world over the past decade. The earthquake took nearly 6,000 lives, injured between
40,000-60,000 more, and destroyed the homes and livelihood of hundreds of thousands. Total damage and loss caused
by the earthquake is estimated at Rp. 29.1 trillion, or US $3.1 billion, a scale similar to destruction caused by
earthquakes in Gujarat (2001) and in Pakistan (2005). Several factors contributed to such high losses, including the
shallow location of the epicenter (resulting in more intense shaking on the surface compared to deeper earthquakes of
the same magnitude), combined with high population density and failure to buildearthquake resistant structures.

The damage was heavily concentrated on housing and private sector buildings. The former accounts for over 50
percent (or Rp. 15.3 trillion) of total damage and losses. About 154,000 houses were completely destroyed and
260,000 houses suffered some damage, a scale higher than that of the 2004 tsunami, both in terms of the number of
houses affected and the cost of replacement and repair. Over 650,000 workers were employed in sectors affected by the
earthquake, with close to 90 percent of damage and losses concentrated in small and medium enterprises. About 30,000
enterprises were directly affected as well through supply chain and other disruptions in intermediation. Health and
education were equally hard hit with more than Rp 1.5 trillion in damage and losses. Poverty - already above the
national average in the affected area - will be exacerbated by the earthquake. Nearly 880,000 poor people live in the
affected area and an additional 66,000 may fall below the poverty line. The impact on employment i s especially severe,
with 130,000 people likely to losejobs, much of them in the services and small-scale manufacturing sectors.

The local response was swift, with Rp 5 trillion, or US $500 million, allocated to the relief effort from the national
budget. The international community is providing material relief, coordinatedby the UN.

19.      Despite this setback the current assessment is that Indonesia remains on track to
achieving the MDGtarget on poverty reduction of 7.5 percent, or half the 1990 levels by 2015
but that the government target of an 8.3 percent poverty rate by 2009 is out of reach. The
base-case projection assumes average growth of 6.2 percent and relatively slower growth rates for
the poor that have been the case since the crisis continue. Under this scenario Millennium
Development Goals (MDG) for poverty reduction are met. However, if growth falls from the
projected 6.2 percent to 4.0 percent per year in a low-case projection the goal will not be met.

20.     Another issue is the significant number of people who remain highly vulnerable
despite an otherwise successful track record of poverty reduction.             The level of extreme
poverty - or people living below $1 a day - i s relatively low at 8.5 percent of the population.
However, the proportion of the population living on less than $2 a day i s 49.6 percent, 10
percentage points higher than the comparable average for East Asia (including China), and more
closely resembling the poverty profile of lower income countries in the region. The numbers for
Indonesia bespeak of the high proportion of people who remain highly vulnerable to falling under
the country's poverty line. Indeed, the profile of the "near poor" - people above the poverty line
but living on less than $2 a day - reveals that they have very similar characteristics to the poor.
Hence policies designed to move the poor above the national poverty line will bejust as relevant in
moving the "near poor" above $2 a days3

21.     Glaring regional disparities in development are also a salient characteristic of poverty
in Indonesia. The western areas of Indonesia (Sumatra, Java, and Bali) have enjoyed the fastest
rates of growth over the past three decades, leading to a concentration of relative wealth in these
densely populated islands. The eastern regions of Indonesia, meanwhile, continue to lag well
behind the rest of the country. For example, the poverty rate inJava and Bali i s 15.7 percent, while
that in Papua i s 38.7 percent. Services are also unequally distributed across regions; in Java, the
average distance between a household and the nearest public health clinic i s 4 kilometers, whereas
in Papua, it is 32 kilometers. A challenge for the government is that while poverty incidencei s far
higher in eastern regions, most of Indonesia's poor actually live in the densely populated western
regions. Hence, while the poverty rate i s relatively low in Java and Bali, they are home to 57
percent of Indonesia's poor, compared to Papua, which only has 3 percent of the total poor.4

23.       Efforts to reduce poverty remain susceptible to setbacks. On September 1, 2006, the
Central Bureau of Statistics (BPS) announced that the poverty rate in Indonesia had risen to 17.75
percent in March 2006, up from 16.0 percent in February 2005, in what was the first recorded
increase in poverty since the crisis. The statistic translates to an additional 4 million people having
fallen into poverty during the period. While economic growth during the period was 5.2 percent -
not very different from the previous two years (5.6 percent and 4.4 percent, respectively) during
which poverty fell - there were several events likely to have impacted poverty. The fuel subsidy
reduction in October 2005 increased fuel prices by 114 percent overall and tripled the price of
kerosene, a key fuel used by the poor. To mitigate the impact of the increase, the government
launched an unconditional cash transfer (UCT) program, targeting 19.2 million poor and near-poor
households. This i s the world's largest ever cash transfer program targeting some 34 percent of the
population - well in excess of the number of poor households in the population - with each
beneficiary family receiving about US$11 per month. World Bank analysis indicates the impact of
the fuel price increase would have been mitigated by the UCT and i s unlikely to have been the main
driver behind the poverty increase (see Box 2).

24.      The other significant event impacting poverty rates was the dramatic increase in rice
prices (33 percent) between February 2005 and March 2006. The increase was mostly due to
shortages as a result of the ban on rice imports. Rice plays an important role, as it makes up about
25 percent of the consumption basket of the poor. Moreover, three-fourths of the poor are net
consumers of rice. Thus rice price increases hurt far more people than they help. The World Bank
estimates that 3.1 million people may have been pushed into poverty as a result of the rice price rise
between February 2005 and March 2006. In other words, around 75 percent of the recently
published increase in poverty could be attributable to the rice price increase.

25.      The Bank is actively engaged in dialogue with the Government on rice policy. There i s
a widening recognition in the Government that the increase in the poverty rate between 2005 and
2006 was primarily due to the large increase in rice prices over the same period. The Government
i s therefore considering mechanisms to avoid further rice price instability as part of i t s broader
efforts to formulate an effective strategy for revitalizing agriculture. Effective implementation of
such mechanisms will play an important role in ensuring a return to steady poverty reduction over
the next year.

26.       In response to increased resources and higher poverty numbers the Government is
increasing spending on anti-poverty programs. With the assistance of the World Bank, the
Asian Development Bank, and other development partners, the Government i s scaling up poverty
programs modeled on Bank community driven development (CDD) programs, namely the
Kecamatan Development Program (KDP) and the Urban Poverty Project (UPP). In addition the
current unconditional cash transfer program i s being extended to pilot conditional cash transfer
programs to assist the poor while simultaneously improving health and education outcomes. These
programs reflect an increase in the commitment of resources and better thought-out strategies to
accelerate poverty reduction and assure the achievement of MDGand the Government's own goals.

27.       In the long run, alleviating poverty and achieving development outcomes hinge on
several key policy areas. Historically, a pro-growth agenda has been the main route to poverty
reduction. This implies macroeconomic stability as a prerequisite and entails income generation
that accrues to the poor. Secondly, the Government must improve public services to the poor in
terms of access, targeting, and quality.                 This entails policies and actions that make budget
allocations and public spending more pro-poor.                      Lastly, improving governance and public
institutions remains the lynchpin of any poverty reduction agenda. Without it, efforts to ensure that
growth and public services benefit the poor will not succeed in the long run. These policy areas

28.     The Government's medium-term strategy, or RPJM, represents the country's
medium-term development vision. It lays out three broad and multi-pronged goals: (i)      a safe and
peaceful Indonesia; (ii) a just and democratic Indonesia; and (iii)a prosperous Indonesia. It also
incorporates the eight MDGs in order to frame medium-term targets as part of international
commitments to be achieved by 2015. Within the 2004-09 timeframe of the RPJMi s an ambitious
goal of cutting the national poverty rate by half, to 8.2 percent. For each of the three broad goals
are more specific objectives in a number of sector- or theme-specific policy areas. The RPJM has
been widely endorsed by the Consultative Group on Indonesia (CGI), which has called for
development partners to intensify and structure their policy engagement around the RPJM, both at
the macroeconomic and sectoral levels.

29.     As a precursor to the RPJM,the Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper (PRSP or SNPK)
was initiated during the previous administration. I t was developed through a multi-stakeholder
process, with targets developed for reducing multi-dimensional poverty by addressing 10 basic
rights organized around four fundamental themes: (i)  creating opportunities; (ii)
                                                                                 empowerment; (iii)
human capital and capacity development; and (iv) social protection. An interimpaper was finalized
and included a broad road-map and timetable for completing a full PRSP. The PRSP process took
place against the backdrop of the impending 2004 presidential elections and eventually a change in
administration. With the policy pronouncements and emerging reform agenda articulated by the
new president, and given the likelihood that Indonesia would soon graduate out of IDA, the new
administration decided to incorporate the PRSP's main tenets into the RPJM and the Government's
mainstream planning processes.

30.     Key to implementingthe RPJMis an annualwork plan, or RKP. For 2006, the Cabinet
identified seven priority areas and associatedtargets among the 33 policy areas found in the RPJM.
For 2007, the number of priority areas grew to nine. The priorities of the annual RKP are linked to
the state budget and include quantitative targets and policy direction. Even more than the RPJM,
the RKP has a decidedly pro-poor focus, reinforcing several of the priorities in the SNPK. The
RKP represents the first time that policy priorities, albeit broadly defined, have been so clearly
identified, and a conscious attempt made to use the priorities to allocate resources among
ministries. The 2006 budget builds on the revised budget for 2005 which placed a priority on
poverty reduction objectives, demonstrated by the reallocation of expenditures from the highly
regressive fuel subsidy to the cash transfer program discussed above, as well as to health,
education, rural infrastructure programs that were expected to benefit the poor.           Pro-poor
expenditure allocations have continued in the 2006 budget. Figure 8 illustrates the links between
Indonesia's planning and strategy documents.

31.     Complementing the Government's broad strategy and work plan are several recent
policy packages completed by the new economic team.              Policy packages on infrastructure,
investment climate, financial sector, and anti-corruption have been issued thus far during 2006.
The infrastructure and investment packages address policy issues in corporate regulation, tax,
customs, labor and infrastructure that have been under review for some time.          However, the


                                                 13

packages also demonstrate a willingness to tackle a number of difficult decisions that had been on
hold and provide a time-bound matrix with clearly defined Ministerial responsibilities. The Bank
has helped with advice that underpins some of the actions in the Government's policy packages and
has been asked to support the monitoring of their progress and implementation. The packages also
represent a foundation for future DPLs which are envisioned to deepen the policy dialogue in these
critical areas. The proposed triggers for DPL4 include several actions identified in the policy
packages.

32.     The investment package enjoys heightened political commitment owing to its issuance
as a Presidentialinstruction. It i s divided into five categories: (i) investment policies; (ii)                           general
customs; (iii)tax; (iv) labor policy; and (v) S M E policy. Highlights of the investment package
include: completion of the investment law and reductions inred tape; business friendly revisions to
the tax law; and improved procedures in customs. Most items in the package are not new, having
existed in one form or another in previous policy statements. Many of the items are also quite
general, stating only what the government hopes to accomplish without detailing how to
accomplish them.                   However, some of the reforms are specific, such as cutting the red lane
inspections to 10 percent and compiling a transparent negative list regarding foreign investments,
and most targets include deadlines to ensure rapid implementation (which has begun) and specify
the responsible Minister. On the whole, the package has been well-received despite some
skepticism by the business community over the Government's ability to fully deliver on these
reforms.

33.     The infrastructure package seeks to address policy blockages to infrastructure
investment, and includes a particular emphasis on private investment. The initial package
released in February 2006 included 156 policy outputs (laws, regulations, policy papers, reviews,
etc) to be achieved in 2006. More recently, in the context of the ADB's Infrastructure Reform
Project programmatic loan, the government's reformpackage has been extended to the end of 2009.



                                                                                             14

A central element of the package is the framework for PPPs, including arrangements for project
preparation, vetting, and competitive bidding, allocation of risk between private investors and the
Government, and specific PPP transactions.         Cross-sectoral reform issues addressed include
infrastructure financing, land acquisition, decentralization of infrastructure responsibilities, and
public service obligations (subsidy policy). Sector-specific reforms, applying to each of energy,
transport, water and sanitation and telecommunications, address strategic planning, regulatory
arrangements, tariff policy, removal of SOE monopolies, and governance. The key achievement so
far i s the establishment of the PPP framework.       It will take time and sound implementation,
however, for this new framework to bear fruit in terms of private investment. The Bank and other
development partners are working intensely with the Government to ensure adequate project
preparation prior to tendering of PPPprojects.

34.      A third package addressing issues in the financial sector has also been issued. It was
formulated with three broad objectives (i)    increasing access to finance (ii)reducing the cost of
finance and (iii)  diversifying the financial sector and reducing its vulnerability to future shocks.
The package addresses a wide range of important issues across banking and NBFIsectors and sets
time-bound targets for actions - mostly within a 12 month time frame. On state owned banks, it
proposes improvements in governance, while simultaneously proposing ways to restructure the
substantial NPLs on their balance sheets. On NBFIs, some of the recommendations proposed by
the Bank in its recent ESW report have been taken on board.           Key planned actions include
restructuring the insurance sector - and addressing undercapitalized companies, assessing civil
service pension liabilities with a view to undertake fundamental system reform, improving the
infrastructure for capital markets in general and bondmarkets in particular, and increasing access to
finance for SMEs.     BI, the Ministry of State Owned Enterprises, and MoF have been closely
involved in the process, under the coordination of the Coordinating Ministry of Economic Affairs.
Consultationswith donors and industry were also heldprior to the issue of the package.

35.      The anti-corruption package (RAN-PK) was also endorsed by a special Presidential
Instruction (INPRES 5). The plan incorporates a wide range of measures across all government
ministries under three general headings: i)corruption prevention; ii)corruption case prosecution
and law enforcement system improvement; and iii)monitoring and evaluation. The matrix for
RAN-PKincludes more than 100 action plans for prevention covering these areas. Inthe course of
developing the Plan, consultations were held with local officials and civil society groups in a
number of cities. The action plan was completed in February 2005. RAN-PK will be updated
annually to include the latest corruption eradication initiatives. Bappenas and Menpan have been
given responsibility to monitor implementation of the plan and to report back directly to the
President.

 CAS PR (36856-IND) proposing an extension of the CAS timeframe by one year, it was proposed
 that the DPL program similarly be extended to a fourth operation (DPL 4), tentatively scheduled
 for Boarddiscussion in late-2007. Furthermore, owing to unanticipatedfinancing needs for 2006,

37.    The CAS for FY04-07,discussedby the BoardinNovember2003, envisageda DPLas
part of an "enhanced base case" scenario and subsequent DPLs as part of a "high case"
scenario. The Bank made a corporate assessment last year in the context of DPL 2 and
determined the country had indeed met the high case lending scenario by fulfilling most of the
indicative triggers set out in the CAS. More broadly speaking, the high case lending scenario
required evidence of accelerated reforms to effect a more immediate resumption of growth and
poverty reduction. The ambitious fuel subsidy reductions and a continued trajectory of economic
growth and reform progress provided strong justification for moving to a high case lending
scenario. The Bank's current assessment i s that the Government has sustained progress across a
broad range of policy areas and that the overall reformprogram remains broadly on track. Table 3
outlines progress toward CAS outcomes and Annex 16 also highlights how the DPL program
supports the core of the CAS' foci. Indonesia has made progress against DPL 3 triggers and its
overall CPIA rating increased from 3.6 to 3.7 between 2004 and 2005, having registered
improvements across key categories since 1999. Specifically, there i s clear evidence of progress in
macroeconomic stability and creditworthiness; investment climate; poverty reduction; service
delivery; and encouraging steps on public sector governance and anti-corruption. Taken in total,
there are compelling reasons to support the Government's request to move forward with DPL 3.
The CAS PR's proposal to extend the CAS timeframe to FY08 entails a new set of "high case"
triggers - progress against which would determine whether to move forward with the planned
pipeline of program lending, including DPL 4. The "high case" triggers would provide the basis
for DPL4 triggers.

policy matrix for DPL 1, outcome indicators were introduced to highlight the expected key results

and discussed with Government counterparts and development partners. The results framework
was subsequently strengthened in DPL 2 with the inclusion of baseline data going back to end-
2003 and desired targets to be reached by the end of 2007. During the past three years, outcome
indicators were added, reflecting the expanded scope of the reform agenda supported by the DPL.
Given the extension of the program by one year and to DPL 4, the targets for each outcome

42.     The proposed DPL 3 continues the policy dialogue on and support to Indonesia's reform
program to improve macroeconomic stability, the investment climate, and public financial
management and anti-corruption. It buildson the reforms undertaken in conjunction with the first two
DPLs, while broadening the scope of the policy dialogue by adding public service delivery as a fourth
pillar. The proposed operation reflects the pace and fluidity of the reform process. Continued strong
commitment by the Government in many areas have allowed for a deepening of reforms, such as in
decentralization - where the proposed operation i s supporting efforts to establish very specific
procedures on sub-national bond issuance. Meanwhile, given the importance of the governance and anti-
corruption agendas in Indonesia, the Government agreed with the Bank to incorporate new areas into the
DPLpolicy dialogue, such as anti-corruption, civil service reform, and public service delivery. As such,
DPL 3 lays the groundwork for much deeper engagement in these new policy areas in subsequent
program loans.

43.     As discussed in the previous section, several changes to DPL 3 have occurred since
preparation of the operation began. The DPL 2 program document laid out 16 indicative triggers for
DPL3. Many of these triggers containedmultiple sub-measures as a means to lay out a specific blueprint
for Government counterparts at the technical level. Of the original 16 triggers, one - the Trade Law -
was dropped and replaced by a comparable measure, namely the Warehouse Receipts Law. At the time
of DPL 2 negotiations, it was expected that the trade bill would help to ensure the free movement of
goods within the country and a competitive trade regime. An initial assessment of the draft law pointed
to considerable work that was still needed to bring the provisions in line with the original objectives as
envisioned last year by the Ministry of Trade. Moreover, the changes to the Cabinet's economic team
brought forth several new initiatives and a policy package in investment climate - none of which
identified trade as a priority for this current year. The Bank and the Government, therefore, decided to
drop this trigger, perhaps to be picked up in a subsequent DPL operation in the near future. Despite this
decision, the Bank remains engaged in on-going discussions with the Ministry of Trade (MOT)to achieve
the trigger's overall objective. The Government has expressed the view that focusing on specific areas of
reform in the trade area may be more beneficial than the drafting and approval of an over-arching trade
law. The Government has sought the Bank's assistance in putting together a multi-donor trust fund that
would help support capacity building in the MOT,as well as provide analytical support for the future
trade reform agenda.

     D. POLICYAREA I:SUPPORTINGMACROECONOMIC             STABILITY AND CREDITWORTHWESS

45.      Indonesia has had good success in reestablishingmacroeconomic stability since the financial
crisis. This has been achieved in large measure through prudent fiscal and monetary policies across two
administrations. Last year's increase in domestic fuel prices and the tightening of monetary conditions in
response to rising global oil prices and interest rates were painful but necessary policy adjustments that -
more broadly speaking - reflected the Government's commitment to ensure continued macroeconomic
stability over the medium- to long-term. The fuel price adjustments resulted in savings of $5 billion in
2005 and approximately $10 billion in 2006, and have enabled a massive reorientation of Government
spending toward higher priority social areas. It was informed in part by Bank analytic and advisory work
requested by the Government and was part of the reform program supported by DPL 2. Under the DPL
program, controlling inefficient spending has been a key strategy for maintaining macroeconomic
stability. Others include enhancing revenue through tax and fiscal reforms, improving debt management;
and streamlining inter-governmental borrowing, all of which are covered under DPL 3.

46.       The next DPL will continue the policy dialogue on macroeconomic stability but with the
intention of not including specific triggers under this pillar. Instead, maintenance of a stable
macroeconomic framework will be considered a prerequisite for continued program lending, in line with
OP8.60 Development Policy Lending. This decision testifies to the confidence development partners have
in the Government's commitment to sound macroeconomic management, based on a convincing track
record. As noted in Section II,     many indicators have improved in recent years - some dramatically -
including those that the DPLprogram will be evaluated against. Duringthe past three years, credit ratings
have improved (S&P and Moody's have upgraded the country's rating); public debt-to-GDP ratio has
dropped by more than 10 percent, from 60 percent to below 47 percent; and domestic non-oil and gas
revenues are projected to comprise about 13.6 percent, from 10.4 percent in 2003. These underlie the
more poignant fact the Indonesian economy has enjoyed one of the highest growth rates among similarly-
sized economies in the region.

47.      Despite these successes, there are many issues which the Government will need to continue to
address. While last year's fuel price hikes were a bold move, Indonesia's retail prices for gasoline and
kerosene still remain at below and well below international prices, respectively and are not market based.
It i s also noteworthy that Indonesia, as an oil and gas producer, does not benefit from a revenue increase
when the global price of oil rises, given the remaining subsidies and the fixed domestic price of fuel. The
Government also faces a critical choice on electricity subsidies.        The increases in fuel prices have
increased the subsidies required by PLN (the state-owned electricity company) as they pass into
production costs. In the 2006 budget, the central government set aside Rp. 17 trillion ($1.8 billion) for
electricity subsidy assuming an increase of 20-30 percent in tariffs.         The decision not to increase
electricity tariffs in 2006 as originally planned - due primarily to concerns over timing, political
ramifications, and the short-term economic impact that would have come at the heels of the fuel price
hikes- will result in an additional subsidy of Rp. 20 trillion (about $2 billion). As was the case for fuel,
electricity subsidies distort consumer and producer incentives and constrain higher value spending. Most
of the electricity subsidy i s absorbed through a regressive subsidy to consumers (66 percent), followed by
industry (29 percent), and business (5 percent) in 2006.          Reducing inefficient spending with due
consideration for growth outcomes and the impact on the poor will continue to be an important topic of
analytic work and policy engagement between development partners and Government.

alleviation prospects. After the crisis, investment fell from 30 percent to 20 percent of GDP. Such low

and tax administration. The improvement was particularly pronounced in the areas of national level
corruption. The 2005 Transparency International Global Corruption Barometer found that 81percent of
Indonesians surveyed believed that corruption would decrease next year - the largest share of optimists of
69 countries surveyed.

92.      The passageof the "Witness and Victim Protection Law" will be an important breakthrough
in facilitating anti-corruption cases. It provides a comprehensive set of protections for witnesses who
testify in criminal cases, including crimes of corruption. The law mandates the establishment of a special
commission and secretariat to ensure implementation of the provisions of the bill. The next year should
see a properly resourced commission and secretariat. The Law includes both whistleblower and witness
protection and its passage would provide adequate safeguards for those providing information relevant to
legal cases. The Law i s critical to going forward with the prosecution of corruption cases. The drafting of
the Witness Protection Law was initiated and undertaken by the Parliament and subsequently sent to the
Government (the Cabinet), which provided comments and submitted the law back to Parliament for
consideration and discussion. The Law was approvedby Parliament in June 2006.

94.     The Government, through its medium-term and annual plans, has laid out an ambitious
poverty reduction agenda linked to the achievement of the MDGs. Driving this agenda i s an
overarching emphasis on improving pro-poor public service delivery, specifically in the priority areas of
health, education, rural infrastructure and social protection. This i s exemplified by the 2005 reallocation
of savings from the two rounds of fuel subsidy reductions to programs in these areas targeting the poor.7
Shortly after the March 2005 reductions, government established the operational aid to schools (BOS)and
health insurance (Askeskin) programs to benefit poorer households by increasing access to primary health
and basic education services primarily through reduced or eliminated fees, and the village infrastructure
program to provide infrastructure in less developed areas and create employment.

95.     With the second wave of subsidy reductions in October that saw kerosene rise prices almost
triple, government took a markedly different step to compensate households.                      Anticipating a
relatively greater negative impact on the poor and near poor, government introduced a cash transfer
program. Under the program, each beneficiary household receives a Rp. 300,000 transfer distributed
quarterly through the post office. With 19.2 million beneficiary households in 2006, this program i s the
largest ever cash transfer program in coverage and total volume of transfers. The establishment of a
targeted cash transfer program signifies a shift from the generally ineffective and inefficient blunt
instrument, that is, a general subsidy benefiting primarily the non-poor to a social protection regime that i s
leaner and more focused on targeting poor and vulnerable households.

96.     The government is currently developing a conditional cash transfer program, to be
implemented starting in six provinces in 2007, modeled on successful programs in other countries
notably Latin America. The program has both a short-term poverty reduction objective as well as a
longer term objective of improving human capital outcomes for the poor. In addition to piloting a
conditional cash transfer to households, the government also plans to build on Indonesia's extensive
experience with community-driven development projects by piloting a community version of conditional
cash transfer. A rigorous impact evaluation is planned, with results available in 2009.

110.    OP 8.60 (Development Policy Lending) mandates the Bank to determine whether policies

reductions in congestion (see Annex 13).

114.    For DPL 2, the Bank undertook a Poverty and Social Impact Analysis of the October 2005

unconditional cash transfer (UCT) program, which provided cash targeted to 19.2 million poor and near-
poor households, was introduced to mitigate the impact on the poor and vulnerable. There i s on-going
discussion of how successful the U C T and other fuel subsidy-related compensation programs were, given

transfer program targeting some 34 percent of the population-well         in excess of the number of poor

       G.     RISKS

 119.     Continued strong performance by the government on its reform agenda and the achievement of
 medium-termobjectives are subject to several risks.
     a. Fiduciary and reputational risks. In the past, the Bank had been associated with Indonesia's
          weak governance environment. Concerns have also been raised by civil society groups about the
          proper use of donor funds. The Bank's fiduciary assessment concludes that since 2000-01, there
          have been considerable improvements in the overall fiduciary environment in Indonesia.
          However, risks remain. Indonesia has an enormous public sector institutional reform agenda to
          implement to eliminate concerns over the quality of Indonesia's public financial management.
          The Bank's GFMRAP and other donor programs are helping the Government lock in and
          implement institutional reforms in the PFMarena.
     b. Slow down in reform efforts and coordination problems: Economic volatility could worsen if
          the Government does not sustain the reform momentum to follow up on the recent reduction in
          fuel subsidies (including maintaining appropriate interest rates and investment climate reforms).
          However, the currency market volatility in 2005 increased the recognition of the need for rapid
          policy adjustment in line with key global developments. The Government and Bank Indonesia
          have also considerably enhanced their coordination as evidenced in the recent joint work on
          policy packages.
     c. Electoral cycles and politics. With direct Presidential election in 2004, Indonesia entered a new
          era of democracy. With the next Presidential election due in 2009, the window for significant
          policy reform i s likely to be open only for the next year and a half or so - assuming, as i s common
          in several countries, that the last one year is focused on the election. This presents two problems:
          first difficult implementation decisions are needed shortly if they are to avoid the political cycle
          and conversely and especially in infrastructure a desire to get infrastructure going too quickly may
          have an adverse impact on long-term systemic reforms. Finally, the reform agenda remains
          sensitive to coalitionpolitics and tensions between partners may grow as the election nears.
     d. External vulnerabilities and petroleum prices: With improvements in its macroeconomic
          indicators, Indonesia resilience to external shocks has increased. Foreign exchange reserves are

                                                        53

      continuing to rise and the foreign debt/GDP ratio to decline. Significant subsidies continue in
      fuel and electricity, but by more-than-doubling domestic fuel prices last year, Indonesia
      significantly reduced its vulnerability to volatility in global petroleum prices.      At present,
      marginal changes in world prices are budget neutral. Significant price increases, accompanied by
      further domestic fuel price increases, would generate a terms of trade advantage and provide the
      Government more resources and fiscal space - since Indonesia i s a net exporter of oil and gas.
      Significant price decreases would be largely neutral on the central government budget, but would
      have an impact on local government revenues - as transfers from the central government to the
      regions decline. This i s a medium term concern as local governments have built up substantial
      cash reserves which would allow them to adjust gradually to a lower level of transfers.
   e. Rice price developmentsand policies. There i s a widening recognition in the Government that
      the increase in the poverty rate between 2005 and 2006 was primarily due to the large increase in
      rice prices over the same period. Further large rice price increases could raise the risk of social
      unrest. The Government will need to develop better mechanisms to avoid rice price instability as
      part of an effective strategy to revitalize agriculture. Effective implementation of such a strategy
      would greatly ensure steady poverty reduction over the medium-term. The Bank has been and
      stands ready to continue to provide analysis and advice as may be requested by the Government
      on the issue.
   f. Corruption and weaknesses in the governance framework. Corruption and weaknesses in
      governance remain serious impediments to development in Indonesia. However, prospects of
      genuine reform are now better than in decades.            The Government has triggered genuine
      momentum in the fight against corruption. The main driver of the anti-comption effort has been
      a new set of powerful independent oversight and prosecutorial institutions such as the Anti-
      corruption Commission          (KF'K), Anti-Corruption Court, Judicial        Commission,     Police
      Commission, and the Prosecutorial Commission. High profile corruptioninvestigations have been
      launched, leading to a number of successful prosecutions. Indonesia's relative ranking in East
      Asia on perception measures of measures of corruption i s also improving - albeit from a low
      base. Overall therefore, while risks of corruption and weak governance continue to remain, a
      significant set of measures i s being taken to address them.
   g. Security risks. Heightened security alert stemming from sectarian and regional conflicts, and
      particularly radical Islamic terrorism could slow F D I and a recovering tourism industry. Security
      was tightened across Indonesia in October 2005 after suicide attacks in the islandof Bali lulled 19
      and injured nearly 130. The attacks have stalled the recovery of Bali's tourism industry which
      had seen a return of visitors since the deadly 2002 terrorist attacks.
   h. Fears of a bird-flu epidemic. In the first half of 2006, over a third of human cases (31 of 80)
      and nearly half of human deaths (25 of 51) attributed to HPAI worldwide have been in
      Indonesia."    The recent cluster of cases in Medan, for which the source of infection remains
      unknown and human-to-human transmission cannot be ruled out, has highlightedthe weaknesses
      in human surveillance, case management, and follow-up. Indonesia's strategy to respond to the
      challenge of avian flu i s judged to be sound, but the implementation arrangements are still not
      fully developed. These needto be given highpriority.
  i. Naturaldisasters. Indonesiaisoneoftheworld's mostdisaster-pronecountries. Locatedatthe

                                          Annex 4: IMFAssessment Letter
Staff Assessment of Developments and Policies

Recent Developments and Short-Term Prospects

After the recent slowdown, GDP growth has begun to recover. Following the significant hike in interest rates and
fuel price increases in the fall of 2005, real GDP growth slowed to under 5 percent inthe last quarter of 2005 and first
quarter of 2006 (y/y). However, growth picked up to 5.2 percent (y/y) in the second quarter, mainly reflecting strong
external demand and some acceleration inpublic consumption. Though still weak, private consumption appears to be
picking up, indicating that the adjustment to the domestic fuel price hike last fall i s nearly complete. Nevertheless,
unemployment i s edging up and poverty rates remain high. Growth i s expected to pick up gradually over the course of
2006 and in2007, with IMFstaff projecting annual growth to reach 5.2 percent and 6 percent, respectively, as interest
rates are reduced. Nevertheless, there are risks to this outlook, most importantly a weakening of the international
environment and slow implementation of structural reforms.

Inflationary pressures have been declining. Headline inflation declined to 14.6 percent (yly) in September, from
17.1percent at end-2005, reflecting weak activity and some appreciation of the rupiah. Meanwhile, three-month
annualized inflation (both headline and core) has remained below 6 percent since the beginning of the year. On
current trends, Bank Indonesia's (BI's) end-year target of 7-9 percent for headline inflation i s well within reach.

The overall banking sector continues to perform reasonably well. Partly due to reclassification, nonperforming
loans (NPLs) increased substantially in 2005, especially at the two largest state banks, but the rise inNPLs moderated
in January-June 2006. The sharp deceleration in credit growth in 2006, as a result of high interest rates and weak
demand, will to some extent undermine bank profitability, but the expected reduction in interest rates should prevent
excessive adverse effects on banks. Capital adequacy ratios remain at over 20 percent.

External vulnerabilities continue to be reduced. The current account i s expected to remain in surplus in 2006.
Foreign exchange reserves, at about $39 billion after the repayment of remaining debt to the Fund of $3?4billion on
October 12, are equivalent to about 130 percent of short-term debt. The current account i s expected to move into small
deficit over the medium term as imports rise faster than exports, financed in large part by private sector capital
inflows. The ratio of external debt to GDP i s expected to be reduced to below 25 percent of GDP in 2011, from 48
percent of GDP in 2005. Nevertheless, the market volatility experienced in May/June 2006 i s a reminder that
Indonesia remains susceptible to adversedevelopments in international capital markets.

Macroeconomic and Structural Policies

The budgeted policy stance is broadly appropriate. The fiscal deficit targets of 1.3 percent of GDP for 2006 and
0.9 percent of GDP for 2007, as outlined inthe President's budget speech in mid-August, are appropriately geared to
further reducing the debt burden, to below 30 percent in 2010 (compared with 47 percent at end 2005), while
supporting activity in2006. After considerable under spending in 2005 and the first quarter of 2006, budget execution
improved inthe secondquarter of 2006.

Monetary policy continues to be geared to reducing inflation. BI's gradual approach to reducing its policy rate has
helped contain inflationary pressures and increase foreign exchange reserves. There should be room for further
gradual rate cuts given the benign inflation outlook and still large differential with U.S. interest rates, though the
authorities need to be careful that excessive rate decreases do not undermine their credibility and prospects for
achieving the 2007 inflation target of 5-7 percent. The clarity and consistency of statements by BI officials in
signaling the central bank's policy intentions, within the inflation targeting framework, has improved and i s essential
to maintaining credibility.

Steps are being taken to deal with NPLs at state banks. The recently signed Presidential decree allowing state

                                                         69

banks to provide haircuts on loans, together with stepped-up efforts to enforce collections, should help to resolve the
NPL problem, and avoid erosion of bank capital. IMFstaff continue to advise that medium-termstrategies be adopted
for the state banks, which should involve privatizing those that do not have a clear public policy objective. As regards
private banks, supervisors will need to remain vigilant to enforce regulations, and to monitor any deterioration in
credit quality.

The government is proceeding on a broad range of fiscal reforms. Adoption of the tax package now before
Parliament, along the lines endorsed by the business community, as well as modernization of the tax department, will
help enhance revenue collection while restoring investor confidence. The government's intention to better manage
potential risks stemming from the operations of public enterprises and private-public partnerships i s welcome.

To improve the businessclimate and boost growth, the government has prepared an impressive reform agenda.
Given the business community's concerns about implementation, some major early reform successes are needed to
increase confidence that the entire package will be implemented. The government's efforts to secure early approval of
customs, investment, and tax laws are welcome, which apart from the signaling effect would also bring immediate
benefits for trade and investment.

Fund staff recognizes the difficulties of amending labor legislation. Staff supports the authorities' intention to
press ahead with the introduction of regulations that ensure a balanced framework for labor-business relations. The
new regulations need to protect the rights of workers, while enhancing flexibility for business, in particular with
regard to severance pay and outsourcing requirements. More flexible labor markets will help Indonesia become more
competitive vis-his neighboring countries and should encourage employers to make hiring decisions more freely.

Status of IMF Relations

    Indonesia's Extended Arrangement with the Fund expired inDecember 2003. I t was followed by a close policy
    dialogue with the authorities through post-program monitoring (PPM) (until the recent repayment of all
    obligations to the Fund) and extensive technical assistanceactivities.

    The 2006 Article IV consultation and fifthPPM were completed on July 31.

    The next Fundmission i s scheduled for early December, and technical assistance missions from the Fiscal Affairs
    Department and the Monetary and Capital Markets Department will be inthe field during November.


    The Fundwelcomes Indonesia's early repayment of its obligations to the Fund.This is a reflection of the strength
    of the economy and especially, its balance of payments position. The Fund expects to continue a constructive
    dialogue with the Indonesian authorities through regular staff visits, technical assistance missions, and in the
    context of the annual Article IV consultations, the next of which i s scheduled for mid-2007.

                                           Annex 6: Fiduciary Aspects
BACKGROUND

This assessment of fiduciary aspects of the loan has drawn upon several studies on public financial
management and accountability that have been completed over the last 12 months, both internally within
the Bank and those undertaken by donor partners. Studies undertaken internally by the Bank include a review
of the progress made in implementing the secondDPL approved inyear 2005, the results of a supervision mission
to assess progress in implementing the World Bank-financed Government Financial Management and Revenue
Administration Project (GFMRAP) that became effective in October 2005, a review of the developments in the
audit sector and the findings of a Public Expenditure Review completed this year. We have also received and
considered specific inputs and advice from donor partners, including a progress report from the ADB on
implementation of the State Audit Reform Project and a recent report by IMF on Observance of Standards and
Codes on Fiscal Transparency. These provide the analytical underpinnings for public financial management and
fiduciary assessmentsfor this operation.

The reform agenda i s clearly being led by the Government, with support from several donor agencies, and has
consistently received good support under the current Government that has been in office for two years.
Deficiencies in Indonesia's PFM systems have been well documented in recent years and a reform program to
address these was formulated by the Government in their White Paper on Public Financial Management reforms
that was prepared in 2002. This reformprogram included, amongst others, the following main areas:

        Modernizing an outdated legal environment for PFM, by issuing a modern set of laws and implementing
        regulations;
        Reforming an opaque and inefficient budget formulation process, by discontinuing the separate recurrent
        and development budgets, and introducing performance budgeting systems and forward estimates;
        Reforming budget implementation, in particular the inefficient payments system and weak monitoring
        arrangements;
        Modernizing management of the Government Treasury, to consolidate fragmented cash management and
        make government banking arrangementsmore efficient;
        Introducing modem Government Accounting Standards and accounting practices to improve the reliability
        of Government financial reporting;
        Reforming arrangements for government auditing, in particular strengthening the mandate and capacity of
        Supreme Audit Institution (BPK), addressing overlaps between external and internal audit agencies and
        clarifying their respective roles and responsibilities.


Some specific aspects of reforms have been supported under the series of Development Policy Loans over the last
three years. A commentary on the progress in implementing these reforms i s given below to help determine the
attendant risks arising from the borrower's capacity to receive and manage this loan and help determine the
fiduciary arrangementsfor this operation.

CURRENTSTATUS OF PFMREFORM A SUMMARY           -

The more notable achievements accomplished and shortcomings are summarized below.
A. Regulatory Framework for Public Financial Management

Substantial progress has been made in establishing the regulatory framework for improved PFM by the
issue and development of appropriate implementing regulations under the troika of laws that were passed
by Parliament during 2003 and 2004, and the other complementary laws on Planning and Procurement that were
passedduring the same period.

                                                           74

The Law on State Finances (No. 17/ 2003), although at a highlevel of generality, underpins the reform of the
Government budgeting process and is currently under implementation. I t provides for the first time for such
basic budget processes as developing a macro-economic and fiscal framework, establishing a budget timetable,
setting out the budget information to be supplied to Parliament and the use of internationally recognized
classification standards (GFS) in developing the budget. Reforms being undertaken inthe budget formulation area
include the unification of the previous two separate budgets, the development of an MTEF system and the
introduction of performance based budgeting. A complementary piece of legislation has also been passed, viz., the
State Development Planning System Law (2004), which provides the legal basis for the national development
planning process, and for the linking of planning with budgeting.

The State Treasury Law (No. 1/ 2004) provides the basis for modernizing budget execution and reporting.
Treasury reforms being undertaken include steps to rationalize the current proliferation of government bank
accounts and to centralize cash management, simplify and speed up the payments system, and to improve reporting
on budget execution. Government financial reporting will also be improved through the implementation of
government accounting standards on a modified accrual basis, with an eventual move to full accrual accounting.

The State Audit Law (No. 15 / 2004) lays out the broad legal framework for the operation on the country's
supreme audit institution, BPK" to reinforce its position and mandate as an external audit institution
reporting to Parliament. While the audit law takes the important step of establishing BPK as the only external
auditor and the Supreme Audit Institution in the country, the Law does not adequately resolve some remaining
conflicts arising from BPK's role nor does it remove restrictions on its scope as articulated in some older
legislation, for example with respect to BPK's access to records of Tax Revenues department. Inaddition, the State
Audit Law did not cover the institutional arrangements for the management and oversight of BPK, for which a
separatelaw (BPK Law) has recently beenpassedand i s awaiting the President's approval.

Procurement Presidential Decree (S0/2003) introduces many improvements in the procurement regime in
Indonesia and lays out a plan for establishing a national policy formulation and oversight agency. An omnibus
procurement law i s envisaged for consolidating, clarifying and simplifying the numerous procurement rules and
regulations. This law, currently under preparation, would further strengthen the legal foundation for modernizing
public procurement.

An analysis of the recent PFM laws and their implementing regulations indicates that substantial progress
has been made in preparing and issuing the required implementing regulations, although some draft
regulations are pending formal release as of the date of this analysis. InIndonesia, given the traditionally high
level of generality in legislation, laws usually require one or more Government Regulations to define and govern
their implementation followed by Decrees from the President and or Ministers to establish the systems and
procedures necessary for their operation. The State Finances Law, Treasury Law and the State Audit Law are no
exception.

All the main implementing regulations for the State Finance Law have been issued, with the exception of a
detailed manual on implementing the national budget (Article 14), for which a draft has been prepared and i s being
finalized. The issued regulations include Government regulations on Government Work Plan, National
Government Departments Annual Plan and Budget Deficit. A set of Ministerial Decrees have also been issued to
regulate RegionalBorrowings and Adjustment Funds.

With respect to the State Treasury Law, a number of Government Regulations have been issued to regulate
aspects of budget execution and others are at an advanced stage of preparation. These include specific
financial management provisions for designated General Public Service Agencies (23/2005), promulgation of
Government Accounting Standards (24/2005), write off of State receivables (14/2005), State Asset Management
(6/2006) and Finance and Performance Reporting (8/2006). In addition, Ministerial decrees have been issued on
aspects such as management of inter-governmental debt and draft regulations are at an advanced stage of

preparation or approval, including cash management, internal controls and Budget Implementation, which are
awaiting issue pending the resolution of some institutional issues within the Government.

The Government Regulation on Government Internal Controls Systems (Pengendalian Inter Pemerintah) is
expected to cover the objective, scope, and responsibility of internal control systems, including the role of internal
audit agencies. However, there remains disagreement within the Government over the scope and content of some
parts of the regulation, inparticular the implications for the future of internal audit agencies involved inexercising
internal controls. The issue of this regulation i s pendingresolution of this matter.

A new regulation on CashManagement has similarly been drafted and is considered a key step inunderpinningthe
proposed Treasury Single Account. The full implementationof this will involve shifting large cash surpluses of the
Government from commercial banks, where these are currently placed free of charge, to the central bank ina zero-
balance arrangement. An agreement has not yet been reached with the central bank on the extent and manner of
compensation to the government for these balances, pending which the draft regulation has not been issued.

A new Government Regulation on Budget Implementation (Pelaksanaan Anggaran) currently being drafted will
underpin new budget implementation procedures. Many of the procedures proposed in this draft regulation are
already being applied, under cover of interimcirculars issued by the Director General Treasury to help test out and
develop the detailed procedures.


For the State Audit Law (W2004) no implementing legislation has yet been issued. We understand that 7
Regulations will be required, comprising Government auditing standards, out-sourcing of experts to BPK, issue of
audit summons, investigative audits, issue of fraud audit findings, issue of audit reports by BPK to Parliament, and
guidance on settlement of losses to the State. For many of these, inparticular the more important ones on auditing
standards and issue of audit reports to Parliament, drafts are ready and under discussion with members of the
Executive (e.g. the Accounting and Auditing Standards Committee) and the Parliament. These regulations will be
issued by the BPK Board, not by the Government, and the enabling legal provisions for this authority are now
included in the recently passed(but not yet ratified) BPK Law referred to above.

In conclusion, the regulatory framework to enable and guide the implementation of PFM reforms is now
substantially in place, and the year ahead will see the focus shift more clearly to implementation, where the
challengeswill no doubt be substantial.

B. Early Experience inImplementing Reforms

The Government has recognized early some key challenges in moving beyond the development of
appropriate laws and regulations to their effective implementation. As an integral part of the change
management process, the Government reorganized the Ministry of Finance. This was accomplished reasonably
well during 2005, in particular by creating a new DGBudget and Fiscal Balance and a new DGTreasury, broadly
inline with earlier IMFrecommendations. Boththe budget and treasury functions have been strengthened by these
changes. Inparticular DGTreasury now overseesall treasury functions including cash and debt management, fixed
asset management and government fiscal reporting.

Most recently in October 2006, the Minister of Finance has sought to further strengthen and synergize the
management of reforms, by setting up an office for Policy Analysis and Harmonization reporting directly to
her. I t i s anticipated that this office will help expedite and coordinate the completion of the reformagenda.

Budgeting Reforms

New measures that have been introduced through the new State Finance Law are gradually improving fiscal
transparency. Budget documents for 2005 and 2006 have become more comprehensive and informative. These


                                                              76

improvements include (i) new economic and fiscal framework (ii) expenditure classification, including
                            the                                          clearer
the integrationof routine and development budgets, and presentation of budgets consistent with the GFS 2001.

Planning and budgeting functions have also been clarified, although more needs to be done to improve
qualitative aspects and improve coordination between Bappenas and Ministry of Finance. Planning and
budgeting have been integrated by formally linking line ministry work plans with budgets. The respective roles of
BAPPENAS and MOF in budget preparation have been clarified, with BAPPENAS assuming full responsibility
for the planning process (including preparation of the work plans) and MOF having the leading role in the
formulation of the annual budget. However, early experience suggests that there i s a need to further strengthen
cross agency coordinationbetween BAPPENAS, MOF and the line ministries to facilitate the move to performance
based budgeting process. Improvements include the timely submission of budgets to Parliament within the budget
cycle, although there are persistent concerns about the quality of performance budgets submitted by Line
Ministries.The implementation of the MTEF has not been very effective to date and will require more intensive
training and wider dissemination of the concept.

Some specific implementationexperience during 2005 and 2006 i s highlighted below:

a. A new functional and program classification consistent with the IMFGFSM 2001 for cashbasedtransactions
has been implemented. Budget allocations have begun to be based on sector strategies and programs rather than
historical expenditures. However the program classification requires further review to ensure it adequately reflects
the objectives of or the programmanagement approach used by individual spending ministries.

b. The earlier model of separate recurrent and development budgets have now been formally unified, with
components from the former development budget reclassified to the appropriate economic categories. However,
unification of the underlying substance of these two groups may not be entirely consistent inall agencies.

c. Progress inimplementing MTEFhas been limited. Forward estimates have been introduced for the first time,
although much more work needs to be done to fully adopt the MTEF approach. The Work Plan regulations provide
for indicative budget ceilings to be issued for two years out but in the 2006 budgets indicative ceilings have been
issuedonly for the forthcoming budget year.

d. There has been limited progress in developing a more performance oriented budget. Although a start was
made with the fiscal 2005 budget, full implementation will take more time as there appears to be limited
understanding and no clear strategy yet developed to operationalize this concept. Spending ministries do not appear
to fully understand the objectives of the new work plans and budget documents and so far these appear to have
been an extensive paper exercise with little discernible impact on resource allocation. Strong collaboration
between DG Budget and Bappenas is needed, ensuring that they act collaboratively and consistently and that
planning i s fully integrated into the budget as envisaged under performance budgeting.

e. Severe delays were experienced in issuing budget authorization documents (DIPA) in 2005, although
significant improvements were made in 2006. In the past years, these documents were often made available to
line agencies not until after April of the running year. As a result, a high proportion of the total funding provided
under the approved budget to spending agencies could not be executed or disbursed until the last quarter of the
fiscal year. Often virtually no such commitments or disbursements could occur in the first two quarters other than
purely routine expenditure (such as salaries of civil servants).

Among the main reasons for this delay during 2005 was the inadequate understanding of the newly
introduced budgeting procedures among a large number of spending agenciesinthe Government. It appears
that Line Agencies hadnot been adequately trained inthese. These budgeting changes introduced a whole new way
of preparing budget estimates, performance based budgets and underlying work plans, and these often had to
undergo several revisions before being accepted, leading to significant delays. In addition, current procedures
require that spending patterns are set centrally and mid-year adjustments are often difficult. Allowing for more

                                                          77

flexibility in this process without putting in place other safeguards will speed up spending but may increase the
riskof excess waste.

For the budget year 2006, the DIPA issue processseemed to have stabilized. These documents were issued for
all spending agencies in January 2006 and the disbursement position i s better than before, although spending
patterns have still not improved dramatically, with aggregate government spending as of September 2006 standing
at 62% of the budgeted funds. A related constraint that continues to affect expenditure patterns i s the absence of
authorization to carry-over of unspent commitments at year-ends, given the one-year life of all DIPAs.Although a
one-time waiver for this was authorized in 2005, this needs to be regularized for all development expenditure so
that this can be distributed more evenly through the year.

Treasury Management Reforms

The implementation experience with Budget execution processes i s less satisfactory, with a gradual
modernization at Ministry of Finance and very little impact on the control environments at spending
agencies. Some highlights are discussedbelow:

a.         Introduction of a Treasury Single Account (TSA) and automation of the treasury payments system
are key reforms and progress in pilot testing of the underlying concepts has been slow. Although a detailed
work plan for this was developed in 2005, especially for the establishment of the Treasury Single Account (TSA)
which involves the progressive rationalization of the vast number of separate government bank accounts.
Supporting the management of state finance resourcesrequires minimizing central government idle casldcash float,
reducing maintenance cost of too many accounts, facilitating cash forecasting and cash management control, as
well as enhancing transparency and accountability of central government banking.

Pilots for zero balance transitory accounts have been completed in three locations: Jakarta, Bekasi and Batam
since 2005. Expansion and roll-out of the pilot are on hold due to delays inpassage and issuance of Government
Regulation on Cash Management (see above). A committee has been established within DG Treasury to seek
quotations from commercial banks for remuneration of transactional banking services (TBS). An extension of this
pilot to an additional 50 KPPNs has commenced effective Sept. 1, 2006. Gradual implementation of TSA
(expenditure and revenue accounts) inall KPPNs i s proposed for mid-2007 ina phasedmanner.

In the meanwhile, the discovery of unauthorized and unaccounted Bank accountscontinues. A recent audit
by BPK has discovered Government bank accounts at 63 banks which are in the personal name of a Government
official, a project or a Badan (unincorporated body or agency). Many of these were not earlier known to the
Treasury. These include 680 current accounts and 623 term deposit accounts. Total balances in these accounts
amount to a sizable Rp8.5 trillion. The Ministry of Finance has so far not been successful ininventorizing all such
accounts that exist and bring them into accountability and control.

As part of improving cash management there i s a need to reduce delays in revenue remittances into government
bank accounts, which sometimes can be up to a few months. For instance, balances in the services clearing account
in 2005 from the ReforestationAccount were not deposited in the State Treasury for a few months. Treasury cash
management and payment modernization will be supported by the planned computerized treasury information
management system (SPAN) under the World Bank GFMRAP project.

b. Some parts of the MOF (Directorate of Treasury) organization has been rationalized to support
rationalization of the treasury system. Regional accounting offices (KAR) and local verification offices
(KASIF'A) have been merged with regional administrative offices (KANWIL) under DG Treasury, with treasury
payment and accounting field offices (KPPNs) coming under KANWILs. While there has been training for MOF
staff this has not yet extended to staff in the Spending Ministries, who thus lack awareness of the proposed
changes. This i s likely to severely constrain effective implementation of on-going reforms at the centre. Further
complementary ongoing reengineering of business processes i s needed to introduce internal checks and balances,


                                                          78

and diminishthe potential for rent-seeking that hitherto characterized virtually all stages of budget formulation and
execution inIndonesia.

c. The procurement of a new treasury system (SPAN) to be funded by the Bank under GFMRAPis delayed.
The new treasury management system is intended to improve accounting and reporting for treasury transactions,
increasetransparency inbudget formulation and budget execution and reduce transaction costs.

d. The development and adoption of new Government Accounting Standards is a good step forward to
improve financial reporting, though the requirement of the State Treasury law of the complete introduction
of accrual accounting by 2008 is very ambitious and may not be realistic. The Accounting Standards
Committee (KSAP) has been in operation for a few years now during which time they have put in place a due
process for development of Government accounting standards that are well aligned with comparable international
standards (IPSA'S). An initial set of 11government accounting standards have been formally adopted through the
issue of a Government Regulation (No. 25/2005). These standards are on a modified accrual basis, and the
intention i s to move to full accrual standards by 2008.

Effective 2006, these accounting standards are now already applicable across government and each ministry and
agency i s expected prepare a financial accountability statement based on these standards. Double entry accounting
has now been introduced in all Ministries, and standalone accounting packages have been installed at line
ministries in a modular fashion - SPM for Budget execution and SA1for accounting and reporting. Implementing
these new standards across government however i s a major challenge and will require extensive training as well as
some re-engineering of related business processesinline ministries and agencies. This task will impose challenges,
given inherently weak capacities inMinistries.

e. In the area of fiscal reporting, progress has been made by the Ministry of Finance in the timely
preparation of aggregate financial statements, including a Balance Sheet, Budget Realization report and
Cash Flow Statement. Government prepared an-aggregate set of financial statements for the year 2004 for the
first time. This exercise was repeated for fiscal 2005, and aggregate financial statement were handed over to BPK
end-March 2006 for audit, well within mandatory time targets. This aggregation process is a complex one,
involving consolidation of over 19,000 budget reporting units from 1000 reporting entities, and this i s a
commendable achievement. Improvements have also been made in the comprehensive disclosures made in these
financial statementson the accounting policies and details on revenues, expenditures, assets and liabilities.

The DG Treasury now has the ability to generate flash reports on budget disbursements (LRA)and revenue
receipts transactions conducted through the local State Treasury Service Offices (KPPN) and the State
Treasury, though there appears to be a reluctance at MOF to publicly disclose such (unaudited and
unreconciled) data. Mechanisms and procedures for the preparation of Budget Realization Reports (LRA)are
outlined in PerMen Keu 59/2005. Delays have been reduced in the aggregation of KPPN transactions and
reconciliation of these with ministry work unit (satker) records by the DG Regional Offices (Kanwil DJPBN). In
the past, such differences have been significant, including differences with some agencies within the Ministry of
Finance such as Tax and Customs & Excise Directorates. Inpreparing the Financial Statements for FY 2005 such
differences continue to be significant, although early in FY 2006 some new processes have been introduced to
allow reconciliation between treasury offices and line agencies on a monthly basis at KPPN level and quarterly
basis at provincial / Kanwil level. The relevant instructions and operating manuals to guide this process have been
issued as Circular Letters (SE)by Ministryof Finance.

Activity Lists (DIPA) to enable reporting of expenditure as a percentage of allocated budget resources. Although
LRAs" basedon new classification structures can be generatedwith ease, the periodic publishing of suchreports -
for internal and external purposes - has yet to be institutionalized. Rather, reporting i s ad-hoc and limited to

Internal Audit and Controls

The mandates of the three internal audit institutions continue to be unclear as is the allocation of resources
between them. Internal audit i s within the remit of the BPKP, the Inspector General (IG) of each ministry, and the
Bawasdas of municipalities and regional governments. Ironically, even with (or perhaps because of) multiple
internal audit agencies in existence, the internal audit function continues to be a chronically weak link in
governance.

At present the BPKPs mandate is unclear. It can assist IGs, municipalities and regions upon invitation, it
provides training in auditing for these entities. Although its mandate has reduced significantly, it maintains fully
staffed decentralized offices in some 26 provinces in the country.

The IGs have different roles in different ministries. Their actual mandate, staffing and activities are determined
by the minister in charge of the ministry in which they operate. The IGs are therefore not operating as one
institution, but rather as 20 institutions corresponding to the number of ministries and ministers. Generally they
focus on routine technical and performance audits (including compliance with technical standards) conducted on a
random basis or in accordance with an approved annual audit plan, but not on a risk based methodology. Reports
are not available to the public or the DPR. Professional backgrounds of staff IG therefore normally include
technical qualifications and not accounting or auditing.

The audit of financial transactions in the 35 provinces, 442 districts and 2 regencies are undertaken in a
very complex institutional set up. The BPK i s the external auditor of all local governments. In each district and
province a Bawasda i s established to undertake what they call `general' audit of regional budget expenditure.
However, Bawasda's generally do not have the skills of staff to undertake all audit responsibilities.

The allocation of resources between the internal audit institutions is unclear and there does not seem to be
strong cooperation and coordination between them. This hampers the realization of synergies between the
institutions. The audit of local governments inparticularly lacks transparency and is needlessly complex.

Internal control mechanisms across government need strengthening. These comprise all rules, procedures and
business processes that are applied in the execution of the budget, to ensure that public funds are safeguardedfrom
waste, inefficiency and fraud and comply with approved laws, regulations and policies. A key internal control in
public expenditure management is the Expenditure Control and Payment System. In order to strengthen the
responsibility and accountability over the appropriated budget by the line ministry, recent budget execution
regulations have transferred the function of payment order issuance to the line ministries, resulting in a better
segregation of functions of verifying transactions and issuing payments. While this segregation of duties is well
designed, implementation experience i s not entirely satisfactory. One endemic weakness that has been noted in
accounting practices has been the quality of accounting evidence that i s accepted for payments by the Treasury as
well as Line Agency financial staff.

Procurement Policy

The issuance of Presidential Decree (80/2003) promotes the basic principles of procurement: transparency,
open and fair competition, economy, and efficiency. I t covers all areas of procurement that use public funds and
contracting entities at all levels of government. The decree meets most of what i s generally regarded as accepted
international practice. However, the pace of Indonesia's commendable decentralization reforms has had an impact
on public sector procurement as different levels of government - from ministers, governors, and even mayors - are
                                                         81

able to issue their own decrees, regulations, and instructions. The plethora of regulations i s often inconsistent with
one another and many do not meet accepted international practice. As a consequence, national procurement
reforms and the need for national procurement policies and standards have become urgent. One key step in this
direction i s the creation of a National Public Procurement Office (NPPO), which the Government hadcommitted to
undertaking in 2001. However, progress has lagged in making the NPPO fully operational. The Presidential
Decree 80/2003 required that the NPPO be established by January 1, 2005. A lot of work has already been done
and i s on-going, inbothpreparing for the establishment of the NPPO and also indeveloping institutional capacity.

This measure however remains a contentious issue and the Minister of Finance has stated that many other
aspects of the procurement framework could be improved beyond the specific issue of the NPPO. For
example, a centralized training and procurement practitioner's certification system i s under development with
certification at various levels. The system has commenced targeting government procurement practitioners with
initial certification to the basic level by means of a computerized testing system.

Decentralization

Indonesia has transformed itself from one of the most centralized countries in the world to one of the most
decentralized countries within a short period of time. In 2004, the regions commanded about 35 percent of total
Government spending. Given the sharp increase in regional transfers in 2006, the regional share in total public
investment i s likely to rise above 50 percent in 2006. One of the most important pending decentralization reforms
i s on on-lending and on-granting regulations and the transition to market-oriented system of sub-national credit.
The Government has recently issued revisions to on-lending and on-granting decrees to facilitate immediate
support for local infrastructure finance and service delivery. More work i s however needed to establish a
regulatory framework for the development of more market-based system of sub-national credit, including the
issuanceof regional bonds (based on Law 33/2004).

The BAPPENAS blue book decree has been signed and issued (PerMen PPN No. 5 2006) and the on-granting and
on-lending decrees have been signed by the Minister of Finance.

Respective roles and responsibilities of MOF and Home Affairs with respect to financial reporting by local
governments have not been fully resolved, although some amendments to earlier decrees have been made to
harmonize local government financial reporting with that applicable to central government.

The foreign exchange control environment is assessed to be generally satisfactory. The country i s no longer

GFMRAP project and other initiatives. These will generate some fiduciary risks for this operation. Although the
pace of implementing the planned reforms has been somewhat slow over the last year, the trajectory of reform i s in
the right direction and the current Government continues to demonstrate a commitment to continue the task of
completing the planned reforms in public financial management. Taking these into consideration, we do not
propose putting inplace any additional fiduciary arrangementsfor this operation.

                               Annex 7. Technical Note on Investment Climate

A. Description

Following a series of macroeconomic shocks in 2005, the government took measures to stabilize the economy
during the second half of the year, including raising interest rates and fuel prices. This contributed to a decline in
growth and investment which lasted through the first half of 2006. However, with inflation dropping from 18% in
February 2006 to a projected value of less than 7% by the end of the year, interest rates trending down, and a stable
exchange rate, investment i s expected to pick up in the second half of 2006. Real sector indicators such as
purchases of cars, motorcycles and cement were turning up sharply in the third quarter of 2006 and imports of
capital goods were beginning to recover, suggesting that a recovery of investment was already underway.

Inaddition to an effective macro stabilization program, the Government initiated a series of reform measures
designed to improve the business environment in 2006. This included three policy packages - on the investment
climate, infrastructure and finance; submission of a new investment law to Parliament; the drafting of four
presidential regulations to implement the investment law; revisions to three tax laws incorporating suggestions
from the business community; activation of a team to review local government regulations and cancellation of 85
local regulations harmful to businessactivity; and simplification of visas and work permits for expatriate workers.

B. Challenge

Despite an improved macroeconomic outlook and reform efforts, Indonesia's competitive position i s deteriorating
relative to other countries. According to Doing Business 2007, Indonesia reduced the time to start a business from
151 days to 97 days but nonetheless experienced a decline in its overall ranking on the ease of doing business,
from 13lStto 135'hplace. This happened because other countries reformed more rapidly than Indonesia, notably
China, India and Thailand. The pace of reform will need to accelerate if Indonesia i s to avoid slipping further
behind its neighbors.

The effects of a decade of low investment are now apparent, with problems of insufficient capacity and power

Institutional Framework for Private Investment

The Government has recognized the urgency of the issue and has made infrastructure a national priority. The
Government i s seeking to gradually restore private infrastructure investment to its pre-crisis level. The new
institutional arrangements for infrastructure public-private partnerships (PPPs) represent a landmark shift in
infrastructure governance, in direct contrast with pre-1997 modalities for attracting private investment which gave
an inappropriate share of rents to political insiders and left the Government bearing an inappropriate share of the
risks.

A Presidential decree (Perpres 67) was issued in November 2005 which requires open and transparent competitive
bidding for infrastructure PPPs, and a risk management framework has been created to ensure an appropriate
allocation of risk between the State and private developers. An inter-ministerial committee (the KKPPI) has been
given a coordinating role, and i s supported by a Secretariat. Line ministries, SOEs and regulatory agencies are
responsible for preparing PPP transactions, but if government support (either guarantees or direct fiscal support) i s

sought, the transaction must be vetted by the KKPPI and the RiskManagement Unit within the Ministry of Finance
must approve the allocation of government support.

The new institutional framework i s fully in place but not yet functional. A Central PPP Unit within the KKPPI
Secretariat, and PPP Nodes within line ministries, have largely been established and being staffed with the focus of
building expertise in project preparation. Within the Ministry of Finance, the Risk Management Unit has been
established and staffed.    Around US$250 million has been set aside within the Budget to be allocated to
government support for PPP projects. It i s intended to create a much more substantial "Guarantee Fund", which
would grow over time and be professionally managed, to be used inthe event of calls upon government guarantees.

Moreover, the new institutions will take time to bear fruit interms of new private investment. The difficulties were
highlighted with a high profile Infrastructure Summit in January 2005, which sought US$22.5 billion of private
investment in 91projects. The reaction to these offerings was disappointing: investor confidence was inevitably
affected by the post-crisis renegotiation of many infrastructure projects; existing policy blockages remained to the
preparation of bankable projects; and in practice the projects offered had not been well-prepared. None of the
projects has reached financial closure.

A second Infrastructure Summit was held in early November 2006. Although a wide range of potential projects
was again offered, particular emphasis was placed on 10 "model" transactions, worth around US$4 billion. I t i s
intended that for these transactions, project preparation will be more thorough than has been previously the case.
Experience gained inthese model transactions will serve to scale up the PPP program.

Sound preparation of infrastructure PPPs i s likely to take 18 months to 2 years to financial closure. In the

government support to assist in achieving financial closure for projects that have not been competitively bid or
which did not envisage government support in their bidding documents. While the need for investment is pressing,
whether or how the Government provides support to such projects will be an important test of the distance
Indonesia has traveled towards international standards for the procurement of infrastructure PPPs.

                                Annex 9: Technical Note on Decentralization

In2001, Indonesialaunchedanambitious programofdecentralization. Theefforthaditsgenesisintwo laws, both
promulgated in May 1999: Law 22/99 on administrative matters and Law 25/99 on fiscal and finance issues. In
December 2000, Law 34/00, an additional and essential piece of decentralization legislation on regional
government taxation was passedby parliament (DPR). These three laws were supplemented by a large number of
implementing regulations and ministerial decrees.

Inlate 2004, Indonesia initiated are-design of its basic decentralization regulatoryframework by issuingrevisions
to the two major pieces of legislation, Law 32/04 on administration and Law 33/04 on fiscal affairs. Many of the
basic lower order follow-on legislative instruments have now been completed, although a few remain to be
produced. Draft amendmentsto Law 34/00 have also been finalized and are now being reviewed by DPR.

Now well into the second phase of the design of its decentralization architecture, Indonesia has accumulated six
years of experience with the implementation of its regional autonomy program. This note examines the major
challenges indecentralization and the government responseto those challenges and provides and assessment of the
government response. The discussion on these topics i s organized according to five themes: spending, taxes,
transfers, savings, and borrowing. The note concludes with a short summary.

Challenges and Government Response

Spending

The substantive basis for assigning services to various levels of government has not been made obvious and
expenditure assignments have been imprecisely defined. Central departments have encroached upon (apparent)
sub-national functions, thus confounding service delivery responsibilities and mechanisms and undermining
outcomes. While the evidence regarding citizen satisfaction with local service delivery i s ambiguous, there i s a
widespread belief among central government officials (and others) that sub-national governments do not spend
their funds wisely and that the quality of local public service delivery is sub-optimal.

MoHA will soon issue a government regulation, based on Law 32/04, on the assignment of services across levels
of government, which it hopes will clarify functional responsibilities. The official policy of government as outlined
inLaw 33/04 is to re-channel central agency spending inthe regions ondecentralized tasks through the DAK.One
of the main strategies of MoHA and central technical agencies i s to improve the responsiveness of sub-national
spending to needs and quality of service delivery i s by mandating the achievement of national minimum service
standards (MSS), as basedon GR 65/05.

Taxes

Local government own-source revenues are negligible (just 8 percent of total local revenue budgets). No
significant (traditionally local) tax bases (e.g. property tax) have been decentralized to kabupatedkota. Instead
local governments have been allowed to create their own taxes and charges. Inthe absence of sufficient central
controls, this authority has resulted in many new revenue mechanisms but little revenue generation. The
conventional wisdom is that many sub-national own-source revenues are either nuisances or economically harmful.

MoF has drafted revisions to Law 34/00 on regional taxation, which have been forwarded to DPR for review.
Pending amendments to the law do not devolve additional authority over any significant local tax bases (e.g.
property tax) to local governments. The draft revisions do, however, restrict sub-national governments' authority to
levy taxes and charges to those enumerated on positive lists. The government expects that the new policy will
reduce the proliferation of nuisance and harmful sub-national taxes.




                                                          90

Transfers

Challenges confront all major types of intergovernmental transfers, including revenue sharing, DAU, and DAK.
The main concern with central-regional revenue sharing i s the late disbursement of those funds, which i s
sometimes delayed untilthe third and fourth quarter (and occasionally the following fiscal year). The mainpurpose
of the DAU i s to equalize fiscal capacities across sub-national governments to deliver basic services; while it does
succeed in equalizing fiscal capacities to a certain extent, its performance could be much improved inthis regard.
The precise purpose of the DAK (above and beyond funding capital needs of fiscally poor regions) is unclear and
the grant has become fragmented across a growing number of sectors and uses. A final concern about the transfer
system ingeneral i s that it provides no incentives for sub-national governments to improve their performance.

Law 33/04 stipulates that shared revenues must now be transferred to sub-national governments on a quarterly
basis. The law also makes two major changes to the DAU. DAU allocations will now be required to cover 100
percent of the salaries of sub-national civil servants; and in 2008 the hold harmless rule linked to DAU
distributions will no longer be operable. The above noted concerns with the DAK have not yet been addressed.
Many MoF officials support the idea of structuring performance incentives into the transfer systembut this has not
yet been translated into any concrete action.

Savings

Sub-national governments have saved significant amounts of their revenue since decentralization. As of end
August 2006 cumulative sub-national reserves amounted to about Rp 100trillion (or about 50 percent of aggregate
sub-national spending in 2005 and over 3 percent of GDP inthat year). Reservesare not being put to good purpose,
such as to pay off arrears on past sub-national loans or to finance public capital development.

The government i s keenly aware of the situation but has not yet developed a policy agenda related to sub-national
reserves.

Borrowing

Borrowing has been underutilized by sub-national governments to finance infrastructure. From 1975 through 2004
it i s estimated that all sub-nationals borrowed just Rp 5,747 billion in nominal terms or Rp 26,168 billion in
constant 2004 terms; these figures represent about 0.2 percent and 0.9 percent of 2004 GDP, respectively. The
principal concerns with sub-national borrowing have been the general lack of demand and creditworthiness of sub-
national borrowers and constraints on supply of finance, particularly as related to the government's on-lending
mechanism.

MoF has drafted legislation that will provide a basis for working out PDAh4 debt and officials intend to do the
same for PEMDA. The regulatory framework for on-lending has now been completely revised. Inaddition, MoF i s
presently developing the regulatory framework to support the issuanceof sub-national government bonds.

Assessment

Spending

The draft government regulation on service assignment does provide some additional clarity on functional
responsibilities compared to the regulation that i t will replace, although not as much as i s desirable. The draft
regulation notes that further clarification will be forthcoming via individual central department decrees. This
provides central agencies with the opportunity to legally re-centralize expenditure assignments as they see fit. It
seems that it i s no longer feasible to correct these flaws inthe draft legislation.

Central departments have so far managed to forestall implementation of the MoF agenda to re-channel their
spending in the regions on decentralized tasks through the DAK. They have been able to go their own way at least
                                                            91

in part because of legal ambiguities about precise service assignments across levels of government. Central
departments' cooperation on reducing their expenditures on apparently decentralized function will likely remain
elusive.

MoHA's agenda on minimum service standards i s very ambitious. The innate difficulty of the exercise coupled
with the less than satisfactory experience with a similar effort in the recent past casts doubt on the feasibility of the
complete program. While full implementation i s more than a little uncertain, it i s possible that the exercise may at
least serve to help clarify service responsibilities (since, in principal, service responsibilities must be clear before
standards are set). And it may prompt sub-national governments to set their own standards for use in planning and
budgeting.

Taxes

Given that draft amendments to Law 34/00 do not devolve additional authority over the property tax (or other
significant local taxes) to kabupatenkota (or provinces), further reform in this area would appear to be a longer
term goal. Draft revisions to Law 34/00 that restrict sub-national government own-source revenues to those on
positive lists are useful. Government's ability to monitor compliance with forthcoming regional taxation legislation
will be a key determinant inthe success or failure of the intended reforms.

Transfers

Although Law 33/04 requires sub-national shares of domestic natural resource revenues to be transferred on
quarterly basis, MoF officials are not optimistic that this can be implemented. This i s because the determination of
natural resource allocations are based in the first instance on sectoral ministry production estimates. And sectoral
departments are under no obligation to provide their production estimates to MoF in a timely manner. The regular
and predictable transfer of revenue shares (especially those related to gas and oil) to sub-national governments i s
likely to remain a challenge.

Recent and pending changes to the DAU allocation mechanism will have an uncertain net impact on fiscal
equalization. The new policy (already being implemented) to cover 100 percent of the civil service wage bill of
sub-nationals i s relatively un-equalizing, for example. In addition the new policy provides an unfortunate
disincentive for sub-national governments to rationalize their civil services. On the other hand, the intended
removal (in2008) of the hold harmless rule should improve equalization outcomes. A key concern i s MoF's ability
to follow through with the intended reform. Lobbying by rich regions to delay implementationhas already begun.

Inattention to the design and use of the DAK has now become especially worrisome, given recent growth in size of
the grant and the potential for additional increases. A recent MoF and Bappenas plan to establish a working group
to addresspertinent DAK issues may help provide the beginnings of a solution.

The general idea of integrating performance incentives into the transfer system i s a good one. Significant structural
change to the transfer system (and revisions to appropriate legislation) to operationalize the concept would,
however, be required. As such, this must be considered a worthy but long-term goal.

Savings

Two aspects to the problem of excessive sub-national reserves can be distinguished. The first concerns the
appropriate use of the current stock of funds. In this regard sub-national governments might be encouraged to
employ reserves to clear away repayment arrears on past loans or to invest in local public infrastructure, for
example. This will be a challenging but not impossible task.

The second relates to the underlying reasons for the over accumulation of reserves in the first instance. To the
extent feasible, the basic causes of the excessive build-up of reserves need to be corrected. This might require,
among other things, the central government to address head-on the means by which the pools of finance for natural
                                                           92

resource revenue sharing and the DAU are determined. Currently funding i s based on fixed percentages of
domestic revenues, which are influenced significantly by oil prices. Steep increases inoil prices lead to significant
increase in domestic revenues, transfers, and ultimately surpluses. Reform in this area will be difficult, given the
inevitable resistanceby sub-national governments.

More generally, the central government needsto develop its own stance regardingthe proper accumulation and use
of reserves and provide guidelines to sub-national governments on same. Current legislation i s inadequate both in
terms of establishing the basis for a sound central policy stance and as a course of action for sub-national
governments.

Borrowing

The legislative initiatives of MoF to provide a framework for PDAM and PEMDA debt work-outs are useful. The
key will be for government to develop a pragmatic implementationplan and follow-up with concerted action.

The extent to which recent regulatory reforms to the government's on-lending mechanism will facilitate increased
lending to creditworthy sub-nationals i s unclear. The new regulatory framework i s probably an improvement over
the previous one. However, loan approval processes are likely to remain burdensome and, as such, will create
disincentives for sub-nationals to borrow from or via the center and, in any case, will cause delays in project
implementation and finance. The new system continues to bind on-lending to revenue generating projects, thereby
unnecessarily limiting the volume of lending.

Ultimately, what i s probably neededin order to improve the performance of lending to sub-national governments i s
the adoption of approaches that rely less on direct government involvement and that are more market-oriented. In
this regard, MoFinitiatives to establish a regulatory framework for the issuanceof sub-national revenue bonds are
helpful. Several PDAM and PEMDA are now drawing up concrete plans to issue bonds as early as next year and
these efforts have received the support of MoF. Finally, MoF has recently approved the creation of the Indonesian
Water Fund, which i s a pooled infrastructure financing mechanism targeting well managed and fiscally healthy
PDAM. This is also a useful initiative in the context of the developing more market-oriented sub-national credit
markets and increasing the volume of sub-national finance.

Summary

The new and still emerging re-design of the decentralization regulatory framework devises some potential reforms
to the old system: increased clarity on service assignments, re-channeling of central spending on decentralized
tasks to sub-national budgets, the establishment of a positive list for sub-national taxation, insisting on the timely
payment of sub-national revenue shares, removal of the hold harmless condition attached to the DAU, and
finalization of procedures for on-lending and on-granting. Significant risks exist, however, to the successful
implementation of most of these agenda items. At the same time, some additional and substantial challenges have
been created by the new regulatory framework: delegation to central departments of the responsibility for further
modifying expenditure assignments and requiring the DAU to cover the sub-national civil service wage bill.
Finally, some long-standing and well-known problems have not been addressedby the new framework; particularly
important in this regard i s the lack of decentralization of tax authority to local governments.

Notwithstanding the above mentioned obstacles, there are some clear opportunities for advancing the
decentralization reform agenda. Some of these prospects relate to recently recognized challenges, such as those
concerning the over-accumulation of reserves by sub-national governments. Although nothing concrete has been
accomplished as of yet, the government i s insistently seeking out solutions. In other cases, the government i s
already taking action to make needed improvements. MoF's efforts to establish enhanced sub-national
creditworthiness through PDAM and PEMDA debt work-outs and its attempts to expand sources of sub-national
infrastructure finance by developing the sub-national bond market are especially important and merit support.

3. Indonesia's anti-corruption preventionefforts

The Government has laid out an anti-corruption strategy in two documents -- INPRES 5December 2004 and
the National Action Plan for the Eradication of Corruption (RAN-PK) from February 2005. INPRES 5 is the

reform measures from each institution to implement the presidential instruction. Both documents are
comprehensive in scope, covering prosecutions, prevention and education. However, neither document specifies

4. Civil service reform is coming on to the national agenda

The lack of administrative capacity and distorted incentives inthe government bureaucracy has constrained
policy implementation as well as delivery of public services. Although this challenge has been widely
recognized for many years, progress to date has been slow. However, there are very encouraging signs that the
Government i s ready to consider a more comprehensive reformprograminthis area

     Organizational structure: A large number of agencies with overlapping authority share responsibility for

6. The problem of "money politics"

The main driver of the increased momentum on anti-corruption and governance reform is the continued
transformation of Indonesia's political system. With direct elections for executive positions at all levels of
government, competing candidates are jockeying to demonstrate to voters their commitment to good governance
and the fight against corruption - issues that remain very highon the agenda of the Indonesian electorate. This i s
leading many politicians to search for innovative approaches that could make demonstrable progress on these
difficult issues.

But the link between political competition and incentives for governance reform is most threatened by
corruption in the very process of political competition itself - what is commonly referred to as "money
politics" in Indonesia. B y weakening the channels of accountability between competing politicians and the voters,
money politics undermines the very drivers of the broader governance and anti-corruption agenda and distorts the
entire process of policy-making in Indonesia. But the key areas of reform that could have an impact on reducing
money politics are not yet a major component of the governance reform agenda.

To combat money politics, further reforms inthree main areas will be required:

     Electoral and political party financing: With the advent of direct elections, the financing of election
     campaigns has taken on critical importance. Anecdotal reports suggest that payments by potential candidates
     to political parties to be included on party lists are still a widespread practice. And small incentive payments
     to voters from competing candidates are also common.               International experience suggests that a strong
     regulatory framework to ensure the transparency of election financing and political party finances i s essential
     including published and audited accounts of political parties and political contributions. The recent high
     profile corruption case against commissioners of the Central Elections Commission (KPU) presents an
     opportunity for pushing broader reforms in these areas.

     Legislative corruption: The issue of money and influence in the legislative process i s a problem that even the
     most advanced democracies continue to grapple with. Recent revelations of payments by a government
     ministry to the national legislature highlight that such practices are still quite common in Indonesia. The
     problem has been compounded, though, with the rising importance of local legislatures - DPRDs - in the
     decision-making on budgets and service delivery across Indonesia. Local legislatures generally face less
     public and media scrutiny than the national legislature and, therefore, may be more prone to such practices.

    Foundations (yayasans): The network of yayasans or quasi-state foundations closely linked to existing state

Challenges
Given the lagging development outcomes at a time when spending on service delivery has increased, the quality of
services has increasingly come to the forefront. This i s most notably the case for the education and health sectors.
Indonesian students' performance i s lower when compared with students inneighbouring countries, and teachers'

accountable for their performance. Survey data also show that Indonesian teachers have a lower level of formal

and teaching skills than teachers in the other countries. The performance of professional health workers similarly

comply with best practice protocols inmanaging specific health problems.

Current Efforts
1. Fuel Compensation Programs
Several ministries are making efforts in their respective sectors to enhance access for the poor as well as to
improve the quality of services. The fuel compensation programs (BBM-PKBS) are an example of a set of
programs intended to improve the poor's access to services and ensure public services were sufficiently resourced
to provide quality services. The BBM-PKBS program in education (BOS) was introduced in an attempt to ensure
schools had sufficient resources to deliver quality services at same time as costs were reduced for families. In
previous block grant programs, schools received the same amount of funding irrespective of the number of students
served. Under BOS, the size of the block grant i s based on enrolment figures, providing an incentive to schools to
expand their coverage. The simple formulation also makes program funding transparent, at least down to the
school management level, and more predictable. While the BOS program offers value in terms of improving
quality of schooling, it may not be as effective as a demand-side transfer in improving school affordability and
raising enrollment rates for children from poor families.

The BBM-PKBS program in health (Askeskin) i s aimed at increasing access for the poor as well as improving
service quality. It provides free-of-charge healthcare services at puskesmas (public health centers) and in-patient
treatment in third-class hospital beds for the poor. The program i s very similar to its predecessors in that it
provides block grants to puskesmas for operational requirements, equipment and medicines, and free access to
those poor who have health cards. The in-patient treatment component of the program (60% of funding) i s now run
by PT Askes. The program is, in essence, supply driven whereby funds flow through the providers and the poor
are not really empowered to question the quality of services they receive. There are no incentives for Puskesmas
staff to improve service quality for the poor, since they are not compensated on a fee-per-service basis. Even PT
Askes, which compensates hospitals on a fee-per-visit basis, receives funding from government inblock grant form
-regardlessofthequantityorqualityofservicesprovidedtothepoorthroughthehospitalsystem.Althoughthe
intention of the program was also to cover the services of private providers inrural areas, the mechanism through
which this would take place i s not clearly outlined, with the result that private providers are generally not included.
With the majority of poor people in Indonesia preferring to use private providers, this has serious consequences in
terms of increasing access to and utilization of healthcare services by the poor. Going forward, greater impact may
be possible if program design takes into account these program limitations. For instance, private providers need to
be included in the scheme and this would be possible if funding were channeled to the poor through alternative
mechanisms. Even a voucher system -similar to the health card -that operates on a fee-for-service basis would
imply a change in incentives for the service provider. The funding that goes through PT Askes also needs to be
more carefully monitored.

2. Teacher Performance and Education Quality
The Government of Indonesia has long recognized that the professional competency and performance incentives of
its teachers need to be enhanced. InDecember 2005 GO1passeda law that requires teachers to (a) have a minimum
academic qualification of at least 4 years of post-secondary education, (b) obtain practical experience as a
classroom teacher, and (c) pass a certification' examination which will examine proficiency in four competency
domains: pedagogical, professional, personal and social before being certified as a teacher. Once certified teachers
will receive a professional allowance, (equal to 100 percent of their base civil service salary), a functional
allowance, and a special allowance for assignment in disadvantaged areas, (also equal to 100percent of their base
civil service salary). Intotal this means that under the new law, all teachers will have their salary doubled upon
certification, with approximately a further 20 - 30 percent loading on base salary depending on their civil service
grade, and teachers in remote /disadvantaged areas will have their salaries tripled upon certification and on receipt
of the special location allowance.

This new strategy has the potential to be a major turning point in Indonesian education over the long term. Butthe
immediate task for the Government i s to manage this transformation for the incumbent teaching force with nearly
2.7 million in total, 80 percent of whom will not have the newly prescribed minimum four year postsecondary
qualification. The new teacher law states that during the next 10 years, the government will allocate budget inthe
form of scholarships or educational grants for incumbent teachers to enhance their academic qualification and
prepare for the certification examinations. B y 2016, all teachers in the teaching force should be certified. To
facilitate these changes the new law also emphasizes that teacher management and development needed to be
strengthened, specifically through teacher rewards and promotion; ability to evaluate students; and professional

3. Community Driven Development
Inaddition to programs delivered through health and education service providers, government also has a long
history of using communities to provide basic services to poor people through community driven development
projects.    These have demonstrated that communities are well equipped to build and maintain village
infrastructure, most often roads, bridges, water supply, irrigation systems, and markets. Post-decentralization,
KDP and UPP have made proactive efforts to promote collective action to address community level problems in
education. This has resulted in increased use of grants by communities to buildor renovate schools, as well as to
provide multi-year scholarships. Inthe health sector, communities using KDP grants have supported health service
posts (posyandu), provision of health information, and supplements to improve young children's nutrition levels.
The BBM-PKBS village infrastructure program was designed using the experience of KDP and UPP, although
time and other constraints prevented implementation of the participatory approach. More recently, the President
announced the government's intention to build on and expand KDP and W P through the creation of a national
community empowerment program (PNPM). Set up to be implemented in2000 sub-districts in 2007, the program
will be expanded to cover the entire country by 2009. As the PNPM i s institutionalized within national and local
budgets, it will be important to provide a facilitative operating environment, for example, by allowing automatic
carryover of unspent budget into the following year and greater simplicity inprocurement procedures.

4. Conditional Cash Transfers
Conditional cash transfers can be used to stimulate demand for services by the poor, and give them more of a voice
in frontline service provision. Programs that transfer resources directly to poor families have proved effective in
several other countries at raising enrolment and attendance and utilization of health facilities. Community-based
approaches that aim at increasing utilization are also relevant in the Indonesian context. The President has
announced that government will pilot both the `conventional' conditional cash transfer to households and a
community-based alternative starting mid-year 2007. Detailed design of the two approaches is currently underway,
led by Bappenas, and involving the Ministries of, Social Affairs, Education, Health, Public Works and Home
Affairs. Program agreements between the central government and district/municipal governments are expected to
lay out the rights and responsibilities of the respective levels of government as well as service providers in
implementing the household conditional cash transfer, so that it i s clear what i s expected of them and what they are
accountable for. A communications strategy and social mobilization strategy should also assist in ensuring all
stakeholders, most importantly household beneficiaries, understand the program and its expected results. As the
household program i s scaled up, government may wish to consider the experience of Brazil's Bolsa Familia
program in which the national government provides performance-based incentives to partner local governments.
Plans also include participatory monitoring and complaints resolution mechanisms, although they have yet to be
concretely defined.    I t will be important that household beneficiaries are able to use the services of non-
government health care providers (eg. midwives) to meet the health conditionalities.

5. Monitoring and Evaluation
Within central agencies (Bappenas and Finance) there i s a strong recognition that the effective and efficient use of
public funds i s crucial to government achieving its ambitious development goals. The ability to translate public
spending into improved services depends, in part, on having better information on what works, what doesn't, and
why. It was for this reason that government asked that the Bank support them in assessing the four major BBM-
PKBS programs in 2006. In addition to providing financial support for independent agencies to conduct the

data. Going forward, government would like to take greater ownership and institutionalize the use of program
assessments. To do so will require establishing a multi-year plan with appropriate budget and capacity building
measures, as well as clearly defined steps to ensure findings are used to inform decisions on whether to expand,

                                    Annex 12: Analysis of Recent Poverty Increase

Why didpoverty increase from 2005 to 2006?

On September 1 2006, the Central Bureau of Statistics (BPS) announced that the poverty rate in Indonesia had
risen to 17.75 percent in March 2006, up from 16.0 percent in February 2005. In what was the first recorded
increase in poverty incidence since the economic crisis, this meant that an additional 4 million people fell into
poverty during the period. How did such an increase come about? Economic growth during the period was 5.2
percent, not very different from the previous two years (5.6 percent and 4.4 percent, respectively) during which
poverty fell continuously. If the increase inpoverty cannot be explained through changes in growth patterns, other
events must have affected relative income distribution in such a way as to hurt the poor more than other groups.

The 2005-06 period witnessed several events likely to have impacted poverty. The fuel subsidy reduction in
October 2005 increased fuel prices by 114percent overall and tripled the price of kerosene. Meanwhile, rice prices
rose dramatically (33 percent) between February 2005 and March 2006. While the increase in fuel prices would
have had a limited direct impact on the poor, the large increase in kerosene and rice prices undoubtedly had a
larger negative effect. Conversely, the unconditional cashtransfer (UCT) program, which provided cashtargeted to
19.2 million poor and near-poor households, was introduced to soften the blow. This i s the world's largest ever
cash transfer program targeting some 34 percent of the population-well                           in excess of the number of poor
households in the population-with            each beneficiary family receiving about US$11per month.

World Bank analysis indicates the impact of the fuel price increase i s unlikely to have been the main driver behind
the poverty increase. It i s estimated that in the absence of the UCT, the fuel price increase would have reduced the
welfare of the poor and near-poor by some 5 percent, mainly through the tripling of kerosene price and the overall
inflationary impact. The cash transfer of around 14 percent of poverty line would have more than offset the
negative impact of the fuel price increase for those among the poor who received it. The overall impact depends on
the targeting performance of the cash transfer program. Simulations of the combined effect of the UCT and fuel
price increase point to a positive net income gain for the poorest two deciles (see Figure 6.ld in Chapter 6 on
Social Protection). The preliminary analysis of the UCT targeting (see Figure 6.3) suggests that the actual targeting
was between Scenario 2 (slight mistargeting) and Scenario 3 (greater mistargeting). Both scenarios pointed
towards net income gains for the poor. Even assuming significant mistargeting, the lower deciles were, on average,
more than compensated for the fuel price increases."

This means, then, that the increaseinthe rice price by 33 percent betweenFebruary 2005 and March 2006-mostly
due to shortages as a result of the ban on rice imports-is             the main factor behindthe increase inpoverty rates. Rice
plays an important role, as it makes up about 25 percent of the consumption basket of the poor. Moreover, three-
fourths of the poor are net consumers of rice. Thus rice price increases hurt far more people than they help. The
World Bank estimates that 3.1 million people may have been pushed into poverty as a result of the rice price rise
between February 2005 and March 2006. In other words, around 75 percent of the recently published increase in
poverty i s attributable to the rice price increase.

With rice prices still on an upward trend and the UCT program drawing to an end, poverty rates may well increase
again next year. The loss of the UCT will be a major income loss for the poor who receive it, while the conditional
cash transfer (CCT) program to be piloted next year will be too small to offset the impact of the ending of the
UCT. The immediate impact of plans to upscale the national community empowerment program on poverty will
also be limited. In order to reduce poverty in the short runthe government might consider both (i)                        continuing with
a scaled-down version of the UCT program, and (ii)                adopting a rice policy that promotes stabilized rice prices at
international levels.

Counting the poor inIndonesia
Inestimating poverty incidence, we require data on welfare measures and poverty line estimates. InIndonesia, the
welfare measure used i s that of per capita consumption. Households having a per capita consumption below the
poverty line are considered poor. The poverty line is usually anchored to the notion of a minimumcalorific intake
to fulfill nutritional requirement, usually set at 2,100 calories.

Household consumption i s obtained from the Susenas consumption survey. This module collects data on quantities
and expenditure for 218 food items, and expenditure for 109non-food items. Previously, official poverty estimates
could only be calculated once every three years, when the consumption module was administered to around 60,000
households across the country. Since the start of the Susenas panel survey, which administers the consumption
module to a nationally representative sample of around 10,000 households, national poverty statistics have been
updated yearly.

The most difficult issue incounting the poor i s deciding at which level to set the poverty line, and how to update it
over time so that it reflects the same level of welfare. The poverty line used by BPS has two components: one for
food and one for non-food items. Both the food and non-food components of the poverty line boil down to costing
a bundle of basic minimumneeds. This i s done for each province separately, by urban and rural areas, to account
for differences in consumption patterns.

There are two main methods in computing a poverty line in developing countries: the `food energy intake' and the
`cost of basic needs' methods. BPS uses an adaptation of the food energy intake method to obtain the food poverty
line. The chart below summarizes the method. In setting its national poverty line, BPS proceeded as follows: (i)t  I

The method used by BPS contains several flaws. First, the bundle changes over time and across regions, both for
the food andnon-food poverty line. It is therefore not guaranteedthat the bundle of goods reflects the same welfare
level. Food energy intake does not take into consideration changes in tastes, activity levels, relative prices or
publicly provided goods that can affect the relationship between food energy intake and total consumption
expenditure. For example, taste differences could result in a higher food poverty line for regions where the poor
tend to consume high cost per calorie foodstuffs (Wodon 1997; Ravallion and Lokshin 2003). Second, the bundle
of goods that i s used for the poverty line corresponds more to the typical expenditure of those above the poverty
line, not of the poor. It would be better to use a reference group that reflects the actual consumption patterns of the
poor. Poverty lines are quite sensitive to the choice of reference group (Pradhan et al, 2001) and using wealthier
reference groups generally results in higher poverty lines. Third, the sample size of the 10,000 Susenas panel
makes setting the poverty line separately for each province very sensitive to outliers. On average, there are only
around 33 households in the reference group for each region. Since not every household consumes every item
included in the bundle, the poverty line may be basedon actual consumption of just a few households.

BPS could consider adopting a `cost of basic needs' method to measure poverty. This method fixes the bundle of
goods across regions and time to set the poverty line. Only the prices are allowed to change to correct for regional
price differences and inflation. The advantage of this method i s that it guarantees that the poverty line is always
sufficient to purchase the same bundle of goods and, thus, i s sufficient to attain the same level of welfare. The
bundle of goods can be updated periodically to reflect changes in consumption patterns, but should remain the
same when doing comparisons of poverty over time.

                       Annex 13: EnvironmentalAnalysis of Fuel Subsidy Reductions

Under OP 8.60, the Bank i s required to consider whether a DPL operation i s likely to cause significant effects on
environment and natural resources, and for those significant adverse effects assess the borrower's systems to
reducing such effects. InDPL 2, the Bank committed to looking particularly at the fuel subsidyremoval in order to
better determine.the direction of environmental impact. Removing subsidies raises kerosene prices which most
Indonesians use to cook (prices tripled last October and are still 5 times lower than global prices). Rising fuel
prices may cause some switching to alternative fuels, including firewood in rural areas. However, there will also
be important environmental benefits, if consumers switch to less fuel-consuming means of transport and if
industries adopt fuel-conserving means of production. A decline infuel consumption would lead to environmental
gains inreducing air pollution. Higher energy prices or an overall decline in economic activity could also reduce
industrial demand for timber, which would be a benefit in reducing pressure on forests (though this effect i s likely
to be indirect and small). Thus, the fuel subsidy reduction may have both positive and negative environmental
effects, as fuel users adjust to higher prices.

Based on examination of recent fuel consumption data (2005 Q4), the early changes in fuel use appear to be small
relative to historical trends and recent fluctuations.       Effects on environmental and natural resources are not
expected to be significant. For the main pathways through which the fuel subsidy removal will lead to positive

    1    Only minor effects on overall economic growth, meaning any reduction in industrial timber demand would

Electricity Generation Fuel Use. There i s no clear change in fuel consumption for electricity production in the
final quarter of 2005. Rather, prior upward trends continued. Does this indicate that electricity generation
involves fixed facilities dedicated to this purpose, without the short term fuel switching options that may be
available to individuals or manufacturing firms? Note that Diesel fuel i s inconsequential to this story. The lower
graph shows that the last quarter of 2005 saw a 15% reduction in fuel oil use, substituted by a 45% jump ingas oil
use for an overall increase infuel use for electricity generation.




Didthe fuel usechangeshave broader economicimpacts?

The World Bank's Economic Social Update (March 2006) provides some general information on growth rates,
which shows that there was some slowdown due, at least partially, to higher energy prices. Although Indonesia's
overall GDP growth in2005 was 5.6 percent (the highest innine years), "by year-end the growth story was mixed,
with growth running well over 6 percent early in the year, then slowing as interest rates rose and consumers
adjusted to increases in fuel prices (114 percent in October alone, and 143 percent for the year). The slowdown
reflected a difficult economic adjustment needed to bring Indonesian energy prices closer to market prices, a vital
step to sustainable and higher, longer-term growth. The impact of the slowdown was mitigated by strong exports
and by an expansionary fiscal policy (including a program of cash transfers to the poor) that held up growth inthe
4th quarter. Expansionary fiscal policy i s also expected to limit the pace of contraction during the first half of
2006, before higher consumer and investment spending raise growth in the second half of the year. Currently
interest rates are high and likely to remain so for at least the first half of 2006. However, they are expected to fall
later in the year as inflationary momentum slows, further boosting the economic recovery.. .. The Bank projects
growth of 5.5 percent in 2006. The current slowdown should bottom out in the first half, mitigated by strong
government spending (financed by reduced subsidies) and net exports (partly driven by markedly lower fuel
imports). In the second half, government spending should remain strong with overall growth picking up on higher
private investment and a recovery in consumer durable spending." It might be useful to have a macro or sectoral
update on whether these projections are being borne out inrecent months.

                                                          113

According to Y. Ishihara (pers comm., May 31, 2006), a survey of manufacturing firms conducted by a Japanese
Government source in October 2005, indicated that transportation and manufacturing (?) would be hardest hit by
the fuel price change. Recent press reports bear out this perception.          In August 2005, a Reuters headline
proclaimed "Indonesian Car Purchases Booming; Oil Production Declining: Car sales in Indonesia rose 28.3% in
July compared with the same period in2004." Yet, just eight months later, the same source stated, "Fuel price rise
clobbers 2006 car sales." "Indonesian vehicle sales plunged 56% in April from a year earlier, showing the sector
was still suffering from a government decision last year to raise fuel prices sharply." So, clearly, fuel prices are
having some effect on consumers' willingness to postpone car purchases. If the fuel price hike i s affecting
consumers' driving behavior, then there would be positive impacts on air pollution, road runoff and time delays
due to congestion.

There i s not yet information on specific changes in demand or output at the sectoral level. Given the small very
small impact on overall economic growth rates, it may be unlikely that the subsidy removal had a significant
(beneficial) impact on reducing industrial timber use or exports. As additional modeling work i s done, and as new
quarters of data come in, it would be helpful to check on sectoral impacts, especially in timber and wood
processing.

Didfuel use changeshave beneficial effectson pollutionlevels?

A decline in fuel use (or a slowing in the rate of growth of fuel use) would have some environmentally beneficial
effects. Especially in transport, decreases infuel use would reduce air pollution, especially inurban areas where it
can be quite severe in Indonesia. The data show reductions infuel use intransport (10-15%) and in industrial uses
(15% with some fuel switching. Fuel use for electricity generation increased by 25%, but this i s the smallest
category of fuel use, so i s overbalanced by the other two effects. Overall, these changes would contribute to
reducing air pollution levels.

Would the subsidy removal increase fuelwood consumption? Would that harm the forests?

The FA0 (www.fao.org) has studied this issue intensively since the 1970sand they conclude that fuel wood use i s
not a major driver of deforestation or land use change, certainly not compared to agricultural expansion or
commercial forestry. In a country with pretty large forest, there i s enough waste wood to supply lots of firewood
needs without actual tree felling (imagine the cost of felling and extracting trees....just for firewood?). Also, there
i s lots of more accessible wood for fuel purposes inprivate plots and secondary growth areas.

Households may have one of three main types of responsesto the kerosene subsidy removal:

      1. Households that do not switch to fuel wood. Relatively more well off and more urban households may
          switch to alternative fuels, but most likely not to wood. For many of these households, switching to wood
          would not only be inconvenient, but also would require unacceptable technological changes in the
          household.
     2. A middle group of households - probably more rural -- may switch to fuel wood to the extent that it i s
          available and does not require a major change inhousehold organization. These households may still have
          wood burning cooking facilities available, but infrequently used.
     3. Households that only use fuelwood (before and after). Some poor, rural, or forest dependent households
          will be primarily dependent on fuel wood for cooking. This group will not engage in fuel switching (for
          cooking) due to the price change for kerosene. This i s the group closest to or living innatural forest areas,
          which would not be expected to change wood consumption behavior.

So, the price change would mainly affect the middle group, some of whom would switch to other fuels, some -but
not all -- to wood. It would be useful to know where these people are and what kinds of cooking fuel choices they
are making, but this would need detailed survey data that are not yet available (mid 2006). The 2005 data
presented above indicate that household keroseneconsumption declined in the last quarter after the hefty price hike
                                                           114

by about 13%.      During this period, the poor were compensated monetarily for the increase in prices, SO
consumption patterns may not have changed as much as if the price change hit directly. Also, these data reflect
only the first immediate period after the price change, not the long term adjustment patterns of household energy
use. Still, these preliminary data show that households are not switching dramatically into fuel wood use. This
13% shift brings consumption back to 1999 levels, only, not some dramatic alternative scenario. This fluctuation
i s only about twice as high as prior annual changes, so the effect of concern i s only about 6 or 7% of household
kerosene use. This level of change can easily be accommodated through a combination of conservation and fuel
switching, not a significant increase in fuel wood use.

In addition to the data, several other sources of information indicate that the overall impact of this shift to wood
use would not be `significant.'




     A. First is the overall result from research in many countries over 20 years, summarized by the FAO, which
         finds that the majority of fuelwood (over 60%) originates from non-forest sources and the supply from
         these non-forest sources appears to be sufficient for household uses. This research concludes that
         fuelwood harvesting from forest land is not necessarily non-sustainable, and that fuelwood use i s not
         necessarily linked to deforestation. Although concerns about a fuelwood gap were raised in the 1970s,
         nowadays fuelwood use is no longer considered a major or general cause of deforestation. (This is
         summarized inthe text box on the following page and widely available onthe internet.)
     B. Second, a wide variety of country studies strongly suggest that the main reason for deforestation was the
         conversion of forest land into agricultural land andor settlement areas. This conversion process may have
         made more fuel wood available to the rural poor, but this end use was not the main cause of the forest
         degradation.


                                                          115

   C. Third, population distribution and lack of proximity to good quality natural forest mitigates some of the
        concern about fuel wood switching. Most of the people affected by the kerosene price rise - that is most
        of the people in Indonesia about 60% - are on Java, where incursion into natural forests i s less of a
        significant threat, because there are so few and isolated areas of natural forest remaining. Of the middle
        group, that may switch to alternative fuels, not all would switch exclusively to firewood use. Also, as
        noted by ADB, 60% or more, of these fuel wood needs will most likely be met from non-forest sources,
        meaning on farm trees, private timber stands, and agricultural products or waste. The remaining demand
        can easily be met by waste wood generatedfrom the commercial timber harvesting industry.%
    D. Fourth, any small shift into fuel wood at the household levelwould have to be evaluated againstthe overall
        high rate of harvesting and forest destruction caused by poor forest governance and industrial practices.
        These issues are well documented in the World Bank draft report entitled, Sustaining Economic Growth,
        Rural Livelihoods, and Environmental Benefits:             Strategic Options for Forest Sector Assistance in
        Indonesia, DRAFT 5 April 2006, which i s currently being revised to address comments received at a
        review meeting (VC) on May 3,2006.

Based on the 2005 data and the other arguments presented, it can be concluded that the fuel subsidy has not had a
significant effect on fuelwood consumption. In addition, even a moderate change in fuel wood consumption (not
seen) would not harm natural forests, since these supplies would come from home gardens and agroforests
managed by rural people. This i s a very short term analysis. These findings would not be applicable to an
additional 3 to 5 fold increase in kerosene prices (needed to bring domestic prices in line with global prices). I t
will be important to update these findings as additional quarters of results become available. It may be useful to
continue to monitor SUSENAS findings regarding firewood consumption and expenditure, as well.
