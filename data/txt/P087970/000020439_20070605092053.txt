 A. STRATEGICCONTEXTAND RATIONALE

 1.      Country and sector issues

 Almost all agriculture inEgypt takes place insome 30,000 km2(8 million feddan, or about 3% of
 Egypt territory) of fertile soil in the Nile Valley and the Delta region. The Delta region alone
 contributes about 80% o f all cultivable land in the country. Despite the extremely limited land
 available for agriculture, urbanization of these lands is growing. Comparisons of digital mosaics
 of LANDSAT MSS and TM scenes, acquired over the Nile Delta in 1972, 1984, and 1990,
 indicate troublingtrend. Urban areas occupied a minimumof 3.6%, 4.7%, and 5.7% respectively
 of the total Delta land area, or an increase of 58% in the 18 years of study. About half of this
 increase occurred between 1984 and 1990 meaning that by 2010, as much as 12% (720,000
 feddan or about 302,500 ha) ofthe total area of the Deltawould be lost to urbanization.

Although urban growth i s significant around the cities, it is the growth around the thousands of
 small villages that poses the greatest threat to the agricultural lands in the Nile Delta. Between
 1972 and 1990, cities and large villages grew by 37%, while growth of small villages was more
than twice, or 77% during the same period. However, one practical circumstance of the growing
urbanization in the Nile Valley and Delta i s that water savings from these areas can be used
elsewhere.

To compensate for the loss of agricultural land in the Delta region, and to provide opportunities
to generate newjobs, increase production and widen the development base, the Government of
Egypt (GOE) has supported commercial farmers in reclaiming desert lands since the late 1960s.
Inthis context, GOE has identified an area of about 255,000 feddan (equivalent to 107,000 ha),
located approximately 60 kilometers north of Cairo to the west of the Nile Delta, which has
experienced noticeable agricultural growth through exploitation of groundwater resources.
Today, this area is a flourishing agricultural economy estimated between US$300 million to 1/2
billion dollars annually, serving both domestic and export markets in the European Union (EU)
and elsewhere. Moreover, the area is now home to 500,000 people and provides about 250,000
jobs in the agriculture sector alone. However, with the rapid development over the past few
years, there has beenan excessive depletion of the groundwater reserves. With about 47% of the
total 255,000 feddanunder cultivation, water extraction by the year 2000 reached 870 million m3
annually, or an increase of 36.2% injust over a decade. Groundwater is quickly depleting with a
commensurate effect on overall water quality.

To resolve this problem, GOE has been reviewing options to replace groundwater with surface
water irrigation. The goal is to minimize, if not totally halt the depletion of the groundwater
resource. To achieve this goal, GOE wishes to implement a surface water conveyance system
and to introduce important reforms in the sector, particularly full cost recovery tariffs and
volumetric pricing.       Such reforms are part of the GOE's own Integrated Water Resources
Management Action Plan (IWRMAP) developed in2005 to ensure correct incentives to conserve
and utilizewater more efficiently',


I GOE is implementingcost recoveryof operation and maintenance(O&M) inold lands and full cost recovery in
new lands. The project area is considered new lands andtherefore, full cost recoverypolicy will apply.

 Beyond its objective to achieve full cost recovery, GOE also wishes to identify practical ways o f
 involving the private sector in the design, operation and financing o f the new irrigation system.
 While GOE fully endorses the West Delta Water Conservation and Irrigation Rehabilitation
 Project (WDWCIRP), it i s keen to share the implementation and other related risks; and to assign
 responsibilities for the operations, maintenance and loan amortization to a private operator.
 Using the private sector as a partner through Public Private Partnerships (PPP) is a concept that
 has been studied and i s included as a specific action in the 2005, IWRMAP, specifically for
 addressing the needs o fthe West Delta area.

2.      Rationale for Bankinvolvement

 The World Bank's (the Bank's) policy approach to GOE's IWRMAP includes the need to reduce
 subsidies in the irrigation sector and for making the sector financially sustainable. The Bank
 Strategy for Egypt places emphasis in areas where it can provide critical expertise in order to
 implement GOE's strategies (Le., the PPP for the WDWCIRP).

Involving the private sector in infrastructure in general, poses challenges to GOE that the Bank
can address through its extensive experience in this area worldwide, especially in water supply
infrastructure, energy and telecommunications. Inirrigation, private participation adds additional
dimensions to this challenge given: (i)   the relative void o f successful transaction experience in
this sector; and (ii)the added risks perceived in running a private irrigation system on a
commercial/full cost recovery basis. To date, there are few examples o f irrigation projects o f the
magnitude being considered inthe West Delta where the private sector has taken up a substantial
share o f the relatedrisks.

It is inthis context that the Bank and GOE agreed to study options for PPP for the WDWCIRP,
and to develop a transaction model that could be used for implementing a successful surface
water irrigation project to the West Delta Area. Subsequently, the model could also be utilized
for development o f other areas both in Egypt and within the Nile River riparian countries.
Already the work accomplished in the West Delta PPP transaction design i s being utilized for
considering private options inthe irrigation sector inEthiopia.

Usingfunds from the Public-Private Infrastructure Advisory Facility (PPIAF), the Bankprovided
assistance to GOE in analyzing and selecting a preferred option for a PPP that will be
implemented in the proposed project. The choice o f the PPP model was largely governed by a
number o f considerations including: (i)  transferring to the extent possible from public to private,
the various risks associated with the project; (ii)realizing GOE's objective for cost recovery and
for the project to sustain itself financially throughout its project life; (iii)
                                                                               mobilizing long-term
financing in order to render tariffs affordable; (iv) ensuring the maximum possible mitigation o f
demand risk by linking the planning and construction scheduling to actual operation; and (v)
ensuring an appropriate balance o f risks and return, while simultaneously being mindful that the
absolute level o f financial commitment may deter private sponsors and lenders. Based on these
considerations, a Design-Build-Operate (DBO) option, with a capital contribution by the
operator and participating farmers, i s considered as the preferred transaction model for
implementation.

3.    Higherlevel objectivesto which the projectcontributes

The project will help GOE achieve its main growth and poverty alleviation related development
objectives as expressed in the new Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) for Egypt. The CAS
focuses on three pillars:

    a) interventions that support higher and sustained growth;
    b) interventions targeted to direct poverty reduction; and
    c) interventions with major indirect poverty reduction impact.

Implementing the proposed project will contribute to pillars (a) and (c). In (a), it will provide
critical infrastructure to achieve higher and sustained economic growth o f export oriented crops
in the West Delta, as well as spillover effects in the service (packing, market information,
technical advice, logistics) industry and input industries (locally produced fertilizers). In (c), it
will contribute to indirect poverty reduction as agricultural activities inthe project area will have
major direct and indirect generation o f employment opportunities for an estimated half a million
people.

B.      PROJECTDESCRIPTION

1.      Lendinginstrument

Lending Instrument: The 4-year project will utilize a Specific Investment Loan (SIL) instrument.
The SIL will be used together with equity financing from the selected private operator and
participating farmers to design, build and operate and maintain a surface water system in the
project area. This mix o f financing has been chosen to ensure technical and financial viability o f
the project; Le., the private operator will assume key responsibilities for system design,
construction, and O&M activities and will assume some o f the related financing risks o f the
project. This will contribute towards financial sustainability with important incentives in place
such that the system will not be over-designed. Farmers are expected to buy into the project,
knowing that volumetric charges will in part be the basis for water conveyance fees. Cost
recovery policies through volumetric tariffs will promote greater efficiency and conservation o f
water utilizing modern irrigation technologies.

3.      Projectcomponents

The project's total investment cost for Component 1 i s US$205 million, o f which a Bank loan
will finance US$145 million. The remainder will be funded by farmers and the private operator
and Agence Franqaise de DCveloppement through co-financing. An additional US$8 million will
be made available as grants from bilateral donors (AFD and the Government o f Netherlands
(GON) for Components 2 and 3.

Component No. 1: Design, Construction, and Operation of Surface Water System and
ConnectionProgram (US$205 million). This component will finance the design, construction
and operation o f a surface water irrigation system and connection program within the project
area, taking into consideration the specific environment and social requirements as stated in the
ESMPand the RPF. It is estimated the project area will consist o f about 25,200 ha to 37,800 ha
in the West Delta. Initial design work was carried out during the technical preparation studies
which were based on a "demand-driven approach to planning" where the growers' willingness to
pay for connection guidedthe technical design options with commensurate tariffs. Inaddition, a
piped system was chosen as the preferred option given its advantages over open channel systems,
particularly with regard to efficient water resource use and lower environmental and social
safeguards risks. The final design will be completed by the private operator contracted to
construct and operate the system on a long-term basis. A fixed allocation o f water resource will
be made available by GOE to the project area based on the estimated average annual requirement
o f 12,376 m3per year per ha. The preliminary design o f the system has been sized to meet the
peak demand in the summer months. Over the entire year, total usage converges to the total

annual allocation. Based on this, it i s anticipated that the surface water system would meet most
of the water resource needs of the farms that will be connecting, allowing the aquifers to
recharge and to benefitfarmers inadjacent areas.

ComponentNo. 2: Market-DrivenTechnicalSupport to Smalland MediumScale Farmers
(US$2 million).     This component will provide market-driven technical support to small and
medium scale farmers in the project area to increase their market share, at the national and
international level. The component will initially be funded through donor grants by AFD and
will provide technical assistance (TA) to small andmediumgrowers, traders and food processors
to increase West Delta products' (fresh or processed) market share on national and international
markets. TA to small and mediumsize growers, traders, exporters and foodprocessorswould be
provided inthe following areas:

    a) production, post harvest technology and farm management to small and medium size
         growers to improve competitiveness and quality of products;
    b) market intelligence (for local and export market) and logistics to small growers, traders
         and exporters, to look for newmarket opportunity and/or increasemarket share;
    c) food processing, packing and marketing to food processors, to improve competitiveness
        and/or create new food products; and
    d) organizational arrangement for growers, traders and/or food processors to work in a
        coordinated manner within formal or informal organizations to achieve economy of scale
        and improve supply chain competitiveness.

Following a successful evaluation of the first phase activity which is funded by grants cited
above, loanfacilitation could, ifnecessarybe provided directly to participating farmers.

Component No. 3: Support for Institutional Development and Capacity Building of the
Project Management Unit (PMU), Regulatory Office and Water Users Council (WUC)
(US$6 million). This component will be funded through donor grants from the Government of
the Netherlands and will support capacity building of the Ministry of Water Resources and
Irrigation (MWRI) for PPP contract management, construction supervision consultants,
regulatory oversight, and to the WUC association along the lines of the policies for institutional
reforms proposed by the MWRI and in cooperation with the GON. Inaddition, it will support
activities to disseminate a possible replication of the adopted PPP approach to other areas in
Egypt and its riparian neighbors.

The main capacity building activities financed under this component include: (i)strengthening
the PMU and the contract management activities which would oversee contractual matters
betweenMWRI and the private operator on all aspects of the implementation phases, including
environmental and social safeguard compliance during implementation of the project, inclusive
of groundwater monitoring; (ii)capacity building o f the economic regulatory office to ensure
effective regulatory oversight and equitable treatment of interests between the farmers and the
private operator; and (iii)capacity building of the WUC that has been formed to oversee the
relationship between farmers vis-a-vis entitlements and usage of surface and ground water
resources. Given the unique nature of the PPP transaction arrangement, the TA will also provide
for oversight supervisory engineersand technical audits oftechnical milestones achieved.

4.      Lessons learned and reflected in the project design

The proposed project design has benefitedfrom some broad lessons learned from: (i)         successful
experience in the water supply and sanitation (WSS) sector; (ii)a "participatory approach" to
systemplanning and design; and (iii) involvement of private sector and users in all stages of
                                        the
project preparation. In addition, lessons gained from the design of comparable Bank financed
projects are also being used, including the Provincial and Peri-Urban Water and Sanitation
Project that features DBODesign-Build Lease (DBL) contracts to construct and operate and
maintain provincial water town systems in Cambodia; and the LGU Urban Water and Sanitation
Project APL2 that features a DBLcontract inprovincialtowns inPhilippines.

Lessons in transaction design are also taken from the Guardane Private Irrigation Project in
Morocco. Relevant lessons include: (i) project-related risks should be allocated to the
                                           various
party (i.e., public or private) that is more able to handle them; and (ii)contract management
capacity at the public sector usually needs strengthening for negotiating, overseeing and
monitoring contracts with the private sector.

Given the pioneering nature of the project concept, particularly because o f the potential
continuation of groundwater exploitation, the feasibility of the project had to be assessed under a
markedly different approach. This approach aimed at designing and implementing a system that
would largely correspond to the needs of growers, their willingness to connect to the new
system, and to pay the full cost of service. As such, project preparation activities shiftedfrom the
traditional supply-driven approachwhere demandforecasts andtechnical specifications guide the
overall planning process, to a "demand-driven approach to planning" where the growers'
willingness to connect and pay would guide the development of technical design options with
commensurate tariffs.

A key feature of this approach relied on a promotional outreach to the fanners, soliciting their
involvement inkey aspects of project planning, system design, risk allocation, tariff structure and
commercial arrangementsas well as inenvironmental and social safeguards. A Farmer Advisory
Council (FAC) composed of more than 20 fanners representing the area actively participated in
all aspects of project design and has been tremendously beneficial in providing promotional
outreach to other fanners inthe area. A series of stakeholder workshops were held with larger
fanner representation as part of a formal promotional and participatory campaign to share
informationsurrounding all aspects of the project.

5.      Alternatives considered and reasons for rejection

Giventhe important policy reforms that GOE is willing to undertakethrough the implementation
of the proposed project, including: (i)full cost recovery for irrigation infrastructure to be
implementedinnew lands like the one being financed by this project; (ii)      demand management
through volumetric tariffs; (iii)involvement of private sector in project implementation and
OPM of irrigation infrastructure; and (iv) the introduction of water entitlements, the alternatives
for project implementation substantially focused on the institutional options of different PPP
models. However, the preparation work did consider the technical option of implementing a



                                                 6

project encompassing the entire 255,000 feddan area in the West Delta. This broader project
concept was not pursued for a number o f reasons, including: (i)the technical constraints o f
implementing a project o f a much larger scope given the need to complete construction in the
shortest period possible such that the loan could be amortized over the longest possible time
frame in order to render tariffs affordable; (ii)the feasibility o f a private operator taking on the
entire concession area in an already challenging project concept; (iii) intention to have a
                                                                             the
smaller pilot effort that can be tried and successfully managed prior to potential replication to

The preliminary technical alternatives considered the use o f an open channel for water
conveyance in addition to the selected piped system. In addition, water management alternatives
studied the possibility o f prohibiting continued groundwater use. The open channel option was
ruled out due to environmental and commercial reasons while the explicit prohibition o f
groundwater use could not be practically enforced.

With regard to the alternative institutional options, the project team reviewed transaction models
that could better balance risks between GOE, the private operator and the beneficiaries in the
area. The DBO option contract managed by a P M U and a Regulatory Office (RO)/Independent
Panel o f Experts (IPE) under MWRI, was chosen as the most viable option in order to introduce
and successfully implement such policy reforms under the investment project. Alternatives
considered andreasons for rejection follow.

GOE uses the concept of a West Delta Holding Company (WDHC) as the DBO contract
manager. This option was rejected in the'short-term because a WDHC had not yet been
established and even if its establishment is confirmed it would require substantial organizational
and institutional development inorder to become operational.

GOE contracts out construction of surface water system infrastructure to the private sector and
thereafter signs a performance management contract with a private operator - instead of the
DBO contract -for     the O&M of such inpastructure. This option was rejected because, in
addition to the reasons cited above, under a performance management contract most of the risks
would be retained by GOE, leaving the private sector with few opportunities and incentives to
achieve GOE's policy reforms mentioned above.

GOE signs a PPP full concession contract with the private sector to finance entirely, build,
operate and maintain the surface water for the irrigation system. This option was rejected
because project preparation studies concluded that a full concession-type PSP, which would
mobilize private financing on commercial terms, would be too costly for growers or would likely
require substantial capital subsidies from GOE in order to successfully implement, as inthe case
o f the Guardane Project in Morocco. Moreover, it was also found that private operators and
financiers would be reluctant to assume the total financing risks related to a first-time project o f
this nature. The blendo f public-private financing inthe proposedDBO contract tends to balance
more equitably the associated risks o f the project at the outset and will provide beneficial
demonstration effects for replication elsewhere inthe sector.

D.      APPRAISAL SUMMARY

1.      Economicand FinancialAnalysis

Economic analysis. Major project benefits will come from increased agriculture production
resulting from increased water availability. The proposed project will allow for an additional
49% of area to be cultivated resulting in an estimated Economic Rate of Return (ERR) of 20%,
(meaning that the Egyptian economy would realize a 20% rate of return from implementing the
project, which is well in excess of the opportunity cost o f capital (OCC), taken at the rate o f


                                                   13

 10%). To show the impact o f the project, three separate ERRShave been calculated, one each for
increased water availability, command area development, and a combined total project. For
increased water availability alone, the ERR i s 22%, for improvements in the command area the
ERR is 16% and for the combined total project the ERR is 20%. Clearly, the returns to
improvements inwater availability are higher than those for improvements inthe command area,
which can be explained by the fact that farmers are already using highly effective irrigation
techniques. However, both economic rates o f returnare possible only after implementation o f the
surface water system to improve water availability.

Financial Analysis. A financial internal rate o f return to total invested capital o f 9.26% was
calculated based on an assumed consumer tariff o f LE0.394 per cubic meter. The actual tariff
and costs may change however, as they will be based on a competitive bid among private
operators. At the estimated tariff, the project would fully cover all investment and O&M costs.
Moreover, affordability surveys indicate that such a tariff is affordable and promotes a high
participation among the farmers. The return to the contributions o f farmers and the private
operator (inthe form or equity and security deposits), was estimated at 20'3'0, makingthe project
attractive to potential investors. Also, as the proposed project will be implemented under a full
cost recovery basis, the impact on GOE's fiscal budget will be negligible.

The financial analysis focused on: (i)   determining the average tariff level and appropriate tariff
structure that will recover all costs through the duration o f the project and ensure a sustainable
cash flow to the operation; and (ii)  determining an appropriate capital structuring and financing
planmaximizing the mix o f public and private financing, including contributions from farmers;
and (iii)the development o f long-term financial projections in order to assess financial
sustainability o f the project under different financial and management assumptions. The model
will be available to prospective private bidders inconducting their due diligence o f the expected
risks and returns o fthe proposedDBOcontract.

2.      Technical

Final technical design under this project will be entrusted to a private operator selected through
international competitive biding. Under the terms o f the contract, the private operator will
complete the final designand determine the size o f the system o f the project at the start based on
a final subscription o f farmers to surface water services and a set o f service standards. At that
time, security deposits will be collected by the operator together with the execution o f a legally
binding agreement that spells out responsibilities for both the beneficiary and the private
operator to each other. Then, detailed design and construction o f the irrigation system will take
place based on this final determination. Late subscribers after the initial subscription period will
be subject to late fee adjustment to account for inflation.


The private operator will have the opportunity to extend service to the rest o f the concession area
for which he will be responsible to provide the investment costs. He may extend the service
outside the concession area during the period o f the contract if available water resources
allocated to the project have not been fully utilized. A number o f technical studies have been
carried out by GOE on the proposed project's feasibility. Additional technical work was

undertaken through grants obtained by PPIAF and from GON. Annex 12 provides a list o f the
relevant technical studies and surveys conducted under project preparation that have beenplaced
inthe project files.


Technical design will conform to international standards by virtue o f the clauses in the contract
to be signed between GOE and a reputable private operator. The private operator or (consortium
o f firms) is expected to be well experienced in the construction, operation and management o f
surface water irrigation systems and be familiar with local business conditions. The technical
design is expected to enhance the use o f water efficient techniques in irrigation. In addition, the
design will incorporate environmental and social mitigation measures that have been identified
through the Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA).

This approach to technical design is appropriate to Egypt's needs because the country faces
water constraints, having the Nile River as its only source. Technical design and implementation
o f the proposed project will go hand in hand with the implementation o f other projects in Egypt
that are intended to improve water usage efficiency, including IIIMP.

4.      Social

Participationof key stakeholders. To avoid potential marginalization of poorer stakeholders,
and to ensure project sustainability, a participatory approach to system design and planning has
beenused during the project preparation. During the past two years several background studies
have been carried out. As part of the PPIAF study, an NGO, the Egyptian Water Partnership
(EWP) undertook an information campaign and a survey to identify farmer's needs that should
be reflected into the early design work. Also the Private Growers Advisory Council was formed
to give qualified feedback on the proposed project's technical options. GOE received financial
support from BNWPP to carry out a social, economic, and environmental impact assessment
applying the integrated "DRAINFRAME" approach, which is a fully participatory planning
exercise. Stakeholder consultations were conducted both within and outside the project area.
This comprehensive assessment was the scoping study for ESIA. The Bank further
commissioned a Social Impact Study to assess the expected social impacts of the introduction of
surface irrigation to the West Delta Area on the establishment of new settlements and/or the
expansion of existing ones. This included an analysis of the situation of bothpermanent workers
and commuters in the project area and its surroundings on how the project is likely to impact
their situation. Finally, the private operator will continue with the participatory approach to
system design and planning to ensure that all categories of farmers will benefit fully from the
surface water for irrigation services.

Application of Integrated Water Resources Management Principles (IWRM). The
DRAINFRAME study has set up the basis for mainstreaming IWRM principlesinthe proposed
project area. This includesways and means to treat water as a holistic resource, to manage water


                                                17

at the lowest appropriate level ("the subsidiarity principle") using appropriate public and private
sector instruments, and ensuring stakeholder participation, including the involvement o f women
inplanningandimplementation.

Main issues and project strategy to overcome them. The farmers in the project area and
contiguous areas do not represent a homogenous group, and as such they are not likely to have
similar interests and needs. This difference in perceptions has been documented with regard to
groundwater problems (e.g., increased salinity and low water level). Groundwater problems are
seen as much more serious among farmers inthe central area where the proposed project will be
implemented. Inaddition, TA delivered through Component 2 will address specific needs o f the
small farmers. The activities will promote cooperative arrangements among small farmers to
achieve economies o f scale and to share common water supply connections, to acquire relevant
technical knowledge and to access irrigations services. Small farmers will be represented through
the WUC andtheir respective user association.

Monitoringof social impacts. Appropriate mechanisms will be established to monitor social
and environmental impacts o f proposed project. Baseline, mid-term and end-of-project
assessments are included as part o f ESINESMP framework.

5.      Environment

The proposed project i s rated a Category "A" project in accordance with the Environmental
Assessment Policy (OP 4.01) o f the World Bank because o f the potential scale o f impacts in
terms o f water quantity, water quality, and water access issues; as well as the potential corporate
risk posed to the Bank by the diversion o f water from the Nile River. The project team had
commissioned the DRAINFRAME study which takes an integrated look at irrigation (including
an analysis o f economic, social and environmental aspects o f the project). The study has done an
excellent job in scoping out key environmental and social issues, and has provided important
inputs to the ESIA and the ESMP which includes appropriate alternatives and mitigation
measures to fully comply with the required World Bank guidelines for a Category "A" project.

The main issues raised and discussed in the ESINESMP included: (i)         the withdrawal o f water
from the Nile system at the expense o f uses outside the proposed project area; (ii)  the continuing
groundwater use inconjunction with surface water; and (iii)    the inducedregional development as
a result o f the intensification o f agriculture. The impacts resulting from these issues have been
assessed and mitigation measures posed which should ensure that the negative impacts are
mitigated effectively. In effect, the proposed project should generate positive environmental
benefits in terms o f groundwater recharge, and more effective water management through the
realization o f effective cost savings inwater use.

I. CountryandSectorBackground

Country Background - "Egypt i s the gift of the Nile" wrote a Greek historian 25 centuries ago.
Its reliance on the Nile i s reflected in the fact that 90% of the population live on 5% of the land
area around the stem of the Nile River and the Delta. Just north of Cairo, the Nile spreads out
over what was once a broad estuarythat has been filled by riverine deposits to form a fertile Nile
Delta about 250 kilometers wide (150 miles) at the seaward base and about 160 kilometers (96
miles) from south to north. Currently, approximately one third o f the Egyptian labor force i s
engaged directly in farming and many others work in the processing or trading of agricultural
products. Most o f the Egyptian agriculture takes place in some 25,000 km2(6 million feddan, or
about 2.5% o f Egypt territory) of fertile soil in the Nile Valley and the Delta. The Delta alone
contributes to 80% of all Egyptian cultivable old lands.

Main Irrigation Sector Related Institutions - MWRI i s spearheading GOE's efforts to manage
the limited water resources in an optimal way, utilizing best practices o f IWRM. Farmers are
key stakeholders as water users for agricultural activities, while the Ministryof Agriculture and
Land Reclamation (MALR) is the corresponding government policy making institution involved
inthe development andmanagementofagricultural activities andlandreclamation. The Ministry
of State for Environmental Affairs (MSEA) and MWRI are jointly concerned with the protection
of the environment while the Ministry of the Interior (MOI) carries out regulatory and law
enforcement duties. Several other ministries are concerned with water resources management
issues: Ministry of Local Development (MOLD), Ministry o f Industry and Trade (MOI),
Ministry of Electricity and Energy (MEE), Ministry of Transport (MOT), and the Ministry of
Tourism (MOTR). Several researchinstitutes withinthe stakeholderMinistries carry out research
activities relevant to the water resources management. The universities also are involved in
addressingmany of the researchneeds of IWRM.

111.      GovernmentStrategy

To resolve this problem, GOE has been reviewing options to replace groundwater with surface
water for irrigation. The goal is to minimize if not totally halt the depletion o f the groundwater
resource. However, there are a number o f complexities in achieving this, since realistically the
growers cannot be forced to connect to a new surface water system, particularly if they are
expected to pay for the cost o f service. Consistent with recently approved policies, it i s GOE's
intent to fully recover the cost o f the system and to introduce volumetric tariffs to ensure correct
incentives to conserve and utilize water more efficiently. Moreover, beyond its objective to
achieve full cost recovery, GOE also wishes to identify practical ways o f involving the private
sector inthe design, operation and even financing o f the surface water system. While GOE fully
endorses the project, it is also keen to transfer as much o f the related risks and to assign
maximum responsibilities o f the operations, maintenance and loan amortization to a private
operator.

The ChosenPPPOptionfor the West Delta

Involving the private sector inthe West Delta poses a significant challenge. Private participation
in traditional infrastructure projects in power, telecommunication, transport and water supply
have suffered serious declines over the past decades and as o f 2005 the trends are still
downward. The large dominant international operators are playing a much lesser role in direct
financial placements. Their preferred choice o f PPP options are those that transfer financing and
demand risk to the public partner (as in management contracts and leases) and it i s not certain
whether these investors'can be led back to assume financing risks, regardless o f how these are
allocated.

Inirrigation, private participation adds to the challenge giventhe void o f successful transaction
experience in this sector, together with the obstacles perceived in running a private irrigation
system on a commercial and cost recovery basis. To date, there are very few examples o f
successful irrigation projects o f the magnitude being considered in the West Delta where the
private sector has taken up a substantial share o f the related risks o f the undertaking. It i s inthis
context that the Bank accepted to cooperate with GOE to study the option for PPP for the
WDWCIRP.

Preparation o f the proposed project focused on identifying a number o f different public-private
models with different risk allocations between public and private parties for implementation.
The choice o f PPP model was largely governed by a number o f considerations including: (i)
transferring to the extent possible from public to private, the various risks associated with the
project; (ii)realize GOE's objective for cost recovery and for the project to sustain itself
financially throughout its project life; (iii)
                                            the ability to mobilize long-term financing in order to
render tariffs affordable; (iv) ensure the maximum possible mitigation o f demand risk by linking
the planning and construction scheduling to actual operation; and (v) ensure an appropriate
balance o f risk and return, at the same time being mindful that the absolute level o f financial
commitment may deter private sponsors and lenders. Based on these considerations, the DBO
model i s considered as the preferred transaction model for the West Delta.

ProjectDevelopmentObjective(PDO) -To improve the livelihood and increase the income of
people inthe West Delta region through: (i)      mitigating further environmental degradation caused

financial sustainability o f irrigation infrastructure inthe use o f water resources.

Intermediate Results - The proposed project will have the following IPBs during
implementation:

        An operational surface water supply system covering Phase Iarea, constructed and
        operated on a financially sustainable basis;

        Participation by the farming community in the area participated in the determination o f
        system design options based on their willingness and capacity to pay for the cost o f
        service;

        Development and successful implementation o f institutional arrangements that will
        ensure the successful implementation o f the project which will guide relationships
        between: (i)the GOE and the private operator; (ii)the private operator vis-a-vis the
        farmers; and (iii)relationshipsbetween farmers; and,

        Effective reduction in the declining trend o f the groundwater table within the connected

        ProjectComponents

Over a 4-year implementationperiod, the project components include:

The project's total investment cost for Component No. 1 i s US$205 million, o f which a World
Bank loan will finance US$145 million, with the remainder sourced from the farmers and the
private operator, and AFD co-financing. In addition, an additional US$8 million will be made
available as grants and sub loans to farmers from bilateral donors (AFD and the Netherlands) for
Component Nos. 2 and 3.


Connection Program for Participating Farms (US$205 million). - This component will
Component No. 1: Design, Construct, and Start Up of the Surface Water System, and

finance activities leading to the final design and construction o f a surface water irrigation system
for the project area o f between 25,200 ha to 37,800 ha inthe West Delta. Initial design work was
carried out during the technical preparation studies which were based on a "demand-driven
approach to planning" where the growers' willingness to pay for connection guided the technical
design options with commensurate tariffs.          In addition, a piped system was chosen as the
preferred option given its advantages over open channel systems, particularly with regard to

 efficient water resource use and lower environmental and social safeguards risks. Final design
 will be completed by the private operator contracted to construct and operate the system on a
 long term basis. A fixed allocation o f water resource will be made available by GOE to the
 project area based on the estimated average annual requirement o f 12,376 m3per year per ha.
 The preliminary design o f the system has been sized to meet the peak demand in the summer
 months. Over the entire year, total usage converges to the annual average. Based on this, it i s
 anticipated that the surface water system would meet most o f the water resource needs o f the
 farms that will be connecting, allowing the aquifers to recharge and to benefit farmers inadjacent
 areas.

For the purpose o f the appraisal, the pipeline option has been included as the baseline estimate
given its preferred characteristics over the open channel system. The system will include
construction o f culverts, a liftlpumping station, transit storage capacity, conveyance a main
distribution system, roads and energy sources and other related civil works to serve an initial
90,000 feddan inthe southern part of the West Delta Area. This area was selected during project
preparation given its relatively higher declining groundwater table's area and the commensurate
greater willingness by farmers to pay for surface water irrigation. The implementation o f the
proposed project inthis area will serve as model for development o f the other areas inthe West
Delta as well as other agricultural lands inEgypt.

A.     Public-PrivateOptionsfor Allocatingthe Risks

While a wide variety o f private-public models and variations o f such models have been
successfully transacted ininfrastructure projects, the essential trade-offs are between which party
retains the finance and management functions. With that determination, the various risks are
then generally allocated to the appropriate party who can best mitigate or control these risks.
Ownership o f the assets i s also an important consideration but usually this i s retained by the
party that also bears the financing risks, as the assets are often utilized and needed as security for
mobilizing the financing.




                                                 38

The four options considered were:

    (a) Public Company Option; Inthe case o f a purely public option, a number o f institutional
         alternatives could be developed with participation by both MWRI and other public
        entities such as a Public Asset Holding Company (holding company)'. This particular
        option was considered only insofar as the holding company, which was set up under the
        public enterprise law", would be its own legal standing and balance sheet. However, the
        holding company i s still in its formative stages and would require substantial time and
        resource to become operational, and more importantly to be able to effectively manage a
        project o f this complexity. Moreover, since this i s a public company, this option provides
        little other than GOE retaining all functions as well as the risks associated with the
        implementation o f the project.

    (b) Private ConcessionBOT Option: In direct contrast to the "public company option",
        concessions and other such schemes as, BOT transfer all risks to the private party. As
        indicated, these options have fallen severely out o f favor among private sponsors and
        financiers largely because o f the significant losses experienced in the past.             Today,
        private sponsors perceive that there is serious equity issues associated with these
        arrangements and would be to costly to implement in order to compensate for the risks to
        be taken up by the private party.

    (c) Performance Management Contract Option (PMC); PMC largely allocates risks to the
        public party with the exception that a private operator shares in gains achieved from
        performance improvements o f the operation. Performance contracts are usually
        implemented through incentive schemes and usually tied to agreed performance targets
        such as operational efficiency improvements, reducing water losses through metering or
        controlling thefts, improving collections or expanding coverage to increase revenue. This
        contracting mode differs from the traditional management contract in that it does place
        some pressures on a private manager besides performing duties on a straight fee-for-
        service basis. This option was rejected because most risks remain within the public
        sector (see Table 2) below.

B.    RecommendedPPPModel

The DBO option was chosen by the Government for project implementation. The choice o f
Design-Build-Operate PPP model i s largely governed by the following main considerations.

  (a) Allocation ofRisks to Private Party. Transferring to the extent possible from public to
      private, the various risks associated with the project while at the same time maintaining a
      good probability for successful implementation. The pure public model for example
      would have a highprobability for successful implementation but transfers few risks to the
      private sector.     By comparison, the BOT/concession model proposes to transfer
      essentially all risks, but would be too costly and would likely receive low interest from


                                               40

    prospective private parties in the absence o f sizable subsidy support, as recently
     demonstrated inthe Guerdane Concession inMorocco.

(b) Cost Recovery. Realize GOE's objective for cost recovery and for the project to sustain
     itself financially throughout its life. This effectively means the absence o f capital or
     operating subsidies to stop any financing gap that would result inany given year. While
     all models could, intheory, be implemented on a cost-recovery basis, inpractice only the
    models that take on the demand and commercial risks can actually shield GOE's need to
     intervene in the event o f a funding gap. While GOE have bailed out infrastructure
    projects in the past, this normally occurs only as a last resort measure when the cost
     structure and dynamics o f the project materially change and following substantive
    negotiations with private sponsors and financiers. In the pure public and management
    contract options, GOE's financial support would be solicited in all occurrences where
    cash positions fail to meet ongoing expenses.

(c) Favorable Financial Support. At the same time, GOE would assume some roles and
    related risks to achieve project development objectives for sustainability. As part o f this,
    the ability to utilize its credit capacity to mobilize long-term donor financing can greatly
    support the objective o f affordable tariffs which would not be feasible under more
    commercial financing terms and maturities. Moreover, the concern over conjunctive use
    requires that surface water tariff rates converge closely to the actual current cost o f
    pumping groundwater.         Any financing terms beyond what could be made available
    through international donor financing would substantially increase demand risk. This
    means that the pure private option that requires the private party to raise financing from
    commercial sources may substantially fall short on achieving affordable and competitive
    tariffs.

(d) Linking Planning, Constructionand OperatingRisks. To ensure the maximumpossible
    mitigation of demand risk, it will be essential to link the planning and construction
    scheduling to actual operation. Otherwise, there i s great danger that the system will be
    over-built and largely non sustainable from its inception.        As indicated, the actual
    implementation o f the proposed project would likely be carried out through a modular
    construction approach where certain parts o f the system would be extended to connect
    paying customers. In this regard, the utilization o f a closed conduit was determined as
    the more feasible technical solution in the event only clusters o f growers choose to
    connect to the system inthe early period o f project implementation. The pure concession
    and DBO agreements provide the sponsors full control o f these aspects o f project
    planning and implementation, while the other modes would actually dilute the
    accountability to a number o f different entities.

(e) Absolute Financial Commitments. Returns for a private party have to correspond not
    only to the level o f risk being undertaken in the project, but also have to consider the
    absolute value o f financial commitment that would be needed. In other words, at some
    level o f financial commitment, there would be little or no interest for investors to buy-in,
    despite how highthe expected returns may be. Moreover, lenders, who are by profession
    risk averse, would force equity sponsors to increase their financial commitment by



                                               41

       requiring them to secure the loan portion o f the project. Accordingly, the pure private
       model in this case would require a substantial financial commitment in the form o f
       personal or corporate guarantees from private sponsors beyond what i s typically
       requested by lenders under limitedrecourse financing.

Based on the foregoing considerations, DBO option proposes to be the more suitable model to
pursue for the West Delta although its attractiveness to potential investors would still need to be
confirmed.    The following phases o f this work would test the attractiveness o f the
recommendations with potential private investors.


       As in the pure private option, the DBO transfers most risks to the private sector but
       requires a relatively lower level o f financial commitment at the outset. Since GOEwould
       assume the role o f raising debt funds, private sponsors would only need to secure to
       contractual agreement to pay a pre-determined concession fee for the duration o f the
       tenure. The private party would be expected to raise the equity counterpart funds which
       are now estimated at 15% o f total investment. Typically, the equity contribution would
       be much greater intraditional private project finance schemes for BOTSand concessions.
       All this translates into lower costs andmore affordable tariffs for the growers.

       Sourcing loan funds through the international donor assistance would allow for maximum
      maturities typically not available though commercial arrangements. This would then
      maintaintariffs to affordable levels but without explicit capital or operational subsidies.

      The DBO approach also offers the private party control o f the critical phases o f planning
      and constructing the systems along with the need to connect actual paying customers to
      ensure financial sustainability at the outset. The bid agreements would however need to
      specify the minimumproject size inorder to ensure an adequate level o f coverage.

      The DBO allows private operators to earn a profit on the construction portion o f the
      contract which can be utilized to meet its own counterpart financing requirements, thus
      lowering the initial cash outlays commensurately.

      As commercial risk taker, the operator o f the DBO assumes the full upside benefits and
      would be able to expand coverage as ina straight concession to increase revenue.

ExecutiveSummary:

A financial management assessment was conducted to assess the adequacy of the projects
financial management arrangements. Inthis context, the assessment was based upon discussions

Contract Administration Unit (CAU), and reviewing the draft DBO contract and the draft
connection agreement.

The project i s comprised o f 3 different components as follows: 1) Design, Construct, and Start
Up o f the Surface Water System, and connection program for participating farms (US$205
million); 2) Market-driven Technical Assistance to Small and Medium Scale Farmers (US$2
million); and 3) Support for Institutional Development and Capacity Building o f Project
Management Unit,Regulatory Office and Water User Organization (US$6 million).

Given the Bank experience with the creation, structuring and placement o f PMUs, vagueness
about P M U responsibilities and lines o f reporting can hold up smooth implementation. For the
project earlier activities purpose that involve mainly prequalification and contract preparation
activities, MWRI's contract administration unit (CAU) i s already set up and staffed. When
formed, the P M U will be taking full responsibility with possible support by some o f the C A U
staff inproject activities on full and part time basis. The P M U i s envisaged to be staffed mainly
from one sector (horizontal expansion) which minimizes the chances for bottlenecks. MWRI
should however clearly define and communicate the set up o f the P M U and its relationship with
the C A U and lines o f reporting to avoid any possible overlap. In addition, the P M U
establishment i s set as an effectiveness condition per the loan agreement.

The nature o f the project gives rise to the risk o f setting the value of payments corresponding to
specific milestones not in line with the right level o f physical progress. Since the C A U i s
relatively newly formed, the "transaction advisors" including engineering and law consultants
are helping the C A U and project team with the milestones determination, valuation and
reflection in the DBO contract and a draft milestone schedule o f payments has already been
prepared.

Review and certification o f milestones for payment represents another critical function.
Financing under Component 3 (institutional support) will also reinforce P M U capacity to
monitor contract implementation. In addition, the MIS should be equipped to monitor the
physical progress and link it to the milestone schedule and payments. Finally, engineering

A.      General

Public sector procurement inEgypti s governed by a legal framework anchored inPublic Tender
Law No. 89 for the year 1998 and further detailed inthe CPAR o f December 2003.

One procurement, for the DBO, inthe amount o f $205 million, o f which US$145 million will be
financed from the Bank Loan. Contract award is expected to be completed using advance
procurement before loan effectiveness. This i s being carried out in accordance with the Bank's
"Guidelines: Procurement under IBRD Loans and IDA Credits".            The general description of
items under different expenditure categories is described below. The timeline for the Bank
financed component i s listed inthe Attachment to this Annex.

This procurement is beingconducted usingICB Procedures.

Procurementof Design-Build-OperatePackage

The proposed project to be financed by the Bank covers an area between25,200 ha and 37,800
ha located approximately 60 kilometers northwest o f Cairo. It will implement a surface water
conveyance system that extracts water from the Nile River. With the implementation of the
project, GOE will set a fixed allocation o f water to connecting farms and will require full cost
recovery through volumetric tariffs by a private operator.

The project conceptual design i s based on a demand-driven approach, in which the final design
will be based on firm commitments by subscribers to pay a volumetric charge for the quantity of
water supplied. Hence, unlike conventional irrigation projects there is no fixed area for which
the investments are estimated inadvance. Based on the feasibility studies and technical designs,
and consultations with farmers, the project area is estimated to be between 25,200 ha and 37,800
ha. The bid will be for one contract with a Private Operator to DBO a surface water irrigation
system within a concession area o f 79,800 ha (190,000 feddan).

The Bank with co-financing from AFD will make available financing o f US$145 million and
US$30 million respectively, towards the design-build portion o f the contract to the winning
bidder on an 85%-15% basis o f his own equity contribution. Some o f the specific details o f the
project bidand contractual requirements are summarized below:

         The basis for the bid will be the lowest combined tariff per cubic meter offered to
         connecting farmers. The bid guidelines will provide a maximum reference tariff. Bids
         above the reference tariff will be deemed nonresponsive.
         The tariff will be accompanied by the submission o f a conceptual technical design and
         the supporting financial model for the connection o f the 90,000 feddan evenly
         distributed over the concession area,
         For the amount o f financing drawn down from the loan, the operator commits to repay
         a concession fee that will allow the MWRIto repay its own loan obligation to the Bank
         and the other financier.


                                               56

           The selection process will be done on the basis o f the lowest tariff with a responsive
           technical proposal and supporting financial model.
           The conceptual design must meet the design standardsthat will be specified inthe bid
           documents and bidders must agree to the terms o f the DBO and connection contracts to
           avoid substantial delays afterwards. The private operator will use the initial system
           layout o f the system to estimate the expected expropriation and compensation costs.
           Following execution o f the concession DBO contract, the operator will conduct a
           "subscription period" to sign up farmers to the system. It will do so by signing a
           connection agreement with each farm and by collecting security deposits. If less than
           60,000 feddan are subscribed, GOE has the option to void the contract. If the
           subscription i s less than 90,000 feddan but more than 60,000, the operator may at his
           option continue with the contract and build the system with lower subscribers, but he
           must hold his bid tariff based on the 90,000 connection. The contract will be effective
           upon successful farmer subscription.
           Following the subscription period, the private operator would execute the contract and
           complete the detailed design with a detailed cost estimate.
           Based on the final design amount, the payments to the operator would be made
           according to a pre-determined formuldschedule based on the achievement o f real
           outputs.
           The private operator will be required to post a performance bond inaddition to infusing
           hisown capital inthe infrastructure.
           The period of the concession will be for 30 years. At expiration, the private operator
           must turn over the system in good working order according to the terms of the DBO
           contract and associated service standards.
           The DBO contract provides for other provisions and penalties and fines in case the
           private operator does not fulfill hisresponsibilities.

Due to the size and complexity o f the required works, pre-qualification is being conducted to

project, which is well in excess o f the opportunity cost o f capital, taken at the rate o f 10%. To

improve water availability i s completed.

                             Assumptions to the FinancialProjections



 I. Introduction

A fully integrated financial projection model was developed to support the financial analysis o f
 WDWCIRP and to assess the financial implications to the project o f the various policy options
that are currently under consideration. The model addresses the implications o f the new water
tariff levels and structure and the new installation charges that are under consideration.
Essentially, all important elements o f the proposed project were incorporated in order to assess
the related financial implications and long-term viability and to assess the critical years where
financing gaps may result. The financial model accepts as inputs, basic assumptions on revenues,
operating expenses, new investment and ongoing capital expenditures, financial management and
current operating performance efficiency, in addition to other assumptions on financing and
amortization o f existing loans.

The objective i s to assemble the financial information of various technical proposals for decision
makingby private growers and policy officials and to come up with a recommended transaction
model for implementing the project.        The methodology consists of: (i)developing a fully
interactive financial projection model that is capable o f simulating financial solutions under
number o f different cost, tariff and volumetric demand scenarios; (ii)developing feasible
financing assumptions which would be incorporated into a financial projection model as basis
assumptions; (iii)incorporating applicable tax and the other policies that would impact on the
financial projections; (iv) simulating for various technical options of the project; (v) calculating
the investment and O&M cost data and derive per-unit cost estimates that would be through the
growers for decision to participate in new system; and (vi) developing a framework for setting
tariffs as well as the adjustments over time.

11.     West DeltaPPPFinancingProgram

The financial model was programmed to simulate the activity of a potential operator (public or
private) which focuses essentially on constructing and operating a surface water conveyance
system to supply irrigation water to its customer base. It is expected to carry out these activities
on a cost recovery basis and while maintaining a positive cash flow. Generally applied financial
and accounting principles were incorporated to simulate as much as possible the FM principles
that would be utilized by the operator under the WDWCIRP. As such, the total funds available
through internal and external financing sources are allocated based on specific priorities as
would typically be done by their respective directors o f finance. Requirements for working
capital and obligations for debt servicing are hlfilled first inorder to finance ongoing operations
o f the operation. New investment requirements are then satisfied, first from the remaining
internally generated funds and then estimated secured debt and new equity financing.
Specifically, external sources o f funds, including both borrowings and equity contributions are
drawn as independent variables inorder to bridge whatever financing gap may result between the
estimated capital expenditures and remaining cash available after debt service and working
capital obligations. As such, the model draws only those funds that may be absolutely necessary
to meet the needs of the project while still maintaining adequate levels for working capital.


                                                73

Annual cash surpluses are initially reinvested in short-term deposits earning a minimal rate o f
return. Cash deficits that may result in any given year during the projected period would be
financed, first by the sale o f excess short-term deposits or other current assets while still
maintaining an appropriate level o f current assets to meet ongoing working capital requirements.
Unidentified debt financing is not considered an option for meeting shortfalls in cash during the
latter years o f the projection period. Therefore, new equity contributions are regarded as the
only final source o f financing year-end shortfalls o f cash. However, since these additional equity
contributions may not necessarily be identified or assumed at the outset o f this assessment, other
assumptions such as, scaling down or extending the program are revised first in order to derive a
workable solution. If,on the other hand, there i s absolutely no conceivable way to achieve this
and still retain the integrity o f the proposed project, it would therefore be necessary to assume
contributions o f additional equity that would necessarily be agreed in advance with the private
operator, the farmers and GOE.


111.     SignificantAccountingPrinciples

The projections incorporate a number o f accounting principles which are important for the
interpretation o f The West Delta projected financial operations and statements. These include:

1       Accrual Accounting. Revenues and expenses are accounted on the accrual basis for
which collections andpayments are governed by acceptable FMparameters.

2.      Base CostEstimates. Construction costs were estimated in 2005 prices and brought
forward to year-end2006 prices through bothtechnical and escalation adjustments.

3.       ConstructionPeriod. Construction is accounted as work inprocess until completed and
operational. Duringthis period, interest charges on debt and commissions on performance bond
are capitalized as are any other pre-operating expense.      Fixed assets are then depreciated once
they are placed in service. Once operational status is achieved, the fixed assets, capitalized
interest and other pre-operating expenses are amortized over a specified period based on the life
o f the project.

4.      Foreign Exchange Losses. The projections assume that the loan and fixed assets related
to the proposed project will be booked in the accounts o f GOE which would also bear the
currency risk. Under a DBO arrangement, the private operator would be obligated to pay a
concession fee based on the actual amount o f loan drawdown at a fixed interest rate o f 7%,
which would be 3 to 3.5 basis points over the GOE cost o f funds to cover the estimated currency
losses.

5.      Fixed Asset Revaluation. At present, the accounting rules in Egypt do not allow for the
revaluation o f fixed assets originally recorded at cost on the books. As such, the projections do
not provide for a formal asset revaluation as such.

6.      Contributions to Werfare and Enterprise Funds. The proposed project i s expected to
make contributions from retainedearnings into bank deposit accounts.

           provide the cost for such expropriation.

           The Bank will provide its "No Objection" to the successful bidder provided it fully
           complies with the RPF and the draft resettlement plans prepared by the successful
           bidder. The contract should stipulate that no civil works can start until the PAP are
           fully compensatedat market rate.

           After contract signature, the DBO contractor will officially submit to MWRI the
           necessarydocumentation for their review and approval.

           MWRI will be responsible to proceed and complete the expropriation process in
           accordance with the RPF and RPs.

           Once the expropriatiodland acquisition process i s officially completed, MWRI will
           require that the DBO contractor provides PAP the full amount of compensation
           before issuing the working orders for construction.

           MWRI will submit to the Bank in its semi-annual progress report, the status of
           expropriatiodlandacquisition and compensations.

Potential impacts of increased water levels of the Rosetta Branch will be examined in a special
study by the NRI, as indicated in the ESIA. For the temporary and permanent expropriation and
rights of way in the project area, the corridors have not yet been designed, so the extent and
location of the impact cannot be given. The DBO contractor will optimize his design to avoid
andminimize the needfor expropriation.

The NaturalHabitatsPolicy(OP 4.04) is not triggered. It is expected that the proposedproject
will have a positive effect on the hydrology of Wadi el Natrun, the only natural habitat expected
to be under some influence of the project, due to anticipated groundwater recharge from
irrigation. Wadi el Natrun has no officially protected status. Other natural habitats in Egypt are
far away from the project and hydrologically hardly connected. The coastal wetlands like Lake
Mariut and Lake Edku will probably get slightly better quality freshwater due to the diluting
effects of the bigger Rosettadischarge.

The PhysicalCultural ResourcesPolicy (OP 4.11) is triggered for precautionary reasons. For
the scale of works envisaged under the proposed project, Egyptian Law always requires a
number of procedures to be followed before and during construction, in order to obtain and
maintain clearance to proceed. The Supreme Council of Antiquities i s responsible for the
protection and conservation of Cultural Properties.

The matter is taken care of inthe contracts preparedby the HorizontalExpansion Sector (Clause
25 o fthe MWRI general contract no. 3333-b), which states:

      "All antiquities that can be found by the contractor during the excavations should be
      remitteddirectly to the office belonging to the High Council of Antiquities. Inthe case of
      projects occurring in or close to an antiquities area, the High Council of Antiquities is
      responsible to bring technical laborersto observe the location andthe antiquities which cost
      i s covered by the contractor"

Measures

The Supreme Council of Antiquities is the Authority which applies Law 117. If the Law is
applied correctly, it provides sufficient safeguard for the preservation of physical cultural
resources. It is thus an avoidance measure. Clause 25 o f the MWRI's general contract
specifications is inagreementwith Law 117. It will be part of the DBO contract.

The PestManagementPolicy(OP 4.09) is triggered inas far as the project triggers an increase
in cultivated area and hence an increase in the application of pesticides, insecticides and
herbicides inabsolute terms. The use of fertilizers will equally grow. It has beensaid before that
most of the farmers in the area are export oriented and are producing under the Europe-GAP
protocol. This organization promotes integrated pest management and monitors the producers
whichthey have certified.

Measures

Integrated pest management not only serves the quality of the produce, but also contributes to
protection of the groundwater and has therefore been included in the number of measures that
protect groundwater quality and forms part of the sustainable groundwater management plan.
The aim is to spreadthe goodpractices of most of the farmers to those who are lagging behindin
this regard.

The InternationalWaterways Policy (OP 7.50) is triggered in as far as WDWCIRP will have
an annual abstraction from the Nile River, which is shared by 10 riparian countries. By 2010
(when the project is operational) the abstraction from the Nile to serve the project area is
expected to range between300 mcdyear (for 25,200 ha) and 450 mcdyear (for 37,800 ha). As
explained earlier, the water extracted from the Nile to serve the farms will be more than
compensated by loss of water usage because of rural-urban land conversion, and water savings
from irrigation improvement initiatives by GOE.

Measures

The Nile riparian countries have formed the NBI for enhanced collaborative development and
management of the Nile River for the benefit of all. While the NBI is not yet established as a
permanent institution, the Nile Council of Ministers (Nile-COM) acts as the body governing the
activities under the NBI. The WDWCIRP is one of a series of NBI projects which the Nile-
COM has approved for project preparationunder the umbrella of the NBI. GOE had developed a
project profile for WDWCIRP that summarizes the key components and identifies the potential
water related impacts. The project profile has been shared with the riparian countries under an
agreed procedure thus meeting the requirementso f the Bank's policy on International Waterways
(OP 7.50). WDWCIRP Profile for discussion is presentedinAnnex 1ofthe MainESIA Report.
