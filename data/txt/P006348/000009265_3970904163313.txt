in better quality and efficiency in research and human
resource development projects, multiyear finance for such
projects, and more cost effective services for the science
and technology sector.

I. OVERVIEW AND PLAN OF REPORT
1.01      In Brazil, the worldts ninth largest economy, the character and pace
of technological change-and ultimately the rates of future economic
growth-have been compromised by sharp reductions in investment for science and
technology in a time of economic crisis as well as by weaknesses in the policy
framework and in the institutional, human, and physical infrastructure for
scientific and technological research.
1.02       In recommending a Bank loan of US$72 million as the first phase of
Bank suipport for the requisite infrastructural improvements of science and
technology in Brazil, this report addresses several questions:
- What role do science and technology play in Brazilian
development? Which characteristics of the Brazilian science and
technology sector most inhibit its contribution to growth? What is
the Brazilian government's strategy to respond to those problems
(Chapter II)?
- What comprises the investment program-called Programa de Apoio ao
Desenvolvimento Cientifico e Tecnologico (PADCT)--proposed by the
government as the vehicle to put the strategy into practice
(Chapter III)?
- How will PADCT be managed (Chapter IV)?
- How will PADCT be financed (Chapter V)?
- What are the potential benefits and foreseeable risks associated with
PADCT (Chapter VI)?
- What covenants and special conditions would be attached to Bank
cofinancing of PADCT through the proposed loan of US$72 million
(Chapter VII)?
II.   SCIENCE, TECHNOLOGY AND BRAZILIAN DEVELOPMENT
A. Science, Technology and Change
2.01     Wihatever else it may entail, economic development always involves
employing resources in new and more efficient ways. This technological
development is the engine of growth in agriculture, industry, and services.



2.02      The scope and pace of a nation's technological development depend upon
its collective ability (i) to assess the adequacy of existing technologies in
the context of constantly changing product markets and factor endowments;
(ii) to screen proven foreign technologies, and adapt those selected to local
needs and conditions; (iii) to generate new technologies when necessary; and
(iv) to incorporate technological advance, whatever its source, into productive
processes to achieve optimum use of available resources. In each of these
areas, the depth and breadth of a nation's infrastructure for scientific and
technological research is a crucial determinant of success. Rich and poor
countries differ in their relative reliance upon assimilation and in'igenous
generation of technology.
2.03      Large and rapidly industrializing countries like Brazil have
particularly strong motivation for indigenous development of technology and its
underlying base of scient'fic research. Heavy reliance upon imported technology
involves a major drain on scarce foreign exchange and would in any case be
difficult to assimilate in such a heterogeneous environment. Imported
technologies are often ill suited to the variety of local conditions and factor
endowments. Heavy dependence upon foreign technology may also be politically
unpalatable.
B. Scientific Underpinnings of Brazilian Growth
2.04      The vitality of science and technology is an increasingly important
factor in the success of Brazil's evolving growth strategy.
2.05      Following four years of unprecedented negative real growth, Brazil's
immediate objective is to make the transition from a harsh short term
stabilization program to sustained development later in the 1980s. Resumption
of growth requires a strategy quite different from that which produced the
'Brazilian miracle" in the 15 years prior to 1979. Brazil must generate more of
its own resources for development and rely less on foreign borrowing; more
attention will have to be given to employment generation, and to the quality as
well as the availability of basic social services such as health care and
education. An enhanced ability to compete successfully for a wider range of
industrial and agricultural products in international markets over the
intermediate and long term is a fundamental objective of Brazilian economic
policy. So is the drive (i) to increase domestic sources of energy to alleviate
the drain on foreign exchange of importing oil, (ii) to improve public sector
management so that more limited governmental resources are used with greater
effect and (iii) to develop numerous diverse Brazilian owned, small and medium
size private enterprises, and reduce reliance on the large state enterprises and
multinational corporations. Brazilian science and technology will play an
important part in moving to and keeping on this new growth path.
2.06      Agricultural output-for home consumption of a growing population, for
export, and as an alternative energy source-will depend increasingly upon
sophisticated biotechnology to alter the genetic makeup of crops. This can
result in higher nutrient content, enhanced disease resistance, better control



-3-
of pests, and improved viability under adverse conditions of drought, and poor
or toxic soils. Biotechnology also has important applications in human and
animal health, especially in improving diagnostic procedures and facilitating
low cost production of vaccines. Exploitation of Brazil's considerable mineral
resources requires state-of-the-art capacity in geosciences and mineral
technology to find, assay, mine, beneficiate and incorporate minerals into
industrial processes. Reduced dependence upon imported oil not only means
finding more oil and gas, where again expertise in geosciences is a limiting
factor, it also presupposes an advanced national capacity for basic research and
engineering in chemistry to develop alternatives for sugarcane as a feedstock
for alcohol, to determine the viability of vegetable oils as a substitute for
diesei, and to make effective use of Brazil's substantial coal and forest
resources. Subdisciplines of chemistry also constitute the essential scientific
underpinnings of the food processing, pharmaceutical, and plastics industries.
2.07      Significant increases in manufactured exports, including food
products, require incorporation of up-to-date technology in the production
processes of a much broader range of enterprises. This presupposes
sensitization of managers and entrepreneurs to the potential gains from more
efficient use of technical resources. Wider dissemination of practical
information on available technologies must be accompanied by higher levels of
quality control and component standardization. This requires improvement in the
underlying metrological, standardization, and patent services, and wider use of
modern process control instrumentation. A national capability to produce
appropriate instruments-and to maintain and repair a growing inventory of
equipment for various uses-is also an essential input to improvement of medical
care, and for support of science and engineering research and education at all
levels. As higher levels of technology are applied throughout the economy, a
widely diffused understanding of the physical environment, acquired in part
through science education in the schools, becomes a more important ingredient
both of overall labor productivity and of the costs of training workers.
Finally, an ability to monitor the performance of the science and technology
sector and to adjust the policy framework and specific plans on the basis of
fact rather than conjecture is an important determinant of public sector
efficiency.
2.08      In these and other zreas of science and technology, much basic
knowledge and technology is already available in Brazil, and much more can be
acquired from abroad. But in an age of rapidly increasing technological
sophistication, a highly developed base in the underlying sciences, engineering,
and technological support services is essential to screen and select among
available technologies, to adapt them where possible, and to develop entirely
new approaches when necessary.
C. Bank Experience in the Sector
2.09      The Bank has supported research in Brazil in previous loans.
Assistance for applied research on farming systems and crop-specific production
technology was provided through Loans 1249-BR and 2016-BR to the Corporation of



-4-
Agricultural Research (Empresa Brasileira de Pesquisas Agropercuarias -
EMBRAPA). Both have been effective in producing and disseminating research
results and helping to improve EMBRAPA's capacity to manage research. Loan
1989-BR, for the government's Alcohol and Biomass Energy Program, provided funds
for research on various aspects of alcohol production systems and has already
produced important results. Additional information is available in Annex 1.
These efforts, and others in fields such as hydroelectric power and sewerage
technology, were made through intermediaries whose mandate is limited to
specific applied activities. The Bank loan recommended in this report would
address broader critical issues which are holding back overall productivity of
Brazilian research and development, and thus would enhance ongoing and future
area and problem specific support by the Bank.
D. Sector Management
2.10      In 1972, Brazil established the National System for Scientific and
Technological Development (SNDCT), which encompasses all organizations using
public resources for purposes of planning, coordinating, executing and
evaluating research, and disseminating research results. Under the SNDCT,
responsibility for sector coordination lies with the Presidential Planning
Secretariat (SEPLAN), which, in turn, acts through the National Council for
Scientific and Technological Development (CNPq). The Scientific and
Technological Council (CCT, formally a part of CNPq; CNPq's staff acts as its
executive secretariat) formulates broad science and technology priorities, and
seeks to assure adequate coordination of the nation's efforts in science and
technology. CNPq also administers seven separate research institutes and other
activities for support of basic research. In addition to CNPq, there are three
other key intermediary agencies through which public funds for nonmilitary,
scientific research and technology development and related training programs are
channeled. The Agency for Financing Studies and Projects (FINEP) was
established to finance preinvestment studies and technology development and to
support technology transfer to Brazil. FINEP also administers the National Fund
for Scientific and Technology Development (FNDCT), the principal government
source of grant funding for science and technology. The Agency for Training of
High Level Personnel (CAPES) of the Ministry of Education and Culture is the
coordinating agency for second and third degree higher education. Finally, the
Secretariat for Industrial Technology (STI), under the Ministry of Industry and
Commerce, is responsible for metrology, standards and quality control, patents,
and applied technology development, all of which are carried out through various
institutes. All four agencies have long and successful experience in project
funding. Annex 2 contains additional background on the sector and its major
institutions, and provides a few examples of Brazilian scientific research which
have made a significant contribution to growth.
2.11      Brazil's current policy for science and technology is described in its
1980-85 Basic National Plan for Scientific and Technology Development (PBDCT).
The two main objectives of the plan are (i) to further develop the nation's
scientific capacity through quality improvement and expansion of research and
development activities and (ii) to support improvements in capacity to select
and generate more adequate technologies for Brazil's economic and social growth.

E. Major Issues
2.12      Important issues regarding human resource development, institutional
coordination, and finance were identified during Bank sector work, and agreement
was reached with the government on how they should be addressed in order to
achieve the objectives of PBDCT. The central concerns are inadequacy and
fragmentation of the resource base, inefficiencies of the education system,
imbalances in the composition of the national effort, and ineffectiveness of
institutional arrangements. Greater detail is contained in "Brazil: Issues in
Science and Technology Development" (Report No. 4819(a)-BR, January 27, 1984).
Resource Base
2.13      Since about 1979, Brazil has failed to sustain, let alone generate,
critical mass efforts in its science and technology sector. Too few resources
have been spread too thinly across too many discrete activities. The major
government funding agencies have usually approved support for one or at most two
years at a time, a period too short for meaningful results to be attained from
most scientific and technological development activities. In order to piece
together the many inputs typically required for a project, institutions engaged
in high level research and/or graduate education have had to approach several
government funding agencies simultaneously, each of which supplied only one or
two of the required inputs under policies, procedures, and schedules
unsynchronized or even incompatible with the others. Delays between approval of
support and actual delivery of the funds in a period of rapid inflation eroded
the real value of grants so much that achievement of originally envisioned
results was precluded. As the competition for funds increased in the late 1970s
with return of manv Brazilians from graduate training abroad, the average size
of awards became so small that significant results could not realistically be
expected. Each of these trends was exacerbated by the shortfall of public
sector resources for investment occasioned by Brazil's current economic crisis.
Tightly restricted access to foreign exchange had particularly severe
repercussions in a sector which depends on imports of equipment and technical
expertise. These deficiencies in the resource base have resulted in
deterioration of installed capacity in relatively advanced fields and in failure
to generate new capacity in relatively underdeveloped areas important for
growth.
Educational System
2.14      Brazil's educational system is not yet producing at any level
graduates with the qualities needed to sustain economic development. When
science is taught in primary and secondary schools, it typically is limited to
memorization of outdated facts usually of questionable pertinence to the
students' physical environment. Rarely employed is the experimentally based
"discovery mode" of teaching which would prepare students for a world of
accelerating technological change. The general population does not acquire the
minimum understanding of science and technology which is the foundation for
development of modern agriculture, industry and services. Explosive expansion
of first degree higher education in the 1970s (enrollments multiplied nearly
three times in the 12 years ending in 1982) was achieved only by sacrificing the
quality of academic programs, especially in the capital intensive fields of



-6-
science and engineering. Too often graduate (second and third degree higher)
education today is remedial in content and orientation. While in certain fields
(for example some areas of engineering), overproduction of dubious quality
personnel is already evident, in other fields (especially in the sciences)
the numbers of highly trained professionals needed for improving the quality of
higher education, for intensifying research and development in priority areas,
or for immediate employment in the productive sectors have not materialized.
Composition of Effort
2.15      Brazil's science and technology sector suffers from important
imbalances in the composition of national effort. Capacity for research and
development in several disciplines critical for economic growth has lagged
behind, in part because of inadequate production of high level personnel in
these areas (notably chemistry and related engineering fields, biotechnology,
geosciences and mineral technology, and the engineering subareas essential to
development of modern instrumentation for science and Industry). Eighty five
percent of Brazilian infrastructure both for production of high level personnel
and for research and development is concentrated in the south of the country.
The result is a mismatch between availability and orientation of scientific and
technological expertise, and the unexploited natural resource base and acute
development challenges in the northeast, centerwest and north of Brazil.
2.16      Host important, the unavailability of crucial support services for
science and technology imposes significant constraints on the productivity of
the sector as a whole. Science cannot be efficient without access to a wide
variety of information sources, without facilities for equipment maintenance and
repair, without reliable supplies of research reagents and consumables.
The transfer of scientific results to the productive sectors and the diffusion
of proven technology depends on such elements of technological infrastructure as
metrological services, access to information on patents and standards, and
mechanisms for quality control. In all these areas Brazil is severely
deficient.
Institutional Arrangements
2.17      Brazil's mechanisms for setting and enforcing priorities for
scientific and technological development are not effective. Under the law,
CNPq, operating through its CCT, is supposed to take the lead in formulating
sector policies and in coordinating their implementation by the many constituent
units of the SNDCT, most especially by the four key government science and
technology funding agencies. The CCT is a large top-heavy body without
effective mechanisms to oversee the intermediary agencies. CNPq's supposed
service in this area is compromised by its being a major funder of scientific
development activities itself. The conflict of interest inherent in being
simultaneously an arbitor of the activities of other agencies and a key actor
has undermined the confidence of the user communities, especially as resources
have shrunk and choices among competing uses have become much more difficult.
CNPq's potential effectiveness is further undermined by its lack of control over
sector resources. While the legal mandate to oversee sectoral development rests
with the CCT and the technical staff of CNPq serving as its secretariat, power



-7-
over the purse really resides in FINEP, as executive secretariat of the FNDCT,
and in the Budget and State Enterprise Control Secretariats of the SEPLAN. This
separation of oversight and funding responsibilities means (i) that decisions on
substance taken by the competent sectoral bodies are not always enforceable and
(ii) that decisions on flows of resources can be taken without due consideration
of their likely impact by the appropriate sectoral authorities.
2.18      Ambiguity in defining roles and responsibilities of the several
governmental science and technology funding agencies, and conflicting policies
and regulations of government agencies outside the sector further compromise the
effectiveness of institutional arrangements. Resources from several agencies
may be destined for the same ends (for example, graduate training abroad of
researchers), while other needs of the sector (for example, equipment
maintenance and repair facilities) are left unattended. The restrictions on
microelectronic components imposed by the Special Secretariat for Informatics
(Secretaria Especial de Informatica - SEI) have deprived researchers and
graduate students of state-of-the-art instrumentation, and have idled a
significant fraction of Brazil's most costly and sophisticated scientific
equipment for lack of replacement parts. Rationing of foreign exchange by the
Foreign Trade Bureau (Carteira de Comercio Exterior - CACEX) has drastically
curtailed such fundamental inputs to scientific research and technological
development as subscriptions to foreign journals, chemical and biological
reagents, nonelectronic equipment and spare parts/consumables, and foreign
patents and standards.
2.19      Decision making in the sector is also faulty.  Decisions on research
and development program objectives, content, and implementation have often been
taken without adequate involvement of Brazil's expert community of scientists
and engineers. At the project level too, systematic peer review and evaluation
of individual applications to the funding agencies are incipient or poorly
operated. Inadequate decision mechanisms have led to misdirected programs,
wasteful misallocation of resources, and poor quality research and development
effort--all of which have contributed to a resurgence of mutual dissatisfaction
and distrust between the government funding agencies and the scientific
community.
F. The Program for Science and and Technology Development (PADCT)
2.20      In response to the aforementioned issues which constrain the
development of the sector, the government prepared the Program for Support of
Science and Technology Development (PADCT) over a two year period. In the first
phase of a larger, longer term effort, PADCT has been launched as an investment
program for science and technology with total commitments over the first five
years of about $360 million. During the first three years (1984-86), PADCT
commitments are expected to total about US$215 million. This initial time slice
of commitments is the basis for the proposed Bank loan.
2.21      The main objectives of the PADCT are (i) to provide funding for
longer term and targeted research and human resources development projects in
six areas of science, (ii) to consolidate and improve the functioning of science



-8-
service systems through national programs for equipment maintenance and repairs,
supply of research consumables, science information services, and basic
industrial technology, and (iii) to introduce an evolutionary approach to
institutional improvement for the sector.
2.22      An detailed later in this report, this approach to improvement of
sector management utilizes existing institutions and provides them with the
means to work better separately and together. Responsibilities allocated to the
institutions would be those which have constituted the core of their work for
many years. Where necessary, the processes by which they arrive at and execute
decisions would be reformulated or otherwise strengthened; new bodies would be
established only to assure that the legal mandates of each agency, and
especially of the CCT to coordinate policy formulation and program
implementation, can be effectively carried out.
2.23      The principal new bodies which have been established are Technical
Advisory Groups (GTs) to set priorities in each area, to elaborate operational
guidelines, to monitor implementation of programs, and to recommend adjustments
to ongoing programs on the basis of accumulating experience. The GTs have
exercised a high degree of selectivity with respect to specific activities to be
supported, thereby assuring that limited resources are sufficiently concentrated
to have impact.
2.24      Peer review mechanisms for decision making on project funding would
also be rationalized and strengthened. Through participation in Appraisal
Committees (CAs), the communities of scientists, engineers, and technologists
would be involved in a deliberative fashion in selection of projects for funding
and in evaluation of results obtained.
2.25      Finally, under a new management unit (Executive Secretariat - SE)
created to ensure coordination, the four government intermediaries (CNPq, FINEP,
CAPES, STI) have been brought to a clear and public agreement on their
respective roles so that, as a function of specific characteristics of their
projects, applicants for government funding will know unambiguously which agency
should consider the application and will be able to acquire from that single
agency all the inputs needed to undertake the project. This one-stop shopping
approach would replace the current piecemeal system which compels an applicant
to package the required inputs supplied by a variety of agencies on different
conditions and schedules. Multiyear financing, in monetary units of reasonably
constant value, would replace the current one to two year allocations.
2.26      Annex 3 contains more information on the policy framework and
objectives of PADCT.
III. CONTENT OF PADCT AND OF THE PROJECT
A. Overview
3.01      In physical terms, PADCT comprises increased and carefully coordinated
investments in two kinds of subprogram. The first provides for investments in
lagging fields of science and technology which are crucial to economic growth.
The second involves investments in the support services which are essential



- 9 -
underpinnings for scientific and technological productivity in every field.
Whether in discipline or service oriented subprograms, investments will be in
the form of discrete subprojects of three general types, implemented over
periods of two to five years. The government's four major science and
technology funding agencies (CNPq, FINEP, CAPES, and STI) will (i) disseminate
information on program priorities, content guidelines, and available funding as
delineated by the Technical Advisory Groups; (ii) solicit subproject
applications; (iii) appraise those subproject proposals with the help of
committees of scientific peers; (iv) provide financial support for approved
subprojects; (v) monitor subproject execution; and (vi) evaluate subproject
results. Each intermediary will assume exclusive responsibility for appraising
certain types of subprojects according to the published guidelines specific to
each subprogram, but using common mechanisms and procedures. Those proposals
which are approved will be implemented by the applicant institution under a
single contract for funding with the intermediary whiich originally appraised the
proposal and which will assume responsibility for supervision. Some subprojects
will be executed directly by the four government intermediary institutions or by
agencies which fall under their immediate supervision. The central features of
the PADCT investment program are discussed below.
B. Subproject Types
3.02      PADCT investment subprojects, for which commitments totalling
US$350 million are expected to be made during 1984-88, will be of three major
types, according to their fundamental purpose. Concrete examples of each are
presented in Annex 4 for illustrative purposes only, without intent to exhaust
all possibilities.
Human Resource Subprojects-US$93 million
3.03      The overriding objective of the first type of subproject is to
consolidate and, where still necessary as in the northeast, north, and
centerwest regions, to extend the human resource base for science and
technology. Most subprojects of this type will strengthen, or occasionally
create, master's and doctoral programs in Irazil's universities. A lesser
number will support nondegree training, including training for specialized
technicians. Research institutes and high technology enterprises (especially
the large parastatals) will host some of this training.
3.04      The expected output of these human resource subprojects is more and
better trained personnel for the sector. Many should be capable of leading or
participating in major research and development projects, and of infusing higher
quality into lower levels of the educational system. Others will have acquired
particularly sound training for application in the productive sectors. Trainees
will usually be drawn from and reintegrated into employment within the sector.
Research and Development Subprojects-US$148 million
3.05      The fundamental purpose of the second type of PADCT subproject is to
shed light on a defined scientific or technological problem, through systematic



- 10 -
research or developmental activity. Frequently these subprojects will be
directly related to a specific problem within significant development sectors
such as agriculture, energy, industry. However, these subprojects may also
support basic research where no direct or immediate application is envisioned.
3.06      The expected output of research and development subprojects is a
report on a completed research project or ongoing line of research, a series of
papers published in scientifically and industrially oriented professional
journals, a prototype item reacy for commercialization, or a proven process
ready for application.
Service Subprojects--US$109 million
3.07      This third type of PADCT subproject will improve or establish some
infrastructural service to enhance the productivity of science and technology in
many disciplines.
3.08      The expected result of such subprojects is a broad range of inputs for
scientific and technological activity that are of higher quality, and that
are both more widely and more rapidly available. As noted later, about
two thirds of these service subprojects will be concentrated in four articulated
subprograms whose broad purpose is to increase the overall productivity of
science and technology; the remaining third of the service subprojects will be
specific to targeted disciplines.
C. Subproject Appraisal Guidelines
3.09      For each subprogram, the Project file contains an extensive Brazilian
Subprogram Proposal setting forth the rationale, overall goals, strategy to be
followed, technical detail of subproject content with the associated resource
allocations and model budgets, and special criteria, procedures, and mechanisms
for subproject management. This material has been recapitulated in briefer
public announcements for PADCT and separately for each subprogram. These
announcements, also contained in the Project file, are the government's requests
for subproject proposals. Widely disseminated in Brazil in April 1984, these
program announcements contain a listing of appraisal guidelines. While varying
in detail across subprograms, the appraisal guidelines fall into four general
categories involving (i) scientific/technological content orientation with
respect to priority themes for research, sought directions for graduate or other
training program development, and infrastructural service specifications;
(ii) regional targets for resource allocation; (iii) institutional eligibility
criteria; and (iv) economic or financial parameters for subproject size, unit
costs for common inputs, required counterpart contribution of the applicant
agency, and, where appropriate, efficiency indicators such as ratio of doctoral
to predoctoral level staff, number of graduate student enrollees per course or
senior professor, etc. Regional targets for resource allocation generally apply
to infrastructural services and human resource development activities,
especially when the creation of a new training program is particularly requested
for the development of a less privileged region of the country. Regional

targets do not typically apply for research activities where resource allocation
is determined essentially by the technical merit of the subproject itself.
3.10      These Subprogram Proposals and announcements were exhaustively
examined by the Bank's appraisal consultants at various points in their
iterative development by Brazilian working groups of scientists and agency
staff.IJ As set out in the proposals and announcements, the subprogram

D. Subprogram Areas
3.11      Given the extent of the sector and its uneven development, it is not
feasible or desirable for PADCT to encompass more than a small portion of it.
Eligibility criteria for subprojects are necessary to limit PADCT's scope.
Drawing heavily upon (i) the Third Basic Plan for Scientific and Technological
Development (Plano Basico para o Desenvolvimento Cientifico e Tecnologico -
PBDCT), (ii) the Acao Programada (Programmed Action - AP), the breakdown of the
PBDCT into programs) and its constituent action plans, and (iii) the progress
assessments contained in Avaliacoes e Perspectivas (see Annex 2, paras. 15-17),
the CCT identified 10 substantive areas for special attention under PADCT.
3.12      In relation to the sector as a whole, these 10 PADCT subprograms
constitute a sharply constrained effort. In financial terms, all activities of
the 4 governmental funding intermediaries broadly construed as falling within
the 10 subprogram areas accounted for just under one quarter of the expenditures
of those agencies for 1981 and 1982, equivalent to about 4% of Brazil's overall
science and technology effort. Because not all such activities are covered
within the PADCT investment program, a maximum of 15% of the four
intermediaries' total projected regular budgetary resources for the five year
period 1984-88 would be destined for PADCT subprojects. PADCT will thus account
for roughly 3% of Brazil's overall science and technology effort.
3.13      In making its selection of subprogram areas, the CCT applied three
main criteria. The areas are of central importance to Brazil's long term
development strategy (paras. 2.04-2.08). They are also highly interdependent
and mutually supportive; progress in any one of them enhances the chances for

3.16      Penetration of a wider range of international markets, protection of
the health and safety of the Brazilian consn mer, and wider acceptance of local
goods and services within the Brazilian market all depend upon Brazilian
capability to embody technological innovation in production and to produce goods
and services to exacting standards of quality in terms of physical
characteristics, technical performance, and reliability. These requirements for
innovation and quality assurance become more difficult to meet as the number of
production units and their geographical dispersion increase, and as their
average size decreases-i.e., as greater reliance is placed on diverse
development of small and medium size private enterprises, a central theme of
Brazil's growth strategy.
3.17      Technological change and quality assurance presuppose (i) widespread
understanding of the need to produce to high quality standards, and of the costs

technologies and on quality standards; and (iii) the means of producing to tight
specifications, including most fundamentally a capability for physical
measurements and calibrations of sufficient accuracy to ensure uniformity of
output and compatibility among products of local and foreign origin. The

will be executed in-house by IBICT.
(c) The Subprogram for Reagents and Other Research Consumables--US$8.6 million.
3.21       In most fields of teaching and research in science, the most highly
trained personnel and most sophisticated equipment cannot be productive without
secure and rapid access to a wide range of chemicals and other research
consumables. Such access is lacking in Brazil today because of the virtual
absence of a fine chemical and consumables industry, and of mechanisms which
ensure procurement of these items from foreign sources within a time frame
compatible with productive research and advanced teaching.
3.22      Thus, the two central objectives of the Subprogram for Reagents and
Research Consumables are creation of a system for rapid supply of chemicals from
local and foreign sources, and development of the Brazilian fine chemical and
consumables manufacturing base, initially for local use and eventually for
export, especially to Latin America and Africa.
3.23      To assure rapid access to imported chemicals and supplies, this
subprogram will include: establishment of a series of Brazilian supply centers
(a central node and about six regional satellites) at which the most frequently
requested foreign items will be stocked and distributed upon demand of users;
and development in CNPq of a clearing house for expediting rapid importation
requests for chemicals and consumables. Emphasis in 1985 will be on the
clearing house and the central node supply center. To stimulate national
production of research chemicals and consumables, and eventually their export,
the subprogram will include: development and regular updating of catalogs for
nationally produced items; studies to ascertain Brazilian needs and potential
production capacity; contracted batch production of items identified in the
studies (occasionally these will be produced by university students as part of
their training); and establishment of quality control laboratories to assure
users of domestic (and foreign) research reagents and consumables that their
technical characteristics (e.g., purity and concentration) fully meet tequired
specifications. Work on the first edition of the catalogs and on the studies
will be undertaken in 1985. FINEP will assume lead responsibility for
implementation, in conjunction with CNPq which will be the site of the clearing
house.
(d) The Subprogram for Maintenance and Repair of Scientific Equipment-
US$19.3 million.
3.24       Lack of repair and maintenance for equipment has become a major
impediment to scientific and technological productivity in Brazil. Within
enterprises, equipment maintenance and repair needs tend to be reasonably
straightforward, and are usually well attended to, if often at very high cost,



- 17 -
by manufacturers of the equipment under service agreements, by specialized
firms, or by in-house technicians. Within the universities, research institutes
and hospitals, however, the situation is different. The rapid expansion of
higher education, the increased importance given to research, and the
microelectronic revolution in instrumentation occasioned huge growth in the size
and sophistication of the Brazilian equipment park. It has ceased to be
technically or economically feasible to rely upon original manufacturers from
abroad for maintenance and repair. Effective local mechanisms must still be
developed. This is the goal of the Subprogram for Equipment Maintenance and
Repair, which will function in especially close collaboration with the
Subprograms for Instrumentation and for Reagents and Research Consumables.
3.25      The subprogram will thus involve phased establishment of a network of
equipment maintenance and repair centers within existing institutions of higher
education and research, each with the necessary specialized personnel,
diagnostic facilities, and stock of parts to deal with given classes of
instruments. Envisioned are (i) approximately 100 small units with capacity for
preventative maintenance and minor repairs (such as fuse replacements and
exchange of other simple components not requiring specialized technicians);
'ii) about 25 centers with capacity to do such things as identify and replace a
defective circuit board, conduct optical alignments, and clean sensitive
components; and (iii) perhaps 15 centers fully equipped and staffed to diagnose
and resolve complex problems such as circuit board repair, fabrication of glass
parts, and calibration of sensitive measuring devices. Equipment and staff
training appropriate for the mandate of each center will be provided. Each
center would undertake training of technicians from research and teaching
laboratories in proper maintenance ard rudimentary repair procedures. The
subprogram would also involve production of manuals for maintenance and repair
in Portuguese for some of the most widely available instruments; technical
assistance to universities and research institutes on repair and maintenance
considerations in equipment procurement; development of catalogs of available
parts; and promotion of local production of replacement parts for widely used
items. Activity in 1985 will involve further diagnostic surveys of maintenance
needs and current capabilities, design of the master inventory control system
whereby all centers will know what parts are available throughout the system,
and funding for pilot centers of each class whose numbers and location will
emerge from the surveys. FINEP will assume lead responsibility for most aspects
of these services.

F. The Bank Project
3.27      A PADCT investment program, totalling US$350 million for commitment
during the five year period 1984-88, was proposed by the government and
appraised by the Bank. With the addition of about US$9 million in incremental
administrative costs, the content of such a program was found to constitute a
suitable basis for a Bank loan of about US$120 million. However, rather than
subject a loan of that magnitude to formal tranching arrangements proposed by
the Bank, the government requested and the Bank approved a reduction in the loan
to the proposed amount of the first unrestricted tranche (which had been set at
60% of expected overall commitments for investment expenditures) to be coupled
with a clear statement of willingness by the Bank, contingent upon smooth
functioning of PADCT during 1985 and 1986, to consider a supplementary loan
request should the government decide to prepare one. 4/ Assuming that at least
US$48 million of additional foreign exchange, complemented by counterpart
financing equivalent to approximately US$96 million, will be provided to PADCT
for commitments to be made in 1987 and 1988, the proposed content of PADCT for
1984-86 remains a reasonable basis for a Bank loan. Under a more stringent
assumption, the proposed scope of PADCT even in 1984-86 would be questionable.
In this context, the Project recommended for Bank support would comprise all
PADCT subprojects and concommitant incremental administrative expenses approved
during 1984-86 up to a total of US$215.4 million provided however that two
groups of PADCT subprojects would not be eligible for disbursements from the
Bank loan: (i) those subprojects financed by FINEP through its onlending
facility to profit making enterprises, and (ii) those subprojects with military
purposes or for which a military institution is the executing entity. In total,
these exclusions of PADCT activities from eligibility for financing under the
Bank loan are expected to amount to less than 15% of overall PADCT investments.
At negotiations, assurances were obtained from the government that these two
groups of PADCT subproject would not be eligible for Bank finance.

arrangements--for planning, decision making, implementation, and
evaluation-involve fundamer.tally improved interaction among the four key
science and technology funding agencies of the government, and between them and
the various communities of practicing scientists and technologists.

A. General Management
4.03      Ultimate responsibility for PADCT rests with Brazil's CCT.  The intent
is to use the CCT's involvement in PADCT to enhance its ability to function as
the central organ for science policy and program coordination. The principal
requirement in this regard is to evolve practical means for achieving w,ore
effective (i) collaboration among the four principal government funding agencies
for science and technology and (ii) participation of practicing scientists in
all phases of program management. To achieve these objectives, the CCT will
work through a smaller subcommission, an executive program management unit, and
a program review group. These provide the heretofore missing link between the
CCT as the highest level science and technology policy organ, and the four major
operating agencies for support of scientific and technological research and
human resources development.
The Comissao Transitoria do PADCT
4.04      The CCT established a Temporary Commission (Comissao Transitoria -
CT), with delegated responsibility fcr PADCT program development, supervision,
and evaluation within the guidelines and periodic review by the CCT. The CT
consists of the chairmen of the relevant CCT Subcommissions for the Basic Plan,
the heads of the government's four intermediary funding agencies, and four of
the presidential appointees to the CCT drawn from and representing the interests
of the relevant communities of practicing scientists and technologists. It has
responsibility for approving the objectives and content of each subprogram;
recommending allocation of resources among subprograms, translating general
policies and principles into operational criteria, procedures, and mechanisms
for each subprogram; and monitoring and evaluating subprogram activities. In
the course of preparing PADCT, the CT has met several times formally and
informally, and would be expected to meet at least twice annually during the
life of PADCT to consider progress reports and act on recommendations submitted
to it.
The Secretaria Executiva do PADCT
4.05      Management responsibility for PADCT is vested in an Executive
Secretariat (Secretaria Executiva do PADCT - SE). Located physically within
CNPq in Brasilia, the SE consists of an Executive Secretary and Deputy appointed



- 22 -
by the President of the CCT upon recommendation of the CT, one senior official
of each of the four intermediary agencies designated by their executive heads,
and seven full time administrative personnel reassigned from the four
intermediaries. In conducting its activities, the SE will draw heavily upon the
specialized staff units of the four funding intermediaries. The SE will ensure
operational coordination and performance.
4.06      Specific functions of the SE, detailed in full in the Documento Basico
and Manual Operativo, include: control of the flow of SEPLAN resources to the
intermediary agencies; logistical support for the meetings of the CT, GTs and
review group; elaboration of PADCT operational guidelines with the assistance of
and for approval by the GTs; preparation in draft and final review of all PADCT
documentation including subprogram announcements and application forms;
consolidation of PADCT program records from four intermediary agencies
(consolidated financial records will be kept by a specialized unit within FINEP
reporting to the SE); developoent of the annual operational plan in conjunction
with the intermediary agencies; and preparation of twice yearly PADCT monitoring
and evaluation reports on the basis of and following review of reports submitted
by the intermediary agencies. The SE will also be the point of routine Bank
contact with PADCT.
The Grupo Especial de Acompanhamento
4.07      Given PADCT's scope and its potential ramifications for broader
aspects of science and technology development in Brazil, a mechanism is needed
to obtain independent assessment of PADCT progress and disinterested advice on
how PADCT may be perfected. For these purposes, a 15 member international
Review and Advisory Group (Grupo Especial de Acompanhamento - GEA) has been
established to examine and formulate recommendations on the significance and
quality of scientific and technological activities in PADCT programs, the
program and project management activities of the PADCT funding agencies and
coordinating structures, and the experience of project executing agencies.
Consisting of a Brazilian chairman and distinguished Brazilian and foreign
scientists and science managers appointed initially for three years, the GEA has
been constituted as an advisory body to the CT. The GEA would meet in Brazil
twice yearly for approximately two weeks and render written reports to the CT
and the Bank. Constitution of the GEA and the holding of its preparatory
meeting occurred on schedule in September 1984. At negotiations assurances
were obtained from the government that the GEA would be maintained and would
function under terms of reference agreeable to the Bank. The government
informed the Bank of GEA's membership and terms of reference as approved by the
CT and satisfactory to the Bank prior to Board presentation.
B. Program Management - GruDos Tecnicos de Assessoramento
4.08      In order to function effectively as a deliberative body for subprogram
management, the CT required a mechanism for 'staffing" the issues it would have
to consider in a way which would blend the best available technical expertise
and judgment from pertinent communities of practicing scientists and
technologists outside of government with the accumulated administrative
experience of the four agency bureaucracies.



- 23 -
4.09      The detailed planning, supervision, and evaluation of the PADCT
subprograms were thus delegated to 10 Technical Advisory Groups (Grupos Tecnicos
de Assessoramento - GTs) created especially for this purpose by the CT and
acting in an advisory capacity to it. Each GT consists of one member of the
technical staff of each of the four funding intermediaries, plus a minimum of
eight practicing scientists, technologists, and businessmen from outside the
science funding bureaucracy. In the case of the GTs for the four National
Services Subprograms, four or five of these eight nongovernme-nta1 members are
typically drawn from the GTs of the six Disciplinary Subprograms, thus assuring
coordination among them. GT chairmen are selected from among the nonagency
members by the CT, usually after informal consultation among the members. GT
members are appointed by the CT for once renewable, two year terms following
wide canvassing of professional, technical, and business organizations for
nominations.  Nonagency members are remunerated for their services.  in the
course of designing PADCT, the GTs have each met several times, and will
continue to meet at least three times annually.
4.10      Since their establishment in 1983, the GTs have been directly
responsible for elaboration of the Subprogram Proposals in each area, including
specification of strategies, objectives, general content of subprojects, and
subproject processing criteria, procedures, and mechanisms specific to the
subprogram in question. The GTs will periodically assess all activities wichin
their respective subprograms, propose those adjustments to content and process
as experience may suggest, review and approve annual operations plans, advise on
resolution of disagreements in their respective subprograms among the
intermediary agencies, and maintain close contact with the communities of
scientists, technologists, and businessmen who are the subprograms'
constituents.
C. Project Management
4.11      Management of subprojects entails the familiar processes of
preparation, appraisal, implementation, supervision, and evaluation for the
multitude of individual subprojects within the PADCT subprograms. These tasks
are shared between the many PADCT executing agencies and the government's four
funding intermediaries in ways which assure that technically correct decisions
will be made under common policies and standards, and that implementation of a
large array of decentralized subprojects is efficient.
Subproject Executing Agencies
4.12      PADCT subprogram announcements have been disseminated widely within
all Brazilian higher education establishments and research institutions, and
otherwise made known within the industrial agricultural and service sectors.
Any institution which meets the eligibility criteria established for each
subprogram is free to prepare and submit subproject epplications to the
intermediaries in accordance with the guidelines and calendar provided in the
announcements and application forms. Subproject implementation is the
responsibility of the sponsoring institution, although assistance in such
matters as procurement from foreign sources may be obtained from the funding
intermediary.

Funding Intermediaries
(a) Allocation of Subproject Responsibility to Funding Intermediaries.
4.13      The allocation of responsibility for subproject administration awong
the funding intermediaries and the schedule for consideration of subproject
proposals, are set forth in the Documento Basico, the Manual Operativo, and the
general PADCT and individual subprogram announcements. CAPES is the lead dgency
for human resource development subprojects, except that STI and CNPq will
directly administer subprojects involving nondegree, short course training.
FINEP will administer research and development subprojects and service
subprojects of the Reagents and Research Consumables, and the Maintenance and
Repair Subprograms. The remainder of infrastructural service subprojects for
science will be handled by CNPq and those for technology by STI. CAPES will
continue to handle all subprojects under the Science Education Subprogram.
Initially, subproject applications will be considered twice yearly for most of
the subprograms.
4.14      Thus CNPq, FINEP, CAPES, and STI will receive subproject applications
which the sponsors ascertain fall within the respective jurisdiction of each
agency, as described in the overall PADCT and subprogram announcements. The
agency will forward a summary of all subproject applications to the SE. Any
subproject proposals which staff suggest may better be handled by another
intermediary agency will be forwarded to the SE who, after consultation with the
GT as is deemed necessary, will make the final agency assignment. Subprojects
are appraised by a single funding intermediary and, if approved, funded entirely
by that intermediary. The four intermediary agencies each have between 12 and
33 years of experience in funding development projects in science and
technology.
(b) Subproject Appraisal and Final Approval.
4.15      Subproject appraisal is the responsibility of the intermediary
agencies and final approval is a legal prerogative of each agency head. To
assure that PADCT subproject funding decisions are made on the basis of
objective and expert assessment of the technical merits within the subprogram
guidelines as established by the GTs (paras. 4.08-4.10), the four funding
intermediaries have improved the system of Appraisal Committees (Comites
Assessores - CAs) used in the past, and increased the rigor of their appraisal
procedures.
4.16      CAs are now comprised of up to six voting members, four regular
observers, and informal observers. Five voting members are drawn from a pool of
20-30 distinguished practicing scientists from pertinent fields; for each
subprogram this pool is constituted by the funding intermediaries for a two year
period with half the membership up for renewal in any given year. One voting
member may be a staff member of the intermediary. One or more observers from
the funding intermediary, one each from the other funding intermediaries and
from the GT may attend. The CA Chairman is elected from among the nonagency
members, three of whom must be present in order to constitute a quorum.
Nonagency CA members are remunerated for their services.

4.17      Except as noted below for major elements in the National Services
Subprograms, each subproject application will be fully appraised, in accordance
with subprogram guidelines, by at least two outside experts contracted
specifically for the purpose from among those individuals with expertise of
particular relevance for the subproject but not competing with the subproject
sponsor for funding. When appropriate, one of these experts may be a member of
the CA. For each proposal to be appraised. a CA member will be designated by
the Chairman as the rapporteur for that proposal, charged with leading CA
discussion of the proposal on the basis of a summary of the outside expert
judgments. All subproject appraisals will be written, utilizing forms supplied
by the intermediary. The merit of the proposal would be judged in terms of
compliance with subprogram guidelines, quality of ktey personnel, technical
feasibility, and adequacy of resources requested. The appraisal experts would
also judge whether requests for procurement of equipment and other items by
noncompetitive procedures is justified on technical grounds. The written
appraisals for all subprojects will be reviewed by all members of the CA, and
the CA will then rank all projects in order of merit. Anonymous summaries of
appraisal reports will be sent to sponsors of all subproject proposals, whether
successful or not.
4.18      Subprojects will be formally approved by the head of the funding
intermediary, according to the order of merit, up to the limit of funds
available for commitment in any given competition. If an agency head determines
that a subproject recommended for funding by the CA should not receive final
approval, he or she will communicate the reasons for such a determination in
writing to the CA. At negotiations, assurances were obtained from the
government that the agency head would not finally approve subprojects
inconsistent with the order of merit established by the CA. Except as noted
below for National Services Subprograms, subprojects approved by the
intermediary agency which have not been recommended for approval by the CA
within the normal appraisal process are not eligible for financing within PADCT.
Cc) Subproject Supervision.
4.19      In line with long established standard operating procedures of the
four intermediary agencies, a contract is signed between the funding
intermediary and the executing agency for each approved proposal, specifying
activities to be undertaken, expected results, costs organized according to
standard budget categories and expressed in constant value terms (ORTNs 5/ or
dollars), financing plan, disbursement schedule and procedures, reporting and
auditing requirements. Staff of the intermediary agency oversee the physical
and financial implementation of each subproject, through review of periodic
reports from the executing agency, through site visits, and by providing

(d) Subproject Evaluation.

(e) Special Provisions for National Services Subprograms.
4.21      Greater coordination (in chronology and content) is required in
implementation of subprojects within the National Services Subprograms in order
that the proper services are provided in a timely and efficient manner.
Further, as stipulated in the Subprogram Proposals, some National Service
subprojects will be executed directly by the responsible intermediary agency,
while others would be subcontracted on a competitive basis to outside executing
agencies following submission of proposals to peer group appraisal. The role of
the CAs will thus necessarily be somewhat different.
4.22      Wher activities within the National Services Subprograms are to be
executed directly by an intermediary agency itself or an administrative
dependency of it, in accordance with the guidelines provided by the GT in the
Subprogram Proposal (e.g. provision of equipment for the primary metrological
laboratories undertaken by INMETRO/STI, or of data base services by IBICT/CNPq),
the proposals describing these activities will be submitted to a CA func:toning
as an advisory committee for outside review of technical merit and feasibility.
Implementation will similarly be periodically reviewed and evaluated by that
group. The findings and recommendations of the CA will be transmitted in
writing to the agency head. For activities of the National Service Subprograms
to be awarded to outside executing agencies, the responsible intermediary agency
will normally prepare and widely publicize a request for proposals and submit
the resulting project applications to competitive CA appraisal of their
technical merit conducted along the lines of the peer review process within the
Disciplinary Subprograms. The CA would retain its responsibility for periodic
review and evaluation of subprojects eventually funded.
4.23      In both cases, however, the agency head will be held responsible for
concrete results and therefore would enjoy considerably more latitude for
altering the recommendation of the CA based on judgment of the overall needs of
the subprogram for coordinated, economic, and timely delivery of services.
Whenever differences emerge between the CA's findings and recommendations and
the course of action deemed appropriate by the responsible agency head, the
agency head will prepare and circulate to the CA, the GT, and the Executive
Secretary a memorandum justifying the actions on the basis of those
considerations.

4.24      In view of the requirements for coordinated implementation, lead
responsibility for all aspects of each National Services Subprogram has been
assigned to a single intermediary agency, rather than being shared among
intermediaries according to the comparative advantage of each in administering
given kinds of subprojects, as is the case for the Disciplinary Subprograms.
The responsible intermediary agency will designate a Subprogram Manager,
reporting directly to the head of agency, with day-to-day executive
responsibility for implementation within the guidelines in the Subprogram
Proposal. These National Subprogram Managers may be members of the
corresponding GT and/or may represent the agency as a voting member of the CA
and will in any event utilize its advice as discussed earlier. Working under
the legal authority of their agency heads and, as necessary, the Executive
Secretary, the National Subprogram Managers will ensure that the several
subprojects comprising the subprogram are implemented in the proper sequence on
the basis of careful monitoring and evaluation of ongoing experience so that the
needed services are supplied with efficiency and timeliness, and have the
cooperation of other agencies have the required. The performance of these
National Subprogram Managers will be regularly evaluated by the pertinent GT,
with a view to assuring expeditious implementation of the needed services.
D. Specially Assigned Implementation Responsibilities
4.25      Within the management structure sketched above, global responsibility
for key aspects of implementation has been assigned to FINEP and CNPq.
FINEP-Financial Agent
4 .26     As a natural outgrowth of its role as administrator of FNDCT, FINEP
will act as the overall financial agent for PADCT. In this role FINEP is
responsible to the PADCT Execttive Secretariat, operating under policy guidance
of the Comissao Transitoria.  This role is in addition to FINEP's participation
as one of the four intermediary funding agencies for PADCT subprojects. In its
capacity as overall financial agent operating under instructions from the
Executive Secretariat, FINEP will manage the flow of both local counterpart and
loan funds, as explained in Chapter V. FINEP was chosen for this task of
overall financial administration because (i) it has long and successful
institutional experience in dealing both with the other-intermediaries and with
international lenders; (ii) it has organizational units already carrying out
these functions and requiring only modest growth in staff to handle the PADCT
burden; and (iii) it has recently been authorized to act as a financial agent of
the Republic of Brazil under Decree Law 2115 of April 25, 1984; this greatly
simplifies and strengthens its capacity to handle funds from both internal and
external sources.
CNPq-Inport Agent

Subproject Supervision and Evaluation
4.32      The essence of a sector loan is delegation to intermediaries of the
borrower (functioning under policies, criteria, procedures, and mechanisms
approved by the Bank) of the prima'y responsibility for subproject management,
including supervision and evaluation. Bank supervision focuses on the
performance of the intermediaries, not the individual subproject executing
agencies.
4.33      However, reasonable measures have been taken and are contemplated to
ensure that the subproject appraisal process is in fact rigorous and results in
selection of technically and economically sound investments. As noted in the
next section, the Bank appraisal team closely observed the process during the
first (test) round of subproject proposals and verified that subprogram
guidelines were enforced and that subprojects recommended were sound.  As an
added safeguard, a major Bank supervision mission would observe the second round
of subproject appraisals in the same fashion during May 1985, in order to verify
that lessons learned during the test are applied. Through this participation in
initial appraisal rounds, the Bank will effectively review at least a third of
early PADCT subprojects, as well as subproject proposals which are not
recommended. Finally, to ensure ongoing special scrutiny of large subprojects,
the Bank will review individual subprojects following appraisal and prior to
final approval by the pertinent agency head when their estimated annual costs
(total estimated costs divided by the number of years for implementation) exceed
US$1.0 million.  During the 1986 Midterm Review, this limit will be reviewed on
the basis of experience to date, so that for the remainder of PADCT the Bank
will exercise prior review of approximately 5X of approved subprojects, measured
in terms of their total estimated costs. At negotiations, assurances were
obtained from the government that subprojects recommended for approval with
estimated annual costs in excess of US$1.0 million (net of beneficiary
counterpart) would be submitted for review and approval by the Bank prior to
final approval by the intermediary agency.
4.34      Ic addition, supervision missions will regularly review a sample of
approved subprojects for compliance with PADCT standards. Emphasis would be
placed on 'problem subprojects'--i.e., those in which CA advice has not been
fully accepted, implementation delays are developing, or results are judged by
agencies and/or CAs to be of lower quality than expected. As noted in the
following chapter, certain procurement decisions will also be subject to prior
Bank approval.
4.35      Special efforts are also needed to avoid having the Bank finance
through this loan research activities already included under other loans.
Subprojects for applied research or development on agricultural or energy
related topics (although not for basic research in these areas, or for human
resource or service subprojects related to these areas) would thus be subject to
a further round of Brazilian review after action by the CA and prior to final
approval by

Uses of Funds
(a) Basis for Calculations.
5.07      (i) Overall investment plan.  As must be the case given the nature of
PADCT, the government's investment plan consists of resource allocations among
PADCT subprograms, and estimates of how those resources will be spent. The
investment plan is not the result of an aggregation of the costed physical
content of a predetermined number of subprojects of given content. The CCT has
determined the overall size of PADCT and the allocation among subprograms on the
basis of prudent assessment of availability of funds and judgment as to which
portions of the sector exhibit the most urgent unattended needs. The appraisal
mission carefully reviewed these allocations for PADCT Basic Plan and found them
reasonable as a point of departure. As set out in Table 5.2, they are the basis
of Schedule 1 of the Loan Agreement.
5.08      (ii)  Expenditures.  Subprojects will be approved within those
subprogram limits. Actual aggregate PADCT costs are determinable only after the
fact as subprojects are completed. The expenditure figures that follow cannot
be more than rough approximations. They are based on examination of recent
experience of the funding intermediaries with similar subprojects, on judgment
of how PADCT's peculiar characteristics might modify that experience, and on
joint assessment of what are reasonable costs for the few activities within
PADCT for which there is no experiential base. The expenditure figures are
nevertheless useful as a guide to order of magnitude expectations and as an
a priori monitoring yardstick.
5.09      (iii)  Foreign exchange.  The estimation of the foreign exchange
content of PADCT is subject to uncertainty. Experience of the recent past is
not a reliable guide. The access of the science and technology sector to
imports of goods and services has been sharply constrained in recent years
through reduction of the CACEX quotas and SEI's zeal in protecting the Brazilian
microelectronics industry. The removal of these restrictions, by supplying
foreign exchange through the loan itself and by requiring change in the practice
of SEI (para. 4.28), are central purposes of the Project.
5.10      (iv)  Contingencies.  Finally, there are no contingencies, price or
physical, incorporated in the tables. Subproject budgets will incorporate both
as appropriate, and the totals of those budgets will be committed against the
subprogram allocations.

B. Financial Management Procedures
Flow of Funds and the Annual Operational Plan
5.16      Operating under instructions of the Executive Secretary, FINEP has
been designated as the overall financial agent for PADCT (para. 4.26). The
US$49.6 million allocated for PADCT from the regular budgets of the intermediary
agencies will flow directly to them in routine fashion. But PINEP would serve
as the conduit to the intermediaries of the incremental appropriation by SEPLAN
for PADCT and that portion of the Bank loan disbursed in local currency. FINEP
would receive the additional counterpart from SEPLAN in the form of periodic
earmarked additions to FNDCT. Procedures for transfer of funds from the
government to FNDCT are long 4stablished and work well. As discussed below,
FINEP will also manage disbursements to, or on behalf of, the intermediaries of
proceeds of the proposed Bank loan.
5.17      Planning and control of the flow of funds for PADCT would be assured
through the Annual Operations Plan. This would be prepared each year by FINEP
under the overall direction and control of the SE, with inputs from the four
intermediary agencies in their capacity as subproject fumders. Among other
sections, the Annual Operations Plan would contain a consolidation of the
projected cruzeiro requirements for the four intermediaries to meet their
contractual obligations to subproject executing agencies. These obligations
consist of the locaJ. currency requirements of subprojects approved in prior
years, whose scheduling is specified in subproject contracts, plus the initial
disbursements on subprojects which will be approved during the year. In
considering the Annual Operations Plan, the CT and CCT will establish the annual
commitment authority for new subproject approvals so that the combined



- 38 -
requirements for cruzeiro resources from the intermediaries for new and old
subprojects does not exceed the yearly PADCT allocations set out in Annex 8.
FINEP in turn bases its programming of requests for delivery of the SEPLAN
counterpart to FNDCT and its scheduling of disbursements to the other
intermediaries on this consolidated estimate of cruzeiro requirements. Under
PADCT, SEPLAN's incremental financial resources, as well as all proceeds from
the Bank loan, are allocated to subprograms and actually delivered to the
intermediary agencies only on the basis of commitments against subprojects
already approved according to the subprogram guidelines. Agencies will not
receive funds ex ante to finance whatever projects they please. In this way,
PADCT succeeds for practical purposes in unifying responsibility for
programming/oversight and funding (para. 2.17).

VI. BENEFITS AND RISKS
6.01      PADCT offers great potential benefits for development in Brazil, but
also entails significant, although acceptable, risks.
A. Benefits
6.02      PADCT is expected to enhance the productivity--i.e., the relevance,
effectiveness, and efficiency-of the science and technology sector in Brazil,
and thereby fulfill a necessary condition for improving Brazil's long term



- 44 -
international economic position and for attending to the welfare of its
population. Enhanced productivity of the sector will be reflected in more
robust long term growth in agricultural output, manufactured exports, and
domestic sources of energy achieved in conjunction with development of small and
medium size Brazilian owned firms and more efficient management of public sector
resources-all of which were identified earlier as central elements in Brazil's
revised growth strategy (paras. 2.05-2.07). Important features of PADCT-such
as the improved balance of Brazil's science and technology by field and
geographic region, the enhanced influence of the expert communities outside
government in program and project planning and implementation, and the gradual
realigning of institutional responsibilities--are central means needed to
enhance all three components of productivity.
Relevance
6.03      The PADCT management improvements coupled with its carefully targeted
investment program should assure, respectively, that (i) major obstacles to
better utilization of science and technology in fostering rapid economic growth
are correctly identified and (ii) increased resources are directed to them on a
priority basis. Brazilian science and technology will become more relevant to
those development problems whose solutions are predicated at least in part on
technological change.
Effectiveness
6.04      PADCT should also improve performance of the sector in generating
internally or assimilating successfully from abroad appropriate solutions
to growth constraints whose roots are scientific or technological. The
effectiveness of the sector in developing scientific and technological responses
suited to Brazil's resource endowments should be enhanced.
Efficiency
6.05      PADCT should result in increased output of science and technology per
unit of invested resources. Waste associated with inadequate utilization of
installed capacity for advanced training, for research, and for technological
infrastructure, and with insufficient and oscillating levels of support, should
decline. Gains from consciously targeting highly synergetic activities should
increase. The nation's more general capacity to produce and absorb basic
knowledge in the sciences should also grow. Overall efficiency in the use of
public resources in the sector should increase.
Systems Development fcr Sustained Productivity Gains
6.06      These productivity gains are potentially important.  However, the most
significant long run benefit of PADCT probably lies in the institutional
development it will engender; productivity gains for the sector will become a
permanent feature of the environment. PADCT's organizational arrangements and



- 45 -
management mechanisms offer real promise that the resilience of Brazilian
science and technology in a rapidly changing world will be great enough to bring
about continued growth in the relevance, effectiveness and efficiency of the
sector, and therefore in its ability to attract and absorb additional
resources. This is a prerequisite for survival in the next century.
B. Risks
6.07      Given the scope of PADCT, the depth of policy and institutional change
it pursues, and the difficult economic environment in Brazil, there is risk
associated with PADCT. Two characteristics of the situation engender
particularly important uncertainty. Their impact would be carefully examined in
1986 during the Midterm Review, which provides an opportunity to encourage
corrective action if necessary.
Remaining Gaps in the Policy Framework
6.08      Two elements of the policy framework essential for maximization of the
long run impact of PADCT are lacking and may not be fully addressed through
PADCT.
6.09      The incentive structure for Brazilian enterprise to generate and adopt
new technology could be improved. One indicator of the need for reshaping
policies in this area is the small proportion of the nation's overall effort in
science and technology funded by or conducted in enterprises. In the absence of
improvements in incentives which enhance demand for science and technology,
there is a limit to the possible impact of better supply which PADCT should
produce. The important questions here are unavoidably broad and outside the
scope of a science and technology program, in that they involve such things as
industrialization, trade, and tax policy. The Basic Industrial Technology and
Science Policy Planning and Management Subprograms of PADCT nevertheless contain
funds for policy oriented studies on these and related issues, which are also
the subject of increasing attention in various parts of the government and the
Bank.
6.10      Within the supply system for science and technology, an important
element in the incentive structure also remains to be addressed. The improved
facilities for work (including in some cases salary supplements for research
activities) provided through PADCT will meaningfully increase the attractiveness
of careers in research and graduate teaching. But the movement of Brazil's most
highly skilled out of the universities and research institutes, which has
accelerated in recent years, cannot ultimately be reversed without overall
attention to basic levels of remuneration and promotion standards for the entire
Brazilian system of higher education. The situation is particularly serious
with respect to technicians employed in universities and research institutes.
The government will soon complete a thorough sector analysis of these and other
problems, in the context of an overall retrospective evaluation of the
university reform of 1966. This should provide the basis for appropriate
action.

Change in Administration
6.11      The change in Presidential administration in Brazil in March 1985
will ensure that the majority of senior managers within the many agencies of the
federal government who have been instrumental in designing and launching PADCT
will not be central figures beyond the first stages of its execution. Some
staff changes in key units of the funding intermediaries are also probable.
Delays in implementation are certainly possible, if a new team of civil servants
has to familiarize itself with PADCT's objectives, content, structure, and
resource requirements, and arrive at its own judgments as to the astuteness of
each. However, there are excellent reasons to be confident that the possible
disruption to PADCT will not be great. PADCT was designed with exceedingly
broad and iterative participation from all segments of the interested
communities. While conceived by a given administration, the program has a much
broader constituency. Implementation is highly decentralized, depending more on
the comparatively stable executing agencies and on the dedication of scientific
peers to their responsibilities in program and subproject management than on any
narrow and vulnerable group within the federal bureaucracy. As demonstrated by
the response to the first general request for subproject proposals (paras.
4.37 ff), PADCT is enthusiastically welcomed by a segment of the population
which inevitably will make itself heard in a more democratic political
environment. Finally, PADCT is not a fragile mosaic of activities wherein a
temporary setback at one point would necessarily halt progress everywhere.
PADCT was designed as a dynamic system, with built-in mechanisms for adjustment
to changing circumstances, a characteristic which would provide resilience in
the unlikely event of a prejudicial stance by a new administration.

INTERNATIONAL ASSISTANCE FOR SCIENCE AND TECHNOLOGY IN BRAZIL
1.        Sciqnce and technology in Brazil have been the object of considerable
international effort since the 1960s. Bilateral public and private technical
assistance organizations and the agencies of the United Nations pioneered in the
building of the human resource foundations of modern science and technology.
The Inter-American Development Bank and the World Bank have also made important
contributions.
A.   Inter-American Development Bank (IDB)
University Development
2.        Since 1967 IDB has made loans totalling US$170 million in support of
three projects with total costs of US$431 million designed to assist in the
implementation of the Brazilian higher education reform of 1966. 1/
3.        Prior to the reform, Brazilian universities were loose conglomerates
of separate profession or career oriented faculties or schools, often widely
dispersed physically within a city, in each of which were offered the necessary
disciplines by a few, usually part time, professors. The 1966 reform sought to
alter this structure. It mandated the reorganization of public universities
around discipline based academic departments, staffed by full time professors
offering courses in their disciplines, for students preparing for many different
careers. Implementation of this reform, in the context of exploding demand for
higher education, presupposed an immense construction and equipment program to
consolidate disparate facilities into central campuses congenial to the
departmental structure and to accommodate increased undergraduate enrollments.
4.       The first two IDB projects were heavily focused on bringing the
physical plant of 14 federal universities, 1 state university, and 1 private
university into compliance with the requirements of the reform, including
especially those for undergraduate science teaching. The third IDB project, now
in implementation, continues the emphasis on physical facilities in nine
additional federal universities. But it also includes significant funds for
quality improvement, particularly for staff development for both academic and
administrative personnel. The third project also seeks to increase the capacity
of universities to relate to their particular surroundings, through encouraging
curricular reform, applied research on local problems, and extension activities
in health and education. The universities were selected with a view to
strengthening the weakest units of the federal system and reducing regional
quality disparities in higher education.

BRAZILIAN SCIENCE AND TECHNOLOGY AS A DEVELOPMENT SECTOR
1.        The science and technology sector in Brazil consists of a substantial
mass of programs and highly qualified people engaged in scientific and
technological pursuits, a structured system of public and private organizations,
and a periodic, multistage planning process to guide both individual and
institutional participants. 1/ Despite weaknesses it now must confront, past
investments in research had noteworthy repercussions for economic growth.
A. Summary Indicators of the Scope of Brazil's Science and Technology Sector
2.        Although Brazil compares poorly on most objective measures with other
rapidly industrIalizing, export oriented, middle income countries, scientific
and technological activity in Brazil is considerable. The sector's share in GDP
rose from less than 0.25Z to about 0.50% during the 1970s. By 1982, federal
expenditures in the sector were estimated to approach US$1.85 billion,
equivalent to roughly 0.60% of GDP. By comparison, OECD countries allocate
about 2% of GDP to the sector, and Korea about 1Z. In Brazil; about 17% of the
sector's expenditure was for purchase of technology from abroad, 11% was used by
public and private productive enterprises, 12% was used by institutions of
higher education, and the remaining 60% was spent by the government directly
(including for research related to national security) or indirectly through
support of research activities in universities and research institutes. The
government's share of science and technology expenditures is notably higher, and
the private sector's share much lower, than in Brazil's major competitors.
3.        In 1981, the nation's 875 institutions of higher education (of which
65 are true universities) enrolled about 1.4 million students In first degree
programs, more than 35,000 in 736 master's degree programs, and nearly 4,800 in
285 doctoral programs. Thousands more were enrolled in nondegree, post graduate
specialization courses. But of those students pursuing graduate degrees, only
20% were enrolled in any of the natural or biological sciences and a further 15%
in engineering disciplines. Brazilian institutions awarded 860 master's degrees
and 508 doctorates in that year. More than 32,500 people were engaged in
research activities at some 433 different institutions, including 126
governmental agencies with 7% of the active research population, 112 productive
enterprises (22% of the research labor force), 91 educational establishments
(51% of researchers) and 74 institutes dedicated exclusively to research (15%).
But, of this overall labor force in research, less than half (14,800) had
completed some graduate degree; about one sixth (5,250) had been trained at the
doctoral level. Fewer than one third were engaged in all of the exact sciences
and engineering combined, roughly half the proportion in Japan or the United
States.
I/   For a more complete overview of the Brazilian science and technology
sector, see the Bank report entitled "Brazil: Issues in Science and
Technology Development" (Report No. 4819(a)-BR, January 27, 1984).

B.   The National System for Scientific and Technological Development
4.        Established under Decree No. 70,553 of May 17, 1972, and Decree
No. 5,225 of January 15, 1975, SNDCT encompasses all organizations in Brazil
which use public resources for purposes of planning, supervision, coordination,
promotion, execution, and evaluation of scientific and technological research,
or for transfer of research results to the economic and social sectors. At the
federal level alone, some 100 organizations are involved. This legal framework
places overall responsibility for sector coordination in the SEPLAN. Each major
ministry, through a secretariat for science and technology (or equivalent unit),
is charged with immediate coordination of policy and programs of the
organizations under its jurisdiction. Excepting the activities of the Brazilian
military and parastatal enterprises, four governmental agencies share the major
responsibility for federal support for science and technology in Brazil. The
place of these agencies within the overall structure of SNDCT is shown
schematically in the attached diagram (Annex 2, page 8).

5.        The central organ of SNDCT is CNPq.  Located in Brasilia, and given
its current legal status as a public foundation in 1974, CNPq is the SEPLAN
agency with responsibility for assisting the Minister of Planning to formulate
and coordinate national policy for scientific and technological development.
CNPq carries out this function primarily through CCT, for which the
headquarter's personnel of CNPq (numbering some 900 employees) in effect serve
as staff secretariat. The highest science and technology policy body of the
government, the CCT has a mandate to formulate sector policies, establish
sectoral objectives and priorities, monitor and evaluate sector activities, and
above all to assure adequate coordination of the nation's efforts in science and
technology. The CCT consists of 17 statutory members by virtue of their
positions as heads of governmental or quasi-governmental sectoral organs for
science and technology plus 15 distinguished individuals from the scientific,
technological, and business communities appointed by the President of the
Republic.
6.        Among the first group of CCT members are (i) the the heads of two
agencies of SEPLAN: the President of CNPq, who also is President of CCT, and the
President of FINEP; (ii) the Secretary for Industrial Technology of the Ministry
of Industry and Commerce; and (iii) the Secretary for Higher Education of the
Ministry of Education and Culture, who is normally represented by the Director
General of CAPES (see below). The President of the Brazilian Academy of
Sciences is also a statutory member of CCT.
7.        The CCT has two permanent and three temporary committees to
accomplish its functions of policy formulation, priority setting, monitoring and
evaluation, and coordination. The CCT's Standing Committee on the Plan for
Scientific and Technological Development operates with 10 subcomissions each
dealing with a specific development sector (such as agriculture, industry,
social sectors, energy, information and communications, transportation). The
CCT's newest Temporary Committee was formed in 1983 to oversee preparation and
execution of PADCT.

8.       In addition to its position at the policy formulation and coordination
hub of the SNDCT, CNPq has been the home of basic scientific research in Brazil

Brazilian activity in this area. CNPq administers seven research institutes

Financiadora de Estudos e Projetos
9.        FINEP was established in 1967 as a public corporation under the
supervision of SEPLAN to finance preinvestment studies, transfer of foreign
technology to Brazil, development of new technology in Brazilian enterprises,
and appurtenant technology oriented research. FINEP was to pursue these
objectives through lending at subsidized (although now positive real) interest
rates and other forms of venture capitalism designed to encourage application of
new technologies in the Brazilian manufacturing, services and agro-industrial
sectors. Outrighlt grants of funds were not envisioned as a major FINEP
instrument.
10.       Since establishment of the SNDCT in 1972, however, FINEP has also
served as the executive secretariat of FNDCT, the government's principal source
of grant funding for science and technology. Indeed, for want of other
institutions willing to and/or capable of shouldering the burden, FINEP in its
role as administrator of FNDCT resources, has become by default the prime source
of support not only for large research projects in all areas of science but also
for the research infrastructure in the universities essential to graduate
education. Headquartered in Rio de Janeiro, FINEP employs 585 people, of whom
roughly 185 are engaged in project evaluation and supervision (not including the
personnel of the 27 state development and commercial banks upon which it relies
heavily for project appraisals for its lending activities). A further 257 staff
conduct the financial administrative activities of FINEP. In 1982, FINEP's
total expenditures were about US$194 million, of which roughly half was grant
funding under FNDCT. The value of FNDCT contracts signed decreased from
US$250 million in 1977 to US$72 million in 1983.

the Ministry of Education and Culture responsible for the promotion, planning,
supervision, and evaluation (but only for a small part of the funding) of second
and third degree higher education in Brazil. In this capacity, CAPES is the
official SNDCT coordinating agency for the education sector.

12.       Traditionally, CAPES's principal instrument for support of graduate
education has been fellowships for study abroad for individuals preparing for

C.   National Planning for Science and Technology
14.       The planning process for science and technology at the national level
is dynamic, and entails periodic activities at several levels of specificity.
15.       The decrees establishing the SNDCT stipulate that the harmonious
behavior of the many components of the system shall be acquired through
preparation and periodic updating of the PBDCT, itself a further elaboration of
the section on science and technology in the National Development Plan. The
first and second science and technology development plans (1973-74 and 1975-79,
respectively) listed priority programs, projects and actlvities in detail. The
general thrust was an across the board effort to increase capacity for high
level research as a precondition for application of local expertise to concrete
development problems.
16.       By contrast, the third PBDCT covering the period 1980-85 sets forth
instead overarching policy directives intended to constitute flexible guidelines
for action. But in place of the singleminded effort to create research groups
through personnel training and massive provision of physical facilities which
had characterized earlier efforts, the objectives are now two: the
consolidation of capacity for scientific research and the attainment of greater

select and generate the most adequate technology among the options available and
to promote its effective absorption in productive processes, is to be achieved

17.       Operational specification of the third PBDCT at the level of programs

program adjustments during the plan period. PADCT was designed, essentially, to
implement five of the AP action programs for which adequate financial and
organizational resources had failed to materialize.
D. Economic Consequences of Brazilian Scientific Research
18.       Despite the need to rectify diagnosed weaknesses which now limit the
sector's contribution to development, Brazilian science and technology boasts
numerous examples of the impact of indigenous research on economic growth, even
under the suboptimal sectoral conditions currently prevailing. With
improvements to be introduced through PADCT, it is reasonable to expect that
such success stories will increase in size and frequently.
19.       The correspondance between scientific research and economic returns is
clearest in agriculture and energy. Research conducted by plant physiologists
at the Federal University of Vicosa, in its later stages in collaboration with
EMBRAPA agronomists, led to development of a strain of soybeans sufficiently
resistent to disease and parsimonious in its requirements for fertilizer and
water to be grown economically in the vast Brazilian serrado. As a result,
Brazil is a major world producer of soybeans. Research in connection with the
Alcohol and Biomass Energy Program (Ln. 1989-BR) has pioneered the use of
acid-catalized hydrolysis processes, now being scaled up from the laboratory to
demonstration units, to convert bagasse (and other woody substances) into sugars
for fermentation into alcohol, with potential increases of up to 30% in alcohol
productivity. Research on continuous batch fermentation processes have
increased ethanol yield from sugarcane by 7.5% while simultaneously reducing the
volume of stillage to one tenth the previous volume, thereby facilitating
pollution control as well as making stillage an economic fertilizer through
reduction in its transport costs. Materials scientists at the Federal
University of Parana combined forces with their mechanical engineering

colleagues to develo? farm machinery at lower cost, and having longer wear,
greater energy efficiency, and improved fit with the special needs of
agriculture (including soybean production) in the frontier areas of the
southwest. A collaborative effort of Brazilian scientists from several
universities achieved the structural identification of the active component in
an Amazonian plant which, it had been observed, insects assiduously avoided and
which when pulverized and put into streams killed fish. The further discovery
that this active component is not water soluable, but depends for its effects on
the simultaneous presence of surfactins (soapy substances which made the
compound water soluable) in the plant led to development of one of the world's
most benign insecticides now marketed in many countries.
20.       Examples also abound outside of agriculture.  Scientists at the
Federal University of Campinas identified the peculiar properties of Brazilian
niobium deposits and upon that discovery built economic methods of extraction
and refining of this metal, of which Brazil has 95% of the world's supply.
Already an important component in Brazilian specialty steels, niobium is a
superconductor whose potential role in the development of much lower cost, long
distance transmission of electricity from hydro dams to industrial users and in
producing the magnetic fields necessary for the 'levitated train' is now the
subject of intense basic research. A group of physicists, also at the
University of Campinas, conducted research in fiber optics which led to an
indigenous technology of production of fiber optic cable, now being
commercialized. Collaboration among groups of electrical engineers and
scientists at two Rio universities and the University of Sao Paulo led to
establishment of the Centro de Teenologia da Informatica which produces
microprocessors for machine control based on locally designed (but still foreign
manufactured) chips. In research directly related to health, Brazil has a long
track record, beginning perhaps with the seminal work on vector identification
of trypanosomiasis mansoni (Chagas disease) and including the early protein
chemistry work at the University of Sao Paulo which underlay the emergence of
the Instituto Butanta as a leading manufacturer of antitoxins for poisonous
snake bites. High vacuum technology necessary to produce liquid oxygen and
nitrogen (importart in many industrial processses) was initially developed in
the high energy physics laboratory at the University of Sao Paulo; only one of
many examples of technology developed in the course of pure science research
being adapted to uses never envisioned. More recently, a group of biochemists
at the Federal University of Minas Gerais developed a chemical method for
purification of insulin which provided the foundation for Brazilian insulin
manufacture at prices competitive in world markets.
21.       Despite these and many more similar successes, the continuing economic
productivity of scientific research in Brazil has recently been undermined by
the severe shortage of investment resources during a time of economic crisis as
well as by institutional weaknesses. In this context, a major challenge of the
future is to maintain an appropriate balance between efforts which are narrowly
applied in rature and promise concrete short term payoff and those, including

POLICY FRAMEWORK AND OBJECTIVES
1.        In consultation with the scie atific and technological community
outside government, senior Brazilian public sector managers devised a
comprehensive long term strategy to address the central weaknesses of the
sector. The strategy consists of a hierarchically related set of objectives,
coupled with the means and defined actions to achieve them. As a framework for
an essential infusion of incremental financial resources, the strategy entails a
significant overhaul of the policy regime for science and technology.
2.        These refined guidelines for action stress four fundamental
objectives. Achievement of each objective is a necessary condition for
achievement of the next. While the four objectives are not uniquely responsive
to specific problems, coordinated progress towards the objectives would
significantly alleviate the sector's institutional weaknesses, educational
deficiencies, and content imbalances which today hold back technological change
and thus threaten to impede economic growth. These four objectives, to be
sought through an investment program coupled with management improvements, are
summarized below. Greater detail is available in the Project file (items MO(d)
and I(h)).
A. Rationalization of Responsibilities
3.        The government seeks to rationalize the effective division of
responsibilities among its major science and technology funding agencies,
particularly CNPq (in both its capacities), FINEP, CAPES, and STI. This will be
achieved gradually and without disrupting the operation of the agencies by:
'i  activating existing but ineffectively utilized legal authority of the
CCT for overall coordination of sector policies and operations,
including increased influence in the budget process;
(ii)  establishing effective machinery to coordinate the responses of the
four principal project funding agencies at the level of individual
project requests, rather than having applicants deal separately with
each agency in a frustrating and often fruitless effort to put
together a funding package;
(iii)  reinforcing the capacity of the Ministry of Education (CAPES) to
provide the needed infrastructural support for graduate education; and
(iv)   refocusing FINEP's efforts on its area of special mandate, at the
intersection of technology and industrial processes, through
effectively shifting a portion of its management burden for FNDCT to
CAPES and CNPq.

B. Increased Productivity
4.        The government seeks to obtain greater productivity in the use of
available resources in the science and technology sector. This will be
approached through:
(i) assuring more efficient use of current capacity (by such
means as instituting cost recovery practices for services,
strengthening equipment maintenance and repair capability,
establishing mechanisms for expeditious supply of research reagents
and consumables);
(ii) avoiding undue dispersion of limited funds, so that critical mass
efforts may be sustained in research and higher level graduate
training;
(iii) relying more heavily on expert advice from the scientific and
technological conmunities in formulating policies, allocating
resources to alternative lines of activity, deciding among competing
applications for project funding, and evaluating the results obtained
at all levels; and
(iv) encouraging long term planning of scientific and technological
development activities, rather than relying on year-by-year project
proposals.
C. Improved Financial Base
5.        The government seeks to strengthen the sector's financial base by:
(i) assuring greater continuity of public support for high priority
projects;
(ii) halting the decline in real public resources for scientific and
tecbnological research and development; and
(iii)  strengthening pricrity disciplines and services by directing
complemuentary and incremental resources to them, not by reallocating
resources away from activities which, while comparatively more
developed, are still precarious.
D. Better Distribution of National Effort
6.        The government seeks a more balanced distribution.of national efforts
in science and technology. This will involve:

(i) developing lagging disciplines of particular importance
to Brazil's growth prospects; and
(ii) intensifying effort to create basic capacity for scientific and
technological development outside the south, in large measure by
concentrating on creation of new and strengthening of existing graduate
programs in the universities of the northeast, north, and centerwest
regions.
7.       These four objectives will be sought through a prudently targeted
investment program and through improved management arrangements. Together these
directed investments and management reforms will alleviate many of the sector's
weaknesses which currently impede its contribution to growth.

C.  The Geosciences and Mineral Technology Subprogram-US$52 million
8.        Brazil is one of the world's richest repositories of important

either be developed from scratch for Brazil's particular mineral lodes or, in
cases where it exists in temperate climates, must be significantly modified to
fit Brazil's tropical conditions. Successful technology development or
modification requires (i) basic infrastructure for graduate education and
training, for research, and for development in both geosciences and mineral
technology, which so far (and in contrast with the situation In chemistry and
chemical engineering) has remained embryonic; and (ii) a coordinated program of
attack on outstanding problems which focuses limited capacity on the highest
priority questions.

D. The Instrumentation Subprogram-US$40 million
11.       Instrumentation refers to the science and associated technology used
to detect, observe, measure, register or record any reaction or phenomenon, and
transfer, or use for control the information derived. Instruments are the
concrete result of applying such knowledge. As such, instrument is not a
synonym for tool or for equipment, which may or may not incorporate one or more
instruments. Most modern instruments incorporate electronic (and increasingly,
digital) components; very few nowadays are purely mechanical. A great deal can
be economically produced by specialized small and medium sized firms, especially
if close collaboration with university based research and development activities
can be established.
12.       The influence and use of instrumentation extends into every aspect of
science and technology and has a significant impact on the quality of life.
Instrumentation is an indispensable ingredient of teaching and research,
especially in the physical and life sciences and engineering; health care,
including medical screening, diagnosis and treatment; public utilities including
electricity, telecommunications, transportation, sanitation, and pollution
control; industrial production utilizing automated processes and quality
control; and innumerable aspe:ts of national defense.
13.       Brazil's rapid industrialization beginning in the mid-1960s and the
explosive expansion of higher education that occurred simultaneously were
supported with imported instrumentation. In 1979 (the most recent date for
which reliable and comprehensive data is available), 90% of Brazil's
instrumentation needs were still supplied from abroad. The constraints on
foreign exchange after the oil shocks of the 1970s stimulated recognition of the
dangerous economic and political consequences of this situation, motivated
several government sponsored analyses and studies, and ultimately resulted in a
counterproductive series of further sharp restrictions on imports with a view to
stimulating emergence of national capability to produce instruments.

instrumentation industry comprises mainly micro firms (fewer than 10 employees),
firms which are unable to support in-house research and development activity.

should occur, lack modern equipment, ready access to the frontier technologies

15.       Instrument production involves well-defined steps, which are
(i) determination of the technical specification, (ii) research and
experimentation into the ability and means to meet the specification at a
competitive cost, including full consideration of the characteristics of the
potential market, (iii) prototype development, construction, and testing,
(iv) production engineering and planning, (v) trial batch prodtietion and
testing, and finally, (vi) full scale production. The PADCT Instrumentation
Subprogram seeks to increase the capacity of universities and research
institutes to undertake the first four activities, and of various industrial
sectors to undertake the last three, each group in close interaction with the
other tbroughout the process. Attention will be limited to priority needs in
the industrial process control, biomedical, analytical, e'lectrical/electronic
measurement and testing, and mechanical measurement and test areas.
16.       Subprojects will thus concentrate on on-the-job practical skills
rather than formal graduate training to create the highly trained and skilled,
often interdisciplinary, personnel base in the physical sciences and engineering
needed to support development of instrumentation; collaborative efforts by
universities and research Institutes, and manufacturing enterprises to design,
develop, test, produce, and market instruments of quality with full supporting
literature for which reasonable markets exist; determining, and disseminating,
information on market potential for specific instruments and classes of
instrument.
E. The Science Education Subprogram-US$28 million
17.       In the long term, technological advance is fundamentally dependent
upon the extent and level of scientific literacy in the population. Youngsters
with well-engrained habits of scientific observation and reasoning, and a
modicum of knowledge about their physical surroundings require less remedial
educational effort as they advance in the school system and are easier to
incorporate in the modern sector labor force with its growing requirement for
adaptability to changing technologies. Particularly at the primary level,
Brazilian schools instill nelther the appropriate modes of thought nor the
relevant knowledge base.
18.       Improvement of science and mathematics teaching has been a persistent
theme in Brazil since the 1950s. The central objective was clear at the
beginning and has not changed much: replace a heavily theoretical content

Page 6 of 10
orientation mastered through rote memorization with a practical process
orientation acquired by an activity and inquiry based interaction with concrete
elements of the physical environment. A variety of efforts were sponsored
beginning in the 1960s to attack what were perceived as the critical weaknesses
in teacher preparation and in-service training, teaching materials, textbooks,
and curriculum design. But for a variety of reasons, most of these efforts were
typically conducted in isolation, and little was generalized. Recent evaluation
of these activities suggests that success in the future will require much
greater effort in the universities to undertake basic research on learning
processes and applied curricular experimentation and to develop more appropriate
teacher training programs. Closer collaboration is also required, between
classroom teachers and university scholars, and between both those groups and
personnel of the education bureaucracies. Special attention must be paid to the
development of local leadership for science education at the school level and to
increasing regional coordination among different initiatives.  Effective
channels for generalizing successful experience must be sought. These are the
goals of the Science Education Subprogram.
19.       Subprojects will typically integrate activities from at least two of
these areas:
(i)  Science Curriculum Development.  About 10 strong regionally
distributed, university based teams will be created for research on
teaching and learning of science and experimental development of
improved curricula including texts and teaching materials. In a break
with past tradition, these teams will include scientists and
educators.
(ii)  Pre-service and In-service Teacher Training.  The methodology and
curriculum of teacher preparation programs in selected universities
and normal schools will be reoriented so as to be supportive of
process and activity based teaching. In-service retraining courses
will be developed to support introduction of new curricula, texts, and
teaching materials.
(iii) Extracurricular Community Based Science Activities. When they are
part of a broader plan to improve science education and are undertaken
in close collaboration with the schools, science centers and clubs,
science fairs, and science competitions, extracurricular science
activities may receive support.

itself, whose legal shell and planning mandate are only a decade old. But as

KEY AGENDA ITEMS FOR THE MIDTERM REVIEW
1.        In addition to compliance with formal loan covenants, various
matters will be examined with special care during the Midterm Review. Most
of these questions will have been regularly addressed by the CCT/CT, GEA,
GTs, or CAs, and by the PADCT/SE, the four intermediary funding agencies, and
by Bank supervision miss ions. Many othe, more mundane issues will also be
examined, as noted at various points in the text of this report. As an input
to the Midterm Review, the GEA will use one of its regular semiannual reports
to the government and the Bank to summarize its evaluative judgments on the
questions below under four headings: balance within PADCT subprograms,
especially among activities with concrete near term and less certain or more
distant economic payoff; the functioning of the new management structures and
procedures at all levels; the adequacy of governmental financial and other
support; and the achievement of critical mass concentrations of effort in an
environment of acute scarcity of public resources.
2.        Sector Performance Issues
(i) Has the decline in science and technology investments, which began
in 1980, been reversed? In addition to real value comparisons of
expenditures by key funding agencies of the government, the ratio
of sector expenditures to-GNP and to overall public expenditures
will be useful indicators?  Given their critical importance to
scientific infrastructures, resources allocated to federal
universities and to the FNDCT will command special attention.
Cii) Have incentives been increased for private sources of investment in
science and technology?
(iii) What impact has the commitment of institutional and financial
resources to PADCT subprograms had on areas not originally included
in PADCT? Has Brazilian counterpart for PADCT represented truly
incremental investment and not reallocation away from fields not
included in PADCT? Have government resources for non-PADCT areas
been released to the funding intermediaries no less rapidly than,
and with similar inflation corrections as, for PADCT counterpart?
Has the privileged access to foreign exchange within PADCT been
damaging to non-PADCT areas?
(iv) What measures have been taken to ensure that universities and
research institutes in the public sector will be able to attract
and maintain high quality technicians essential to research and
graduate education?

SUMMARY OF PADCT TEST ROUND
1.        The test round of subproject generation and appraisal for nine
PADCT subprograms was conducted during the period mid-April to early August
1984. (The Science Education Subprogram completed this trial phase in July
1983).
2.        Statistical recapitulation of this 1984 test round (see table at
end of this annex) reveals that subproject competition was very stiff. Only
17X of the proposals submitted (comprising in value terms 28Z of the total
money requested in all subproject proposals) were recommended for approval.
The budget available for the test round will limit final approval to 47% of
the total subproject amount recommended.
3.        The Bank's appraisal team observed the work of the Brazilian
subproject CAs in Rio de Janeiro July 16-21, 1984. One hundred and seven
appraisal consultants, nominated by Brazil's scientific associations and
grouped in nearly 20 judgment panels, considered proposals in the 9
subprograms; this constituted an unprecedented incorporation of the
collective expertise of the community of scientific peers in project decision
making. The mission's views of this process, substantiated by analysis of
the appraisal process evaluation questionnaire prepared by the PADCT/SE and
completed by CA members at the conclusion of their work, are briefly
summarized below.
4.        Subproject proposals not in accord with announced program criteria
(such as content orientation and institutional eligibility) were summarily
rejected.
5.        Carelessly presented subproject proposals, and those obviously
devoid of technical merit, were systematically rejected, even when submitted
by powerful individuals or agencies that customarily have relied on
reputation and "connections" for favorable treatment in the past.
6.        Despite an unexpectedly large number of subproject applications,
those which met the criteria and were not obviously without technical merit
received a through professional review of technical merit and implementation
feasibility by scientific peers. The budget proposals of these fully
appraised subprojects were also closely scrutinized; often very substantial
cuts were recommended.
7.        The best of the subproject applications were in fact recommended
for approval, and they were of high quality.
8.        Despite the short time available between the application deadline
and the July meeting of the CAs (and the considerable complication of a
strike in the federal universities), most proposals had been critically
scrutinized by an ad hoc consultant prior to consideration by the CA.
(Ad hoc denotes an outside scientific peer not a member of an: CA or of the
technical staff of any of the intermediary agencies. Written appraisals of
many subprojects were also available from agency staff and CA members). The
ad hoc peer reviews were accorded great weight by the CAs. Future subproject
rounds will aim to have two such outside peer reviews for each subproject
application.

ANNEX 7
Page 2 of 3
9.        When problems emerged, the envisioned corrective mechanisms were
effective. For example, ambiguities in certain subprogram criteria, as stated in
the subprogram announcements, were referred by the CAs to the GT members present
for adjudication.
10.       The attempt via PADCT to encourage multiyear, multiple input subprojects
(e.g., equipment, staff development, support for technicians, visiting
professors/researchers and consultants-all based on a long range work or
development plan), thereby assuring integrated critical mass efforts likely to
produce significant results, was only partly successful. Among the most important
of the evident reasons were (i) the difficulty of abruptly changing commurnity
perceptions of how the intermediary agencies would behave; (ii) the government's
decision, prior to Bank approval of the loan request, to limit test round
subprojects to a two year time horizon; and (iii) the shortness of time between
issuing the program announcements and the application deadline, which led to many
institutions simply to -take off the shelf" project ideas developed for the
pre-PADCT policy regime without much reworking. More explicit discussions of
PPDCT objectives in the next round of subprogram announcements combined with more
reasonable time allowances should facilitate greater progress on this crucial
matter in the future.
11.       With rare exceptions, the inevitable conflict of interest
situations were handled professionally. CA members with personal or
institutional interest in a subproject application absented themselves from
debate and decision on that proposal.
12.       The short time between program announcement and application
deadline had two unintended consequences. It reduced the quality of many
proposals, and it gave advantage to those individuals and groups (typically
concentrated in the south) with a tradition of project preparation. By contrast,
one anticipated result of the short lead time-a meager response in terms of the
number of proposals--failed to materialize. The extra burden on the appraisal
process of this misjudgment was substantial.
13.       The demonstration of administrative capacity by the SE and all four
intermediary agencies was impressive. The structures established by PADCT to
rationalize project decision making and to simplify the institutional arrangements
worked in practice.

PADCT USES OF FUNDS BY EXPENDITURE CATEGORY AND SUBPROGRAM
1.        PADCT subprojects of all three types can involve, in varying
configurations and irrespective of their fundamental purpose, investment
expenditures in five common categories. Only rarely will a subproject entail
expenditure in fewer than three of these categories. These common categories
and estimated amounts of investment expenditures (for which further quantitative
detail is supplied in the ensuing table) follow.
A.   Education and Training-US$86 million
2.        This category covers all costs of travel, maintenance, tuition and
fees, books and other bibliographic material and personal equipment required for
formal training of varying levels and durations, including short term
visitation. Much of this will be in doctoral and post doctoral programs and
shorter study tours abroad.
B.   Research and Instructional Support-US$124 million
3.        Tnis includes incremental costs of personnel, particularly
technicians, consumable supplies and spare parts, specialized bibliographic
materials, travel to scientific meetings, and institutional overheads (to cover
communications, administration, office space) necessary for conduct of research
and instructional activities. Incremental costs of intermediary agency staff
are incorporated here.
C. Equipment and Durable Materials-US$83 million
4.        This category includes costs of all equipment and instrumentation
which is used in research and graduate training or which are essential for
provision of a given service, including laboratory and office furniture.
Journal subscriptions are counted as durable materials and included here.
D.   Technical Assistance-US$44 million
5.        Technical assistance includes all costs of short term consultancies
and longer term visiting professors and researchers, both local and
international, including those required for PADCT program and project management
bodies. (Costs of fellowships are included in category (A) above.)
E.   Civil Works-US$22 million
6.        With the exception of completing construction of primary metrological
laboratories at Xerem, little or no large scale new construction is envisioned.
In addition to US$13.4 million for the Xerem laboratories, this category covers
expenses of minor extensions and remodelling of current premises used for
research, teaching, service, or technological development activities. It also
includes costs of construction for pilot plants.

BRAZILIAN PROCUREMENT REGULATIONS
1.        Decree LAw 200 (Title XII) and Law 6946 of September 17, 1981,
establish procurement regulations for all 'direct administration' public
entities.  Other (-indirect administration") public and private entities
generally conform to these, or often more stringent, regulations for
procurement. While there is no formal exclusion of foreign suppliers from
participation, no special effort is made to acquire their participation, with
the practical result that foreign suppliers bid infrequently and only when they
have strong local representatives.
2.        This Brazilian legislation establishes four sets of procurement rules
according to the expected size of the contract in question. The limits
triggering each set of procurement procedures are semiannually reviewed in May
and November on the basis of a Brazilian price index. Amounts shown above
represent the dollar value of that index in May 1984. These rules do not apply
to coatracts for services of individual consultants.  The procurement categories
are:
(I) No Restrictions on Procurement. Applies to contracts estimated to
cost less than US$4,050 for civil works, and less than US$490 for
goods and services.
(ii) Request for Informal Quotations (Convite). Entails shopping from
known suppliers, with a minimum of three price quotations recorded
prior to decision. This applies to contracts estimated to cost
between US$4,050 and US$40,500 for civil works, and between US$490 and
US$8,100 for goods and services.
'ii)   Limited Bidding (Tomada de Precos).  Entails formal request for bids
in writing to potential suppliers from a list of prequalified firms
maintained by the organization.  This applies to contracts estimated
to cost between US$40,501 and US$1.14 million for civil works, and
between US$8,101 and US$805,000 for goods and services.
(iv)   Formal Competitiva Bidding (Concorrencia).  Is equivalent with respect
to procedures ro Bank ICB--i.e., broad advertisement (although not
necessarily internationally) of request for bids, careful production
of bidding documents, specification of time period between requests
for bids and deadline for submission, defined procedures for bid
evaluation. This applies to contracts estimated to cost more than
US$1.14 million for civil works and more than US$805,000 for goods and
services.
3.        Brazilian legislation provides for exemption from the above
procurement rules when grouping of small requirements into suitable bid packages
is impractical, and for technical reasons of compatibility, complementarity,
maintenance and service requirements, and unusually exacting technical
specifications. At the option of the purchasing entity, more stringent
procedures may be adapted than are required--i.e. Concorrencia instead of Tomada
de Precos.

APPRAISAL PROCESS FOR PADCT
1.      The sectoral quality of the proposed Bank involvement in a new field,
the extent to which policy reforms were to be required as a basis of Bank
involvement, the highly technical nature of the PADCT subprograms, and tne
institutional development objective of alterations in the nature and extent of
collaboration among the four PADCT intermediary agencies posed exceptional
challenges in readying PADCT for Board consideration. Appraisal involved four
overlapping phases which began in November 1982 on the basis of the tentative
conclusions of Bank sector work. First agreement had to be reached with the
government on the central issues impeding better performance of the science and
technology sector, and on the requisite changes in policy framework to address
them. Scrutiny of the several proposed PADCT components--the rationale for
their inclusion and their technical content-and of the overall organizational
design of PADCT comprised a second appraisal phase. Major attention was also
devoted at this stage to assessment of the viability of the investment program-
e.g., the likely availability of counterpart funds and overall implication for
the agencies. Review of subproject decision criteria, procedures, and
mechanisms was the third major task. Finally, close observation of the first
(test) round of processing of subprojects--from elaboration of the government's
requests for proposals through its appraisal decisions and subproject contract
terms--was a key basis for assessment of the intermediaries' implementation
capacity and for further refinement of subprogram parameters and administrative
mechanisms. All phases in this lengthy process involved intense interaction of
Bank staff and consultants with Brazilian scientists from outside the
intermediary agencies, through dialogue with the several PADCT management bodies
(CCT, CT, GTs, SE, and CAs) and during less formal visits to universities,
research institutes and business firms throughout the country.
2.      Personnel required for this appraisal process significantly exceeded
normal Bank standards. In addition to two staffyears from the LAC Education
Projects Division, upstream assistance from personnel on the Operations Policy
staff and the Legal and Loan Departments was required. A team of nine outside
technical consultants, whose identities and responsibilities are listed below,
had to be identified and supervised. Major missions were mounted in March and
July 1983, and February and July 1984, supported by smaller field efforts in
November 1982, and May, October, and December 1983. The cost of consultants for
the final appraisal field work in February and July 1984 was supported through a
Bank administered United Nations Development Programme project.
3.      The following ineividuals participated as consultants in one or more of
the appraisal missions (an asterisk preceding the name indicates that this
person completed work in 1983):
(a) George Ember, formerly Chief of Operations and Assistant to the Director,
Canada Institute for Scientific and Technical Information, Ottawa;
scientific and technological information services subprograms.
