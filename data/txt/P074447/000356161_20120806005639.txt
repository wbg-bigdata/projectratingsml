F. Results Framework Analysis

Project Development Objectives (from Project Appraisal Document)
The PDO is to enhance efficiency and accountability in the management of financial and human
resources in participating states in line with state poverty reduction strategies.

Revised Project Development Objectives (as approved by original approving authority)
The objective of the project is to strengthen capacity in the relevant participating states for: (i)
multi-year fiscal planning, expenditure policy, and budgeting; (ii) timely auditing and reporting;
(iii) effective and transparent public procurement management; (iv) transparency in personnel
registry and payroll controls; (v) effective tax registration and assessment measures in Bauchi and
Kaduna states; and (vi) enhanced delivery of civil service training.

I. Disbursement Profile
 
1. Project Context, Development Objectives, and Design
1.1 Context at Appraisal

Country and sector background
Nigeria returned to democracy in 1999 with the election of President Olusegun Obasanjo.
Past periods of non-elected government had left the federation with a centralized
structure, opaque public financial and human resource management, weak oversight
institutions, and significant discretion exercised at all levels. The State Governance and
Capacity Building Project (SGCBP) was prepared in 2004 and 2005, just after the 2003
election in which President Obasanjo regained the presidency.

Nigeria has a long-established tradition of national planning but limited success in
implementing these plans. In an effort to introduce more comprehensive planning, the
reelected administration drafted the National Economic Empowerment and Development
Strategy (NEEDS), which departed from previous planning approaches by emphasizing
the importance of reforming all levels of government and of changing values as well as
structures and policies. 6 Each state was encouraged to develop its own State Economic
Empowerment and Development Strategy (SEEDS).

Economic context
The NEEDS was drafted in the context of economic growth rates that barely kept up with
population increases and a continuing expansion of public expenditures fueled by oil and
gas revenues. According to the legally mandated revenue-sharing formula, close to 47
percent of the growing petroleum revenues were allocated to subnational governments.

At the time of project preparation, there were growing concerns that some states were
building up structural deficits through unsustainable expenditure and investment
programs, and that the programs were being put in place without strategic direction.
There was also an increasing recognition that capacity and management constraints in the
state civil services were undermining the quality of this increased spending.

Rationale for Bank assistance
It was clear that most states had limited exposure to good practices in public financial
management in particular, and public sector management more generally. Few states had
designed and managed institutional reforms previously. Commitment to reform was
strong in some states, and there was recognition at the federal level of the need to develop
institutional structures at all levels of government, as expressed in the NEEDS.

In this context, the SGCBP provided a framework for helping civil servants better
understand, through learning and experience, which reforms to undertake and how to
design and implement them. It was decided that the project would focus on strengthening

would be central to improving service delivery and thus achieving the Millennium
Development Goals (MDGs); (ii) states in particular could benefit from the World Bank’s

increased revenue, faced a financing shortfall for governance reforms, creating demand
for an IDA Credit; and (iv) there was a need to complement federal-level reforms with
those at the state level.

1.2 Original Project Development Objectives and Key Indicators (as approved)

The PDO, as set out in the PAD, was to “enhance efficiency and accountability in the
management of financial and human resources in participating states in line with state
poverty reduction strategies.? The PDO was aligned with the Government’s NEEDS,
with the first pillar of the 2001 Joint Interim Strategy (JIS) for Nigeria on improving
economic governance, and with the third pillar of the 2005–2009 World Bank Group
Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) on strengthening transparency and accountability for
better governance.

The key associated performance indicators were as follows:
   1. The target share of budget allocations to priority poverty-reducing
      sectors/programs as defined in SEEDS is met.
   2. The deviation of actual spending from approved budget in participating states
      does not exceed thirty percent.
   3. The time taken to prepare audited annual reports in participating states is reduced
       to less than six months.
   4. There is no significant discrepancy between payroll and a “clean nominal roll.?

1.3 Revised Project Development Objectives and Key Indicators

The PDO was revised as part of a major project restructuring in 2007. The new PDO was
to “strengthen capacity in the relevant participating states for: (i) multiyear fiscal
planning, expenditure policy, and budgeting; (ii) timely auditing and reporting; (iii)
effective and transparent public procurement management; (iv) transparency in personnel
registry and payroll controls; (v) effective tax registration and assessment measures in
Bauchi and Kaduna states; and (vi) enhanced delivery of civil service training.?

The Project Outcome Indicators were revised to the following:

   1. Using computerized systems for multiyear fiscal planning, expenditure policy,
      and budgeting in FY2009.
   2. Reduction in the time to prepare and publish audited annual report for the account
      of the state to less than 5 months.
   3. 70% of public contracts are published by FY2010.
   4. 75% of projected Internally Generated Revenue (IGR) in Bauchi and Kaduna
      states to be collected by FY2010

      5. Reduction in the discrepancy between the civil service nominal roll and clean
         payroll to less than 5% in FY2010.
      6. 75% of professional civil servants are satisfied with training received.

The primary motivation for this change was a desire within the Bank to make it easier to
measure achievement of the objectives. The change was well accepted by the
implementing agencies, which found the new PDO easier to relate to and better aligned
with actions they could influence directly.

1.4 Main Beneficiaries

The main beneficiaries were public sector institutions and employees in the three
participating states of Bauchi, Cross River, and Kaduna. The type of beneficiaries were
the same before an after the 2007 restructuring.

1.5 Original Components (as approved) 7

Component 1: The Core Reform Program – USD 13.8 million. This component aimed
to increase the ability of the three states to strengthen development and implementation
of public policies and programs. To that end, it aimed to help rebuild basic systems of
public financial and human resource management to meet minimum efficiency,
accountability, and transparency standards; to promote the standardization of public
financial management procedures and of financial data production among the states to
allow appropriate data aggregation and analysis; and thereby to facilitate the formulation
and implementation of national fiscal policies.

Activities included support to adopt new public financial management legislation; reform
budget preparation; strengthen accounting, expenditure control, and financial reporting
functions; strengthen external audit functions; implement integrated financial
management information systems; modernize human resource management; and provide
training.

Component 2: State-Specific Reform Programs – USD 1.9 million. This component
aimed to support additional institutional reform and capacity building initiatives specific
to each state. USD 510,000 was set aside to address needs that emerged during
implementation, while USD 1,490,000 was allocated to fund taxpayer identification and
data management systems in Bauchi and Kaduna states, to strengthen the MDI in Cross
River State, and to improve judicial service delivery in Cross River State through: (i)
revision of the rules of civil procedure applicable in Magistrate and High Courts in Cross
River State; (ii) a comprehensive skills development program for judicial and
administrative officers of the Magistrate and High Courts; and (iii) implementation of a
model court.

In addition to these two components, the project included USD 1 million in unallocated
funds to meet two types of needs as they arose: (i) to pave the way for institutional and

1.6 Revised Components

Revised Component 1: Public Financial Management Reform – USD 10.23 million
The restructuring resulted in the following additions to Component 1:
• Sensitization of Executive Council members to the proposed Public Finance Law.
• Inclusion of activities to strengthen the capacity of Public Accounts Committees of
   the State Houses of Assembly.
• Functional reviews in pilot ministries.
• Providing Budget and Treasury Management Information System (BATMIS)

• Provision of logistical support and capacity building to relevant staff in monitoring

New Component 2: Public Procurement Reform – USD 1.2 million
Reform of public procurement became a priority for the new state governments elected in
2007, reflecting procurement reforms at the federal level. The new component aimed to
improve the institutional and legal framework for public procurement in the states;
introduce due process of public procurement for goods, works, and consulting services;
broaden the participation of the private sector; and increase transparency. The new
component was funded through reallocation of credit proceeds from the BATMIS.

New Component 3: Human Resource Management – USD 2.342 million
The restructuring resulted in the following additions to a new Component 3. In Bauchi
and Kaduna, a new activity was added to strengthen the capacity of the state public
service training institutes, including staff training, equipment, and furniture for the
College of Administration and Business Studies in Bauchi and the Staff Development
Center in Kaduna. Both state governments committed to financing the physical
renovation of the training institutes in their 2008 budgets.

New Component 4: State Specific Programs – USD 1.9 million
Taxpayer education was added to tax administration reform in Bauchi and Kaduna.

1.7 Other Significant Changes

Several funding reallocations were made as part of the 2007 restructuring. USD 1.2
million was moved from the BATMIS component to the public procurement component;
USD 390,000 from the unallocated amount to civil service training institutions; and USD
899,000 from the unallocated amount to the NPCU-executed provision to finance
procurement of consultancy services to support states in implementing the BATMIS and

NPCU’s capacity to support the states and meet growing operating costs.

Additional restructurings in 2009, 2010, and 2011 resulted in the following adjustments:
• In 2009, the project was restructured to extend the closing date from March 31, 2010,
   to March 31, 2011, to enable the completion of all outstanding activities; and to
   reallocate funds to reflect cost changes and new program priorities. USD 360,000 was
   divided equally among the three states from the provision that was intended to be
   implemented by the NPCU. The safeguards category was amended from “C? to “B.?
• In 2010, SDR 160,000 in undisbursed funds were allocated from the NPCU to the

   resources at state level. The restructuring pooled all undisbursed funds under a single
   disbursement category in each state to maximize flexibility.
• In 2011, the project closing date was extended to December 31, 2011, to enable
   Kaduna and Bauchi to complete implementation of BATMIS contracts signed in
   October and December 2010, respectively.

Implementation arrangements were adjusted as follows during the life of the project:
• It was originally anticipated that the NPCU would collate and ensure quality of
   reporting from the states to the World Bank. From about 2008, states reported directly
   to the Bank. The NPCU was also envisioned to provide consultancy services on
   BATMIS implementation that could be shared by all three states, but this did not
   materialize. The activities were instead undertaken by each state.
• In Cross River, responsibility for project oversight was moved from the Head of
   Service to the State Planning Commission after the 2007 restructuring to the newly
   established Department for International Donor Support (carved out of the Planning
   Commission) in February 2009. Both changes were intended to improve oversight.
• In Bauchi, project oversight was moved from the Ministry of Budget and Planning to
   the Office of the Head of Civil Service to enhance responsiveness and supervision.

2. Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes
2.1 Project Preparation, Design, and Quality at Entry

Selection of states
The project used a logical evaluation method to select beneficiary states and activities:
• 24 states were invited to submit initial diagnostics and proposal documents.
• 13 states responded to the invitation.
• 6 states were shortlisted.
• 3 states were selected.

The 13 proposals were evaluated according to four criteria:
• The quality of the diagnostics of existing constraints.
• Existence of an overall reform program or strategy for the state.
• Relevance of proposed activities.


                                             5
•   Track record in the period from 1999 to 2004.

As a result of this process, it was assumed that activities were aligned with the selected
states’ reform priorities and that project ownership was ensured. However, a review of
Bank support for public sector governance programs, conducted by the Bank’s Quality
Assurance Group (QAG) in 2008, 8 found that the final selection of states relied on more
than merely technical considerations, including geopolitical concerns:

         The Nigeria State Governance Project required restructuring partly because the
         design parameters were changed during preparation to suit the Bank’s desire to
         focus on a different set of states to those which had competed for inclusion and to
         impose a mandatory IFMIS component, thereby destroying ownership. 9

         The project, which was altered from a competitive to a Bank selection of states
         and from elective to mandated composition of sub-components during
         preparation, had serious design flaws, including lack of state involvement in its
         final format. 10

This assessment was partially confirmed by this ICR, though it is important to note that
the states that were eventually selected did participate in the competition. Geopolitical
considerations were explicitly mentioned in the CAS for this period as a state selection
criterion. 11 Geopolitical representation was and is explicitly requested by the Nigerian
Government, given the internal political preference for all geopolitical zones to benefit
from development support. Interviews conducted during the ICR visits to states did not
confirm that the selection process contributed to “destroying ownership?. Interviewees
indicated that the process ensured high level ownership within the executive in the states
but not ownership at lower levels of the executive. As described below, the issue of
broader ownership was remedied as part of the restructuring process in late 2007.

Component design
The components were logically designed with activities grouped in discrete
subcomponents according to their focus and counterparts. The components did not
differentiate among the three states. Implementation in each state progressed at different
speed for different components thus “adapting? the prioritization and phasing of the
components. Differences between states were also captured in the base line values in the
Results Framework which was set in ranges.

Adequacy of government commitment
Implementation stalled in the first two years after project effectiveness (see Section 2.2),
in part because project beneficiaries and implementing agencies at the state level were
not adequately involved in or apprised of the project and its implementation arrangements

beyond top decision makers and lack of basic capacity. This was subsequently addressed
through broader consultations as part of the 2007 restructuring process and through
extensive training.

Lending instrument
The operation was prepared and implemented as a Technical Assistance
Credit/Investment Credit to allow for the provision of consultancy services and
investments in information and communication technology (ICT). The option of a
Learning and Innovation Credit was discarded because this instrument could not
accommodate the lending volume needed. The choice of lending instrument was
appropriate given the instruments available in the Bank at the time and the nature of
assistance envisioned.

Adequacy of stakeholder involvement
The competition between states to be included in the project seems to have assured high-
level commitment and ownership of the project. However, the approach did not assure
appropriate understanding at this level of the need for intensive facilitation of
implementation, nor did it ensure sufficient understanding at lower levels in the states of
reform objectives, activities to be implemented, or World Bank procedures.

Reflection of lessons learned in project design
The SGCBP was the first Bank-funded project in Nigeria that aimed to address
governance and institutional development at the state level. Project design and
implementation support therefore relied on international experience.

Risk identification and mitigation
Critical risks were rated substantial in the PAD.

It seems that identified risks related to high-level ownership either did not materialize or
were successfully mitigated through the measures included in the PAD, e.g. careful
selection of states and enactment of the National Fiscal Responsibility Law. Ownership
even seems to have strengthened after the 2007 elections, which brought new governors
into all three participating states.

On the other hand, identified risks related to the capacity of implementing agencies seem
to have been underestimated. Some of the mitigating measures were not implemented,
including the support envisioned from the NPCU in the FMOF and the anticipated
spillover effects from the federal-level Bank-financed Economic Reform and Governance
Project. Implementation of the latter has been much delayed and is still ongoing. Other
mitigating measures turned out to be inadequate. These included the ICT training, which



                                             7
was neither sufficiently broad-based nor timely, and the provision for procurement
consultants, which was insufficient to mitigate the lack of knowledge of, experience with,
and initial acceptance of Bank Procurement Guidelines in the three participating states.

There were a few gaps in risk identification. i) The level of capacity and priorities at the
federal level were not identified as risks. ii) The consultation with stakeholders did not
include of State Assemblies even though they played an import role in the project through
the envisioned passing of new state-level legislation 12 . iii) Finally, the PAD did not
mention the risk of fraud and corruption in project implementation, though it must be
noted that this was aligned with Bank practice at the time of project preparation.

2.2 Implementation

Overview
The project was fully disbursed at closing. Four of six revised project development
objectives were exceeded, one objective was substantially achieved and one objective
was exceeded in two states and substantially achieved in one state. Of the twelve revised
intermediate outcome indicators, 13 nine were substantially achieved, achieved or
exceeded, one was achieved in two states and partially achieved in one state, one was
achieved in one statebut not achieved in two states, and one indicator is inconclusive.
Interviews conducted in the three states and in the FMOF, together with the
Implementation Completion and Results (ICR) Reports prepared by the three states (see
Annex 8) , support the conclusion that the project brought about major improvements in
the areas it covered and provided the three states with the project design and reform
implementation experience needed to support further reform of public sector institutions.

Some project results stand out. These include the more timely publication of the states’
audited accounts, cleaning of the payrolls and nominal rolls, broadening of the tax base
and related revenue increases in Kaduna and Bauchi, and increased coverage of external
audit in Bauchi. These results are noteworthy in light of the project’s relatively small
IDA Credit of USD 18 million, disbursed over a period of about six years in three states.

At the same time, implementation of most activities in all components in all three states
was delayed, and some were not completed at project closing in December 2011 (see
section 3.2.2 and Annex 8 and 10 for details). As argued in section 3.2.2 below, overall
this did not affect the achievement of the project development outcomes. All three states
seem committed to completing initiated activities, including the major investments in
BATMIS, HRMIS, and possibly the passage of outstanding legislation. This will be
facilitated by a follow on operation as discussed below.

the three states, and the campaigning process diverted top-level attention from project
implementation.

The process of project restructuring was used by the task team to sensitize beneficiaries
and stakeholders on project goals and activities and provided an opportunity to confirm
ownership after the 2007 gubernatorial elections. By 2007, many Project Implementation
Unit (PIU) staff had received procurement training. Their growing familiarity with Bank
procurement guidelines and implementation processes accounts for the major
improvements in implementation following restructuring.

Legislative capacity and commitment
The project intended to facilitate the drafting and eventual passage of several state laws
related to public financial management, such that the extent of legislative commitment
was an important factor affecting project implementation and outcomes. In Bauchi, most
laws were enacted in 2008 and 2009. In Cross River, the laws were enacted in late 2011.
In Kaduna, most of the laws have not been enacted (see Annex 3). The project primarily
funded consultancy services to provide technical input to legislative revisions and some
study trips to help sensitize relevant officials to the issues involved. However, there were
no provisions for working directly with the State Houses of Assembly to build
understanding of the proposed changes. In some cases, such as the procurement law in
Kaduna, the State House of Assembly heavily opposed the proposed revisions.

Implementation of ICT activities
Implementation of the project’s support for integrated financial management systems
(IFMIS) in all three states could have benefited from more careful attention to some key
lessons learned in implementing such systems in other countries. 15 In short, more effort
could have been put into sensitizing stakeholders, building capacity to implement such
complex systems before initiating reforms, providing outside expertise to help move
program components along as this capacity was being built, and strengthening the realism
and simplicity of funding modalities and implementation mechanisms.

More accurate costing of the project’s ICT activities could have prevented some delays.
The budget allocated to BATMIS implementation was insufficient to fund a full rollout to
all MDAs in the three states as well as all envisioned modules. This shortfall resulted in
revisions to the scope of the systems (specifically, the functional and technical
specifications) during implementation, which required additional work and time. This
problem was exacerbated by the 2007 restructuring, under which funds were allocated
from BATMIS activities to facilitation of procurement reforms.

From a procedural standpoint, the BATMIS contract was laid out in such a way that
about 70 percent of the funds were paid upon delivery and only 30 percent upon
operational acceptance. While this feature was intended to speed up disbursement, it
carried with it the risk (which materialized) of delays in achieving full implementation.
These lessons are presented in more detail in Annex 4.

Role of the NPCU
The NPCU suffered from high staff turnover, long periods of staff vacancies, and lack of
familiarity with Bank procurement and financial management guidelines. Staff who had
become familiar with the project and procedures quickly moved on, requiring training
and sensitization for new NPCU staff. The high turnover of accountants was recognized
by the Bank and NPCU as the principal reason for inadequate bookkeeping and
accounting throughout most of the project’s duration.

Delays by the NPCU and disagreements between the NPCU and the Bank on consultant
selection hindered the procurement of the BATMIS consultants that were to be hired by
the NPCU and provided to the states to save resources; after over a year, the procurement
process was eventually cancelled.

It is possible that the NPCU’s limited engagement in the project stemmed from
insufficient incentives for federal-level entities and staff to take an active role in state-
level institutional reform. Moreover, the FMOF, where the NPCU was located, does not
have expertise in project implementation, monitoring, or financial management and
accounting.

2.4 Safeguard and Fiduciary Compliance

Safeguards compliance
The project funded two works activities in Cross River State: (a) a renovation of the MDI,
and (b) a renovation of a building and compound housing four Magistrate Courts. These
activities were identified in the PAD and USD 0.5 million allocated to the works
disbursement category. The expectation at the time was that the project would fund
painting of walls and procurement of some furniture. The project was thus classified as a
category “C? project in the PAD and no safeguard arrangements were made. The works
activities were expanded during the 2007 restructuring to include comprehensive
renovation of buildings. The safeguards category was maintained at “C.?

During the mid-term review of the SGCBP in February 2009, an Environmental
Safeguards Review concluded that Cross River State did not comply with the Wold Bank
Safeguards Guidelines. Recommendations included the appointment of a safeguards
coordinator in the state PIU. It was decided that the M&E specialist already in the PIU
could function in this capacity, and though the specialist had no prior experience in
safeguards, the task team provided ongoing coaching.

A restructuring was initiated by the task team in June 2009, inter alia to change the
safeguards category from “C? to “B? to reflect the more comprehensive works activities.
ISR ratings for safeguards compliance were moderately satisfactory from 2009 due to
lack of implementation of safeguard measures recommended by the Bank task team. This
rating was upgraded to satisfactory in the final ISR filed in December 2011, as the

Fiduciary compliance
At effectiveness, fiduciary issues brought about by unfamiliarity with Bank requirements
included non-economical use of funds, incomplete documentation, problems with timely
retirement of advances, low timeliness and quality of accounting due to lack of
automation, issues with timeliness of withdrawal applications, and overcommitments by
one state.

Improvements occurred over time as implementing agencies gained experience and
received guidance, but some issues persisted, especially in Cross River State and the
NPCU. While financial management was rated satisfactory from project effectiveness to
December 2009, the rating was downgraded to moderately satisfactory until closing, due
mainly to inadequate accounting.

In 2011, an application for withdrawal was submitted from one state in which the same
expenditures were claimed twice. This led to an in-depth review by the Bank’s financial
management team, which concluded that the amounts were mistakenly entered twice and
that no fraudulent practices or intentions were behind the submission.

Sub-objective 1: Multi-year fiscal planning, expenditure policy, and budgeting

Financial management legislation
Progress has been made toward strengthening the legislative framework for public
financial management in two of the three states. In Cross River, the Fiscal Responsibility
Law and Public Finance Law were passed in December 2011. In Bauchi, a Fiscal
Responsibility Law was passed in 2008, but the Public Finance Bill is still awaiting
approval by the legislature. In Kaduna, both bills are pending legislative approval.

Reform of budget preparation
The project has introduced a multiyear perspective to budgeting, improved budget clarity,
and strengthened some processes.

In Cross River, multiyear budgets have been piloted in 10 MDAs and have been attached
as annexes to the annual budgets since 2009. A new budget manual was issued, which
streamlined the budget preparation process. A new budget and accounts classification
code was developed and implemented.

In Kaduna, a new Chart of Accounts was introduced in 2009, improving the analysis of

Sub-objective 2: Timely auditing and reporting

Internal control
In all three states, monthly state financial statements are now produced no later than 25

submitted to the SHoA within three months. The Public Accounts Committee has started
hearings on the submitted audit reports, which makes Cross River one of only three states

3.3 Efficiency

Rating: Substantial

Annex 8 of the PAD notes that the project does not lend itself to “meaningful quantitative
analysis of the economic return.? 24 Benefits listed in the PAD included qualitatively
better budgets, reduction of payroll fraud, and addressing practices of patronage. Given
the satisfactory rating of performance against the project’s outcome indicators, which are
intended to measure such qualitative results, the project’s social and economic return can
be considered significant.

Though a meaningful quantitative assessment of the investment’s net present value and
economic rate of return cannot be undertaken due to data constraints, some insights can
be gained by considering the efficiency of outputs as well as inputs, including training
and operating costs.

Output efficiency
The overall level of efficiency, effectiveness, and transparency in public financial
management, tax administration, and human resource management improved in all three
participating states as a result of the project. These improvements brought about cost
savings for the states. It is estimated that improved IGR collection between 2007 and
2011 yielded USD 16.4 million in real terms. 25 This figure provides only an indication,
as it does not include potential savings from other project outcomes such as improved
procurement, implementation of BATMIS, or the removal of ghost workers from the
payroll in Cross River and Bauchi States. These potential savings cannot be estimated
due to insufficient data and measurement difficulties.

       judicial business processes.
•      The two civil service training institutions supported by the project in Kaduna and
       Cross River States have been given a higher formal status in the hierarchy of state
       institutions. 28

Activities funded and facilitated by the project could be seen as first steps that require
deepening in terms of institutionalization and quality and broadening in terms of
institutional coverage. These changes could be sustainable if reforms are continued.

(c) Other Unintended Outcomes and Impacts (positive or negative)

Beyond the indicators, almost all stakeholders consulted during the ICR visits highlighted
the project’s positive impact in exposing them to Nigerian and international good practice
and project implementation experience as an added value. Although intangible, such
mindset changes could potentially be lasting, making future reforms easier to design and
implement.

A temporary unintended consequence occurred in 2008–2009, when the Internal Revenue
Board of Bauchi state was restructured and unqualified personnel removed or reassigned.
This change resulted in a temporary drop in revenues collected (subsequently more than
reversed).

5.1 Bank Performance

Rating: Satisfactory

(a) Bank Performance in Ensuring Quality at Entry

Rating: Moderately Satisfactory

The Bank’s assurance of quality at entry was adequate in the following respects:
• The project was and remains relevant. Services that are critical to achieving the
   MDGs are dependent on the quality of financial management and control and human
   resource management at the state level in Nigeria. Therefore, the project was
   strategically relevant in terms of its substantial scope and focus at the state level.
• The financial and economic assessment was on par with similar projects in the World
   Bank at the time of project preparation.

The Bank’s assurance of quality at entry was inadequate in the following respects:

•   Inadequate attention was given to lower-level ownership in the implementing
    agencies.

At state level, implementation arrangements were adequately designed, paying attention

Rating: Satisfactory

The project has been supervised well, with a focus on solving weaknesses and achieving

implementation capacity, and adjusting the scope of the project. The DO and IP ratings
turned around in early 2007, and disbursement picked up in October 2008 and January

The following examples from ISRs, aide-memoires, and client interviews undertaken

•   Additional supervision resources were provided in 2007 (see Annex 11) to increase
    supervision intensity, organize project sensitization workshops, and improve donor
    coordination. During ICR visits, all states reported increased intensity of
    implementation facilitation from that year, with many “troubleshooting missions? led
    by task team leaders based in country.
•   In response to delays a participatory restructuring was initiated in 2007, following
    what seems to have been close dialogue with a broad stakeholder group in the three

    eventually of disbursement.
•   The task team paid appropriate attention to project management issues and proposed

6. Lessons Learned
Implementation of the SGCBP yielded the following lessons.

•   Support from key stakeholders needs to be nurtured during project design and
    supervision. Governors are all-important centers of influence in most Nigerian states,
    and their explicit support is necessary to ensure implementation of projects in their
    state. Commissioners for the implementing agencies are also important stakeholders
    whose support must be secured. In addition, a broader group of stakeholders within
    implementing agencies must be involved and sensitized during project preparation to
    ensure adequate project design and ownership at implementation level.

•   Capacity building should be undertaken during the design phase, in the period
    between design and effectiveness, and throughout project implementation. The
    capacity for project design and implementation is low at the state level in Nigeria, and
    targeted capacity building and sustained “hand-holding? are needed. Given staff
    rotation policies in the civil service, turnover of project staff is highly likely over the
    life of a project and ongoing training is needed to bring new staff up to speed.

•   In projects being implemented at the state level, the role envisioned for federal-
    level institutions should correspond to their mandates, incentives, and capacity.
    State-level institutional reform is not within the mandate and expertise of federal-
    level Institutions. Project design should take this into account and assign only
    supervisory functions to federal-level project coordination units.

•
•   There may have been room for greater synergies and improved efficiency if
    some project activities had been implemented in collaboration across the three
    participating states. There was some effort to take a cross-state approach, as in the
    provision that the NPCU would provide consultancy services on BATMIS
    implementation that could be shared by all three states. This did not materialize


                                              27
       owing in part to a lack of NPCU engagement and in part to the state implementing
       agencies’ desire to manage the funds for their respective states. Nevertheless, some
       activities could have been undertaken collaboratively, including the design of budget
       and reporting formats, Chart of Accounts, and audit manuals, all of which are based
       on generally acceptable standards.

•      Donor coordination related to substantive areas (e.g. PFM or civil service
       reform) should be formalized through agreed meeting schedules and clear
       assignment of responsibility for organizing such meetings. The existence of some
       substantive overlaps between SGCBP activities and those of other donors (for
       example, on MTSS and MTBF support in Kaduna) points to the value of more
       formalized structures for donor coordination in order to promote synergies and avoid
       overlaps. Informal donor coordination is insufficient, as such arrangements may fizzle
       over time when personnel changes occur and other priorities arise.

•      Intensive supervision supported by adequate additional supervision budgets can
       turn around a project. The project’s remarkable resurgence after restructuring
       stemmed from frequent problem-solving missions led by staff based in Abuja. The
       missions targeted a broad group of stakeholders at all levels and provided capacity
       building to support implementation.

•      A focused piecemeal approach to human resource management is likely to be
       more successful than broad-based and ambitious reforms. Global experience with
       World Bank-assisted civil service reforms is mostly negative. 29 By contrast, the HRM
       activities supported by this project achieved progress in a narrow but foundational
       aspect, namely the cleaning of the nominal roll and work toward integrating the
       nominal roll and payroll. Even these limited reforms took considerable effort. A more
       ambitious reform approach is unlikely to have been implementable.

•      Intensive project implementation facilitation and capacity building are needed if
       the World Bank is to assist in the modernization of the judiciary in Nigeria. The
       implementation of this component in Cross River was tortuous, with long delays and
       minor wastage. The judiciary’s project implementation capacity is minimal, and
       intensive implementation support is needed if support is to be provided in the future.

•      Political economy considerations have to be understood and taken into account
       when designing and implementing integrated financial management information
       systems. Supporters of legacy systems and existing business processes are likely to
       include actors that have financial interests (such as existing providers, their
       government counterparts, and employees that fear becoming redundant) as well as
       actors with professional, technical interests (including those who find existing
       business processes and ICT to be adequate). Actors with “private interests? are likely
       to work actively against the new systems. Project preparation and implementation
       facilitation will need to counter such opponents to reduce delay and risks of

29

       initiatives and efforts to secure the support of the state leadership and top

Comments on the Draft ICR
The workshop began by sharing general findings from the draft ICR. Discussion centered
on the following issues:

•   The representative from Kaduna State expressed reservations about the state not
    having met the indicator on the time of publication of the Auditor-General’s Report
    on the State Annual Accounts. The representatives argued that the preliminary report
    of the Auditor-General was usually published within the agreed time, though formally
    submitted to the Parliament much later in the year. The World Bank clarified that the
    indicator derives from the existing legal framework in the Kaduna State, which
    stipulates the Parliament as the reporting authority of the Auditor-General.
    Consequently, unless the Auditor-General’s report was approved by the Parliament
    and officially published, for the purposes of assessing achievement of the indicator it
    should be considered a document in the internal process of the executive.

•   The DFID representative raised three issues: (a) that political economy considerations
    should have been included in the risk section of the ICR; (b) that the binary scoring of
    the indicators as fulfilled or not fulfilled oversimplified the assessment of
    achievement; and (c) to include lessons learned on “working with the development
    partners.?

•   The NPCU requested that it be clarified in the report that shortcomings in financial
    management were related to the high turnover of accountants posted from the Office
    of the Accountant-General of the Federation, as distinct from the issues in Cross
    River State which relate to inadequate accounting.

Save for the foregoing and other specific issues mentioned hereafter, the workshop
generally agreed that the draft ICR reflected the experiences, challenges, outcomes, and
lessons of SCGBP implementation between 2005 and 2011.

Key issues

Slow start of the project
Participants felt strongly that capacity was the binding constraint for slow project startup,
and agreed with the text in the draft ICR, including approaches to mitigating capacity
issues in future projects.

Change of PDO
Participants agreed that the change of PDO reflected a scaling down of the development
ambitions of the project. Nevertheless, there was consensus that this was a “sensible
move? because: (a) achievement of the revised PDO was within the control of the
implementing agencies, whereas agencies felt that the original PDO would to a greater
extent result from activities of other stakeholders; (b) ownership increased at the
implementing agency level due to a closer identification with the revised PDO and
indicators, which were seen as much more immediate and realistic than the poverty


                                             36
indicators; and (c) implementing agencies felt that achievement of the new PDO was
easier to measure.

BATMIS
The discussion about BATMIS in the three states centered around lessons learned:

•   Stakeholder sensitization is important when making significant changes to the way
    things are done. Implementation of an integrated BATMIS was a new concept in two
    of the three participating states. As such, it took some time for the state governors to
    appreciate the importance and potential of the new systems and lend their essential
    backing to their implementation. There was a sense that communication and advocacy
    could have been improved, with a view to winning over opponents of reform. For
    example, implementing agencies suggested leveraging the Governor’s Forum as a
    vehicle for early sensitization. There was some feeling that change management
    activities were not sufficient to secure support and ownership.

•   Participants highlighted that there should have been a “clear and robust directive?
    from the highest level of government to implement BATMIS. Even though the
    Governor of Kaduna hopes to implement BATMIS in all MDAs, the absence of a
    clear executive directive on its adopted application has continued to sustain some
    tensions between supporters of the legacy system and supporters of the new system.

•   Implementation of a new technology such as BATMIS is a complex task that must be
    accompanied by sufficient external and internal capacity. The project design should
    have emphasized hiring specialized and experienced experts to help with
    implementation, and more attention could have been paid to the states’ supervision of
    BATMIS implementation. In addition, closer attention could have been given to
    building critical local capacity before initiating implementation. In the area of
    procurement, the evaluation of BATMIS bids proved to be a challenge for the
    procurement committees, as the members had not been involved in similar ICT
    procurements before.

•   There was consensus that the faulty design of the procurement process (classification
    as ‘Goods’ rather than ‘Consultancy’) contributed to the delays and challenges
    encountered during the early stages of the implementation. In addition, more
    attention ought to have been paid to the capacity of the evaluator during the
    procurement process.

•   Meeting participants felt that IFMIS implementation might be better suited to a
    standalone project. Given the large size and technical nature of these activities, a
    standalone project would allow for more focused supervision and fewer distractions
    than a broader project of which IFMIS implementation constituted only one portion.

•   The meeting agreed that ICT risks should not only be identified but also mitigated
    early at the project preparation stage. Participants emphasized the necessity of
    having both functional and technical expertise available on the side of the
    counterparts to ensure sustainability.


                                            37
•   Realistic and accurate costing of technical systems is essential. The budget allocated
    to BATMIS implementation was insufficient to fund a full rollout to all MDAs in the
    three states as well as all envisioned modules. This shortfall resulted in revisions to
    the scope of the systems (specifically, the functional and technical specifications)
    during implementation, which caused additional work and delay. More accurate
    costing could have mitigated this risk at the design stage. This problem was
    exacerbated by the 2007 restructuring, under which funds were allocated from
    BATMIS activities to facilitation of procurement reforms.


Donor Coordination
The DFID representative felt that donor coordination could have been better. Despite a
mechanism being established, donor coordination was limited to a personal level rather
than being institutionalized. Coordination was and is needed at a higher level than the
project itself. Appropriate reform sequencing was also important to avoid duplication if
donors were working on similar issues.

Roles of Federal and State Governments
This was the most contentious issue during the workshop. State representatives were in
agreement that the SGCBP provided an important “window of opportunity? to carry out
reforms at the state level and that states were now ready to deepen reform efforts. Though
the stakeholders appreciated the efforts of the FMOF, they felt it had remained “a lone
ranger? within the federal bureaucracy. Consequently, the states called for an assessment
of how to improve collaboration on institutional reform at state level.

Annex 6: Follow-on Operations
Three projects continue and expand the initial SGCBP engagement:

   •   The Public Sector Governance Reform and Development Project (USD 80
       million) was approved by the Board of Executive Directors of the World Bank on
       June 29, 2010. It is a follow-on project to the SGCBP and will provide additional
       support to Cross River, Bauchi, and Kaduna states, as well as support to Ondo,
       Kogi, Adamawa, Kebbi, Plateau, Niger, Ekiti, Abia, and Imo States. The project
       aims to facilitate reforms in three areas: (i) public financial management
       (including accounting and financial reporting, budgeting, external audit,
       procurement, tax administration, and IFMIS); (ii) human resource management
       and capacity development; and (iii) results-based M&E systems.
   •   The State and Local Governance Reform Project (USD 87 million) is a proposed
       trust fund funded by the EU. It complements the SGCBP and the Public Sector
       Governance Reform and Development Project, and would extend similar
       programs to the 6 EU focal states of Anambra, Jigawa, Yobe, Kano, Osun, and
       Cross River.
   •   The State Employment and Expenditure for Results Project (USD 200 million)
       was approved by the Board of Executive Directors of the World Bank on March 6,
       2012. A USD 100 million trust fund financed by the EU is being set up to provide
       additional support to achieving the development objectives. The project provides
       technical assistance and investments in: (i) youth employment and skills
       acquisition, and (ii) public financial management (accounting and financial
       reporting, budgeting, external audit, procurement, tax administration and
       integrated financial management systems). The beneficiaries are 4 Niger Delta
       states of Bayelsa, Delta, Edo, and Rivers.

Implementation of these three projects would expand the World Bank’s engagement to 22
of Nigeria’s 36 states. The projects all build on the design and implementation
experiences of the SGCBP. Key lessons identified in this ICR are important for and
reflected in the design of the above three operations.

2. Cross River State
The report from Cross River State presents the background and context of the project, the
results framework, and developments in implementation arrangements over time. It
provides details of component objectives, planned activities, implemented activities,
outstanding activities, and plans for inclusion of the latter activities in the follow-on
project. It also reports on performance toward the state’s project results indicators, as
reported in the last project ISR and provides an overview of resource reallocations during
the life of the project. An annex provides details on all planned and completed

The report shares the following lessons, challenges, and recommendations:

“Lessons learned from the project

Government Ownership
One of the lessons learned from the Project was that the State was not actively engaged in
the preparation process. This may have weakened ownership and interests over time.
Drawing on this lesson, the various restructuring exercises of the Project adopted a more
participatory approach, involving the World Bank, Federal Ministry of Finance and State
Government officials. In view of this, during Project Implementation Support Missions
by the World Bank Task Team, participatory dialogue and discussions were always held
with key stakeholders, including the State Governor, Commissioners, Special Advisers
and SSC Members. This helped to improve ownership of the Project.

Strengthening demand/supply side Governance
Based on experience gained in the implementation of the Project, the role of the State
House of Assembly (SHoA) in the implementation of some key components such as
budget reform and external audit in the State became very essential. In addition, the
absence of Civil Society Organisations (CSOs) under the Project weakened demand side
governance. It is, therefore, expected that the follow-on project will strengthen demand
side governance by mainstreaming independent monitoring and public involvement in
some activities, including budget, procurement, M&E and demand for audit.

On the supply side, the Project acknowledged the erosion of the training capacity within
the State’s civil service over the years. Building on recent efforts, it is expected that the


                                             44
follow-on project will (i) stress the implementation of a well thought through training
policy as a condition for effective skills development; (ii) include training of trainers in
areas where training capacity was limited, such as procurement; (iii) focus on training
locally which is more cost effective than training abroad; and (iv) seek to support the
rehabilitation of training institutions and development of curricula in key PFM areas in
the State for sustainability.

Involvement of Local Government
A recent review of the Nigeria country portfolio by the Independent Evaluation Group
(IEG) of the World Bank noted that local government was a missing link between state
level projects and Community Driven Development (CDD) projects. Whilst the full
inclusion of local government was not feasible in this Project given the size of local
governments in Nigeria, it is expected that the follow-on project will include support to
pilot local governments in capacity building activities under PFM, including procurement.
This is expected to complement support provided under other new World Bank CDD
projects (e.g. CSDP and Fadama III) and provide some evidence and lessons for future
full scale support to local governments.

Use of full time Project Coordinators
Prior to the restructuring of in 2007, the position of Project Coordinator was part-time.
This partly contributed to the slow take-off and implementation of Project activities
because the Project Coordinator was often overloaded and had too much demand on his
time. Based on this experience, the restructuring in 2007 made the position of Project
Coordinator full time and this helped to move the project forward in implementation. It is
expected that the State will draw on this experience and keep the position of the Project
Coordinator as a full time job.

Identification of clear component champions
The Project experienced challenges with ownership of certain activities such as
development of budget classification system and implementation of integrated financial
management information systems. To ensure accountability for expected outcomes and to
provide leadership and coordination for implementation of a follow-on project, key
activities should be assigned to officials with the technical competence to drive the
various components.

Linking training to operational needs of the civil service
Finally, it was observed that the Project did not establish a strong link between training,
institutional changes and operational objectives in the State. Because the State has huge
staff training needs for the civil service, the Project should have been selective by
focusing on MDAs and functions that are directly concerned with the reforms targeted in
the various components. It is therefore expected that the follow-on project is purposely
designed for effectiveness and impact, which will hopefully serve as catalyst for service-
wide reforms and capacity development. The knowledge and skills development
activities under the follow-on project (combining on-the-job training/coaching, specific
technical skills development workshops and seminars, study tours, knowledge sharing
and dissemination activities etc.) should not be packaged as isolated and discrete



                                            45
“training components?, but rather integrated in each component under the HRMTE
Component in the Office of the State Head of Service.?

Kaduna State

Component 1- Public Financial Management

(i) A consultant to support the drafting of a new Fiscal Responsibility Law was hired in
2008. A bill has been drafted but was only recently presented to the State Assembly. The
delay in presenting the law is attributed to the omission of stakeholder consultations,
which were only completed in 2011. Irrespective of the passing of the laws, some of the
elements of the law have already been implemented, including the preparation of the
budget on a medium-term basis.

The project did not provide for sensitization of legislators to facilitate the passing of the
laws. Such support would have been helpful, particularly as there are no staff to support
legislators in their deliberations of these fairly technical provisions, and as their exposure
to the issues has been limited. A DFID-funded study tour for legislators to Jos in Plateau
State in 2011 seems to have increased appreciation of the issues and commitment to
possibly passing the laws in the future.

(ii) A new budget manual has been drafted. Medium Term Sector Strategies have been
produced for education, health, agriculture, and water sectors, and budgets since 2011 are
presented with a Medium Term Budget Framework attached. However, the budget lacks
credibility, as execution remains low at 25 percent. Monthly budget execution by
ministries is not published.

(iii) A new Chart of Accounts was introduced in 2009 to allow disaggregation of
financial reports by source of funds, service delivery level of organization (e.g. primary
health centers), programs/projects, functions of government (COFOG), geographic
locations and economic items. Other envisioned reforms (improved commitment control,
payment authorization and financial reporting) would be actualized with the
commissioning of the BATMIS.

(iv) A new external audit law was passed in 2010, facilitated by the project. The project
also provided training in using a computer-aided audit tool (CAAT) to audit a financial
management database similar to the database of the proposed BATMIS for audit purposes.
However, this training was provided years ago and the system is not yet used for external
audit purposes.

The Public Accounts Committee in Kaduna state has not convened in the last six years,
and none of the activities it was envisioned to undertake was implemented.

(v) The project funded the implementation of a BATMIS in Kaduna state. At the time of
the ICR visit, the system was operational, the hardware was installed, and 10 pilot MDAs
were connected to the system through fiber cable or radio. A data entry unit (the Project
Implementation Team) was operational and is currently assisting the 90 or so non-pilot


                                             55
MDAs to enter data at the Ministry of Finance campus in Kaduna. The 2012 budget had
been prepared using the budget module of the system. The system would only be able to
generate in-year budget execution reports during the 2012 financial year. Execution
reports for 2011 were generated using the legacy system.

Commitment to implementation of the BATMIS was mixed throughout the project
lifetime. A legacy system was in place, and the provider was actively promoting its
continued use at higher levels. Some staff in the Accountant General’s Office feared that
the new system would lead to redundancies, which would in turn lead to resistance at
lower levels. As part of the efforts to maintain the legacy system, the provider has
pursued a strategy of continuous improvement of the system in parallel with BATMIS
implementation. At the time of the ICR visit, both systems were being used in parallel.

While the project funded some training in the use of the system, there were not enough
resources to train all users.

The Commissioner for Economic Planning and the state Accountant General referred to
the Governor’s deep interest in the BATMIS and mentioned plans to provide the
Governor with online access to the BATMIS to allow him to generate budget execution
reports.

Component 2 – Public Procurement

The project provided funding for some procurement training and study trips to Uganda
and Senegal. The project also funded a pickup truck for the procurement unit to inspect
completed procurements in the state.

The envisioned procurement reform strategy was not drafted and a law was not passed.
However, the project facilitated the establishment of a procurement agency and funded
equipment in addition to the above-mentioned staff training. A procurement bill was
drafted but met resistance in the executive and in the State Assembly. The PIU attributed
this resistance to the project having pursued international best practice standards too fast
in a low-capacity environment. While the PIU in Kaduna State was under the impression
that only a best-practice law and approach would be acceptable to the World Bank, the
Bank team encouraged a phased approach in other states.

Irrespective of the project’s limited impact with respect to procurement in Kaduna, some
adjustments to public procurement have been introduced under the heading of due
process, mirroring similar reforms at the federal level. Most notable is the
decentralization of some procurement decisions following a threshold approach. Training
and study trips funded by the project are given credit for having raised awareness of due
process and best practice.

Component 3 – Human Resource Management

(i) The component has funded the procurement of an HRMIS. Biometric information on
31,000 state employees has been captured and entered into the database. Additional
employment verification was ongoing at the time of the ICR visit to the state. Initial
analysis of the data by Kaduna State has resulted in the identification of 276 ghost

The intension is to use the nominal payroll in the HRMIS to push salary payments and
other processes through the BATMIS when the nominal payroll is cleaned and verified.
This was expected by the implementing agency to be completed in April 2012.

Implementation of the HRMIS was delayed. Initially, the beneficiaries were under the
impression that the legacy system could function as an HRMIS proper and did not want
to proceed with procurement of the new software and needed equipment. Implementation
only commenced after a review revealed the deficiencies in the legacy system two years
later.

(ii) The project funded consultancy services to facilitate the drafting of a civil service
development strategy. Based on a training needs assessment undertaken in 2009, the
strategy focused on capacity building through training. Beneficiaries interviewed for the
ICR pointed to the following four effects of this activity: (a) the facilitation of the
strategy greatly sensitized high level officials to the importance of capacity building; (b)
the strategy initiated the drafting of a bill to strengthen the formal mandate of the Civil
Service Training Center; (c) the strategy process facilitated the commitment and
allocation of state funds to an expansion of the capacity of the Center, as discussed
below; and (d) the state legislature has committed to allocating 5 percent of personnel
costs in the budget to training. This is reflected in the 2012 State Budget.

(iii) It was planned that the project would fund furniture and other equipment for new
buildings at the Civil Service Training Center. The new buildings were fully funded by
the state outside the project. However, completion of the buildings was delayed and some
construction work was still outstanding at the time of the drafting of the ICR, including
finishing of the roof of an auditorium. Appropriately, the equipment was not procured by
the project and the funds were allocated to other purposes. Equipment will have to be
funded either by the follow-on project or by the Kaduna Government.

Observations and Lessons Learned in Kaduna State
All beneficiaries attributed slow project implementation and delays to the World Bank
Procurement Guidelines, which they found cumbersome. The World Bank was described
as “extra meticulous? and there was a call for “relaxation? in the interest of speeding up
implementation. PIU members suggested considering the nature of the state and people
involved in the project and their absorptive capacity in the application of the procurement
guidelines. They pointed out that procurement committees in practice had to adhere to
both the World Bank guidelines and the state procurement legislation and practices,
making their task difficult and time consuming. Delays were also attributed to late
signing of the subsidiary agreements.

Beneficiaries pointed to the limited state-to-state peer exposure as a possible design
improvement. While some such exposure was in fact organized during the project,
beneficiaries interviewed for the ICR had no recollection of having participated in these
events.

Many beneficiaries pointed to the transformative effect of exposure to good practice from
other states and countries through consultancies and study trips as an important added
value of the project in a setting where such exposure were previously limited or absent.

Some pointed to the need for additional allocations for training, which was seen as
insufficient given the scale of change. This was especially the case for MDAs feeding
data to the HRMIS.

Long-term projects are vulnerable to staff rotations, which are common in the Nigerian
public administration. Changes of personnel imply that technical capacity and
understanding of project objectives and implementation has to be rebuilt. Current World
Bank projects in Nigeria include provisions to reduce staff rotation.

Component 1 – Public Financial Management

(i) The new fiscal responsibility law was signed by the Cross River Governor and thus

disaggregation of reports by source of funds, service delivery level of organizations (e.g.
primary health centers), programs/projects, functions of government (COFOG),

resulted in a substantial reduction in the time needed to issue reports. The project also
facilitated the introduction of State Audit Forums, where state audit reports are subjected
to public scrutiny with the participation of civil society.

The Public Accounts Committee was provided with training. The committee is active
compared to the committees in the two other states. Sanctions are being applied and
refunds requested.

Audit committees have been set up in all MDAs. Contrary to common practice, internal
and external audit are merged.

Component 4 – State Specific Programs

Rehabilitation of the Management Development Institute in Calabar
The project funded the drafting of a business plan for the institute, a thorough renovation
of the institute, some furniture, 20 computers for a computer lab, training for staff, a
study trip to Ghana, and a generator. The business plan has led to an upgrade in the legal
status of the institute. The business plan has yet to be implemented, and the institute is
providing the same kind of introductory training as before the project.

At the time of the ICR visit, the facility stood ready and in good condition. The only
outstanding works activity was the finalization of the entry gate, which, like the perimeter
fencing, is funded outside the project by the state. The computer lab was not operational
and the computers had been in a central state storage for two years, first because the
compound’s perimeter security was inadequate and, second, because cooling was not
installed in the lab. Additionally, while according to staff at the institute the generator
was working, no operating budget for fuel is available, making it doubtful that the lab can
become operational.

Observations and Lessons Learned in Cross River State
Capacity to implement the Project was limited at project effectiveness. The state had not
implemented institutional reforms of this scale or nature before, and World Bank
Procurement Guidelines and financial management processes were unknown to PIU
members. The PIU was not established before project effectiveness. This lack of capacity
and basic knowledge contributed to slow implementation during the first two years of the
project. In addition, the project was seen as having been designed largely in a process
involving the governor’s office and the World Bank team without involvement of
technical officials in the implementing agencies.

A new governor was elected in 2007 and the entire team of commissioners, senior
management, and PIU members was replaced. Restructuring of the project was
undertaken with this new team and was tied to the implementation of the SEED.

As in the other states, the exposure to good practices and experiences outside the state
(within Nigeria and in other countries in the region) was seen by most beneficiaries
consulted to be a major benefit of the project.

The project was initially manned by a part-time project coordinator, but this arrangement
proved to be inadequate. Employment of a full-time coordinator was seen as contributing
to professionalization of project implementation.
